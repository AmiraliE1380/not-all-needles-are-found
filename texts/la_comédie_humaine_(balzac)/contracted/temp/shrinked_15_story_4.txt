In October 1800 a foreigner, Bartolomeo di Piombo, stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and young daughter. He was an over-sixty, grave man, deeply troubled; his wife, though sorrowful, tried to keep calm for their child. Their despair drew passersby, but the stranger’s fierce gaze discouraged sympathy.

After a moment’s thought Bartolomeo made a desperate decision: he handed a dagger to his wife and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier who refused him entry without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted, creating a tense standoff until Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, arrived, recognized him, and took him inside to meet his brother, Napoleon.

Napoleon dismissed his aides to speak privately with Bartolomeo. The Corsican sought asylum and confessed he had killed the Portas, a family who had burned his vineyard and killed his son. He told how he avenged his son by killing all seven Portas, a revelation that shocked Napoleon. Bartolomeo complained that the Bonaparte brothers had been indifferent, reminding them of his prior protection of their family.

Now the leader of a nation, Napoleon felt bound by law and could not openly endorse Bartolomeo’s vengeance. Yet he acknowledged the Corsican tradition of vendetta and offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris, promising financial assistance while warning that law would not tolerate personal justice in the city.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty to the Bonapartes, thanked them for recognizing his misfortune, and seemed hopeful about life in Paris. Napoleon, seeing a potential ally, hinted he might need trustworthy friends in future.

Afterward Lucien asked about their finances and learned the family had arrived in Paris with nothing. Lucien gave him money and promised further help, noting the value of Bartolomeo’s Corsican property would not support them in Paris. Fifteen years passed since Bartolomeo’s arrival, leading to events that require this earlier context.

Servin, an upright, respected artist of about forty married to a general’s dowerless daughter, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to paint. Initially mothers accompanied their daughters, but they soon trusted Servin’s methods enough to send them alone. He admitted only young ladies from wealthy families—refusing aspiring professionals who would demand more thorough instruction—to avoid class disputes. Careful and exacting, he gained a stellar reputation among the aristocracy; when a girl showed aptitude for drawing, mothers recommended Servin without hesitation. He became synonymous with feminine art in the same way Herbault was with bonnets or Leroy with gowns, and while well connected to Parisian elites he retained an independent, patriotic wit.

The studio was arranged for pupils. The attic entrance above his apartment was sealed and reached by a small spiral staircase. The large room, lit by broad panes veiled with green linen shades, had dark-gray walls hung with caricatures and plaster models—a controlled disorder that suited study.

On a bright July day the studio hummed with young women at easels, each with distinct expressions and styles. They were divided into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants and the aristocratic set led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and somewhat apart, appearing anxious. Though the girls laughed and chatted as they worked, Ginevra’s talent and beauty inspired jealousy in the aristocrats. To ostracize her, Amelie slid Ginevra’s easel away from the group, provoking whispers that the insult carried more than personal malice.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return sharpened such tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, met disdain from royalist peers; Amelie’s slight was both personal and political. Yet Ginevra remained absorbed in her work, apparently indifferent to the growing hostility. Her composure only deepened the others’ envy.

A mysterious sound behind a partition shifted the scene: Ginevra discovered Servin was sheltering a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. Excitement and concern immediately followed. Ginevra chose to protect the man, preferring to sit near him rather than risk his exposure. Madame Servin’s arrival soon after suggested she knew of the hidden guest; her calm announcement that her husband could not attend that day heightened the tension.

To mask the faint breathing of the hidden officer, Ginevra began humming a Corsican song. She sketched the officer’s head with a fervor that surprised even her; Servin praised her talent, and the others, including curious Amelie, were drawn to her sheet. Ginevra deftly deflected attention from her drawing, keeping her true subject concealed. Amelie, increasingly suspicious, tried to uncover the secret but was foiled by Ginevra’s quick thinking.

Servin, aware of the undercurrent, moved among his pupils yet watched Ginevra. Their shared knowledge of the fugitive created a tacit understanding, and Ginevra felt empowered to help protect him. As the session ended, her resolve to keep the secret and aid the officer’s escape grew firmer.

Political turmoil pressed on the world beyond the studio, but within its walls the young women navigated rivalries and friendships, united and divided by art, ambition, and allegiance.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra thought they were alone, Servin knocked on the garret door, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, arm in a sling and face pale with pain. He started at Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he replied. "The wound is healing."

Cries in the street announced a death sentence: "Condemned to death!" At the name "Labedoyere," the officer turned ashen and collapsed on a stool, despairing that while Labedoyere faced death for their cause he was hiding. Ginevra blocked his exit. "Can you restore the Emperor?" she asked. "Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?"

He felt helpless after losing his protector and spoke of being willing to die, saying an honest man's life was worth more than two traitors'. He even contemplated selling his life to the executioner. Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining her father's wealth and that he would not blame her for aiding a soldier. She urged him to wait until the storm passed, suggesting escape and service elsewhere.

Her maternal grace and hope moved him. He admired her beauty and felt a surge of emotion. "You are an angel of goodness," he said, though his thoughts kept returning to Labedoyere. In the silence that followed, their acquaintance deepened into an unspoken bond. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere; the officer said he could only avenge him. Ginevra felt stirred by the idea of vengeance and recognized the officer's devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set on his face, Ginevra experienced a magical connection, recalling childhood memories; she left smiling, a spark of hope ignited between them.

The next day she returned to the studio. The officer, now called Luigi, shared stories of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened, admiration growing; speaking Corsican together deepened their closeness. Days passed and Ginevra’s absences from social gatherings attracted gossip. Mademoiselle Thirion, worried by rumors, withdrew from class, and attendance declined. Ginevra, lost in thoughts of Luigi, remained oblivious.

One day Laure, a fellow student, confided that her mother insisted she leave the studio because of the gossip around Ginevra and Luigi. Shocked, Ginevra reassured Laure they would meet again; Laure, saddened, left, and Ginevra felt the pang of loss.

Ginevra and Luigi’s bond deepened into a quiet, mutual love. One evening Luigi signaled to Ginevra but retreated on seeing Laure. Unaware of broader tension, Ginevra soon learned the others had left because of scandal and confronted Servin, who dismissed the rumors, focused on his exhibition. Luigi entered, jokingly claiming responsibility for the lost students; the painter playfully suggested marriage. Their shared silence acknowledged what words had not—the first open recognition of their love. Servin encouraged their union, celebrating the joy they found together. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, confident in her father’s influence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious about her late returns. Bartolomeo paced, worried for his daughter. Although used to her late hours, he and his wife felt unease. Bartolomeo, a man of integrity who prioritized his family’s well-being over appearances, reflected on their close-knit life built on love and mutual respect and on their shared political loyalty to the Emperor. When Ginevra finally arrived home, her father embraced her, relieved; their warm reunion made clear that her parents were her greatest supporters.

Back in the studio, Ginevra and Luigi's relationship blossomed amid challenges. Their love, forged in adversity and rooted in shared ideals, became a source of strength that united them against the world. As they navigated complexities, they remained steadfast in commitment to one another, embodying the resilience of their common cause.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but was presently set down with respect and assured her mother it was a jest to escape. The baroness recovered, Piombo rubbed his hands in courtly delight; the two parents seemed revived.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo announced, offering his hand to Ginevra, who smiled.

“Your mother says you spend more time at the studio. Painting matters more than us?” Piombo remarked.

“Oh, father!” Ginevra protested. Her mother suggested a surprise: a portrait, perhaps. Piombo eagerly asked to see it.

Ginevra, hesitant, answered slowly, “I am very busy at the studio.” Her mother worried at her pale look. Ginevra then declared she would not lie to them: she loved a young man. Her parents were astonished.

“Is he a prince?” Piombo asked ironically; his wife trembled. Ginevra answered: no, he had no fortune—he was Labedoyere’s comrade, proscribed and hidden by Servin. Piombo praised Servin but reproved her for loving any man but her father.

“It does not depend on me to love or not love,” Ginevra replied softly.

“I thought my Ginevra would remain faithful to me,” Piombo lamented. Ginevra reminded him that she had borne his long absences during his devotion to Napoleon; she had never reproached him. “You don’t love me for myself; your reproaches reveal your egotism,” she told him. He flared, she answered gently that she did not blame him—each had reasons: he for his egotism, she for seeking a husband to protect her after a lifetime at his side.

Piombo bristled at the notion of numbering benefits. In anguish, Bartolomeo pleaded, “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man.” Ginevra assured him she need not leave; they could both love him, and he would come to know the man she intended to bequeath to him. Piombo recalled an old opportunity—marriage offered when Napoleon ordered—but she had refused then to avoid leaving him alone.

“You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us,” he cried. Elisa sat stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” Piombo declared, falling into despair. Ginevra, expecting violence, instead found him gentler. She implored him to love her for herself and insisted the man loved her truly and would never make her unhappy.

Comparisons enraged Piombo. “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not love you, I would kill him,” he raged. Ginevra met his gaze calmly: she loved him, though she felt unworthy. When Piombo asked if she loved this man more than them, she insisted the two feelings could not be compared but thought one might be stronger. Piombo vowed she would not marry him; she replied she would.

The baroness feared ruin. Piombo softened briefly, asking Ginevra to say nothing that night and let him think, even begging on his knees. “Ginevra Piombo does not break her word,” she answered. Elisa urged sense: “We are sent into the world to marry.” Piombo called any refusal to obey an unjust order disobedience, but Ginevra argued that refusing an unjust order is not disobedience. He insisted no order from a father could be unjust; she countered that forbidding her marriage would make her hate them. Piombo accused her of not loving them; she denied it, but suggested that if she honored them for their lives only, it would be empty.

“You shall not marry him,” he repeated; she would. The argument faltered into a domestic crisis: Piombo threatened long life and curses on children who dishonor parents; she said all the more reason to marry and be happy. The dialogue cooled him; she clung to him, weeping that she could not outlive him. His anger melted into affection; he played with her as a child. Yet he playfully refused any introduction to Luigi.

Days passed. Ginevra grew affectionate, thanking her father as if for consent, singing and playing for him. A week later, Mrs. Piombo whispered that she had persuaded her husband to receive the man. Ginevra rejoiced and returned home with Luigi on her arm. The officer, who had emerged from hiding after Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre restored his name to the lists, felt anxious to meet Piombo. Ginevra noticed his pallor and fear, which deepened her love.

At home, Piombo sat stern. Ginevra announced she brought a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo glanced at Luigi and sneered that he was not decorated. Luigi, shy, replied he no longer wore the Legion of Honor. Ginevra, embarrassed, pulled a chair for him; his modest conduct seemed to appease Piombo. Madame Piombo, noting Piombo’s softening, attempted to be civil, remarking that Monsieur resembled someone known in Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi confirmed Nina was his sister. Piombo then asked bluntly, “Are you Luigi Porta?”

“Yes,” Luigi answered.

The name staggered Piombo; he and Elisa left the room in silence, abandoning Ginevra in horror. Luigi looked bewildered at Ginevra. The silence thickened; the revelation implied disaster. Ginevra explained that her parents had never spoken of the tragic past; she had been too young to know. The vendetta emerged: her mother said the Portas had killed Ginevra’s brother and burned their house; Piombo had massacred their family in return. Luigi had been tied to the bed before the house was set on fire.

“How did you survive?” Ginevra asked. Luigi recounted being taken to Genoa at six, raised by an old man named Colonna, told he had enemies, and advised to take the name Luigi to evade them. He had no memory of the rest.

Ginevra warned him to leave: he was safe only while in her father’s house; once he left, Piombo’s Corsicans would hunt him. Luigi trembled, asking if the vendetta existed between them. She smiled sadly and nodded: their love must be pure and sincere to give her strength for the hard path ahead, though she foresaw difficulty. Luigi squeezed her hand; Ginevra, resolved, led him from her father’s house and saw him safely to his lodging. She returned with calm determination, greeting her parents, noting her mother’s tears. They dined in silence; in the dim salon Piombo tried to speak but doubted himself, requesting a servant light the fire because he felt cold. Both suffered inwardly, pride and pain masking love; Ginevra’s eyes reproachful, Piombo’s haunted.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, desperate to repair the breach, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study—Ginevra understood her intent and smiled sadly. Two notaries arrived with witnesses to fulfill legal formalities for Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta. Bartolomeo watched the notaries with a foreboding glare. The older notary announced their business: to fulfill the law relative to the daughter’s marriage. Piombo erupted, denouncing any law that destroyed paternal authority, tore a daughter from her father, or deprived an old man of his last consolation. The notary spoke calmly about the law; Piombo’s rage swelled uncontrollably.

In a sudden, desperate motion, he seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but he pushed them aside with the fury of a man who sees his world topple. When Ginevra saw him advance, she knelt: “No, no! I cannot!” he cried, hurling the dagger aside. Ginevra, imploring, begged his mercy, kneeling before him and asking for his consent with a trembling voice. Bartolomeo pushed her away, pronouncing the wife of Luigi Porta could not be a Piombo, declaring he had no daughter, mourning as if his daughter were dead. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added, turning her out.

Ginevra fled to Luigi’s humble lodging. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love,” she said. Luigi answered, “Then I am richer than kings!” She told him her parents had cast her off; he pledged to love her in their place. They resolved to be happy despite the shadow over them—joy and dread mingling in her voice as she declared, “Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!”

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly, resentful of the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud yet shocked by this selfishness, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning Ginevra received trunks of essentials from her mother, accompanied by a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter, full of love and sorrow, deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived and her resolve to abandon him faded. She chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was alone; Luigi brought witnesses to sign the marriage certificate. They were simple men in ordinary clothes, their presence contrasting with the usual joy of a wedding. Ginevra's beauty silenced them, creating a somber atmosphere. As they walked to the mayor's office, they passed other wedding parties full of celebration, which only emphasized the difference in their situation.

Inside, Ginevra and Luigi sat together while the festive mood around them made their own love feel overshadowed. The mayor's clerk noted the absence of Ginevra's parents, and when the mayor asked about their protest, the clerk confirmed it. This bureaucratic formality felt oppressive—a reminder of her family's disapproval. The ceremony was stripped of festivity and reduced to a legal act.

After signing the registers, Ginevra and Luigi left the office with a mix of relief and sadness. Luigi expressed his gratitude, and Ginevra felt a sense of happiness despite the circumstances. They hurried to the church, where an old priest married them in a modest chapel among grander weddings. The priest's remarks about their parents' absence stung, but a sergeant's blessing comforted Ginevra.

Once married they moved into a modest apartment, where Luigi embraced Ginevra, calling it their true wedding. They explored the space, which was thoughtfully arranged with artistic supplies for Ginevra. She was delighted by these details, and they reveled in their shared home, feeling they had found paradise despite its limitations.

In the early days of marriage they indulged in their love, often losing themselves in each other's presence. Reality soon set in and they recognized the need to work. Ginevra began copying paintings and gained clients, while Luigi initially struggled until he used his handwriting skills to offer copying services. His efforts paid off; he eventually obtained enough work to hire assistants, providing a stable income for both.

This period marked the happiest time of their lives, filled with love and labor. They enjoyed evenings together, with Ginevra always smiling and never complaining. Each worked for the other, finding joy in shared effort, though Ginevra occasionally felt pangs of remorse for the happiness she had sacrificed by leaving her family. To commemorate an anniversary, she painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing their love. Another year passed in contentment; no significant events disrupted their quiet happiness.

At the beginning of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition intensified. Madame Porta regretted not taking up genre painting earlier, which might have improved her standing. Ginevra tried portraiture but faced stiff competition from artists in greater need. Despite modest savings, she and Luigi grew anxious about their future.

As winter progressed, Luigi labored relentlessly against a shrinking market for writing. Unable to afford help, he worked longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers avoided lesser-known artists, and even low prices failed to sell her paintings.

Their household deteriorated: love and happiness were overshadowed by looming poverty. They concealed worries from each other—Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears—clinging to the hope that love would sustain them.

One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late, a habit to avoid burdening her. Moved, she took up coloring lithographs, laboring through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. Both pretended to sleep to spare the other their anxieties.

On another sleepless night, feverish and exhausted, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. Confronted, she said she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watching the dawn together and feeling hopeful.

Their struggles deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their financial burdens, but soon they began selling possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, escalating their desperation.

Months later, Ginevra, frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought aid but found none; he sold Ginevra’s paintings and remaining furniture for a meager sum, which only prolonged their hardship. Ginevra showed remarkable strength, managing the household and caring for the boy while often starving herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread and urged him to eat. Realizing her sacrifice, he was consumed by despair and left to find help. In desperation he decided to sell himself as a military substitute, hoping the money would save Ginevra and their child.

After securing an advance, he hurried home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he roused her, she smiled, then saw Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Luigi, horrified, sought aid, but it was too late. In delirium, Ginevra spoke her love for Luigi and sorrow over their lost child. She died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra’s parents sat at home reflecting on their daughter, whom they had not seen in years. Memories surged as they noticed the empty chair that had once belonged to her. Elisa Piombo, Ginevra’s mother, spoke of hoping to rekindle Bartolomeo’s love for Ginevra. Just as Bartolomeo seemed to soften, a man arrived and revealed the tragic fate of Ginevra and Luigi.

The old couple was shattered, realizing their daughter was gone; Bartolomeo’s heart was broken by the loss of his beloved child. The cycle of suffering and retribution had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
