The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou who had fought for the Bourbons in La Vendée, was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—noble but penniless—and took his large family to Paris seeking relief. Unexpected promotion and royal decorations later revived his hopes, and during the 1815 upheavals he reentered service. His fidelity won him the confidence of Louis XVIII; he became a valued adviser, and the family prospered: his sons secured important posts and his daughters advantageous matches, usually with lesser nobility. Emilie, the youngest, became the spoiled center of Parisian society—beautiful, exacting, and indifferent to most suitors, whom she often snubbed for trivial faults. As the King’s health declined and political uncertainty grew, the Comte pressed harder to find Emilie a husband to secure the family’s standing, but her disdain through the winter left their future uncertain.

Nature had given Emilie every quality to play the part of Célimène. Tall and slender, she could shift from dignity to playfulness; her long neck and arched brows conveyed scorn and impertinence, her fine black hair and melodious voice charmed or silenced. Her alabaster complexion rarely flushed, and many rejected suitors accused her of acting, while she claimed only to inspire desire so she might govern it.

Monsieur de Fontaine recognized too late that home adoration had spoiled her. Constant admiration had inflated her pride and selfishness; his gentle counsels slid off her like water from marble. Her affection for her parents was often patronizing, a child’s demand for attention between bouts of play. Though she sometimes showed tenderness, she more often withdrew, jealous of the shared love lavished on others. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that contentment is inward. Determined to resist her sisters’ matches, she nevertheless envied their happiness; her unpredictability led her mother to suspect a touch of madness.

This proud, rebellious temperament was common among beautiful girls of affluent families: feeling misunderstood by mothers, suspecting maternal envy, nursing secret tears, and rebelling against perceived tyranny. They build elaborate ideals of the perfect partner and learn, often painfully, that reality rarely matches fantasy.

Emilie’s standards were precise and unyielding. Her husband must be a peer of France and a soldier bearing an Order, so he could be publicly respected; he must be slim, for she despised corpulence and believed slenderness essential to romance. Her disdain for unsuitable suitors was plain, and her father feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that delay in the marriage market might bring consequences.

During the winter of Charles X, the Comte redoubled efforts, hosting gatherings to attract suitors despite his own controversial reputation. After a season of introductions he hoped Emilie would choose, but her whims held firm. One morning he summoned her, arranged his study to look dignified, and pressed the importance of a wise choice. She interrupted with chilly levity, reminded him of their prior agreement about suitors, and mocked his suggestions for appearance or pedigree. Exasperated, he declared he had done his duty and that the decision now rested with her, warning that marital happiness relies on mutual respect more than mere rank. Touched by his sincerity, Emilie feigned affection but remained proud.

At a family gathering she refused to consider marriage, insisting she would only wed a peer; an uncle jokingly suggested she wait for the Duc de Bordeaux, and she stayed resolute.

That summer at the country house she begged to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the anonymity of the crowd. There she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng—his elegance and dignity sparked immediate attraction. Aloof, he watched another dancer, a pale girl named Clara; curious and jealous, Emilie followed them. Days later, learning he was not the noble she’d hoped, she pressed her uncle for an introduction. The promise intensified her interest; her infatuation grew, nourished by imagination and youthful ideals, still blind to the complexities of love and marriage.

The next morning, before Emilie awoke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he’d insulted, he greeted him with the civility of an old courtier. “I’m a vice-admiral,” he said, “and I think as little of dueling as of smoking a cigar. I had too much grog and ran you down. Let’s shake hands; I’d rather face a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than give his family cause for regret.” The young man accepted, and they rode into the woods. Playful about dueling, the Count fired a pistol into a birch and urged the young man to shoot near his mark; the man obliged and won the admiral’s approval.

During the ride the Count probed codes of gentlemanly conduct. “Do you have debts?” he asked. “No,” replied the young man. “What, you pay for everything?” “Punctually; otherwise, we’d lose credit and respect.” The Count teased about mistresses and youthful extravagance, confessed his own past recklessness, and promised to introduce the young man at the Pavillon Planat, urging him to dinner to meet his nephew, the Comte de Fontaine. The young man—Longueville—found the old man odd but affable and accepted.

When the house party gathered the next afternoon and a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” everyone rushed to appraise the newcomer. Simply and elegantly dressed, Longueville seemed at ease in the Receiver-General’s luxury. His manner and polished education suggested a man of the world; when the admiral mentioned naval architecture he answered aptly, prompting a lady to suspect he had attended the École Polytechnique. Longueville allowed himself to be flattered but courteously declined dinner, saying he was “Hippocrates” to his delicate young sister—an explanation that invited ironic speculation about his profession.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract him, felt slighted when he did not give her the attention she expected. Emilie, usually charming with newcomers, became unusually simple and natural, prompting family comment and varied interpretations—some thinking she was withholding to dazzle later. Longueville’s visit was neither too long nor too short; he left pleased everyone and intensified their curiosity. The Count called him “a cunning rascal.” Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s disappointment and Emilie’s restraint puzzled the family, and a sarcastic remark at dinner—Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s warning that universal perfection must hide a defect, “the worst fault is to have none”—kept speculation alive.

Despite efforts at indifference, Emilie’s feelings soon betrayed her. Longueville returned, and by his third visit everyone saw she was affected. Accustomed to being the center of attention, she was unsettled by someone who attracted her in turn; she tried to resist but could not dismiss him from her heart. Anxiety grew when his modesty and discretion clashed with her curiosity: he never spoke of himself or his family and deftly evaded hints. If she discussed painting, he answered like a connoisseur; if she played music, he proved a good pianist. Once they sang a duet, a moment of intimate harmony, yet he preserved the mystery of his social status.

Her uncle and the family schemed to learn more, eager to meet Longueville’s sister, Clara. They proposed an informal dance to entertain her. Emilie felt joy at the thought of another person to care for and grew gentler and more indulgent; the change pleased her family, who read it as love transforming her selfishness. She believed Longueville’s visits were chiefly for her, and though they never spoke openly of passion, she sensed his affection. She led him into revealing small personal details and realized he, too, was studying her and encouraging her improvement.

On the day of the dance Clara and her brother were principal guests. For the first time Emilie took pleasure in another girl’s triumph and lavished Clara with sincere attention. But Clara, more deft in conversation, steered away from personal topics while charming Emilie—who felt both envious and measured. “Mademoiselle,” Clara said, “I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien that I wanted to know you out of affection for him; isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?” Emilie admitted worry about speaking of those not of noble birth; Clara dismissed such concerns with graceful ease, putting Emilie at ease, and they danced with renewed warmth.

Believing him well born and seeing his grace surpass her ideal, Emilie felt satisfaction and sparkle. As autumn deepened, the couple surrendered to the sweet tide of feeling. Small shared incidents—aimless conversations masking deeper affections, admiring sunsets, gathering daisies, singing passionate duets—knitted them closer, each casual talk and stroll confirming the mutual affection they had carefully concealed.
