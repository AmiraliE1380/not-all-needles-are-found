The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou who had fought for the Bourbons in La Vendée, was ruined when his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—noble but penniless—and took his large family to Paris seeking relief. An unexpected promotion and royal decorations revived his hopes, and during the 1815 upheavals he reentered service. His fidelity won Louis XVIII’s confidence; he became a trusted adviser and the family prospered: his sons obtained important posts and his daughters advantageous matches, usually with lesser nobility. Emilie, the youngest, became the spoiled center of Parisian society—beautiful, exacting, and indifferent to most suitors, whom she often snubbed for trivial reasons. As the King’s health declined and political uncertainty grew, the Comte pressed harder to secure Emilie’s marriage to protect the family’s standing, but her disdain through the winter left their future uncertain.

Nature had given Emilie every quality to play Célimène. Tall and slender, she could shift from dignity to coquettishness; her long neck and arched brows expressed scorn and impertinence, her fine black hair and melodious voice charmed or silenced. Her alabaster complexion rarely flushed; many rejected suitors accused her of acting, while she maintained she merely inspired desire in order to govern it.

The Comte recognized too late that home adoration had spoiled her. Constant flattery had inflated her pride; his gentle counsels slid off her like water from marble. Her affection for her parents was often patronizing—a child’s demand for attention between bouts of play. Though she could be tender, she more often withdrew, jealous of the love shared with others. She blamed fate for her solitude, failing to see that contentment is inward. Determined to resist her sisters’ matches, she nevertheless envied their happiness; her unpredictability led her mother to suspect a touch of madness.

This proud, rebellious temperament described a common pattern among beautiful girls of affluent families: feeling misunderstood by mothers, suspecting maternal envy, nursing secret tears, and building elaborate ideals of the perfect partner, only to learn that reality rarely matches fantasy.

Emilie’s standards were precise. Her husband must be a peer of France and a soldier bearing an Order so he could be publicly respected; he must be slim—she despised corpulence and considered slenderness essential to romance. Her disdain for unsuitable suitors was plain, and her father feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that delay in the marriage market might have consequences.

That winter the Comte redoubled efforts, hosting gatherings despite his controversial reputation. After a season of introductions he hoped Emilie would choose, but her whims held firm. One morning he made a dignified appeal; she interrupted with chilly levity, mocked his criteria, and reminded him of their agreement. He declared he had done his duty and that the decision now rested with her, warning that marital happiness depends more on mutual respect than rank. Touched, she feigned affection but remained proud.

At a family gathering she refused to consider marriage, even when an uncle jokingly suggested she wait for the Duc de Bordeaux. That summer at the country house she begged to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the anonymity of the crowd. There she noticed a striking young man apart from the throng—his elegance and dignity sparked immediate attraction. Aloof, he watched another dancer, a pale girl named Clara; curious and jealous, Emilie followed them. Days later, learning he was not the noble she’d hoped, she pressed her uncle for an introduction. The promise intensified her interest; her infatuation grew, nourished by imagination and youthful ideals, still blind to the complexities of love and marriage.

The next morning, before Emilie woke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. Recognizing the young man he’d insulted, he recovered the civility of an old courtier. “I’m a vice‑admiral,” he said, apologized for having drunk too much, and proposed they shake hands to avoid family trouble. The young man accepted; they rode into the woods where the Count, mock‑dueling, fired a pistol into a birch and encouraged the fellow to shoot near his mark. The man obliged and won the admiral’s approval.

On the ride the Count tested codes of gentlemanly conduct—asked about debts, teased about mistresses, confessed his own past recklessness—and promised to introduce the young man at the Pavillon Planat and invite him to dinner so he could meet the nephew, the Comte de Fontaine. The newcomer, Longueville, seemed odd but affable and accepted.

When the house party gathered and a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” everyone hurried to appraise him. Simply and elegantly dressed, he looked at ease in the Receiver‑General’s luxury; his polished manner and ready knowledge of naval architecture led a lady to suspect École Polytechnique training. He accepted flattery yet declined the Count’s dinner invitation, explaining he was “Hippocrates” to his delicate sister—an excuse that invited ironic speculation about his profession.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine, who had dressed to attract him, felt slighted by his lack of attention. Emilie, usually winning with newcomers, behaved unusually simple and natural, prompting family comment and varied interpretations—some thinking she was withholding to dazzle later. Longueville’s visit pleased everyone and intensified curiosity. The Count called him “a cunning rascal.” A sarcastic dinner remark by Mademoiselle de Fontaine—that universal perfection must hide a defect, “the worst fault is to have none”—kept speculation alive.

Despite efforts at indifference, Emilie’s feelings soon betrayed her. By his third visit it was clear she was affected: accustomed to being the center of attention, she was unsettled by someone who returned her interest. She tried to resist but could not dismiss him. Anxiety grew because his modesty and discretion clashed with her curiosity—he never spoke of himself or his family and deftly evaded hints. If she discussed painting he answered like a connoisseur; if she played music he proved a good pianist. Once they sang a duet, a moment of intimate harmony, yet he preserved the mystery of his social status.

Her uncle and family schemed to learn more, eager to meet Longueville’s sister, Clara, and proposed an informal dance. Emilie welcomed another person to care for, grew gentler and more indulgent, and her family read this as love transforming selfishness. She believed Longueville’s visits were chiefly for her and, though they never spoke openly of passion, she sensed his affection and led him into revealing small details; he, too, studied her and encouraged her improvement.

On the day of the dance Clara and her brother were guests. Emilie took pleasure in Clara’s triumph and lavished her with sincere attention. Clara, more deft in conversation, charmed Emilie and dismissed her class anxieties with graceful ease—“I’ve heard so much about you from Maximilien; isn’t wanting to know you a wish to love you?” they said to each other, and danced warmly.

Believing him well born and seeing his grace surpass her ideal, Emilie felt satisfaction and sparkle. As autumn deepened, the couple surrendered to the sweet tide of feeling: aimless conversations masking deeper affections, admiring sunsets, gathering daisies, singing passionate duets—all small incidents knitting them closer and confirming the mutual love they had carefully concealed.
