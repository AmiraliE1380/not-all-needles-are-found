The Comte de Fontaine, head of an ancient Poitou family, had served the Bourbon cause with intelligence and bravery during the war in La Vendée. Having escaped the dangers that had swallowed many royalist leaders, he joked that he was “one of the men who gave themselves to be killed on the steps of the throne,” a wry pleasantry after surviving the bloody battle of Les Quatre Chemins. Though his property had been confiscated and he was ruined, the staunch Vendeen refused lucrative posts offered under Napoleon. Immutable in his aristocratic faith, he obeyed its precepts in marriage, wedding Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—without fortune but from one of Brittany’s oldest families—despite the blandishments of a rich revolutionary parvenu.

When the second Restoration burst upon Monsieur de Fontaine he was encumbered with a large family and very little income. A nobleman would not solicit favors, yet he yielded to his wife’s wish and left his estate—whose income barely sufficed for the children—to come to Paris. Saddened by the greed of former comrades scrambling for places under the new Constitution, he was about to return to his property when a ministerial dispatch named him maréchal de camp, under a rule allowing officers of the Catholic armies to count the twenty submerged years of Louis XVIII’s reign as service. Days later, without being asked, he received the crosses of the Legion of Honor and of Saint-Louis.

Amazed and shaken by these favors, which he supposed due to the monarch’s remembrance, the Count’s aspirations changed. He no longer contented himself with taking his family to cry “Vive le Roi” in the Tuileries on Sundays; he craved an audience. When he at last penetrated the royal drawing-room, full of old adherents whose powdered heads made a carpet of snow, he met old friends who received him coldly. The princes, however, he found “ADORABLE,” an enthusiastic expression that escaped him when the King—whom the Count thought knew him only by name—came to shake hands and called him the most thorough Vendeen of them all. Despite this ovation, no one inquired about the sum of his losses or the money he had poured into the Catholic regiments; he discovered, a little late, that he had made war at his own cost.

Toward evening he ventured a witty allusion to his affairs. The King laughed—any speech bearing the hall-mark of wit pleased him—but replied with a royal pleasantry whose sweetness was more formidable than anger. An intimate of the King politely hinted that the time had not yet come for settling accounts; bills of longer standing—the history of the Revolution—would remain. The Count prudently withdrew, and, disentangling his sword among the lean legs clustered there, crossed the Tuileries courtyard to his hackney-cab. In the cab he bewailed, loudly enough to compromise him, the change at Court: formerly nobles could ask favors and recover what they advanced; now the cross of Saint-Louis and the rank of brigadier-general would not make good the three hundred thousand livres he had spent. He resolved to speak to the King face to face in his own room.

This scene cooled Monsieur de Fontaine’s ardor still more because his requests for interviews went unanswered. He saw upstarts of the Empire obtain offices once reserved for the highest families. “All is lost!” he exclaimed one morning. “The King has certainly never been other than a revolutionary. But for Monsieur, who never derogates, and is some comfort to his faithful adherents, I do not know what hands the crown of France might not fall into if things go on like this. Their cursed constitutional system is the worst possible government, and can never suit France. Louis XVIII and Monsieur Beugnot spoiled everything at Saint Ouen.”

Preparing to retire to his estate and abandon claims to repayment, the Count was warned by the events of 20 March 1815 of a fresh storm threatening the monarch. Like one who does not dismiss a servant in a torrent of rain, he borrowed on his lands to follow the routed Court into exile, uncertain whether emigration would prove more propitious than past devotion. He perhaps hoped for greater profit from accompanying the King abroad than from dangerous service at home. The calculation was not rash. He was among the faithful five hundred who shared the Court’s exile at Ghent, and among the fifty thousand who returned with it. During the short banishment he was employed by Louis XVIII and found opportunities to show political honesty and attachment. One evening, when the King had nothing better to do, the Count recalled his witticism at the Tuileries with vivacity; the monarch, who never forgot anything, might remember it at a convenient season. The King also observed notes the discreet gentleman had recast with elegant style. These small services stamped Monsieur de Fontaine on the King’s memory as one of the Crown’s loyal servants.

At the second Restoration the Count was among special envoys sent through the departments, charged with absolute jurisdiction over leaders of revolt; but he used his powers with moderation. Afterward he served in the Privy Council, became a deputy, spoke little, listened much, and changed his opinions considerably. Certain circumstances brought him into such intimacy with the Sovereign that once the shrewd monarch said to him: “My friend Fontaine, I shall take care never to appoint you to be director-general, or minister. Neither you nor I, as employees, could keep our place on account of our opinions. Representative government has this advantage; it saves Us the trouble We used to have of dismissing Our Secretaries of State. Our Council is a perfect inn-parlor, whither public opinion sometimes sends strange travelers; however, We can always find a place for Our faithful adherents.”

This ironical speech prefaced a rescript giving Monsieur de Fontaine an appointment as administrator in the office of Crown lands. Thanks to the intelligent attention with which he listened to the King’s sarcasms, his name rose to the King’s lips when commissions carrying handsome salaries were to be appointed. He held his tongue about the favor, and entertained the monarch in those familiar chats Louis XVIII delighted in, as much as in a well-written note, with political anecdotes and parliamentary tittle-tattle. The King was immensely amused by every detail of his “Gouvernementabilité,” a word the facetious Majesty adopted.

Thanks to Comte de Fontaine’s wit and tact, every member of his large family, however young, ended, as he jested, by attaching himself like a silkworm to the leaves of the Pay-List. By the King’s intervention his eldest son found a high fixed position as a lawyer. The second, before the Restoration merely a captain, was appointed to command a legion on the return from Ghent; then, thanks to the confusion of 1815, he passed into the bodyguard, returned to a line regiment, and after the affair of the Trocadéro became a lieutenant-general with a commission in the Guards. The youngest, appointed sous-préfet, soon became a legal official and director of a municipal board in Paris, secure from legislative changes. These bounties, bestowed without parade, fell almost unperceived. Though father and sons each had sinecures enough to enjoy incomes almost equal to a chief of department, their political good fortune excited little envy. In those early days of the constitutional system, few had precise ideas of the civil service domain, where astute favorites found equivalents for demolished abbeys. Monsieur le Comte de Fontaine, who had boasted he had not read the Charter and showed indignation at courtiers’ greed, had proved to his master that he understood, as the King did, the spirit and resources of representative government. Still, despite established careers and pecuniary advantages from four official appointments, Monsieur de Fontaine headed too large a family to re-establish his fortune rapidly.

His three sons were rich in prospects, favor, and talent; but he had three daughters and feared wearing out the monarch’s benevolence. He mentioned them one by one. The King, having taste, did not leave his work incomplete. The marriage of the eldest with a Receiver-General, Planat de Baudry, was arranged by a royal speech that cost nothing and was worth millions. One evening, when the Sovereign was out of spirits, he smiled on hearing of another Demoiselle de Fontaine, for whom he found a husband in a young magistrate of inferior birth but wealthy, whom he created Baron. When the next year the Vendeen spoke of Mademoiselle Emilie de Fontaine, the King replied in thin sharp tones, “Amicus Plato sed magis amica Natio.” A few days later he treated his “friend Fontaine” to a harmless quatrain—an epigram—making fun of these three daughters introduced as a trinity. Report said the monarch found the point in the Unity of the three Divine Persons.

“If your Majesty would only condescend to turn the epigram into an epithalamium?” said the Count, trying to turn the sally to good account.

“Though I see the rhyme of it, I fail to see the reason,” retorted the King, who did not relish any pleasantry, however mild, on the subject of his poetry.

From that day his intercourse with Monsieur de Fontaine showed less amenity. Kings enjoy contradicting more than people think. Like most youngest children, Emilie de Fontaine was a Benjamin spoiled by almost everybody. The King’s coolness therefore caused the Count regret, because no marriage was ever so difficult to arrange as that of this darling daughter. To understand the obstacles we must visit the fine residence where the official was housed at the nation’s expense. Emilie had spent childhood on the family estate, enjoying the abundance that suffices for youthful joys; her lightest wishes had been law to her sisters, brothers, mother, and even her father. All her relations doted on her. Having come to discretion just when her family was loaded with fortune’s favors, the enchantment continued. The luxury of Paris seemed natural to her. As in childhood she had never been thwarted, so at fourteen she was still obeyed when she rushed into the whirl of fashion.

Accustomed by degrees to money, elegant dress, gilded drawing-rooms, and fine carriages, these pleasures became necessary to her as the compliments of flattery and the vanities of court life. Like most spoiled children, she tyrannized over those who loved her and kept blandishments for the indifferent. Her faults grew with her growth, and her parents were to gather bitter fruits of this education. At nineteen Emilie had not yet chosen among many suitors brought by her father’s politics. Though young, she asserted all the freedom of a married woman. Her beauty was so remarkable that to appear in a room was to be its queen; but like sovereigns she had no friends, though she drew everywhere that adulation which a finer nature might have used better. Not a man, not even an old man, could contradict a young girl whose lightest look could rekindle love in the coldest heart.

She had been carefully educated: painted pretty well, spoke Italian and English, and played the piano brilliantly; her voice, trained by the best masters, made her singing irresistibly charming. Clever and intimate with literature, she might have made folks believe that people of quality come into the world knowing everything. She could argue on Italian or Flemish painting, on the Middle Ages or the Renaissance; pronounce on books new or old, and expose defects with cruelly graceful wit. The simplest thing she said was accepted by an admiring crowd like a fez accepted by Turks. She dazzled the shallow; to deeper minds her tact revealed itself, and for them she gave so much charm that, under its cover, she escaped scrutiny. This charming veneer covered a careless heart; the common opinion that no one else dwelt in a sphere lofty enough to understand her merits, and a pride based on birth as well as beauty, governed her. In the absence of the overwhelming sentiment that sooner or later works havoc in a woman’s heart, she spent young ardor in an immoderate love of distinction, and expressed deepest contempt for persons of inferior birth. Supremely impertinent to newly-created nobility, she strove to have her parents recognized as equals by the most illustrious families of Saint-Germain.

These sentiments did not escape Monsieur de Fontaine, who, after his two elder girls were married, smarted under Emilie’s sarcasm. Logical readers may be surprised to see the old Royalist bestow his eldest daughter on a Receiver-General, and his second on a magistrate lately made a Baron. This change in a noble on the verge of sixty—an age when men rarely renounce convictions—was due not merely to residence in modern Babylon, where country folks get their corners rubbed down; the Count’s new political conscience resulted also from the King’s advice and friendship. The philosophical prince had taken pleasure in converting the Vendeen to ideas required by the advance of the nineteenth century and the new aspect of monarchy. Louis XVIII aimed at fusing parties as Napoleon had fused things and men; the legitimate King, perhaps not less clever than his rival, acted contrariwise. The last head of the Bourbons was eager to satisfy the third estate and creations of the Empire by curbing the clergy, as Napoleon had courted old nobility or endowed the Church. In the secret of these royal projects the Privy Councillor became one of the most prudent and influential leaders of the moderate party desiring fusion in the nation’s interests. He preached the expensive doctrines of constitutional government and lent his weight to the political see-saw enabling his master to rule France amid storms. Perhaps Monsieur de Fontaine hoped that a sudden gust of legislation might carry him to the peerage. One rigid principle he embraced was to recognize no nobility in France but that of the peerage—the only families that might enjoy privileges.

“As far from Lafayette’s party as from La Bourdonnaye’s,” he engaged in general reconciliation, which promised a new era and fortunes. He sought to convince families frequenting his drawing-room how few openings would henceforth be offered by civil or military careers, urging mothers to give boys a start in independent and industrial professions, explaining military posts and high government appointments must at last pertain, in constitutional order, to younger sons of peers. According to him, the people had conquered enough of practical government by an elective assembly and appointments to law-offices and the exchequer, which, he said, would always be the natural right of distinguished men of the third estate.

These new notions and the prudent matches they led to met strong resistance at home. The Comtesse de Fontaine remained faithful to ancient beliefs no woman could disown who, through her mother, belonged to the Rohans. Though she had opposed the happiness and fortune awaiting her two eldest girls, she yielded to private considerations husband and wife confide when their heads rest on the same pillow. Monsieur de Fontaine calmly showed by exact arithmetic that residence in Paris, entertaining, the magnificence of the house replacing privations shared in La Vendée, and the expenses of their sons swallowed most of their salary income. They must therefore seize opportunities to settle their girls with wealth. Would they not one day enjoy sixty–eighty–a hundred thousand francs a year? Such matches were rare for girls without portions. It was time to economize, add to the estate of Fontaine, and re-establish territorial fortune. The Countess yielded to such cogent arguments, though perhaps with less grace; but she declared Emilie must marry to satisfy the pride she had helped foster.

Thus events that should have brought joy introduced a leaven of discord. The Receiver-General and the young lawyer were objects of ceremonious formality the Countess and Emilie contrived. This etiquette soon found broader scope; the Lieutenant-General de Fontaine married Mademoiselle Mongenod, daughter of a rich banker; the President found a wife in a young lady whose father, twice or thrice a millionaire, had traded in salt; and the third brother, faithful to plebeian doctrines, married Mademoiselle Grossetete, only daughter of the Receiver-General at Bourges. The sisters-in-law and brothers-in-law found political salons and Faubourg Saint-Germain drawing-rooms so charming and advantageous that they formed a little court around overbearing Emilie. This treaty between interest and pride was not so cemented but that the young despot sometimes caused revolts in her realm. Scenes that the highest circles would not have disowned kept up a sarcastic temper among family members; this, without seriously diminishing public regard, sometimes turned private sentiments far from charitable. Thus the Lieutenant-General’s wife, a Baronne, thought herself as noble as a Kergarouet, and imagined her good hundred thousand francs a year gave her the right to be as impertinent as Emilie, whom she sometimes wished happily married, announcing a peer’s daughter had married Monsieur So-and-So with no title. The Vicomtesse de Fontaine amused herself by eclipsing Emilie in taste, magnificence of dress, furniture, and carriages. The satirical spirit in which brothers and sisters received Emilie’s claims roused her to wrath that a hailstorm of sharp sayings could hardly mitigate. So when the head of the family felt a slight chill in the King’s tacit and precarious friendship, he trembled, all the more since, as a result of her sisters’ mockery, his favorite had never looked so high.

In the midst of these circumstances, and when this petty domestic warfare had become serious, the monarch, whose favor Monsieur de Fontaine still hoped to regain, was attacked by the malady of which he was to die. The great political chief, who steered his bark amid tempests, soon succumbed. Certain then of favors to come, the Comte de Fontaine made every effort to gather the elite of marrying men about his youngest daughter. Those who have tried to settle a haughty and capricious girl will understand the trouble taken by the unlucky father. Such an affair, carried out to Emilie’s liking, would worthily crown the decade the Count had spent in Paris. From the way his family claimed salaries under every department, it might be compared with the House of Austria, which by intermarriage threatens to pervade Europe. The old Vendeen was not discouraged in bringing forward suitors, so much had he the daughter’s happiness at heart, but nothing could be more absurd than how the impertinent young thing pronounced verdicts on her adorers. It might have been supposed that, like a princess in the Arabian Nights, Emilie was rich and beautiful enough to choose from all princes. Her objections were each more preposterous than the last: one had too thick knees and was bow-legged, another was short-sighted, this one’s name was Durand, that one limped, and almost all were too fat. Livelier, more attractive, and gayer than ever after dismissing two or three suitors, she rushed into the winter festivities and balls, where her keen eyes criticized the celebrities of the day and delighted in encouraging proposals she invariably rejected.

Nature had given Émilie de Fontaine every advantage for the part she loved to play. Tall and slight, she could assume dignified or frolicsome airs at will. Her somewhat long neck let her affect graceful attitudes of scorn and impertinence. She had cultivated the feminine turns of head and gestures that emphasized a smile. Fine black hair and strongly arched brows gave her a proud look, which her coquettish instincts knew how to soften into gentleness or make terrible with a stare. When she meant to charm, her clear voice had melody; when she meant to silence, it could be decisive. Her colorless face and alabaster brow were like a lake ruffled by a breeze, soon regaining serenity. Young men often accused her of acting; she justified herself by inspiring desire and then subjecting admirers to contemptuous caprice. Among fashionable women none knew better how to assume reserve before a man of talent, or to display the insulting politeness that treats an equal as inferior. Wherever she went she seemed to accept homage rather than compliments; even in a princess she would have made a chair resemble an imperial throne.

Monsieur de Fontaine discovered too late how utterly his daughter’s education had been ruined by the family’s tender devotion. Early admiration had fed Émilie’s pride and self-confidence. Universal subservience developed the selfishness natural to spoiled children, who, like kings, toy with everything that comes to hand. Her graces and talents hid these faults from most, but nothing escaped a good father’s eye. He often tried to teach her the more important pages of life, in vain. He had to lament her capricious indocility and ironical shrewdness too often to persevere in correction. He contented himself with occasional gentle advice and watched in sorrow as his tenderest words slid from her heart as if it were marble. A father’s eyes are slow to be unsealed; it took experience for the old Royalist to perceive that Émilie’s rare caresses were bestowed with condescension. She was fond of her parents, but by sudden whims often kept aloof and complained of having to share their hearts with others, jealous even of her brothers and sisters. After creating a desert about her, she accused all nature of her unreal solitude and wilful griefs. Strong in her twenty years, she blamed fate, not knowing that the mainspring of happiness is within. She would have fled the planet to escape marriages like her sisters’, yet was horribly jealous to see them married, rich, and happy. Sometimes her mother suspected a touch of madness.

Such hidden pride is common among young women of high families and great beauty. They imagine mothers cannot sympathize with their young souls and will dress them to eclipse them; hence secret tears and dumb revolt against supposed tyranny. They compose schemes of life and paint a model to which any future lover must correspond. Over years the colours fade; one day they are astonished to find themselves happy without the nuptial poetry of their day-dreams. Mademoiselle Émilie de Fontaine had drawn such a programme for suitors; hence her disdain and sarcasm.

She insisted on rare qualifications. Though young and of an ancient family, her husband must be a peer of France; she could not bear not to see her coat-of-arms on the panels of her carriage or to drive like princes down the Champs-Élysées. He must be a soldier—though she reserved the right to make him retire; he must wear an Order so sentries might present arms to them. He must have amiable temper, a fine figure, intelligence, and above all be slender. To be lean, she considered indispensable. Émilie had an exacting ideal; a young man who failed these conditions did not earn a second look. “Good Heavens! see how fat he is!” was her utmost expression of contempt. To her, respectable corpulence meant incapacity for sentiment and unfitness for civilized society. Fat seemed a misfortune in a woman, a crime in a man. These paradoxes amused by her lively rhetoric. Her father feared her affections would become the subject of ridicule and that her eccentric notions might become bad style. He trembled at the thought of the pitiless world laughing at a young woman who remained so long on the stage without concluding the drama she was playing.

So during the first winter after Charles X’s accession the Count redoubled efforts—helped by sons and sons-in-law—to assemble in his official rooms the best matches Paris and the departments could offer. The splendour of his entertainments and truffles rivalled the banquets by which ministers secured votes. The Honorable Deputy was pointed at as an influential corrupter of legislative honesty. A whimsical result: his efforts to get his daughter married secured him splendid popularity. Mocking Liberals accused him of selling truffles twice over, but the Poitevin gentleman was so noble that he avoided the epigrams aimed at ministers and directors. After the campaign, the Count believed this procession of suitors would not be a dissolving view to his daughter. He was inwardly satisfied at having fulfilled his duty and hoped among so many hearts there might be one to which her caprice would give a preference. Tired of his daughter’s conduct, one morning toward the end of Lent—when the Chamber’s business did not demand his vote—he determined to ask her views.

While his valet powdered his bald head and set the room in order, the Count prepared himself, anxious not to compromise his dignity. Émilie came humming an air, kissed him with light carelessness and mocked the gravity of his summons. He told her gravely that she must make such a choice of husband as would secure durable happiness. She interrupted him in a coaxing tone, jesting that the armistice as to suitors was not yet expired. He forbade jesting on so important a matter, reminding her that the efforts of those who loved her had been concentrated on settling her suitably and that she would be guilty of ingratitude if she met proofs of kindness with levity.

Émilie sat facing him, feigning solemnity, then mocked his troublous face. He persisted, explaining he could no longer compromise his reputation by recruiting the regiment of dancers she put to rout each spring. She had caused dangerous misunderstandings; she was twenty-two and should have been married nearly three years. Her brothers and two sisters were well provided for, and the expenses of their marriages and her mother’s housekeeping had so reduced their income that he could hardly promise a hundred thousand francs as her portion. He would henceforth think only of providing for her mother. He hoped his children had agreed not to expect future compensation for an over-favored sister.

Émilie tossed her head. He asked her not to depreciate those who loved her. Only the poor are generous as a rule; the rich have reasons for not handing over twenty thousand francs to a relation. He spoke of suitors: Monsieur de Manerville he dismissed as mincing and fair; Monsieur de Beaudenord as not noble and stout; Rastignac had been made a banker by Madame de Nucingen; the Vicomte de Portenduère was a mere boy with no fortune. She wanted at least to be a countess like her mother. He told her frankly that peers were a recent creation and had no great fortunes; the wealthiest peer had not half the income of the least rich English lord. Thus French peers looked to marry great heiresses; the necessity to marry for money would last for long. Her fastidiousness might cost her the best years of her life, yet her attractions might work a miracle, for men often married for love now. He counselled modesty: conjugal happiness rests less on brilliance and fortune than on reciprocal esteem.

Touched, Émilie concealed her emotion and coaxed him until his brow cleared. When he gave his consent beforehand—saying he would help her but her choice must be serious and final—she slipped from his knees, proud of being mistress of her fate, and went off singing.

That day the family kept an anniversary fête. At dessert Madame Planat spoke of a young American of immense fortune who had proposed to her sister. Émilie dismissed bankers as money dealers. The Baron de Villaine noted she did not like lawyers either; the Lieutenant-General joked about slimness. The Baronne de Fontaine declared Émilie wanted a fine name, a fine young man, prospects, and a hundred thousand francs a year. Émilie retorted she would take no advice but her own about a matter that concerned no one but herself. Silence fell as guests looked from the spoilt girl’s pout to Monsieur and Madame de Fontaine’s severe faces. The Count announced he had made his daughter mistress of her own fate; the words declared fatherly affection was weary of contest. Relations and guests ceased to take interest in the haughty girl’s prospects; from that moment few troubled to help her.

When fine weather came and the budget was voted, the whole family went to Aulnay, Antony, and Châtenay; Émilie accompanied her sister to the Villa Planat at Sceaux, less from affection than because fashion ordained leaving Paris in summer. The green seclusion suited good style and the official position. The “Bal de Sceaux” had a rotunda open on all sides sheltering a dancing-floor; it was a medley of town clerks, medical disciples, and youths—many bourgeois marriages began there. This mixture gave the Sceaux balls a certain spice; Émilie, who liked to be seen and to see, was first to propose playing at being common folk and promised herself amusement in mingling with the crowd. She imagined leaving bewitching glances on shopkeepers’ hearts, laughed at the damsels’ airs, and sharpened pencils for scenes to sketch in a satirical album. Sunday could not come soon enough.

She expected to comment, to satirize, but soon the motley crowd’s charm gave way to the sight of one figure who fascinated her. Seated by a column, a stranger leaned absorbed; his arms were folded, posture elegant and unaffected. Half-turned, his gaze followed a dancer, betraying feeling. His slender frame recalled noble proportions; fine black hair curled over a high forehead. His linen was fine, gloves fresh, boots well made; he had none of the trinkets of National Guard dandies. A black ribbon with an eyeglass hung over a fashionable waistcoat; long curled lashes shaded his eyes. Melancholy and passion showed in his face, an olive complexion and a mouth ready to smile added grace. He seemed more than simply handsome; one wanted to know him. Émilie, always scanning a room like a critic, made these observations in a minute and found in the stranger the subject of a secret admiration.

She did not yet think “He must be a peer!” but hoped he might be noble. Rising and followed by her brother the General, she made her way to the column, feigning to watch the quadrille but really watching him. The stranger politely moved to make way and leaned against another pillar. Émilie, nettled by his politeness, contrived to attract his attention by raising her voice and laughing; none of her arts succeeded. She followed his eyes and discovered the cause of his indifference: in the quadrille a pale girl danced whose face recalled Girodet’s Ossian divinities. Émilie fancied she recognized her as a distinguished milady living nearby. Her partner was a lad of fifteen. Émilie watched more closely when the girl returned to her place. The stranger went up to her, and, leaning over, said in a gentle but commanding tone, “Clara, my child, do not dance any more.” Clara pouted, bowed, and finally smiled. When the dance ended, the young man wrapped her in a cashmere shawl with lover’s care and seated her sheltered from the wind. Émilie saw them rise and walk as if to leave and followed under the pretext of admiring garden views. The young man turned toward the crowd and cast a glance at her; she enjoyed the feeble pleasure of being looked at. The lady did the same. Was this jealousy?

The next day Émilie expressed a wish to ride. She habitually rode early and sought the hamlet where Lady Dudley was staying. Despite her perseverance, she did not meet the stranger at first. She went several times to the Bal de Sceaux without seeing the Englishman who had entered her dreams. Though obstacles often quicken passion, Émilie nearly gave up. Clara was not English and the man escorting her did not dwell in Châtenay, so the hope of finding him there faded.

One evening, riding with her uncle—who enjoyed a truce from gout—she met Lady Dudley in an open carriage with Monsieur Vandenesse. Émilie recognized them and was piqued. She spurred her horse and the old sailor, the Comte de Kergarouet, moderated his horse to follow quietly. A sudden incident: Émilie’s quick motion nearly caused a collision; the Comte spurred to come between her and a young man on the bank, forcing him onto the grassy verge. The Count, affronted, rebuked the youth; an angry exchange ensued. The young man objected to provocation and hinted at duels and honour. The Count called him to account and demanded silence before the young lady. The stranger smiled and gave his visiting card, saying he lived at a country-house at Chevreuse; then he hurried away.

“You very nearly damaged that poor young counter-jumper, my dear,” the Count said to Émilie. He scolded her for not knowing how to hold her horse; she retorted the fault was his for riding poorly. The Count suggested the stranger was probably a shopkeeper, while Émilie thought him to have fine manners and perhaps noble birth. The admiral teased her about her haste to judge; she coloured but grew animated. The Count, who understood her temper, promised to be her confidant and to bring the stranger into the drawing-room—if she would behave. He made her give a solemn promise to be gentler, less noisy, less wilful, to spend less, and to tell him everything. Émilie signed the treaty with a kiss on her brow.

At home the Count read the visiting card: “Maximilien Longueville, Rue de Sentier.” He told Émilie a half-jest that the young man belonged to a historical family and if not a peer he would infallibly become one. Émilie tried every coquettish art to make him reveal more; the admiral enjoyed the scene, teasing until she sulked and, by entreaty, recovered. He spelled Longueville and showed only that; he refused to reveal more. This intensified Émilie’s secret sentiment. That night she evolved brilliant pictures of gilded married life: the imagined wealth, the expected splendour. Thanks to chance she could see something very unlike a chimera at the fountain-head of her imaginary fortune. Ignorant, as young girls are, of the perils of love and marriage, she was passionately captivated by externals. This birth of feeling—sweet but cruel—was like other youthful mistakes that exert fatal influence on the lives of inexperienced girls who trust only their judgment to secure future happiness.

Next morning, before Emilie was awake, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. In the courtyard of an elegant little villa he recognized the young man he had brusquely insulted the day before and approached him with the abounding politeness of the old court.

He made a brisk apology, insisting that, though a vice-admiral of seventy-three, he had erred the night before after too much grog and was more of a duelist in jest than otherwise. The admiral’s candid cordiality disarmed the young man; despite a chilly reception, Longueville returned his hand. The admiral invited him to dinner at the Villa Planat and proposed introducing him to the Comte de Fontaine and five of the prettiest women in Paris, urging him to accept for his nephew’s sake. Longueville demurred only enough to be courteous.

When they rode into the woods, the admiral stopped, took a pistol, and shot into a birch fifteen paces away to show he was not afraid of a duel. Longueville replied by shooting close to the admiral’s hole, earning hearty praise. During the ride the admiral questioned him about the trivial accomplishments of a gentleman—debts, gallantries, savoir-faire—and mocked Longueville’s apparent refusal to embrace the less scrupulous pleasures of youth. Longueville’s seriousness and punctiliousness troubled the admiral, who declared him not yet “fully fledged,” but said he would take him to the Pavillon Planat and hoped the boy would learn a little immodesty of life. Longueville, while amused, resolved not to trust the old man too far.

The next afternoon, when the house party were in the drawing-rooms, a servant announced “Monsieur DE Longueville.” The arrival stirred curiosity, particularly about the gentleman who had bested rivals. Longueville’s simple but elegant dress, easy manners, polished grace, and pleasant voice won immediate approval; he seemed perfectly at home amid the Receiver-General’s ostentation. His conversation combined worldly knowledge with a learned touch; he answered the admiral on naval architecture so well a lady guessed École Polytechnique. He declined pressing invitations to dinner, saying he must attend his young sister’s needs—thus maintaining the air of a man of independence.

At once the family fixed their eyes on Emilie. She had planned arts and languid graces to charm strangers, and in Longueville’s presence her affectation dropped; whether because the stranger’s voice and manner genuinely enchanted her or because she had fallen, she became simple and natural—arguably more beautiful. Her uncle suspected artifice, but the household watched the exchange keenly. During dinner everyone attributed qualities to Longueville; Emilie, roused by an uncle’s sarcasm, remarked epigrammatically that perfection often hid defect and warned against judging at first sight: “Those who please everybody, please nobody,” she said, adding that the worst fault was having none.

Like many girls in love, Emilie tried to hide her feelings, but within a fortnight the family had guessed. Longueville called a third time and Emilie believed it was mainly for her, a discovery that brought intoxicating pleasure and wounded pride. Accustomed to be the center, she recognized an attraction outside herself and could not dislodge the charming image of the young man. His unexpected modesty and discretion inflamed her anxiety: he never spoke of himself, his family, or pursuits. Her hints and traps for information were artfully evaded. If she spoke of painting he answered like a connoisseur; if she played he accompanied her; one evening he delighted all by singing a duet. He baffled questions about whether he was a professional singer, preserving his mystery with such tact that curiosity never overstepped good breeding.

Emilie turned to his sister Clara, believing a woman might divulge his secrets. With the admiral’s help she brought Clara to the Villa Planat. Emilie’s joy was renewed: life was delightful when linked with another. Whether the change made her better or simply occupied her, she became more gentle and indulgent; people watched with delight as her selfishness seemed to soften into love. She led Longueville with skill: he observed and studied her, and she, perceiving it, sought to remedy defects in her education—an early homage to love and a chastening reproach. The lovers walked together, admired sunsets, gathered daisies, sang duets—petty but enchanting incidents that strengthened their attachment.

At the informal dance Clara and her brother were the principal guests. For the first time Emilie felt sincere pleasure in another girl’s triumph and lavished on Clara real kindness. Emilie wanted to learn secrets; Clara, however, displayed more tact than her brother. She kept conversation untied to personal matters yet managed to elicit from Emilie—without offense—confessions that revealed her nature. When Emilie, who had spoken scornfully of the commonalty earlier, feared she had displeased Clara, the girl answered gently, “I have heard so much of you from Maximilien that I desired to know you, out of affection for him; but is not a wish to know you a wish to love you?” This response delighted Emilie and reconciled the parties; she danced with new freedom and watched Longueville with admiration. The lovers’ fingertips thrilled in the dance; they understood each other as never before.

By early autumn their sentiment had deepened and many small incidents knit their feelings. The admiral observed the relation wryly, and the Count and Countess Fontaine grew nervous. The Count, who had ceased being indifferent to his daughter’s prospects, went to Paris to inquire about Longueville and could find nothing. He warned Emilie to be prudent; she mockingly promised to wait for his permission before confessing love, assuring him she had made an irrevocable choice. The Count reminded her that they knew nothing of Longueville’s family; Emilie answered that good breeding, manner, and the leisure of a gentleman in pursuit of her were evidence enough.

One day, at the lovers’ arbor, Emilie intended to learn the man’s origins without compromising herself. Both were proud and each feared to confess; Longueville kept silence and the romance was sustained by looks and reserve. He suspected Emilie’s prejudices and, before attempting to undo them, wished to be sure she loved him. She, for her part, sought assurance. On a rustic bench, Maximilien surprised her and declared their mutual secrecy.

Emilie at last asked a question that weighed on her: “Are you of noble birth?” Longueville, touchingly, said he would answer only after she replied to his question: “What is your reason for asking?” The suspense broke when he declared, “I love you.” Her joyful exclamation made the question redundant; the lovers embraced the meaning of love rather than pedigrees. When she brought up dignity and titles, he replied, “I have no titles to offer my wife… But if I choose one of high rank and among women used to a wealthy home, love gives everything, but once married they need something more.” He hinted, more gravely, that in less than two months he might be proud of what he could offer her; he kept that practical secret locked in his heart, saying the success on which his happiness depended would make a difference. Emilie rejoiced, reassured that he would soon be able to offer the comforts she expected. They sang and were warmly applauded; the day strengthened her attachment.

Left alone with her father, Emilie told him she had learned everything and was happier than she had hoped; she declared Longueville the only man she could marry. The Count said he would act accordingly, but warned again that the young man was unknown to him. The admiral’s explanation was evasive: he had only judged the man by his manners and skills—shooting, riding, dancing, knowledge of wines and arithmetic. He had found the card Longueville gave him—Rue du Sentier, No. 5. Inquiry there discovered Palma, Werbrust & Co., wholesale dealers in muslins and printed cottons—flatly commercial. The Count discovered that Longueville the deputy had an interest in such a house and that Longueville had one son of thirty-two whom he had largesse for so he might marry a minister’s daughter. The admiral observed that anyone could call himself Longueville; the address supported the Count’s unease. The Count thought the firm half-ruined by speculation, while the admiral only laughed that fortune could be made or remade by a man’s past dissipations.

A few days later Emilie, wrapped in a new fur cape, was shopping with two sisters-in-law when she suddenly saw Maximilien behind the desk at a shop, paying out change to a workwoman and handling patterns. A cold shiver ran through her; she concealed it with practiced breeding and said, “I knew it,” with affected nonchalance. She went straight to the desk. Longueville looked up, bowed, and handed a thousand-franc note to the girl in secret, saying he would settle that account and pleading private forbearance. Emilie replied with sarcasm—she would not concern herself with his business. Longueville asked, in a voice shaken, if she meant it; she turned her back with amazing insolence. After buying the cape and getting into the carriage, she cast one last glance and saw him standing with folded arms, unshaken. Their looks met: implacable, each hoping to wound, and in a moment they were as if in different hemispheres.

Vanity and prejudice withered the tender prospect. Emilie, mortified by the merchant connection, suffered anguish: her face grew streaked and blotched, sometimes with a green pallor; she laughed by fits to hide despair and barbed others with sarcasms and violent paradoxes heaping insult on merchants. At home she had a febrile attack which for a time was dangerous; after a month the physician and family restored her.

People hoped the lesson would correct Emilie, but she plunged back into fashion and irony, declaring she had made only a trivial mistake. She set herself to satirize merchants and calico dealers; none but nobles, she said, should wear antique French costumes, and if she had a vote she would edict merchants to be branded. Yet those who loved her saw a tinge of melancholy under the banter: Maximilien still reigned over her heart. Sometimes she was as gentle as in the brief summer of love; sometimes unendurable; all excused it as the result of secret suffering. Her uncle tried consolation with increased prodigality, which often helps Parisian girls.

At the first ball she attended, at the Neapolitan ambassador’s, she saw Maximilien across the room with an attaché. She asked scornfully whether he was the man’s friend; “Only my brother,” the attaché answered. Emilie pricked up her ears. The attaché explained that his brother had sacrificed much to him: his sister Clara and the brother had renounced their shares so that the attaché might be first; the elder brother had joined a banking firm and made a speculation in Brazil that might make him a millionaire. He talked lavishly about how his brother had given up 100,000 francs a year to make an eldest son of the attaché—revelations that suggested the family was complicated and involved in commercial ventures and political ambitions. Emilie, skilled at questioning and seizing their confiding partner’s chatter, partly learned that the Longuevilles had commercial ties and conversely that the brothers had made sacrifices.

Emilie’s injured pride continued to show. Late in the evening she found herself beside the group where Maximilien sat chatting with a Neapolitan duchess. The duchess, who declared she had more passion than Frenchwomen, hinted at sacrifice; Emilie, stung, took the chance to deliver another of her cruel paradoxes: a woman would follow her lover to a desert and live in a tent—“but not to sit in a shop.” The disdainful gesture implied that commercial life was beneath a woman of rank; in a tone shaped by her education, Emilie struck again at the man she loved. Thus, by an education that set prestige above character, she delivered the second and fatal blow to her budding happiness.

Longueville, in low voice as the party rose, assured Emilie he desired her happiness above all and told her he was starting for Italy in a few days. She joked about him going with a duchess; he answered with deeper gravity that he went perhaps with a wound that never healed. She tried to be imperious—“You will find me married on your return,” she said coquettishly—and he answered soberly, “I hope so.” Two weeks later he left with his sister for Italy, leaving Emilie prey to violent regret.

During their separation the attaché brother took up the cause and avenged Emilie’s disdain by making known the lovers’ estrangement. He told the world of the girl who condemned bankers and of the scandal of a misses’ crusade against traders; the Count de Fontaine obtained an appointment in Russia for the attaché to protect his daughter from ridicule. Meanwhile the Ministry raised a levy of peers and gave Monsieur Guiraudin de Longueville a peerage, the title of Vicomte; Monsieur de Fontaine also received a peerage for fidelity. These promotions complicated the picture and foreshadowed further changes.

Emilie, now of age and married to the Comte de Kergarouet—whose family and an elderly admiral uncle had secured the match—changed her outward bearing to the strict decorum of an older school. She lavished attention on her uncle, riding in his carriage, bringing him his stick, reading to him and learning to play piquet to match him. The admiral had often jocularly declared he was too old to be captured by a corvette, yet Paris heard of Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s marriage to the Comte. The match allowed Emilie to maintain a high place in society: she gave splendid entertainments to distract herself and flashed a face that hid melancholy. The old husband quipped about embarking as pilot at seventy-two; people observed the curious inequality between an energetic, youthful Countess and an older husband who sometimes seemed more guardian than spouse. The admiral’s conduct, ambiguous between patriarchal oversight and affectionate pride, gave room for gossip; nevertheless, Emilie performed with exemplary propriety and society could find no real fault.

Two years after her marriage, in a Faubourg Saint-Germain drawing-room where she charmed the company in a manner of the old school, she heard the Vicomte de Longueville announced. Playing piquet with the Bishop of Persepolis, she turned and saw her former lover restored to youth’s freshness. His father and brother had died—the latter in Saint Petersburg—so Maximilien inherited the title and estate. His fortune matched his learning and merits. The day before he had dazzled the Assembly with his eloquence. There he stood, a peer, free and graced with every advantage Emilie had once demanded. The society thronged him; every mother with a marriageable daughter made advances to a man of his character. Emilie, however, who alone knew the secret of his steadfastness and noble nature, realized the full extent of her error. She looked at the admiral, who still lived on, and cursed the follies of her youth.

The scene closed with an Episcopal jest: Monsieur de Persepolis, addressing the lady who had lost the best match in his view, said with a sly grace, “Fair lady, you have thrown away the king of hearts—I have won. But do not regret your money. I keep it for my little seminaries.” The remark, half satire, half pity, emphasized society’s judgment: Emilie had sacrificed true worth to prejudice and vanity, and though she had gained title and rank, she had lost the man who might have been both a peer and equal in worth—too late a discovery of what really counts in a husband.
