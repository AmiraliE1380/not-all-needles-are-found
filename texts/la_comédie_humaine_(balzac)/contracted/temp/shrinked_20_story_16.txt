In the spring of 1822, Parisian doctors sent a young man, M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil, to Lower Normandy for recovery from an inflammatory illness, likely caused by overwork or excess. Seeking rest, a light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux, where he was warmly welcomed by his relatives, who viewed his arrival as a blessing.

Small towns share common traits, and after a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, Gaston quickly met the local elite, who embodied the typical characters found in provincial France. Among them was a noble family with a long but obscure lineage, whose patriarch was a determined sportsman, dismissive of modernity and the new political order. His wife, a loud and domineering figure, believed their daughters would secure wealth through their name alone. They clung to outdated customs, reflecting a bygone era of nobility without the accompanying wealth or influence.

In contrast, another family, wealthier but of more recent origin, embraced contemporary Parisian trends. The wife, though somewhat behind the fashion, was a woman of society, while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, was savvy in financial matters and well-versed in political gossip. Their Royalism was less fervent than that of the noble family, as they engaged with modernity while still maintaining a connection to their roots.

The local bishop, a former Vicar-General, navigated the tensions between these two families, respected for his religious standing but often reminded of his plebeian origins. Other notable figures included retired military officers and their families, who, while maintaining a semblance of aristocracy, were more concerned with local affairs than national politics. The women in these families often dressed in a manner reminiscent of courtly life, yet their lives were marked by routine and tradition.

This exclusive social circle also included a few wealthy townspeople who had managed to infiltrate the aristocracy, often viewed with skepticism by the older generation. The presence of ecclesiastics added a touch of diversity, as they brought wit and a bourgeois element to the otherwise staid gatherings.

The collective mindset of this society was characterized by antiquated beliefs and a resistance to change. Conversations echoed the same themes, with members offering immutable judgments on various matters, creating a stagnant intellectual environment. New ideas were rarely welcomed, and any outsider who dared to critique their way of life was quickly labeled as a misfit.

Before Gaston arrived, he had already been evaluated by the local elite, with his cousin sharing details about his fortune and family background. At just twenty-three, he was welcomed into their fold, recognized as a promising young man with an income of eighteen thousand livres from his land in the Auge valley. His father was negotiating to acquire additional woodland, further solidifying Gaston’s status.

However, Gaston’s family dynamics were complicated by the existence of an elder brother, who was consumptive and likely to die soon, a fact that was conveniently omitted by Mme. de Sainte-Severe. Initially, Gaston found amusement in observing the quirks of his new acquaintances, sketching mental portraits of their eccentricities and enjoying their quaintness. Yet, as time passed, he grew weary of their monotonous lives, feeling trapped in a cloistered existence devoid of stimulation.

This sense of confinement led to a crisis of spirit, a blend of ennui and discontent. As he adapted to the unchanging environment, he began to lose touch with the vibrant life he once knew in Paris. The once vivid memories of intellectual engagement faded, and he risked becoming just another fossil among the fossils, content to exist in a dull routine, much like the companions of Ulysses who settled for a life of complacency.

One evening, Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room, where family portraits overlooked four card tables and sixteen people engaged in whist. As he digested a delightful dinner, he began to appreciate the customs of the provincial life around him, understanding why these people clung to their old cards and threadbare tablecloths. He found a certain philosophy in their monotonous routine and ignorance of luxury, leading him to question the value of opulence. The vibrant life of Paris felt like a distant memory.

His attention was caught by a young lady with red hands and a shy demeanor, initially appearing awkward and unattractive. However, a chance remark about Mme. de Beauseant stirred emotions within him, reminiscent of the stirring of good music amidst a tedious opera. An elderly lady mentioned that the Vicomtesse was unwell and had declined an invitation to dinner, which piqued Gaston's interest. The conversation revealed that Mme. de Beauseant had a scandalous past involving M. d’Ajuda-Pinto, leading to her isolation in Courcelles. Despite her noble lineage, society shunned her due to her separation from her husband, a situation that Gaston found intriguing.

Gaston was captivated by the idea of Mme. de Beauseant, viewing her as a figure of hope and desire amidst the dullness of provincial life. He felt a growing obsession with her, imagining her as a woman worth pursuing in a world that felt stagnant. His thoughts were consumed with how to gain access to her, recognizing the challenges posed by her intelligence and social standing. The next morning, he found himself wandering near Courcelles, hoping for a chance encounter, his imagination fueled by romantic notions.

He considered writing to her but hesitated, fearing rejection. Instead, he devised a plan to enlist M. de Champignelles to facilitate an introduction. He approached the Marquis, claiming he had a delicate matter to discuss with the Vicomtesse and requested that the Marquis inquire if she would receive him. Gaston’s charm and sincerity convinced the Marquis, who was eager to assist a pretty woman. M. de Champignelles hurried to Courcelles, where he relayed Gaston’s request, piquing Mme. de Beauseant’s curiosity despite her initial reluctance.

After some deliberation, she agreed to meet Gaston, intrigued by the mystery surrounding him. Meanwhile, Gaston was filled with excitement and anxiety about the upcoming meeting, hoping to impress her. He meticulously prepared for the encounter, believing that his appearance would influence her perception of him. Unbeknownst to him, true attraction stemmed from wit and character rather than mere looks.

As Gaston approached Courcelles, he felt a mix of confidence and nervousness. He was aware that entering a room with just one woman was more daunting than facing a crowd. When he was announced, he entered with a blend of ease and trepidation, ready to engage with the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite the mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman seated in a high-backed bergère by the fire. The Vicomtesse, with her graceful movements, stirred the flames and reached for a fallen glove, her delicate hand pointing to a chair for M. de Nueil to sit. Her charm and poise were striking, a blend of fastidious carelessness and high-bred elegance that made her stand out from the automatons he had encountered during his two-month exile in Normandy. In her presence, he felt transported back to Paris, surrounded by the beauty and sophistication he had missed.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion, exuding a regal air reminiscent of a fallen angel. Her hair, gathered into a crown of plaits, enhanced her noble appearance. Despite her beauty, there was an underlying sadness in her expression, hinting at past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon, she was a woman cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a vibrant youth now replaced by emptiness. M. de Nueil felt humbled by her presence, captivated by the combination of beauty, nobility, and misfortune that surrounded her.

When she acknowledged him, he felt the weight of his own deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but the realization of his dishonesty made him blush. Summoning his courage, he confessed to her, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” However, his apology was met with disdain; she rang for her servant to escort him out, her demeanor shifting from gracious to frigid.

As he left, M. de Nueil realized that if he departed, he would never return, and the Vicomtesse would think of him as a fool. He hesitated, contemplating the possibility that she might regret dismissing him. Driven by a sudden impulse, he turned back, claiming he had forgotten something. The servant, unaware of the true nature of his return, followed him back into the salon.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half-sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit, and he did so eagerly, his eyes shining with happiness. She felt a thrill at his delight, grateful for his understanding of her complex emotions.

“Madame,” he began, “my blunder you know, but you do not know how much I am to blame.” She interrupted him with a playful gesture, warning him to be careful. Her expression darkened as she reflected on her changed life, stating, “This is all very ridiculous! I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think. What brings you here? Is it curiosity? If so, I am paying dearly for a fleeting pleasure.”

She accused him of disrespect and questioned whether he had come to pity her, asserting that she would rather be scorned than pitied. The tension in the room was palpable as she expressed her pain at his intrusion, urging him to understand that his behavior was improper. She commanded him not to return, fearing that their connection would lead to gossip about their relationship.

M. de Nueil acknowledged his wrongdoing, attributing it to youthful enthusiasm and a desire for happiness. He explained that his longing to see her was natural, describing the weariness of his exile and the fires of life burning within him. He painted a picture of a young man yearning for love, emphasizing that she had become the ideal woman in his dreams. He spoke of his feelings and the wild thoughts that had driven him to seek her out, hoping to evoke a sense of indulgence in her heart for the folly committed in her name.

An impassioned voice resonated in the chill solitude, bringing warmth and youthful charm. Mme. de Beauseant, stirred by the genuine expression of feeling, found herself captivated by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance, untouched by the world's harsh realities. Gaston, in the prime of his youth, seemed to embody the ideal man, untainted by ambition or vanity. Both were drawn to each other, reflecting on their feelings while trying to conceal their thoughts. M. de Nueil saw in the Vicomtesse a rare woman, whose beauty was eclipsed by her depth of feeling and goodness. Mme. de Beauseant, listening to Gaston’s youthful troubles, recognized the struggles of those who, through hard work, remain untainted by the corrupting influences of society. 

As they delved into their sentiments, Gaston’s explorations were instinctive, while Mme. de Beauseant had a purpose, seeking to understand his views by presenting opposing opinions. Her wit and grace made her feel at ease with him, leading Gaston to exclaim, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” This remark struck her deeply, reviving her sense of innocence and condemning the world for her past. It provided her with a sense of absolution and validation, allowing her to rise above her fall. 

She looked at the clock and, with a smile, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston, sensing the gravity of their situation, expressed his desire to prolong their time together. Mme. de Beauseant, however, explained that she must maintain her solitary life, fearing that any further connection would tarnish her reputation. She spoke candidly about her past, revealing the pain of her forsaken love and the lessons learned from her solitude. 

Her eloquence and beauty captivated Gaston, who felt a longing to express his feelings but restrained himself, fearing rejection. He struggled with his emotions, torn between admiration and the fear of making a fool of himself. Eventually, he confessed his feelings, expressing a desire to make her forget her past wounds. Mme. de Beauseant, however, reminded him that they must remain strangers, emphasizing the pain of being unable to follow the man she loves through life’s phases. 

After their conversation, Gaston left feeling more distant from her, questioning whether her charm was merely a display of coquetry. He returned home, consumed by thoughts of her, oscillating between despair and hope. At twenty-three, he grappled with the diffidence of youth, feeling unworthy of her love and fearing the difficulties of expressing his feelings. 

That night, he was restless, unable to sleep, and began writing letters to her, none of which satisfied him. The next day, he returned to Courcelles under the cover of night, hesitant to be seen. After much deliberation, he penned a heartfelt letter, expressing his deep feelings and the impact she had on his life. He implored her not to dismiss his words, revealing his vulnerability and desperation. 

However, as he awaited her response, he was plagued by doubts, fearing she would laugh at his letter and his love. He wished he could take back his words, feeling they were inadequate and pretentious. Overwhelmed by his emotions, he struggled to cope, longing for the comfort of friends who could distract him from his turmoil. 

In this state of despair, Gaston faced the harsh reality of unrequited love, feeling the weight of his youthful passion and the uncertainty of his future without Mme. de Beauseant.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At length, M. de Nueil received a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, marked with the arms of Burgundy. He locked himself away to read and re-read her words:

“You are punishing me for my attempt to spare you a rebuff and for my attraction to intellect. I trusted in your youth's generosity, but you have disappointed me. I spoke frankly about my position to show you that I am not to be swayed by youthful passion. My distress is heightened by my interest in you. I am kind-hearted, but circumstances force me to act otherwise. Another woman might have discarded your letter, but I read it and am responding. My answer will clarify that while I acknowledge your feelings, I do not share them. I wish to use the authority you give me over your life to help you see clearly.

“I am nearly thirty; you are just over twenty. You cannot know what your thoughts will be at my age. The vows you make lightly now may become burdensome later. I believe you would give your life for a moment of happiness, but as you age, you will find it hard to make sacrifices for me, and I would feel humiliated to accept them. A day will come when everything will urge you to leave me, and I have said that death is preferable to desertion. I must tell you that I do not love you; I cannot, and I will not. It is too late for me to yield to a blind impulse of the heart. My solace comes from God, not from earthly attachments. I read hearts too clearly to accept your friendship. I forgive your youthful folly, but for your sake and mine, I urge you to stay in your own country. Do not ruin a fair life for an illusion that cannot last. In the future, when you have achieved your destiny, you will appreciate my answer, even if you find it harsh now. You will cherish the friendship of an older woman, untainted by passion, and it will be sweet and precious to you. Farewell; think of me as you would an absent friend.”

Gaston de Nueil read her letter and wrote back:

“MADAME,--If I could cease to love you and accept the ordinary life you propose, I would deserve my fate. I will not do as you ask; my oath of fidelity will only be absolved by death. Take my life, unless you fear to carry remorse.”

When the messenger returned, M. de Nueil asked where he had left the note. 

“I gave it to Mme. la Vicomtesse; she was in her carriage, about to leave.”

“For the town?”

“I don’t think so; she had post-horses.”

“Ah! then she is going away,” said the Baron.

“Yes, sir.”

Gaston de Nueil immediately prepared to follow Mme. de Beauseant. She traveled to Geneva, unaware of his pursuit. His mind was consumed with one question: “Why did she leave?” He speculated that if she cared for him, she would choose Switzerland, where they could be free from judgment.

The Vicomtesse rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening, Gaston visited her. Jacques, the servant, announced him without surprise. Mme. de Beauseant dropped her book in shock, giving him a moment to approach her and express his joy at following her.

For three years, they lived in the villa by Lake Geneva, alone and without visitors, enjoying a happiness that seemed like a dream. Their home was simple yet perfect for lovers, with beautiful views of the lake and mountains. They cherished their time together, but weighty matters soon called M. de Nueil back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They bought the house, wishing they could take it with them.

Mme. de Beauseant followed him, selling her property to buy an estate near Manerville, adjacent to Gaston’s lands. They lived together, with Gaston graciously allowing his mother to occupy Manerville, enjoying their secluded happiness once more.

For nine years, they experienced bliss that transcended description. However, Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, remained in good health, complicating their situation. He was a methodical man, aware of the benefits of his life, which made it impossible for the Marquise to marry M. de Nueil.

After nine years of happiness, they found themselves in a precarious situation. Mme. la Comtesse de Nueil, Gaston’s mother, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant. The Marquise understood that the dowager would try to pull Gaston away from their relationship. She considered selling her estate and returning to Geneva but could not bring herself to do so, fearing it would show distrust in M. de Nueil.

A young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, moved nearby, and Gaston met her at Manerville. This prompted a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, revealing her inner turmoil:

“My beloved angel, it seems absurd to write when we are together, but there are things I cannot say face to face. I feel anguish. You have erased my past pain, and I did not know what love was until I met you. I have never felt jealousy in our nine years together, and I have known infinite happiness. But now, I fear that you may feel burdened by our relationship. You are thirty; I am forty. This age difference weighs heavily on me. You may have thought of your future, of a marriage that would bring you wealth and children. To owe your love to pity is a painful thought. 

“Have you considered leaving me? I sensed your hesitation, and it made me cry. I am too proud to let you see my tears, but I did not reproach you. I should not bind your future to my fading life. But what if I am mistaken? If you still love me, come to me after reading this letter. I will love you more than ever. Speak openly; do not deceive me. If you wish for your freedom, I will not let you see my suffering. I love you enough to prioritize your happiness over mine.”

When Mme. de Beauseant learned that her letter was in M. de Nueil’s hands, she felt overwhelmed, her thoughts numbing her senses. Meanwhile, M. de Nueil, reading her letter, found himself in a difficult position, a crisis that many young men face.

################################################
By this time, he had nearly succumbed to his mother’s pleas and the allure of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a rather unremarkable young woman with a substantial dowry of forty thousand livres. Mme. de Nueil, with maternal affection, sought to guide her son toward virtuous choices, emphasizing the honor of being chosen by Mlle. de la Rodiere, who had turned down many prestigious suitors. She urged him to consider his future, noting that he could one day have an income of eighty thousand livres from land, which would make life easier. If Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him, she should encourage him to marry. Thus, the well-meaning mother employed every argument to sway her son, leaving him in a state of indecision.

Just as Gaston’s feelings for Mme. de Beauseant were resisting the pull of a conventional life, a letter from her changed everything. He resolved to end his relationship with the Marquise and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, but he was also acutely aware of the pain this decision would inflict on Mme. de Beauseant. His vanity and conscience amplified his sorrow, and he felt a genuine pity for her. He decided to soften the blow by gradually preparing her for their separation, initially sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition. He planned to marry later, respecting Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes, and intended to write her a letter to quell her suspicions. However, he realized that for a woman with such keen perception, a letter could be devastating.

When Jacques delivered the letter to Mme. de Beauseant, she trembled, sensing the impending loss. If he did not come to her in person, she feared all was lost. Yet, the hopes of a woman in love can endure much pain. As she read his words, a voice within her cried, “He lies!” She quickly discerned that this was not a heartfelt declaration but a calculated message. In despair, she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil left to find Jacques, who soon returned with a note. “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau,” he informed him. M. de Nueil opened the note to find it was his own letter from nine years prior, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant. At the bottom, Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Shocked, M. de Nueil went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If this ordinary tale ended here, it would seem trivial. However, the notoriety of the tragedy, being a true story, resonates with those who have experienced the profound joys of love and the pain of its loss. After parting from M. de Nueil, Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen by anyone except her servants, and attended mass daily.

M. de Nueil, shortly after his marriage, fell into a state of emotional indifference, which could be interpreted as either happiness or unhappiness. His mother claimed he was perfectly happy. Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young women, was sweet but lacked depth. A month after their marriage, she expected a child, which aligned with societal norms. However, two months later, M. de Nueil became increasingly pensive. 

After seven months of this lukewarm happiness, a seemingly trivial incident occurred that hinted at deeper turmoil. One day, after hunting, M. de Nueil crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and asked Jacques if she still enjoyed game. Jacques confirmed, and M. de Nueil offered him a sum of money to set aside the game he shot for her. Jacques saw no harm in this, and for days, M. de Nueil returned home with an empty bag, while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise. 

Eventually, M. de Nueil grew bold enough to write a letter to Mme. de Beauseant, but it was returned unopened. One evening, as his wife played the piano, he suddenly rushed to the Marquise’s room. There, he found her in a state of despair, her posture reflecting hopelessness. When she saw him, she threatened to throw herself out the window if he approached. Terrified, M. de Nueil fled.

Back at home, he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later, the servant returned with the letter unopened. In despair, M. de Nueil retreated to another room and shot himself.

The tragic conclusion of this story, though contrary to the norms of young France, was perhaps inevitable. Those who have experienced the profound connection between two souls will understand Gaston de Nueil’s suicide. Love requires time and nurturing, and when a man forsakes a deep love for a loveless marriage, he faces a choice between despair and a cold, materialistic existence.

Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish could lead to such a drastic end. She may have believed she alone would suffer. Nevertheless, she rightly refused to accept a humiliating position; a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot accept such a fate, as the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
