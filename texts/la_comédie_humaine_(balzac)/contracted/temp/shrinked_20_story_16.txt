In spring 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness attributed to overwork or excess. Seeking rest, a light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with welcoming relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte‑Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who represented provincial French types. One was an old, impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a resolute sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife convinced their daughters’ prospects rested on the family name. They clung to obsolete customs—nobility without wealth or influence.

Opposite them stood a wealthier, more recent family attuned to Parisian trends. The wife was a society woman, slightly behind the latest fashions, while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, handled finances shrewdly and traded political gossip. Their Royalism was lukewarm; they engaged with modern life while preserving ancestral ties.

The local bishop, formerly Vicar‑General, mediated between families—respected for his office but often reminded of his plebeian origins. Retired officers and their families kept a veneer of aristocracy while focusing on local affairs rather than national politics. The women dressed as if for court yet lived by routine and tradition. A few wealthy townspeople had infiltrated this circle and were viewed skeptically by the older generation. Ecclesiastics introduced wit and a bourgeois element to otherwise staid gatherings.

Collectively the society was marked by antiquated beliefs and resistance to change: conversations circled familiar themes, judgments were fixed, intellectual life stagnated, and critics were dismissed as misfits.

Before his arrival Gaston had been appraised: his cousin disclosed his fortune and family. At twenty‑three he was welcomed as promising, with eighteen thousand livres from land in the Auge valley and a father bargaining for more woodland; omitted was that his elder brother was consumptive and likely dying. At first Gaston amused himself sketching their quirks, but weeks of monotony left him weary and confined. Ennui and mounting discontent dimmed memories of Parisian intellectual life, threatening to make him one more fossil among the fossils.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing room where family portraits overlooked four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After dinner he noted provincial habits—the old cards, threadbare cloths—and their philosophic indifference to luxury, which made Paris feel remote.

His attention fell on a young lady with red hands and a shy, awkward manner, but a casual remark about Mme. de Beauseant struck him like a fine passage in a dull opera. An elderly guest said the Vicomtesse was unwell and had declined an invitation; the conversation revealed her scandalous liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and her retreat to Courcelles. Though of noble birth, she had been shunned after separating from her husband—an exile that intrigued him.

He became enthralled by the idea of Mme. de Beauseant, imagining her a focus of hope amid provincial stagnation. The thought of gaining access consumed him, checked only by the barriers her rank and intelligence posed.

Next morning he wandered near Courcelles, romanticizing a chance meeting. Fearing a direct letter would be refused, he resolved to have M. de Champignelles arrange an introduction. Gaston confided in the Marquis and secured his good offices. M. de Champignelles hastened to Courcelles; his message met resistance but piqued the Vicomtesse’s curiosity, and after reflection she agreed to receive the young suitor.

Alternately excited and anxious, Gaston prepared carefully—believing appearance would shape her judgment, unaware wit and character might matter more. Approaching Courcelles he felt confidence and trepidation; when announced, he entered composed yet nervous, ready to meet the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman in a high‑backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals, picked up a fallen glove, and motioned to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poised, fastidious carelessness contrasted sharply with the automata he had met during two months’ exile in Normandy; in her presence he felt transported back to Paris and its sophistication.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that gave her a regal, almost fallen‑angel air; her hair, arranged in a crown of plaits, intensified that bearing. Beneath her beauty lay an underlying sadness—traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a once‑vibrant youth now emptied. M. de Nueil felt humbled and captivated by this blend of beauty, nobility, and misfortune.

When she acknowledged him he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but awareness of his falsehood made him flush. Summoning his courage he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” Instead of mercy she rang for a servant to escort him out; her manner shifted from gracious to icy.

As he left he realized that if he went away now he would never return and that the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, hoping she might relent. On impulse he turned back, saying he had forgotten something; the servant followed him into the salon unaware of the real reason.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half‑sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly, eyes bright with relief. She felt a thrill at his pleasure, grateful for what she took as his comprehension of her complex emotions.

He began, “Madame, my blunder you know, but you do not know how much I am to blame.” She stopped him playfully, then darkened, reflecting on how her life had changed. “This is all very ridiculous! I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think. What brings you here? Is it curiosity? If so, I am paying dearly for a fleeting pleasure.” She accused him of disrespect, rejected pity, warned of impropriety, and commanded him not to return. M. de Nueil acknowledged his fault, blaming youthful enthusiasm and exile’s weariness; he had idealized her and, in delirious longing for love, committed a foolish intrusion for which he begged indulgence.

An impassioned voice warmed the chill solitude; Mme. de Beauseant, moved by its sincerity, found herself captivated by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance—Gaston, in the bloom of youth, seemed the ideal man, free from ambition or vanity. Both were drawn to one another, reflecting on and concealing their feelings. He perceived in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness; she, hearing his youthful troubles, recognized the purity of someone resisting society’s corruptions.

Gaston’s approach was instinctive; Mme. de Beauseant’s deliberate—she tested him with opposing opinions. Her wit and grace soon put him at ease, prompting his exclamation, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” The remark revived her sense of innocence, condemned the world for her past, and offered an absolution lifting her above former shame.

She checked the clock and, with a gentle smile, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston wished to prolong the moment; she insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing any new connection would tarnish her reputation. Candidly she spoke of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her.

Her eloquence and beauty captivated Gaston, who longed to declare his feelings but feared rejection. Torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he finally confessed his love and offered to heal her old wounds. Mme. de Beauseant, moved yet resolute, reminded him they must remain strangers and spoke of the pain of being unable to follow the man she loved through life’s changes.

Afterward Gaston left unsettled, unsure whether her charm was genuine or coquetry. Diffident at twenty-three, he wrote faltering letters, then, to avoid being seen, returned to Courcelles at night and penned a heartfelt plea laying bare his feelings. Awaiting her response, doubts gnawed at him; fearing ridicule, he longed for friends to distract him and confronted the harshness of unrequited longing.

M. de Nueil received a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, sealed with the arms of Burgundy. She shut herself away and read that she was refusing his advances. Trying to spare him a rebuff and be candid, she said youthful passion would not move her. Though she cared for him, she did not share his love and would not yield to blind impulse: she was nearly thirty while he was just over twenty, and they could not know how feelings would change with age. Vows made lightly might become burdens; she feared he might one day resent the sacrifices she required, and would rather die than endure desertion. Her consolation was in God, not earthly ties; she could offer only the friendship of an older woman, untainted by passion. For both their sakes she urged him to remain in his country and not wreck his life for an illusion.

Gaston de Nueil answered at once with fervor: if he could cease to love her and accept the ordinary life she suggested, he would deserve his fate; his oath of fidelity could be absolved only by death, and if she feared his remorse she might take his life.

When the messenger returned he reported delivering Mme. la Vicomtesse’s reply to her as she was about to depart in a carriage with post-horses. Realizing she was leaving, Gaston followed her to Geneva, convinced that if she truly cared she would seek the freedom of Switzerland.

Mme. de Beauseant had rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston surprised her; she dropped her book and embraced him as he explained he had followed her. For three years they lived in the villa on Lake Geneva, alone and apparently in a dreamlike happiness: a simple home, beautiful views, quiet days cherished together.

Weighty obligations soon called Gaston back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They refrained from selling the villa and instead bought the family house; Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to buy an estate near Manerville adjoining Gaston’s lands. His mother took the family house at Manerville, and they resumed the secluded happiness they had known.

For nine years they enjoyed an almost indescribable bliss. Yet a legal obstacle remained: Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, was still alive, so any lawful union was impossible. The situation was precarious. Gaston’s mother, the dowager comtesse, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant; the Marquise feared the dowager’s influence might one day pull Gaston away. Though tempted to sell her estate and return to Geneva, Mme. de Beauseant could not bring herself to act in a way that might betray distrust.

The arrival nearby of a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, and Gaston’s meeting with her disturbed the fragile peace. Mme. de Beauseant replied with a letter revealing her inner turmoil and pride. She confessed she had not known love until she met him and that, for nine years, she had felt no jealousy and had experienced infinite happiness. But now she anguished at the thought that, at forty, she might be a burden to his future: he was thirty and might wish a marriage that would bring children and security. She recoiled at the idea his devotion could be motivated by pity rather than desire.

She admitted sensing his hesitation and being unable to hide her tears, yet she had not reproached him. Too proud to bind his future to her own fading life, she urged him to be frank: if he still loved her, he should come after reading the letter and she would love him more than ever; if he sought freedom, she would not display her suffering, because she loved him enough to put his happiness first.

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her letter had reached Gaston, she was overwhelmed and stunned into numbness. Reading her words, M. de Nueil found himself in a painful dilemma—a crisis where passion, duty, pride, and the prospect of a conventional future collided.

Gaston had nearly yielded to his mother’s entreaties and to the attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil pressed a respectable match, praising the honor of being chosen by a woman who had refused distinguished suitors and noting that future land revenues might raise his income to eighty thousand livres. She argued that if Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him she should encourage his marriage. Her words left him torn between passion and duty.

Just as his attachment to Mme. de Beauseant resisted convention, a letter from her changed everything: he resolved to end their liaison and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, though he knew the choice would wound her. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt genuine pity for her. He meant to prepare her gently—at first sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition and planning to marry later if required to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes. He decided to send a letter to calm her suspicions, yet feared that a woman of such keen perception might be devastated by a mere note.

When Jacques brought that letter, Mme. de Beauseant trembled, dread of loss closing in. If he did not come in person, she felt all was lost; still, a woman in love can endure much. As she read, an inner cry rose: “He lies!” She took the message as calculated rather than sincere, and in despair tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil fetched Jacques, who soon returned with a note stating, “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau.” M. de Nueil opened it and found, instead of a farewell, his own letter from nine years earlier in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned by the consciousness of his past promises and her apparent renunciation, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If the tale ended there it might seem merely domestic, yet its notoriety harks to those who know love’s profound joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, was no longer the companion she had been, and attended mass daily. The Marquise’s withdrawal became a silent testimony to the rupture.

Soon after his marriage M. de Nueil sank into emotional indifference—variously interpreted by observers as contentment or melancholy. His mother insisted he was happy. Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young brides, was amiable but shallow; a month after the wedding she was expected to be with child, fitting social hopes for the union. Two months later, however, M. de Nueil grew increasingly pensive, and his melancholy could not be dismissed as mere domestic routine.

After seven months a seemingly trivial incident revealed deeper turmoil. Returning from a hunt, he crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and, as if driven by habit, asked Jacques whether she still enjoyed game. Jacques confirmed, and M. de Nueil paid him to set aside whatever he shot for her. For days M. de Nueil returned from the hunt with an empty bag while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise—an act at once tender and humiliating, revealing a man unable to live without the small intimacies of his former passion.

Eventually M. de Nueil dared to write Mme. de Beauseant, but the letter was returned unopened. One evening, as his wife played the piano, he suddenly rushed to the Marquise’s room and found her muted despair, emptied of hope. When she saw him she threatened to throw herself from the window rather than be approached; terrified, he fled.

Back home he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later the servant returned with the unopened note. In despair M. de Nueil retired to another room and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with young France’s conventions, may have seemed inevitable to those who have known a profound soul-connection. Love needs time and care; abandoning such love for a loveless, conventional marriage produces either despair or a cold, material life. Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish might lead to such an extreme act. She may have believed herself the only sufferer, yet she rightly refused humiliation: a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to degradation, for the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
