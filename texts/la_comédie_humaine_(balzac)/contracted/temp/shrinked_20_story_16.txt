In the spring of 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness likely due to overwork or excess. Seeking rest, a light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with welcoming relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who embodied provincial French types. One was an ancient but impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a determined sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife convinced their daughters’ fortunes rested solely on their name. They clung to obsolete customs, representing nobility without wealth or influence.

In contrast stood a wealthier, more recent family who embraced contemporary Parisian trends. The wife was a society woman—just behind the latest fashions—while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, was shrewd with finances and versed in political gossip. Their Royalism was less zealous than the old nobles’, engaging with modern life while preserving ancestral ties.

The local bishop, a former Vicar-General, mediated between these families: respected for his office but often reminded of his plebeian origins. Retired military officers and their families preserved a veneer of aristocracy while focusing on local affairs rather than national politics. The women dressed as if for court, yet their days were governed by routine and tradition.

This exclusive social set included a few wealthy townspeople who had infiltrated the aristocracy and were regarded skeptically by the older generation. Ecclesiastics brought wit and a bourgeois element to otherwise staid gatherings.

Collectively, the society was marked by antiquated beliefs and resistance to change. Conversations circled familiar themes, members issued immutable judgments, and intellectual life stagnated. New ideas were unwelcome; any outsider who criticized their way of life was quickly labeled a misfit.

Before his arrival, Gaston had been appraised by the local elite: his cousin disclosed his fortune and family background. At twenty-three he was welcomed as a promising young man with an income of eighteen thousand livres from land in the Auge valley; his father was negotiating to buy more woodland, further securing their status. Yet his situation was complicated by an elder brother who was consumptive and likely dying—a fact Mme. de Sainte-Severe omitted.

At first Gaston amused himself by observing and sketching his acquaintances’ quirks, enjoying their quaintness. But as weeks passed he grew weary of their monotony, feeling trapped in a cloistered existence that drained his spirits. This confinement produced a crisis of spirit: ennui and mounting discontent. Gradually he lost touch with the vibrant life of Paris; memories of intellectual engagement dimmed, and he risked becoming another fossil among the fossils—content to exist in a dull, complacent routine.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room where family portraits looked down on four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After a pleasant dinner he noted provincial customs—the old cards, threadbare tablecloths—and their philosophic indifference to luxury, which made him question the value of opulence. The vibrant life of Paris felt far away.

His attention fell on a young lady with red hands and a shy demeanor, at first awkward and unattractive. A chance remark about Mme. de Beauseant, however, stirred him like a passage of good music in a tedious opera. An elderly woman mentioned the Vicomtesse had been unwell and had declined an invitation to dinner, which piqued Gaston’s curiosity. The conversation disclosed Mme. de Beauseant’s scandalous liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and her ensuing isolation at Courcelles. Despite noble lineage, she was shunned because of her separation from her husband—a circumstance that intrigued him.

He became captivated by the idea of Mme. de Beauseant, seeing her as a figure of hope and desire amid provincial dullness. An obsession grew; he pictured her as a woman worth pursuing in a stagnant world. His thoughts turned to gaining access to her, aware of obstacles posed by her intelligence and social standing.

The next morning he wandered near Courcelles, hoping for a chance encounter, his imagination suffused with romance. He considered writing but feared rejection. Instead he resolved to enlist M. de Champignelles to arrange an introduction. He told the Marquis he had a delicate matter to discuss with the Vicomtesse and asked him to inquire whether she would receive him. Gaston’s charm and apparent sincerity convinced the Marquis, who was glad to oblige a pretty man.

M. de Champignelles hurried to Courcelles and, conveying Gaston’s request, aroused Mme. de Beauseant’s curiosity despite her initial reluctance. After deliberation she agreed to meet him, intrigued by the mystery surrounding this young suitor.

Meanwhile Gaston brimmed with excitement and anxiety, hoping to impress her. He prepared meticulously, believing appearance would shape her impression—unaware that true attraction would hinge more on wit and character than looks.

Approaching Courcelles he felt a mix of confidence and nervousness, conscious that entering a room with one woman could be more daunting than facing a crowd. When announced, he entered with a blend of ease and trepidation, ready to engage the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite the mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman seated in a high-backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals, picked up a fallen glove, and pointed with a delicate hand to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poise—an elegant mix of fastidious carelessness and high breeding—set her apart from the automatons he had met during his two months’ exile in Normandy. In her presence he felt transported back to Paris, immersed again in the sophistication he had missed.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that gave her a regal air like a fallen angel; her hair, gathered in a crown of plaits, heightened that noble appearance. Yet beneath her beauty lay an underlying sadness, traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon, she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a youth now emptied. M. de Nueil felt humbled, captivated by the blend of beauty, nobility, and misfortune that surrounded her.

When she acknowledged him, he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but the consciousness of his dishonesty made him flush. Summoning his courage, he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” His apology met disdain; she rang for her servant to escort him out, her manner shifting from gracious to icy.

As he left, he realized that if he went away now he would never return, and the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, considering she might repent. On a sudden impulse he turned back, claiming he had forgotten something; the servant, ignorant of the truth, followed him into the salon.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half-sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly, eyes shining with happiness. She felt a thrill at his delight, grateful for what she took as his comprehension of her complex emotions.

“Madame,” he began, “my blunder you know, but you do not know how much I am to blame.” She interrupted with a playful gesture, warning him to be careful, but her expression darkened as she reflected on how her life had changed. “This is all very ridiculous! I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think. What brings you here? Is it curiosity? If so, I am paying dearly for a fleeting pleasure.”

She accused him of disrespect and asked whether he had come to pity her, asserting she would rather be scorned than pitied. The tension grew as she voiced her pain at his intrusion and urged him to see the impropriety of his conduct. Fearful gossip would follow any continued contact, she commanded him not to return.

M. de Nueil acknowledged his fault, blaming youthful enthusiasm and a craving for happiness. He explained that his longing to see her was natural—the weariness of exile and inner fires that drove him. He painted himself as a young man yearning for love, who had made her the ideal of his dreams, and spoke of the feelings and wild thoughts that had led him to seek her out, hoping to awaken in her some indulgence for the folly he had committed in her name.

An impassioned voice warmed the chill solitude with youthful charm. Mme. de Beauseant, moved by its genuine feeling, found herself captivated by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance—Gaston, in the bloom of youth, seeming the ideal man, untouched by ambition or vanity. Both were drawn to each other, reflecting on their feelings while concealing them. M. de Nueil saw in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness. Mme. de Beauseant, hearing Gaston’s youthful troubles, recognized the struggles of someone kept pure by hard work against society’s corruptions.

As they explored their sentiments, Gaston’s approach was instinctive; Mme. de Beauseant’s was deliberate—she tested him by posing opposing opinions. Her wit and grace put him at ease, prompting his exclamation, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” The remark struck her, reviving a sense of innocence, condemning the world for her past, and offering an absolution that helped her rise above former shame.

She glanced at the clock and, with a gentle smile, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston, sensing its gravity, wished to prolong the moment. Mme. de Beauseant, however, insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing further connection would tarnish her reputation. She spoke candidly of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her.

Her eloquence and beauty captivated Gaston, who longed to declare his feelings but feared rejection. Torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he eventually confessed his love and offered to heal her old wounds. Mme. de Beauseant, moved yet resolute, reminded him they must remain strangers and spoke of the pain of being unable to follow the man she loved through life’s changes.

Afterward Gaston left feeling more distant, uncertain whether her charm was genuine or mere coquetry. He returned home consumed by thoughts of her, alternating between despair and hope. At twenty-three he was hampered by diffidence, feeling unworthy of her love and anxious about expressing himself.

That night he was restless and began writing letters, none satisfactory. The next day he returned to Courcelles at night, cautious to avoid being seen. After much deliberation he penned a heartfelt letter, laying bare his deep feelings and the mark she had left on his life. He implored her not to dismiss his words, exposing his vulnerability and desperation.

As he awaited her response, doubts gnawed at him; he feared she would laugh at his letter and at his love. He wished to retract his words, finding them inadequate and pretentious. Overwhelmed by emotion, he longed for friends to distract him from his turmoil, confronting the harshness of unrequited passion and the uncertainty of a future without Mme. de Beauseant.

M. de Nueil received a letter from Mme. de Beauseant, sealed with the arms of Burgundy. He shut himself up to read it.

She reproached him gently for misunderstanding her motives: she had tried to spare him a rebuff and had been drawn to his intellect, but his youthful generosity had disappointed her. She had spoken frankly to show she was not governed by passion. She had read his letter when another woman might have tossed it aside, and she now answered: she acknowledged his feelings but did not share them. Near thirty while he was just over twenty, she argued, he could not foresee what he would think at her age; vows made lightly might become intolerable. She believed he might give his life for a moment’s happiness, but later he would find sacrifices for her humiliating; there would come a day when everything would urge him to leave, and she had said she would prefer death to desertion. She could not yield to blind impulse. Her consolation lay in God, not in earthly attachments; she saw hearts too clearly to accept merely friendly intimacy. Forgiving his youthful folly, she urged him to remain in his country rather than ruin a fair life for an illusion. When he had attained his destiny he would understand and value the friendship of an older woman untainted by passion. Farewell; think of her as an absent friend.

Gaston de Nueil answered at once: if he could cease to love her and accept the ordinary life she advised, he would deserve his fate. He refused to follow her counsel; his oath of fidelity could only be absolved by death. “Take my life,” he wrote, “unless you fear remorse.”

When the messenger returned, he told the Baron he had given the reply to Mme. la Vicomtesse, who was in her carriage and had post-horses. Gaston inferred she was going away and at once prepared to follow her. She had indeed left for Geneva, unaware he pursued her. Convinced that if she cared for him she would choose Switzerland for its freedom from judgment, he followed.

The vicomtesse rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston called; Jacques the servant announced him and Mme. de Beauseant, startled, dropped her book. He advanced, expressed his joy at following her, and they were reconciled.

They lived for three years in the villa by Lake Geneva, alone and without visitors, enjoying a happiness that seemed like a dream. Their home was simple but suited lovers: fine views of lake and mountains, quiet days cherished together. Weighty matters called M. de Nueil back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They sold nothing then; they bought the family house, regretting they could not take the villa.

Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to buy an estate near Manerville, adjoining Gaston’s lands, and followed him. They lived together; Gaston let his mother occupy Manerville, and again they enjoyed secluded happiness.

For nine years their bliss continued. But Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, remained in good health, and that lawful union made a public marriage impossible. The situation grew precarious. Mme. la Comtesse de Nueil, Gaston’s mother, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant, and the Marquise feared the dowager might try to draw Gaston away. The idea of selling her estate and returning to Geneva occurred to her, but she could not bring herself to do so for fear of showing distrust in Gaston.

Complication came when a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, moved nearby and Gaston met her at Manerville. This provoked a letter from Mme. de Beauseant revealing her inner torment.

She began awkwardly—absurd to write when they were together, yet unable to speak face to face—then admitted anguish. He had erased her past pain; she did not know love until him. In nine years she had never felt jealousy and had known infinite happiness, but now she feared he might feel burdened by their relation. He was thirty, she forty; the age gap preyed on her. He might think of his future: a marriage bringing wealth and children. The thought that he might owe his love to pity was painful. She sensed his hesitation and had wept privately; too proud to show him her tears, she had not reproached him. She believed she should not bind his future to her fading life. Yet if she were wrong, if he still loved her, she begged him to come after reading the letter—she would love him more than ever. She asked him to speak openly and not deceive her; if he wished freedom, she would not let him see her suffering. She loved him enough to put his happiness above her own.

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her own letter had come into M. de Nueil’s hands, she was overwhelmed; her thoughts grew numb. Reading her words, M. de Nueil found himself in the painful crisis that confronts many young men.

By this time he had nearly yielded to his mother’s pleadings and the attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil, with maternal concern, urged him toward this respectable match, stressing the honor of being chosen by a woman who had refused many distinguished suitors. She reminded him to consider his future: he might one day secure eighty thousand livres from land and thus an easier life. If Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him, she should encourage his marriage. Using every argument, his mother left him torn.

Just as Gaston’s attachment to Mme. de Beauseant resisted convention, a letter from her changed everything. He resolved to end their liaison and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, though he knew the choice would wound Mme. de Beauseant. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt genuine pity for her. He decided to prepare her gently for their separation, at first sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition. He planned to marry later, to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes, and meant to write her a letter to quiet her suspicions. But he knew a woman of such perception might be devastated by a mere note.

When Jacques delivered the letter, Mme. de Beauseant trembled, dread of impending loss closing in. If he did not come to her in person, she feared all was lost; yet a woman in love can endure much pain. As she read, an inner voice cried, “He lies!” She saw the words were not a true declaration but a calculated message. In despair she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil went to fetch Jacques, who soon returned with a note. “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau,” he said. M. de Nueil opened the note and found it was his own letter from nine years earlier, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If the tale ended there it would seem trivial. But the notoriety of this true tragedy resonates with those who have known love’s profound joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, and attended mass daily.

M. de Nueil, soon after his marriage, fell into a state of emotional indifference—interpreted variously as happiness or unhappiness. His mother insisted he was content. Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young brides, was sweet but shallow. A month after their wedding she expected a child, fitting social expectations. Two months later, however, M. de Nueil grew increasingly pensive.

After seven months of this lukewarm contentment a seemingly trivial incident hinted at deeper turmoil. One day after hunting, M. de Nueil crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and asked Jacques if she still enjoyed game. Jacques confirmed, and M. de Nueil paid him to set aside whatever he shot for her. Jacques saw no harm in this, and for days M. de Nueil returned with an empty bag while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise.

Eventually M. de Nueil dared to write Mme. de Beauseant, but his letter was returned unopened. One evening, as his wife played the piano, he suddenly rushed to the Marquise’s room. There he found her in despair, her posture emptied of hope. When she saw him, she threatened to throw herself from the window if he approached. Terrified, M. de Nueil fled.

Back home he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later the servant returned with the unopened note. In despair M. de Nueil retired to another room and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with young France’s conventions, was perhaps inevitable. Those who have experienced a profound soul-connection will understand Gaston de Nueil’s suicide. Love requires time and nurturing; when a man abandons deep love for a loveless marriage, he faces either despair or a cold, material life.

Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish might lead to such an extreme act. She may have believed herself the only sufferer. Still, she rightly refused to accept humiliation; a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to such degradation, for the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
