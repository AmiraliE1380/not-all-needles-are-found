For effusive souls, twilight invites reflection on art. One such hour finds a young painter, finishing a large canvas, falling from his ladder, striking his head and losing consciousness.

He awakens to the gentle voice of a young woman and her mother, who had rushed to his aid. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, is immediately struck by the girl's beauty, which matches the feminine ideal he has long imagined. The older woman, resembling a refined marquise, tends him kindly and advises on his recovery. They depart with polite reserve.

Afterwards the porter’s wife tells him the visitors are Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother. Hippolyte, newly installed in the attic studio after winning some recognition, is determined to provide better for his own mother and keeps his private life guarded despite growing success.

During convalescence he returns repeatedly to the image of the two women, especially the girl, imagining a bond born of shared misfortune. When he is well enough he visits them, finding poverty in their home but feeling empathy rather than contempt. Mademoiselle Leseigneur greets him politely; the contrast between their humble surroundings and the remnant elegance they display—the faded trappings of past luxury meticulously kept—strikes him.

Conversation begins stiffly but warms when it turns to art. Madame Leseigneur speaks of a cherished but deteriorating portrait of her late husband, a naval officer. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait, both to help and to forge a closer connection; they accept his offer with gratitude, and he senses the emotional weight behind it.

As they speak, Adelaide steps out and returns with candles to light the room. An unexpected visitor arrives—a man who knows Adelaide. His presence provokes Hippolyte’s jealousy. The newcomer, an older gentleman in an outmoded coat, combines an air of nobility and libertinism, hinting at a complicated past and revealing layers of the women’s lives.

Throughout the evening Hippolyte struggles with admiration for Adelaide and uncertainty about their differing social standings, clinging to the slender hope of connection amid the fraught circumstances.

A remarkable figure stood beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry” specimen — the Bonapartists’ nickname for those remnants of the Monarchy. Lean and aged, he was a faded echo of the first: a worn coat, dulled powder, a feebler intellect — the living reflection of Rivarol’s quip on Champcenetz. The painter was puzzled: the old man neither spoke nor was spoken to; his relation to the gallant man was unclear — friend, relative, or mere companion — and he answered no curiosity, especially about any past tie to the gallant’s deceased wife.

The least dilapidated of the group approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat near her. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a pose recalling a famous painting, though his familiarity seemed to irritate her. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at the painter. “This is our neighbor,” Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man acknowledged him, noting Hippolyte’s red ribbon against his own harder-earned honors.

Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, lost himself in admiration and for a moment forgot their squalor. The old gentleman lamented card losses; Adelaide offered help but he insisted she remain where he could see her. When the game ended the gentleman complained about his bad luck, the silent figure echoed the hour like a clock, and Hippolyte left still entranced by the stirrings of love.

The next day Hippolyte felt an overwhelming urge to see Adelaide. He hesitated until afternoon, then awkwardly requested the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone, sent him to fetch it, revealing a modesty that spoke more than words. Their glance conveyed what they would not say, and Hippolyte promised a swift return.

In the following days their relationship blossomed through laughter and shared thought. Another amateur at the table tried to learn piquet but often lost; meanwhile an unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable: Adelaide’s affection grew, Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed undue familiarity, and she fretted that his social life harmed his health. Their love matured without grand gestures, built on trust and delicate exchanges; within two months their lives were intertwined, Hippolyte visiting daily.

As the old gentleman’s visits waned, Hippolyte took his place at the card table and shared in the losses. He spent two months on the Baron’s portrait, calling it one of his best; the Baroness’s silence about payment or thanks puzzled him. When Adelaide came to his studio, awestruck by the portrait, their charged moment overflowed with unspoken feeling. Overcome, Hippolyte declared his desire to marry her — at which moment the old vice-admiral arrived, praised the work, and hinted at funds to pay for both portraits.

That evening, however, Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards and began to suspect a gambling habit. After a night of torment he discovered his purse missing and, blinded by jealousy, suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft. Tormented, he confronted them and, seeing Adelaide pale and sad, felt ashamed. Their conversation revealed mutual longing and cleared his doubts. When Adelaide quietly set a new purse before him during the game, his fears dissolved; he impulsively declared his intention to marry her. The vice-admiral returned, announcing a promotion and openly supporting the young couple. The evening ended with unity as families came together, dispelling the last doubts. The tale closes on love restored: trust, misunderstanding, and ultimately a promise of a brighter future.
