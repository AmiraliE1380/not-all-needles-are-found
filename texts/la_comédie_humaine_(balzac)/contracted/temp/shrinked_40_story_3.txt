At twilight, as day yields to night, a young painter finishing a large canvas falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness. He is roused by a young woman and her mother who rush to his aid. Recovering, he is struck by the girl’s beauty—matching an ideal he had imagined—and by the mother’s marquise-like air. Both are gentle, reserved, and withdraw without pressing acquaintance.

The porter’s wife identifies them as Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, new to the attic studio and beginning to gain recognition, keeps his affairs guarded; he hopes to secure a better life for his own mother. During convalescence he replays the encounter, especially the image of the girl, and soon feels compelled to visit.

In their modest home he finds clear poverty but maintained elegance: traces of past finery kept clean. Conversation is at first awkward but turns to art, allowing Hippolyte to speak freely. Madame Leseigneur reveals a cherished portrait of her late husband, a naval officer, is decaying. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait; they accept, and he senses the emotional weight behind the request.

When Adelaide briefly leaves and returns with candles, a visitor appears—an older gentleman in outmoded dress whose nobility mixes with libertine air. Their exchange hints at layered history and social distinctions. Throughout the evening Hippolyte is torn between admiration and uncertainty, drawn to her spirit yet aware of the fragile boundary between their worlds.

A remarkable, faded figure stood beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry,” Bonapartist sarcasm for the ragged remnants of the Monarchy: lean, aged, powdered hair dulled, intellect shadowed. Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz seemed apt. The silent old man puzzled the painter Chevalier—no one addressed him. His relation to the gallant man—friend, relative, or companion—was unclear; his presence hinted at a past link to the gallant’s deceased wife but offered no answers.

One of the less dilapidated men approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand and sat; another bowed and took a seat. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a posture recalling a famous painting, though her familiarity appeared to irritate him. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at the painter Schinner as if measuring him.

Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man noticed Hippolyte’s red ribbon and contrasted it with his own hard-won honors. Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, was briefly blind to the squalor around them; enthralled, he spoke little while the old gentleman lamented his losing streak at cards. Adelaide offered help; he insisted she stay where he could see her. When the game ended and the gentleman complained of losses, the silent figure only echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to take his leave, still under the spell of budding affection.

The next day Hippolyte longed to see Adelaide again. He waited until afternoon before visiting Madame de Rouville and awkwardly asked for the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone in the drawing-room, sent him to fetch it, revealing modesty with a charged glance. Hippolyte took the portrait and promised to return it soon.

Their relationship blossomed with laughter and confidences. Schinner strove to learn piquet but often lost; the unspoken bond between Hippolyte and Adelaide deepened into inseparability. Adelaide’s affection grew and Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed familiarity with her. Their love unfolded without grand gestures, founded on mutual trust and subtle exchanges. Within two months Hippolyte visited daily, the old gentleman’s visits faded, and Hippolyte took his place at the card table, sharing equally in the losses.

Hippolyte fretted about Madame de Rouville’s finances after spending two months on the Baron’s portrait—a work he prized—yet the Baroness said nothing of payment. Adelaide later visited his studio, moved by the painting; Hippolyte declared his desire to marry her, interrupted by the old vice-admiral’s praise and hint of aid.

That evening strange excitement at cards preceded the theft of Hippolyte’s purse. In pain and suspicion, he feared Adelaide and her mother were guilty; rumors unsettled him. Confronting Adelaide, he was struck by her pallor and sorrow and felt ashamed. During a tender card evening she discreetly placed a new purse before him. Overwhelmed, he vowed to marry her.

The vice-admiral soon returned with word of a promotion and offered support to the young couple, praising the portrait and dispelling doubts. The evening closed in renewed unity. The tale follows characters navigating love, trust, and misunderstanding, finding solace through faith, reconciliation, and shared bonds.
