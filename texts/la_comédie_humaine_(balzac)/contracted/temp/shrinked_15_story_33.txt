In late October 1800 a foreigner with a woman and a little girl stood for a long time before the Tuileries, near the ruins of the house where the wing meant to join Catherine de’ Medici’s chateau to the Valois Louvre begins.

The man stood with folded arms and bowed head, glancing now and then between the consular palace and his wife, who sat on a stone. Though the woman seemed occupied with the nine- or ten-year-old child whose long black hair she toyed with, she watched every look her companion gave. Their bond was not mere love; misery—perhaps the strongest tie—drew their anxious attention.

The stranger had a broad, serious head, thick hair like those in Carracci paintings; the jet black was streaked with white. His noble, proud features were marred by a hardness. Despite an erect, strong figure he looked over sixty. His worn clothes were foreign. His wife’s faded, once-beautiful face showed deep sadness, though she forced smiles when he looked. The little girl stood despite weariness, sun-tanned, her Italian cast marked by large black eyes beneath arched brows, native nobleness, and simple grace. Passersby felt moved by the plain despair; when the stranger noticed attention he looked so savage that bold loungers hurried off.

After standing some time, the tall stranger wiped his face as if to clear his thoughts. He drew a dagger from his breast and gave it to his companion, saying in Italian, “I will see if the Bonapartes remember us.” He walked slowly to the palace entrance, where a consular guard stopped him and refused to permit discussion. Seeing his obstinacy, the sentinel presented his bayonet. Fortune favored the stranger: the guard was changing, and the corporal pointed to the post commander.

“Let Bonaparte know that Bartolomeo di Piombo wishes to speak with him,” the Italian said to the captain.

The officer insisted one could not see the First Consul without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted that the soldier must go to Bonaparte. The officer refused, citing regulations. Bartolomeo frowned, casting a terrible look as if charging him with the consequences of refusal. Then he remained under the portico between garden and courtyard. Those who will things intensely are often aided by chance: at that moment a carriage drew up and Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, emerged.

“Ah, Loucian, it is lucky for me I have met you!” cried the stranger in Corsican patois. Lucien recognized him and, after a word, took the Corsican away.

Murat, Lannes, and Rapp were in the First Consul’s cabinet. As Lucien entered with Piombo, conversation ceased. Lucien took Napoleon by the arm and led him to a window recess. Napoleon signaled Murat and Lannes to retire; Rapp pretended not to see and lingered. Bonaparte spoke sharply and Rapp left reluctantly, though he waited near the adjoining salon. The First Consul opened the door suddenly and found his aide-de-camp close to the wall.

“Do you choose not to understand me?” said the First Consul. “I wish to be alone with my compatriot.”

“A Corsican!” replied the aide-de-camp. “I distrust those fellows too much to—”

Bonaparte smiled and pushed him aside. “Well, what has brought you here, my poor Bartolomeo?” he asked.

“To ask asylum and protection from you, if you are a true Corsican,” replied Bartolomeo roughly.

“What ill fortune drove you from the island? You were the richest—”

“I have killed all the Portas,” said the Corsican in a deep voice, frowning.

The First Consul stepped back in surprise.

“Do you mean to betray me?” cried Bartolomeo, with a dark look at Bonaparte. “Do you know that there are still four Piombos in Corsica?”

Lucien seized his compatriot’s arm and shook it. “Did you come here to threaten the savior of France?” he said.

Bonaparte signaled Lucien to be silent, then looked at Piombo. “Why did you kill the Portas?”

“We had been friends,” the man replied. “The Barbantis reconciled us. The day after we drank to end our quarrels I left for Bastia. The Portas stayed in my house and set fire to my vineyard at Longone. They killed my son Gregorio. My daughter Ginevra and my wife had taken the sacrament that morning and escaped; the Virgin protected them. When I returned my house was ashes. I found Gregorio’s body by accident at night and knew the Portas had done it. I went to the woods, called together all the men who had ever served me—do you hear me, Bonaparte?—and we marched to the Portas’ vineyard. We arrived at five; at seven they were all before God. Giacomo says Eliza Vanni saved a child, Luigi. But I myself bound Luigi to his bed before setting fire to the house. I left the island with my wife and child without learning whether Luigi is alive.”

Bonaparte listened with curiosity but not surprise. Lucien asked how many there had been.

“Seven,” replied Piombo. “All of them persecuted you in olden times.”

The brothers showed no hatred. “Ha! you are no longer Corsicans!” cried Piombo in despair. “Farewell. In other days I protected you,” he added reproachfully. “Without me your mother would never have reached Marseille,” he said to Bonaparte, who stood silent and thoughtful, his elbow on a mantel-shelf.

“As a matter of duty, Piombo,” said Napoleon at last, “I cannot take you under my wing. I have become the leader of a great nation; I command the Republic; I am bound to execute the laws.”

“Ha! ha!” said Bartolomeo scornfully.

“But I can shut my eyes,” Bonaparte continued. “The tradition of the vendetta will long prevent law's reign in Corsica,” he added to himself. “But it must be destroyed, at any cost.”

After a pause he spoke more personally. “Live here, in Paris,” he said to Bartolomeo. “We will know nothing of this affair. I will cause your property in Corsica to be bought to give you enough to live on for the present. Later, we will think of you. But remember, no more vendetta! There are no woods here to fly to. If you play with daggers you must expect no mercy. Here, the law protects all citizens; no one is allowed to do justice for himself.”

“He has made himself the head of a singular nation,” said Bartolomeo, taking Lucien’s hand and pressing it. “But you have both recognized me in misfortune, and I am yours henceforth, for life or death. You may dispose as you will of the Piombos.”

At these words his Corsican brow unbent and he looked pleasantly about him. “You are not badly off here,” he said, smiling, “You are all in red, like a cardinal.”

“Your success depends on yourself; you can have a palace too,” Bonaparte replied, watching him keenly. “It will often happen that I shall need some faithful friend in whom I can confide.”

A sigh of joy heaved the Corsican’s chest. He held out his hand to the First Consul. “The Corsican is in you still.”

Bonaparte smiled and looked at the man who seemed to bring a breath from the island that had saved him—the land he would never see again. He signaled to his brother, who then took Piombo away. Lucien asked about the family’s finances. Piombo showed his wife and Ginevra sitting on a heap of stones.

“We came from Fontainebleau on foot; we have not a single penny,” he said.

Lucien gave him his purse and told him to come the next day so arrangements could be made for their comfort. The value of Piombo’s Corsican property, if sold, would scarcely maintain him honorably in Paris.

Fifteen years elapsed between Piombo’s arrival in Paris with his family and the following event, which would be scarcely intelligible without this account of the preceding circumstances.

Servin, one of our distinguished artists, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to take painting lessons.

About forty, of strict morals and wholly devoted to his art, he had married for inclination the dowerless daughter of a general. At first the mothers of his pupils brought their daughters themselves; then, seeing the pains he took to deserve their confidence, they were content to send them alone.

He meant to accept only young ladies of wealthy, well-placed families, to avoid complaints about social mix. He even refused girls who wished to become professional artists, for they required a different, more technical instruction. Gradually his prudence and skill in initiating pupils, and the security mothers felt that their daughters would be among well-bred companions, made him an esteemed teacher in society. When a young girl wished to learn to draw, friends invariably advised: “Send her to Servin’s.”

Servin thus became a specialty for feminine art; like Herbault for bonnets, Leroy for gowns, Chevet for eatables. It was believed a young woman trained by Servin could judge the Musée, make a striking portrait, copy an old master, or paint a genre picture. He satisfied the educational needs of the aristocracy, and though he kept relations with the best Parisian families he remained independent and patriotic, preserving among them that easy, brilliant, half-ironical tone and freedom of judgment which characterize painters.

He had carried careful precaution into the arrangement of the studio. The entrance to the attic above his apartments was walled up. To reach this retreat—a space as private as a harem—one had to climb a small spiral stair within his rooms. The studio occupied nearly the whole attic under the roof, an unexpectedly vast gallery, lit from above through enormous panes with green linen shades used by artists to temper light. On dark-gray walls hung caricatures, heads drawn at a stroke in color or with the point of a knife, proving that, apart from expression, distinguished young girls have as much fun and folly as men. A small stove with a large pipe, a shelf running round the walls with plaster models—most covered with gray dust—Niobe’s woe-struck head, a smiling Venus, a hand thrust out like a pauper’s, yellowed écorchés, lay figures, frames without paintings and paintings without frames gave the room that studio physiognomy of jumble and bareness, poverty and riches, care and neglect. The vast receptacle of an atelier, where everything seems small, even man, has something of an opera coulisse; ancient garments, gilded armor, fragments of stuff, machinery lie there. Yet there is something mysteriously grand: genius and death, Diana and Apollo beside a skull—beauty and destruction—colors glowing in shadows, often a whole drama motionless and silent. Strange symbol of an artist’s head.

At the moment when our history begins a bright July sun lit the studio; two rays traced gold lines where dust shimmered. A dozen easels rose like masts. Several young girls animated the scene by varied expressions, attitudes, and dresses. Strong shadows cast by the green serge curtains, arranged for each easel, produced piquant light-and-shade effects. This group was the prettiest of all the pictures in the room.

A fair young girl, simply dressed, sat at some distance from her companions, working bravely and seeming in dread of mishap. No one looked at or spoke to her; she was the prettiest, most modest, and, it seemed, the least rich among them. Two principal groups, distinctly separated, showed two cliques, two minds even here, where rank and fortune might seem forgotten.

But whatever these divisions, the young girls, sitting or standing amid color-boxes, playing with brushes, handling dazzling palettes, painting, laughing, talking, and singing, completely natural and exhibiting their real selves, composed a spectacle unknown to man. One, proud, haughty, capricious, with black hair and fine hands, cast her glance here and there; another, light-hearted with chestnut hair and delicate white hands, smiled and seemed thoughtless; a third was dreamy, melancholy, pale, like a drooping flower; her neighbor, tall and indolent with long Asiatic eyes, said little and reflected, glancing at Antinous’s head.

Among them, like the jocoso of a Spanish play, another girl watched the rest with a comprehensive glance, making them laugh and tossing up her head, arch and pretty. She seemed to rule the first group—daughters of bankers, notaries, and merchants—rich, but aware of the cutting slights the aristocratic group put upon them. The latter were led by the daughter of one of the King’s ushers, a little creature as silly as she was vain, proud of being the daughter of a man with “an office at court.” She pretended to understand the master at the first word, seemed to do her work as a favor, used an eyeglass, came very dressed and always late, and entreated companions to speak low.

In this second group were several girls with exquisite figures and distinguished features, but little simplicity or candor. Their attitudes were elegant and movements graceful, but their faces lacked frankness; they belonged to a world where polite manners form the character early, and the abuse of social pleasures destroys sentiment and develops egotism.

Yet when the whole class assembled, childlike heads were seen among this bevy—ravishingly pure and virgin faces with half-open lips and spotless teeth; a virgin smile flickered. The studio then resembled not a studio, but a group of angels on a cloud.

By mid-day on this occasion, Servin had not appeared. For days he had spent most of his time in another studio, giving the last touches to a picture for the Exposition. All at once Mademoiselle Amélie Thirion, leader of the aristocrats, began to speak low and earnestly to her neighbor. A great silence fell on the patrician group, and the commercial party, surprised, were equally silent, trying to discover the subject. The secret of the young ultras was soon revealed.

Amélie rose, took an easel near hers, carried it away from the noble group, and placed it close to a board partition which separated the studio from the attic’s extreme end, where broken casts, defaced canvases, and winter wood were kept. Amélie’s action caused a murmur, yet she hastily rolled aside the stool, the box of colors, and even the Prudhon picture her absent pupil had been copying. After this coup d’état the Right began to work in silence, but the Left discoursed at length.

“What will Mademoiselle Piombo say to that?” asked a girl of Mademoiselle Matilde Roguin, the lively oracle of the banking group.

“She’s not a temper to say anything,” replied Matilde; “but fifty years hence she’ll remember the insult as if done the night before, and revenge it cruelly. She is a person I don’t want to be at war with.”

“The slight these young ladies mean to put upon her is all the more unkind,” said another, “because yesterday Mademoiselle Ginevra was very sad. Her father, they say, has just resigned. They ought not to add to her trouble, for she was considerate during the Hundred Days. I think our ultras act more from jealousy than party spite.”

“I have a great mind to get Mademoiselle Piombo’s easel and place it next to mine,” said Matilde. She rose, but sat down again.

“With a character like hers,” she said, “one can’t tell how she would take civility; better wait events.”

“Ecco là,” said the girl with black eyes, languidly.

The steps of someone coming up the narrow stairway sounded. “Here she comes!” passed from mouth to mouth; then absolute silence reigned.

To understand the importance of Amélie Thirion’s ostracism, add that this scene took place toward the end of July, 1815. The second return of the Bourbons had shaken friendships that had held under the first Restoration. Families, almost all divided in opinion, renewed many deplorable scenes that stain history in times of civil war. Children, young girls, old men shared the monarchical fever. Discord glided beneath roofs; distrust dyed words and actions of the most intimate friends.

Ginevra Piombo loved Napoleon to idolatry; how then could she hate him? The emperor was her compatriot and the benefactor of her father. Baron di Piombo was among those devoted servants who helped most in the return from Elba. Incapable of denying his political faith and anxious to confess it, the old baron remained in Paris among enemies. Ginevra Piombo was the more open to condemnation because she made no secret of the grief the second Restoration caused her family. The only tears she had so far shed had been drawn from her by news of Napoleon’s captivity on the Bellerophon and Labédoyère’s arrest.

The studio’s aristocratic girls belonged to the most devoted royalist families in Paris. It is hard now to convey the exaggerations and horror of the Bonapartists then prevalent. However petty Amélie’s action may seem now, it was a natural expression of prevailing hatred. Ginevra, one of Servin’s first pupils, had occupied the place now taken from her since her first day. The aristocratic circle had gradually surrounded her. To drive her from a spot that in some sense belonged to her was not only to insult her, but to inflict an artistic wound; artists have predilect spots where they work.

Nevertheless, political prejudice was not the chief motive of the Right clique. Ginevra, much the ablest of Servin’s pupils, was an object of intense jealousy. The master testified admiration for her talents and character; she outshone others. In fact, without anyone able to explain the ascendancy she obtained over those around her, she exercised a prestige not unlike Bonaparte’s over his soldiers.

The aristocracy of the studio had for days resolved upon the fall of this queen, but none had yet openly avoided the Bonapartist. Mademoiselle Thirion’s act was a decisive stroke intended to force others to become open accomplices of her hatred. Though Ginevra was sincerely loved by several of those royalists, most were indoctrinated at home with party ideas, and felt it best to keep aloof.

On Ginevra’s arrival she was received in profound silence. Of all the young women who had come to Servin’s studio, she was the handsomest, tallest, and best made. Her carriage and demeanor had a noble, graceful character commanding respect. Her face, instinct with intelligence, seemed to radiate light, inspired with the enthusiasm peculiar to Corsicans—without excluding calm. Her long hair and black eyes and lashes expressed passion; the corners of her mouth and the slightly marked lips gave signs of that kindliness strong beings derive from conscious strength.

By a singular caprice the charm of her face was somewhat contradicted by a marble forehead on which lay an almost savage pride, from which seemed to emanate Corsican moral instincts. All the rest of her person, the simplicity and ease of her Lombard beauty, was so seductive that it was difficult to hurt her. She inspired such attachment that her old father had her accompanied to and from the studio. The only defect of this poetic creature came from the very power of a fully developed beauty; she looked a woman. She had refused marriage out of love for her parents, feeling herself necessary to their comfort. Her taste for painting took the place of passions and interests that usually absorb her sex.

“You are very silent to-day, mesdemoiselles,” she said after advancing a little among her companions. “Good-morning, my little Laure,” she added in a soft, caressing voice, approaching the young girl painting apart. “That head is strong,—the flesh tints a little too rosy, but the drawing is excellent.”

Laure raised her head and looked tenderly at Ginevra; their faces showed mutual affection. A faint smile brightened the Italian’s lips, who seemed thoughtful; she walked slowly to her easel, glanced at the drawings and pictures, and greeted the young girls of the first group, not observing the unusual curiosity excited by her presence. She was like a queen in her court; she paid no attention to the profound silence of the patricians and passed before them without a word. Her absorption seemed so great that she sat before her easel, opened her color-box, took up her brushes, drew on her brown sleeves, arranged her apron, looked at her picture, examined her palette—apparently not thinking of what she was doing. All heads in the banking group turned toward her. If the Thirion camp did not show impatience frankly, their sidelong glances were none the less directed at Ginevra.

“She hasn’t noticed it!” said Mademoiselle Roguin.

At this instant Ginevra abandoned the meditative attitude and turned toward the aristocrats. She measured the distance that separated her from them at a glance, but said nothing.

“It hasn’t occurred to her they meant to insult her,” said Matilde; “she neither colored nor turned pale. How vexed these girls will be if she likes her new place as well as the old! You are out of bounds, mademoiselle,” she added aloud, addressing Ginevra.

The Italian pretended not to hear; perhaps she really did not. She rose abruptly, walked with a certain deliberation along the side of the partition that separated the adjoining closet from the studio, and seemed to examine the sash through which her light came—giving so much importance to it that she mounted a chair to raise the green serge higher. Reaching that height, her eye leveled with a slight opening in the partition—the real object of her curiosity. The glance she cast through it compared to a miser discovering Aladdin’s treasure. Then she sprang down hastily, returned to her place, changed the position of her picture, pretended dissatisfaction with the light, pushed a table close to the partition, placed a chair on it, climbed lightly to the summit of this frail erection, and again looked through the crevice. She cast but one glance into the space beyond, lit through a skylight; what she saw produced so strong an effect that she tottered.

“Take care, Mademoiselle Ginevra, you’ll fall!” cried Laure.

All the girls gazed at the imprudent climber; fear of their coming to her gave her courage; she recovered her equilibrium and replied, while balancing on the shaking chair: “Pooh! it is more solid than a throne!”

She then secured the curtain, came down, pushed the chair and table as far as possible from the partition, returned to her easel, and seemed to arrange it to suit the light. Her picture, however, was not in her mind; she was wholly bent on getting near the closet door where she finally settled. Sitting there she could distinctly hear a sound that had excited her curiosity the evening before and had whirled her young imagination across vast fields of conjecture. She recognized the firm and regular breathing of a man she had just seen asleep. Her curiosity was satisfied beyond expectation, but she felt saddled by an immense responsibility. Through the opening in the wall she had seen the Imperial eagle; upon the flock bed, faintly lighted from above, lay the form of an officer of the Guard. She guessed all. Servin was hiding a proscribed man!

She now trembled lest any companion come near and, by the regular breath or some deeper one, reveal the political victim. She resolved to keep her place by that door, trusting her wits to baffle danger.

“Better that I should be here,” she thought, “to prevent some luckless accident, than leave that poor man at the mercy of a heedless betrayal.”

This was the secret of the indifference Ginevra had seemed to show to the move of her easel. She was inwardly pleased because the change enabled her to gratify her curiosity naturally; besides, she was too preoccupied to perceive the motive for her removal.

Nothing is more mortifying to young girls, or any of us, than to see a piece of mischief, an insult, or biting speech miss its effect through the contempt or indifference of the intended victim. Hatred to an enemy seems to grow as that enemy is raised above us. Ginevra’s behavior was an enigma to all; friends and enemies were equally surprised; her friends claimed all virtues for her except forgiveness of injuries. Though occasions for that vice rarely arose in a studio, the specimens she had sometimes shown of vindictiveness left a strong impression.

After many conjectures, Mademoiselle Roguin concluded the Italian’s silence showed grandeur of soul; the banking circle, inspired, formed a project to humiliate the aristocracy. They succeeded, by a fire of sarcasms, in humbling the Right coterie.

Madame Servin’s arrival put a stop to the struggle. With a servant-wife’s shrewdness, Amélie Thirion had noticed, analyzed, and mentally commented on the extreme preoccupation of Ginevra, which prevented her from hearing the bitterly polite war of words. The vengeance Mademoiselle Roguin and her companions inflicted on Thirion’s group had the effect of driving the young ultras to search for the cause of the silence obstinately maintained by Ginevra. The beautiful Italian became the center of all glances and was henceforth watched by friends and foes alike.

It is very difficult to hide even a slight emotion from fifteen inquisitive, unoccupied young girls whose wits and mischief ask for secrets to guess and schemes to create; they find many interpretations for each gesture, glance, and word.

At that moment Madame Servin produced an interlude in the drama below the surface. As soon as she entered, her eyes turned to the door near which Ginevra sat. Under present circumstances this glance was not lost; at first none noticed, but Mademoiselle Thirion recollected it later, and it explained to her the doubt, fear, and mystery that gave something wild and frightened to Madame Servin’s look.

“Mesdemoiselles,” she said, “Monsieur Servin cannot come to-day.”

She then went round complimenting each girl, receiving in return those feminine caresses given as much by tone and looks as by gestures. She reached Ginevra with uneasiness she tried in vain to disguise. They nodded in a friendly way but said nothing; one painted, the other stood looking at the painting. The soldier’s breathing could be distinctly heard, but Madame Servin appeared not to notice it; her feigned ignorance was so obvious that Ginevra recognized wilful deafness. The unknown man turned on his pallet.

The Italian then looked fixedly at Madame Servin, who said with no change of face: “Your copy is as fine as the original; if I had to choose between the two I should be puzzled.”

“Monsieur Servin has not taken his wife into his confidence as to this mystery,” thought Ginevra, who, after replying with a gentle smile of incredulity, began to hum a Corsican canzonetta to cover the noise made by the prisoner.

It was so unusual to hear the studious Italian sing that all the other girls looked up in surprise. Later this circumstance served as proof to charitable suppositions of jealousy.

Madame Servin soon went away, and the session ended without further events; Ginevra allowed her companions to depart and seemed to intend to work later. But unconsciously she betrayed desire to be left alone by impatient, ill-disguised glances at pupils slow in leaving. Mademoiselle Thirion, cruel to the girl who excelled her, guessed her rival’s industry hid some purpose. By dint of watching she was struck by the attentive air with which Ginevra seemed to be listening to sounds no one else had heard. The expression of impatience she detected was like a flash of light to her.

Amélie was the last to leave; from there she went down to Madame Servin’s apartment and spoke for a moment; then she pretended to have left her bag, ran softly back to the studio, and found Ginevra once more mounted on her frail scaffolding, absorbed in contemplation and not hearing the slight noise of her companion’s footsteps. True, Amélie stepped as if on eggs. She hastily withdrew outside the door and coughed. Ginevra quivered, turned, saw her enemy, blushed, hastened to alter the shade to give meaning to her position, and came down leisurely. She left the studio, bearing in memory the image of a man’s head as beauteous as Girodet’s Endymion, a masterpiece she had lately copied.

“To banish so young a man! Who can he be? for he is not Marshal Ney—”

These two sentences are the simplest expression of many ideas Ginevra turned over for two days. On the third day, despite haste to be first at the studio, she found Mademoiselle Thirion already there, having come in a carriage.

Ginevra and her enemy observed each other for a long time, keeping their faces impenetrable. Amélie had seen the handsome head of the mysterious man, but fortunately and unfortunately the Imperial eagles and uniform were so placed she did not see them through the crevice. She was lost in conjectures. Suddenly Servin came in, much earlier than usual.

“Mademoiselle Ginevra,” he said after glancing round, “why have you placed yourself there? The light is bad. Come nearer to the rest and pull down that curtain a little.”

Then he sat down near Laure, whose work earned his cordial attention.

“Well, well!” he cried; “here is a head extremely well done. You’ll be another Ginevra.”

The master went from easel to easel, scolding, flattering, jesting, his jests more dreaded than his reprimands. Ginevra had not obeyed his order but remained at her post, resolved not to quit it. She took a sheet of paper and sketched in sepia the head of the hidden man. A work done under emotion has its own stamp. Passion often takes the place of genius; under her circumstances the intuition lent her, for the moment, a supernatural talent. The head was dashed on the paper amid an awkward tremble she mistook for fear, in which a physiologist would recognize inspiration’s fire. From time to time she glanced furtively at companions to hide the sketch if any came near. But despite her watchfulness, there was a moment when she did not see Amélie’s eyeglass turned full upon the drawing from a great portfolio. Mademoiselle Thirion, recognizing the portrait of the mysterious man, showed herself abruptly, and Ginevra hastily covered the sheet.

“Why do you stay there in spite of my advice, mademoiselle?” asked the professor gravely.

The pupil turned her easel so only the master could see the sketch, which she placed upon it, and said in an agitated voice: “Do you not think, as I do, that the light is very good? Had I not better remain here?”

Servin turned pale. As nothing escapes malice’s piercing eyes, Mademoiselle Thirion became as it were a sharer in the sudden emotion of master and pupil.

“You are right,” said Servin; “but really,” he added with a forced laugh, “you will soon know more than I do.”

A pause followed while the professor studied the officer’s head drawing.

“It is a masterpiece! worthy of Salvator Rosa!” he exclaimed with the energy of an artist.

All the pupils rose, and Mademoiselle Thirion darted forward with the velocity of a tiger. At that instant the prisoner, awakened perhaps by the noise, began to move. Ginevra knocked over her stool, said a few incoherent sentences, and began to laugh; she had thrown the portrait into her portfolio before Amélie could reach her. The easel was now surrounded; Servin descanted on the beauty of the copy his favorite was making, and the whole class was duped by this stratagem, except Amélie, who, slipping behind companions, attempted to open the portfolio where she had seen Ginevra throw the sketch. The latter took it up without a word and placed it before her. The two girls then looked at each other fixedly, in silence.

“Come, mesdemoiselles, take your places,” said Servin. “If you wish to do as well as Mademoiselle di Piombo, you mustn’t be always talking fashions and balls, trifling your time away.”

When they were reseated, Servin sat down beside Ginevra.

“Was it not better that I should be the one to discover the mystery rather than the others?” asked the girl in a low voice.

“Yes,” replied the painter, “you are one of us, a patriot; but even if you were not, I should have confided the matter to you.”

Master and pupil understood one another, and Ginevra no longer feared to ask: “Who is he?”

“An intimate friend of Labédoyère, who contributed more than any other man, except the unfortunate colonel, to unite the 7th regiment with the grenadiers of Elba. He was a major in the Imperial Guard and was at Waterloo.”

“Why not have burned his uniform and shako and given him citizen’s clothes?” asked Ginevra impatiently.

“He will have them to-night.”

“You ought to have closed the studio for some days.”

“He is going away.”

“Then they’ll kill him,” said the girl. “Let him stay here with you till the storm is over. Paris is still where a man can be hidden safely. Is he a friend of yours?” she asked.

“No; he has no claim on me but ill-luck. He came into my hands by my father-in-law, who returned to the army during the campaign and very cleverly rescued him from those who captured Labédoyère. He came here to defend the general—a foolish fellow!”

“Do you call him that!” cried Ginevra, casting an astonished glance at the painter who was silent a moment.

“My father-in-law is too closely watched to keep him in his own house,” Servin resumed. “So he brought him to me by night about a week ago. I hoped to keep him out of sight in this corner, the only spot in the house where he could be safe.”

“If I can be useful, employ me,” said Ginevra. “I know the Maréchal de Fétre.”

“Well, we’ll see,” replied the painter.

This conversation lasted too long to escape the other girls. Servin lingered, giving long lessons, and was still there at the hour pupils usually left.

“You are forgetting your bag, Mademoiselle Thirion,” said the professor, running after the girl now condescending to spy to satisfy jealousy.

The baffled pupil returned for the bag, feigning surprise at her carelessness; but Servin’s act was fresh proof to her of a mystery’s existence and importance. She now noisily ran down the staircase and slammed the door into the Servins’ apartment to give an impression she had gone; then she softly returned and stationed herself outside the studio door.

When the painter and Ginevra thought themselves alone, Servin rapped softly on the garret door, which opened on its rusty hinges. Ginevra saw a tall, well-made young man in an Imperial uniform; one arm was in a sling and his pallor betrayed suffering. He recoiled at the sight of an unknown woman.

Amelie, afraid to remain and unable to see into the room with the door closed, slipped away silently when she heard the garret open.

“Fear nothing,” said the painter to the officer. “Mademoiselle is the daughter of a faithful friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo.”

At once the soldier’s doubt of Ginevra’s patriotism vanished.

“You are wounded,” she observed.

“Oh, it is nothing, mademoiselle,” he replied; “the wound is healing.”

From the street came the vendors’ cry: “Condemned to death!” They all shuddered, and the soldier heard a name that blanched him.

“Labedoyere!” he cried, falling on a stool.

They sat in silence. Drops stood on the young man’s livid forehead; he seized his hair in despair and rested his elbow on Ginevra’s easel.

“After all,” he said, rising abruptly, “Labedoyere and I knew what we were doing. We were certain of the fate awaiting us, whether triumph or defeat. He dies for the Cause, and here am I hiding myself!”

He rushed to the studio door; Ginevra reached it first and barred his way.

“Can you restore the Emperor?” she asked. “Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?”

“But what can I do?” the young man asked the two friends chance had sent him. “I have no relation in the world. Labedoyere was my protector and friend; without him I am alone. Tomorrow I myself may be condemned; my only fortune was my pay. I spent my last penny to come here and try to snatch Labedoyere from his fate; death is, therefore, a necessity for me. When a man decides on death he ought to know how to sell his life to the executioner. I was thinking just now that the life of an honest man is worth that of two traitors, and the blow of a dagger well placed may give immortality.”

This outburst alarmed Servin and even Ginevra, who understood the young man’s temper. She admired his handsome face and sweet voice, which kept its fervor even in anger. She tried to soothe him.

“Monsieur,” she said, “as for your pecuniary distress, permit me to offer my savings. My father is rich; I am his only child; he loves me, and I am sure he will never blame me. Have no scruple in accepting my offer; our property comes from the Emperor; we do not own a penny that is not the result of his munificence. Is it not gratitude to assist his faithful soldiers? Take what you need as indifferently as I offer it. It is only money!” she added with contempt. “Now, as for friends—those you shall have.”

She raised her head proudly and her eyes shone.

“The head which falls tomorrow before a dozen muskets will save yours,” she went on. “Wait till the storm is over; you can then escape and take service in foreign countries if you are not forgotten here; or in the French army, if you are.”

There is a delicacy in consolation from a woman, something maternal, foreseeing, and complete. When words of hope and peace are spoken with graceful gesture by a beautiful benefactress, a young man yields. The prisoner breathed in love through all his senses. A rosy tinge colored his white cheeks; his eyes lost some of their dulled sadness and he said in a peculiar tone:

“You are an angel of goodness—But Labedoyere!” he added. “Oh, Labedoyere!”

At that cry they looked at one another in silence, each understanding the others’ thought. They were no longer mere acquaintances: in those moments the links of friendship knit them as if for years.

“Dear friend,” said Servin, “can you save him?”

“I can avenge him.”

Ginevra quivered. Though the stranger was handsome, what touched her more was pity for suffering that was not ignoble; yet a cry of vengeance, a proscribed soul devoted to Napoleon, Corsican generosity—this stirred her deeply. She looked at the officer with respectful emotion that shook his heart. For the first time a man had produced a keen sensation in her. At once her sympathy deepened into something more: she put the stranger’s soul on a par with his noble beauty and the proportions she admired as an artist. Led by curiosity to pity, from pity to powerful interest, she felt such profound sensations she was uneasy at staying.

“Until tomorrow, then,” she said, giving him a gentle parting smile.

Seeing that smile revived her features; the stranger forgot all else for a moment.

“Tomorrow,” he said sadly; “but tomorrow, Labedoyere—”

Ginevra put a finger to her lips and looked at him, as if saying: “Be calm, be prudent.”

The young man cried in his own language: “Ah! Dio! che non vorrei vivere dopo averla veduta?—Who would not wish to live after seeing her?”

The peculiar accent made Ginevra quiver.

“Are you Corsican?” she cried, returning toward him with a beating heart.

“I was born in Corsica,” he replied; “but I was brought, while very young, to Genoa, and as soon as old enough for military service I enlisted.”

The young man’s beauty, his attachment to the Emperor, his wound and misfortunes—all blended into a new, delightful feeling in Ginevra. This persecuted man was a child of Corsica; he spoke its language. She stood motionless, held by a magical sensation; before her eyes was a living portrait to which every feeling lent vivid color. At Servin’s invitation the officer sat on a divan; the painter, after removing the sling, undid the bandages to dress the wound. Ginevra shuddered at the long, broad sabre gash across his forearm and could not help a moan. The stranger raised his head and smiled at her. Servin lifted the lint and touched the lacerated flesh with delicate care; the wounded man’s pale face expressed, as he looked at the girl, more pleasure than pain. An artist might have admired the contrasts: white linen and bared arm against the red and blue uniform.

A soft half-light pervaded the studio; an evening ray lit where the soldier sat, bathing his blanched face, black hair, and clothes. To Ginevra’s Italian imagination it was an omen. He seemed a celestial messenger, speaking her language and unconsciously conjuring childhood memories; in her heart dawned a feeling as fresh and pure as her innocence. For a short moment she was motionless and dreamy, then blushed, exchanged a soft glance with the wounded man, and fled with his vision before her eyes.

The next day, though not a class day, Ginevra came to the studio and the prisoner sat beside her easel. Servin, with a sketch to finish, played mentor as the two talked chiefly in Corsican. The soldier related the sufferings of the retreat from Moscow; at nineteen he had made the passage of the Beresins, and was almost the last of his regiment. He described with fiery words the disaster of Waterloo. His voice was music to the Italian girl. Brought up as a Corsican, Ginevra was, in some sense, a child of Nature; falseness was unknown to her; she gave herself up without reserve to her impressions, showing them without the coquetry of Parisian girls. That day she often sat with palette and brushes in her hands without touching a color, eyes fixed on the officer, lips slightly parted, listening as if painting a stroke never painted. She was not surprised at the softness in his eyes; she felt her own despite a resolve to remain stern. After pauses she painted diligently without raising her head, because he sat near and watched. The first time he sat by her in silence she asked after a long pause:

“Does it amuse you to see me paint?”

That day she learned his name was Luigi. They agreed that on class days, when they could not see each other, any important political event would be signaled by Ginevra singing certain Corsican melodies.

The following day Mademoiselle Thirion informed the class, under pledge of secrecy, that Ginevra di Piombo had a lover, a young man who came during lesson hours and concealed himself in the garret.

“You, who take her part,” she said to Mademoiselle Roguin, “watch her carefully, and you will see how she spends her time.”

Ginevra was observed with malicious attention. They listened to her songs and watched her glances. At times, when she supposed herself unseen, a dozen pairs of eyes were furtively upon her. Enlightened thus, the girls read truly the emotions crossing the beautiful Italian’s features—her gestures, the tones in which she hummed, and the attention with which she listened to sounds only she could hear through the partition.

By the end of a week, Laure was the only one of Servin’s fifteen pupils who had resisted peeking at Luigi through the crevice; she still defended her friend. Mademoiselle Roguin tried to make her wait on the staircase after class to prove the intimacy by entering the studio and surprising them, but Laure refused to condescend to such espionage, and thus became the object of reprobation.

Soon Mademoiselle Thirion declared it improper to attend classes with a painter whose opinions were tainted with patriotism and Bonapartism (then synonymous), and she ceased to attend. Though she forgot Ginevra, the harm she planted bore fruit. Little by little the other young girls told their mothers of the strange events at the studio. One day Matilde Roguin did not come; the next another was missing, and so on, until the last three or four left. Ginevra and Laure were the sole occupants of the deserted studio for several days.

Ginevra did not notice the falling off nor ask the cause. As soon as she had invented means of communicating with Luigi she lived in the studio in a delightful solitude, thinking only of the officer and the dangers that threatened him. Though a sincere admirer of noble characters who never betray their political faiths, she urged Luigi to recognize royal authority to be released and remain in France. He refused. If passions are born and nourished under romantic causes, never were so many such circumstances conjoined to unite two young souls. Their friendship progressed in a month more than a society friendship would in ten years. Adversity tested and revealed character. Ginevra studied Luigi and soon esteemed him. Older than Luigi, she found charm in being courted by a youth so grand, so tried by fate—a youth who combined manly experience with adolescent graces. Luigi felt unspeakable pleasure in being apparently protected by a woman of twenty-five. Was it not proof of love? The union of gentleness and pride, strength and tenderness in Ginevra was irresistible, and he was subjugated. In short, they loved each other so profoundly there was no need to deny or proclaim it.

One evening Ginevra heard the accustomed signal. Luigi scratched with a pin on the woodwork, making no more noise than a spider fastening its thread. The signal meant he wished to come out.

Ginevra glanced around, and not seeing Laure, opened the door; but Luigi caught sight of the pupil and abruptly retired. Surprised, Ginevra saw Laure and said as she went to her easel:

“You are staying late, my dear. That head seems finished; you only want a highlight—see! on that knot of hair.”

“You would do me a great kindness,” said Laure in a trembling voice, “if you would give this copy a few touches; then I could carry away something to remind me of you.”

“Willingly,” said Ginevra, painting a few strokes. “But I thought it was a long way from your home, and it is late.”

“Oh! Ginevra, I am going away, never to return,” cried the poor girl sadly.

“You mean to leave Monsieur Servin!” exclaimed Ginevra, less affected than she might have been a month earlier.

“Haven’t you noticed, Ginevra, that for some days past you and I have been alone in the studio?”

“True,” said Ginevra, as if struck by recollection. “Are all those young ladies ill, or going to be married, or are their fathers on duty at court?”

“They have left Monsieur Servin,” replied Laure.

“Why?”

“On your account, Ginevra.”

“My account!” repeated the Corsican, springing up, brow threatening and eyes flashing.

“Oh! don’t be angry, my kind Ginevra,” cried Laure in distress. “My mother insists on my leaving. The young ladies say you have some intrigue, and that Monsieur Servin allows the young man you love to stay in the dark attic. I never believed these calumnies nor told my mother. But last night Madame Roguin met her at a ball and asked if she still sent me here. When my mother answered yes, Madame Roguin told her the falsehoods. Mamma scolded me severely; she said I must have known all, and that I failed in proper confidence between mother and daughter. Oh! my dear Ginevra! I, who took you for my model, how grieved I am that I can’t be your companion.”

“We shall meet again in life; girls marry—” said Ginevra.

“When they are rich,” sighed Laure.

“Come and see me; my father has a fortune—”

“Ginevra,” Laure added tenderly, “Madame Roguin and my mother are coming to see Monsieur Servin tomorrow to reproach him; hadn’t you better warn him?”

A thunderbolt could not have surprised Ginevra more.

“What matter is it to them?” she asked naively.

“Everybody thinks it very wrong. Mamma says it is immoral.”

“And you, Laure, what do you say?”

Laure looked up and their thoughts joined. She could no longer hold back tears; she flung herself on Ginevra’s breast and sobbed. At this moment Servin entered.

“Mademoiselle Ginevra,” he cried with enthusiasm, “I have finished my picture! It is now being varnished. What have you been doing meanwhile? Where are the young ladies; are they on holiday or in the country?”

Laure dried her tears, bowed to Monsieur Servin, and went away.

“The studio has been deserted for some days,” replied Ginevra, “and the young ladies are not coming back.”

“Pooh!” said the artist.

“Oh! don’t laugh,” said Ginevra. “Listen: I am the involuntary cause of the loss of your reputation—”

The artist smiled and interrupted:

“My reputation? Why, in a few days my picture will make it at the Exposition.”

“That relates to your talent,” replied the girl. “I speak of your morality. Those young ladies have told their mothers that Luigi was shut up here, and that you lent yourself— to—our love.”

“There is some truth in that, mademoiselle,” replied the professor. “The mothers of those young ladies are foolish; had they come to me I should have explained. But I don’t care a straw! Life is short.”

And the painter snapped his fingers above his head. Luigi, who had heard part of the conversation, came in.

“You have lost all your scholars,” he cried. “I have ruined you!”

The artist took Luigi’s hand and Ginevra’s and joined them.

“Marry one another, my children,” he said with fatherly kindness.

They dropped their eyes; their silence was the first avowal of love.

“You will surely be happy,” said Servin. “There is nothing to equal the happiness of two beings like yourselves when bound together in love.”

Luigi pressed his protector’s hand and presently, with emotion, said:

“To you I owe it all.”

“Be happy! I bless and wed you,” said the painter with comic unction, laying his hands on the lovers’ heads.

This jest ended the strained feeling. All three looked at one another and laughed. Ginevra pressed Luigi’s hand with the simple ardor of her native habits.

“Ah ca, my dear children,” resumed Servin, “you think all will go right now, but you are much mistaken.”

The lovers looked astonished.

“Don’t be anxious. I’m the only one your romance will harm. But Madame Servin is a little straitlaced; I don’t see how to settle it with her.”

“Heavens! and I forgot to tell you,” exclaimed Ginevra, “that Madame Roguin and Laure’s mother are coming here tomorrow to—”

“I understand,” said the painter.

“But you can easily justify yourself,” continued the girl, proudly. “Monsieur Luigi,” she added to him with an arch look, “will no longer object to entering the royal service. Well then,” after his smile, “tomorrow morning I will send a petition to a most influential man at the War ministry—a man who will refuse nothing to the daughter of the Baron di Piombo. We shall obtain a ‘tacit’ pardon for Captain Luigi, for, of course, they will not allow him the rank of major. And then,” she added to Servin, “you can confound the mothers of my charitable companions by telling them the truth.”

“You are an angel!” cried Servin.

While this passed at the studio, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious at her delay.

“It is six o’clock, and Ginevra not yet home!” cried Bartolomeo.

“She was never so late before,” said his wife.

The two old people looked at one another with unusual anxiety. Too restless to remain, Bartolomeo paced the salon with an active step for a man over seventy-seven. Thanks to a robust constitution he had changed little since arriving in Paris; despite his tall figure he walked erect. His white sparse hair left uncovered a broad protuberant skull suggesting character and firmness. His face, seamed with deep wrinkles, had taken with age a nobler expression, preserving pallid tones that inspire veneration. The ardor of his passions lived in the fire of his eyes; his still-not-wholly-white eyebrows retained mobility. His head’s aspect was stern but conveyed that Piombo had a right to be so. His kindness and gentleness were known only to his wife and daughter. In functions, or before strangers, he never laid aside the majesty time had impressed; the habit of frowning with heavy eyebrows and giving his eyes a Napoleonic fixity made his manner icy.

During his political life he had been so generally feared he seemed unsocial. The life, morals, and fidelity of Piombo made him obnoxious to courtiers. Despite delicate missions entrusted to him which would have been lucrative to others, he possessed an income of not more than thirty thousand francs from an investment in the Grand Livre. Considering cheap government securities under the Empire and Napoleon’s liberality toward faithful servants, the Baron di Piombo must have been a man of stern integrity. He owed his barony to the Emperor’s need to give him a title before sending him on missions to foreign courts.

Bartolomeo always professed hatred for the traitors Napoleon surrounded himself with. It is told that he took three steps to the door of the Emperor’s cabinet after advising him to get rid of three men in France on the eve of the 1814 campaign. After the second Bourbon return Bartolomeo ceased wearing the Legion of Honor. No man better represented those old Republicans, incorruptible friends to the Empire, relics of two energetic governments. Though he displeased courtiers, he had Darus, Drouots, and Carnots as friends. As for other politicians, he cared not a whiff of his cigar’s smoke for them, especially since Waterloo.

Bartolomeo had bought, for the moderate sum Madame Mère had paid him for his Corsican estates, the old Portenduere mansion, in which he made no changes. Lodged usually at government expense, he did not occupy it until after Fontainebleau. Following simple habits, the baron and his wife gave no heed to outward splendor; their furniture was what they bought with the mansion. The grand apartments, lofty, sombre, and bare, the wide mirrors in blackened gilded frames, and Louis XIV–period furniture suited Bartolomeo and his wife, personages worthy of antiquity.

Under the Empire and during the Hundred Days, while exercising well-rewarded functions, the old Corsican maintained a great establishment, more to honor his office than to shine himself. His and his wife’s lives were so frugal and tranquil that their modest fortune sufficed. To them their daughter Ginevra was more precious than all wealth. When in May, 1814, the Baron di Piombo resigned, dismissed his servants, and closed his stable, Ginevra, quiet and unpretending like her parents, saw nothing to regret. Like all great souls, she found luxury in strength of feeling and happiness in quiet and work. These three loved one another too well for externals to matter.

Often, especially after Napoleon’s second fall, Bartolomeo and his wife passed delightful evenings with their daughter, listening while she sang and played. They took vast secret pleasure in her slightest word; their eyes followed her with tender anxiety; they heard her step in the court-yard. Like lovers, the three often sat silently together, understanding better than by speech the eloquence of their souls. This profound sentiment animated their every thought. If memories of Napoleon’s benefits or public events distracted them, they could always return to this source without affecting their community of thought, for Ginevra shared their political passions. What more natural than the ardor with which they found refuge in their only child?

Until now public life had absorbed Bartolomeo’s energy; after leaving office he felt the need to cast it into the last sentiment remaining. Apart from parentage, there may have been another reason for their intense reciprocal love: it was undivided love. Ginevra’s whole heart belonged to her father, as his belonged to her; and if we are bound by defects more than virtues, Ginevra echoed her father’s youthful passions. This was the sole imperfection of their triple life. Ginevra was born unyielding, vindictive, and passionate, like her father.

The Corsican delighted in developing these savage sentiments in his daughter, as a lion teaches lion-cubs to spring at prey. But this apprenticeship to vengeance having no outlet in family life, the child turned the principle against her father; as a child she forgave him nothing, and he yielded. Piombo saw nothing more than childish nonsense in these quarrels, but Ginevra acquired a habit of ruling her parents. Amid the tempests he loved to excite, a look or word of tenderness sufficed to pacify them, and often they were nearest a kiss when threatening vehemently.

Nevertheless, for five years Ginevra, grown wiser, avoided such scenes. Her faithfulness, devotion, love filling her every thought, and admirable good sense overcame her temper. Yet a great evil resulted from her training; she lived with her parents on an equality always dangerous.

Piombo and his wife, without education, allowed Ginevra to study as she pleased. Following youthful caprices she studied many things for a time, abandoning each train of thought until painting proved her dominant passion. Ginevra would have made a noble woman had her mother guided her studies and harmonized her gifts; her defects came from the fatal education the old Corsican delighted in giving her.

After pacing and ringing the bell, a servant entered.

“Go and meet Mademoiselle Ginevra,” said his master.

“I always regret our carriage on her account,” remarked the baroness.

“She said she did not want one,” replied Piombo, looking at his wife, who, used to obedience, lowered her eyes and said no more.

Already a septuagenarian, tall, withered, pale, and wrinkled, the baroness resembled those old women Schnetz paints in Italian genre scenes. She was habitually silent, sometimes seeming another Mrs. Shandy; occasionally a word or look betrayed that her feelings retained youthful vigor. Her dress, devoid of coquetry, was often in bad taste. She usually sat passive, buried in a low sofa like a Sultana Valide, awaiting or admiring her Ginevra. All was well with her if Ginevra was happy. Her hair was white, and a few strands only showed above her wrinkled forehead and hollow cheeks.

“It is now fifteen days,” she said, “since Ginevra made a practice of being late.”

“Jean is so slow!” cried the impatient old man, buttoning his blue coat and seizing his hat, which he dashed on as he took his cane and departed.

“You will not get far,” said his wife, calling after him.

As she spoke the porte-cochere opened and shut, and the old mother heard Ginevra’s steps in the court-yard. Bartolomeo almost instantly reappeared, carrying his daughter, who struggled in his arms.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!”

Ginevra was set down respectfully and nodded to her mother, who had been frightened by the cry. The baroness’s pale face regained color and gaiety. Piombo rubbed his hands violently—his token of joy; when his features relaxed they became kindly. The two old people looked like a drooping plant refreshed by a little water.

“Now, to dinner!” cried the baron, offering his large hand to his daughter, whom he called “Signora Piombellina.” Ginevra smiled.

“Ah ca!” said Piombo as they left the table. “Your mother tells me you have stayed at the studio much longer than usual. It seems painting is more to you than your parents—”

“Oh, father!”

“Ginevra is preparing some surprise for us, I think,” said the mother.

“A picture of your own! will you bring us that?” cried the Corsican, clapping his hands.

“Yes, I am very much occupied at the studio,” replied Ginevra slowly.

“What is the matter, Ginevra? You are turning pale!” cried her mother.

“No!” declared the girl resolutely. “No! it shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie.”

Piombo and his wife looked at her in astonishment.

“I love a young man,” she added, her voice full of feeling, lowering her eyelids as if to veil the fire of her eyes.

“Is he a prince?” asked her father ironically.

“No, father,” she said gently, “he is a young man without fortune.”

“Is he very handsome?”

“He is very unfortunate.”

“What is he?”

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed, without refuge; Servin concealed him, and—”

“Servin is a good fellow,” cried Piombo, “but you, my daughter, you do wrong to love any man except your father.”

“It does not depend on me to love, or not to love,” replied Ginevra.

“I flattered myself,” continued her father, “that my Ginevra would be faithful to me until I died; that my love and that of her mother would suffice her till then; I did not expect our tenderness would find a rival in her soul—”

“Did I ever reproach you for your fanaticism for Napoleon?” said Ginevra. “Have you never loved any one but me? Did you not leave me for months when you went on missions? I bore your absence courageously. Life has necessities to which we must all submit.”

“Ginevra!”

“No, you don’t love me for myself; your reproaches betray intolerable egotism.”

“You dare blame your father’s love!” exclaimed Piombo, eyes flashing.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” replied Ginevra, gentler than her trembling mother expected. “You have grounds for your egotism, as I have for my love. Heaven is my witness that no girl has fulfilled her duty to her parents better than I. I have never felt anything but love and happiness where others see obligation. It is fifteen years that I have never left your wing, and it has been a dear pleasure to charm your life. But is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me hereafter?”

“What! do you reckon benefits with your father, Ginevra?” said Piombo in a dangerous tone.

A dreadful pause followed. Bartolomeo at last broke the silence in a heart-rending tone: “Oh! stay with us! stay with your father, your old father! I cannot have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, remember I need not leave you; we shall be two to love you; you will learn the man to whose care you bequeath me. You will be doubly cherished—by him who is my other self, by me who am all his.”

“Oh! Ginevra, Ginevra!” cried the Corsican, clenching his fists; “why did you not marry when Napoleon brought me to accept the idea? Why did you not take the counts and dukes he presented?”

“They loved me to order,” she said. “Besides, they would have made me live with them, and I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone,” said Piombo, “and yet you marry!—that is leaving me alone. I know you, my daughter; in that case you would cease to love us. Elisa,” he added to his stupefied wife, “we have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry.”

The old man sat down, raised his hands to heaven as if invoking Divine power; then bowed himself over as if weighed down with sorrow. Ginevra saw his agitation, and the restraint with which he checked his anger touched her to the heart; she had expected a violent crisis and had not armed her soul against paternal gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “no, you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. But love her a little for her own sake. If you knew how he loves me! Ah! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” cried Piombo, terrible. “No, I can never endure the idea of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve he would kill me; if he did not love you, I should put a dagger through him.”

His hands and lips trembled, his body shook, but his eyes flashed lightnings. Ginevra alone matched his look, her eyes flaming; the daughter was worthy of the sire.

“Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “To love you as a father is paradise on earth; who is there worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” said Ginevra; “he of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” repeated Piombo mechanically; “who is he?”

“He whom I love.”

“How can he know you enough to love you?”

“Father,” said Ginevra with impatience, “whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” cried Piombo.

Ginevra bent her head softly.

“You love him more than you love us?”

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger than the other?”

“I think it is,” said Ginevra.

“You shall not marry him,” cried the Corsican, his voice shaking the window-panes.

“I shall marry him,” replied Ginevra, tranquilly.

“Oh, God!” cried the mother, “how will this quarrel end? Santa Virgina! place thyself between them!”

The baron, who had been striding, now sat; an icy sternness darkened his face; he looked fixedly at his daughter and said in a weak gentle voice: “Ginevra, no! you will not marry him. Oh! say nothing more to-night—let me think the contrary. Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not pass her word and break it,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” said the baroness. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” said the baron to his wife, who, terrified, had gone to marble.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” said Ginevra.

“No order can be unjust from the lips of your father, my daughter. Why do you judge my action? The repugnance I feel is counsel from on high, sent perhaps to protect you from some great evil.”

“The only evil could be that he did not love me.”

“Always he!”

“Yes, always,” she answered. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you he would be ever in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” cried Piombo.

“Oh!” said Ginevra, shaking her head.

“Well, then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” cried Ginevra.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” said the father, driven to exasperation.

“All the more reason why I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

This coolness and power of argument increased Piombo’s trouble; the blood rushed to his head, his face turned purple. Ginevra shuddered; she sprang like a bird on her father’s knee, threw her arms around his neck, and caressed his white hair, exclaiming tenderly: “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father, my kind father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” replied Piombo, whose anger melted under this caress like snow beneath the sun.

“It was time you ceased,” said the baroness, trembling.

“Poor mother!”

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!”

The father played with his daughter as though she were six. He amused himself by releasing the waving volume of her hair, by dandling her on his knee; there was something of madness in these expressions of his love. Presently she scolded while kissing him, tried by jesting to obtain admission for Luigi; but her father, also jesting, refused. She sulked, coaxed again, and sulked until, by the end of the evening, she had impressed on her father both her love for Luigi and the idea of an approaching marriage.

The next day she said no more about her love; she was more caressing to her father than ever, and testified the utmost gratitude, as if to thank him for the consent he seemed to have given by silence. That evening she sang and played to him long, exclaiming now and then: “We want a man’s voice for this nocturne.” Ginevra was an Italian, and that says all.

At the end of a week her mother signed to her. Ginevra went; Elisa Piombo whispered: “I have persuaded your father to receive him.”

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!”

That day Ginevra had the joy of coming home on Luigi’s arm. The officer came out of hiding for the second time. Ginevra’s earnest appeals to the Duc de Feltre, then minister of war, had succeeded. Luigi’s name was replaced upon the roll of officers awaiting orders. This was the first great step. Warned by Ginevra of the difficulties he would meet with her father, the young man dared not express his fear of offending the old man. Courageous under adversity, brave on the battlefield, he trembled at the thought of entering Piombo’s salon. Ginevra felt him tremble; this emotion, the source of which lay in her, was to her another proof of love.

“How pale you are!” she said when they reached the house.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—”

Though Bartolomeo had been notified of the presentation, he would not advance to meet him, but remained seated in his arm-chair; the sternness of his brow was awful.

“Father,” said Ginevra, “I bring you a person you will no doubt be pleased to see,—a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.”

The baron rose, cast a sidelong glance at Luigi, and said in a sardonic tone: “Monsieur is not decorated.”

“I no longer wear the Legion of honor,” replied Luigi timidly.

Mortified by her father’s incivility, Ginevra dragged forward a chair. The officer’s answer seemed to satisfy the old servant of Napoleon. Madame Piombo, seeing her husband’s eyebrows resume their natural position, remarked by way of conversation: “Monsieur’s resemblance to a person we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is really surprising.”

“Nothing could be more natural,” replied the young man, on whom Piombo’s flaming eyes now rested. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” asked the old man.

“Yes.”

Bartolomeo rose, tottered, leaned against a chair and beckoned his wife. Elisa came. Then the two old people, each supporting the other, left the room, abandoning their daughter with a sort of horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned as white as marble, and stood gazing at the door through which her parents had disappeared. This departure and silence seemed so solemn that, for the first time, a feeling of fear entered her. She struck her hands together and said in a voice so shaken that only a lover could catch the words: “What misery in a word!”

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” asked Luigi.

“My father,” she replied, “never spoke to me of our deplorable history, and I was too young when we left Corsica to know anything.”

“Are we in vendetta?” asked Luigi, trembling.

“Yes.” The girl smiled sadly and bowed, then raised her head with pride: “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength to tread the path I am about to enter. But it involves a happiness that will last throughout our lives, will it not?”

Luigi answered with a smile and pressed her hand.

Ginevra understood that true love could despise vulgar protestations at such a moment. His calm and restrained expression foreshadowed their strength.

The destiny of the pair was decided. Ginevra foresaw a cruel struggle, but the idea of abandoning Luigi vanished. She dragged him from her father’s house with desperate energy, and did not leave him until she saw him reach the lodging Servin had found.

By the time she returned, Ginevra had attained the serenity of firm resolution; no sign of uneasiness showed. She turned on her parents, whom she found about to dine, with a gentle glance devoid of hardihood. She saw her mother had been weeping; the redness of those withered eyelids shook her heart, but she hid her emotion. No one touched the dinner. All three rose from table without a word.

When Ginevra placed herself between her parents in the gloomy salon, Piombo tried to speak but his voice failed; he tried to walk but had no strength. He rang the bell.

“Pietro,” he said at last, “light the fire; I am cold.”

Ginevra trembled and watched him anxiously. The struggle within him must have been horrible; his face was distorted. Ginevra knew the peril but did not flinch. Bartolomeo cast furtive glances at his daughter, as if fearing a character whose violence he had formed.

Between such natures all things were extreme. The certainty of impending change in father and daughter gave the baroness an expression of terror.

“Ginevra, you love the enemy of your family,” said Piombo at last, not daring to look at her.

“That is true,” she replied.

“You must choose between us. Our vendetta is a part of our being. Whoso does not share my vengeance is not a member of my family.”

“My choice is made,” replied Ginevra calmly.

His daughter’s tranquillity misled Bartolomeo.

“Oh! my dear child!” he cried, letting her see his eyes moistened with tears, the first and only tears he ever shed.

“I shall be his wife,” said Ginevra abruptly.

Bartolomeo seemed dazed but recovered his coolness and replied: “The marriage will not take place in my lifetime; I will never consent.”

Ginevra was silent.

“Ginevra,” continued the baron, “have you reflected that Luigi is the son of the man who killed your brother?”

“He was six when that crime was committed; he was therefore not guilty,” she replied.

“He is a Porta!” cried Bartolomeo.

“I have never shared that hatred,” said Ginevra eagerly. “You did not bring me up to think a Porta must be a monster. How could I know one you thought you had killed survived? Is it not natural you should yield your vendetta to my feelings?”

“A Porta!” repeated Piombo. “If his father had found you in your bed you would not be living now; he would have taken your life a hundred times.”

“It may be so,” she answered; “but his son has given me life, and more than life. To see Luigi is a happiness without which I cannot live. Luigi has revealed to me the world of sentiments. I may have seen faces more beautiful than his, but none has charmed me thus; I may have heard voices—no, never any so melodious! Luigi loves me; he will be my husband.”

“Never,” said Piombo. “I would rather see you in your coffin, Ginevra.”

The old Corsican rose and strode up and down, dropping sentences after pauses that betrayed his agitation.

“You think you can bend my will. Undeceive yourself. A Porta shall never be my son; that is my decree. Let there be no further question between us. I am Bartolomeo di Piombo; do you hear me, Ginevra?”

“Do you attach some mysterious meaning to those words?” she asked coldly.

“They mean that I have a dagger, and that I do not fear man’s justice. Corsicans explain themselves to God.”

“And I,” said the daughter rising, “am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta. You are a tyrant, my father,” she added after a terrifying pause.

Bartolomeo clenched his fists and struck them on the marble mantel.

“Ah! we are in Paris!” he muttered.

Then he was silent, crossed his arms, bowed his head, and said nothing more that evening.

After once declaring her will, Ginevra affected inconceivable coolness. She opened the piano and sang, played nocturnes and scherzos with grace and sentiment displaying perfect freedom, thus triumphing over her father, whose dark face showed no softening. The old man was cruelly hurt by this tacit insult; he gathered bitter fruits of the training he had given his daughter. Respect is a barrier that spares grief to parents and remorse to children.

The next day when Ginevra sought to leave at her usual hour she found the gates closed. She said nothing but managed to inform Luigi of her father’s severity. A chambermaid, who could neither read nor write, carried letters between the lovers. For five days they corresponded thus, thanks to the youth’s invention.

Father and daughter seldom spoke. Both nursed hatred in their hearts; they suffered proudly and in silence. Each tried to break their ties but without success. No gentle thought brightened Piombo’s face at sight of his Ginevra. The girl had something savage in her eye when she looked at him; reproach sat on that innocent brow; she gave herself to happy thoughts, yet at times remorse dulled her eyes. It was not difficult to believe she could never enjoy peacefully any happiness that caused her parents sorrow.

With Bartolomeo, as with his daughter, hesitations caused by native goodness yielded to pride and the rancor of their Corsican nature. They encouraged each other in anger and closed their eyes to the future, perhaps flattering themselves the other would yield.

At last, on Ginevra’s birthday, her mother, despairing at the growing estrangement, attempted to reconcile them by recalling family memories. They were all three sitting in Bartolomeo’s study. Ginevra guessed her mother’s aim by her hesitation and smiled sadly.

A servant announced two notaries with witnesses. Bartolomeo looked fixedly at these persons whose cold formal faces grated on souls so strained. The old man turned to his daughter uneasy. He saw on her face a triumphant smile that made him expect a shock, but affected to maintain deceitful indifference as he gazed at the notaries. They sat after his gesture.

“Monsieur is, no doubt, M. le Baron di Piombo?” began the oldest.

Bartolomeo bowed. The notary glanced at Ginevra with a sly look, took out his snuff-box, and slowly began: “Monsieur, I am Monsieur Roguin, your daughter’s notary, and we have come—my colleague and I—to fulfil the intentions of the law and—put an end to the divisions which—appear—to exist—between yourself and Mademoiselle, your daughter—on the subject—of—her—marriage with Monsieur Luigi Porta.”

This pedantic delivery probably amused Roguin. He paused and looked at Bartolomeo with the familiar air of a business lawyer. Notaries feign interest and wear a mask of benevolence that irritated Bartolomeo so he had to collect all his reason to prevent throwing Roguin through the window. Anger ran through his wrinkles causing the notary to think, “I’ve produced an effect.”

“But,” Roguin continued in honeyed tones, “monsieur le baron, on such occasions our duties are preceded by—efforts at—conciliation—Deign to listen. It is in evidence that Mademoiselle Ginevra di Piombo—attains this very day—the age at which the law allows a respectful summons before marriage despite parental non-consent. Now—it is usual in honorable families—to avoid publicizing differences—and not to injure the future of a young couple—for whenever a young lady has recourse to respectful summons she exhibits determination too marked to allow a father or mother,” here he turned to the baroness, “hoping she will follow their wishes—Paternal resistance being null—by reason of that fact—and also from law—it is customary—for every sensible man—after a final remonstrance—to leave her at liberty—”

Roguin stopped, seeing he might talk for two hours without answer; he felt a singular emotion at the man before him. An extraordinary change had taken place on Piombo’s face; his wrinkles contracted into narrow lines giving him an indescribable cruelty; he cast the look of a tiger. The baroness was mute and passive. Ginevra, calm and resolute, waited silently; she knew the notary’s voice more potent than hers and seemed determined to say nothing. When Roguin ceased, the scene was so terrifying the witnesses trembled. The notaries looked at each other, consulted, and walked to the window.

“Did you ever meet people born into the world like that?” Roguin asked his colleague.

“You can’t get anything out of him,” replied the younger. “In your place read the summons. That old fellow isn’t a comfortable person; he’s furious, and you’ll gain nothing arguing with him.”

Roguin then read a stamped paper containing the respectful summons; after which he asked Bartolomeo his answer.

“Are there laws in France which destroy paternal authority?” demanded the Corsican.

“Monsieur—” said Roguin.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?”

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?”

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?”

“Monsieur, permit me—”

There is nothing more horrible than the cool precise reasoning of notaries amid passionate scenes.

The forms about him seemed to Piombo escaped from hell; his repressed rage knew no bounds as the calm fluted “permit me.” Suddenly he sprang to a dagger hanging above the fireplace and rushed toward his daughter. The younger notary and a witness threw themselves before Ginevra; Piombo knocked them down, his face on fire and eyes casting flames. When Ginevra saw him approach she looked at him with triumph and slowly knelt.

“No, no! I cannot!” he cried, flinging away the weapon, which buried itself in the wainscot.

“Well, then! have mercy! have pity!” she said. “You hesitate to be my death and refuse me life! Oh! father, never have I loved you as I do at this moment; give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees: a daughter can humble herself before her father. Luigi, give me my Luigi, or I die!”

The violent excitement that suffocated her stopped her words; her convulsive movements showed she lay between life and death. Bartolomeo roughly pushed her away.

“Go,” he said. “The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. I have not the strength to curse you, but I cast you off; you have no father. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said deeply, pressing his heart. “Go, leave my house, unhappy girl,” he added after a pause. He took her by the arm to the gate and silently put her out.

“Luigi!” cried Ginevra entering her lover’s humble lodging, “my Luigi, we have no other fortune than our love.”

“Then am I richer than the kings of the earth!” he cried.

“My father and my mother have cast me off,” she said in deepest sadness.

“I will love you in place of them.”

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she cried with a gayety that had something dreadful.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after Ginevra was driven from her father’s house she went to Madame Servin for asylum and protection until the legal period before her marriage to Luigi.

Here began a hard apprenticeship to trouble. Madame Servin received her coldly, annoyed by the harm Ginevra’s affair had brought on her husband, and politely told her not to count on future help. Too proud to persist yet amazed at this selfishness, Ginevra took a room in the lodging-house nearest Luigi’s. The son of the Portas spent his days at his future wife’s feet; his youthful love and pure words dispersed the clouds from the banished daughter’s mind. The future he painted was so beautiful that she ended by smiling joyfully, though she never forgot her father’s severity.

One morning the lodging-house servant brought trunks and packages containing stuffs, linen, clothes, and many articles necessary for a young wife setting up a home. In this welcome provision she recognized her mother’s foresight, and, on examining the gifts, she found a purse in which the baroness had put Ginevra’s money, adding her own savings. The purse was accompanied by a letter in which her mother implored her to forego the fatal marriage if possible. It had cost untold difficulty to send these few things; she entreated her daughter not to think her hard if she were henceforth forced to abandon her to want, feared she could never again assist her, yet blessed and prayed for her happiness in this fatal marriage if she persisted, assuring her she should never cease to think of her darling. Tears had effaced some words.

“Oh, mother!” cried Ginevra, deeply moved.

She felt the impulse to rush home, breathe the blessed air of her father’s house, fling herself at his feet, to see her mother. She was springing forward when Luigi entered. At his sight her filial emotion vanished; her tears stopped, and she no longer had the strength to abandon that loving and unfortunate youth. To be the sole hope of a noble being, to love him and then abandon him—such sacrifice is treachery young hearts cannot commit. Ginevra had the generosity to bury her own grief silently.

The marriage day arrived. Ginevra had no friend with her. While she was dressing, Luigi fetched the witnesses needed for the certificate. They were worthy men: one, a cavalry sergeant, was under obligations to Luigi from the battlefield—obligations never obliterated from the heart of an honest man; the other, a master-mason, owned the house where the young couple had hired their apartment. Each witness brought a friend, and all four, with Luigi, came to escort the bride. Little accustomed to social functions and seeing only a matter of business, they were dressed plainly, and nothing denoted the usual joy of a marriage procession.

Ginevra herself was dressed simply as befitted her fortunes; yet her beauty was so noble that the witnesses’ customary greetings died on their lips. They bowed with respect; she returned the bow; they looked at her with admiration. This reserve cast a chill over the whole party. Joy never bursts forth freely except among equals. Thus chance made all dull and grave around the bridal pair; nothing outwardly reflected the happiness within their hearts.

The church and the mayor’s office being near, Luigi and Ginevra, followed by the four required witnesses, walked the short distance with a simplicity that stripped pomp from this greatest social event. They found a crowd of carriages in the mayor’s court-yard; in the great hall where civil marriages take place they found two other wedding-parties impatiently awaiting the mayor.

Ginevra sat beside Luigi at the end of a long bench; their witnesses remained standing for want of seats. Two brides, elaborately dressed in white with ribbons, laces, and pearls, crowned with orange-blossoms whose satiny petals nodded beneath their veils, were surrounded by joyous families and their mothers, looking up with content and timid eyes; all the faces reflected happiness. Fathers, witnesses, brothers, and sisters went and came like a happy swarm disporting in the sun. Each seemed impressed with the importance of this moment when the heart sits between two hopes—the wishes of the past, the promises of the future.

As she watched them, Ginevra’s heart swelled; she pressed Luigi’s arm and gave him a look. A tear rolled from the young Corsican’s eyes; never did he so well understand the joys his Ginevra was sacrificing to him. That precious tear caused her to forget all else but him—even the abandonment in which she sat. Love poured down its treasures of light upon their hearts; they saw nothing but themselves amid the joyous tumult; they were there alone in that crowd, as they were destined to be henceforth in life. Their witnesses, indifferent, conversed quietly on their own affairs.

Then they walked round the hall.

“How one loses time here,” said the mason, replacing a thick silver watch in his fob.

Luigi and Ginevra, sitting pressed to one another, seemed like one person. A poet would have admired their two heads inspired by the same sentiment, colored in the same tones, silent and saddened amid that humming happiness sparkling in diamonds and gay with flowers—a gaiety with something fleeting. The joy of those noisy, splendid groups was visible; that of Ginevra and Luigi was buried in their bosom. On one side the tumult of common pleasure, on the other the delicate silence of happy souls—earth and heaven!

But Ginevra was not wholly free from female weakness. Superstitious as an Italian, she saw an omen in this contrast, and felt a terror as invincible as her love.

Suddenly the office servant in the town livery opened a folding-door. Silence reigned, and his voice, like a dog’s yapping, called Monsieur Luigi da Porta and Mademoiselle Ginevra di Piombo. This caused some embarrassment. The celebrity of the bride’s name attracted attention, and spectators seemed to wonder that the wedding was not more sumptuous. Ginevra rose, took Luigi’s arm, and advanced firmly, followed by the witnesses. A murmur of surprise increased; a general whisper reminded her that all were wondering at the absence of her parents; her father’s wrath seemed present.

“Call in the families,” said the mayor to the clerk who read aloud the certificates.

“The father and mother protest,” replied the clerk, phlegmatically.

“On both sides?” inquired the mayor.

“The groom is an orphan.”

“Where are the witnesses?”

“Here,” said the clerk, pointing to the four men, who stood with arms folded like statues.

“But if the parents protest—” began the mayor.

“The respectful summons has been duly served,” replied the clerk, rising to lay before the mayor the papers annexed to the certificate.

This bureaucratic decision had something blighting; in a few words it contained the whole story. The hatreds of the Portas and the Piombos and their terrible passions were inscribed on this page of civil law as annals of a people are engraved on a tombstone. Ginevra trembled. Like the dove on the waters, having no resting place but the ark, she could take refuge only in Luigi’s eyes amid the cold waste around her.

The mayor assumed a stern, disapproving air, and his clerk looked up with malicious curiosity. No marriage was ever so little festal. Like other humans deprived of accessories, it became a simple act in itself, great only in thought.

After a few questions, to which the bride and bridegroom responded, and a few words mumbled by the mayor, and after signing the registers with their witnesses duly, Luigi and Ginevra were made one. Then the wedded pair walked back through two lines of joyous relations who did not belong to them, and whose only interest in their marriage was the delay caused to their own weddings by this gloomy bridal. When Ginevra finally found herself in the mayor’s court-yard, under the open sky, a sigh escaped her.

“Can a lifetime of devotion and love suffice to prove my gratitude for your courage and tenderness, my Ginevra?” said Luigi.

At these words, spoken with tears of joy, the bride forgot her sufferings; she had indeed suffered to appear before the public to obtain a happiness her parents refused to sanction.

“Why should others come between us?” she said with artless feeling that delighted Luigi.

A sense of accomplished happiness lightened the young pair’s step; they saw neither heaven nor earth nor houses; they flew, as it were, on wings to the church. When they reached a dark little chapel in one corner of the building and stood before a plain undecorated altar, an old priest married them. There, as in the mayor’s office, two other marriages were taking place, still pursuing them with pomp. The church, filled with friends and relations, echoed with the roll of carriages and the hum of beadles, sextons, and priests. Altars glowed with sacramental luxury; the wreaths of orange-flowers that crowned the Virgin were fresh. Flowers, incense, gleaming tapers, velvet cushions embroidered with gold were everywhere. When it came to hold above Luigi and Ginevra the symbol of eternal union—that yoke of satin, white, soft, brilliant, light for some, lead for most—the priest looked about in vain for the acolytes whose place it was to perform that joyous function. Two of the witnesses fulfilled it. The priest addressed a hasty homily on life’s perils and duties, throwing in an indirect reproach to Ginevra for her parents’ absence; then, after uniting them before God as the mayor had before the law, he left the married couple.

“God bless them!” said Vergniaud, the sergeant, to the mason at the church porch. “No two creatures were ever more fitted for one another. The parents are foolish. I don’t know a braver soldier than Colonel Luigi. If the whole army had behaved like him, ‘l’autre’ would be here still.”

This blessing of the old soldier, the only one bestowed on their marriage-day, shed a balm on Ginevra’s heart.

They parted with hearty handshakes; Luigi thanked his landlord.

“Adieu, ‘mon brave,’” he said to the sergeant. “I thank you.”

“I am now and ever at your service, colonel—soul, body, horses, and carriages; all that is mine is yours.”

“How he loves you!” said Ginevra.

Luigi hurried his bride to the house they were to occupy. Their modest apartment was soon reached; and there, when the door closed upon them, Luigi took his wife in his arms, exclaiming,—

“Oh, my Ginevra! for now you are mine, here is our true wedding. Here,” he added, “all things will smile upon us.”

Together they toured the three rooms of their lodging. The first served as salon and dining-room; on the right was a bedchamber; on the left a large study Luigi had arranged for his wife; in it she found easels, color-boxes, lay-figures, casts, pictures, portfolios—in short, an artist’s paraphernalia.

“So here I am to work!” she said with childlike happiness.

She looked long at the hangings and furniture, turning again and again to thank Luigi, for there was something approaching magnificence in the little retreat. A bookcase held her favorite books; a piano filled an angle. She sat on a divan, drew Luigi to her side, and said, caressing his hand,—

“You have good taste.”

“Those words make me happy,” he replied.

“But let me see all,” said Ginevra, to whom Luigi had made a mystery of the adornment.

They entered the nuptial chamber, fresh and white as a virgin.

“Oh! come away,” said Luigi, smiling.

“But I wish to see all.”

The imperious Ginevra looked at each piece of furniture with the minute care of an antiquary examining a coin; she touched the silken hangings and went over every article with the artless satisfaction of a bride inspecting her wedding treasures.

“We begin by ruining ourselves,” she said in a half-joyous, half-anxious tone.

“True! for all my back pay is there,” replied Luigi. “I have mortgaged it to a worthy fellow named Gigonnet.”

“Why did you do so?” she asked reproachfully, though inwardly pleased. “Do you believe I should be less happy in a garret? But,” she added, “it is all charming—and it is ours!”

Luigi looked at her with such enthusiasm that she lowered her eyes.

“Now let us see the rest,” she cried.

Above these three rooms, under the roof, were Luigi’s study, a kitchen, and a servant’s room. Ginevra was pleased with her little domain, though the view from the windows was limited by a high neighboring wall, and the court-yard, from which their light came, was gloomy. But the two lovers were so happy that hope adorned their future, and they chose to see nothing but what was charming in their hidden nest. In that vast house, lost in the immensity of Paris, they were like two pearls in their shell in the depths of the ocean; to others it might seem a prison; to them it was paradise.

The first few days of their union were given to love. The effort to work at once was too difficult; they could not resist their passion. Luigi lay for hours at his wife’s feet, admiring the color of her hair, the moulding of her forehead, the enchanting sockets of her eyes, the purity and whiteness of the two arches beneath which she turned her eyes slowly, expressing the happiness of satisfied love. Ginevra caressed Luigi’s hair, never weary of gazing at his “belta folgorante,” and the delicacy of his features. She was constantly charmed by the nobility of his manners, as she herself attracted him by her grace.

They played together like children with nothings—nothings that brought them back to their love—ceasing their play only to fall into reveries of far niente. An air sung by Ginevra reproduced to their souls the lights and shadows of their passion. Together, uniting their steps as they did their souls, they roamed the country, finding their love everywhere—in flowers, in the sky, in the glowing tints of sunset; they read it in the capricious vapors that met and struggled in the ether. Each day resembled none before; their love increased because it was true. They had tested each other in what seemed only a short time; instinctively they recognized that their souls were of a kind whose inexhaustible riches promised unceasing joys.

Theirs was love in all its artlessness, with interminable conversations, unfinished speeches, long silences, oriental reposes, and ardor. Luigi and Ginevra comprehended love. Love is like the ocean: seen superficially or in haste it seems monotonous to common souls, whereas some privileged beings can pass their lives admiring it and finding ceaselessly varying phenomena that enchant them.

Soon, however, prudence and foresight drew the young couple from their Eden; it was necessary to work to live. Ginevra, with a talent for imitating old paintings, took up copying and soon found many customers among picture-dealers. Luigi, whose talents had been restricted to strategy, sought long and actively for occupation, but it was hard for a young officer in Paris.

At last, weary of vain efforts and despairing at seeing the whole burden fall on Ginevra, he made use of his excellent handwriting. With persistency he went among lawyers and notaries asking for papers to copy. The frankness of his manners and his situation interested many; he soon obtained enough work to need assistants, and this employment became little by little a regular business. The profits of his office and the sale of Ginevra’s pictures gave the young couple a competence they were justly proud of, for it was the fruit of their industry.

This, to the busy pair, was the happiest period of their lives. Days flowed rapidly, filled with occupation and the joys of love. At night, after working all day, they met with delight in Ginevra’s studio. Music refreshed their weariness. No expression of regret or melancholy clouded the young wife’s features; she never complained. She appeared to Luigi with a smile and eyes beaming. Each cherished a ruling thought that would have made them take pleasure in still severer labor: Ginevra worked for Luigi, and Luigi for Ginevra.

Sometimes, in his absence, Ginevra imagined the perfect happiness they might have had if this life of love could have been lived in her parents’ presence; the thought of remorse dropped her into melancholy; mournful pictures passed like shadows across her imagination; she saw her old father alone, or her mother weeping in secret lest the inexorable Piombo perceive her tears. The two white, solemn heads rose before her, and she felt that never again should she see them except in memory. This thought pursued her like a presentiment.

She celebrated the anniversary of her marriage by giving her husband a portrait he had long desired—the portrait of his Ginevra, painted by herself. Never had the young artist done so remarkable a work. Aside from the resemblance, the glow of her beauty, the purity of her feelings, the happiness of love were there depicted by a sort of magic. This masterpiece of her art and joy was a votive offering to their wedded felicity.

Another year of ease and comfort went by. The history of their life may be given in three words: They were happy. No event happened to them of sufficient importance to be recorded.

Early in 1819 the picture-dealers asked Ginevra for something besides copies; competition made sales difficult. Madame Porta, recognizing she had neglected “genre” painting that might have made Ginevra known, tried portrait work but found many artists as needy as she. Luigi and Ginevra had saved a little, so urgent distress had not yet come.

Toward the end of winter Luigi worked without intermission. He too faced competitors. Pay for writing had fallen so low he could no longer hire help; he had to labor longer for the same income. Ginevra had completed several noteworthy paintings, yet dealers bought mostly for names; her canvases had little chance. Offers for her work were scarce.

Household danger loomed. Amid their happiness love overwhelmed them with its riches even as poverty rose like a skeleton within their joy. They hid anxieties: when Ginevra wanted to weep at Luigi’s worn face she redoubled her tenderness; Luigi, concealing his fears, expressed even greater affection. They heightened feeling to compensate; words, caresses, and hopes grew intense. They feared the future: what passion survives when threatened by death or want? Each felt the need to deceive the other, clinging to any hope.

One night Ginevra woke and missed Luigi. A faint light on the opposite courtyard wall showed him writing in his study; he now waited till she slept to go to his garret and work. Four o’clock struck. Pretending to sleep, she watched him return exhausted; toil and care had already begun to etch his face.

“It is for me he spends his nights,” she thought, weeping—then resolved to help. That day, using a letter of recommendation from Elie Magus, a dealer, she secured an order for coloring lithographs. She painted by day and colored engravings at night, sharing Luigi’s clandestine labor. They entered their bed only to deceive each other; both feigned sleep and rose as soon as the other seemed asleep.

One night Luigi, feverish with fatigue, opened his garret window for air and saw the reflection of Ginevra’s lamp on the opposite wall. He went down softly and surprised her coloring engravings.

“Oh, Ginevra!” he cried.

She rose convulsively and blushed.

“Could I sleep while you wore yourself out?” she said.

“But to me alone belongs the right to work in this way,” he answered.

“Could I be idle,” she asked with tears, “when every mouthful we eat costs a drop of your blood? I should die if I could not add my efforts to yours. All should be in common between us: pains and pleasures.”

“She is cold!” Luigi cried in despair. “Wrap your shawl closer, my Ginevra; the night is damp.”

They went to the window; she leaned on his breast, he held her, and together in silence they watched the sky brighten toward dawn. Clouds moved swiftly; the East grew luminous.

“See! It is an omen. We shall be happy,” Ginevra said.

“Yes, in heaven,” Luigi replied with a bitter smile. “Oh, Ginevra! you who deserved all the treasures on earth—”

“I have your heart,” she answered.

“Ah! I complain no more!” he said, drawing her close and kissing the face growing thin from hardship.

They spoke of night’s majesty and the hush that awoke Ginevra. She preferred to sit awake. Luigi praised the delicacy of her soul, and when dawn came he urged sleep.

Their courage was briefly rewarded; then an event usually crowning household happiness proved fatal. Ginevra bore a son, “as beautiful as the day.” Motherhood doubled her strength. Luigi borrowed to meet confinement expenses. At first the new burden felt like joy; they delighted in their child. It was their last happiness.

Like swimmers joining to breast a current, they struggled; sometimes apathy resembling death’s sleep overcame them. Soon they sold jewels. Poverty appeared—neither hideous nor raving, but plainly clothed and insistent; it broke their habits of comfort and dried the springs of pride. Then want in all its horror came, indifferent to feelings, trampling human sentiments.

Seven or eight months after little Bartolomeo’s birth, it was hard to recognize in the mother nursing her sickly babe the original of the earlier beautiful portrait — the sole remaining ornament. Without fire through a hard winter, Ginevra’s outlines were wasted; her cheeks grew white as porcelain, her eyes dulled as if life’s springs were drying. Watching her shrunken, discolored child, her only suffering was his misery; Luigi had no courage to smile on his son.

“I have wandered over Paris,” he said one day. “I know no one; can I ask strangers? Vergniaud, my old sergeant, is imprisoned in a conspiracy and has lent all he could. As for our landlord, he has not asked rent in over a year.”

“But we are not in want,” replied Ginevra gently, forcing calm.

“Every hour brings new difficulty,” Luigi answered, terror in his voice.

Another day Luigi sold Ginevra’s pictures, her portrait, and household items for a miserable sum, prolonging their agony. In this wretchedness Ginevra displayed the sublimity of her nature and resignation.

Stoically she bore misery’s blows; her strong spirit upheld her. She worked beside her dying son with unfaltering hand, performed household tasks with amazing activity, and sufficed for all. She was even gladdened to see Luigi’s surprised smile at the cleanliness she produced in their one poor room.

“Dear, I kept this bit of bread for you,” she said one evening when he returned, worn out.

“And you?”

“I? I have dined, dear Luigi; I want nothing more.”

The beseeching tenderness in her face urged him more than words to take the food she denied herself.

Luigi kissed her with one of those despairing kisses like friends mounting a scaffold. In such moments two beings see each other heart to heart. Realizing suddenly that his wife was starving, Luigi was seized by the fever consuming her. He shuddered and went out, pretending business called, for he would rather have drunk poison than secure life by eating the last morsel of bread in their home.

He wandered wildly about Paris amid flaunting luxury, hurrying past glittering windows. At last he resolved to sell himself as a substitute for military service, hoping the sacrifice would save Ginevra and that her father would take her home during his absence.

He went to an agent and found an old officer of the Imperial guard. “It’s two days since I have eaten,” he said in a slow, weak voice. “My wife is dying of hunger and has never complained; she will die smiling, I think. For God’s sake, comrade,” he added bitterly, “buy me in advance; I am robust; I am no longer in the service, and I—”

The officer gave Luigi a sum on account. The wretched man laughed convulsively as he grasped the gold and ran breathless home, crying, “Ginevra! Oh, my Ginevra!”

It was almost night when he reached their room. He entered softly, fearing to shock his wife, whom he had left so weak. The last rays of sun faded from Ginevra’s face as she sat sleeping in her chair, holding her child on her breast.

“Wake, my dear one,” he said, not seeing the infant, who at that moment shone with a strange pallor.

Hearing his voice, the poor mother opened her eyes, met Luigi’s look, and smiled; but Luigi cried out in horror; he scarcely recognized his wife, half mad. With a savage gesture he showed her the gold. Ginevra laughed mechanically, then cried in a dreadful voice, “The child, Luigi, he is cold!”

She looked at her son and swooned. The little Bartolomeo was dead. Luigi took his wife in his arms; after laying her on the bed he went out to seek help.

“Oh! my God!” he cried as he met his landlord on the stairs. “I have gold, and my child has died of hunger, and his mother is dying, too! Help me!”

He returned like one distraught to his wife, leaving the mason and neighbors to gather a few things for needs they had been too proud to reveal.

Luigi had cast his gold upon the floor and knelt by the bed. Ginevra, delirious, murmured, “Father! take care of my son, who bears your name.”

“Oh, my angel! be calm,” Luigi said, kissing her; “our good days are coming back.”

“My Luigi,” she said, looking at him with extraordinary attention, “listen. I feel that I am dying. My death is natural; I suffered too much; besides, such happiness as mine must be paid for. Yes, Luigi, be comforted. I have been so happy that if I lived again I would accept our fate. I am a bad mother; I regret you more than my child—My child!” she added hollowly.

Two tears escaped her dying eyes, and suddenly she pressed the little body she could not warm.

“Give my hair to my father, in memory of his Ginevra,” she said. “Tell him I have never blamed him.”

Her head fell on her husband’s arm.

“No, you cannot die!” cried Luigi. “The doctor is coming. We have food. Your father will take you home. Prosperity is here. Stay with us, angel!”

Her faithful heart, full of love, grew cold. Ginevra turned her eyes to him she loved, though she was conscious of nothing else. Confused images passed before her; memory of earth faded. She knew Luigi was there and clasped his hand tightly as if to hold herself from some precipice.

“Dear,” she said at last, “you are cold; I will warm you.”

She tried to put his hand upon her heart, and died.

Two doctors, a priest, and neighbors came into the room, bringing what might save the poor couple and calm despair. They made some noise entering; afterwards an awful silence filled the room.

While that scene took place, Bartolomeo Piombo and his wife sat in their antique chairs at the corners of a vast fireplace in their salon. A friend had just left; the chair he had occupied remained between the two Corsicans. The clock told midnight. The old couple had lost the ability to sleep; they sat silent, like persons in their dotage, gazing at things they did not see. Their deserted salon, filled with memories, was feebly lit by a single expiring lamp. Without the hearth’s spark they might soon be in total darkness.

Piombo cast glances at the empty chair—glances full of remorse—for it was Ginevra’s seat. Elisa Piombo watched expressions cross her husband’s pallid face; tonight they seemed threatening and melancholy, making his soul incomprehensible to her.

Would Bartolomeo yield at last to the memories that chair awakened? Had he been struck to see a stranger occupy it, used for the first time since his daughter left? Had the hour of his mercy struck—the hour Elisa had prayed and waited for?

These thoughts shook the mother. For an instant her husband’s countenance became so terrible she trembled at having spoken of Ginevra. The wintry night blew snow sharply against the blinds; the old woman dropped her head to hide tears. Suddenly a sigh burst from the old man; he seemed crushed. She risked speaking—for the second time in three years—of his daughter.

“Ginevra may be cold,” she said softly.

Piombo quivered.

“She may be hungry,” she continued.

The old man dropped a tear.

“Perhaps she has a child and cannot suckle it; her milk is dried up!” said the mother in despair.

“Let her come! let her come to me!” cried Piombo. “Oh! my precious child, thou hast conquered me.”

The mother rose as if to fetch her daughter. At that instant the door opened noisily, and a man, his face no longer human, stood before them.

“Dead! Our two families were doomed to exterminate each other. Here is all that remains of her,” he said, laying Ginevra’s long black hair upon the table.

The old people shook as if struck by lightning.

Luigi no longer stood before them.

“He has spared me a shot, for he is dead,” Bartolomeo slowly said, gazing at the ground.
