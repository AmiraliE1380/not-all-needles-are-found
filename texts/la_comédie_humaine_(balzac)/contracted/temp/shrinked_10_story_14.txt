DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini.

Madame, recalling the pleasure of your conversation to a traveler in Milan, I place this work at your feet, hoping you will shelter it with your name as you once protected the tales of a beloved writer. You have a daughter, Eugénie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a happy childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing of the Eugénie in these pages. Though Frenchmen are often called fickle, I am Italian in my constancy and memory. Writing “Eugénie” recalls the laughter and warmth of our time in Milan; I now regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a sumptuous blue-velvet boudoir on rue Neuve-des-Mathurins two young women wept by the fire. The room belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who bought social standing by marriage but lacked genuine aristocratic ease. Madame du Tillet clasped her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand as they exchanged confidences. Raised by a strict, pious mother and married without youthful freedoms, they entered matrimony naive about life and love. Schooled only in religion and needlework, they retained innocence but lacked experience to handle adult passions; marriage, once imagined as escape, revealed unforeseen realities.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, distraught by marital troubles, poured out her despair over a husband indifferent to her inner life. Madame du Tillet, equally helpless, described her own cold, listless domesticity. Each confessed a craving for suppressed passion and excitement; each sought consolation and encouragement in the other.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, had married hoping to recreate an idealized love. The duties and constraints of his class, however, cooled the relationship. Though kind and attentive, he could not supply the romantic intensity his wife desired. Monotony awakened in her a longing for deeper emotional fulfilment and tempted thoughts of love beyond marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an advancing literary figure, entered society with talent, audacity, and caustic wit. His unconventional manner—attractive and unsettling—caught Madame de Vandenesse’s attention at Madame de Montcornet’s salon; their early exchanges hinted at an unspoken attraction. Nathan, juggling a fraught career and private troubles, saw her as a potential muse, though social position and decorum threatened any open liaison.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a gifted actress and Nathan’s long-term companion, balanced affection with practicality: she sustained his spirits and career while depending on him for protection and status. As Nathan’s admiration for Madame de Vandenesse grew, Florine felt the shift and grew insecure and jealous.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Nathan and Madame de Vandenesse bonded over art and literature. Their encounters grew intimate, bringing exhilaration and peril. She struggled with guilt and desire, torn between loyalty to her husband and the lure of a passionate life, aware of the social consequences of yielding.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Crushed by career setbacks and despair, Nathan contemplated suicide. A chance meeting with Madame de Vandenesse revived his will to live; seeing his desperation, she resolved to protect and encourage him, countering both his darkest impulses and the self-destructive streak in his ambitions.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

After his crisis, Madame de Vandenesse became more involved, balancing protection with her own feelings. Their relationship became both strength and turmoil as secrecy and social risk complicated matters. As Nathan’s fortunes improved, he found himself torn between Florine’s practical devotion and the countess’s inspiring influence; Florine’s jealousy intensified the strain.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix, sensing his wife’s attachment to Nathan, moved to preserve their marriage. Determined to discover the truth and shield her from scandal, he acted to defend her honor and their union. The characters remain intertwined by love, ambition, loyalty, and expectation, leading to a dramatic clash between desire and duty.
