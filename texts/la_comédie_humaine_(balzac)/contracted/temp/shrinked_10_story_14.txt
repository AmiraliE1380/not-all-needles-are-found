**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work expresses my gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. I often reflect on our cherished moments in Milan and hope this book serves as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse are distressed. Their father, Comte de Granville, and mother, Comtesse de Granville, raised them under strict religious and social constraints, leading to marriages devoid of true understanding of love or life. The sisters share a deep bond, having grown up in an environment that stifled their emotions and desires.

Madame du Tillet, married to wealthy banker Ferdinand du Tillet, feels trapped in a loveless marriage, while Madame de Vandenesse enjoys a more affectionate relationship with her husband, Comte Felix de Vandenesse. However, both women are ultimately dissatisfied, longing for the freedom and passion they have been denied.

Their childhood was marked by strict religious education, leaving them innocent yet naive. They were married off without truly knowing themselves or the world, resulting in a sense of loss and yearning for something more fulfilling.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her unhappiness to her sister, revealing the emptiness of her luxurious life. She feels trapped by societal expectations and her husband's political ambitions. Madame du Tillet, despite her own struggles, tries to comfort her sister, but both feel the weight of their circumstances.

The sisters reflect on their past and the constraints imposed by their upbringing. They lament their innocence and the lack of genuine love in their marriages. Madame du Tillet expresses a desire to help her sister but feels powerless against societal norms.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix de Vandenesse marries Madame de Vandenesse, hoping to provide her with a loving home. He is aware of her oppressive upbringing and seeks to nurture her spirit. Their early marriage is filled with happiness, but as time passes, Madame de Vandenesse begins to feel monotony in her life.

She becomes increasingly aware of her desires and the limitations of her marriage. The count, while kind, fails to recognize the changes in his wife's heart. As she seeks fulfillment, she finds herself drawn to the idea of romantic love, complicating her feelings for her husband.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, enters the social scene, capturing Madame de Vandenesse's attention. Their initial interactions are filled with tension and unspoken attraction. Nathan, aware of his growing feelings for the countess, struggles with his ambitions and career demands.

As he navigates his life, Nathan's relationship with Florine, an actress, becomes strained. He is torn between his love for Madame de Vandenesse and his obligations to Florine, who has supported him throughout his struggles.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine, a talented actress, has built her career with Nathan's support. Their relationship is complicated; she relies on him for success while feeling possessive. Despite their bond, Nathan's ambitions pull him away, leading to tension and jealousy.

Florine's life is chaotic, and she grapples with her feelings for Nathan as he becomes increasingly involved with Madame de Vandenesse. Their relationship is tested as Nathan's ambitions grow, and Florine fears losing him to the countess.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's relationship deepens, filled with passion and longing. The countess is captivated by Nathan's artistic spirit, while he is drawn to her innocence and beauty. Their love blossoms in secret, but societal pressures loom large.

As they navigate their feelings, both experience joy and anxiety. The countess is torn between her duty to her husband and her desire for Nathan, while he grapples with the implications of their love on his career and reputation.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's struggles with debt and societal expectations lead him to contemplate suicide, believing it to be his only escape. The countess, unaware of his despair, continues to nurture her feelings for him.

When she learns of his plight, she rushes to save him, demonstrating her love and commitment. Their reunion is fraught with emotion as they confront the reality of their situation and the sacrifices they must make for each other.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess's determination to save Nathan leads her to seek financial assistance from her family. As she navigates her love for Nathan, she faces societal judgment and personal turmoil.

Despite her efforts, Nathan's situation remains precarious, and the pressures of their lives threaten to pull them apart. The countess's love for Nathan is tested as she grapples with the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
In the aftermath of their struggles, the countess and Nathan's relationship faces new challenges. The count, aware of the bond between his wife and Nathan, takes steps to protect his family's honor while seeking to understand their connection.

As the countess confronts her feelings for Nathan, she must navigate her responsibilities to her husband and societal expectations. The tension between love and duty reaches a climax, forcing the characters to make difficult choices that will shape their futures.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, illustrating the interconnectedness of his literary universe. Each character's journey reflects the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
