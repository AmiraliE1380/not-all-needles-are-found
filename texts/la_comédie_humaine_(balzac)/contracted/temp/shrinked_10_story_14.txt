To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini. Remembering the pleasure of our conversation in Milan, I dedicate this work to you and hope you will shelter it with your name as you once protected a beloved writer’s tales. You have a daughter, Eugenie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a happy childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing of the Eugenie in these pages. Though Frenchmen are called fickle, I am Italian in constancy and memory; writing “Eugenie” revives the laughter and warmth of our time in Milan. Your devoted servant, De Balzac.

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES
In a sumptuous blue-velvet boudoir on rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, two young women—Madame du Tillet and her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse—weep by the fire. The room belongs to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who has bought social standing but lacks aristocratic ease. Both sisters, raised by a strict, pious mother and married without youthful freedoms, are naive about life and love, repressed and unprepared for adult passions.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS
They confide in one another: Madame de Vandenesse laments a husband indifferent to her inner life, while Madame du Tillet describes a cold, listless domesticity. Each craves passion and excitement suppressed by society and duty, seeking solace and encouragement from the other.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN
Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, married hoping to recreate idealized love, but class duties and restraint cool their relationship; his kindness cannot supply the romantic intensity his wife desires, awakening longings for love beyond marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN
Raoul Nathan, an audacious literary figure with caustic wit, enters society and attracts Madame de Vandenesse at Madame de Montcornet’s salon. His talent and unconventional manner intrigue her, though social position and decorum make any open liaison dangerous.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE
Florine, Nathan’s long-term companion and a gifted actress, balances affection with practicality—supporting his career while depending on him for protection and status—and grows insecure as his admiration for the countess deepens.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE
Nathan and Madame de Vandenesse bond over art and literature; their encounters grow intimate and perilous, leaving her torn between marital loyalty and the lure of passionate fulfillment despite social consequences.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE
Crushed by setbacks and despair, Nathan contemplates suicide; a chance meeting with Madame de Vandenesse revives his will, and she resolves to protect and encourage him, countering his self-destructive impulses.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST
After his crisis she becomes more involved, offering strength yet inviting turmoil; secrecy, social risk, and Florine’s jealousy complicate the fragile relationship as Nathan’s fortunes and loyalties shift.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH
Félix, sensing his wife’s attachment to Nathan, seeks to preserve their marriage, investigate the truth, and shield her from scandal. Ambitions, loyalties, and desires collide in a dramatic clash between passion and duty.
