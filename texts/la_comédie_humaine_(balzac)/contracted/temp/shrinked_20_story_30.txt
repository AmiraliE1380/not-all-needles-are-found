Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, highlighting the story's melancholy and the necessity of confronting sorrows for someone cherished like Anna.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut stands in a small square in Lower Provins, admiring Pierrette Lorrain's renovated house. He notices a calico curtain fluttering in an attic window and sings a Breton wedding song. When Pierrette appears, they greet each other, and Brigaut, back from Paris, expresses his desire to settle nearby. However, Pierrette urges him to flee when a noise alarms her.

Alone, Pierrette reflects on her life, marked by three years of sorrow. Memories of her childhood starkly contrast with her current misery. After her father, Major Lorrain, died, her mother faced financial struggles, inheriting little and marrying a physician, which led to further ruin. Pierrette's grandparents, unable to support her, sent her to live with the Rogrons in Provins, who became her guardians after receiving a letter from them. This unexpected turn shaped her future, making her dependent on her new guardians.

The narrative explores themes of social class, familial duty, and the impact of financial struggles on relationships. Pierrette's delicate health and emotional turmoil reflect broader societal issues, particularly for women in precarious situations. The story foreshadows the challenges she will face with the self-interested Rogrons, poignantly examining the intersection of personal aspirations and societal constraints in her life.

Pere Rogron, an unattractive and selfish innkeeper in Provins, lives with his equally unappealing wife on an annual income of about two thousand francs from their land and inn. After selling the inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from Pierrette’s grandfather, old Auffray. The Rogrons have two poorly raised children, leading to their physical and emotional decline, as Rogron provides minimal support, expecting them to fend for themselves.

Their daughter, Sylvie, becomes a forewoman in Paris, while her brother, Jerome-Denis, follows a similar path. Together, they save enough to buy a retail establishment called the “Family Sister.” Despite their success, both siblings are unattractive and socially inept, earning a reputation for being miserly.

Sylvie effectively manages the business, while Jerome struggles with broader trade aspects. Their lives revolve around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the outside world. They dream of returning to Provins to improve their inherited property, but their aspirations are tinged with nostalgia.

When the Lorrains seek to place their orphaned cousin Pierrette in their care, the siblings debate the implications. Viewing Pierrette as a threat to their inheritance, they ultimately refuse the request, prioritizing their own interests. Four years later, the Rogrons return to Provins, where their actions will significantly impact Pierrette’s life, highlighting themes of selfishness and familial duty.

A provincial shopkeeper returning from Paris brings new ideas but soon succumbs to provincial life, transitioning from shopkeeper to bourgeois. This shift feels like an illness as they struggle to adapt to a quieter lifestyle. With their savings, they indulge in long-held desires through travel, politics, or hobbies like hunting and fishing. The Rogrons exemplify this as they remodel their old house into a fashionable one, aiming to emulate the wealthier bourgeois of Provins.

Their renovations reflect their aspirations, with the contractor consulting them on every detail, emphasizing items once owned by local notables. The Rogrons' first year back in Provins is consumed by discussions about their home and attempts to integrate into local society, which they had previously avoided. They reconnect with former acquaintances, including the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenees, who dominate the social scene.

Madame Tiphaine, the town's belle, is determined to elevate her husband, Monsieur Tiphaine, to a deputy position, using her charm to gain influence. She hosts gatherings that attract the local bourgeoisie, while the Rogrons, eager to fit in, struggle with their lack of social grace. Sylvie's attempts at card games reveal her poor manners, and her brother, Rogron, is socially awkward, leading to their exclusion from elite circles.

Despite their efforts, the Rogrons are perceived as vulgar and unrefined, resulting in social ostracism. Their lavish dinners, intended to showcase their wealth, are met with indifference, leaving them isolated. Sylvie's attempts to maintain their social standing fail, prompting her to abandon their extravagant lifestyle in favor of frugality.

As time passes, the Rogrons grow increasingly lonely, reminiscing about their past business and feeling the weight of isolation. They engage in mundane routines, with Rogron obsessively checking his barometer and seeking gossip from townsfolk. Their social ambitions crumble, leaving them in ennui, longing for the excitement of their former lives.

In a bid to revitalize their household, Sylvie invites her cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping to regain social standing. However, their plans are complicated by Pierrette's unfortunate circumstances and the Rogrons' past misdeeds regarding family property. Their attempts to ingratiate themselves with the upper class backfire, leading to further alienation. The Rogrons' growing resentment towards the local elite culminates in their alignment with marginalized figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, who seek to exploit the Rogrons' wealth and ignorance for their own political ambitions.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette Lorrain felt burdened by charity until she discovered wealthy relatives. Her childhood friend Brigaut, an apprentice cabinet-maker, offered her money for her journey to Paris, which she accepted, highlighting their deep bond.

In October 1824, at eleven years old, Pierrette left Nantes, entrusted to a diligence conductor who took her to her cousins, Mademoiselle Sylvie and Rogron. Upon arrival, she was met with indifference, treated more like a package than a family member. Despite her modest attire, she radiated innocence and charm, but her cousins were more concerned with the costs of her arrival than her well-being.

Sylvie and Rogron's initial surprise at Pierrette's presence quickly turned to criticism of her clothing, as they planned an expensive wardrobe for her. Having known love and freedom in Brittany, Pierrette felt a stark contrast in her new home, where her cousins' harshness stifled her spirit.

As she adjusted to her new life, Pierrette faced constant scrutiny from her cousins, who prioritized their financial investment in her clothing over her happiness. Her innocent nature clashed with their coldness, leading to feelings of isolation.

Despite attempts to dress her like a little lady, Pierrette's playful spirit often led to wear and tear on her clothes, resulting in further criticism. The Rogrons' harshness transformed their relationship from curiosity to tyranny, imposing rigid expectations.

The arrival of Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, who sought to manipulate the Rogrons, complicated Pierrette's situation, deepening their resentment and neglect towards her. As her life in Provins progressed, she became increasingly confined and controlled, losing the joy and freedom she once enjoyed, trapped in a cycle of fear and repression.

The narrative centers on Pierrette, a young girl living with her wealthy cousins, the Rogrons. During a family gathering, she is coldly dismissed by her cousin Sylvie, which deeply wounds her. Pierrette longs for the affection of her late grandmother, contrasting it with the indifference of her new guardians. Sylvie and her husband, Vinet, trivialize Pierrette's feelings, asserting that while her grandmother took her money, they will leave her theirs.

As Pierrette adjusts to her new life, she faces harsh treatment from the Rogrons, who see her as a burden. They impose strict educational expectations, leading to feelings of inadequacy and despair. Her efforts to please them are met with criticism, resulting in increasing isolation and suffering, both physically and emotionally.

Despite having a writing master, Pierrette struggles academically and is often punished for her perceived failures. The Rogrons' lack of kindness exacerbates her difficulties, making her feel like a servant in their home. The only warmth comes from Adele, the servant, but this comfort is stripped away when Sylvie insists that Pierrette take on household duties herself.

Over time, Pierrette's health declines under the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household. The relentless criticism and absence of affection contribute to her deterioration. The narrative starkly contrasts Pierrette's innocent nature with her relatives' coldness, highlighting the harsh realities faced by those dependent on wealthier family members.

The story also delves into the political dynamics of the time, as the Rogrons become involved in local politics through their connections with Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, leading to the establishment of a local newspaper.

As Pierrette approaches her first communion, she finds solace in her faith, which offers hope amid her suffering. However, the Rogrons remain indifferent to her spiritual growth, viewing her as an obstacle to their ambitions. The arrival of Bathilde de Chargeboeuf complicates matters, as she becomes a rival for Rogron's affections and a source of jealousy for Sylvie. Ultimately, the narrative presents a bleak picture of Pierrette's life, marked by neglect and cruelty from her relatives, who prioritize their ambitions over her well-being.

To understand the turmoil in the Rogron family after Jacques Brigaut's arrival, we must first consider his journey to Provins. Troubled by Pierrette's distress, Brigaut fled her home, shaken by her transformation from a lively child to a sickly shadow. He climbed to a vantage point to observe her house, consumed with worry and realizing his deep feelings for her.

At sixteen, Brigaut was determined to improve himself for Pierrette's sake. After a brief stint in Paris, he returned to Provins and became a journeyman with Monsieur Frappier, the town's best cabinet-maker. His dedication to his trade and desire to protect Pierrette drove him to work hard, earning a modest living while keeping a watchful eye on her.

As Brigaut settled into his new life, he learned about the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and the oppressive nature of her living situation. He often saw Pierrette in the market, where she appeared burdened and unhappy. Despite his longing to approach her, he sensed her distress and chose to keep his distance.

Meanwhile, Pierrette faced her cousin Sylvie's jealousy, which intensified after Brigaut's arrival. Sylvie, an old maid fixated on marrying Colonel Gouraud, grew envious of Pierrette, perceiving a potential romantic connection between her and the colonel. Sylvie's insecurities about aging led her to consult a doctor regarding marriage risks, complicating her feelings further.

The dynamics within the Rogron household were tense. Sylvie's jealousy manifested in her treatment of Pierrette, whom she accused of having a secret admirer. Pierrette, innocent and unaware of the implications, remained silent, fearing repercussions. Sylvie's relentless scrutiny exacerbated Pierrette's fragile health.

As the story unfolds, Sylvie's jealousy becomes increasingly irrational, leading her to plot against Pierrette, convinced that her cousin threatens her chances with the colonel. The tension culminates in a breakfast scene where accusations and distress reach a breaking point, leaving Pierrette feeling isolated and ill.

Ultimately, the narrative explores themes of jealousy, innocence, and complex familial relationships, contrasting Brigaut's love for Pierrette with Sylvie's bitterness, highlighting the destructive nature of envy and its impact on those caught in its web.

Colonel Gouraud ceased delivering the “Constitutionnel” to the Rogrons for a month, opting for brief visits instead. Sylvie, eager for his attention, dressed attractively and awaited his arrival with her reluctant brother, Rogron. Upon entering, Gouraud complimented Sylvie, but she feigned concern for her cousin's illness to avoid going out.

Gouraud felt uneasy about his partnership with Vinet, who seemed to dominate their political efforts, sidelining him. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which made him wary of his own standing. To gauge Rogron's feelings, Gouraud mentioned his interest in Bathilde, provoking jealousy and hostility from Rogron.

As Gouraud conversed with Sylvie, he suspected her intentions were influenced by Vinet, who had suggested he marry Pierrette instead. Sylvie aimed to control Pierrette and was suspicious of any connection between her cousin and the colonel. Despite Gouraud's attempts at affection, Sylvie remained calculating, wanting to test his loyalty.

Sylvie's jealousy intensified as she noticed Pierrette's growing beauty and began plotting against her cousin. She sent Pierrette to dress nicely for dinner, suspecting the colonel's interest in her. Meanwhile, Bathilde and her mother arrived, with Bathilde intent on winning Rogron's affection, encouraged by Vinet to view marriage as a means to gain power.

Tensions rose during the evening's card game, with Sylvie's jealousy flaring when she believed Pierrette had helped Gouraud win. After a confrontation, Pierrette fled, injuring her head. Sylvie, indifferent to her injury, focused on her frustrations with the colonel. The next day, Sylvie cruelly attributed Pierrette's injury to divine punishment for disobedience, revealing her harshness and control over her cousin. The dynamics of power, jealousy, and manipulation highlighted the domestic tyranny Sylvie exerted over Pierrette.

Pierrette endured a week of torment from her cousin Sylvie, suffering both physically and emotionally while remaining silent about Brigaut's arrival for fear of his safety. Despite her pain, thoughts of Brigaut provided solace, akin to an exile longing for home.

Brigaut, aware of Pierrette's struggles, devised a plan to communicate by having her lower a letter from her window at midnight. Their mutual longing allowed them to connect despite their separation. One day in the market, Brigaut slipped a note to Pierrette, expressing his love and urging her to escape her oppressive situation and return to Brittany.

Moved by Brigaut's words, Pierrette wrote back, revealing her suffering and desire for freedom. After exchanging letters, she felt renewed hope, believing Brigaut would help her escape her misery.

However, Sylvie grew suspicious of Pierrette's newfound happiness and began to monitor her closely. Despite her illness, Pierrette clung to the hope of Brigaut's nightly visits. When she fainted during a gathering, the colonel, who had feelings for her, expressed concern, but Sylvie dismissed it.

As tensions escalated, Sylvie discovered Pierrette's secret correspondence and confronted her. In a violent struggle, Sylvie attempted to seize Pierrette's letter, leading to a brutal confrontation that left Pierrette injured. Just as Sylvie's cruelty peaked, Pierrette's grandmother arrived, alerted by Brigaut. The old woman intervened, taking Pierrette away from the oppressive environment.

Filled with anguish upon seeing her granddaughter's condition, Pierrette's grandmother vowed to seek medical help and take action against the Rogrons. Meanwhile, Brigaut sought a doctor for Pierrette's recovery. As she recuperated, her grandmother provided comfort and support, promising a better life for them both, underscoring themes of love, resilience, and the quest for freedom from oppression.

Monsieur Martener visited Monsieur Tiphaine to address Pierrette's mistreatment by the Rogrons, which had caused her severe illness. The feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions in Provins intensified as the Rogrons spread damaging rumors about the Tiphaine family. Tiphaine viewed Pierrette's situation as a chance to politically undermine the Rogrons.

Tiphaine consulted notary Auffray and Martener to strategize on legally removing Pierrette from the Rogrons' guardianship. They planned a Family Council to appoint Auffray as her guardian, aiming for her release. News of Pierrette's distress incited public outrage in Provins.

As tensions escalated, Vinet, the Rogrons' lawyer, learned of potential criminal charges against them. He manipulated the Rogrons into agreeing to a marriage contract with Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf to gain public favor and distract from the allegations. Court proceedings commenced, with the Tiphaines and their supporters rallying around Pierrette, while the Rogrons defended their actions.

Pierrette was moved to a safer environment under Auffray's care, where doctors confirmed her injuries resulted from the Rogrons' abuse. A court ruling prevented her return to their home, and despite Vinet's attempts to discredit her, the Family Council decided to remove Rogron as her guardian.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette's health deteriorated. She expressed love for her grandmother and friend Brigaut, forgiving her cousins. Tragically, she passed away, leading to a public funeral that the Rogrons avoided due to backlash.

Brigaut, devastated, vowed to seek justice. The legal proceedings against the Rogrons collapsed with Pierrette's death, while Vinet exploited the situation for political gain, critiquing societal indifference and moral decay. In the aftermath, Vinet gained political power, and Brigaut became a soldier, haunted by memories of Pierrette.
