To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron,—Your encouragement of my vast “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” has been a sustaining force, and I associate you with this portion of the work. Your methodical, documentary scholarship and your sympathetic interest in civilizations beyond our own have often buoyed my ardor during long nights of labor. May this token of respect reach you at Döbling from one of your sincere admirers and friends.

DE BALZAC.

There stands, in a small prefectural town of France, a corner house whose name must be disguised: the Hotel d’Esgrignon. The title, half jest, half earnest after years, refers to the residence of the old Marquis—Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol, Marquis d’Esgrignon. The novelist will disguise names as conventional devices do, yet truth inevitably peeps through.

The Carol family traced a long, unbroken line to the Northmen who established the feudal order in Gaul. Proud provincial nobles, their marquisate had originally meant real military and administrative responsibility; across generations they remained lords paramount of their March, neglected by court but revered by the countryside. For thirteen centuries their daughters had married without dowries or entered convents; younger sons became captains or bishops; a cadet rose to be a duke and peer but left no issue. The old Marquis of the elder branch refused any dukedom: he clung to his marquisate “as His Majesty holds the realm of France.”

When the Revolution came, the d’Esgrignons’ old blood made them neither flighty nor cowardly. The Marquis would not emigrate; he was answerable for his March. His standing in the countryside saved him from the guillotine, though he was forced into hiding and his lands were dishonored and sold by the District. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, his half-sister, saved some of the property through a pre-succession claim, and the steward Chesnel, faithful through turmoil, bought certain pieces in his own name that he held for the family.

In October 1800 Chesnel brought the Marquis back to the ruined castle. The patron, now Citizen Carol, wandered the dismantled courtyard and looked up at the sky as if asking the reason for the social upheaval that had torn the house down. He decided he could not yet live in the castle until pacification was complete; “they” would not permit him to display his scutcheon. Returning to town—where the old aristocratic house had been replaced by factories—Chesnel invested the Marquis’ last louis in buying the remaining ancestral building, a modest old presidial once in the family, and christened it the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

By 1800 a few emigres returned. Among them were the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, ruined; the Baron died in the Marquis’ house, and the Marquis married the daughter to continue his line. She died in childbirth—one of those blows that reverberate through years and change a man. The Marquis, fifty-three but aged by grief and adversity, watched his dead wife with dry eyes, kissed her forehead, broke his watch at eleven—an hour linked to ancestral deaths—and prayed that the hour might not again be fatal to his house. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, twenty-seven and beautiful, shared his devotion to the family. She lived in virtue and duty; she embodied a feudal ideal in the modern age.

Maitre Chesnel, the steward who had saved much for the family, erred once by carrying a suitor’s proposal—an ex-contractor, Monsieur du Croisier—for Mlle. Armande. The Marquis and his sister were indignant; thereafter, though gratitude endured, their relations cooled. Chesnel, raised almost to the family rank by affection and loyalty, felt wounded; he had known high friendship and resented that the Marquis’ gratitude implied an obligation rather than equality. The steward’s subsequent aversion to du Croisier—and du Croisier’s smoldering, vengeful hatred—seeded the long provincial enmity that would shape much of the town’s social life.

Mlle. Armande—tawny-gold hair, dreamy emerald eyes, delicate features, and a gracious, saintly bearing—became, in the memory of Emile Blondet, an emblem of all he loved and revered. As a child he would watch her at the Parade, enthralled by a presence that seemed to combine medieval dignity and feminine grace. Even after youthful fancies faded, the image of Mlle. d’Esgrignon persisted in his imagination as a sacred type: Agnes Sorel, Marie Touchet, the spirit of feudal nobility. Blondet’s recollection shows how profoundly such a character might inspire awe and devotion; Mlle. Armande, an exemplar of pure goodness, also illustrates how innocence and devotion without intelligence may cause mischief.

During 1804–1805 many emigres returned. Some accepted Napoleon’s rule and prospered; they went to Paris. Others remained faithful to the proscribed noblesse—the La Roche-Guyons, Nouastres, Verneuils, Casterans, Troisvilles—families for whom pedigree and age of descent mattered above wealth. The Marquis d’Esgrignon became their acknowledged head; his house became their cenacle. In the Hotel d’Esgrignon one continued to call the Emperor “M. de Bonaparte” and spoke of Louis XVIII. as the true king; to them Prefecture was Intendance and Department the Province. Their salon gained the nickname “The Collection of Antiquities,” a jibe from those excluded by birth and the new order, but it also reflected a genuine revival of aristocratic forms.

The Marquis’s personal honor and conduct in adversity won him respect across the town; he was looked up to as a moral arbiter and conciliator. But there arose a Liberal party spearheaded locally by the resentful du Croisier. After his rebuffs, du Croisier married an elderly maid who had at first refused him; that marriage aggravated provincial factionalism. Du Croisier’s salon, full of younger, more energetic men, stood in opposition to the d’Esgrignon salon: where the latter remained attached to ancestral titles and manners, the former represented manufacturing interests and a rising capitalist class. Competition for influence and for seats in departmental elections hardened into personal hatred.

Local politics were no abstract contest but a matter of personal stakes. In a provincial town, reputation and party identity fused: political adversaries became private enemies. The stakes were social access, appointments, and esteem. Du Croisier, in seeking to consolidate support among magistrates and capitalists, aligned with a center-left current that made his local party influential but never able to capture the department’s preferences, where Royalist votes, steered by the Marquis and his allies, kept him out. Each electoral defeat deepened his bitterness. The “Collection of Antiquities,” for its part, was at once a fortress of decayed aristocratic dignity and a court whose etiquette and assumptions men of the new order found intolerable.

Balzac paints the salon itself vividly through Emile Blondet’s memory. The salon occupied the old Hall of Audience over vaults that had been prison cells; a great open hearth, an equestrian portrait of Henri III., chestnut beams, faded gilding, Flemish tapestries depicting Solomon’s judgment—these furnishings made the room a “glass cage,” visible to the passing world. Around the salon hovered a line of aged dowagers: shriveled, sepulchral, painted, mechanically moving about like automata—images that blended pity with eerie fascination. The men too, with their white hair and waxen faces, seemed more like relics than living beings. For a boy like Blondet, the salon was at once a museum and a shrine, and the sight of Mlle. Armande among these ghosts was a startling, almost mystical contrast: a fresh, living presence amid relics of feudal society.

The post-1813 and 1814 reversals—Napoleon’s fall and the Bourbon restoration—rekindled hopes among Royalists. Yet the vicissitudes of 1815 and unstable government delayed their expectations, and by 1822 the Marquis’s fortunes remained poor. The restoration of 1814 and 1815 had not restored the old feudal revenues. Many families like the d’Esgrignons had drawn most of their income not from land ownership as we would conceive it, but from feudal dues, seigneurial rights, and incidentals which had been abolished or transformed through the Revolution. Laws and indemnities did not fully compensate them. Attempts to reconstitute property rights or to pay indemnity were insufficient; some suppressed rights were restored only as taxes or State revenues, not as private income. This economic reality kept even ostensibly monarchical adherents in straitened circumstances.

Politically, the Marquis belonged to the most uncompromising Royalists—Ultras—who saw any accommodation with Napoleonic or revolutionary ideas as betrayal. For them, the Charter granting limited constitutional rights was a misstep to be annulled. The Marquis would not compromise; he was aloof from the King’s government when it fell short of full restoration, and patient aged conviction hardened into immobility. The “antiquities” thus ended up representing an ideal that the times had outlived. Their leader-figure, sixty-seven in 1822, preserved the old dignity but lacked the vigor necessary to effect political recovery. The provincial salon remained resolute, but it was more ceremonial than dynamic, and in local life it provoked both respect and ridicule.

The feud with the Liberals intensified as each side found opportunities to wound the other. The nobles had wit and delicate irony on their side; the Liberals, resentful and rising, used slander and political maneuvers. In a town setting, party spirit meant personal warfare: attacks by epigram, social exclusion, and calculated petty cruelties. Du Croisier, having set himself as the leader of manufacturers and the new order, labored to displace the d’Esgrignon influence. The department repeatedly refused du Croisier’s candidacy by following the Marquis’s lead; the former’s defeats fed a patient, implacable desire for revenge. Du Croisier’s coolest and most dangerous phase was that in which he smiled while nursing hate—a pose which made him more threatening to his enemies.

Despite diminished finances—though the family lived without ostentation—the hotel sustained the appearance and functions of a noble household. The income of the d’Esgrignons, carefully managed by Chesnel, barely met the necessities: the Oratorian tutor for the heir, Victurnien, household staff, Mlle. Armande’s needs, and a concern for preserving honor and old ways. Chesnel delayed the restoration of the ruined castle until leases fell in and rents could be raised; he managed with thrift and devotion, preserving the family’s dignity on meager means while aiming at a future rebuilding. Thus the Hotel d’Esgrignon, its salon, the ancestral comportment, and the fragile finances of the Marquis all formed a tableau of fading feudal order—nobility maintained more by ceremony and loyalty than by wealth.

This chapter of provincial life—the proud, ruined Marquis presiding over a salon of antiquities, the devoted but impractical Mlle. Armande, the faithful steward Chesnel, and the embittered du Croisier leading the rising class—sets the scene for the conflicts of manners, politics, and personal passions that will unfold. The old house, the ruined castle, the salon-cum-museum, and the social antagonisms of the town encapsulate the larger drama of France in the early Restoration: the collision of inherited privilege and rising bourgeois energy, the stubbornness of ideals unmoored from power, and the costs of pride and devotion in an age of transition.

The Marquis knew nothing of his household’s management or finances. Nearing the end of his life, he and his circle clung to the illusion that the d’Esgrignon house—ignored at Court and by government—would revive in his handsome heir, Victurnien, who would go to Court, enter royal service, and marry into an illustrious family. Their intimates—the Troisvilles, La Roche-Guyon, the Casterans, and the Duc de Verneuil—encouraged this belief. It would have required erasing forty years of history: the restored monarchy and public opinion demanded not only noble birth but money, political relevance, and uninterrupted prestige. The d’Esgrignons lacked continuity and modern resources; old renown could not substitute for wealth and influence. The Troisvilles’ daughter’s marriage to General Montcornet, rather than flattering antiquated pride, nearly split social circles—showing how times had changed.

Only Chesnel, the family’s old notary and former steward, saw matters clearly. Devoted but practical, he had witnessed the Revolution’s violence and its quieter triumph in new social ideas and vested interests. He understood that deaths and confiscations had become binding facts and that the new order would not be overturned; his attachment to the d’Esgrignons was therefore affectionate and clear-sighted. He tried to warn the Marquis tactfully; the Marquis received warnings with a prophet’s smile and the conviction that history would repeat in their favor. Chesnel feared the harm the family’s superstitions and self-delusions would inflict on Victurnien’s mind and habits.

Idolized by his father and aunt, Victurnien was thoroughly spoiled yet justified their hopes in many ways. Mlle. Armande, his aunt, loved him as a mother might but lacked maternal intuition and severity; she could not foresee danger as a real mother could. Chesnel’s vigilance resembled a mother’s instinct more than the aunt’s blind adoration, but the household generally paid little heed to his counsel—he had even fallen into a kind of disgrace after proposing an imprudent marriage for the family.

From infancy Victurnien was taught to assume his superiority: peers among the great nobles, the King as his only superior, and the rest of mankind as inferiors. With beauty, tact, a quick mind, and thorough education, he had every advantage. He was of average height, graceful, an excellent horseman and marksman, and intellectually promising—qualities that might have made him a great man. But indulged and unopposed, he grew insolent, audacious, and self-willed. The Chevalier, a charming old roue, misled the boy by glorifying the reckless manners and witty immorality of an earlier era; the Chevalier’s jocular maxims mingled with the family’s rigid emphasis on honor. Thus both good and bad counsel shaped Victurnien.

At eighteen he entered society and quickly misread appearances. Accustomed to deference at home and to unguarded talk in the presence of an elderly father, he did not see the hypocrisies and practical cautions of the larger world. His reckless behavior—shooting over neighbors’ lands, amorous imprudences requiring settlements and legal cover-ups—led to lawsuits and scandals quietly hushed by Chesnel, who paid to avert public shame. Encouraged by adroit enemies—particularly Du Croisier, who plotted revenge against the family—Victurnien’s faults were fanned. President du Ronceret and other officials, while outwardly lenient, fostered liberal clamor and public suspicion to discredit the nobles; du Croisier hoped to drive the d’Esgrignons into ruin through the young man’s vices. A young instigator, Fabien du Ronceret, befriended Victurnien to lead him on.

Between eighteen and twenty-one Victurnien ran up considerable debts—Chesnel had paid out roughly eighty thousand francs to cover lawsuits and settlements and later was disclosed as having lent “something like a hundred thousand livres.” The family’s mode of life left little margin: the Marquis’s income barely met household expenses. Victurnien’s tailor bills, tastes for English saddles and carriages, livery, and fashionable accessories swallowed his allowance. Chesnel, with modest private savings, had acted as the young man’s secret benefactor, bailing him out repeatedly. He hoped to see Victurnien settle into a sensible marriage, but the young man’s temperament—capable of lofty thought yet habitually surrendering to impulse and pleasure—made him dangerously likely to repeat mistakes.

Chesnel’s patience and warnings met with shrugging complacency from the Chevalier and indulgence from the family. The Chevalier dismissed debt as par for a gentleman’s course, recounted extravagant examples of aristocratic generosity, and minimized the danger. Chesnel, however, foresaw ruin. His fear and affection led him to plan a decisive measure: to have Victurnien sent to Paris and presented at Court, hoping the change would sober the boy and offer opportunities befitting his rank.

One autumn evening in 1822, after cards and salon talk, the household confronted the truth about Victurnien’s debts. The Chevalier forced the disclosure to the Marquis: Chesnel had lent an enormous sum. The Marquis was mortified that his steward would be the family’s creditor; pride and old notions of dignity made it painful to ask Chesnel for help outright. Nevertheless, they resolved to send Victurnien to Paris to present himself to the King and secure an appointment; they accepted Chesnel’s loan rather than humiliate the family by other means.

Chesnel explained that the Count’s companions and enemies in town—the president, Du Croisier and his circle—were seeking vengeance by ensnaring Victurnien, provoking scandals to ruin the family. He had borrowed money himself, mortgaging Le Jard, and arranged the loan in confidence so it would not appear as direct family shame. Mlle. Armande and the Marquis were moved; Chesnel kissed her hand and, with paternal tenderness, urged that Paris provide the boy more diversion and discretion than provincial society could, while placing him where better company might discipline him.

Preparations were hurried. The Marquis and Mlle. Armande instructed Victurnien that he was to go to Court, serve His Majesty, and there ascertain a career—army, navy, diplomacy, or the royal household. They promised a modest provision and sent Josephin, Victurnien’s trusted valet, as companion. Chesnel wrote to an old notary friend in Paris, Maître Sorbier, begging vigilant oversight of the young man’s conduct and intimating that funds would be placed at Sorbier’s disposition. His letter revealed a deep emotional tie: he felt he had reared and protected the d’Esgrignons through revolution and decline, and that the young Count’s fate was intimately his concern.

Victurnien accepted a monthly allowance of two thousand francs and left amid the household’s blessings. He felt no genuine regret; provincial life had been gentle but narrow, and Paris promised to amplify his vanity and gratify his tastes. In the capital he discovered the city’s scale and the fact that noble names no longer guaranteed power or employment; the Court’s attention was limited, and new social forces and money mattered most. Undeterred, he took to luxury and fashion as necessary to command respect and assumed a posture of independence that hid need.

In Paris he sought to recover a position he imagined due to his birth. He bought rooms, stables, and equerries at great expense. His family’s reputation and the Faubourg Saint-Germain’s curiosity opened many doors: old names still worked as social passports. He found a circle of fashionable young men—de Marsay, Rastignac, de Ronquerolles, Maxime de Trailles, and others—who moved easily in the world he coveted and initiated him into its pleasures. The Vidame de Pamiers, a discreet source of gossip and introductions in the Faubourg, welcomed Victurnien and took him to salons and dinners where he met writers and courtiers, and where the Vidame encouraged him toward conquest and social display.

At a dinner with the Vidame, de Marsay, Rastignac, and the young writer Emile Blondet, Victurnien encountered the pleasures and perils of Parisian society. The Vidame introduced him to Mlle. des Touches’ salon and to fashionable circles where literature and wit mixed with vanity and intrigue. The Vidame, who kept a finger on public taste, predicted the young Count’s rapid rise—and fall—if he pursued such extremes.

Victurnien was introduced to the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, a dazzling and complex beauty who immediately attracted him and whom others envied. The Duchesse presented herself as ethereal and virginal—an affectation that masked cleverness and self-regard. She resolved to play an innocence that would grant her influence, a role adopted to conceal and manipulate her passions. Her carefully constructed purity, full of artful glances and guarded gestures, made her all the more alluring; she used sanctity’s appearance to amplify the power of her charm.

Thus Victurnien found himself at the center of fashionable life. His family’s name, his beauty, and the patronage of the Faubourg made him desirable; his own vanity made him determined to sustain an image of wealth and consequence. Chesnel’s precautions were circumvented by Parisian finance and notaries eager for patronage. The young Count’s expenses mounted: lodgings, dress, horses, and the cost of entering high society quickly devoured the money set aside. Despite Chesnel’s hopes that Paris would rescue him from provincial follies, the city offered new temptations, alliances, and risks that promised either brilliant ascent or irreversible ruin.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, and neither Chesnel nor Mlle. Armande knew it. He had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel at intervals to conceal his sources. He wrote deceitful letters to his poor father and aunt, who lived happily deceived. The insidious current of Paris life was preparing a dreadful catastrophe for the great house; only du Croisier knew. He rubbed his hands as he passed the Antiquities, hoping to secure not merely ruin but dishonor for the d’Esgrignons. He had been sure of it since discovering that the young Count’s burden of debt was growing too heavy.

Du Croisier’s first step was to rid himself of his most hated enemy, the good old notary Chesnel. The old man lived in the Rue du Bercail in a house with a little paved courtyard and a small garden. At half-past five he usually sat after dinner in his black leather-covered armchair, with a painted pasteboard contrivance protecting his stockinged legs from the fire; he often ate too much and enjoyed planning how his savings might round the d’Esgrignon estates. Chesnel’s affections were wrapped in that single attachment; he took pride in rebuilding the house and hoped to be remembered by future d’Esgrignons.

His old housekeeper entered with alarm. “M. du Croisier wanting to speak to you—” she said.

“M. du Croisier!” The name gave Chesnel a stab of cold; he dropped the tongs. Du Croisier came in, bowed, sat, and produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, principal and interest, for sums advanced to Victurnien in bills of exchange drawn upon du Croisier and honored by him. He demanded immediate payment, threatening proceedings against the heir-presumptive. Chesnel turned the unlucky letters over and begged secrecy; du Croisier agreed to keep the secret if paid within forty-eight hours. Pressed for money, Chesnel had obliged manufacturers and resorted to the usual financial fictions. His eyes were dim; he could scarcely keep back tears. The only way to raise the money was to mortgage his lands to their full value.

But when du Croisier learned Chesnel could not repay, he suddenly offered to buy the old lawyer’s property. The sale was completed within two days. Poor Chesnel could not bear the thought of the heir of the house undergoing five years’ imprisonment for debt; shortly nothing remained to him but his practice, the sums due to him, and the house in which he lived. Stripped of lands, he paced his oak-paneled office and thought no longer of Le Jard but of Victurnien.

“What will become of him? He ought to come back; they must marry him to some rich heiress,” he said, with dim eyes and a heavy head.

He did not know how to break the news to Mlle. Armande; the man who had paid the family’s debts quaked at the thought. He went from the Rue du Bercail to the Hotel d’Esgrignon with pulses throbbing like a girl’s, fearing the ruin of illusions.

Mlle. Armande had just received a charming, hypocritical letter. Her nephew was the happiest man alive: he had been to baths, traveled in Italy with Mme. de Maufrigneuse, and sent a journal full of love—Venice, Milan, Florence, the Apennines, Chiavari—painting happiness everywhere. The poor aunt was enthralled. She saw the far-off country of love, sat in Victurnien’s gondola, felt the Duchess’ hand in his; she drank in the letter. Repressed passion had ripened; she loved the couple ardently and excused a married woman’s love for Victurnien; to refuse it would have been unpardonable. Mlle. Armande was not an angelic type but a slender, serene figure; at this moment blue buds were unfolding in her saintly eyes. She loved them as apart from life.

Even in her Venetian reverie, someone appeared on the garden walk—Chesnel. The sound of his tread and the hopelessness on his face recalled her to herself.

“What is it?” she cried.

“All is lost!” said Chesnel. “M. le Comte will bring dishonor upon the house if we do not set it in order.” He held out the bills and described the recent agony in simple, touching words.

“He is deceiving us! The miserable boy!” cried Mlle. Armande, feeling her heart surge.

“Let us say mea culpa, mademoiselle,” Chesnel replied stoutly; “we have allowed him his own way; he needed stern guidance—he had no mother.” Fate sometimes deals terribly with a decaying noble house, she thought, with tears.

The Marquis came up; he had been reading the letter sent by his son from Italy. Victurnien’s itinerary was told from an aristocrat’s point of view: he had been magnificently welcomed by the great families, owed in part to his name and perhaps the Duchess. The old Marquis swung himself off with a coxcomb’s air, recounting ancestral glories. The two companions in unhappiness sat together on a garden bench, saying little, watching the father walk away in his contentment.

“What will become of him now?” Mlle. Armande asked.

“Du Croisier has sent instructions to the Kellers; he is not to be allowed to draw any more without authorization.”

“And there are debts.”

“I am afraid so.”

“If he is left without resources, what will he do?”

“I dare not answer that.”

“But he must be drawn out of that life; he must come back to us, or he will have nothing left.”

“And nothing else,” Chesnel said gloomily. Mlle. Armande could not yet understand the full force of his words.

“Is there any hope of getting him away from that woman—the Duchess? Perhaps she leads him on.”

“He would not stick at a crime to be with her,” Chesnel tried to suggest, preparing her for an intolerable thought.

“Crime!” she cried. “Oh, Chesnel, no one but you would think of such a thing!” she added with a withering look. “There is but one crime a noble can commit—the crime of high treason; and when he is beheaded, the block is covered with a black cloth, as for kings.”

“The times have changed,” said Chesnel, shaking his head. Victurnien had thinned the last of his white hairs. That thought soothed Mlle. Armande’s indignation, but she did not realize what Chesnel meant.

“To-morrow we will decide,” she said. “It needs thought. At worst, we have our lands.”

“Yes,” Chesnel replied. “You and M. le Marquis own the estate conjointly; the larger part is yours. You can raise money on it without saying a word to him.”

That evening players at whist and other games noticed Mlle. Armande’s agitation. “That poor heroic child!” said the old Marquise de Casteran; “she must be suffering still. A woman never knows what her sacrifices to her family may cost her.”

Next day it was arranged that Mlle. Armande should go to Paris to snatch her nephew from perdition. If any one could carry him off, it was the woman whose motherly heart yearned over him. She determined to go to the Duchess and tell her all. Some pretext was needed for the journey; for appearances Mlle. Armande allowed it to be thought she suffered from a complaint requiring consultation with celebrated physicians. Goodness knows whether the town talked of this! But she saw that far more than her reputation was at stake. She set out with Chesnel’s last bag of louis; she took it and her capuchine and mittens without comment. “Generous girl!” he said, putting her into the carriage with her maid.

Du Croisier had thought out his revenge as provincials do—meticulously, with every contingency provided for. Had he sounded Victurnien’s nature so well that he foresaw how easily the young Count would lend himself to his schemes? Or was he merely profiting on a long-watched opportunity? Who warned du Croisier of the moment—the Kellers, or President du Ronceret’s son finishing law studies in Paris?

Du Croisier wrote to Victurnien that the Kellers had been instructed to advance no more money; the letter was timed to arrive as the Duchess and the Count faced poverty cunningly hidden. In the letter informing Victurnien the Kellers would make no further advances without security, a wide blank space was left between the formal text and the signature. It was easy to tear off the upper part and convert the lower into a bill of exchange for any amount. The missive had been enclosed in an envelope so the other side of the sheet was blank. When it arrived, Victurnien was in the lowest despair. After two years of luxurious, thoughtless life, he found himself face to face with inexorable poverty: an absolute impossibility to procure money.

There had been throes before the journey’s end. With the Duchess’ help he had extorted various sums from bankers, but those very amounts were about to start up as overdue bills, with stern summonses from the Bank of France and the commercial court. All through the last weeks he had felt the point of the Commander’s sword: at every supper-party a creeping warning that the sirocco of debt was near. For five years his purse had always been replenished. After Chesnel came du Croisier (he thought), then surely another gold-mine would pour out its wealth. Besides, he was winning at play; luck had saved him unpleasant steps before; often a wild hope sent him to the Salon des Etrangers only to lose the winnings at whist. His life had become like Don Giovanni’s immortal finale—disorder, confusion, a frantic struggle to shut out debts and duels. He lived through this infernal poem alone, seeing visions of a friendless outcast reading the last words on the last page—THE END!

Yes; for him all would be at an end and soon. He already saw the ironical eyes of associates amusing themselves over his downfall. Some played high and could not spare a banknote for an intimate. There was no help—Chesnel must be ruined; he had devoured Chesnel’s living.

He sat with the Duchess in their box at the Italiens, the house envying them, and while he smiled the Furies were tearing at his heart. To give some sense of his despair: he had even taken out pistols and thought of suicide. He who would never brook insult was abusing himself in language no man hears except from himself.

He left du Croisier’s letter open on the bed. Josephin had brought it at nine. Victurnien’s furniture had been seized, but he slept none the less. After the Opera he and the Duchess had gone to a voluptuous retreat where they often spent hours after brilliant court balls. Appearances were cleverly saved; their love-nest seemed a garret to all but was luxuriously arranged within. Now on the brink of ruin, he longed to bid farewell to the dainty nest. Adversity would break the enchanted eggs; there would be no brood of white doves, no brilliant tropical birds of fancy. In three days he must be gone; his bills had been placed in the hands of money-lenders, and law proceedings had reached the last stage.

An evil thought crossed him. He would flee with the Duchess to some undiscovered nook in America, but with a fortune, leaving creditors to confront their bills. To carry out the plan he had only to cut off the lower portion of du Croisier’s letter bearing the signature and fill in figures to turn it into a bill, presenting it to the Kellers. There was a dreadful struggle with temptation; tears were shed; but the honor of the family triumphed—subject to one condition. Victurnien wanted to be sure of his beautiful Diane; he would do nothing unless she consented to their flight. So he went to the Duchess in the Rue Faubourg Saint-Honoré and found her in coquettish morning dress.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse was somewhat pensive. Cares gnawed her mind, but she met them gallantly. Of all feminine types, some combine strength and insight with a faculty for prompt decision and a resolution that would shake a man’s nerves; these powers lie beneath an appearance of graceful helplessness. Such women can ascend to the highest feelings or show selfish insensibility. The Duchess, pondering her fall, laid love aside to consider her safety: refuge abroad, confessing to the King, fascinating a du Tillet or a Nucingen, or gambling on the stock exchange—all were reviewed calmly. She could stand aloof and watch the crash as if a spectator instead of being buried beneath it. That power was great but repulsive.

While dressing for a drive she had collected her thoughts and faced the possibilities of terrific downfall. She plucked love out of her heart to consider necessities and was ready to set the passion back when her coronet was safe. She had none of Richelieu’s hesitation or Napoleon’s early doubts; she resolved.

Victurnien came in. With all his suppressed capacity he was in a state of terror; his heart beat violently; he was afraid to touch the cornerstone that upheld his life with Diane. The cleverest men sometimes deceive themselves to avoid self-humiliation. Victurnien committed himself.

“What is the matter?” Diane asked.

“I am hopelessly in debt. I have come to the end.”

“Pshaw! it is nothing,” she said, smiling. “Money can always be arranged; nothing is irretrievable except disasters in love.”

Relieved by her swift comprehension, he unrolled the bright web of his life’s past two years and a half—the seamy side—setting it off with delicate scorn for men and things. He spoke as an aristocrat; the Duchess listened.

She sat with a knee raised, elbow on knee, face in hand; her eyes never left him while a thousand thoughts flitted under their blue surface. Her forehead was calm, mouth intent—grave with love—her lips knotted by his. To have her listening thus seemed a divine love. When Victurnien proposed flight, he cried, “You are an angel!”

The Duchess made no immediate answer; her mind was absorbed in secret plans. “Good, very good,” she said at last. “But that is not the question, dear. Let us think of your affairs. Yes, we will go, and the sooner the better. Arrange it; I will follow. It is glorious to leave Paris and the world.”

When a duchess speaks such words the sacrifice is made. Diane’s promise settled him; he could conceal sordid details better because she did not inquire. Victurnien would not go till the promise was sealed; he needed courage from happiness before doing a deed that might be badly construed. He counted on his aunt and father and Chesnel to hush up the affair; Chesnel might think of some compromise. Besides, this “business” was the only way to raise money on the family estate. With three hundred thousand francs he and Diane would lead a happy hidden life in Venice.

Next day Victurnien drew a bill for three hundred thousand francs and took it to the Kellers. They advanced the money, for du Croisier happened then to have a balance; but they wrote that he must not draw again without notice. Du Croisier, much astonished, asked for a statement of accounts. It was sent. Everything was explained. The day of his vengeance had arrived.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When Victurnien had drawn "his" money he carried it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse. She locked the banknotes in her desk and proposed to bid the world farewell by going to the Opera for the last time. Victurnien grew uneasy; he thought his being seen in the Duchess’ box might cost him dear. Perhaps, he reflected, once the three hundred thousand francs were safe, he should travel post, go to Chesnel and confess all. Before they left the opera-house the Duchess glanced at him; her eyes shone with the desire to visit once more the nest she loved. The boy lost a night.

Next day, at three o’clock, he returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse to learn the Duchess’ plan for that night’s escape. "Why go?" she asked. "The Vicomtesse de Beauseant and the Duchesse de Langeais vanished quietly. If I leave as they did, it will be commonplace. We will brave the storm. It will be finer. I am sure of success."

Victurnien’s face betrayed agitation; his hesitation provoked Diane’s scorn. She interpreted it as a failure of love. A man schooled in flatteries would have agreed at once with whatever fancy the woman took, but Victurnien, for the first time angry, answered imperfectly and clumsily. The Duchess, wounded and majestic, cast him off with coldness.

After eighteen months of tenderness she told him abruptly that he did not love her. He protested. He burst out that he had done much for her, but when he hinted at his sacrifice she retorted that he was unworthy of the truth. Diane drew herself up, bowed her head on her hand, and became implacable. Victurnien, at sight of her suffering, forgot his own peril; he begged, wept, kissed her feet. For two hours he pleaded in vain before her cold, wrapped sorrow; for two more the Count took Diane’s hand—it felt like wood—and Victurnien sank into stupor. Memories of past rapture rose only to be rejected. The Duchess toyed with her scarf, spoke of his rivals and of men who loved with a cruel strength—De Vandenesse, De Marsay, Montriveau—preferring those dark-haired, sunlit men to the fair-haired Victurnien. Her remarks humiliated and wounded him with deliberate art.

At last he could bear it no more. He rushed from her presence in fury, drove heedlessly, collided with vehicles and the curb, and let his horse bolt along the Quai d’Orsay. Turning into the Rue de l’Université, Josephin stopped the runaway and brought the news: officers had come with a warrant for his arrest. Victurnien, thinking only of his crime, believed all was over.

"Drive to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, in the Rue de Grenelle. Mlle. Armande is there; she will take you," Josephin urged. Victurnien flung himself into his aunt’s arms; she wept and comforted him. They drove to Brest, leaving Paris behind. In the carriage they spoke at cross purposes: Victurnien thought of the forgery; his aunt of debts.

At night he was brought by Mlle. Armande to Chesnel’s quiet house in the Rue du Bercail, to hide at midnight. Fate, however, led directly to discovery. Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir’s head-clerk for a hundred thousand francs, had reason to be sanguine. His successor, noticing a travelling carriage at the notary’s door at that hour, watched and saw Mlle. Armande alight. He summoned officers; Victurnien sprang from the carriage and entered the courtyard—whereupon the whites of secrecy vanished and Victurnien was hurried into a room beyond Chesnel’s private office, where, for the moment, no one could enter except over Chesnel’s dead body.

Chesnel, aghast when Victurnien explained, burst into tears that shame could not still. He rocked between despair and resolve. "If only your father may die and never know this," he cried; forgery was a crime that would dishonor the family. He resolved to save the house of d’Esgrignon if possible. Victurnien mentioned that the money was still in Mme. de Maufrigneuse’s keeping. Chesnel clung to that slender hope. He proposed persuading du Croisier to return the draft or buying it back from the Kellers, while hiding the Count in Chesnel’s cellar if necessary.

He packed for Paris at once, leaving Victurnien locked in the little room and giving orders to deter visitors. Chesnel contrived a post coach, coaxing the manager with a tale. By midnight he reached Paris; at nine that morning he learned from the Kellers that the fatal draft had been returned to du Croisier three days before. Francois Keller said nothing could be done at their end without du Croisier’s consent. In desperation Chesnel went to the Duchess.

Mme. de Maufrigneuse was in bed; Chesnel contrived to see her. He told her bluntly that a hundred thousand crowns in her keeping had been obtained by a forgery committed for her sake, and that unless Victurnien were protected the boy and all related would be ruined. The Duchess, feeling ashamed of having scolded a lover who had gone so far, sprang to her desk, gave him the notes, and promised to use her influence "to save him or die." In her own way the Duchess resolved to be an angel.

Chesnel, buoyed, went to Josephin; he found letters that might help—correspondence from du Croisier and the Kellers. He took a diligence back and rushed along the road. After three days he returned to the Rue du Bercail—too late to prevent Victurnien’s being led out of the courtyard in the custody of gendarmes, the commissary, and the clerk of the court. Chesnel thought in despair: "If I cannot hush this up you must kill yourself before the indictment is made out." Victurnien, stupefied, stared.

Chesnel then made for the Hotel du Croisier. It was still in the power of a private individual to withdraw an action of forgery; perhaps du Croisier could be softened. Du Croisier, a man of sturdy bourgeois pride, had invited an inner circle that evening—M. du Ronceret, President of the Tribunal; M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor; M. du Coudrai, and others. With the careful hypocrisy of such gatherings they discussed the news. When the President remarked that at that very hour M. le Comte d’Esgrignon had been arrested, the circle exulted. They spoke with the petty triumph that provincial justice offers when it can abase a noble family.

Chesnel burst in, implored, knelt before du Croisier, and offered any sacrifice—lands, service—in exchange for withdrawing the charge. Du Croisier, enjoying his revenge, replied that the whole town knew of the arrest; still, he considered that though acquittal might come, dishonor would remain. His wife, moved by pleas, interceded.

But du Croisier interrupted with a patriotic, ambitious tirade: this was a question of the people and the law; the nobles must be taught to respect the bourgeoisie. It was not only vengeance but a political lesson. Chesnel recoiled; du Croisier’s purpose was not merely to recover money but to enforce humiliation and secure political advantage. He suggested conditions: votes and social recognition in return for mercy. He demanded that the d’Esgrignon family be bound by a surety of four hundred thousand francs and that a written compromise be kept as a loaded cannon on their heart. He even dreamt of a marriage to bind fortune and hold power.

Chesnel, desperate, yielded in part. Du Croisier, drunk with triumph, relished having the fate of an ancient house in his hand. Mme. du Croisier, religious and loyal to husband and order, was torn. Chesnel, seeing her hesitation, produced the hundred thousand crowns he had taken from Mme. de Maufrigneuse and urged her to accept them and swear that she had received them five days before the draft appeared—thus creating a pretext to stop du Croisier’s action. He dictated a receipt and pressed her to promise to declare before the examining magistrate that she had been paid on the earlier date. She hesitated at the falsehood, but after invoking her confessor, she agreed. Chesnel, who had nothing to lose, risked a grave perjury to save the house.

He then roused Mlle. Armande and sent her to fetch the Bishop—the Church’s influence could not be neglected. He reviewed magistrates and officials in a sleepless survey: some he could influence, others he feared.

The narrative broadens to a picture of provincial justice. In the provinces ambition and hope of advancement shape men’s behavior; young magistrates long for political cases to shine in Paris; older judges are fixed in place and soured. The judiciary divides into the resigned, content section and the hungry younger men. At that time many were full of Royalist zeal against Bonapartist currents, eager to prosecute cases that might display zeal and hence secure advancement. This zeal made justice sometimes indiscreet; officials combined bourgeois spirit with a desire to mimic Court manners. Where duty and party met, petty politics often overrode moderation.

At the Tribunal where the d’Esgrignon case would be tried there were both sorts. President du Ronceret and the venerable Blondet represented the resigned section; the younger, ambitious faction included the examining magistrate M. Camusot and his deputy, Mi. Michu. Du Ronceret himself was a man of petty resentments; he had once hoped higher and now cultivated independence as a substitute. He was tall, spare, a victim of social disappointments, and married to a gaudily proud woman who gave small weekly entertainments. Their house was gloomy and old-fashioned; their entertainments—tea, ill-made pastry, a quarter’s grande dinner—satisfied appearances but were mean. Du Ronceret kept a nest-egg to leave his son Fabien; sociable rivalries and private intrigues preoccupied him.

Old Blondet, by contrast, was a seasoned jurist, once a barrister, later public accuser, gentle in his temperament, and respected for his legal skill. He had been passed over for the presidency, which had gone to du Ronceret because of family pedigree, but Blondet remained on the bench as a sound, conscientious presence.

In du Croisier’s drawing-room that night talk ranged from triumph to strategy. Mme. Camusot, shrewd and unsparing, hinted that though the offence might be technical, political influence—counsel from Paris, Government pressure—could affect the process. She predicted "a sensation" when the family’s defenders came from the capital. The deputy public prosecutor, Sauvager, had acted promptly in obtaining the warrant—a man of weak but ambitious character, eager to curry favor by zealous prosecution. Camusot, the examining magistrate who signed the warrant on Sauvager’s application, had not anticipated its swift execution and now feared complications.

Chesnel, encountering Camusot in the street, drew him aside and hinted that support from high quarters—Mme. la Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and others—would be of inestimable help if Camusot were to favor the d’Esgrignons. He implied he knew the secrets of Paris and could expose any petty misconduct at du Croisier’s—an implicit threat to the magistrate’s career if he opposed them. With prudence, Camusot was swayed. Chesnel hurried to confront du Croisier, and when he found him with his guests, he implored for mercy, knelt, and offered all. Du Croisier answered that the arrest was public; still, he entertained offers. His wife pleaded. But rather than yielding to simple purchase, du Croisier laid down political conditions: votes, acknowledgment, and a surety judgment. His aims were broader than money; he sought social and political triumph.

Chesnel turned to Mme. du Croisier and, after earnest pleadings, bribed her with the hundred thousand crowns. She wrestled with conscience—between wifely loyalty and exalted piety—then consented to the lie, agreeing to swear before the magistrate that she had received the money earlier. Chesnel also sought the Abbe Couturier’s support, recognizing the Church’s weight in those small courts. He returned home exhausted but triumphant in part, having bought a probable reprieve at the cost of perjury and of suborning a pious woman.

In the background, the author paints the provincial judicial world: its hunger for advancement, the mingling of private ambition with public duty, the factionalism between the resigned and the ambitious, and the ease with which justice can be manipulated by social and political pressure. The scene closes with Chesnel’s weary but resolute survey of the magistrates; he tracks their secret ambitions, their likely votes, and the levers by which he may win them—a study of how influence, money, and social standing weigh on the scales of law in a provincial town.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Blondet’s talents and legal learning might have carried him far, but he held a deep contempt for his profession and kept his ambitions, leisure, and capacity for effort for a different pursuit: gardening. Corresponding with celebrated amateurs and keen on botanical discovery, the old judge lived among flowers, favoring the pelargonium. Court business and outward life mattered little beside the inward life of fancies and emotions he led in his greenhouse. Had he not been so absorbed, he would likely have been a deputy under the Empire and prominent in the Corps Législatif.

His marriage drove him further into obscurity. At forty he rashly married an eighteen-year-old who bore him a son, Joseph. Three years later Mme. Blondet, the town’s prettiest woman, inspired a lasting passion in the prefect, who became the father of her second son, Emile. The affair was an open secret: the whole town knew, and even old Blondet suspected. Rather than wake his ambition, the marriage encouraged his botanical tastes; neither he nor his wife wished to leave their places. Unequal to contest with a younger, favored rival, Blondet sought consolation in his greenhouse and kept a pretty servant to help him. While he tended plants, Mme. Blondet spent his substance on dress and finery. Only Emile’s education interested her; she loved Emile, and Blondet loved Joseph, blinded by paternal affection.

For twelve years Blondet bore this with an eighteenth-century grand seigneur composure, but he profoundly disliked his younger son. When his wife died in 1818, he turned Emile out, sending him to Paris on a scant allowance; Emile would have perished without the intervention of his real father. Blondet’s house, opposite the prefecture, had a neat court and admired flowerbeds; climbing plants covered walls, honeysuckle hid piers, cactuses perched in vases. Through glass doors one could see a garden that extended into two acres. Blondet’s income was about four thousand livres from land, plus the house. He intended to leave his property and bench seat to Joseph, making a will favoring him and setting aside money to enable Joseph to buy back portions of the estate that could not legally be taken from Emile.

Emile, cast adrift, gained distinction in Paris as a man of letters and moved in society, buoyed by friendships such as with Mlle. de Troisville (later Mme. de Montcornet). Mme. Blondet had tried to link him to the Troisvilles, who later favored him. Emile’s precocious literary reputation and good looks strengthened his bond with the Countess de Montcornet; by the time his adventurous life pressed on him, their attachment was unshakeable. Old Blondet never learned of Emile’s success; he remained ignorant of his son’s greatness.

Blondet’s integrity matched his passion for flowers. He knew only law and botany. He treated litigants courteously, accepted rare seeds as gifts, but was impartial on the bench; litigants trusted his clear procedure. His steadiness made him indispensable to President du Ronceret, who—apart from matrimonial schemes—would have done all he could to prevent the vice-president’s retiring in favor of his son. Blondet did not know Emile could have fulfilled his wishes quickly.

Their household was frugal to parsimony: fruit saved until it spoiled, no new bread, strict fast days. The maid, Mlle. Cadot, kept keys and watched expenses; she shared the father’s predilection for Joseph. Blondet tried to mold Joseph into a lawyer to satisfy the Blandureaus, who insisted Joseph be an assistant judge to marry their daughter. Joseph spent evenings at the Blandureaus’, where young Fabien du Ronceret also frequented. Everything was measured; the garden, however, escaped thrift. A single gardener kept paths and borders immaculate, and an amphitheatre of benches held thousands of pelargoniums that drew visitors from far departments; Empress Marie Louise once visited and Blondet received the Legion of Honor. Blondet shunned society and was unaware of the President’s machinations.

Michu, a young judge with twelve thousand livres income, cared more for ingratiating himself with high society than for provincial routine. He did enough at the Tribunal to be useful and, with Parisian training as prosecuting counsel, could quickly solve legal questions that puzzled the President and Blondet. He was indispensable socially and professionally, able to mediate delicate matters.

At the crisis moment the public prosecutor was away in Paris. The President and du Croisier used his absence to engineer a forgery charge against Victurnien d’Esgrignon. Normally such a case would meet resistance and might be hushed up; here the President contrived to have M. Sauvager, a young Royalist deputy public prosecutor who owed his place to subservience, take up the charge without consulting his absent chief. The President and du Croisier then persuaded Sauvager—promising advantageous matches and future fortunes if he showed devotion—to act against the d’Esgrignons immediately and preempt Parisian intervention. Sauvager agreed.

Camusot’s unexpected defection from the President’s scheme terrified the plotters. The examining magistrate, Camusot was the son of a silk mercer and had advanced by backstairs influence. Married to Amelie Thirion (daughter of an usher of the Royal cabinet), he had a wife with Parisian instincts and impatience for provincial life. Mme. Camusot meddled in her husband’s career and saw the d’Esgrignon affair as an opportunity to curry favor with powerful families, including the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, to secure promotion and a Paris appointment for Camusot. Perceiving the President’s underhand maneuvers, she advised her husband to side with the d’Esgrignons, foreseeing advantage.

Chesnel, who knew the Tribunal circle, counted on the public prosecutor, Camusot, and Michu to support the d’Esgrignons; he also believed Blondet, if persuaded, would favor justice for a cause of longstanding importance to him. Chesnel planned to go to Blondet to avert the President’s treachery. Before dawn he was roused by a startling visitor: the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, disguised in man’s attire, had driven from Paris to save Victurnien. She declared she had brought a hundred thousand francs from the King’s private purse to buy Victurnien’s innocence if necessary; if bribery failed, she had brought poison to prevent an indictment. She had sent word to the public prosecutor, who was following her.

Chesnel welcomed the Duchess’s aid. They proceeded to see Camusot early; the Duchess would charm Mme. Camusot and secure Camusot’s cooperation. She and Chesnel agreed the Duchess would speak to Mme. Camusot while Chesnel used his authority to persuade Camusot that rescuing the d’Esgrignon was in the kingdom’s interest. The Duchess insisted on secrecy.

Mme. Camusot, quick to grasp the situation, accompanied the Duchess to Blondet’s greenhouse, presenting the Duchess as a distinguished horticulturist. She warned Blondet of the President’s duplicity: the President aimed to thwart Blondet’s hopes for Joseph’s marriage to Mlle. Blandureau, and Fabien du Ronceret had received clandestine proposals for Mlle. Claire’s hand. Blondet, a man of flowers, was shocked. The Duchess promised that if Blondet resigned properly and waited for confirmation from the public prosecutor, his son’s appointment could be secured within a week; she urged him to confer with Camusot and Michu to hush up the affair quickly, securing Joseph’s prospects. Blondet, overwhelmed and suspicious, hurried for confirmation.

At nine the three judges—Blondet, Camusot, and Michu—met secretly. Blondet, furious at the President’s trickery, insisted the matter was a trifle and argued against construing the episode as forgery: forgery requires an intent to defraud, which seemed absent, since Chesnel had repeatedly squared accounts and allegedly deposited money to meet the bill. Michu proposed extracting the Count from the mess at once by hastening examination of du Croisier and his wife and issuing a decision the next morning. They agreed to act swiftly.

Du Croisier was summoned and questioned by Camusot; under searching interrogation he confirmed prior dealings with the Count and that Chesnel had often remitted amounts. Camusot then asked whether du Croisier knew money destined to meet the bill had been deposited with him beforehand; this unnerved du Croisier. Chesnel, summoned, confirmed he had deposited the money and produced letters to prove it; Mme. du Croisier corroborated, declaring Chesnel had entrusted the sum to her in her husband’s absence. With these facts on record, the Count’s innocence emerged legally.

Du Croisier returned home enraged at his wife’s deposition; she showed him bundles of banknotes she had kept concealed, arguing she had acted for his good and future advantage. He threatened hasty departure while his wife believed she had safeguarded the family’s prospects.

The town buzzed over the Count’s arrest; by ten the court dismissed the charge, giving reasons that allowed the Count to sue for libel. Chesnel walked the street exposing du Croisier’s plot. As the day closed, the Marquis d’Esgrignon and family fretted over debts and honor. The Duchess, who had stayed with them, advised practical measures: marry for money if necessary; old noble privileges had been eroded by modern law and fortunes now mattered more than lineage. She produced a tiny phial and joked about decisive remedies; she counseled Victurnien to take lands and a quiet life rather than Paris flirtations. The romantic episode ended: the Duchess, once disguised and passionate, resumed her rank; for Victurnien and Diane, the brief romance dissolved with the crisis’s end.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Six months later Camusot was appointed assistant judge in Paris and became an examining magistrate. Goodman Blondet was made a councillor to the Royal Court; he kept the post long enough to secure a pension, then returned to his pretty house. Joseph Blondet took his father’s seat at the court until his death; with no chance of promotion he married Mlle. Blandereau and now probably leads, in the little flower-covered brick house, as dull a life as any carp in a marble basin. Michu and Camusot received the Cross of the Legion of Honor; Blondet became an Officer. M. Sauvager, deputy public prosecutor, was sent to Corsica, much to du Croisier’s relief; du Croisier certainly did not intend to give his niece to that functionary.

Urged by President du Ronceret, du Croisier appealed the Tribunal’s finding to the Court-Royal and lost. Liberals across the department held little d’Esgrignon guilty; Royalists spread frightful tales of plots by “that abominable du Croisier” seeking revenge. A duel followed: chance favored du Croisier; the young Count was dangerously wounded, and his antagonist stood by his words. The affair embittered party strife, and the Liberals invoked it whenever convenient. Meanwhile du Croisier never carried his election and saw no hope of marrying his niece to the Count, especially after the duel.

A month after the Tribunal’s decision was confirmed, Chesnel died, exhausted by the dreadful strain that had weakened him mentally and physically. He died in the hour of victory, like a faithful hound that brings the boar to bay and dies on its tusks. He died as happily as possible, though he left the great House nearly ruined, the heir penniless, bored by idleness, and with no hope of establishing himself. That bitter thought and his exhaustion no doubt hastened his end. One comfort amid wrecked hopes: at his sister’s entreaty the old Marquis restored their friendship. The great lord came to the Rue du Bercail and sat by his old servant’s bedside, unaware how much the servant had done and sacrificed. Chesnel sat up and repeated Simeon’s cry. The Marquis allowed burial in the castle chapel; they laid him crosswise at the foot of the tomb reserved for the Marquis himself, the last, in a sense, of the d’Esgrignons.

Thus died one of the last representatives of Service in its original meaning: the relation between feudal lord and servitor. Now found only in remote provinces or among a few old royal servants, it did honor to a noblesse that inspired affection and to a bourgeoisie that could conceive it. Such noble and magnificent devotion is no longer possible among us. Noble houses have no servitors; France no longer has a King, hereditary peerage, or land bound irrevocably to historic houses to perpetuate the nation’s glorious names. Chesnel was not merely an obscure great man of private life; he was a great fact. Is there not something indefinably solemn and sublime in his sustained self-devotion, rising above a single beneficent deed or a moment’s supreme effort? Chesnel’s virtues belonged to classes between the people’s poverty and the aristocracy’s greatness, combining homely burgher virtues with heroic noble ideals enlightened by solid education.

Victurnien was not well regarded at Court; there was no chance of a great match or office. His Majesty refused to raise the d’Esgrignons to the peerage—the one royal favor that could have rescued him from his wretched position. He could not marry a bourgeois heiress during his father’s life, so he lived shabbily under the paternal roof, haunted by memories of two years’ splendor in Paris and the lost love of a great lady. He grew moody and depressed, vegetating with a careworn aunt and a half-broken father who blamed a wasting malady. Chesnel was gone.

The Marquis died in 1830. The great d’Esgrignon, followed by the less infirm noblesse of the Collection of Antiquities, went to wait upon Charles X at Nonancourt, swelling the meager train of the fallen king—an act then felt as heroism amid enthusiastic revolt.

“The Gaul has conquered!” were the Marquis’ last words.

By that time du Croisier’s victory was complete. A week after his father’s death the new Marquis d’Esgrignon married Mlle. Duval. His bride brought three million francs; du Croisier and his wife settled the reversion of their fortunes upon her in the marriage contract. During the ceremony du Croisier declared the d’Esgrignon family the most honorable of all the ancient houses in France.

Someday the present Marquis will have an income exceeding a hundred thousand crowns. You may see him in Paris; he comes every winter and leads a jolly bachelor life, treating his wife with more than the indifference of the grand seigneur of olden times—he takes no thought for her.

“As for Mlle. d’Esgrignon,” said Emile Blondet, source of these details, “if she is no longer like the divinely fair woman whom I saw by glimpses in my childhood, she is, at sixty-seven, the most pathetic and interesting figure in the Collection of Antiquities. She queens it among them still. I saw her on my last journey home to fetch the necessary papers for my marriage. When my father learned whom I had married he was struck dumb until I told him I was a prefect.

“‘You were born to it,’ he said, with a smile.

“As I took a walk around the town I met Mlle. Armande; she seemed taller than ever. I thought of Marius among the ruins of Carthage. Had she not outlived her creed and the beliefs that time destroyed? She is a sad and silent woman, with nothing of her old beauty left except her eyes, which shine with an unearthly light. I watched her on her way to mass, book in hand, and could not help thinking that she prayed to be taken out of the world.”

LES JARDIES, July 1837.
