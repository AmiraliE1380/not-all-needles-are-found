DEDICATION

To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to you a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins. He took in the mills along the river already at work, their noises in the crisp air, and the square’s linden trees, simple bourgeois houses, few signs of trade, and a couple of porte-cochères, one belonging to Monsieur Martener, a physician.

He recognized the house he sought: a newly restored white-stone building with gray blinds and yellow iron balconies. Three stone steps led to a fresh front door whose neatness contrasted with the worn exteriors nearby, bearing the complacent stamp of a retired petty shopkeeper. A shadow of sadness crossed his face. The rising sun lit a calico curtain at a garret window, and he sang a Breton ditty used for newlyweds, a song that conjured Brittany’s customs and the bittersweet turns of life.

A girl at the window signaled to him. He revealed a yellow broom-flower; they exchanged words. She was Pierrette, he Brigaut, her childhood companion. He spoke of wanting to settle near her, but she urged him to flee when a woman opened another window—an old maid, grotesque and unflattering, roused by the song.

The old maid searched for the source of the music but saw nothing. Known for sharp observations that spawned grave suppositions and domestic disturbances, she intensified the moment’s tension.

Inside, Pierrette, overcome by Brigaut’s visit, drifted into memory. She sat in a shabby armchair, pale and delicate from illness, recalling childhood joys now tinged by sorrow. Her mother, once Demoiselle Auffray, bore a complicated family past: a father who married twice and left tangled inheritance affairs. Her mother had married a Breton officer, Major Lorrain, who died in battle, leaving scant means. The Lorrains, Pierrette’s grandparents, suffered financial reverses and, after further misfortunes, could not provide for her. Pierrette was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins—people who, unaware of all her history, would soon become the masters of her fate and set the stage for the drama to come.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Pere Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, was a short, fat man with a ruddy face and a taste for indulgence. He and his equally unattractive wife were selfish and brutish, eking out a modest living from their land and the sale of their inn. After selling it they moved into the dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their two children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred, their features coarsened and voices harsh. Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops, following a doctrine of tough love and minimal support.

Sylvie became an apprentice on rue Saint-Denis, eventually winning independence and rising to forewoman; Jerome took a similar path and became a head-clerk. They met only occasionally, content with modest outings, and pooled their savings to buy a retail shop called the “Family Sister.” Though they had some success, fierce competition and their own harshness made them notorious for a lack of empathy.

Sylvie, not conventionally attractive, was shrewd and ran the business effectively; Jerome lacked the intellect to grasp broader trade concerns. Their lives revolved around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the world beyond it. Stinginess and poor treatment of clerks produced high turnover. Sylvie cultivated a dual persona: a pleasing saleswoman to customers and a sour spinster in private.

While mourning their father and weighing their future, the siblings received a letter from the Lorrains about their cousin Pierrette, who stood to inherit property and significant assets. They contemplated selling the business and returning to Provins for a comfortable life but saw Pierrette as a potential burden. Debating whether to take her in, they ultimately decided against it to preserve their wealth for themselves and descendants. Sylvie planned to write a refusal but was sidetracked by a business opportunity. The siblings left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival—an absence and series of choices as consequential for Pierrette as their earlier life in Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A petty shopkeeper returns to his provincial town from Paris with a few imported ideas that soon fade as he sinks back into provincial life. The shift from shopkeeper to bourgeois feels like an illness: the vibrancy of Paris gives way to provincial stillness. With new wealth, the siblings Rogron indulge long-held desires—travel, politics, hunting, fishing—and remodel their house to emulate Provins’s established bourgeoisie.

Their renovations are an expression of aspiration: the contractor consults them on every detail to match local elite tastes. The Rogrons’ first year is dominated by talk of the house and attempts to enter the town’s social fabric, seeking acceptance among the dominant families—the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées. Madame Tiphaine, the town belle, deftly maneuvers to elevate her husband to deputy, hosting gatherings that cement her influence and reveal the rules of provincial society.

The Rogrons struggle to fit in. Sylvie studies card games to participate in salons; Rogron remains awkward and silent. Their attempts to impress expose a lack of refinement and understanding of social norms. Lavish dinners intended to display their wealth instead isolate them: invitations are declined and they become objects of ridicule. Sylvie’s conviction that money guarantees acceptance collapses; she retreats into frugality, abandoning grand entertainments and sinking into ennui. The siblings reminisce about their business days and feel the weight of social exile, their resentment toward the elite growing.

In a bid to regain standing, Sylvie invites their cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping she will bridge them to the upper class. The plan backfires when townspeople discover Pierrette’s neglect, provoking further ostracism. Bitter and sidelined, the Rogrons pivot toward the lower bourgeoisie, aiming to forge a new social base.

Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, themselves marginalized, see opportunity in the Rogrons’ discontent. They plan to exploit the siblings’ wealth and social naiveté for political ends, positioning the Rogrons as a potential center for the town’s liberal opposition. Pierrette’s arrival becomes a catalyst: instead of restoring favor, she exposes the complexity of local power. The Rogrons’ journey illustrates that in provincial life, status and subtle codes often outweigh money; rivalries and entrenched hierarchies make social mobility precarious and influence difficult to buy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette, weary of living on charity, welcomed the news of wealthy relatives. Brigaut, a childhood friend and son of her mother’s friend, offered sixty francs for her trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, reflecting their close bond. Brigaut often visited to console her in difficult times.

In October 1824, after Pierrette turned eleven, she was handed to a diligence conductor for the journey to Paris. Brigaut ran after the coach, crying until he could no longer keep up. Arriving penniless, Pierrette had the conductor pay for her hotel and arrange a transfer to Provins, where she was treated like a package. Four days later a kinder conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s home, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with a small bundle of clothes, charming in her Breton attire. Sylvie and Rogron were initially taken aback and questioned her, but Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, said little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette. Sylvie immediately fretted over her lack of clothing and resolved to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and restraint. The warmth she had known was replaced by reprimands—her exploration of the house and garden the next morning earned her scolding for dirty shoes. Her cousins’ concerns centered on possessions, not her well-being. Sylvie and Rogron’s harshness contrasted sharply with the love she had received before, and Pierrette’s innocence made her acutely aware of their disdain. She began to suppress her natural joy.

The Rogrons fixated on her clothing, seeing her as a burden rather than kin. Their fault-finding and harsh reprimands stifled her spirit; once lively and carefree, she learned to be passive, fearing their scorn. When she accidentally tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, expecting stern rebukes.

Life in Provins grew harder. Sylvie forbade visits to friends, extinguishing the few pleasures left. The Rogrons’ obsession with appearances and conditional affection confined Pierrette to a cycle of fear and repression; their care came from self-interest rather than genuine love. The arrival of Vinet and the colonel intensified this: they encouraged Sylvie to oppose the local elite, drawing her into ambition and social positioning that deepened Pierrette’s isolation. As her cousins became consumed by these schemes, Pierrette was increasingly confined and scrutinized, transformed from a vibrant child into a shadow of her former self, struggling to endure her harsh new life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One evening, beginning a new phase of life with her wealthy cousins, Pierrette went to bid goodnight. Sylvie turned away coldly, making her cry. When asked, Pierrette said her poor grandmother had been kinder than her rich cousin. Sylvie dismissed her feelings, noting the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave some; Pierrette wanted affection more than wealth.

Sylvie accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch and ignoring orders—and the ashamed child left the room. Rogron talked of punishing her; Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school; the colonel thought teaching might keep her quiet. Vinet proposed hiring a writing master, calling Pierrette a rough diamond needing polishing.

The new teacher’s lessons brought chaos: ink stains, scattered papers, and blame placed on Pierrette. Harsh words followed—she must earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference intensified Pierrette’s isolation. Her curiosity and spirit were stifled; she began to feel ill, often complaining of pains her cousins dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; Pierrette’s innocent affection was read as manipulation. The colonel and Vinet supported the Rogrons’ harshness, blaming a stubborn Breton nature. Meanwhile Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper to boost their social standing, while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

She considered escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but two events prevented it: a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian. Vinet hinted at a possible inheritance; Rogron secured from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs after her death. These developments affected Pierrette, especially as preparations for her first communion altered household dynamics.

Sylvie insisted Pierrette be instructed by the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, whose influence proved profound. Pierrette found solace in faith and abandoned escape plans as she embraced the Church’s teachings. Sylvie, noticing Pierrette’s change, grew curious about Habert’s methods and adopted a new piety herself.

Monsieur Habert’s sister, Mademoiselle Habert, befriended Sylvie; they grew close. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette into her school, but the Rogrons refused, saying they would miss her. The colonel and Vinet, uneasy about the priest’s influence, feared he might press for a convent education.

As political and matrimonial intrigues unfolded, Vinet sought to strengthen his position by bringing in Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf—impoverished but socially useful. Their arrival bolstered the liberal faction and alarmed Provins’ aristocrats. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, beautiful and charming, quickly attracted Rogron; Sylvie began to see Pierrette as an obstacle to her own marriage prospects.

Sylvie decided Pierrette should learn domestic skills and effectively made her a servant. Losing her only protector, Adèle, Pierrette accepted the role. Treated with less regard than a servant and scolded for trifles, she strove to please but received no praise. Over the years her health deteriorated under relentless pressure and lack of affection—severe internal pains and stunted growth reduced the once vibrant child to a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role in the events.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing change. He scarcely recognized her; her once vibrant spirit had given way to illness and sorrow. From a high point in the Upper town he looked mournfully at Pierrette’s home, consumed by questions about her well‑being and the depth of his feelings. Their bond, rare between children of opposite sexes, was profound. Brigaut had resolved to improve his prospects for her, learning a trade and moving to Paris with hopes of success so he could support her. But after a fortnight in the city he returned to Provins, quickly finding work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette.

He buried his feelings while trying to learn more about the Rogrons from Madame Frappier, who disclosed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette at the market, where her frailty distressed him. Sundays at church brought them close, though she signaled him to remain hidden, sensing danger. Determined to discover the cause of her pallor and weakness, he feared for her health.

Pierrette, meanwhile, fretted about the changes since Brigaut’s arrival but was unaware of the Rogrons’ internal strife, particularly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for marriage. Sylvie, forty‑two, felt her age as a threat and dreaded dying alone, while Céleste, ten years younger and encouraged by her brother, a priest, pursued marriage. The political shift with the rise of the Martignac ministry emboldened Liberals in Provins and intensified competition for social standing.

Sylvie’s anxiety drove her to consult a doctor about the risks of marrying at her age; the discouraging prognosis heightened her fear. Céleste, seeing an opening, urged Sylvie to delay, hoping to remove a rival. Sylvie’s inner turmoil deepened, and jealousy flared when she noticed the colonel’s growing interest in Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, recognized Sylvie’s vulnerability and schemed to exploit it, intending to wedge the colonel away from Pierrette and secure the Rogron fortune for Bathilde.

Vinet approached the colonel, urging him to court Pierrette rather than Sylvie. He painted a rosy picture of happiness with Pierrette, manipulating the colonel’s feelings while secretly plotting to advance Bathilde’s claim. The colonel, who had shown Pierrette an uneasy mix of affection and reserve, began to seek her out more often, which inflamed Sylvie’s jealousy.

Sylvie’s suspicions sharpened when she overheard Brigaut serenading Pierrette, and she concluded the colonel was involved with the girl. Her jealousy clouded her judgment and made her increasingly paranoid about losing the colonel. This obsession consumed her, warping her perception and driving her to confront Pierrette.

On a tense morning Sylvie accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer, mocking her innocence and demanding answers. Pierrette, frightened and bewildered, remained silent, which only intensified Sylvie’s suspicions. Sylvie’s jealousy turned into a desire for control; she threatened Pierrette with severe consequences if she did not confess.

As Pierrette endured the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household, her health continued to decline, though she remained unaware of its full severity. Her innocence sheltered her from some harsh realities, but emotional strain and heavy labor eroded her strength. Despite her suffering, Brigaut’s presence offered brief comfort and hope that eased her pain.

Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny and bitterness intensified the household’s cruelty, leaving Pierrette isolated and desperate. The old maid’s suspicion escalated into open hostility and confrontation, further undermining Pierrette’s well‑being. As tensions peaked, Pierrette’s condition worsened and she longed for escape from her torment.

Amid this turmoil, love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for a tragic sequence that would affect everyone involved. The struggle for power, affection, and security within the Rogron household threatened devastating consequences for Pierrette, whose innocence and vulnerability made her an easy target in a world rife with envy and desire.

For a month Rogron had stopped delivering the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, who came for it himself and often lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see the colonel, Sylvie dressed in her finest. When Gouraud arrived, Rogron excused himself, leaving Sylvie alone with the colonel.

Noting her solemnity, Gouraud asked about her cousin's health; she said the cousin was unwell. The colonel, uneasy about Vinet's influence over the Rogrons, felt marginalised. Vinet seemed to dominate their political plans, while Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, feared being sidelined. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which threatened his prospects.

Gouraud mentioned an interest in Bathilde, making Rogron pale at the idea of competition. The colonel suspected Vinet might be manoeuvring him into abandoning Sylvie for Pierrette as a ploy to benefit Vinet and Bathilde.

Left alone with Sylvie, Gouraud observed her unease and suspected Vinet's meddling. Sylvie, testing his feelings, raised Pierrette; the colonel dismissed any affection for her, saying he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and suggested her brother had considered Pierrette as a match.

Sensing Sylvie's interest, Gouraud declared his intention to marry her, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their future home but remained wary of Pierrette's interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared, Sylvie scolded Pierrette over a stain on her dress, blaming her for the mishap. Despite her harshness she ordered Pierrette to dress nicely for the evening, suspecting collusion with Gouraud.

The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde was spectacularly dressed. Under Vinet's guidance, Bathilde planned to use a marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, treating him as a means rather than a partner. Vinet had instilled in her a hunger for independence and influence, urging her to exploit beauty and connections.

The evening grew tense. Bathilde's condescension toward Pierrette was obvious, and Vinet's remarks heightened the rivalry. They played cards; Sylvie tried to manipulate the game, while Pierrette's innocent comments provoked Sylvie's ire.

When Pierrette left the room in distress, Sylvie's anger flared and she resolved to make her pay. The evening ended with Sylvie frustrated and feeling betrayed. The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall, which Sylvie blamed on disobedience, showing no sympathy. Rogron suggested caring for Pierrette, but Sylvie dismissed the idea, revealing her coldness and the widening rift between the cousins.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended in relentless torment for Pierrette, who suffered Sylvie’s growing cruelty. She kept Brigaut’s arrival secret to avoid further anger, clinging instead to the memory of him; his presence felt vital, and even a glimpse seemed a lifeline.

Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged to exchange letters. One morning he slipped a note to her while pretending to buy fruit. The exchange unlocked deep feelings in both, far beyond ordinary affection. Pierrette returned home with his letter, which that night revealed his longing, a wish to flee their oppressive situation, and a request that she lower a string from her window so they could pass notes each night. Moved, Pierrette resolved to reply despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie. She poured out her heart, confessed her love, and described the suffering inflicted by her relatives.

At midnight she sent her letter down to Brigaut, who awaited it eagerly. Their correspondence continued—full of hope and plans—despite Sylvie’s latent jealousy. Observing Pierrette’s newfound joy, Sylvie grew watchful and suspicious.

Under Sylvie’s oppressive scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined; one evening she fainted, prompting concern from the colonel and others. Sylvie dismissed the illness as a ruse, but Pierrette’s condition worsened. In desperation Pierrette cried for help, triggering a violent confrontation as Sylvie tried to seize her letters. At the height of Sylvie’s fury, Pierrette’s grandmother arrived, witnessed the struggle, and rushed to her granddaughter’s aid.

Her arrival brought relief after days on the brink of despair. Confronted, Sylvie seemed confused and frightened. The grandmother, determined to protect Pierrette, sought medical help, fearing for her life.

Meanwhile Brigaut wrote to Madame Lorrain urging her to come and rescue Pierrette, stressing the urgency—he feared for her health and safety. Receiving his appeals, the grandmother felt both joy and dread and immediately traveled to Provins.

She found Pierrette distressed and traumatized, bearing physical and emotional wounds; the sight of her injured hand—caused by Sylvie’s violent attempt to control her—filled the grandmother with anguish. She vowed to secure justice and ensure Pierrette’s safety.

As Pierrette recovered, her bond with her grandmother deepened, strengthening her for what lay ahead. Medical attention and the promise of a better life with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a glimmer of hope amid recent darkness. The struggle for freedom and love continued, with Pierrette determined to reclaim her life from oppressive relatives.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine that morning Monsieur Martener went to Monsieur Tiphaine to report the mistreatment Pierrette had suffered at the Rogrons’ hands. Tiphaine summoned Auffray, a notary and relative of Pierrette. Tensions between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions — already inflamed by damaging revelations about Madame Tiphaine’s family — linked personal grievances to local political strife.

Tiphaine saw an opportunity to damage the Rogrons, influential monarchist opponents. After consulting the public prosecutor, he, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian, enabling a Family Council—supported by medical testimony—to remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries the night before and speculation spread. Doctor Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed the Rogrons’ abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, rushed to warn of possible criminal charges. Sylvie, initially evasive, eventually confessed her jealousy and the truth of the incident, revealing the gravity of their situation.

Exploiting their fear, Vinet suggested a marriage announcement might quiet the scandal. Pressured, the Rogrons agreed to a marriage contract that suited their political interests. Vinet then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, seeking to recast the Rogrons as victims.

The court hearing was postponed, fueling public interest. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who remained unaware of the turmoil and was placed in the care of her grandmother and Brigaut, who tended her recovery. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

When the court reconvened, Auffray presented physicians’ reports arguing for Pierrette’s protection. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered that Pierrette be kept away from the Rogrons until a Family Council could convene—a notable public rebuke.

The Family Council of relatives and allies voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray. Despite Vinet’s lengthy defense, the evidence of neglect and abuse led to a civil suit.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; her grandmother’s care and Brigaut’s visits provided comfort. In March 1828 a critical operation was performed, but recovery was unlikely. Pierrette, declining, expressed a wish to forgive her cousins; she died soon after. Her funeral drew the town, though the Rogrons, fearing public outrage, stayed away.

Grief-stricken, Brigaut made her coffin. The Rogrons tried to exploit the situation legally, but community support for Pierrette blocked their efforts. Vinet, defending them, faced backlash as the truth of the abuse became more known.

Afterward Brigaut left Provins for military service. Vinet and the Rogrons nevertheless continued to prosper politically; the scandal did not wholly destroy their reputations. The story of Pierrette’s fate became distorted, with many believing the Rogrons had acted nobly. Only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like-minded people—remembered the reality of her suffering. The account closes reflecting on justice, accountability, and the lasting impact of Pierrette’s life and death on those who truly understood her plight.
