To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate such a melancholy story to you? Yet sorrows should be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them. It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, looking at working mills and simple bourgeois houses framed by linden trees. He recognized his house: a recently restored white-stone dwelling with gray blinds and yellow iron balconies—its neatness betraying the pride of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As the sun lit a calico curtain in a garret window, the boy began a Breton ditty for newlyweds, stirring memories of Brittany and life’s bittersweet turns. A girl, Pierrette, signaled from the window; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, and he spoke of settling nearby. Pierrette, alarmed, urged him to flee when a grotesque old maid below—sharp-eyed and prone to imagining scandal—appeared at a window.

Overwhelmed by Brigaut’s visit, Pierrette reflected on her past. Seated in a shabby armchair, frail and thoughtful, she recalled childhood joys tinged with illness and uncertainty. Her mother, a Demoiselle Auffray from a tangled family—her father had married twice and left a complicated inheritance—later married Major Lorrain, a Breton officer who died in battle, leaving the family impoverished; the Lorrains could not care for Pierrette.

Ultimately she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Unaware of the full intricacies of her lineage and her parents’ fortunes and losses, Pierrette's fate becomes increasingly shaped by these relatives. The Rogrons’ control sets the stage for the drama to follow.

Père Rogron, an indulgent, ruddy-faced innkeeper in Provins, and his unattractive, selfish wife scraped by on modest returns from their land and inn before moving into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred, with coarsened features and harsh voices; Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops with little guidance.

Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis and became a forewoman; Jérôme rose to head-clerk. They met rarely and pooled savings to buy the retail “Family Sister.” They found some success, but their severity and competitiveness made them notorious. Sylvie, shrewd though plain, ran the business effectively; Jérôme was unsuited to broader trade. Their lives revolved around the shop, leaving them oblivious to life beyond it. Their stinginess and poor treatment caused high clerk turnover. To customers Sylvie was pleasant; at home she was a sour spinster.

While mourning their father, they learned of cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. They thought of selling and returning to Provins but refused to take Pierrette in to protect their wealth, delayed replying, and left for Provins four years before Brigaut arrived.

Back in Provins, their Parisian airs soon faded as provincial life reasserted itself. The shift to bourgeois life felt like an illness: Parisian vibrancy yielded to local stillness. With newfound means, the Rogrons indulged travel, politics, hunting, and remodeled their house to mimic wealthier families. Their renovations advertised ambition; the contractor even consulted them on elite details. Their first year was consumed by talk of the house and efforts to join the town’s social circle led by the established Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées. Madame Tiphaine, the local belle, advanced her husband toward deputyship by hosting influential gatherings. The Rogrons lacked polish: Sylvie studied card games while Rogron remained awkward and silent.

Lavish dinners failed to win acceptance; invitations dwindled and ridicule spread. Sylvie’s faith that money guaranteed status collapsed; she went frugal, abandoned entertainments, and the siblings sank into ennui, nostalgic for business days and resentful of isolation. Bringing cousin Pierrette Lorrain backfired as neglect became public. Bitter and sidelined, the Rogrons courted lower bourgeois figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, who exploited their wealth and naiveté. The story shows provincial hierarchies and subtle social codes ultimately trump money.

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—was relieved when wealthier relatives were found. Brigaut, a childhood friend and her mother’s friend’s son, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, a sign of their close bond. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel and treated her like a package; four days later a kinder conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with a small bundle, dressed in Breton clothes. Sylvie and Rogron were startled and questioned her, but Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette, and Sylvie immediately fretted over her lack of clothing, resolving to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and criticism. Exploring the house and garden the next morning, she was reproved for dirty shoes. Her cousins’ concern for possessions over her well‑being and their constant fault‑finding sharply contrasted with the love she’d known. Pierrette’s innocence made their disdain more painful; she began to suppress her joy and became passive for fear of scorn.

The Rogrons obsessed over her appearance, treating her as a burden and stifling her spirit. When she tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, expecting stern rebukes. Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions encouraged by Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a shadow of her former self, enduring harsh, self‑interested care that was not love.

One night, when Pierrette went to say goodnight, Sylvie turned away coldly, making her cry. Asked why, Pierrette said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted that the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth.

Sylvie then accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch, disobeying orders—and Pierrette left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment; Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school; the colonel thought instruction might keep her quiet. Vinet called Pierrette a rough diamond needing polishing and a writing master was hired. His lessons produced ink stains and scattered papers, blamed on Pierrette, and she was sharply told she must earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; her curiosity and spirit were stifled and she began to feel ill, complaining of pains the cousins dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; the colonel and Vinet attributed Pierrette’s behavior to a stubborn Breton nature. Meanwhile Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper to boost their standing, while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

She considered escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian prevented it. Vinet hinted at an inheritance; Rogron secured from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs upon the old woman’s death. Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion shifted household dynamics. Sylvie insisted on the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, who profoundly influenced Pierrette. Finding solace in faith, she abandoned escape plans and embraced the Church’s teachings, a change that even made Sylvie adopt new piety.

Habert’s sister offered to take Pierrette to her school, but the Rogrons refused, fearing a convent education. Later Vinet introduced the Chargeboeufs; Bathilde attracted Rogron, and Sylvie forced Pierrette into domestic tasks. Without Adèle’s protection she was treated worse than staff—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded into a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant but passive role in the events.

Brigaut had fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing change; her once-vibrant spirit had grown pale and sorrowful. From a high point in the Upper town he watched her home, anxious for her welfare and his feelings. Their rare bond led him briefly to Paris to learn a trade; he returned to Provins and worked as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could remain near Pierrette.

He buried his feelings while learning more about the Rogrons. Madame Frappier revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. Brigaut often saw Pierrette at the market, where her frailty distressed him; Sundays at church brought them near, though she signaled him to remain hidden, sensing danger. Determined to learn the cause of her pallor, he feared for her health.

Pierrette, meanwhile, fretted about changes since Brigaut’s return but was unaware of the Rogrons’ internal strife, especially between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for advantageous marriages. Sylvie, forty-two, feared growing old alone; Céleste, ten years younger and encouraged by her brother the priest, actively sought a husband. A recent political shift had emboldened local Liberals and intensified competition for social standing in Provins.

Sylvie’s anxiety led her to consult a doctor about marrying at her age; his discouraging prognosis heightened her fear. Céleste, seeing an opening, urged delay to remove a rival. Sylvie grew jealous when she noticed the colonel’s increasing attention to Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, exploited Sylvie’s vulnerability: he plotted to separate the colonel from Pierrette and divert the Rogron fortune toward Bathilde.

Vinet persuaded the colonel to court Pierrette, painting a picture of happiness while secretly maneuvering to benefit Bathilde. The colonel, a mix of affection and reserve, began seeking Pierrette more, which inflamed Sylvie’s fears. Her suspicions sharpened after overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette; she concluded the colonel was involved. One tense morning she accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer, mocked her innocence, and demanded answers. Pierrette, frightened and bewildered, stayed silent, which intensified Sylvie’s wrath and threats.

As Pierrette endured the oppressive Rogron household, her health continued to decline. Her innocence sheltered her from some realities, but emotional strain and heavy labor eroded her strength. Brigaut’s presence offered brief comfort, yet Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny deepened Pierrette’s isolation. Amid this turmoil, love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for a tragic sequence that would affect everyone involved.

For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, so Gouraud came himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see him, Sylvie dressed her best; when Gouraud arrived Rogron excused himself, leaving them alone.

Gouraud asked after her cousin; Sylvie said the cousin was unwell. He grew uneasy: Vinet’s rising influence with the Rogrons made him feel sidelined. A half-pay officer, he feared Vinet controlled their political plans and that Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf threatened his prospects. He even suspected Vinet might try to push him toward Pierrette as a ploy to favor Vinet and Bathilde.

Alone with Sylvie he sensed her unease; when she tentatively mentioned Pierrette he dismissed any affection for the girl, saying he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and hinted her brother had once considered Pierrette a match. Sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home but remained wary of Pierrette’s interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain but ordered her to dress nicely—suspecting collusion. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde, spectacularly dressed under Vinet’s guidance, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence.

The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent comments provoked Sylvie, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity and her precarious position. The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed disobedience and showed no sympathy, widening the rift between the cousins.

The week that followed was relentless torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. She concealed Brigaut’s arrival to avoid anger, but her longing and fear for him endured. Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged a nightly note exchange: one morning he slipped a message while pretending to buy fruit and asked her to lower a string from her window.

Pierrette returned with his heartfelt letter, full of longing and plans to escape. Despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie, she resolved to reply, pouring out her love and describing her relatives’ abuse. At midnight she sent the letter down; their correspondence continued, hopeful and intimate, while Sylvie’s jealousy and vigilance grew.

Under Sylvie’s scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined; one evening she fainted, drawing concern from the colonel and others, though Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse. Desperate, Pierrette cried for help, provoking a violent struggle when Sylvie tried to seize her letters. Pierrette was injured and left distressed.

At the height of Sylvie’s fury Madame Lorrain, Pierrette’s grandmother, arrived after Brigaut’s urgent appeals. She witnessed the violence, rushed to Pierrette’s aid, and sought medical care. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened; the promise of protection and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. Pierrette remained determined to reclaim her life.

At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary relative, and longstanding enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—inflamed by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political force.

Seeing an opportunity to weaken the influential royalist Rogrons, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed after neighbors heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Exploiting fear, Vinet proposed a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal. Under pressure the Rogrons signed a marriage contract that also served their political aims. Vinet then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, trying to recast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed, heightening interest. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who, unaware of the turmoil, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

At the reconvened hearing, Auffray presented the physicians’ report to justify protecting Pierrette. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council could meet—a public rebuke. The Council of relatives and allies voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray. Despite Vinet’s defense, the evidence produced a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of her; her grandmother’s care and Brigaut’s visits comforted her. A critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope; she forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Brigaut made her coffin. Though community support hampered the Rogrons’ maneuvers and Vinet faced backlash, they largely retained political standing; only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like‑minded friends—remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on questions of justice, accountability, and the lasting impact of her life and death.
