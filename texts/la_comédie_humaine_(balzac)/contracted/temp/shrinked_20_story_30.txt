PIERRETTE

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,--You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to _you_ a story full of melancholy? Yet, should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827, a sixteen-year-old boy, dressed in what modern terms call proletarian clothing, stood in a small square of Lower Provins. He observed the houses around him, noting the mills along the river already at work, their sounds echoing in the crisp air. The square, bordered by linden trees, featured simple bourgeois architecture, with few signs of commerce and only a couple of luxurious porte-cocheres, one belonging to Monsieur Martener, a physician.

The young man identified the house he sought, a newly restored white stone structure with gray blinds and yellow-painted iron balconies. The front door was reached by three stone steps, and the house's fresh appearance contrasted with the timeworn exteriors of its neighbors, reflecting the self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As he admired the details, a hint of sadness crossed his face. The rising sun illuminated a calico curtain at one of the garret windows, and he sang a Breton ditty, a song traditionally sung to newlyweds, evoking memories of Brittany's customs and the bittersweet nature of life.

As he sang, a girl appeared at the window, signaling him. He revealed a yellow flower, the broom, and they exchanged words. She was Pierrette, and he was Brigaut, her childhood companion. He expressed his desire to settle near her, but she urged him to flee as a woman opened a window below, revealing an old maid who had been awakened by his song.

The old maid, a grotesque figure, searched for the source of the music but saw nothing. Her appearance was unflattering, and she was known for her keen observations, which often led to grave suppositions and domestic dramas.

Pierrette, overwhelmed by Brigaut's visit, reflected on her past, recalling her childhood memories and the joy they brought her. She sat in a shabby armchair, lost in thought, her delicate appearance marred by illness. Her mother, a Demoiselle Auffray, had a complicated family history, with her father marrying twice and leaving behind a tangled inheritance.

Pierrette's mother married a Breton officer, Major Lorrain, who died in battle, leaving her with little means. The Lorrains, Pierrette's grandparents, struggled financially, and after a series of misfortunes, they were unable to care for her. Pierrette was eventually sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins, who became the unexpected masters of her fate.

The Rogrons, unaware of the complexities of Pierrette's past, would soon play a significant role in her life, setting the stage for the unfolding drama of her existence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Pere Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, was a short, fat man with a ruddy face and a penchant for indulgence. He and his equally unattractive wife were selfish and brutish, living off the modest income from their land and the sale of their inn. After selling the inn, they moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. They had two unattractive children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis, who were poorly raised and neglected, leading to their coarsened features and harsh voices. Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops, believing in tough love and minimal support.

Sylvie became an apprentice in a shop on rue Saint-Denis, eventually earning her independence and rising to a forewoman position. Jerome followed a similar path, becoming a head-clerk. The siblings met occasionally, enjoying modest outings and pooling their savings to buy a retail shop, the “Family Sister.” Despite their success, they struggled against fierce competition and were known for their harshness and lack of empathy in business.

Sylvie, though not conventionally attractive, was shrewd and managed the business effectively, while Jerome lacked the intellect to grasp the broader aspects of their trade. Their lives revolved around their shop, and they were oblivious to the world beyond it. They were known for their stinginess and poor treatment of employees, leading to a high turnover of clerks.

Sylvie had developed a dual persona: a charming saleswoman and a sour spinster. When they received a letter from the Lorrains regarding their cousin Pierrette, they were in mourning for their father and contemplating their future. They inherited property and had significant assets, which led them to consider selling their business for a comfortable life in Provins.

However, they viewed Pierrette as a potential burden and debated the implications of taking her in. Ultimately, they decided against it, prioritizing their own interests and the prospect of keeping their wealth intact for future generations. Sylvie intended to write a refusal but got sidetracked by a business opportunity, leading to their departure for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival, which would significantly impact Pierrette’s life. Their actions in Provins would prove just as consequential for Pierrette as their previous life in Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When a petty shopkeeper returns to his provincial town from Paris, he brings back a few ideas that quickly fade as he immerses himself in provincial life. This transition from shopkeeper to bourgeois often feels like an illness, as the vibrancy of Parisian life is replaced by the stillness of the provinces. With their newfound wealth, these individuals often indulge in long-held desires, whether through travel, political engagement, or hobbies like hunting or fishing. The Rogrons, a brother and sister duo, exemplify this as they remodel their old house into a charming new one, driven by a desire to emulate the wealthier bourgeois of Provins.

The renovations of their home reflect their aspirations, with the contractor meticulously consulting them on every detail, ensuring that their choices align with the tastes of the local elite. The Rogrons' first year back in Provins is consumed by discussions about their home and attempts to integrate into the social fabric of the town. They seek acceptance among the established families, including the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenees, who dominate the local social scene.

Madame Tiphaine, the belle of the town, is particularly ambitious for her husband, hoping to elevate him to a deputy position. She skillfully navigates social dynamics, hosting gatherings that solidify her influence. The Rogrons, however, struggle to fit in. Sylvie, the sister, attempts to learn card games to engage socially, while Rogron remains awkward and silent. Their efforts to impress the local elite are met with disdain, as their lack of refinement and understanding of social norms becomes apparent.

Despite their attempts to host lavish dinners to showcase their wealth, the Rogrons find themselves increasingly isolated. Their invitations are often declined, and they become the subject of ridicule among the townspeople. Sylvie's ambition to establish a social standing leads to frustration as they realize that wealth alone does not guarantee acceptance.

As their social aspirations crumble, Sylvie turns to frugality, dismissing their extravagant dinners and opting for a simpler lifestyle. The brother and sister find themselves in a state of ennui, reminiscing about their past business and feeling the weight of their isolation. Their attempts to engage with the community yield little satisfaction, and they become increasingly resentful of the social elite.

In a bid to regain their social standing, Sylvie decides to invite her cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping to use her as a means to connect with the upper class. However, their plans are thwarted when the townspeople learn of their neglect towards Pierrette, leading to further ostracism. The Rogrons' bitterness towards the local elite grows, and they begin to align themselves with the lower bourgeoisie, seeking to establish a new social order.

Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, both marginalized figures in Provins, see an opportunity in the Rogrons' discontent. They aim to leverage the Rogrons' wealth and ignorance to further their own political ambitions. As the Rogrons become more entrenched in their resentment, they unwittingly position themselves as a potential center for the liberal opposition in the town.

The arrival of Pierrette becomes a catalyst for the Rogrons' ambitions, as they hope to use her to regain favor in society. However, the dynamics of power and social standing in Provins are complex, and the Rogrons must navigate a treacherous landscape filled with rivalries and ambitions. Their journey reflects the challenges of social mobility and the often harsh realities of provincial life, where wealth does not always translate to acceptance or influence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents entered a hospital, Pierrette, feeling the weight of living on charity, was relieved to learn of her wealthy relatives. Brigaut, her childhood friend and the son of her mother’s friend, offered her sixty francs for her journey to Paris, which she accepted without thanks, reflecting their deep bond. Brigaut often visited her, trying to console her during her difficult times.

In October 1824, after Pierrette turned eleven, she was entrusted to the diligence conductor for her journey to Paris. Brigaut ran after the coach, tears in his eyes, until he could no longer keep up. Upon arriving in Paris, Pierrette had no money, but the conductor paid for her hotel and arranged for her transfer to Provins, treating her like a package. Four days later, a kind conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s home, where her cousins greeted her with astonishment.

Pierrette arrived with only a small bundle of clothes, looking charming in her Breton attire. Sylvie and Rogron, her cousins, were initially taken aback by her appearance. They asked her questions, but Pierrette was shy and overwhelmed. Sylvie and the maid, Adele, took her to her new room, which was modestly furnished but seemed luxurious to Pierrette. Sylvie expressed concern over Pierrette’s lack of clothing and decided they needed to buy her a new wardrobe.

Pierrette, a child of love, had previously known only affection and freedom in Brittany. However, her new life with the Rogrons was starkly different. The warmth she had known was replaced by the coldness of her relatives, leaving her feeling lost and confused. The next morning, she awoke early, exploring the house and garden, but was soon reprimanded by Rogron for her dirty shoes.

As Pierrette settled into her new life, she faced constant criticism from her cousins, who were more concerned about their possessions than her well-being. Sylvie and Rogron’s harshness contrasted sharply with the love she had received in Brittany. Pierrette’s innocence and sensitivity made her acutely aware of their disdain, leading her to suppress her natural joy.

The Rogrons became fixated on Pierrette’s clothing, viewing her as a burden rather than a beloved family member. Their constant fault-finding and harsh reprimands stifled her spirit. Pierrette, who had once been lively and carefree, learned to be passive in their presence, fearing their scorn. When she accidentally tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, knowing the reprimands that awaited her.

As time passed, Pierrette’s life in Provins became increasingly difficult. Sylvie’s refusal to let her visit friends marked the end of any joy she had experienced. The Rogrons’ obsession with appearances and their harsh treatment of Pierrette turned her life into a cycle of fear and repression. They saw her as a means to fill their lives with purpose, but their love was conditional and rooted in self-interest.

The arrival of Vinet and the colonel introduced new dynamics. They encouraged Sylvie to form an opposition to the local social elite, further isolating Pierrette. As her cousins became more consumed by their ambitions, Pierrette was increasingly confined to the house, subjected to constant scrutiny and criticism. The once vibrant child was transformed into a shadow of her former self, struggling to navigate the harsh realities of her new life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One evening, marking a new phase in Pierrette’s life with her wealthy cousins, she entered the room to bid goodnight. Sylvie coldly turned her cheek away, causing Pierrette to tear up. When questioned, she expressed that her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother had been. Sylvie dismissed her feelings, stating that her grandmother had taken her money, while her cousin would leave her hers. Pierrette, feeling unloved, wished for affection over wealth.

Sylvie accused Pierrette of mischief, claiming she broke her watch and ignored instructions. Pierrette, ashamed, left the room. Rogron, her cousin, expressed a desire to discipline her, while Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school. The colonel chimed in, suggesting that teaching might keep Pierrette quiet. Vinet proposed hiring a schoolmaster, emphasizing that Pierrette was a rough diamond needing polishing.

As discussions continued, Pierrette was given a writing master, leading to chaos in the Rogron household. Ink stains and scattered materials were blamed on her, and she was harshly told she must earn her keep. Pierrette suffered under Rogron’s strictness and Sylvie’s indifference, leading to her feeling increasingly isolated and misunderstood. Her natural curiosity and spirit were stifled, and she began to feel ill, often complaining of pains that were dismissed by her cousins.

The only kindness came from Adele, the servant, who secretly warmed Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children, while Pierrette’s innocent affection was seen as manipulation. The colonel and Vinet supported the Rogrons’ harsh treatment, attributing Pierrette’s behavior to her stubborn Breton nature. Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper, which began to elevate their social standing, while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

As time passed, Pierrette contemplated escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but two events—a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian—prevented her. Vinet hinted at the potential inheritance from Pierrette, leading Rogron to secure her grandmother’s promise of the eight thousand francs upon her death. Pierrette was deeply affected by these developments, especially as she prepared for her first communion, which brought significant changes in the household.

Sylvie insisted Pierrette be taught by a zealous priest, Monsieur Habert, who profoundly influenced her. Pierrette found solace in faith, abandoning thoughts of escape as she embraced the teachings of the Church. Sylvie, noticing Pierrette’s transformation, became curious about the priest’s methods, leading to a newfound piety in her as well.

Meanwhile, the priest’s sister, Mademoiselle Habert, befriended Sylvie, and the two women grew close. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette into her school, but the Rogrons insisted on keeping her, claiming they would miss her. The colonel and Vinet, sensing a threat from the priest’s influence, worried that he might push for a convent education for Pierrette.

As political and matrimonial intrigues unfolded, Vinet sought to strengthen his position by bringing in Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, who were impoverished but socially advantageous. Their arrival bolstered the liberal faction and alarmed the aristocrats of Provins. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, beautiful and charming, quickly became a rival for Rogron’s affections, while Sylvie began to see Pierrette as an obstacle to her own marriage prospects.

Sylvie decided to make Pierrette a servant, believing she should learn domestic skills. Pierrette willingly accepted the role, losing her only protector, Adele. Her cousins treated her with less regard than a servant, scolding her for trivial matters. Despite her efforts to please them, she received no praise, only criticism, which deepened her sense of isolation and despair.

As the years passed, Pierrette’s health deteriorated under the relentless pressure and lack of affection from her cousins. She experienced severe internal pains, and her natural growth was stunted. The once vibrant child became a shadow of her former self, suffering in silence as her cousins remained oblivious to her plight.

Before recounting the domestic drama that Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is essential to explain how he came to be in Provins, as he plays a significant yet passive role in the unfolding events.

Brigaut fled from the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing transformation. He barely recognized her; her once vibrant spirit was now replaced by illness and sorrow. As he climbed to a vantage point in the Upper town, he gazed mournfully at Pierrette’s home, consumed by questions about her well-being and the depth of his feelings for her. Their bond, though rare for children of opposite sexes, was profound. Brigaut had dedicated himself to improving his life for her sake, learning his trade and moving to Paris with dreams of success to support her. However, after a fortnight in the city, he felt compelled to return to Provins, where he quickly secured a position as a journeyman cabinetmaker, allowing him to stay close to Pierrette.

Brigaut buried his feelings, seeking information about the Rogrons from Madame Frappier, who revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette in the market, where her frail state troubled him. Sundays at church brought him closer to her, but he sensed her need for caution, especially after she signaled him to stay hidden. He was determined to uncover the cause of her pallor and weakness, fearing for her health.

Meanwhile, Pierrette was anxious about the changes in her life since Brigaut’s arrival. She was unaware of the turmoil brewing among the Rogrons, particularly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert, both of whom were vying for marriage. Sylvie, at forty-two, felt the pressure of her age and the fear of dying alone, while Celeste, ten years younger, was encouraged by her brother, a priest, to pursue marriage. The political climate shifted with the rise of the Martignac ministry, emboldening the Liberals in Provins and creating a competitive atmosphere for social standing.

Sylvie’s fears led her to consult a doctor about the risks of marriage at her age, receiving discouraging news that only heightened her anxiety. Celeste, sensing an opportunity, advised Sylvie to wait, hoping to eliminate her rival. Sylvie’s internal struggles intensified, and her jealousy flared when she noticed the colonel’s growing interest in Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with ambitions of marrying Bathilde, saw Sylvie’s vulnerability and plotted to use it to his advantage, aiming to drive a wedge between the colonel and Pierrette.

As tensions escalated, Vinet approached the colonel, suggesting he pursue Pierrette instead of Sylvie. He painted a picture of happiness with Pierrette, manipulating the colonel’s feelings while secretly planning to secure the Rogron fortune for Bathilde. The colonel, who had been treating Pierrette with a mix of affection and aloofness, began to seek her out more frequently, further igniting Sylvie’s jealousy.

Sylvie’s suspicions deepened when she overheard Brigaut serenading Pierrette, leading her to believe that the colonel was involved with the girl. Her jealousy clouded her judgment, and she became increasingly paranoid about losing the colonel to Pierrette. This obsession consumed her, distorting her perception of reality and driving her to confront Pierrette.

On a morning filled with tension, Sylvie accused Pierrette of having a secret admirer, mocking her innocence and demanding answers. Pierrette, confused and frightened, remained silent, which only fueled Sylvie’s suspicions. Sylvie’s jealousy morphed into a desire to control Pierrette, leading her to threaten the girl with severe consequences if she did not confess.

As Pierrette navigated the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household, she struggled with her health, unaware of the severity of her condition. Her innocence and naivety shielded her from the harsh realities of her situation, but the emotional turmoil and physical labor took a toll on her well-being. Despite her suffering, the presence of Brigaut brought her a flicker of hope and joy, momentarily alleviating her pain.

However, Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny and jealousy created an unbearable environment for Pierrette. The old maid’s bitterness and suspicion only intensified, leading to a confrontation that left Pierrette feeling isolated and desperate. As the tension reached a breaking point, Pierrette’s health deteriorated, and she found herself wishing for escape from her torment.

In the midst of this turmoil, the dynamics of love, jealousy, and ambition intertwined, setting the stage for a tragic unfolding of events that would ultimately impact all involved. The struggle for power, affection, and security within the Rogron household would lead to devastating consequences for Pierrette, whose innocence and vulnerability made her a target in a world rife with envy and desire.

For a month, Rogron had stopped delivering the “Constitutionnel” to Gouraud, who now came to fetch it himself, often engaging in idle gossip and walks with Sylvie. Sylvie, eager to see the colonel, dressed in her most eye-catching attire. When Gouraud arrived, Rogron excused himself, leaving Sylvie alone with the colonel. 

Gouraud, noticing Sylvie's solemn demeanor, inquired about her cousin's health, to which Sylvie replied she was unwell. The colonel, wary of Vinet's influence over the Rogrons, felt uneasy about his position. Vinet seemed to dominate their political endeavors, while Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, felt sidelined. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which made him anxious about his own prospects.

During their conversation, Gouraud mentioned his interest in Bathilde, causing Rogron to pale at the thought of competition. The colonel realized that Vinet might be trying to manipulate him into abandoning Sylvie for Pierrette, which he suspected was a ploy to benefit Vinet and Bathilde. 

When left alone with Sylvie, Gouraud noticed her unease and suspected Vinet's involvement. Sylvie, trying to gauge his feelings, brought up Pierrette, and the colonel dismissed any affection for her, claiming he preferred a more mature partner. Sylvie, relieved, decided to keep her intentions hidden and suggested that her brother had considered a match with Pierrette.

Gouraud, sensing Sylvie's interest, professed his desire to marry her, which delighted Rogron. Sylvie began planning their future home but remained suspicious of Pierrette's interactions with the colonel. 

As dinner approached, Sylvie berated Pierrette for a stain on her dress, blaming her for the mishap. Despite her harshness, Sylvie ordered Pierrette to dress nicely for the evening, suspecting her of plotting with Gouraud. 

The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived, with Bathilde dressed to impress. Bathilde, under Vinet's guidance, aimed to use her marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, viewing him as a means to an end rather than a partner. Vinet had instilled in her a desire for independence and influence, encouraging her to leverage her beauty and connections.

As the evening progressed, the atmosphere became charged with tension. Bathilde's condescension towards Pierrette was palpable, and Vinet's remarks only heightened the sense of rivalry. The group played cards, with Sylvie attempting to manipulate the game to her advantage, but Pierrette's innocent comments drew Sylvie's ire.

When Pierrette left the room in distress, Sylvie's anger boiled over, and she resolved to make her pay for the perceived slight. The evening ended with Sylvie feeling frustrated and betrayed, reflecting on the duplicity of men and her own precarious position.

The next day, Pierrette suffered a painful injury from a fall, which Sylvie attributed to her disobedience, showing no sympathy. Rogron suggested care for Pierrette, but Sylvie dismissed it, revealing her coldness and the growing rift between her and her cousin.

The week concluded in relentless torment for Pierrette, who suffered under Sylvie's increasingly cruel tyranny. Pierrette refrained from voicing her pain, both physical and emotional, stemming from her cousin's anger over her silence regarding Brigaut's arrival. Despite her suffering, thoughts of Brigaut brought her solace; she longed for his presence while fearing for his safety. Their connection was palpable, and a single glimpse of him felt like a lifeline.

Brigaut, determined to communicate, devised a plan to exchange letters with Pierrette. One morning, he managed to slip a note to her while pretending to buy fruit. The exchange ignited a deep emotional response in both, leaving them with a wellspring of feelings that transcended ordinary affection. Sylvie remained oblivious to their connection, allowing Pierrette to return home with the note.

That night, Pierrette read Brigaut's heartfelt letter, which expressed his longing for her and his desire to escape their oppressive circumstances. He urged her to let down a string from her window so they could communicate nightly. Pierrette, moved by his words, resolved to respond, despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie. She poured her heart into her letter, revealing her love for Brigaut and her suffering at the hands of her relatives.

At midnight, she successfully sent her letter down to Brigaut, who eagerly awaited her response. Their correspondence continued, filled with hope and plans for a future together, despite the looming threat of Sylvie's jealousy and suspicion. Sylvie, noticing Pierrette's newfound joy, became increasingly watchful, suspecting that Pierrette had seen someone.

As Pierrette's health deteriorated under Sylvie's oppressive scrutiny, she fainted one evening, prompting concern from the colonel and others. Sylvie dismissed her illness as a ruse, but Pierrette's condition worsened. In a moment of desperation, Pierrette cried for help, leading to a violent confrontation with Sylvie, who sought to seize Pierrette's letters. Just as Sylvie's fury reached its peak, Pierrette's grandmother arrived, witnessing the struggle and rushing to her granddaughter's aid.

The grandmother's presence instilled a sense of hope in Pierrette, who had been on the brink of despair. Sylvie, realizing the gravity of her actions, was left in a state of confusion and fear. The grandmother, determined to protect Pierrette, sought medical help, fearing for her granddaughter's life.

Brigaut, meanwhile, wrote to Madame Lorrain, urging her to come and rescue Pierrette from her dire situation. His letters conveyed the urgency of the matter, as he feared for Pierrette's health and well-being. The grandmother, upon receiving Brigaut's letters, was filled with a mix of joy and dread, prompting her to travel to Provins immediately.

Upon her arrival, the grandmother found Pierrette in a state of distress, having endured both physical and emotional trauma. The sight of her injured hand, a result of Sylvie's violent attempts to control her, filled the grandmother with anguish. She vowed to seek justice for Pierrette and ensure her safety.

As Pierrette recovered, the bond between her and her grandmother deepened, providing her with the strength to confront the challenges ahead. The arrival of medical assistance and the promise of a better life with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a glimmer of hope amidst the darkness of her recent experiences. The struggle for freedom and love continued, with Pierrette determined to reclaim her life from the clutches of her oppressive relatives.

At nine o’clock that morning, Monsieur Martener visited Monsieur Tiphaine to discuss the distressing situation involving Pierrette and the Rogrons, who had subjected her to severe mistreatment. This prompted Tiphaine to summon Auffray, a notary and Pierrette’s relative. At that time, tensions between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions were escalating, fueled by damaging rumors about Madame Tiphaine’s family. These truths, though painful, intensified the animosity between the two parties, intertwining personal grievances with political strife.

Tiphaine recognized the opportunity to undermine the Rogrons, who were influential in local opposition to the monarchy. He consulted with the public prosecutor and, along with Auffray and Martener, devised a plan to petition the courts for Auffray to become Pierrette’s guardian. This would allow for a Family Council to be convened, supported by medical testimony, to free Pierrette from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday, the town buzzed with news of Pierrette’s cries heard the previous night, leading to speculation about her condition. Doctor Bianchon and Brigaut arrived to assess her injuries, confirming the Rogrons’ abuse. Vinet, the lawyer, rushed to the Rogrons to inform them of the potential criminal charges they faced. Sylvie, initially evasive, eventually admitted to her jealousy and the truth of the incident, realizing the gravity of their situation.

Vinet exploited their fear, suggesting that a marriage announcement could mitigate the scandal. After much manipulation, the Rogrons agreed to a marriage contract that would benefit them politically. Vinet then filed a petition against Brigaut and the widow Lorrain for abducting Pierrette, positioning the Rogrons as victims.

The court hearing was postponed, heightening the town’s excitement. The Tiphaines, meanwhile, rallied support for Pierrette, who remained unaware of the turmoil surrounding her. She found solace in the care of her grandmother and Brigaut, who were devoted to her recovery. The doctors, horrified by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment of Pierrette.

When the court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report, arguing for Pierrette’s protection. Vinet countered, but the judge ruled in favor of keeping Pierrette away from the Rogrons until a Family Council could be held. This decision was a significant blow to the Rogrons, who were now facing public scrutiny.

The Family Council, composed of Pierrette’s relatives and allies, ultimately decided to remove Rogron as guardian, appointing Auffray instead. Despite Vinet’s lengthy defense of the Rogrons, the evidence against them was overwhelming, leading to a civil suit against them for their neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle unfolded, Pierrette’s health deteriorated. Despite the doctors’ efforts, she faced a dire prognosis. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of Pierrette, who remained a source of inspiration despite her suffering. Her grandmother’s unwavering support and Brigaut’s visits provided her with comfort.

In March 1828, a critical operation was performed on Pierrette, but the prognosis remained grim. As her condition worsened, she expressed her desire to forgive her cousins, demonstrating her grace even in suffering. Tragically, Pierrette passed away, prompting a funeral attended by the entire town, while the Rogrons, fearing public outrage, stayed away.

Brigaut, devastated by her death, crafted her coffin, pouring his grief into the task. The Rogrons attempted to exploit the situation legally, but their efforts were thwarted by the community’s support for Pierrette. Vinet, seeking to protect his clients, faced backlash as the truth of the Rogrons’ actions became widely known.

In the aftermath, Brigaut left Provins, seeking a new life in the military, while Vinet and the Rogrons continued to thrive politically, their reputations largely intact despite the scandal. The narrative of Pierrette’s tragic fate became distorted, with many in the town believing the Rogrons had acted nobly. Only a few, like Martener and Brigaut, remembered the truth of her suffering and the injustices she faced.

The story concludes with a reflection on the nature of justice and the societal dynamics that allowed such tragedies to occur, emphasizing the need for accountability and the enduring impact of Pierrette’s life and death on those who truly understood her plight.
