**Summary of "Pierrette" by Honoré De Balzac**

**Dedication**  
Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, emphasizing the story's melancholy and the need to confront sorrows for someone cherished like Anna.

**I. The Lorrains**  
In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut stands in a small square in Lower Provins, admiring the newly renovated house of Pierrette Lorrain. He notices a calico curtain fluttering in an attic window and sings a traditional Breton wedding song.

Pierrette appears, and they greet each other. Brigaut reveals he has returned from Paris and wishes to settle nearby. However, when a noise alarms Pierrette, she urges him to flee, and he escapes just as an old maid awakens and looks out.

Alone, Pierrette reflects on her life, marked by three years of sorrow and entrapment. Memories of her childhood starkly contrast with her current misery. She recalls her mother, Madame Lorrain, who married a Breton officer, Major Lorrain, and their struggles after his death. Pierrette's mother inherited little after her father's passing, and her subsequent marriage to a physician led to further financial ruin.

Pierrette's grandparents, the Lorrains, could not support her after their decline and sent her to live with her relatives, the Rogrons, in Provins. The Rogrons, unaware of Pierrette until a letter from her grandparents reached them, became her guardians. This unexpected turn of events shaped Pierrette's future, now dependent on her new guardians.

The narrative highlights themes of social class, familial duty, and the impact of financial struggles on personal relationships. Pierrette's delicate health and emotional turmoil reflect broader societal issues, particularly for women in precarious situations. The story foreshadows the challenges she will face with the self-interested Rogrons.

Overall, "Pierrette" poignantly explores the intersection of personal aspirations and societal constraints in the life of a young girl ensnared by her family's misfortunes.

**Summary of "The Rogrons"**

Pere Rogron, an unattractive and selfish innkeeper in Provins, lives with his equally unappealing wife on an annual income of about two thousand francs from their land and inn. After selling the inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from Pierrette’s grandfather, old Auffray. The Rogrons have two poorly raised children, leading to their physical and emotional decline. Rogron's parenting philosophy is simple: he provides minimal support, expecting his children to fend for themselves.

Their daughter, Sylvie, is sent to Paris as an apprentice and eventually becomes a forewoman. Her brother, Jerome-Denis, follows a similar path, and together they save enough to buy a retail establishment called the “Family Sister.” Despite their success, both siblings are unattractive and socially inept, earning a reputation for being miserly and unpleasant.

Sylvie proves to be the more competent sibling, effectively managing the business, while Jerome struggles with the broader aspects of their trade. Their lives revolve around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the outside world. They dream of returning to Provins to improve their inherited property, but their aspirations are tinged with nostalgia and frustration.

When a letter from the Lorrains arrives, seeking to place their orphaned cousin Pierrette in their care, the siblings debate the implications. Viewing Pierrette as a potential threat to their inheritance, they ultimately refuse the request, prioritizing their own interests over familial obligations. Four years later, the Rogrons return to Provins, where their actions will significantly impact Pierrette’s life, highlighting themes of selfishness and familial duty.

### Summary of "Pathology of Retired Mercers"

A provincial shopkeeper returning from Paris brings new ideas but soon succumbs to the habits of provincial life, transitioning from shopkeeper to bourgeois. This shift often feels like an illness as they struggle to adapt to a quieter lifestyle. With their savings, they indulge in long-held desires through travel, politics, or hobbies like hunting and fishing. The Rogrons, a brother-sister duo, exemplify this as they remodel their old house into a fashionable one, aiming to emulate the wealthier bourgeois of Provins.

Their renovations reflect their aspirations, with the contractor consulting them on every detail, emphasizing items once owned by local notables. The Rogrons' first year back in Provins is consumed by discussions about their home and attempts to integrate into local society, which they had previously avoided. They reconnect with former acquaintances, including the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenees, who dominate the social scene.

Madame Tiphaine, the town's belle, is determined to elevate her husband, Monsieur Tiphaine, to a deputy position, using her charm to gain influence. She hosts gatherings that attract the local bourgeoisie, while the Rogrons, eager to fit in, struggle with their lack of social grace. Sylvie Rogron's attempts at card games reveal her poor manners, and her brother, Rogron, is socially awkward, leading to their exclusion from elite circles.

Despite their efforts, the Rogrons are perceived as vulgar and unrefined, resulting in social ostracism. Their lavish dinners, intended to showcase their wealth, are met with indifference, leaving them isolated. Sylvie's attempts to maintain their social standing fail, prompting her to abandon their extravagant lifestyle in favor of frugality.

As time passes, the Rogrons grow increasingly lonely, reminiscing about their past business and feeling the weight of isolation. They engage in mundane routines, with Rogron obsessively checking his barometer and seeking gossip from townsfolk. Their social ambitions crumble, leaving them in ennui, longing for the excitement of their former lives.

In a bid to revitalize their household, Sylvie invites her cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping to regain social standing. However, their plans are complicated by Pierrette's unfortunate circumstances and the Rogrons' past misdeeds regarding family property. Their attempts to ingratiate themselves with the upper class backfire, leading to further alienation.

The Rogrons' growing resentment towards the local elite culminates in their alignment with marginalized figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, who seek to exploit the Rogrons' wealth and ignorance for their own political ambitions. As they navigate their new social landscape, the Rogrons unwittingly become pawns in a larger game of political maneuvering, revealing the complexities of ambition, class, and social dynamics in provincial life.

**Summary of "Pierrette"**

After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette Lorrain felt burdened by living on charity until she discovered wealthy relatives. Her childhood friend Brigaut, an apprentice cabinet-maker, offered her money for her journey to Paris, which she accepted, reflecting their deep bond.

In October 1824, at eleven years old, Pierrette left Nantes, entrusted to a diligence conductor who took her to her cousins, Mademoiselle Sylvie and Rogron. Upon arrival, she was met with indifference, treated more like a package than a family member. Despite her modest attire, she radiated innocence and charm, but her cousins were more concerned with the costs of her arrival than her well-being.

Sylvie and Rogron's initial surprise at Pierrette's presence quickly faded into criticism of her clothing, as they planned an expensive wardrobe for her. Having known love and freedom in Brittany, Pierrette felt a stark contrast in her new home, where her cousins' harshness and obsession with material possessions stifled her spirit.

As she adjusted to her new life, Pierrette faced constant scrutiny from her cousins, who prioritized their financial investment in her clothing over her happiness. Her innocent nature clashed with their coldness, leading to feelings of isolation.

Despite their attempts to dress her like a little lady, Pierrette's playful spirit often led to wear and tear on her clothes, resulting in further criticism. The Rogrons' harshness transformed their relationship with her from curiosity to tyranny, imposing rigid expectations.

The arrival of Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, who sought to manipulate the Rogrons, complicated Pierrette's situation. They encouraged Sylvie and Rogron to adopt a more adversarial stance against social rivals, deepening their resentment and neglect towards her.

As Pierrette's life in Provins progressed, she became increasingly confined and controlled, losing the joy and freedom she once enjoyed. Her cousins' obsession with propriety stifled her natural vivacity, forcing her to suppress her true self to avoid their wrath. The once carefree child was now trapped in a cycle of fear and repression.

### Summary of "History of Poor Cousins in the Home of Rich Ones"

The narrative follows Pierrette, a young girl living with her wealthy cousins, the Rogrons. During a family gathering, she is coldly dismissed by her cousin Sylvie, which deeply wounds her. Pierrette yearns for the affection of her late grandmother, contrasting it with the indifference of her new guardians. Sylvie and her husband, Vinet, trivialize Pierrette's feelings, claiming that while her grandmother took her money, they will leave her theirs.

As Pierrette adjusts to her new life, she faces harsh treatment from the Rogrons, who view her as a burden. They impose strict educational expectations, leading to feelings of inadequacy and despair. Her attempts to please them are met with criticism, resulting in increasing isolation and suffering, both physically and emotionally.

Despite having a writing master, Pierrette struggles academically and is often punished for her perceived failures. The Rogrons' lack of kindness exacerbates her difficulties, making her feel like a servant in their home. The only warmth comes from Adele, the servant, but this comfort is stripped away when Sylvie insists that Pierrette take on household duties herself.

Over time, Pierrette's health declines under the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household. The relentless criticism and absence of affection contribute to her deterioration. The narrative starkly contrasts Pierrette's innocent nature with her relatives' coldness, highlighting the harsh realities faced by those dependent on wealthier family members.

The story also explores the political dynamics of the time, as the Rogrons become involved in local politics through their connections with Vinet and Colonel Gouraud. Their ambitions lead to the establishment of a local newspaper, further entangling them in community power struggles.

As Pierrette approaches her first communion, she finds solace in her faith, which offers hope amid her suffering. However, the Rogrons remain indifferent to her spiritual growth, viewing her as an obstacle to their aspirations. The arrival of Bathilde de Chargeboeuf complicates matters, as she becomes a rival for Rogron's affections and a source of jealousy for Sylvie.

Ultimately, the narrative presents a bleak picture of Pierrette's life, marked by neglect and cruelty from her relatives, who prioritize their ambitions over her well-being, leaving her in despair as she confronts the harsh realities of her existence in the Rogron household.

**Summary of "An Old Maid's Jealousy"**

To grasp the domestic turmoil following Jacques Brigaut's arrival in the Rogron family, we must first examine his journey to Provins. Brigaut, troubled by Pierrette's distress, fled her home, shaken by her transformation from a lively child to a sickly shadow. He climbed to a vantage point to observe her house, consumed with worry and realizing his deep feelings for her.

At sixteen, Brigaut was determined to improve himself for Pierrette's sake. After a brief stint in Paris, he returned to Provins and secured a position as a journeyman with Monsieur Frappier, the town's best cabinet-maker. His dedication to his trade and desire to protect Pierrette drove him to work hard, earning a modest living while keeping a watchful eye on her.

As Brigaut settled into his new life, he learned about the Rogrons' dubious acquisition of Pierrette's grandfather's property and the oppressive nature of her living situation. He often saw Pierrette in the market, where she appeared burdened and unhappy. Despite his longing to approach her, he sensed her distress and chose to keep his distance for her sake.

Meanwhile, Pierrette struggled with her cousin Sylvie's jealousy, which intensified after Brigaut's arrival. Sylvie, an old maid fixated on marrying Colonel Gouraud, grew envious of Pierrette, perceiving a potential romantic connection between her and the colonel. Sylvie's insecurities about aging led her to consult a doctor regarding marriage risks, complicating her feelings further.

The dynamics within the Rogron household were fraught with tension. Sylvie's jealousy manifested in her treatment of Pierrette, whom she accused of having a secret admirer. Pierrette, innocent and unaware of the implications of Sylvie's accusations, remained silent, fearing repercussions. Sylvie's relentless scrutiny exacerbated Pierrette's fragile health, causing her to suffer in silence.

As the story unfolds, Sylvie's jealousy becomes increasingly irrational. She misinterprets Pierrette's innocence as guilt and plots to rid herself of her cousin, convinced that Pierrette threatens her chances with the colonel. The tension culminates in a breakfast scene where Sylvie's accusations and Pierrette's distress reach a breaking point, leaving Pierrette feeling isolated and ill.

Ultimately, the narrative explores themes of jealousy, innocence, and complex familial relationships. Brigaut's love for Pierrette contrasts sharply with Sylvie's bitterness, highlighting the destructive nature of envy and its impact on those caught in its web. As Pierrette's health deteriorates under the strain of her cousin's jealousy, the story foreshadows a tragic outcome driven by misunderstandings and harsh realities.

### Summary of "Domestic Tyranny"

For a month, Colonel Gouraud stopped delivering the “Constitutionnel” to the Rogrons, opting instead for light visits. Sylvie, eager for his attention, dressed attractively and awaited his arrival with her reluctant brother, Rogron. Upon entering, Gouraud complimented Sylvie, but she feigned concern for her cousin's illness to avoid going out.

Gouraud felt uneasy about his partnership with Vinet, who seemed to dominate their political efforts, leaving him sidelined. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which made him wary of his own standing. To gauge Rogron's feelings, Gouraud mentioned his interest in Bathilde, provoking jealousy and hostility from Rogron.

As Gouraud conversed with Sylvie, he suspected her intentions were influenced by Vinet, who had suggested he marry Pierrette instead. Sylvie aimed to keep Pierrette under her control and was suspicious of any connection between her cousin and the colonel. Despite Gouraud's attempts at affection, Sylvie remained calculating, wanting to test his loyalty.

Sylvie's jealousy intensified as she noticed Pierrette's growing beauty and began plotting against her cousin. She sent Pierrette to dress nicely for dinner, suspecting the colonel's interest in her. Meanwhile, Bathilde and her mother arrived, with Bathilde intent on winning Rogron's affection, encouraged by Vinet to view marriage as a means to gain power.

Tensions rose during the evening's card game, with Sylvie's jealousy flaring when she believed Pierrette had helped Gouraud win. After a confrontation, Pierrette fled, injuring her head. Sylvie, indifferent to her injury, focused on her frustrations with the colonel.

The next day, Pierrette's injury became evident, and Sylvie cruelly attributed it to divine punishment for disobedience. Rogron, showing rare concern, suggested care for Pierrette, but Sylvie dismissed it, revealing her harshness and control over her cousin. The dynamics of power, jealousy, and manipulation highlighted the domestic tyranny Sylvie exerted over Pierrette.

**Summary of "The Loves of Jacques and Pierrette"**

Pierrette endured a week of torment due to her cousin Sylvie's cruel treatment, suffering both physically and emotionally while remaining silent about Brigaut's arrival for fear of his safety. Despite her pain, thoughts of Brigaut provided her solace, akin to an exile longing for home.

Brigaut, aware of Pierrette's struggles, devised a plan to communicate by having her lower a letter from her window at midnight. Their mutual longing allowed them to connect despite their separation. One day in the market, Brigaut slipped a note to Pierrette, which she cherished. The letter expressed his love and concern, urging her to escape her oppressive situation and return to Brittany.

Moved by Brigaut's words, Pierrette wrote back, revealing her suffering and longing for freedom. She described the harsh treatment from her cousins and her desire to reunite with her grandmother. After exchanging letters, Pierrette felt renewed hope, believing Brigaut would help her escape her misery.

However, Sylvie grew suspicious of Pierrette's newfound happiness and began to monitor her closely. Despite her illness, Pierrette clung to the hope of Brigaut's nightly visits. When she fainted during a gathering, the colonel, who had feelings for her, expressed concern, but Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse.

As tensions escalated, Sylvie discovered Pierrette's secret correspondence and confronted her. In a violent struggle, Sylvie attempted to seize Pierrette's letter, leading to a brutal confrontation that left Pierrette injured. Just as Sylvie's cruelty peaked, Pierrette's grandmother arrived, alerted by Brigaut. The old woman intervened, taking Pierrette away from the oppressive environment.

Filled with anguish upon seeing her granddaughter's condition, Pierrette's grandmother vowed to seek medical help and take action against the Rogrons. Meanwhile, Brigaut sought a doctor to ensure Pierrette's recovery. As Pierrette recuperated, her grandmother provided comfort and support, promising a better life for them both. The narrative concludes with the promise of healing and the possibility of a brighter future, underscoring themes of love, resilience, and the quest for freedom from oppression.

**Summary of "The Family Council"**

Monsieur Martener visits Monsieur Tiphaine to discuss the mistreatment of Pierrette by the Rogrons, which has led to her severe illness. The feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions in Provins escalates as the Rogrons spread damaging rumors about the Tiphaine family. Tiphaine sees Pierrette's plight as an opportunity to undermine the Rogrons politically.

Tiphaine consults notary Auffray and Martener to strategize on legally removing Pierrette from the Rogrons' guardianship. They plan to convene a Family Council to appoint Auffray as her guardian, allowing them to seek her release. News of Pierrette's distress spreads through Provins, inciting public outrage.

As tensions rise, Vinet, the Rogrons' lawyer, learns of potential criminal charges against them. He manipulates the Rogrons into agreeing to a marriage contract with Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf to gain public favor and distract from the allegations. Court proceedings begin, with the Tiphaines and their supporters rallying around Pierrette, while the Rogrons defend their actions.

Pierrette is moved to a safer environment under Auffray's care, where she receives medical attention for her injuries. Doctors confirm that her condition results from the Rogrons' abuse, leading to a court ruling that prevents her return to their home. Despite Vinet's attempts to discredit her, the Family Council ultimately decides to remove Rogron as her guardian.

As the legal battle continues, Pierrette's health worsens. She expresses love for her grandmother and friend Brigaut, forgiving her cousins for their mistreatment. Tragically, she passes away, prompting a public funeral that the Rogrons dare not attend due to backlash.

Brigaut, devastated, constructs her coffin and vows to seek justice. The legal proceedings against the Rogrons collapse with Pierrette's death, and Vinet exploits the situation for political gain. The narrative critiques societal indifference to Pierrette's suffering and the moral decay of those involved, drawing parallels to historical injustices. In the aftermath, Vinet rises in political power while Brigaut becomes a soldier, haunted by memories of Pierrette.
