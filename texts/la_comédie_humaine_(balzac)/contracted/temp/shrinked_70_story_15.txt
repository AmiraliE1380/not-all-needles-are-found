In April 1813, Julie is enchanted by a military review and falls for Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, leading to their strained marriage by March 1814. Despite having a daughter, Helene, Victor neglects Julie emotionally. She later meets Lord Grenville, complicating her feelings of guilt. By late 1820, the Marquise d’Aiglemont retreats to Saint-Lange, managing her estate while grappling with grief and societal expectations.

At a ball hosted by Madame Firmiani, M. Charles de Vandenesse, preparing for the Congress of Laybach, meets the Marquise. Their conversations deepen as he learns of her tragic past, igniting his admiration. However, she struggles between her loyalty to a lost love and her feelings for Vandenesse. The narrative explores love, loss, and personal fulfillment amid social constraints.

Years later, family tensions arise during a dinner hosted by the Marquis, revealing deeper tragedies. Meanwhile, a General faces a moral dilemma when a fugitive seeks refuge, and Helene asserts her independence. The story concludes in June 1844, with the Marquise reflecting on her sacrifices and her daughter Moïna beginning to understand her mother's struggles. Themes of love, regret, and complex familial relationships are central throughout.
