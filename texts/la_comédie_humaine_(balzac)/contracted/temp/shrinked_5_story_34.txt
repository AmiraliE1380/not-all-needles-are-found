THE MUSE OF THE DEPARTMENT

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by James Waring

DEDICATION

To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,--If these lines endure in the world of literature, they will be a small token of gratitude for your efforts. You, the Hozier, the Cherin, the King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life, have contributed to the noble lineage of the “Human Comedy” with your heraldic designs. Your work, a complete manual of French Heraldry, reflects profound knowledge of the feudal spirit and the elegance of learned symbolism, which has greatly influenced my own.

Your old friend,  
DE BALZAC.

THE MUSE OF THE DEPARTMENT

On the skirts of Le Berry lies Sancerre, a town that captivates travelers with its scenic beauty. Perched atop a hill, it overlooks the fertile Loire flats, which occasionally flood, leaving rich alluvium though some areas are ruined by sand. The town's distance from the river allows the nearby port of Saint-Thibault to thrive, facilitating the shipment of wine and other goods.

By the time this story unfolds, the suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault have been built, ending the ferry era and marking progress since the upheaval of 1830, as the House of Orleans invested in substantial improvements.

Sancerre's steep, narrow streets, framed by the Great Ramparts, serve as the town's main thoroughfares. The region's primary industry is wine production, yielding vintages close in quality to Burgundy, making Sancerre a vital supplier to Parisian wine shops. Below the town, villages like Fontenoy and Saint-Satur recall the picturesque vineyards of Neuchâtel.

Despite its ancient charm, Sancerre's military significance has waned, and its future appears bleak as trade shifts to the Loire’s right bank. The town’s population of around 3,500 pales beside Cosne's 6,000, indicating a reversal in fortunes over the past fifty years. Nevertheless, Sancerre retains historic allure, with breathtaking views, pure air, and a friendly, if watchful, community, predominantly Calvinist yet devoid of strict Puritanism.

The town prides itself on notable figures, including Horace Bianchon, a luminary in modern medicine, and Etienne Lousteau, a successful journalist. However, the municipality struggles under the sway of a few large landowners who manipulate elections, prompting a failed attempt by locals to break free. This conspiracy, driven by vanity, ultimately faltered through jealousy among its members.

The proposed remedy was to unite behind a champion for the next election, specifically one of the two prominent representatives of Sancerre in Paris. This idea was progressive for the provinces, especially since parochial dignitaries have surged since 1830, making true statesmen increasingly rare in the lower chamber.

Though dubious in origin, this plan sprang from the Superior Woman of the borough, motivated by personal interests. Understanding her past and prospects is essential to grasping the full context of her ambitions.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Sancerre, around 1836, was home to a notable figure called the Superior Woman, long overlooked but now gaining local notice. At the same time two Sancerrois prospered in Paris: Etienne Lousteau, a writer, and Bianchon, a physician and professor.

George Sand's influence produced "Sandism," a charming but excessive sentimentality that revealed many superior women in France, who had previously remained out of public view.

The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town-and-country house near Saint-Satur. The current Baudrayes were a diminished branch of an illustrious family that had once joined the Crusades.

The story begins with a sheriff named Milaud, a former Calvinist who converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. King Louis XIV rewarded him with a title and fief, but later measures against heretics brought misfortune. Under Louis XV a Milaud de la Baudraye advanced, securing a military commission for his son, who died in battle leaving a child later favored by Louis XVI.

In 1800, after exile, an ex-farmer-general returned to Sancerre and bought La Baudraye out of vanity. His only son, Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, was frail—a product of his father’s excesses. His noble mother devoted herself to raising him until her death, which brought his father back to France.

On his father's death Polydore inherited a fief, some money, and a collection of autographs. He survived by strict habits and Sancerre's healthy air. From 1802 to 1815 he expanded his vineyards but hesitated to press Parisian debts during the Restoration. After Napoleon's fall he tried to monetize his father's collection but failed and returned to Sancerre.

In 1823, at forty-three, Polydore sought marriage to secure an heir. He chose Dinah Piedefer, a beautiful, educated, and intelligent woman from a once-Calvinist family now fallen into poverty. Educated in a prestigious school, Dinah hoped to rise socially by converting to Catholicism to attract a suitable husband.

The Archbishop of Bourges, seeing Dinah's promise, attempted to arrange a marriage, but families hesitated because of her beauty and intellect. When Polydore learned of her situation he hurried to Bourges just as Dinah's mother considered a wealthy suitor. Polydore sought the Cardinal's support to recover debts, but his blunt approach offended the Minister.

Polydore’s marriage to Dinah stirred Sancerre, provoking speculation about his motives. Local elites, including President Boirouge, observed that Polydore was partly driven by jealousy of a rival, the handsome Milaud.

Polydore’s financial dealings in Paris were fraught. He went to press debtors, including the Duc de Navarreins. A government agent proposed a bargain: Polydore could obtain a tax-collector post if he forgave the Duc’s debts. Tempted, he accepted and returned to Sancerre as a tax collector.

After a brief tenure he went back to Paris, secured a series of lucrative appointments, and ultimately became a Royal Commissioner. By 1827 he realized a long-held ambition, buying the estate of Anzy and raising his regional standing.

Dinah transformed La Baudraye into a pleasant residence. She laid out gardens, improved interiors, and maintained a refined, cultured atmosphere while keeping her husband’s financial maneuverings private, which added an air of mystery to him.

As Dinah emerged socially, her beauty and intellect drew both admiration and envy. Her refined tastes and correspondence with a Paris friend kept her informed on fashion and culture, distancing her from provincial women. Her desire for meaningful conversation alienated local women, who found her superiority intimidating. Local men admired her, but the women resented and isolated her. Dinah's mother, Madame Piedefer, basked in her daughter's success, while Polydore reveled in having a Superior Woman as a wife.

This dynamic shows how societal perceptions shape reputations: Dinah’s superiority became both a source of admiration and resentment, ultimately isolating her in a community unable to accept exceptionalism.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By the end of 1825 Dinah de la Baudraye faced accusations of socializing only with men and became the target of criticism and misrepresentation. Trying to preserve her reputation, she once made a rash remark to a false friend about her isolation—“I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!”—which backfired. Childless after five years of marriage, she became fodder for ridicule.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was a frail man with thin legs who sometimes wore false calves and favored peculiar dress. Gentle and seldom contradicting Dinah, he let her dominate conversation. Dinah, admired for beauty and intelligence, drew local gentlemen who traveled from nearby towns. She enjoyed the attention and grew adept at delivering prepared speeches based on wide reading across varied subjects.

Her charm created a protective aura that shielded her from much censure. Monsieur often excused himself from her long expositions, preferring walks or business. As Dinah established herself she championed the romantic arts, collected antiques, and decorated their home with Renaissance treasures. Her husband supported these interests but concentrated chiefly on his land.

Over the years she filled their house with a notable collection of antiques that impressed visitors and furnished material for her talks on art and history. She tried to form a literary circle in Sancerre, but it degenerated into social gatherings lacking literary focus—an evident disappointment for Dinah, who had hoped to elevate local culture.

The circle became a venue for games and casual chat, much to her chagrin. Her admirers—including local magistrates and a priest—were often diverted from intellectual pursuits, producing a mixture of admiration and frustration. One young man who yawned during a lecture fell from her favor, showing the strict expectations she set for companions.

When Dinah turned twenty in 1826, suitors continued to court her. She remained devoted to her role as wife, sheltered by her mother and by the visible presence of admirers, which kept slander at bay. The jealousy among suitors amused onlookers and made it clear none dared leave her unguarded.

Monsieur de la Baudraye recognized the leverage his marriage provided and used it to secure favors and legal victories through his connections. Local women speculated about the nature of the marriage, imagining hidden complexities. In reality a slow, monotonous domestic drama played out at La Baudraye—one that might have remained private if not for the era’s appetite for exposing human relationships. That domestic tension helped shape Dinah’s seemingly virtuous demeanor during her early married life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A young woman whose academic successes sprang from pride was unlikely to halt her promising trajectory. Monsieur de la Baudraye, though seemingly frail, proved an unexpected match for Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer. Yet she wondered why he married a seventeen-year-old and what she might gain.

At first he surprised her: he let her use five acres for pleasure and financed house improvements so she could buy furniture and redecorate in varied styles. Dinah struggled to reconcile his reputation for stinginess with this apparent generosity, but the illusion dissolved after his second trip to Paris. When she asked for supplies she learned he strictly allotted a monthly allowance and paid her mother a yearly sum, stressing caution because their winegrower income was uncertain.

Dinah’s attempts to provoke jealousy produced only indifference; he stayed steady and unyielding. Frustration grew as she felt trapped in La Baudraye and Sancerre, longing for a more ambitious life. She briefly believed he might share such ambitions when he bought the estate of Anzy and applied for a patent of entail, and for a time she hoped for a brighter future. But their short reconciliation only revealed deeper contrasts: Dinah’s energy and potential clashed with Monsieur de la Baudraye’s lack of ambition and rigid routine.

Over six years Dinah became increasingly provincial, losing individuality and spirit. The difference between Parisian and provincial life became clear: Paris offered a variety of social classes and opportunities, while the provinces confined women to a single, often uninspiring identity. Despite initial resolve, Dinah succumbed to mediocrity as the absence of stimulating company and the monotony of rural life dulled her aspirations.

Her beauty and charm, once celebrated, faded as she adapted to local expectations. Her wardrobe shrank; she began making her own clothes and lost the elegance she once maintained. The provincial atmosphere stifled her creativity and intellect, and she adopted the mannerisms and attitudes around her, further diminishing her distinctiveness.

A pivotal moment came when Dinah met her former school friend Anna, now a sophisticated Parisian. The comparison left Dinah feeling inadequate and despondent: Anna had learned to thrive while Dinah had only endured. This encounter ignited a deep despair.

In her anguish Dinah turned to poetry for solace. Her piece "Paquita la Sevillane" drew attention in the provinces, revealing her talent and offering a temporary escape. Yet the success brought new anxieties: she feared exposure as the author and possible backlash from her peers.

Despite literary efforts, Dinah remained trapped in provincial life, oscillating between dreams of fame and the suffocating routine that constrained her. Her writing intensified resentment toward these limitations, leaving her in a state of perpetual dissatisfaction.

After the Revolution of 1830, George Sand's fame prompted many French towns to celebrate local female talent, spurring a wave of aspiring women writers who began to voice previously unacknowledged ideas. Monsieur de Clagny seized this trend by publishing a collection by Jan Diaz, a poet who had died young. The volume, including poems and an elegy, struck a chord with readers, especially in the regions where the poet had lived and died, largely unknown until then.

Two hundred copies were printed; many sold across several departments, though some sent to Paris were lost in editorial offices. It later emerged that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym for a woman, and Madame de la Baudraye — Dinah — rose as a celebrated figure, viewed as a rival to George Sand. The provincial public, often polarized, lavished praise on work that might otherwise have been dismissed.

Dinah had long felt unrecognized and sought fame to fill unmet ambitions. The late Abbe Duret had warned her about her husband’s character, but she resisted his counsel. Gradually she discovered her husband incapable of love and initially denied his capacity for hatred until she recognized his deep resentment. The Abbe had cautioned that offending him would invite vengefulness rather than forgiveness.

As Dinah’s reputation grew, her domestic life felt increasingly constraining. Public sympathy for her plight deepened her despair. Her husband, outwardly civil, harbored resentment which Dinah eventually perceived. After a confrontation she vowed to stop writing, withdrawing into desolation at Sancerre where her absence from the literary scene was keenly felt.

Despite fame, Dinah faced jealousy and opposition among local rivals. She had hoped to bolster Monsieur de Clagny’s political ambitions, but his electoral defeat left her disillusioned. The household’s troubles masked a deeper dissatisfaction that intensified her longing for freedom from marriage.

At the Chateau d’Anzy, Dinah contemplated her future and the possibility of meeting influential figures. She admired Lousteau, a journalist noted for his charm with women, and sought to draw him to Sancerre. Her efforts to engage another prominent figure, Bianchon, at first met indifference.

When Lousteau and Bianchon finally arrived—initially unrecognized—Dinah, eager to impress, invited them to her chateau and played the gracious hostess. In conversation she aired frustrations with provincial life, stressing its monotony and the despair it bred. Her frankness about the limitations of rural existence intrigued her guests, who appreciated her intelligence and wit.

Dinah’s remarks revealed her yearning for more than the mundane. She criticized societal constraints that stifled creativity and individuality and lamented culture’s centralization in Paris. Her observations sparked lively discussion about provincial versus urban life; she argued that the narrow range of experiences in the provinces created a stifling atmosphere for women.

As the evening progressed, Dinah’s charm and intellect captivated her guests, especially Lousteau, who found her both fascinating and exasperating. Despite attempts to project superiority, she disclosed vulnerabilities that heightened his interest. The dynamic between them suggested a potential deeper connection as Dinah navigated attraction alongside ambition.

In a lighter moment, Dinah asked for autographs, revealing her desire for validation and a tangible link to the literary world. Bianchon and Lousteau complied, leaving their marks in her album—a symbol of the recognition she craved. The exchange highlighted the complexities of her character as she tried to reconcile aspiration with the realities of provincial life.

Ultimately, Dinah’s journey exemplifies the struggle of a woman caught between societal expectations and a pursuit of personal fulfillment. Her interactions with Lousteau and Bianchon catalyze a process of self-discovery as she grapples with her identity as a writer and a woman in a restrictive environment. The narrative captures the tension between ambition and domesticity, illustrating the challenges women face when trying to carve out their own paths in a world that limits their possibilities.

################################################
At five the next morning, the party at the Chateau d’Anzy was awake; little La Baudraye was organizing a day of sport for the Parisians, chiefly to feed his vanity. He was eager to show the twelve hundred acres of waste he intended to reclaim—an undertaking costing about a hundred thousand francs and promising an annual profit of thirty to sixty thousand.

“Why hasn’t the Public Prosecutor joined us?” Gatien Boirouge asked Monsieur Gravier.

“He said he had to sit today; minor cases are before the Court,” Gravier answered.

“Did you believe that?” Gatien exclaimed. “My father told me Monsieur Lebas wouldn’t come early because Monsieur de Clagny asked him to sit in his place!”

“Really?” Gravier said, concerned. “And Monsieur de la Baudraye has gone to La Charite!”

“Why do you meddle?” Bianchon asked Gatien.

“Horace is right,” Lousteau added. “I don’t understand why you’re so concerned about each other; it’s a waste of time.”

Bianchon glanced at Lousteau—their Parisian newspaper humor not translating in Sancerre.

At a copse, Monsieur Gravier left the two notable men and Gatien with a keeper to navigate a ravine.

“We should wait for Monsieur Gravier,” Bianchon suggested upon reaching a clearing.

“You may be a great physician,” Gatien replied, “but you don’t know provincial life. By now he’s probably at Anzy for breakfast.” He checked his watch. “He’ll be just in time.”

“At the chateau with Madame de la Baudraye and Monsieur de Clagny. I can’t rest easy knowing they’re alone together. Two of them will watch each other. Dinah will be guarded.”

“Ah, so Madame de la Baudraye hasn’t decided?” Lousteau remarked.

“Mother thinks so. But I fear Monsieur de Clagny has bewitched her. If he has a chance at the Keeper of the Seals, he may hide his faults and seem charming. If Dinah imagines him Attorney-General, she might see him as handsome. Eloquence has privileges. Besides, Madame de la Baudraye is ambitious; she dreams of Paris.”

“But what interest have you?” Lousteau asked. “If she loves the Public Prosecutor—”

“You in Paris meet different women every day,” Gatien replied. “In Sancerre, with hardly six women—and five pretending to be virtuous—a young man of twenty-two can guess her secrets when she treats him with consideration.”

“Consideration? Is that what you call it here?” Lousteau smiled.

“I’d think Madame de la Baudraye has too much taste to bother with that ugly ape,” Bianchon said.

“Horace,” Lousteau said, “let’s set a trap for the Public Prosecutor; we’ll do Gatien a favor and have a laugh. I don’t like Public Prosecutors.”

“You have a keen intuition of destiny,” Bianchon replied. “But what can we do?”

“After dinner, we’ll tell stories of wives betrayed and husbands avenged—burnings, hewn or cut to pieces. We’ll see how Madame de la Baudraye and de Clagny react.”

“Good idea!” Bianchon agreed. “One of them must betray something by expression.”

“I know an editor,” Lousteau continued, addressing Gatien, “who shows his wife stories of lovers boiled or baked to frighten her into fidelity—‘See what the smallest error can lead to!’”

“Madame de la Baudraye is innocent; this youth sees double,” Bianchon said. “Madame Piedefer is too pious to invite her daughter’s lover to the chateau. Dinah would have to deceive her mother, her husband, her maid, and the housekeeper; that’s too much. I acquit her.”

“Especially since her husband never ‘quits her,’” Gatien laughed.

“We can remember a few stories to make Dinah quake,” Lousteau said. “Young men—stay stern; be diplomats, casual but observant. This morning we’ll hunt the hare; this evening we’ll hunt the Public Prosecutor.”

The evening began with a triumph for Lousteau, who returned an album to the lady with an elegy written in it—melancholic verses on despair and unrequited feeling signed by Etienne Lousteau, September 1836, Chateau d’Anzy. Clagny suspected the poem was recent.

“You wrote those since yesterday?” he asked.

“Indeed, yes, as I was following the game,” Etienne feigned. The reader will suspect he’d memorized the lines for years. Madame de la Baudraye gazed at him with that pity genius evokes; Monsieur de Clagny, seeing her expression, turned in hatred at the sham "Jeune Malade." He sat down to play backgammon with the cure of Sancerre. The Presiding Judge’s son placed a lamp near the two players, illuminating Madame de la Baudraye, who took up her embroidery. The three conspirators sat close by.

“For whom are you decorating that basket?” Lousteau asked. “For some charity lottery perhaps?”

“No,” she replied. “Too much display in charity done to the sound of a trumpet.”

“You are indiscreet,” Monsieur Gravier said.

“Can there be any indiscretion in asking who the happy mortal is?” Lousteau teased.

“It is for Monsieur de la Baudraye,” Dinah said.

The Public Prosecutor glanced slyly, as if to say, “I have lost my paper basket!”

“Why, madame, may we not think him happy in having a lovely wife, happy in her decorating his baskets?” Lousteau persisted.

“If ever I marry, I hope twelve years after my wife’s embroidered baskets may still be for me,” he added.

“And why should they not be for you?” she said, fixing her fine gray eyes on Etienne with invitation.

“Parisians believe in nothing,” the lawyer bitterly replied. “You Paris authors turn on adultery—farces, dramas—”

“Come, Monsieur the Public Prosecutor,” Etienne laughed, “I left you to play in peace; now you bring an indictment against me. I’ve written against those writers to attempt criticism. If you condemn them, you must condemn Homer, Milton. Eve and her serpent are symbolical adultery. You must burn many works if you insist on removing illicit love. Except Bossuet’s History of Variations and Pascal’s Provinciales, what’s left?”

“Much loss that would be!” said Monsieur de Clagny.

Etienne, nettled by Clagny’s superiority, set out to provoke him with paradoxes. “Suppose we’re Public Prosecutors of all ages: the Catholic religion is, some might say, infected at its fountain by a startling instance of illegal union—Joseph’s wife seen as an adulteress by earthly judges. Pilate or Anytus represented established social interests defending themselves—Socrates and Jesus were criminals to aristocracies. Today’s public prosecutors are as logical as those judges.”

“Your argument is blasphemy,” Clagny said.

“I grant it,” the journalist replied, “but I mean no malice. Historic fact can’t be suppressed. Pilate and Anytus defended social orders. If their followers had triumphed, there’d have been a ‘ninety-three’ in Rome or Attica.”

“What are you trying to get at?” the lawyer asked.

“To adultery! A Buddhist might say Christianity is founded in adultery, as we think Mohammed an impostor. All these claims show how helpless institutions are before new ideas.”

“If there were many men like you in France—all government would be impossible.”

“And there would be no religion at all,” Madame Piedefer interjected. She made faces through the discussion.

“You are paining them,” Bianchon told Lousteau. “Do not talk of religion.”

“If I were a writer,” Monsieur Gravier said, “I should side with the luckless husbands. Among deceived husbands there are dramatic, energetic men.”

“You are right,” Lousteau said. “I never thought deceived husbands ridiculous. Do you not think a husband’s confidence sublime?” Bianchon asked. “He trusts. But if he trusts, you mock him; if jealous, you hate him. What’s the happy medium?”

“If Monsieur de Clagny had not just condemned immoral stories, I could tell you of a husband’s revenge,” Lousteau said.

Clagny threw the dice and dared not look up.

“A story from you!” cried Madame de la Baudraye. “I should hardly have hoped for such a treat.”

“It was told me—delightfully—by Charles Nodier,” Etienne began.

He narrated a tale set after the 18th Brumaire about a young Chevalier de Beauvoir sent by the Chouans, captured, and sent to the Castle of l’Escarpe. The commandant, a Corsican, treated him well; his wife took a fancy to Beauvoir. Whether from imprudence or love, the commandant grew jealous and the prisoner was put in a dungeon and fettered. Longing for freedom, Beauvoir was secretly aided by a turnkey who provided a file and a rope—but the turnkey’s plan betrayed him: the rope would drop him onto rocks below. Sensing a trap, Beauvoir feigned escape; when the jailer came to check the supposed scene, Beauvoir struck him down, donned his clothes, mimicked his walk, and walked out through the gate, escaping.

The lawyer and Madame de la Baudraye felt no application of this narrative to themselves. The conspirators looked at one another, surprised at the perfect composure of the two supposed lovers.

“Oh! I can tell a better story,” said Bianchon.

At Lousteau’s cue, Bianchon told La Grande Breteche—the tale of a Spanish grandee starved to death in a walled-up cupboard by a jealous husband—which stunned the company. Madame de la Baudraye wondered at love’s force. Monsieur Gravier then related a Spanish adventure: at a Madrid ball, a surgeon-major was abducted by a waiting-woman and made to help deliver a woman in labor secretly so a husband would not know. The operation failed to save the infant, but the surgeon, after much peril, was given diamonds by the lover—later stolen by the surgeon's servant. The waiting-woman, poisoned, appeared to warn him, then died. Later, the surgeon awoke to find the husband holding an amputated arm with a distinct mole; the husband stabbed him to the heart. Clagny interjected: half of Paris’s startling crimes would outdo these tales.

“Do you suppose such adventures could occur now, in France?” Madame de la Baudraye asked.

“Of the ten or twelve startling crimes committed annually here,” Clagny said, “many rival such romantic details. The Gazette des Tribunaux is a bad Press abuse, but true stories abound. In Saint-Pierre-des-Corps at Tours, a widow, zealous in piety, confessed to murdering her husband, salting and cutting his body into pieces and throwing them into the Loire; the head remained in a cask. She was sentenced and executed.”

“And do you suppose,” Bianchon asked, “we know all tragedies behind private life’s curtain? Human justice is ill adapted to judge crimes between husband and wife. It can intervene as police but knows little of the heart.”

“The victim has often been the tormentor so long,” Madame de la Baudraye remarked, “that the crime might seem almost excusable if the accused could tell all.”

This left the Parisians puzzled about Dinah’s position.

At bedtime, the bachelors lingered in the passages for a council. Monsieur Gravier was briefed on the evening’s object: to probe Madame de la Baudraye. Lousteau warned that her serenity could indicate cunning; Monsieur Gravier proposed sealing the doors of Madame de la Baudraye and the Public Prosecutor with hairs affixed to wax—simple country spycraft—to see if anyone went in or out. In the night, the doctor, the journalist, the receiver of taxes, and Gatien barefoot applied the seals and agreed to return at five.

At dawn, they found the hairs unbroken on both doors.

“Is it the same wax?” asked Gravier.

“Are they the same hairs?” asked Lousteau.

“Yes,” replied Gatien.

“This alters the matter,” cried Lousteau. “You’ve been chasing a will-o’-the-wisp.”

Gravier and Gatien exchanged glances suggesting puzzlement. Lousteau whispered to Bianchon that if Dinah was virtuous, she deserved effort. He pictured storming a fortress that had resisted Sancerre’s besiegers for nine years.

With this in mind, Lousteau sought out his hostess in the garden. She wished to converse with him. Half such encounters are planned.

“You were out shooting yesterday,” Dinah said. “This morning I’m puzzled how to amuse you; unless you’d like to come to La Baudraye to study provincial life—”

“That simpleton Gatien told you I said I adored you,” Etienne said. “Your silence at dinner betrayed a sort of indiscretion we never commit in Paris. I plotted those stories to see if I could rouse you and Monsieur de Clagny. Oh! be at ease; your innocence is proved. If you fancied that magistrate, you’d lose value in my eyes—I love perfection. You cannot love that cold, taciturn usurer who’d leave any man for twenty-five centimes. At twenty-eight, handsome, well-conducted, and childless—Monsieur de la Baudraye expresses virtue admirably.”

By opening like this, Lousteau cut past sentimental subtleties and forced the offering women sometimes make to men. Madame de la Baudraye, true to her reputation as a Superior Woman, consoled him: “You have sought pleasure but never loved. True love often comes late.”

“I can still believe in love,” he said, “but I have ceased to believe in woman. Perhaps I have defects that hinder me from being loved; I’ve often been thrown over. I may have too strong a feeling for the ideal.”

Madame de la Baudraye was pleased: he represented Paris’s daring axioms, its almost artless depravity, its advanced convictions. Etienne, performing before Dinah, scored a success. She spent one of the most delightful days of her life with Lousteau and Bianchon, who told her anecdotes about great men—sayings that would be the Ana of the century—novelties to provincial ears. Lousteau disparaged a great Berry celebrity to flatter Dinah, suggesting she could rival that writer. Flattered, she warmed to Etienne; Clagny, Gravier, and Gatien thought her more affectionate.

The exaggerated reports brought sixteen persons to Anzy that evening—coaches, wagonettes, and bachelors on hired horses. By seven the drawing-room glittered, Dinah having prepared the furniture for the assembly, regarding it as a triumph. Lousteau, Bianchon, and Dinah exchanged looks as they watched the visitors.

What invalided ribbons, ancestral laces, and imaginative caps! The Presidente Boirouge extracted medical advice from Bianchon, who gravely prescribed a daily cup of tea for her “English malady.” Rumors circulated that Dinah had summoned him not about elections but to inquire why she had no children.

Lousteau, feeling on show among townsfolk, saw their provincial desires. A footman brought in letters and a printer’s packet; Lousteau left proof sheets for Bianchon. Dinah, seeing the parcel, exclaimed at literature’s pursuit of him.

“What is it?” asked Etienne.

“A fascinating romance on a spoiled proof used to wrap yours,” Bianchon said, offering “Olympia, or Roman Revenge.”

Lousteau read aloud fragments: brigands in a cavern, Rinaldo, Lamberti, a Duchess’s glove, Cardinal Borborigano, intrigues—then the page broke off. He mocked local color blunders and speculated on the plot and authorship with comic erudition, weaving improvised commentary—Rinaldo as a man of spirit, the Duchess as possibly guilty, the Cavaliere Paluzzi a figure. Dinah, delighted, said she could picture the Roman scene: a murdered husband and a wicked wife haunted by discovery.

The listeners were captivated. Lousteau continued, teasing the audience with missing pages and printing mechanics: a waste sheet, printed only on one side, a “back sheet” and the complications of making-up a volume. He and Bianchon joked about the style, vignettes, and the probable literary status of the author. The assembly, from the aging grandees to the curious young, drank in the reading and commentary, amused and bewildered by Lousteau’s mock-critique and the romance’s fragments.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For three days Dinah’s imagination was stirred by the talk of her two Parisian visitors. Lousteau watched her growing distraction, waiting for the moment she would be wholly captivated. Dinah wanted to show La Baudraye to her guests, and the charade began when Bianchon’s papers were left behind in Anzy. Gatien rode off to fetch them, while Madame Piedefer went shopping, leaving Dinah alone with Lousteau and Bianchon. Lousteau sat beside her; Bianchon rode backward. They mocked the local men and debated the unhappy circumstances of her life, leaving Dinah defensive.

As they crossed the Loire, Bianchon told her plainly she had shut herself against love but now needed it. Dinah, taken aback, asked if he prescribed love; he warned that her present way of living would make her unattractive. Lousteau half-jokingly defended Bianchon, saying his counsel was for her good.

At Cosne a crowd gathered near the old carriage bearing Louis XIV’s arms. Dinah insisted they disembark; she took Bianchon’s arm and walked briskly along the Loire. Bianchon suggested Lousteau loved her but was frivolous and hard to hold; better to befriend him, he said, since Lousteau could bring her beauty and happiness. Under Bianchon’s scrutiny Dinah felt vulnerable and uncertain about competing with Lousteau’s past lovers.

Their coachman announced Gatien’s return. Bianchon blessed them as they entered town. Lousteau took Dinah’s hand and declared his love, but she resisted, asking if she should give herself to a man who regarded her as a passing pleasure. Gatien’s arrival complicated things: Lousteau whispered that she should pretend resistance to preserve his honor and, in a reckless moment, crushed her muslin dress, leaving her disheveled.

Gatien, oblivious to the tension, rode up, and Lousteau tried to distract him. Dinah urged Gatien to fetch Bianchon’s papers from the inn; he complied reluctantly. Lousteau then urged them to hurry back to La Baudraye, claiming Dinah was unwell. Humiliated, Dinah retreated inside, overwhelmed, and Lousteau followed, begging forgiveness.

Madame Piedefer appeared, anxious at Dinah’s distress. Hearing Gatien’s horse, Dinah fled to her room. Outside, Lousteau and Gatien confronted one another; Gatien accused Lousteau of trickery, which Lousteau shrugged off, suggesting Dinah had mocked Gatien. Dinah changed into a velvet gown; when she rejoined the men, Gatien awkwardly commented on her dress. Lousteau explained the change as a response to the cool air; Dinah blushed and her mother glared. Conversation turned to Bianchon, whom Lousteau praised, and Dinah, weighed down by events, stayed silent.

Lousteau, intent on winning her, declared he wished to stay in Sancerre to be near her and swore he had left Parisian vices behind. On the road back to Anzy his charm began to pierce Dinah’s reserve; she laughed, a sign her feelings were shifting. During dinner he remained buoyant, sometimes lost in thought, savoring the happiness he imagined with Dinah. After coffee the women left the men to stroll in the gardens; Lousteau seized the moment to speak to Dinah, who was now more receptive.

The next day Lousteau learned he must soon return to Paris. He and Dinah visited the elderly artist restoring the château’s coat of arms; Lousteau found the old man’s plans amusing, while Dinah begged for one more day with him. They exchanged pledges of affection, and Dinah accompanied him to Cosne, disregarding social proprieties.

Back at La Baudraye Dinah praised Lousteau, saying he had shown her what true love felt like. Her three admirers, including Monsieur de Clagny, were left to reckon with their feelings; Dinah took Clagny as a confidant, lavishing affection on him while he remained devoted despite her new happiness.

Back in Paris, Lousteau quickly lost the joyful memories of the Chateau d’Anzy. He lived by his pen, and in this era—after the rise of the bourgeoisie—writing was almost servitude: to live by the pen meant constant creation, or the appearance of it, both demanding effort. Lousteau wrote daily for a newspaper and several literary magazines, but he lacked artistic integrity, ranking among Paris’s literary hacks. Deprived of literary pretensions and advancement, he had become a journalist.

Still, his life was not unpleasant. He mingled with blue-stockings, aspiring actresses, publishers, and authors, enjoying a lifestyle that required little beyond rent. He had theatre boxes, and the sale of books he reviewed covered minor expenses. His daily life was social, and his writing the price of that easy existence. Yet after ten years of struggle he was as financially constrained as when he arrived, still believing a monthly income of five hundred francs would make him rich.

Lousteau lived in well-furnished ground-floor rooms in the Rue des Martyrs, rented for twelve hundred francs. His financial woes peaked in January, April, July, and October, when rent and other expenses drained his resources. He spent lavishly on cabs, meals, and gifts for his lovers, keeping him perpetually broke despite earning seven to eight hundred francs a month.

Occasionally weary of his literary life, Lousteau would reflect with friends like Nathan and Bixiou about their futures, joking about marriage and romantic entanglements. He had a mistress, Madame Schontz, proud to have a writer as her lover; she lived nearby and enjoyed teasing her friends about their relationships.

These details matter because his bohemian existence would later affect Dinah. He could laugh about his romantic entanglements, including his relationship with Dinah, whom he had almost forgotten. When Bixiou asked about his time in Sancerre, Lousteau recounted experiences with three provincial men too timid to pursue a local beauty.

Lousteau received a lengthy, affectionate letter from Dinah, which he read and then burned, dismissing her feelings. He was not particularly concerned about Madame Schontz, who truly loved him, but he had also displaced a friend in the affections of a Marquise who sometimes visited unexpectedly.

A week later another letter from Dinah arrived; he discarded it unread. Madame Schontz, noticing the scented paper, opened hampers Dinah had sent—game and flowers—and suggested they host a dinner. Lousteau wrote back a clever but insincere letter he believed would charm Dinah.

By December Lousteau had stopped reading Dinah’s letters; they piled in a drawer. Then Madame Schontz informed him of a potential marriage opportunity. A young woman had fallen into trouble, and her father, a notary, wanted to marry her off quickly, offering a substantial dowry. Her previous lover had died, and the notary sought a respectable man to marry his daughter.

Madame Schontz urged Lousteau to consider the proposal, emphasizing the financial benefits and potential for a comfortable life. She warned of the mother-in-law, a strict woman who would scrutinize his past. Despite that, she insisted marrying the widow would leave him free and financially secure.

Initially hesitant, Lousteau agreed to meet the notary and his family. He flattered the notary, recalling his own father and presenting himself as a man of ambition. The notary liked Lousteau’s charm and background, and Lousteau was soon introduced to the Cardot family as a prospective son-in-law.

At his first dinner with them Lousteau found the atmosphere dull, but he noticed Félicie, the notary’s daughter, shy yet affectionate. She expressed gratitude for his willingness to marry her, which touched him. Madame Cardot viewed him as a man with a promising future, pleased with his moral standing.

Buoyed by these prospects, Lousteau felt a newfound purpose. He imagined a life of stability, a charming wife, a respectable family connection, and the financial security the marriage promised. Ready to embrace this new chapter, he believed it would lead to greater ambitions and opportunities.

Madame Cardot wanted to see the prints for Gil Blas, an illustrated volume, and borrowed it to visit her future son-in-law, Lousteau, unexpectedly, hoping to learn about his lifestyle from his bachelor rooms. Her sister-in-law, Madame Camusot, was apprehensive about the match, having heard unflattering things about Lousteau from her husband, a Supreme Court Councillor.

On a January day in 1837, Madame Cardot and her daughter took a cab to return the book to Lousteau, eager to inspect his living space. The porter was absent, but his daughter gave them the key after Madame Cardot slipped her a gold coin. Around noon, Lousteau was returning from breakfast when he spotted Dinah, his former lover, arriving in a coach. He was shocked to see her, more so when she revealed she was a mother.

Overwhelmed, Lousteau tried to dismiss Dinah, but she insisted on staying. Meanwhile Pamela, the child in the apartment, sensed the awkwardness and told Lousteau that his mother-in-law was inside. When he entered, he found Madame Cardot and Félicie hiding in a corner. Dinah declared her love, but Lousteau worried about complications.

Madame Cardot confronted Lousteau, expressing disapproval of him as a potential son-in-law. She left, taking Félicie with her, while Dinah remained, trusting Lousteau’s reassurances. Realizing Dinah’s high-mindedness, Lousteau thought she would sacrifice for his future and began to imagine using her to escape his impending marriage.

As Dinah settled into Lousteau’s apartment, she was charmed by its elegance, unaware of the underlying poverty. Pamela, eager to help, borrowed dinnerware from Madame Schontz, who soon learned of Lousteau’s predicament and warned him that Cardot would not pursue the match. Lousteau, however, focused on his new life with Dinah, enjoying their time together.

When Bixiou, a friend, arrived, he questioned Lousteau about abandoning a wealthy marriage for Dinah. Bixiou warned that provincial women often struggle to adapt to Parisian life and would eventually bore him. Lousteau dismissed his concerns, insisting he loved Dinah and preferred her to any fortune.

Bixiou continued to argue that Lousteau was making a mistake, emphasizing marriage’s role in securing legitimacy and stability. Lousteau remained resolute despite warnings about the potential consequences.

As the conversation continued, Lousteau returned to Dinah, who was distressed and questioning her decision to come to Paris. He reassured her of his commitment, but the reality of their situation loomed. Madame Schontz later informed Lousteau that Cardot had arranged Félicie’s marriage to another man, effectively ending Lousteau’s prospects.

Despite losing a stable future, Lousteau became more entangled with Dinah, who believed in their love. They began to settle into a life together, with Lousteau trying to keep Dinah entertained and dependent on him. Their relationship, initially passionate, began to resemble the provincial romance Lousteau had once mocked.

In the end, both Lousteau and Dinah found themselves caught in a cycle of dependency and illusion, unaware of the challenges that lay ahead in their unconventional union.

This winter in Paris was for Madame de la Baudraye what October had been in Sancerre. Etienne, eager to introduce Dinah to Parisian life, took her to the theater, where she at first felt self-conscious and feared recognition from acquaintances in Sancerre. Lousteau, vain and ambitious, helped her shed provincial modesty by taking her to fashionable dressmakers and introducing her to Parisian society. Dinah quickly adapted to the city's vibrant life, immersing herself in its culture and entertainments.

One morning a newspaper article reminded her of Sancerre, mentioning Monsieur le Baron de Clagny's promotion. Dinah joked with Lousteau about his lingering affection, revealing the depth of their passion, which still felt fresh and enchanting. They exchanged exaggerated declarations, promising to die for each other if their love faded. Lousteau, however, secretly planned to deceive Dinah when he grew tired of her, viewing her as a valuable asset.

Despite her love, Dinah kept her dignity and ambition. She was more than a charming companion: intelligent and well read, she could hold her own with the most sophisticated women. Lousteau perceived her worth, seeing in her the potential to be both muse and partner.

As Dinah's pregnancy showed, she prepared to retreat from public life. Before doing so she attended the premiere of a play by Nathan, feeling both excitement and anxiety. The evening soured when she sat in a box beside Anna Grossetete, an old acquaintance, and Lousteau abandoned her to socialize. Dinah felt the weight of scrutiny and discomfort, unable to hide her unease.

When Clagny visited her at the theater he expressed concern for her reputation and warned about the consequences of her liaison with Lousteau. Dinah remained resolute about her happiness and the sacrifices she'd made for love. Clagny's visit stirred nostalgia and regret, but she stayed committed to her choices.

After the birth of her child, Dinah became a devoted mother and housekeeper, resolved to support Lousteau's career. She pawned her jewelry to help with finances, believing her sacrifices would secure a better life for them. Lousteau's financial irresponsibility and laziness soon became apparent, and Dinah bore the household burden.

Despite efforts to help him, Lousteau continued to struggle with writing and debts. Dinah's love fueled her determination to help him succeed, but she felt the strain. As she worked tirelessly to keep their home, Lousteau grew increasingly indifferent and dependent on her.

The arrival of a second child increased Dinah's burdens. Lousteau's debts mounted and he became more detached from domestic life, seeking solace with friends and other women. Feeling the weight of her sacrifices, Dinah began to question whether their relationship was sustainable.

Tensions rose, and Dinah's jealousy and insecurity surfaced. She tried to regain control by confronting Lousteau about his behavior and their finances, but her efforts often led to conflict. She struggled to maintain dignity while managing the complexities of their love.

The turning point came when Dinah's estranged husband, Monsieur de la Baudraye, reappeared seeking a power of attorney to manage an inheritance. The encounter forced her to confront her past and the reality of her situation. Clagny, now Attorney-General, intervened to protect her interests, negotiating an allowance from her husband in exchange for her cooperation.

As Dinah grappled with betrayal and loss, she reached a crossroads. Lousteau's lack of ambition and commitment weighed on her, and she realized her sacrifices might not be reciprocated. The love that once felt like refuge began to feel like a burden, and she questioned whether she could keep supporting a man unwilling to fight for their future.

In the end, Dinah's journey through love, sacrifice, and self-discovery revealed the complexities of her relationship with Lousteau. She learned true love requires not only passion but mutual respect and commitment. Facing motherhood and the consequences of her choices, Dinah emerged stronger, determined to reclaim her identity and find her own path in a world that often sought to define her.

From that day on Etienne lived in luxury, and Dinah could hold her own among the best-dressed women in Paris. Lousteau, however, feigned despair among his friends, claiming he was ruined by Madame de la Baudraye and lamenting that no one could deliver him from Dinah, for he believed her love so strong she would harm herself if he asked. Despite his complaints, Lousteau was unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, distressed by Dinah's disgrace, warned her of betrayal; she answered simply, “I know it.” When Clagny asked if she still loved Lousteau, she responded passionately, “I would lose my soul for you!” The confession left Clagny speechless and in tears.

Dinah gradually recognized Lousteau’s true nature. She described him to her mother as a poet, lazy and pleasure-prone, yet someone she could not hate because she felt responsible for him, believing she was his only support. “Oh, my Dinah!” her mother cried, “what a hell you live in! What gives you the strength to persist?” Dinah replied, “I will be a mother to him!”

In her painful situation Dinah made compromises with herself, only feeling the weight of her dishonor when confronted by others. She resolved to emulate Madame de Pompadour to keep his affection—her love turning into a burden. Lousteau’s insincerity and feigned devotion further degraded her, yet she clung to hope of a revival of his love and found a twisted joy in being his plaything.

She often rehearsed the tragedy of Le Dernier Jour d’un condamné, imagining their separation, but each word or gesture from Lousteau pulled her back into love. She even contemplated suicide while walking in her garden, yet her devotion remained unexhausted. The romance of Adolphe became her guide; she vowed not to become like Ellenore, refusing to express her pain in tears or reproaches.

Meanwhile Monsieur de la Baudraye, trying to reclaim fortune, manipulated his wife’s inheritance—reducing its apparent value while pocketing the difference. He invested in land expecting returns and considered himself free of debt to Dinah, planning to restore her fortune later. He envisioned himself a prominent figure in Sancerre, aspiring to a title and noble lifestyle.

By early 1842 Dinah felt like a mere reserve for Lousteau, sacrificing herself for his comfort. She donned mourning attire as a sign of remorse, and her mother, worried for her, sought to intervene. The reality of their situation proved far from the dramatic conclusions of literature; it was marked by fatigue and disillusionment.

Madame Piedefer, with Clagny’s help, urged Dinah to consider life apart from Lousteau, suggesting her husband would allow her to keep the children and live in Paris. Dinah left negotiations to Clagny, who assured her her husband would support her return to a respectable life.

Monsieur de la Baudraye had been secretly investing in a luxurious Paris residence to elevate his status. He aimed for a peerage and a respectable image with a well-furnished home. He wrote to Dinah, inviting her to live with him in Paris and promising financial support for their children’s education.

Dinah, now a Countess, felt the weight of her new title and its expectations, preparing for social prominence that sharply contrasted with her earlier struggles. On a fateful day in May 1842 she paid off her debts and invited Lousteau to dinner, signaling a decisive change in their relationship.

When Lousteau arrived he was struck by Dinah’s transformation. She told him of her new status and declared their separation, expressing enduring affection but insisting on a calm parting. Lousteau, feeling betrayed, accused her of ruining his life, but Dinah stood firm, asserting her independence. The conversation turned bitter; Lousteau lamented his lost potential and Dinah refused to accept blame. Their final exchange mixed sorrow and defiance. Dinah returned home feeling both liberated and heartbroken.

In her new life Dinah transformed her home into a model of comfort, spending more than her husband anticipated. When Monsieur de la Baudraye returned to Paris he was pleased with her efforts, unaware of the emotional turmoil beneath her polished exterior. Though charming and fashionable, Dinah felt a void, missing the passion and excitement of her past.

As she navigated a new social circle, Dinah found herself haunted by memories of Lousteau. Despite her success she struggled to connect with other women and felt isolated in her achievements. Clagny, ever supportive, tried to introduce her to influential figures, but she remained unfulfilled.

One evening after a theater performance Dinah confided in Clagny about lingering feelings for Lousteau. He revealed that Lousteau was living with another woman, stirring conflicting emotions in Dinah and prompting her to immerse herself in social life, seeking success and recognition.

In March 1843 Dinah was appointed to collect donations for a charitable cause, a turning point in her public image. Her efforts were recognized and she began to regain standing in society, though emotional scars remained and she often reflected on her choices.

As seasons changed Dinah’s life continued to evolve. She returned to Sancerre for the summer, where her husband doted on their children, and she found herself caught between past and present. Relationship dynamics shifted as she faced the challenges of motherhood and societal expectation.

In June 1844 Monsieur de la Baudraye proudly introduced his children to a cousin, highlighting the family’s newfound status. Dinah, maintaining composure, grappled with complexities, balancing roles as mother, Countess, and a woman shaped by her experiences.

The narrative concludes with unresolved tension: Dinah navigates a dual existence, torn between love and ambition, public success and private longing, reflecting the intricate interplay of passion, social pressure, and personal sacrifice that defines her journey.
