**CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS**  

In various provinces of France, many Chevaliers de Valois exist, including one in Alencon, who is the focus of this tale. These chevaliers, though sharing a name, were largely unaware of each other and accepted the Bourbon monarchy without question. The Alencon chevalier, now fifty-eight but claiming to be fifty, was a tall, thin, and somewhat impoverished gentleman, known for his excellent manners and social skills. He dined out daily and was a witty storyteller, often sharing anecdotes from the reign of Louis XV and the early Revolution. 

Despite his age, he maintained a youthful appearance, aided by a vigorous constitution and a notable nose that divided his pale face. His appetite was legendary, and he claimed to suffer from a "hot liver," a condition that seemed to justify his voracious eating habits. The chevalier's charm lay in his delicate grooming and refined manners, which endeared him to the local aristocracy. He was known for his impeccable linen and a maroon coat that, while worn, was always clean. 

Living modestly in a second-floor room rented from Madame Lardot, the town's washerwoman, the chevalier's life was marked by social engagements and card games. His only income was a modest annuity, supplemented by occasional winnings at cards. Despite his financial struggles, he was a beloved figure in Alencon, known for his kindness and discretion. 

However, beneath this charming exterior lay a secret ambition: the chevalier aspired to marry a wealthy old maid, hoping to elevate his social standing. This desire drove much of his behavior and interactions within the community.

**CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS**  

One spring morning, as the chevalier prepared for the day, a young girl named Suzanne entered his room. She was a work-girl at Madame Lardot's and had come to share news that could impact both their lives. Suzanne, ambitious and clever, sought to escape her provincial life and was considering a move to Paris. 

The chevalier, with his characteristic charm, encouraged her ambitions while maintaining a paternal demeanor. He advised her to pursue a wealthy man, suggesting Monsieur du Bousquier, a local bachelor with a dubious past. The chevalier recognized the rivalry between himself and du Bousquier, both of whom were vying for the affections of Mademoiselle Cormon, a wealthy old maid.

Suzanne, eager to leave Alencon, saw an opportunity to manipulate du Bousquier into supporting her move to Paris. She presented herself as a victim of circumstance, leveraging her situation to extract money from him. Du Bousquier, caught off guard by her boldness, ultimately agreed to provide her with funds, revealing his own insecurities and ambitions.

As Suzanne left, she felt empowered by her ability to navigate the complexities of her relationships with men, contrasting sharply with the chevalier's more genteel approach. Her cunning and ambition set the stage for a deeper exploration of the social dynamics at play in Alencon, where both men and women sought to secure their futures through marriage and social maneuvering.

**CHAPTER III. ATHANASE**

Madame Granson, a widow living on a modest pension and her own property, struggled to support her son, Athanase, who contributed to their income with a small salary from the mayor’s office. Their home in a provincial town was characterized by its simplicity and the weight of poverty, which shaped their lives and morals.

Athanase, a 23-year-old with a thin, pale appearance and a contemplative nature, felt stifled by his surroundings. Despite his potential, he lacked the encouragement and opportunities that might have allowed him to flourish. His pride and timidity hindered his ambitions, leaving him to grapple with unfulfilled dreams and a deep-seated love for his cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. This love, initially rooted in a desire for financial stability, evolved into genuine affection, complicated by societal expectations and his own insecurities.

In the provincial setting, marriage was often viewed as a means of securing material comfort, and Athanase's aspirations were further complicated by the presence of rivals like du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois. The societal norms of the time dictated that a young man of limited means could not pursue a relationship with a wealthy woman without facing ridicule.

Athanase's love for Mademoiselle Cormon grew quietly, fueled by his isolation and the limited options available to him. His mother, aware of her son's feelings, recognized the potential benefits of such a match but also understood the obstacles posed by their financial situation and Cormon's age. She remained hopeful, waiting for the right moment to advocate for her son.

Meanwhile, Suzanne, a young woman from a different social class, entered the scene with her own ambitions. After a brief encounter with Athanase, she felt an inexplicable connection to him, which ignited her desire to help him rise above his circumstances. However, her aspirations were clouded by her own struggles and the societal pressures she faced.

As the narrative unfolds, the characters converge at a dinner hosted by Mademoiselle Cormon, where tensions and hidden motivations come to light. Madame Granson, armed with knowledge of Suzanne's predicament, saw an opportunity to leverage the situation against du Bousquier, whom she viewed as a rival for her son's affections.

Athanase, unaware of the machinations surrounding him, prepared for the dinner with a mix of hope and anxiety. He longed to express his feelings to Cormon but was burdened by the weight of his circumstances. Meanwhile, Suzanne, having made a bold decision to leave her past behind, set her sights on a new life in Paris, where she hoped to find success and perhaps rekindle her connection with Athanase.

The chapter highlights the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in a provincial setting, illustrating how the characters navigate their desires and the constraints imposed by their environment.

**CHAPTER IV. MADEMOISELLE CORMON**

In many second-class prefectures of France, there exists a salon that serves as a gathering place for influential yet not elite members of the community. The hosts of these salons are well-regarded and invited to various social events, but they do not mingle with the highest echelons of society, who prefer to maintain distance, communicating through formal invitations instead.

This salon, where lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates convene, wields significant influence. Its members are characterized by their indifference to luxury and fashion, valuing land acquisition over material wealth. They are resistant to outside influences, particularly from Paris, and often dictate local administrative changes, isolating officials who do not meet their approval.

Gossip in this society can carry the weight of judgment, and young women are rarely present unless seeking validation. The dominance of one household can provoke envy among others, leading to gatherings in the homes of more neutral figures when no suitable host is available.

For years, the upper classes of Alencon gathered at the home of Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, an old maid whose fortune was coveted by her relatives and two old bachelors. She lived with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, in a family that had historically mingled with nobility, producing stewards and magistrates. The Cormon family had a long-standing reputation, with connections to the nobility through marriage.

Mademoiselle Cormon's residence, built in the time of Henri IV, is a notable structure in Alencon, admired for its architecture and cleanliness, which reflects the meticulous nature of its owner. The house features a courtyard with a flowerbed, a well-kept garden, and a layout typical of provincial homes, embodying the calm and monotony of provincial life.

The salon itself is modestly decorated, with a large dining room and a salon filled with family portraits and furniture that reflects the provincial spirit. The atmosphere is one of routine and familiarity, with regular gatherings for card games and socializing among the same group of people, who control significant electoral power and wealth in the region.

Despite her social standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remains unmarried, a situation complicated by her desire to marry a nobleman and the societal pressures of her time. The political climate from 1788 to 1815 made it difficult for her to find a suitable match, as she was wary of marrying a soldier or an older man. Her insistence on being loved for herself led her to set traps for potential suitors, ultimately leading to disappointment and a growing distrust of men.

As she aged, her fears of being married for her wealth intensified, and her character became increasingly rigid. Public opinion turned against her, speculating on her reasons for remaining single, while she remained unaware of the affections directed toward her, particularly from Athanase Granson, one of her suitors.

Mademoiselle Cormon's physical appearance, once vibrant, began to fade as she approached her forties. Her attempts to maintain her youth through religious practices only deepened her despair. Despite her longing for companionship and children, she found herself increasingly isolated, as suitable men were either too young or too old, or lacked the qualities she sought.

Her uncle, the Abbe de Sponde, was oblivious to her desires, focused instead on his spiritual life. Mademoiselle Cormon's lack of awareness regarding her suitors' affections, particularly Athanase's, further complicated her situation. She remained steadfast in her principles, refusing to compromise her values for the sake of marriage.

As time passed, her reputation as an old maid became a source of ridicule in Alencon, despite her charitable nature and alignment with local customs. Mademoiselle Cormon's life exemplified the struggles of a woman caught between societal expectations and personal desires, ultimately leading to a profound sense of loneliness and unfulfilled aspirations.

**CHAPTER V. AN OLD MAID’S HOUSEHOLD**

To understand the dynamics of Mademoiselle Cormon's household, we must consider her and the Abbe de Sponde alongside their loyal servants: Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette. 

Jacquelin, a forty-year-old man who had served the household for twenty-two years, was short, stout, and ruddy, resembling a Breton sailor. He performed various tasks, from waiting at table to grooming the mare, Penelope, who was cherished by all. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if they married. They quietly saved their wages, hoping for Mademoiselle’s marriage to free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, was said to have a strong influence over Mademoiselle. Mariette, the cook, had been with the family for fifteen years and was skilled in preparing local delicacies.

Penelope, the old brown-bay mare, was a focal point of affection in the household, having served the family for eighteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon, childless and single, projected her maternal instincts onto the mare, which became a source of constant conversation and care among the household members.

The four servants operated with mechanical precision, but Mademoiselle Cormon, agitated by her fixed ideas, became increasingly difficult to please. She fixated on trivial matters, such as the arrangement of napkins or the timing of Penelope’s meals, leading to petty quarrels that provided her with a sense of purpose. These disputes, while seemingly trivial, were cathartic for her, allowing her to vent her frustrations.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s life revolved around her household, her uncle, her health, and her religious duties. Her obsession with her well-being led her to consult doctors frequently, and she often attributed minor ailments to her diet, leading to humorous exchanges with her servants.

Every season, Mademoiselle Cormon visited her estate at Prebaudet, where she tended to her apple trees and prepared for the upcoming harvest. Her departures were significant events in Alencon, drawing attention and speculation from the townsfolk, particularly regarding her marital status and her relationship with Monsieur du Bousquier, a rejected suitor.

On the day of her farewell, the town buzzed with gossip, especially concerning the scandal involving Suzanne and du Bousquier. Mademoiselle Cormon, preoccupied with packing, was unaware of the brewing scandal that would soon unfold.

During her dinner parties, Mademoiselle Cormon took great pride in her role as hostess, hoping to attract a suitable husband. She often struggled to engage in meaningful conversation, relying on her limited wit and the assistance of the Chevalier de Valois, who would deftly turn her awkward remarks into humorous exchanges. Despite her lack of intellectual depth, she was regarded as a kind-hearted woman, though often the subject of ridicule among the more astute guests.

As the evening progressed, the conversation turned to the local theater and the rector of Alencon, with du Bousquier at the center of the gossip. Mademoiselle Cormon attempted to defend him, inadvertently drawing attention to her own naivety. Her remarks, while innocent, sparked laughter and further gossip among the guests.

The atmosphere shifted as the guests began to discuss du Bousquier’s character and his rumored relationship with Suzanne. Mademoiselle Cormon, still oblivious to the true nature of the situation, expressed concern for Suzanne, revealing her growing awareness of the implications of du Bousquier’s actions.

As the night wore on, Mademoiselle Cormon’s thoughts turned to her own future and the possibility of marriage. She contemplated her feelings for Athanase, a young man connected to her through familial ties, but her musings were interrupted by the arrival of du Bousquier, who remained blissfully unaware of the gossip surrounding him.

The evening concluded with the guests departing, leaving Mademoiselle Cormon to reflect on the events of the night. The discussions had revealed the complexities of social dynamics in Alencon, where personal relationships intertwined with societal expectations and gossip.

In the aftermath, Madame Granson engaged in a heated discussion with her son about the connection between love and political beliefs, highlighting the tensions that existed within their household. The chapter closes with a sense of uncertainty regarding the future of Mademoiselle Cormon and the relationships that defined her life.

**CHAPTER VI. FINAL DISAPPOINTMENT AND ITS FIRST RESULT**

The following day, Mademoiselle Cormon, traveling back to Prebaudet with her servant Josette, was unexpectedly propelled toward marriage by a letter from her uncle, the Abbe de Sponde. The letter announced that Monsieur de Troisville, a Russian army officer and grandson of a family friend, sought refuge in Alencon and required hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, Mademoiselle Cormon hastily prepared to return to town, disregarding the rain and her usual meticulousness.

Upon her return, the town buzzed with gossip about her sudden arrival and the potential marriage. Mademoiselle Cormon, filled with hope, envisioned herself as the Vicomtesse de Troisville. She was determined to impress her guest, whom she assumed to be a bachelor around forty years old. As she prepared for his arrival, she felt a mix of excitement and anxiety about her home’s appearance and her own readiness.

When Monsieur de Troisville finally arrived, he was greeted by the Abbe de Sponde and Mademoiselle Cormon. He was distinguished and charming, resembling a refined gentleman rather than a soldier. Their dinner conversation flowed smoothly, with Mademoiselle Cormon eager to impress him with her hospitality and the beauty of her home.

However, the evening took an unexpected turn when Monsieur de Troisville casually mentioned his wife, revealing that he had been married for sixteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon fainted at this revelation, and du Bousquier, a local suitor, rushed to her aid. The incident sparked a flurry of gossip throughout Alencon, with townsfolk speculating about the nature of her relationship with the viscount.

As Mademoiselle Cormon recovered, she realized that her hopes for marriage had been dashed. The town's curiosity about her situation only intensified her embarrassment. In the aftermath, both du Bousquier and the Chevalier de Valois sought her attention, each hoping to win her favor. Ultimately, Mademoiselle Cormon decided to accept du Bousquier's proposal, seeing it as a way to regain her dignity and silence the town's chatter.

In a moment of vulnerability, she agreed to marry du Bousquier, asking him to lie about the timeline of their engagement to avoid scandal. Their engagement was soon announced, and the town buzzed with the news, marking a significant shift in Mademoiselle Cormon's life. Despite the circumstances, she found a sense of resolution in her decision, even as the specter of her earlier hopes lingered in the background.

**CHAPTER VII. OTHER RESULTS**

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he masked his turmoil. He learned of the engagement while at the home of Chief Justice du Ronceret, where his mother was playing cards. Athanase had long harbored hopes for Mademoiselle Cormon, and the news felt like a catastrophe. His education under the Imperial system had instilled a sense of fatalism in him, leading to a deep despair.

In the aftermath, Athanase wandered the picturesque Sarthe region, finding solace in its beauty, though he appeared increasingly withdrawn to those around him. His political engagement faded, and he became a figure of indifference in Alencon, with no one to share his grief. The arrival of Suzanne, who had been inspired by tales of romantic adventures, briefly stirred his heart, but she was unaware of his suffering.

Rumors about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage circulated, with some doubting its validity until the contract was signed. The marriage itself was marked by ominous signs, including a lack of celebration and the bride entering the church with her left foot, which locals interpreted as a bad omen. The couple quickly retreated to Prebaudet, and du Bousquier began modernizing the Cormon estate, alarming the townspeople with his lavish spending.

Athanase’s despair deepened, leading him to contemplate leaving Alencon. He shared his feelings with his mother, who was unaware of his true intentions. After a heartfelt conversation, he left her to go to the Sarthe, where he ultimately took his own life, believing it would spare her further pain.

Madame Granson received the news of her son’s death with disbelief, only to later learn of his love for Mademoiselle Cormon through the Chevalier de Valois. The community reacted with shock, expressing regret for not having supported him during his struggles. Madame Granson’s grief was profound and private, contrasting with the public mourning for a young man whose potential was cut short.

In the wake of Athanase’s death, Madame du Bousquier returned to Alencon, where she encountered Madame Granson. The two women shared a moment of silent understanding, marked by the weight of their respective losses. Meanwhile, du Bousquier’s marriage began to reveal its darker side, as he exerted control over his wife and the household, diminishing her autonomy.

As time passed, Madame du Bousquier’s life became increasingly constrained by her husband’s ambitions and the societal expectations surrounding them. Despite her initial happiness, she found herself trapped in a marriage devoid of affection, leading to a gradual decline in her spirit. The Chevalier de Valois, observing the changes in her life, sought to expose du Bousquier’s true nature, further complicating the dynamics within Alencon’s social circles.

The marriage of Mademoiselle Cormon had significant repercussions, dividing the town into factions and altering the political landscape. Du Bousquier, now a prominent figure, leveraged his wealth and influence to reshape Alencon, but his personal life remained fraught with tension and dissatisfaction.

Madame du Bousquier’s internal conflict grew as she reconciled her duty to her husband with her lingering feelings for the Chevalier. The societal pressures and her own moral compass forced her into a life of quiet suffering, where she maintained a facade of contentment while grappling with her past choices.

Ultimately, the story illustrates the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations, revealing how personal tragedies intertwine with broader social dynamics. The characters’ lives reflect the struggles between individual desires and the constraints imposed by society, leading to a poignant exploration of human relationships and the consequences of unfulfilled dreams.
