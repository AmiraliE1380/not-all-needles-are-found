In this poignant narrative, Honoré de Balzac examines fear and love as young lovers seek solace in each other, blending personal experience with poetic insight.

In 1819, while traveling from Paris to Moulins, the narrator must ride outside a coach for lack of money. He befriends a wealthier traveler, and they bond over discussions of love for women in their mid-thirties to forties, confessing intimate details about their older mistresses and the risks they’ve taken for love. Their camaraderie deepens until the coach overturns, killing the narrator’s companion. In his last moments the young man entrusts the narrator with informing his mistress of his death and gives him a key to his house and letters.

At La Charité, the narrator delivers on his friend’s final request, encountering grief among the household staff but focusing on the sorrow awaiting the Countess de Montpersan. With little money, he walks to her chateau, weighed by the emotional burden of his errand. Upon arrival he has trouble finding the countess, finally aided by a young girl who leads him through the park.

The story captures the complexities of love, loss, and the burden of carrying news that alters lives, showing how a simple journey can become a profound encounter with fate. The narrator reflects on the fragile balance between joy and sorrow in human relationships.

Before meeting Juliette and her husband, he steels himself to avoid being mistaken for a local assessor. He discreetly tells the Count his purpose, which visibly disturbs him. Juliette grows anxious when told of her companion’s illness, pleading for the truth; they find her hiding in a hayloft, sobbing.

That night the Countess seeks confirmation. The narrator recounts the accident but spares the most gruesome details, handing her a lock of her companion’s hair as a tangible reminder. The next day feels surreal as he contemplates Juliette’s nobility and selflessness, which deepen his understanding of love and sacrifice.
