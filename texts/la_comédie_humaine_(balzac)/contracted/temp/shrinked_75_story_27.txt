**The Commission in Lunacy**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Clara Bell*  

**Dedication**  
Dedicated to Monsieur le Contre-Amiral Bazoche, Governor of the Isle of Bourbon, by the grateful writer.

In 1828, at around one o’clock in the morning, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac left a house on Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré. They decided to walk to the boulevard, as neither had a carriage. Their conversation turned to the Marquise d’Espard, whom Rastignac admired. Bianchon, however, cautioned him against pursuing her, comparing her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting that she was not as youthful as Rastignac believed. 

Rastignac argued that the Marquise’s social status and wealth made her an attractive prospect, especially since he was struggling financially after marrying off his sisters. Bianchon, a physician, expressed his disdain for women of fashion, asserting that they often lacked genuine character and were driven by selfishness. He warned Rastignac that the Marquise might be in debt despite her outward charm.

Bianchon recounted his experiences with high society, describing the superficiality and moral decay he observed. He believed that a woman of true virtue would not be fashionable, as the qualities that elevate a woman in society often stem from vices. Rastignac countered that a woman of fashion could provide significant advantages for an ambitious man, likening her to a tool for success.

Despite Bianchon’s warnings, Rastignac remained enamored with the idea of marrying the Marquise, valuing her social connections over the virtues of a more devoted partner. Bianchon reluctantly agreed to help Rastignac by introducing him to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, who was known for his strict adherence to the law.

The next morning, Bianchon set out to visit Popinot, who lived in a dilapidated area of Paris. The judge was characterized by his unkempt appearance and lack of ambition, having been overlooked for promotions despite his legal acumen. His life was marked by a commitment to justice, often at the expense of his own comfort and reputation.

Popinot was a diligent and compassionate judge, known for his ability to discern the truth in legal matters. He was respected for his knowledge of criminal law but remained a supernumerary judge due to his modest nature and lack of political maneuvering. His life was a testament to the struggles of a good man in a flawed system, where ambition often overshadowed merit.

As Bianchon approached Popinot’s residence, he reflected on the challenges of persuading the judge to assist Rastignac. Despite the difficulties, he was determined to fulfill his friend’s request, aware of the complexities involved in navigating the world of high society and the legal system. 

In summary, the narrative explores themes of ambition, social status, and the moral dilemmas faced by individuals in pursuit of success. Bianchon’s skepticism about the Marquise serves as a counterpoint to Rastignac’s aspirations, highlighting the tension between genuine character and societal expectations. The story ultimately critiques the superficiality of fashionable society while portraying the struggles of a principled man within the judicial system.

Twelve years prior to the events of this story, in 1816, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food to the impoverished in his neighborhood during a severe scarcity. Despite his initial reluctance to remain in the Rue du Fouarre, he became deeply involved in alleviating the suffering around him. Over five years, he transformed from a judge focused solely on legal outcomes to a compassionate figure, akin to Saint Vincent de Paul, dedicated to helping the poor. His charitable work consumed his resources, but he found fulfillment in his role as a protector and benefactor to those in need.

By the end of his second year of service, Popinot had converted his storeroom into a parlor for the needy, where he meticulously recorded donations and requests for assistance. His aide, Lavienne, helped manage the influx of people seeking aid, which filled the room with the desperate and destitute from dawn until late morning. The atmosphere was often stifling, yet the poor revered Popinot, who worked tirelessly to provide them with food, shelter, and employment.

Popinot's modest inherited fortune and his late wife's wealth allowed him to live frugally while dedicating himself to charity. He was known for his absent-mindedness regarding personal appearance, a trait often associated with intellectual pursuits. Despite his significant contributions to society, he remained unrecognized by his peers, having not received the Legion of Honor.

The narrative shifts to a case involving the Marquis d’Espard, whose wife sought a Commission in Lunacy for him. Bianchon, a doctor and Popinot's nephew, visits the Rue du Fouarre, where he finds his uncle amidst a crowd of impoverished individuals. The scene is vivid, with various characters representing the struggles of the poor, each with their own stories of hardship.

Popinot's attire is disheveled, reflecting his preoccupation with his charitable work. He engages with the petitioners, providing assistance where he can, while Lavienne manages the flow of people. Bianchon expresses concern about the state of the Marquis d’Espard, and Popinot reads the petition detailing the Marquis's alleged mental decline and the influence of a woman named Jeanrenaud over him.

The petition outlines the Marquis's erratic behavior, financial mismanagement, and obsession with China, suggesting that he may be under some form of undue influence. Popinot and Bianchon discuss the implications of the petition, with Bianchon suggesting that the influence could be akin to a form of possession. Popinot remains skeptical but acknowledges the need to investigate further.

As the conversation unfolds, Popinot reflects on the nature of the allegations and the potential motivations behind them. He considers the possibility that the Marquise may have ulterior motives in seeking the Commission in Lunacy for her husband. Ultimately, Bianchon persuades Popinot to visit Madame d’Espard to hear her side of the story, emphasizing the importance of understanding both perspectives in the case.

Popinot agrees to the visit, recognizing the necessity of gathering all relevant information before making a judgment. The chapter concludes with a sense of anticipation for the upcoming meeting, highlighting the complexities of human relationships and the challenges of navigating the legal and moral implications of the case.

The following evening, Bianchon climbed his uncle Popinot's staircase to find him working on a complex judgment. Popinot, dressed in an old, stained coat due to a delay from the tailor, appeared disheveled, but Bianchon managed to straighten him up a bit before they entered the Marquise’s room.

Madame d’Espard had been a prominent figure in Paris for seven years, having separated from her husband in 1815. At thirty-three, she maintained a youthful appearance through strict beauty regimens, reminiscent of historical figures like Diane de Poitiers. Her efforts to preserve her looks were so extreme that they drew comparisons to a famous Polish lady known for her longevity and vitality. Despite being a mother of two, Madame d’Espard was still considered fashionable, largely due to her social connections and the absence of visible signs of aging.

After her separation, Madame d’Espard lived a quiet life until 1820, when she re-entered society, hosting gatherings and establishing herself among the elite. She became close with influential figures, including a duchess and a countess, which helped her gain political influence. Her drawing-room became a hub for ambitious men, and she skillfully navigated the complexities of Parisian social life, maintaining a façade of innocence while wielding considerable power.

Popinot and Bianchon arrived at the Marquise’s boudoir, where she sat elegantly, flanked by Rastignac and a silent man, the Chevalier d’Espard. The Marquise’s appearance was striking, with a carefully curated environment that reflected her status. Popinot, however, was focused on the case at hand, which involved her estranged husband, M. d’Espard, whom she claimed was mentally unstable.

As the conversation unfolded, Madame d’Espard recounted her version of events leading to their separation, painting herself as a victim of her husband’s whims. Popinot, however, probed deeper, questioning her motives and the financial implications of her situation. He noted the luxurious lifestyle she maintained, which seemed at odds with her claims of financial distress. The Marquise attempted to deflect his inquiries, but Popinot remained steadfast, suggesting that her lavish spending could indicate ulterior motives in her petition for her husband’s incapacity.

The tension in the room escalated as Popinot pressed for clarity on her financial situation, hinting that her request might be driven by self-interest rather than genuine concern for her children. The Marquise, feeling cornered, defended her actions as necessary for securing her children’s future, but Popinot’s scrutiny revealed cracks in her narrative.

After their meeting, Popinot resolved to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, the woman linked to M. d’Espard, to uncover more about the situation. The next day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrived, presenting herself as a comically unassuming figure, far from the seductive temptress implied by the Marquise. She expressed concern for M. d’Espard, indicating a willingness to cooperate, which left Popinot intrigued about the true dynamics at play.

As the story unfolded, it became clear that the Marquise’s polished exterior masked a complex web of ambition and manipulation, while Popinot’s role as a judge required him to navigate these treacherous waters with caution and discernment. The case promised to reveal not only the truth about M. d’Espard’s mental state but also the lengths to which individuals would go to protect their interests in the cutthroat world of Parisian society.

The text explores the life of M. le Marquis d’Espard, a nobleman living in a dilapidated mansion in Paris, and the impact of seemingly trivial events on significant outcomes. Following a minor illness, the judge's absence delayed a crucial meeting, paralleling historical events where small incidents led to major consequences.

D’Espard's home, once grand, had fallen into disrepair, reflecting the decline of nobility in France. The house, located in a steep area, was chosen for its garden, which benefited his children’s health. Despite its state, d’Espard managed to create a peaceful and dignified environment, with modest yet tasteful decor that conveyed a sense of order and tranquility.

His family life was private, with only a few loyal servants. D’Espard’s decision to isolate his children from the outside world led to misunderstandings among neighbors, who viewed his behavior as eccentric. Gossip painted him as a madman, exacerbated by his frugal lifestyle and the perception that he was financially ruined. This reputation was fueled by the tenants’ jealousy and resentment towards his noble status, which they believed was undeserved without wealth.

D’Espard and his wife were true aristocrats, embodying the values of their lineage. They instilled a sense of dignity and pride in their children, who exhibited the characteristics of their noble heritage. The Marquis, at around fifty, presented a refined appearance but was perceived as cold and eccentric due to his mannerisms and speech. His children, Comte Clement and Vicomte Camille, were handsome and graceful, reflecting their noble upbringing. Clement, nearing sixteen, was transitioning into young adulthood, while Camille was still in a more youthful stage of education.

Overall, the narrative illustrates the complexities of social status, the impact of isolation, and the misunderstandings that arise from societal perceptions, particularly regarding the declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday holiday, Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard. Early in the morning, his sons, Clement and Camille, played in the garden, wrestling and making noise, which woke their father, the Marquis. The Marquis enjoyed watching them, finding joy in their playful antics. However, the portress, upon Popinot's arrival, informed him of the Marquis' supposed madness, claiming he encouraged his children to fight.

Popinot was led to the third floor, where he encountered a shabby office filled with papers and an old clerk. After a brief introduction, Popinot met the Marquis, who was reading by the fire. The judge explained that he was there to investigate a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by the Marquise d’Espard. The Marquis, initially taken aback, regained his composure and asked why he had not been notified of the petition. Popinot explained that such notifications were unnecessary for those deemed insane.

The Marquis expressed concern about the implications of the inquiry on his wife. Popinot assured him of confidentiality and asked for an explanation regarding allegations of financial impropriety involving a Madame Jeanrenaud. The Marquis, visibly distressed, revealed a family secret tied to the Jeanrenaud family, whose fortune had been wrongfully acquired during the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. He explained that his ancestors had benefited from the execution of a Protestant merchant, and he felt compelled to make restitution to the Jeanrenauds, whom he had located in France.

Despite his wife's lack of understanding, the Marquis had committed to repaying the Jeanrenauds, which strained his finances. He had chosen to educate his sons himself, sacrificing his social standing to ensure they grew up with integrity. The judge listened intently, moved by the Marquis' noble intentions and the burden of his family's past.

As the conversation continued, the Marquis invited Popinot to his home, revealing that he had rented the office space for his publishing venture on China, which was financially successful. He expressed a desire to provide for his children while maintaining their honor. Just then, his sons returned from riding, full of energy and affection for their father, which further illustrated the strong bond they shared.

Madame Jeanrenaud soon entered, expressing her desire to return the money given to the Marquis, fearing for their honor. Popinot intervened, explaining that the payments were legally justified and rooted in honorable motives. He reassured the Marquis that his actions would not be misinterpreted and praised him for his integrity.

Afterward, Popinot returned to his office, reflecting on the encounter and the nobility of the Marquis. The next day, he was summoned by the President of his Court, who informed him that he would be removed from the case due to a potential conflict of interest stemming from his visit with Madame d’Espard. Although Popinot protested, he ultimately accepted the decision, recognizing the importance of maintaining the Court's integrity.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the complexities of social standing, as the Marquis d’Espard grapples with his family's dark history while striving to restore their honor through acts of restitution and dedication to his children.
