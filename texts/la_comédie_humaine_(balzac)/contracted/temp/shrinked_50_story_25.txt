The story opens in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, an elderly man seeking help from attorney M. Derville. Initially treated as a beggar, Chabert reveals he is a significant figure thought dead after the Battle of Eylau, highlighting societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace in post-war France.

Chabert, desperate to reclaim his identity and fortune, insists on meeting Derville despite the clerks' mockery. Derville is shocked by Chabert's ghostly appearance as he recounts his survival after being gravely injured and left for dead, only to find that his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and inherited his estate. Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville agrees to help him.

Three months later, Derville is approached by Crottat, who wants the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville recalls that Chabert's estate was mismanaged by a disreputable lawyer. He learns that Chabert's widow inherited a substantial sum and that Chabert is living in poor conditions with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him dignified despite his squalor. Chabert wishes to reclaim his status as a general, but Derville warns him of the complexities and potential court battle against his wife. Chabert agrees to trust Derville.

Derville meets Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert and requests financial help. Derville decides to assist but insists Chabert should also support Vergniaud. He confronts Madame Ferraud, who initially dismisses Chabert's survival but becomes defensive when warned of legal implications. Derville proposes a compromise to benefit both parties.

A week later, Chabert and Madame Ferraud arrive at Derville's office. The Countess resists a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate. When Chabert confronts her about her reluctance and financial terms, she leaves abruptly, prompting Derville to caution him about her manipulations.

Later, at her country house, the Countess reveals her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Hurt, Chabert suggests they maintain dignity, but the Countess attempts to charm him while plotting to secure her position. When she mentions putting him in an asylum, Chabert's trust shatters, leading him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life is marked by poverty. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumes a compromise was reached. He later discovers Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride. In a final act of integrity, Chabert seeks to repay Derville, demonstrating loyalty despite betrayal. Derville facilitates payment owed to him by the Countess, underscoring the tragic consequences of their lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story reflects on identity, love, and the harsh realities of post-war society. In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visits Bicetre with Derville, finding Chabert now a pauper, displaying childlike innocence and reflecting on the disillusionment faced by those who served Napoleon.
