The story opens in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks Colonel Chabert, who seeks help from attorney M. Derville. Chabert, believed dead after the Battle of Eylau, reveals his true identity and the societal disdain for those fallen from grace in post-war France. Desperate to reclaim his identity and fortune, he shares his survival story, only to find his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and inherited his estate. Moved, Derville agrees to assist him.

Three months later, Derville learns that Chabert's estate is mismanaged and that he lives in poor conditions with dairyman Vergniaud. Despite his squalor, Chabert remains dignified and wishes to reclaim his status, facing a lengthy legal battle against his wife. Derville decides to confront Madame Ferraud, who initially dismisses Chabert but becomes defensive when warned of legal action. 

A week later, during a tense meeting, the Countess feigns ignorance about Chabert, leading to a heated exchange. Later, she reveals her love for another man and suggests putting Chabert in an asylum, prompting him to reject her. Chabert's life becomes marked by poverty, and Derville, unaware of his fate, assumes a compromise was reached. Ultimately, Chabert is found imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride, reflecting on the tragic consequences of their lives.
