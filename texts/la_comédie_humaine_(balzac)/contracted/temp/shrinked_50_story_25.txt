The story opens in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks an old man, Colonel Chabert, who is seeking help from attorney M. Derville. The clerks, dismissive and absorbed in their gossip, initially treat Chabert as a beggar until he reveals his identity as a significant figure thought dead after the Battle of Eylau. Their skepticism reflects societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace, particularly in post-war France.

Chabert, weary and desperate, insists on meeting Derville despite the clerks' mockery. When Derville finally arrives, he is startled by Chabert's ghostly appearance. Chabert recounts his harrowing story of survival after being gravely injured and left for dead, only to find that his wife, Countess Ferraud, has remarried and inherited his estate. Derville, moved by Chabert's plight, offers to help him reclaim his identity and fortune, promising financial assistance.

Three months later, Derville is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Chabert. Derville hesitates, recalling that Chabert's estate was managed by a disreputable lawyer who fled with clients' money. He learns that Chabert's widow inherited a substantial sum after a contested will and that Chabert is living in poor conditions with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him in squalor but still dignified. Chabert expresses his desire to reclaim his life and status as a general. Derville explains the complexities of proving Chabert's identity legally, warning him of a potentially lengthy court battle against his wife, who may contest his claims. Despite feeling disheartened, Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

Derville encounters Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert's well-being and requests financial help. Derville decides to assist but insists that Chabert should also support Vergniaud. He plans to confront Madame Ferraud to gauge her reaction to Chabert's survival. When they meet, she initially dismisses the idea but becomes defensive when Derville warns her of the legal implications and potential public backlash.

Realizing the seriousness of her situation, Madame Ferraud becomes intrigued by the possibility of leveraging Chabert's affection for a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting an amicable settlement that could benefit both parties, positioning himself to navigate the situation to Chabert's advantage.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, arrived at Derville's office from different parts of Paris. Chabert, now dressed in a new outfit, embodied a soldierly dignity, while the Countess followed elegantly. The office atmosphere was light, with clerks joking about the unusual situation.

Derville prepared a memorandum regarding Chabert's identity and their marriage conditions. The Countess resisted a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, wanting to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert entered, tension rose as the Countess feigned ignorance about him, though her reaction suggested otherwise.

Chabert expressed anger over her reluctance to acknowledge him and her financial terms. Feeling cornered, the Countess left abruptly, prompting Derville to warn Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert left in despair.

Later, the Countess took Chabert to her country house, revealing her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Hurt but understanding, Chabert suggested they maintain dignity and avoid scandal. However, the Countess attempted to charm him while plotting to secure her position, leading to a confrontation with her secretary, Delbecq, who suggested exploiting Chabert financially.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronted the Countess, who tried to manipulate him further. He decided to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions surfaced when she mentioned putting him in an asylum. This shattered his trust, prompting him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life was marked by poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumed a compromise had been reached. He later discovered Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of dignity yet maintaining pride. In a final act of integrity, Chabert sought to repay Derville, demonstrating loyalty despite betrayal. Derville facilitated payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story reflects on identity, love, and the harsh realities post-war. 

In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visited Bicetre with Derville, where they found Chabert, now a pauper. Chabert displayed childlike innocence, claiming to be "Hyacinthe." An observing pauper noted his cleverness despite his apparent childishness. Derville lamented the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse, reflecting on the disillusionment faced by those in the legal profession and society's indifference to the fallen.
