La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, runs between cliffs topped with white stone country houses, gardens and vineyards. With favorable southern exposure, generations have terraced the cliffs to grow warm-climate fruit.

A church spire marks nearby Saint-Cyr; further on the Choisille joins the Loire. La Grenadiere stands halfway up the hillside, a hundred paces from the church. An old-fashioned house two or three hundred years old, typical of Touraine, it is reached by a fissure in the rock that provides steps down to the “dike,” the embankment serving as the Paris–Nantes causeway. At the top a gate opens onto a narrow footpath between terraces; trees partly conceal it until the river view widens.

A crumbling Gothic archway leads into a small garden hewn from the rock on the highest terrace. A wooden summer-house draped in jessamine and honeysuckle stands nearby. The house sits above a vine-covered flight of steps, with an arched doorway giving access to cellars carved into the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees—the source of its name—surround the house. The front shows two large windows flanking a rustic door, three dormers in a steep slate roof, and yellowwashed walls with green doors and shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby. To the right is a large dining room with white tile floors; to the left, a sitting room with saffron-coloured walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor holds two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most doors and windows face south, except one opening toward the vineyard. A small timber-framed kitchen, a later addition, contains a rustic pump and well, suggesting La Grenadiere began as a vintner’s retreat for townsfolk of Tours who came for picnics or the grape harvest.

The property includes two acres of vineyard, with a steep slope at the back. A vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable; stone walls and espaliers enclose and maximize the land, while neglected corners fill with fig trees, wildflowers and strawberries.

Modest yet dignified, La Grenadiere is rich in fruit and fragrance. Its vines yield varied grapes amid figs, peaches and melons, while Spanish broom and Italian oleanders lend an exotic note. The Loire flows below; evening breezes bring a sea-scent and distant flowers. Clouds alter the light, revealing the south bank, Tours and hills dotted with châteaux. Owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, the house is a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers—an atmosphere of contentment where the river’s sounds invite quiet contemplation among perennial flowers and fruit.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce; if he wants orchard fruit the rent doubles, and for the vintage it doubles again. What could La Grenadiere be worth? With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head it appears invaluable. Not on the market since it was sold in 1690 for forty thousand francs, it has remained a cherished family possession. The view embraces three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers; its health and beauty defy monetary valuation.

In the spring of a bright year during the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, aged thirteen and eight, and chose La Grenadiere—perhaps for its distance from town. She converted the drawing-room into a bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and had the housekeeper sleep in a closet. The dining-room served as sitting and drawing room, simply and tastefully furnished with plain walnut furniture; the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens defied easy social classification: her plain dress suggested modesty while her polished manners implied higher status. Her reserve provoked local curiosity. Tall and slender with delicate features and a melancholy expression, she had dark eyes that sometimes revealed secret anguish and premature wrinkles. Always in a long black gown with a silk ribbon at the waist and an English-style bonnet, she signaled mourning. Her health seemed fragile; she walked with her children to the bridge of Tours to enjoy fresh air and sunsets.

She visited Tours only twice—once to inquire about the best teachers for her children and once to arrange their lessons. Her rare appearances on the bridge interested townspeople, but little was known of her rank. The owner of La Grenadiere told friends she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, hinting at her husband’s name; the remark stayed within the business community.

To the local elite she remained a mystery. Her natural manners and sweet voice charmed those around her, but her sadness and beauty made her seem untouchable. Several young men fell in love, yet timidity kept them away. She burned all letters she received, underscoring her wish for solitude.

The three tutors admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys, resembling their mother, drew envy from local mothers. Louis-Gaston, the elder, had dark hair and a robust demeanor; Marie-Gaston, the younger, had almost golden hair and a delicate appearance. Contrasting looks matched contrasting temperaments—Louis protective of his brother. Well-behaved and attentive, they rose early, prayed, and observed a scrupulous morning routine. Their interactions with their mother were full of affection, laughter, and an intuitive understanding of her needs. Mme. Willemsens fostered a nurturing home, ensuring her children felt loved. Louis often noticed signs of his mother’s declining strength—small worries she sought to hide—and remained watchful, their shared life largely untouched by outside influence.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during their early breakfast and play to be a pleasing sight for them. She listened to lessons from ten to three, with a midday summer-house lunch they all ate together. Afterward the children played while she rested on the sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

They roamed the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and examining plants and insects, often returning with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during lessons, following attentively without interrupting. She concentrated on her eldest, Louis, encouraging his ambition and asking little about Marie. When lessons ended she walked the tutor to the gate and frankly inquired about Louis’ progress; the tutors reported his strengths and weaknesses honestly.

Their days balanced work and play, leaving little room for quarrels. Her steady love smoothed difficulties and taught her sons moderation and submission. She often wept for joy watching them play, reflecting on their happiness and her own contentment. The hours in the garden-house were precious, full of laughter and the children’s voices; they spoke both English and French thanks to their English nurse, and their mother guided their learning so they could understand the world. She provided engaging books about great historical figures and used those stories to teach ambition and success.

While the children listened, little Marie often fell asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens sometimes grew pensive and tearful. One evening Louis noticed her tears; she confessed fears for their future—that she might leave them alone and without resources—and expressed the deep love that made her sorrow so poignant. Louis, puzzled, promised to take care of Marie. She reassured him that love kept her alive and hoped Touraine’s sunny air might help her recover.

As she spoke more of her possible death, Louis grew more focused and adjusted the family’s life to her melancholy. The once vibrant household grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with growing concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined. By August life had changed: Mme. Willemsens remained beautiful but increasingly frail, striving to preserve her appearance; the house felt somber and they rarely ventured far. Louis, feeling close to his mother, noticed her exhaustion and suggested shorter walks.

With autumn’s sunny days and ripe fruits, Mme. Willemsens spent her time almost entirely with the children. Meanwhile Louis worked late into the night preparing for the École Polytechnique exam. He frequented the bridge of Tours, where a retired naval officer told him tales of adventure; encouraged by his mother, Louis began long walks, climbed trees and learned to swim, slowly changing from a boy into a young man whose sunburned face reflected a new purpose.

When October arrived, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth made the air almost like the Bay of Naples, and the faculty had recommended the area. At midday she sat in the shade with her two children, Louis and Marie, who never left her side. After lessons they sought a life of closeness, free from outside worries: the elder child lying beside her while restless Marie gathered flowers. Mme. Willemsens, with tired eyes and languid movements, smiled at her vibrant children—a poignant scene touched by autumn’s melancholy.

Eventually the doctor ordered her to stay in her room, brightened daily by her favorite flowers, and her children remained near. One early November day she sat at the piano for the last time, gazing at a Swiss landscape above it and her children outside; her fingers moved with passionate feeling. When the doctor arrived he insisted she stay in bed; stunned, she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. Outside she looked skyward, captivated by the clouds, then, anguished, clutched her children’s hands and lamented their uncertain future, fearing how they would judge her when older. Seeing Louis and Marie kneel beside her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days blurred with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers—an ominous sign. As her condition worsened Louis refused to leave. One quiet Sunday night she moved the curtain and solemnly told him tomorrow would be her last day. She instructed him to fetch important papers—birth certificates and a letter she wished to send to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband. As she dictated the letter she hesitated over how to sign it, finally choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She revealed twelve thousand francs saved—their remaining funds—and urged him to keep it safe, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he apprenticed at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes brightened at his resolve. Strength fading, she requested a priest. The next morning, surrounded by loved ones, Mme. Willemsens received the sacrament with her children kneeling beside her. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she passed away.

Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body for burial according to local custom. Only a few mourners attended. Marie, too young to grasp finality, cried as they lowered the coffin. A simple cross marked the grave. Afterwards the children returned to La Grenadiere for a last look before leaving with Annette to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with Marie’s birth certificate and the money; the next day he took Marie to school, gave earnest advice, and departed to apprentice on a warship. A month later, watching the French coast fade, he embraced life at sea with the resolve to honor his mother’s memory, comforted by an old sailor’s words.
