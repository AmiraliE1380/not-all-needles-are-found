La Grenadière by Honoré de Balzac, translated by Ellen Marriage, presents a charming house on the Loire near Tours, set among cliffs, white stone houses, gardens, and vineyards in a climate favorable to fruit growing.

Near the village church of Saint-Cyr, La Grenadière has terraced gardens, trellises, and a rose-filled plot with a wooden summer-house. The rustic dwelling features large windows, a slate roof, yellow-washed walls, and green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a dining room and a sitting room hung with saffron-colored paper; the first floor contains two simple bedrooms and a modern kitchen attached to the main house, suggesting it once served as a town retreat.

The estate includes a small vineyard and a vinedresser’s cottage, enclosed by stone walls and fruit trees. From the terrace one can see the Loire, making the modest home seem both humble and majestic amid the surrounding beauty.

In the spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, choosing La Grenadière for its distance from town. Her modest furnishings left her social rank ambiguous. Tall and slender with delicate features, she attracted local attention during evening walks along the Loire.

Mme. Willemsens went into Tours only twice, and curiosity grew about her origins. Called Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, she remained mysterious but graceful and devoted to her children, Louis-Gaston and Marie-Gaston. She devoted herself to creating a nurturing household, blending lessons and play and teaching moderation and resilience.

As summer advanced, her health failed, casting a somber air over La Grenadière. Despite illness, she treasured time with her children, often walking in the gardens. Unknown to her, Louis studied by night for the entrance exam to the École Polytechnique.

Confined to her room, she spoke of fears for their future, then disclosed her impending death to Louis, charging him to care for Marie and urging him to pursue a career at sea. Comforted by his resolve, she found peace and died surrounded by her children. After the funeral, Louis and Marie prepared for their new lives, Louis determined to honor their mother’s memory.
