La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, flows between cliffs crowned with white stone country houses amid gardens and vineyards. With a favorable southern exposure, the region yields some of the finest fruit, thanks to generations who terraced the cliffs to grow warm-climate produce outdoors.

A church spire marks the nearby village of Saint-Cyr; further along, the Choisille flows into the Loire through a fertile valley. La Grenadiere stands halfway up the hillside, roughly a hundred paces from the church. It is an old-fashioned house of two to three hundred years, typical of picturesque Touraine. A fissure in the rock provides steps down to the “dike,” the embankment that keeps the Loire in its bed and serves as a causeway from Paris to Nantes. At the top of the steps a gate opens onto a narrow footpath between terraces with retaining walls; trees almost conceal the path, and the river view widens as one ascends.

At the path’s end a crumbling Gothic archway leads into a small garden of flowers and rose bushes hewn from the rock on the highest terrace. A wooden summer-house, draped in jessamine and honeysuckle, stands nearby. The house sits centrally in this garden above a vine-covered flight of steps, with an arched doorway giving access to extensive cellars carved into the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees—whose fruit gives the property its name—surround the house. The front has two large windows flanking a rustic door and three dormer windows in a steep slate roof; the walls are yellowwashed with green-painted doors and shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby. To the right is a large dining room with white tile floors; to the left, a sitting room of similar size with saffron-coloured walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor holds two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most doors and windows face south, except one that opens toward the vineyard. A small timber-framed kitchen, a later addition, connects to the house and contains a rustic pump and well, suggesting La Grenadiere was originally a vintner’s retreat for townsfolk of Tours who came for picnics or the grape harvest.

The property includes two acres of vineyard, with a steep slope at the back. A vinedresser’s cottage leans against the western gable, and stone walls or espaliers enclose and maximize the land. Nature fills any neglected corners with fig trees, wildflowers, or strawberries.

La Grenadiere is modest yet dignified, rich in fruit and fragrances, and embodies Touraine’s beauty. Its vines produce various grapes alongside figs, peaches, and melons, while Spanish broom and Italian oleanders add an exotic touch. The Loire flows below, and evening breezes carry scents of the sea and distant flowers. Clouds alter the light and the landscape, revealing views of the south bank, Tours, and surrounding hills dotted with châteaux and parks.

Owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, this vintage house appeals to the imagination as a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers. Its atmosphere is suffused with happiness; the river’s sounds invite contemplation, and the owner can sit among perennial flowers and fruit, feeling the vibrant world around him.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months, excluding vineyard produce. If he wants orchard fruit, the rent doubles; for the vintage, it doubles again. What could La Grenadiere be worth? With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head, it seems priceless. It has not been on the market since it was sold in 1690 for forty thousand francs, remaining a cherished family possession since then. The view from La Grenadiere embraces three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers, making it impossible to assign a monetary value to the health and beauty it offers.

In the spring of a bright year during the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, a thirteen-year-old and an eight-year-old. She chose La Grenadiere, perhaps drawn by its distance from town. She converted the drawing-room into a bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and the housekeeper slept in a closet. The dining-room served as a combined sitting and drawing room, simply and tastefully furnished with plain walnut furniture; the house's charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens was hard to classify socially; her plain dress invited assumptions, while her manners suggested higher status. Her reserve piqued local curiosity. She was tall and slender, with delicate features and a melancholy expression. Dark eyes, sometimes revealing secret anguish, were framed by premature wrinkles. She wore a long black gown, a silk ribbon at the waist, and an English-style bonnet, signaling mourning. Her health appeared fragile, and she walked with her children to the bridge of Tours to enjoy fresh air and sunsets.

During her time at La Grenadiere she visited Tours only twice: once to inquire about the best teachers for her children and again to arrange their lessons. Her occasional appearances on the bridge interested the townspeople, but little was known of her status. The owner of La Grenadiere mentioned to friends that she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, hinting at her husband's name; this revelation stayed within the business community.

To the local elite she remained a mystery. Her natural manners and sweet voice charmed those around her, but her sadness and beauty made her seem almost untouchable. Several young men fell in love, yet timidity kept them from approaching. She burned all letters she received, indicating a wish for solitude and a life free from outside concerns.

The three tutors allowed at La Grenadiere admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys, resembling their mother, drew envy from local mothers. Louis-Gaston, the elder, had dark hair and a robust demeanor, while Marie-Gaston, the younger, had almost golden hair and a delicate appearance. Their contrasting looks echoed differing personalities, Louis showing a protective nature toward his brother.

The boys were well-behaved and attentive, reflecting careful upbringing. They rose early, prayed, and observed a scrupulous morning routine. Their interactions with their mother were full of affection, laughter, and an intuitive understanding of her needs. Mme. Willemsens fostered a nurturing atmosphere, ensuring her children felt loved and valued.

Mornings included playful moments, with Louis often taking charge to protect Marie from fatigue. He noticed signs of his mother's declining strength—small worries she sought to hide—and though she reassured him, he remained vigilant. Their bond was profound, characterized by a shared life of love and mutual understanding, largely untouched by external influences.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during her children's early breakfast and play because she wanted to be a pleasing sight for them. She was ready to hear their lessons from ten in the morning until three in the afternoon, with a midday lunch in the summer-house where they all ate together. After the meal the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

The children explored the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and investigating plants and insects, and often ran to their mother with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during their lessons, following attentively without interrupting. She concentrated on her eldest son, Louis, treating him with respect and encouraging his ambition, while asking little about Marie. When lessons ended she would walk the tutor to the gate and inquire about Louis’ progress; the tutors answered frankly about his strengths and weaknesses.

Their days struck a careful balance of work and play, leaving little room for discouragement or quarrels. Her love smoothed over difficulties and taught her sons moderation and submission through steady nurturing. She often shed tears of joy as she watched them play, reflecting on their happiness and on her own contentment.

The hours in the garden-house were precious, full of laughter and the children’s voices. They spoke both English and French, thanks to their English nurse, and their mother skillfully guided their learning so they could understand the world around them. She supplied them with interesting books about great figures in history and used those stories to teach them about ambition and success.

While the children listened to these readings, little Marie would often fall asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens would sometimes grow pensive and tearful. One evening Louis noticed her tears and asked why she was crying. She explained that she feared for their future, that she might leave them alone and without resources, and she expressed the deep love that made her sorrow so poignant.

Louis, puzzled, promised he would take care of Marie. She reassured him that love kept her alive and hoped the sunny air of Touraine might help her recover. From that day, as she spoke more of her impending death, Louis became more focused on his studies and adapted the family’s life to her melancholy. The once vibrant household grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with growing concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined.

By August their life had changed markedly. Mme. Willemsens remained beautiful but increasingly frail, and she made an effort to preserve her appearance. The house, once lively, felt somber and they rarely ventured far. Louis, feeling a deep connection to his mother, noticed her exhaustion and often suggested shorter walks.

With autumn’s sunny days and ripe fruits, Mme. Willemsens spent her time almost entirely with the children, cherishing each moment. Meanwhile Louis worked late into the night, making steady progress in his studies in preparation for the entrance examination to the École Polytechnique. He frequented the bridge of Tours, where a retired naval officer told him tales of adventure; Louis asked many questions and learned of the hardships of military life. Encouraged by his mother, he began taking long walks to build endurance, climbed trees and learned to swim, slowly changing from a boy into a young man whose sunburned face reflected a new purpose.

When October arrived, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth made the air almost like the Bay of Naples, and the faculty had recommended the area. At midday she sat in the shade with her two boys, who never left her side. With lessons over they sought a life of closeness, free from outside worries. The elder boy lay beside her, basking in her affection, while Marie, the more restless child, gathered flowers. Mme. Willemsens, with tired eyes and languid movements, never complained, smiling at her vibrant children—a poignant scene heightened by autumn’s melancholy.

Eventually the doctor ordered her to stay in her room. It was brightened daily by her favorite flowers, and her children remained near. One early November day she sat at the piano for the last time, gazing at a Swiss landscape above it and her children outside. Her fingers moved over the keys with passionate feeling. When the doctor arrived he insisted she stay in bed; she was stunned.

After he left she asked her older son, Louis, to take her to the terrace for one last view of her homeland. As they stepped outside her gaze turned skyward, captivated by the clouds. A look of anguish crossed her face as she clutched her children’s hands, lamenting their uncertain future and fearing what they would think of her life when they grew older. After a moment she saw Louis and Marie kneeling beside her, their love evident. Wishing to take their smiles with her, she returned to bed, never to rise again.

Days passed in a blur with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. The atmosphere was heavy with dread. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers from her room, a sign of decline. As her condition worsened Louis refused to leave her side. One quiet Sunday night he noticed her hand move the curtain; she spoke solemnly, telling him tomorrow would be her last day. She instructed him to retrieve important papers from her table, including their birth certificates and a letter she wished to send to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband.

As she dictated the letter she hesitated over how to sign it, finally choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She then revealed that she had twelve thousand francs saved—their remaining funds—and urged him to keep the money close, as everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, planning to send her to school while he apprenticed at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes sparkled with joy at his resolve, and she felt a surge of hope. But her strength waned and she requested a priest. Louis fetched Annette, who hurried to find one. The next morning, surrounded by loved ones, Mme. Willemsens received the sacrament, her children kneeling beside her. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she eventually passed away, leaving her children in shock.

After her death Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body for burial, following local customs. Only a few mourners attended—far fewer than the grand send-off she might have had under different circumstances. Marie, still too young to grasp death’s finality, cried as they buried her.

A simple cross marked the grave, inscribed with a plea for prayers. After the burial the children returned to La Grenadiere for a final look at their home before leaving with Annette, who took them to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis shared his plans with Annette, entrusting her with Marie’s birth certificate and the money for her care. The next day he took Marie to school, offered her heartfelt advice, and then parted ways.

A month later Louis-Gaston, now an apprentice on a warship, watched the French coast fade into the horizon. Alone on the vast ocean he felt lost but was comforted by an old sailor’s words. With newfound resolve he embraced life at sea, determined to honor his mother’s memory.
