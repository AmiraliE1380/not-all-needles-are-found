Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood one of those old Paris houses that let one reconstruct the city’s past. Its threatening façade was crossed by timbers whose Xs and Vs printed parallel cracks in the plaster; every beam seemed to shiver in its mortices. The triangular roof, warped by the climate, projected three feet over the street to protect the threshold and a sill-less dormer. The upper story was of overlapping planks, light as slates, so as not to overload the frail structure.

One rainy March morning a young man, wrapped in his cloak, stood under the awning opposite this relic, studying it with antiquarian enthusiasm. Each story offered curiosities. The first floor’s narrow windows had boards over their lower panes, producing the doubtful light a clever salesman uses to misrepresent his wares. The second floor’s Venetian blinds were up, revealing dingy muslins behind large Bohemian panes. But the young man’s eyes were fixed on the third floor: modest wooden sash-frames, clumsily wrought like pieces in a museum of early carpentry, glazed with small green squares so dark that only his keen sight could make out blue-checked cotton curtains behind them. Now and then, tired of his fruitless scrutiny or of the street’s stillness, he dropped his gaze to the lower region and smiled at the shop sign.

Across the entrance beam—encrusted with layer upon layer of paint—was a quaint old painting of a cat playing rackets. No modern caricaturist could invent a scene more comical: the cat, standing on hind legs, held an enormous racket in one forepaw to return a ball struck by a man in an embroidered coat. Time had rendered the drawing grotesque: the cat’s tail was eaten away so that it might be taken for a long, furry spectator; the gilding of the inscription had flaked; and Us and Vs had swapped places in the old orthography. On an azure field to the right read “Guillaume,” and to the left “Successor to Master Chevrel.”

Balzac pauses here to remind the reader that such signs are not mere whimsy but the faded pictures of once-living attractions—Spinning Sow, Green Monkey—animals that performed to astonish passers-by, and which often enriched their owners far more effectively than moralized signs like “Providence” or “Good-Faith” that still hang on the Rue Saint-Denis. These relics show how roguish and clever early artisans were in drawing customers.

Nevertheless, the young man was not at the window merely to contemplate the cat, which a minute’s attention had engraved on his memory. He had his peculiarities: his cloak fell like antique drapery, revealing a smart pair of shoes and white silk stockings splashed with mud from impatient pacing. He carried white gloves and his black hair, now out of curl and flowing over his shoulders, was dressed à la Caracalla—a style of the day inspired by David and the Greco-Roman vogue. A dazzling white cravat made his face appear paler; his black eyes flashed fire that paled into sadness and back again. His wide, flexible mouth could harden into a smile; his forehead, prone to violent furrows, stamped his features with doom when passion touched him, yet softened into luminous grace when he calmed. In him joy, grief, love, anger, and scorn burned up and down the face, contagiously expressive.

While he waited, a dormer window suddenly opened and three laughing, chubby faces, pink and vulgar as the face of Commerce on a monument, peered out. The apprentices, their cheeks puffed like cherubs and their garret air presumably foul, sniffed street breezes with eagerness. One of them returned with an instrument—a metal shaving-tube then in use—to spray the passer-by with a fine white shower; they grinned and enjoyed the young man’s expected rage. But he betrayed nothing, shaking his cloak with haughty indifference and casting a glance of intense contempt up at the window. The lads’ mischief failed to pierce him; they ceased laughing.

Then a slender white hand raised a lower sash on the third floor by means of a pulley. The watcher’s patience was rewarded. The face of a young girl appeared: fresh as a white water-lily, crowned by a frill of tumbled muslin that lent innocence to her head. Wrapped in brown stuff, her neck and shoulders nonetheless gleamed here and there through the loosened fabric. The candor of her face and the calm repose of her eyes echoed the virginal serenity immortalized by Raphael. Asleep, her cheeks had the round relief of abundant life; now, hardly awake, she let her blue eyes wander beyond neighboring roofs to the sky, then, by habit, dropped them to the gloomy street and met the adorer’s gaze.

Vanity at being seen undressed made her start back; the worn pulley gave way and the sash fell with a rapid run—what later generations called the “fenêtre à la guillotine.” The vision vanished, and to the young man the morning star seemed hidden by a cloud.

At this moment the heavy inside shutters of the “Cat and Racket” were swung back. The old door, with its knocker, was opened against the entry wall by a manservant apparently aged as the sign itself, who hung upon it a square cloth embroidered in yellow silk: “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” Between iron bars guarding the shop windows, packages wrapped in brown linen were as numerous as herrings in a shoal. Despite the shop’s primitive Gothic front, Monsieur Guillaume was the clothier in Paris whose stores were always best supplied, whose connections were broad, and whose honesty was beyond suspicion. When other clothiers contracted with the Government and lacked the required quantity, Guillaume could always supply the needed pieces. He knew tricks of extracting profit without cringing to patrons or making costly gifts. When his brethren could only offer long-dated bills, he would recommend his notary as accommodating and thus secure a second profit—an arrangement that gave rise among Rue Saint-Denis traders to the proverb, “Heaven preserve you from Monsieur Guillaume’s notary!” meaning beware a heavy discount.

Thus, behind the old sign and the grotesque painting, a successful, cunning clothier traded in a setting that preserved the city’s past. The house, its sagging beams, its projecting roof and greened panes, the comical cat and the faded superscription, the apprentices’ prank, the fleeting glimpse of the young girl—all these details knit together a scene where antiquity and the present meet: a relic of old Paris housing modern commerce, a place where appearances, traditions, and cleverness combine to attract customers and conceal shrewd calculation. The young man’s watchfulness before this doorway, his proud indifference to the apprentices, and the sudden vision at the window suggest more than a casual passer-by’s curiosity; the tableau introduces characters and tensions—antiquity and modern enterprise, jest and seriousness, public sign and private life—that Balzac will unfold further.

The old merchant appeared on his shop threshold the moment the servant withdrew, as if by instinct. Monsieur Guillaume looked over the Rue Saint-Denis, the neighboring shops, and the weather like a man returning to France after a long voyage. Having satisfied himself that nothing had changed, he noticed the stranger on guard; the young man, for his part, stared at the patriarchal draper as if the old man were some exotic specimen. Monsieur Guillaume wore plain black velvet breeches, pepper-and-salt stockings, and square-toed shoes with silver buckles. His greenish coat, square-cut and finished with white metal buttons browned by use, hung on a slightly bent figure. His gray hair was carefully smoothed over his furrowed pate. His small green eyes sparkled beneath faintly reddened arches where eyebrows should be. Lines of anxiety furrowed his forehead as deeply as the creases in his coat. His colorless face expressed patience, commercial shrewdness, and the greedy craft necessary to business. Like other established families of the time, his habits and costume survived amid recent civilization as cherished traditions.

The head of the Guillaume family upheld ancient practices; he still regretted the Provost of Merchants and called Tribunal of Commerce decisions the Sentence of the Consuls. He rose first, awaited his three assistants, and scolded them if late. The apprentices feared his silent Monday scrutiny more than anything. But that morning Guillaume paid no attention to them; he tried to divine why the young man in silk stockings and a cloak kept glancing anxiously at his signboard and into the depths of the shop. In the daylight the stranger could see the cashier’s corner, railed and screened by old green silk curtains, where immense ledgers—the house’s silent oracles—were kept. The gawker seemed to covet this nook, sketching a side dining-room lit by a skylight from which the family at meals could observe the smallest incident at the shop-door. So much interest in the dwelling seemed suspicious to a trader mindful of the law of maximum prices; Guillaume naturally suspected the stranger had an eye on the till of the Cat and Racket.

After observing the silent duel between master and stranger, the eldest apprentice, noticing the young man watching the third-floor windows, stepped onto the stone flag beside Guillaume. He went into the street, raised his head, and fancied he saw Mademoiselle Augustine retreating hastily. Annoyed, Guillaume glanced at him; both he and his amorous apprentice were suddenly relieved. The stranger hailed a hackney and jumped in with an air of affected indifference. His departure relieved the other two lads, uneasy about their practical joke's victim.

“Well, gentlemen, what ails you that you stand there with your arms folded?” said Monsieur Guillaume to his three neophytes. “In former days, bless you, when I was in Master Chevrel’s service, I should have overhauled more than two pieces of cloth by this time.”

“Then it was daylight earlier,” quipped the second assistant, whose duty this was.

Guillaume could not help smiling. Though two of these young fellows were entrusted by fathers who were rich manufacturers at Louviers and Sedan and would have a hundred thousand francs when they came of age, he regarded it his duty to keep them under the rod of an old-world despotism—unknown nowadays in flashy modern shops where apprentices expect to be rich at thirty. He made them work hard; these three could do the work of ten clerks whose softer tastes now swell the budget. Not a sound disturbed this solemn house: hinges were oiled, and even mean furniture showed the cleanliness of strict order and economy. The most waggish of the trio amused himself by writing the date of his first appearance on the Gruyere cheese left for breakfast, which they then left untouched. Such mischief sometimes brought a smile to the younger daughter, the pretty maiden who had just appeared to the bewitched man in the street.

Though each apprentice paid a round sum for board, none would have dared remain at the master's table at dessert; when Madame Guillaume spoke of dressing the salad, they trembled at her thrift with oil. They never spent a night away without beforehand giving a plausible reason. Every Sunday, in turn, two of them accompanied the Guillaume family to Mass and vespers at Saint-Leu. Mesdemoiselles Virginie and Augustine, simply attired in cotton print, each took an apprentice's arm and walked ahead under their mother's piercing eye, who closed the little procession with her husband, carrying two large black-morocco prayer-books. The second apprentice received no salary; the eldest, after twelve years' perseverance and discretion, was paid eight hundred francs a year. On certain family festivals he received small gifts whose value Madame Guillaume alone enhanced—netted purses stuffed with cotton wool to show the fancy stitches, braces of the strongest make, or heavy silk stockings. Sometimes, rarely, this prime minister was admitted to share the family's pleasures—going into the country or, after months, taking a theatre box to command a piece Paris had almost forgotten.

The barrier of respect that once divided a master draper from his apprentices made them more likely to steal a piece of cloth than breach etiquette; such reserve may now seem ridiculous, but these old houses were a school of honesty and sound morals. Masters adopted their apprentices: a young man’s linen was mended by the mistress of the house; if an apprentice fell ill he received maternal attention. In danger the master lavished money on the best physicians, accountable not only to parents for conduct and training. If an honest apprentice suffered misfortune, tradesmen valued his intelligence and did not hesitate to entrust their daughters' happiness to men they had long trusted with their fortunes. Guillaume exemplified this old school; if he had its ridiculous side, he had all its virtues.

Joseph Lebas, the chief assistant, an orphan without fortune, was in Guillaume’s mind destined to marry Virginie, his elder daughter. But Joseph did not share his master's symmetrical ideas—he would not, for an empire, have the second daughter married before the elder. The unhappy assistant felt his heart wholly given to Mademoiselle Augustine, the younger. To explain this secret passion, one must look into the springs of the absolute government that ruled the cloth-merchant’s household.

Guillaume had two daughters. The elder, Mademoiselle Virginie, was the very image of her mother. Madame Guillaume, daughter of Sieur Chevrel, sat so upright behind her desk that some wag hinted she was a stuffed figure. Her long, thin face betrayed exaggerated piety. Lacking attractions or amiability, Madame Guillaume, nearly sixty, wore an unvarying cap with long lappets like a widow's. In the neighborhood she was known as the “portress nun.” Her speech was curt, and her movements had the stiff precision of a semaphore. Her cat-like eye seemed to spite the world because she was so ugly. Mademoiselle Virginie, raised under her mother's rule, had reached twenty-eight. Youth softened the graceless likeness, but maternal austerity had endowed her with two great qualities: patience and gentleness. Mademoiselle Augustine, eighteen, resembled neither parent. She was delicately made, full of gracious candor; a man of the world might have found only a petty coarseness of gesture or a want of ease. Her placid face often showed the transient melancholy that comes to girls too weak to resist their mother's will.

Always plainly dressed, the sisters gratified feminine vanity only by a luxury of cleanliness that suited them and blended with the polished counters and shining shelves, on which the old man-servant never left a speck of dust. Their style of living compelled them to find happiness in persistent work; Augustine and Virginie had hitherto satisfied their mother, who secretly prided herself on their perfect characters. Trained for a commercial life, hearing only dreary trade arguments, taught grammar, bookkeeping, a little Bible-history, and the history of France in Le Ragois, and reading only what their mother sanctioned, their ideas had little scope. They knew housecraft, prices, and the difficulty of amassing money; they were economical and respected the qualities that make a man of business. Though their father was rich, they were as skilled in darning as in embroidery; their mother spoke of teaching them to cook so they might know how to order a dinner and scold a cook with authority. They knew little of the world's pleasures; seeing how their parents lived, they rarely let their eyes wander beyond their hereditary home, which to their mother was the whole universe. Family anniversaries furnished their future earthly joy.

When the great drawing-room on the second floor was prepared to receive company—Madame Roguin, Demoiselle Chevrel (fifteen months younger and bedecked with diamonds); young Rabourdin of the Finance Office; Monsieur Cesar Birotteau, the rich perfumer, and his wife; Monsieur Camusot, the richest silk mercer in the Rue des Bourdonnais, with his father-in-law Monsieur Cardot, two or three old bankers, and some immaculate ladies—the arrangements required by packing away the plate, the Dresden china, the candlesticks, and the glass varied the monotonous lives of the three women, who labored like nuns to receive their bishop. That evening, tired from wiping, rubbing, unpacking, and arranging the festival goods, as the girls helped their mother undress, Madame Guillaume would say, “Children, we have done nothing today.”

On very great occasions when the “portress nun” allowed dancing, restricting boston, whist, and backgammon to her bedroom, such a concession was the greatest felicity and made them happier than the two or three grand balls Guillaume took them to at Carnival. Once a year the worthy draper gave an entertainment and spared no expense. However rich or fashionable the guests, they were careful not to be absent; the most important houses on the exchange relied on Monsieur Guillaume's immense credit, fortune, or experience. Still, the merchant’s daughters did not profit much from the world’s lessons. At these parties, which the ledger recorded as credits of the house, they wore dresses so shabby they blushed. Their dancing was unremarkable, and their mother's surveillance did not permit conversation with partners beyond Yes and No. The Cat and Racket's law required them to be home by eleven, the hour when balls begin to be lively. Thus their pleasures, which seemed to match their father's position, were often made insipid by circumstances rooted in family habits and principles.

As to their usual life: Madame Guillaume required her daughters to be dressed very early, to come down each day at the same hour, and she ordered their employments with monastic regularity. Augustine, however, had a spirit lofty enough to feel the emptiness of such a life. Her blue eyes would sometimes be raised as if to pierce the depths of that gloomy staircase and those damp store-rooms. After sounding the profound cloistral silence, she seemed to listen to remote, inarticulate revelations of a life of passion that values feelings above things. At such moments her cheek would flush, her idle hands would lay the muslin sewing on the polished oak counter, and her mother, in a sour voice, would ask, “Augustine, my treasure, what are you thinking about?” It is possible that two romances Augustine had discovered in the cupboard of a discharged cook—Hippolyte Comte de Douglas and Le Comte de Comminges—contributed to the development of the young girl's ideas, which she devoured in secret during long winter nights.

Augustine’s vague longing, her gentle voice, her jasmine skin, and her blue eyes had kindled in poor Lebas’s soul a flame as ardent as it was reverent. From an easily understood caprice, Augustine felt no affection for the orphan; perhaps she did not know he loved her. On the other hand, the senior apprentice—long-legged, chestnut-haired, with big hands and a powerful frame—had a secret admirer in Mademoiselle Virginie, who, despite her dower of fifty thousand crowns, had as yet no suitor. Nothing could be more natural than these two passions at cross-purposes, born in the silence of the dingy shop, as violets bloom in the depths of a wood. The mute and constant looks that made the young people’s eyes meet by sheer need of change amid persistent work and cloistered peace would sooner or later give rise to love. The habit of always seeing the same face leads insensibly to reading there the qualities of the soul and at last effaces all its defects.

“At the pace at which that man goes, our girls will soon have to go on their knees to a suitor!” said Monsieur Guillaume to himself, as he read the first decree by which Napoleon drew in advance on the conscript classes.

From that day the old merchant, grieving as his eldest daughter faded, remembered how he had married Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances much like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie. It had been good business: marrying off his daughter also fulfilled a kind of debt, repaying an orphan the kindness his predecessor had once shown to him. Joseph Lebas, now thirty-three, understood the obstacle a fifteen-year difference posed between himself and Augustine; seeing Monsieur Guillaume’s intent, he was certain the younger would never be allowed to marry before the elder. Warm-hearted and vigorous, the hapless assistant suffered in silence.

Such was life in the little republic of the Rue Saint-Denis, austere as a dependency of La Trappe. To explain fully, however, it is necessary to go back several months. One dusk a young man passing the unlit shop called the Cat and Racket paused, struck by a scene like a painter’s delight. The shop was a dark cave; beyond it the dining-room glowed by a hanging lamp whose yellow light turned white linen, silver, and cut glass into brilliant highlights against deep shadow. The family head and his wife, the apprentices, Augustine’s pure profile beside a fat maid—these figures made a candid, harmonious group, their peace and modest life written plainly on their faces. To an artist used to rendering nature, the scene seemed almost unrepeatable.

The stranger was a young painter who, seven years earlier, had won the grand prize for painting. Fresh from Rome, his mind full of Raphael and Michelangelo, he longed for real, humble nature after Italy’s grandiose art. Passion tempered by Italian ideals made him hunger for one of those meditative maidens he had only seen in paintings. That evening the living picture before him captivated him: Augustine, pensive and barely eating, stood lit by the lamp so that her face and bust were outlined in glowing light. He involuntarily compared her to an exiled angel dreaming of heaven. Limpid love flooded him. Overwhelmed, he left, ate nothing, and could not sleep.

The next day he sealed himself in his studio until he’d fixed on canvas the magic his memory had made sacred; he needed a faithful portrait of his idol. He watched the Cat and Racket, sometimes entering in disguise to study the girl under Madame Guillaume’s wing. For eight months, devoted to love and brush, he forgot friends, theater, and music. At last Girodet, breaking an artistly reserve, asked bluntly, “What will you send to the Salon?” The painter showed two works: a small interior and a portrait. After a long, eager study, Girodet embraced him without speaking; he guessed at the passion that had borne the canvases.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked; both men knew how devotion had produced the greatest portraits of Titian, Raphael, and Leonardo. Girodet praised yet warned: such true coloring and depth would not be understood by the Salon audience, who preferred art that posed and prettified. Still, the painter exhibited both pictures. The Interior revolutionized painting and gave rise to genre painting; the portrait too was unforgettable, and while Girodet had predicted failure, the public at times proved discerning and crowned the portrait. Crowds poured in; speculators and rich collectors would have paid dearly, but the artist refused to sell or to make replicas. Large sums were offered for the right to engrave them; neither print-sellers nor amateurs were favored.

Though these events moved the art world, they penetrated the Rue Saint-Denis only when Madame Roguin, the notary’s wife, visited Madame Guillaume and, in Augustine’s hearing, described the exhibition. Augustine, who liked Madame Roguin, felt a sudden desire and courage to see the pictures. With her cousin’s help, and Madame Guillaume’s reluctant consent for a two-hour outing, Augustine pushed through the crowd at the Salon and trembled when she recognized herself. Terrified, she searched for Madame Roguin and then saw, near her, the impassioned face of the young painter—the loiterer she had often noticed near their street. He bent close and murmured, “You see how love has inspired me,” and she stood dismayed.

Caught in the crush, Augustine and her cousin were swept within a few steps of the second painting. She involuntarily wept at the sight of the amazing study. Near her, the painter’s ecstatic face and the subtle pantomime—he nodded and pointed to Madame Roguin—struck Augustine like hot coals: she felt as if bound to him by a compact. The press of the crowd, dazzling dresses, and glittering frames intensified her alarm; she thought she might faint, and terror mingled with a sudden, ecstatic conviction of sin, as if under the Devil’s sway preachers had warned against. It was an instant of madness.

Yet when she left the Salon she found herself accompanied to Madame Roguin’s carriage by the young painter, radiant. Augustine, agitated and intoxicated, listened to the eloquence of her own heart and kept looking at him, betraying the emotion within her. Never had the rose of her cheeks shown in such contrast with her pallor. The artist saw both her beauty and modesty in full bloom. She felt rapture mingled with fear, thinking her presence might have brought happiness to the man whose name was on every lip—whose talent embalmed the transient scene—and so believed unquestioningly that she was loved. After he vanished, his simple words still rang in her ears. Her heart throbbed and her blood warmed; she feigned a severe headache to avoid answering her cousin’s questions about the pictures. On their return, Madame Roguin told Madame Guillaume of the fame that had fallen on the Cat and Racket, and Augustine trembled when her mother said she should go to the Salon to see her likeness. Augustine again declared herself unwell and was permitted to go to bed.

Monsieur Guillaume scorned the excursion: “A headache. Is it so amusing to see in a picture what you can see any day in your own street? Don’t talk to me of your artists! Like writers, they are a starveling crew. Why the devil must they choose my house to flaunt in their pictures?” Joseph Lebas, practical as ever, added that such publicity might help sell a few ells of cloth. These remarks did nothing to shield art from commercial judgment, nor did they console Augustine. That night she surrendered to the first meditations of love. The day’s events seemed a dream, a joy to recall. She discovered the fears, hopes, and remorse that toss a simple timid heart. She perceived the void in the gloomy house and the treasure in her soul: to be the wife of a genius, to share his glory. For a girl raised amid sordid surroundings who had longed for elegance, a sunbeam had fallen into her prison. At eighteen, love threw a prism between her eyes and the world; she could not foresee the harsh realities that often follow the union of a loving woman with a man of imagination. She believed herself called to make him happy and imagined no disparity between them; to her the future would mirror the present.

When her parents returned from the Salon their faces were dejected. The painter had removed the two pictures from the exhibition, and Madame Guillaume had lost her cashmere shawl. Yet the disappearance of the paintings after Augustine’s visit revealed, to the young girl, a delicate impulse: the painter’s removal of his works suggested a consideration—an instinctive delicacy—that a woman can always appreciate. This fragile, almost reverent act fed Augustine’s rapture and gave her one more reason to feel she had been seen and valued by the artist who had immortalized her simple presence.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux—the name that fame had carved into Augustine’s heart—was splashed by apprentices while he waited outside the shop for the artless girl who did not know he was there. This chance contact marked the fourth meeting of the lovers since the Salon. The household’s rules and watchful eyes made the painter’s passion perilous.

The problem was practical: how could a man approach a young woman seated in a counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume? How could he correspond when her mother never left her side? The painter’s ardor bred fantasies of rivals among the assistants and of discovery beneath the draper’s stern glance. Unable to devise the clever devices lovers sometimes use, Theodore grew restless, prowling the neighborhood in the hope that movement would spark invention. Finally, after pondering, he bribed the shop’s blowsy waiting-maid with gold and from her secured a few notes exchanged at long intervals during the fortnight following that ill-omened morning when Monsieur Guillaume and Theodore had scrutinized one another. Augustine and Theodore arranged to meet during Mass and vespers on Sundays at Saint-Leu. She sent him a list of family relations and friends whom the painter tried to contact, hoping to find some sympathetic soul among the traders; but under the sign of the Cat and Racket nothing changed.

If Augustine was absent-minded, stole upstairs to make signals with a jar of flowers, sighed, or was lost in thought, no one noticed—not even her mother. That seeming indifference stemmed not from negligence but from distraction: the household, composed of Monsieur Guillaume, Madame Guillaume, Mademoiselle Virginie, and five male assistants, was in the grip of stock-taking. For a fortnight each bale was turned, tickets examined to know when goods were bought, retail prices fixed; the five men and the two women were consumed by counting. Monsieur Guillaume, pen behind his ear, paraded like a captain of his ship, issuing inscrutable trade formulas—“How much H. N. Z.?”—“All sold.”—“What is left of Q. X.?”—and a hundred other commercial injunctions that sounded like modern poetry to those outside the trade. Evenings found him balancing accounts with his wife and assistant, reconciling cash, goods, bills and notes, and drawing conclusions that amounted to how much capital was due, how much owed to him, how far investments extended. Their labor pointed only to one rule: to accumulate crown-pieces anew without ever asking to what end.

The annual turmoil had the unintended effect of masking Augustine’s furtive signals. At last, on a Saturday evening, stock-taking was finished. The rows of figures let Guillaume relax his stern rules: dessert was permitted. The shrewd old draper rubbed his hands, allowed his assistants to remain at table, and—after a thimbleful of homemade liqueur had passed their lips—a rumble of a carriage announced the family party’s intention to go to the Variétés. The two younger apprentices each received six francs and permission to be out until midnight.

Despite the revelry, Guillaume, a man of routine, was shaving at six the next morning and by seven had gone to the little office beside the shop. Daylight entered through an iron-barred window over a small yard. He opened the iron-lined shutters, threw up the sash, cooled the small room heavy with the smell of the counting-house, and stood by his morocco-lined cane chair. He surveyed the double desk where his wife’s place fit opposite his in a niche, the numbered boxes, files, implements and the cash-box—objects that seemed immemorial. He placed the worn stool that had belonged to his predecessor Chevrel in the old spot, and with feeling pulled the bell that roused Joseph Lebas.

Joseph appeared and, to his surprise, Guillaume bade him sit—something the old master had never done before. He then asked Joseph about certain notes, and Joseph declared they would never be paid; Guillaume changed the subject, saying stock-taking was done and the dividend—no, the profits—were one of the best ever. He announced, partly as reward, that he no longer intended to pay Joseph a salary; Madame Guillaume had suggested taking him into partnership: “Guillaume and Lebas” would make a good name, perhaps adding “and Co.”

Tears rose to Joseph’s eyes. He protested his unworthiness, calling himself a poor orphan who only did his duty. Guillaume said Joseph had been entrusted with cash-box secrets for two years and had never betrayed him. But then, in a burst of fatherly frankness, he told Joseph he had a soft place and must have some secret—Joseph blushed, thinking his feelings concealed, only to discover Guillaume had read him like a book. “Ah-ha! So you thought you could deceive an old fox like me? When you knew I had scented the Lecocq bankruptcy?” Joseph’s astonished rejoinder revealed that the truth was not what Guillaume assumed. The draper meant one thing: he had been speaking of forming a match between Joseph and his daughter Virginie. But Joseph, in trembling, declared he loved Augustine.

Guillaume’s reaction was odd. He had been discoursing in the most calculated commercial terms—partnerships, succession, the joys of trade—and suddenly Joseph’s confession sent him into confusion. The old man, used to the certainties of business, found himself unmoored in matters of the heart. He vacillated, then, half in jest, half in earnest, suggested Joseph should offer his arm to Augustine on the way to Mass. The suggestion, delivered in a manly, accommodating tone, made the young man’s heart leap; he left the office convinced that all would be well. Guillaume, however, was troubled by the thought of what Madame Guillaume might say.

At breakfast, Madame Guillaume and Virginie cast meaning glances at Joseph, who blushed and found their teasing heartening. The matron, amused by the young man’s bashfulness, indulged in light pleasantries about which of the two children was taller, and allowed a decorous climate to prevail. The draper, in a show of propriety, desired Augustine to take Joseph’s arm to Saint-Leu; Madame Guillaume nodded approval. Thus the procession left in proper order, arousing no suspicion among neighbors.

On the way to Mass, Joseph attempted a shy discourse on the prospects of married life and the role of a merchant’s wife—bookkeeping, correspondence, household management—expressing the hope that, though he would work, he would ensure his wife’s happiness. Augustine answered, in turn, with a question about painting, and Joseph mentioned a successful master-house painter he knew. At Saint-Leu the matron asserted her place beside Augustine, placing Virginie in the fourth pew beside Lebas. During the sermon the lovers were in tune: Theodore, standing behind a pillar, worshiped his beloved with ardent devotion. But at the elevation of the Host Madame Guillaume discovered Augustine was holding her prayer-book upside down. Her fury, coupled with the sight of Augustine’s diverted eyes, shifted to the object of their attraction. With spectacles adjusted, she saw the young artist—so fashionably elegant, so unlike a tradesman—that she was catapulted into panic about her daughter’s clandestine passion. To Madame Guillaume, who took pride in having brought up her daughters to perfection, Augustine’s secret ruined everything; in her mind the girl was “cankered.”

Low and tremulous with anger, she snatched the tell-tale book, turned it right, and muttered, “Do not allow your eyes to look anywhere but at your prayers, or I shall have something to say to you. Your father and I will talk to you after church.” The words struck Augustine like a blow; she felt faint, but hid her distress to avoid a scene. The trembling of the prayer-book and the tears wetting each page betrayed the storm within her. Theodore, seeing the danger from Madame Guillaume’s furious glare, left the church in a rage, resolved to venture everything for his love.

On returning home Madame Guillaume ordered Augustine to her room, saying, “We will send for you; take care not to quit it.” The conference between husband and wife was secret at first. Virginie, who had tried to encourage her sister, listened at the bedroom door and, on her second attempt, learned that Joseph Lebas loved Augustine. The household that day became a hell of clashing affections. Monsieur Guillaume told Joseph of Augustine’s love for a stranger, casting the young assistant into despair: his hopes for Virginie were dashed. Virginie, learning that Joseph had, in effect, refused her, took to her bed with a sick headache. The dispute between Monsieur and Madame Guillaume flared in bitter fashion.

At half-past four Augustine, pale, trembling and red-eyed, was brought before her parents. She artlessly related her brief tale of love, and reassured by her father’s promise to listen, she named Theodore de Sommervieux—with a mischievous emphasis on the aristocratic “de.” Yielding to the charm of speaking of her feelings, she declared she loved Monsieur de Sommervieux, had written to him, and tearfully added that to be sacrificed to another man would make her wretched.

Madame Guillaume was horrified. “But Augustine, you cannot surely know what a painter is?” she cried. Guillaume intervened. He had his own prejudices: artists, he said, were generally little better than beggars—too extravagant to be trusted. He recounted anecdotes of the tricks played upon his predecessor Father Chevrel by dancers, musicians and actors, grouping them as rascals with charm. He stammered through the aristocratic name—“Sumer—, Somm—”—and Augustine supplied the full “de Sommervieux.” Augustine defended her choice by saying Theodore was of good family and had written that he was rich; his father had been called Chevalier de Sommervieux before the Revolution. Guillaume looked at his wife as if seeking counsel, and she, an angry woman who prized propriety, kept sullen silence; she left the responsibility of judgment to her husband while secretly resolving to subject the suitor to a severe inquisition.

Their argument was interrupted by the arrival of Madame Roguin, a notary’s wife and social butterfly who fancied herself a patron of fashion and influence. She entered as though carrying an olive branch. “I know all, my dear cousin,” she said with patronizing cheer, and proceeded to flatter and reassure. She had already seen Theodore’s portrait that morning and declared it worth at least six thousand francs; she had been to his evenings and guaranteed he adored Augustine and would have her. She spoke of his recent distinctions—Chevalier of the Legion of Honor bestowed at the Salon—and of the Emperor’s praise. She declared him to be well off: twelve thousand francs a year in landed estate, with Roguin as his lawyer. Her breathless eulogy of the young painter’s household prospects, pride and charm rang like a trumpet in the draper’s ears.

Madame Roguin’s urbane volubility struck chords in both Guillaume and Madame Guillaume. At that time commerce and finance were seized by a mania to ally with rank; generals of the Empire profited. Guillaume’s usual axiom was to marry within one’s class—“every one is punished sooner or later for climbing too high,” he would say—and to mistrust unions built on vanity. Yet vanity and the prospect of the family rising in social esteem proved potent. Madame Roguin’s assurances of Theodore’s rank, taste, and estate made the old draper, odd as it was, yield: he accepted the match in principle, though he promised to subject the suitor to careful scrutiny. Madame Guillaume, austere and practical, was first to see in Augustine’s affection a reason to abandon rigid principle and to consent to receive Monsieur de Sommervieux, provided he was thoroughly investigated.

Guillaume next sought Joseph Lebas to inform him of the turn in events. At half-past six the household assembled under the dining-room skylight: Monsieur and Madame Roguin, the young painter, Augustine, Joseph Lebas—who had to reconcile patience with disappointment—and Virginie, recovering from her headache. Monsieur and Madame Guillaume envisaged both children happily married and the Cat and Racket’s fortunes secured in skilled hands. The satisfaction ran high when, at dessert, Theodore presented the painting the family had not yet seen: a depiction of the old shop’s interior, to which they owed much happiness.

The presentation of the picture lightened the mood. “Isn’t it pretty?” cried Guillaume, surprised at the thought anyone would pay thirty thousand francs for such work. Madame Guillaume joked that she could see her own lappets; Lebas marveled at how drapery was rendered. Theodore, flattering the tradesman’s pride in drapery, observed the art’s claim to antique perfection; the old man laughed heartily, warmed by champagne and the painter’s deference to trade. The bandage over the artist’s youthful obstinacy was so thick that he thought his future parents amiable; he greeted them with a few well-placed jokes and charm.

Later, when the drawing-room—furnished with what Madame Guillaume called “everything handsome”—was hushed, the clear-sighted draper, always prudent where money was at stake, called Augustine to his knee. He told her that she might marry Monsieur de Sommervieux if she insisted, “you may, if you like, risk your capital in happiness.” Yet he would not let his hard-won crown-pieces be wasted on frivolity. He promised to secure the marriage contract properly: since Theodore had been persuaded—by a man who managed the Lecocq bankruptcy—to marry under a settlement of his wife’s money on herself, Guillaume would keep an eye on the contract to ensure that what Theodore settled on Augustine was safely tied up. He warned that “money lightly earned is lightly spent,” and said he would not see the family’s savings frittered away; he would begin to lay up for grandchildren. He demanded Augustine swear never to sign money papers without his advice, and to consult young Lebas if he were gone to join old Father Chevrel. Augustine swore, and the old man kissed her on both cheeks. That night the lovers slept soundly, as did Monsieur and Madame Guillaume.

Throughout the episode, several tensions remained evident. The household’s strict routines and the draper’s obsession with commercial prudence collided with human passions—a lover’s bribe to a waiting-maid, furtive notes in church, a mother’s sharp discovery of a tell-tale glance. Commerce’s language—ledgers, stock-taking, “dividends,” “capital”—framed everything. Guillaume’s speeches on trade, his anecdotes about artists, and his notion that “to be on the scent of a good bargain…to make goods cheaper than others; then to carry out an undertaking you planned, which begins, grows, totters, and succeeds!” revealed a man for whom business was a perpetual game of skill and caution. Yet when flattered by rank and prize, he was capable of the same vanity that another father might have scorned; when reassured by Madame Roguin, he folded commercial prudence into a demand for contractual safeguards rather than blunt refusal.

Joseph’s fate was ambiguous but not tragic: though his hopes of marriage to Virginie were dashed, Guillaume sought to ameliorate his disappointment by offering him partnership and a role in the household’s future—assurances that kept family equilibrium intact. Virginie’s brief pain soon gave way to new expectations. Madame Roguin’s intervention—her talk of portraits, honors, and the Emperor’s praise—showed the power of social networks: she could convert the draper’s economic caution into a guarded, conditional acceptance.

In the end, the marriage was accepted, provided the girl’s money was protected and the suitor pass a proper test; commerce’s appetite for expansion yielded to fatherly guardianship of capital. Trade and sentiment found a compromise: Augustus’s happiness would be allowed, Theodore’s rank and artfulness would be tolerated, but the draper’s hard cash would be sacrosanct. The household’s routines resumed, reconciling the counting-house’s arithmetic with the private sums of love and discretion; Augustus and Theodore, reassured by settlement and approval, slept that night at peace, while Monsieur and Madame Guillaume, satisfied they had both preserved their daughter’s future and secured the family’s fiscal prudence, also rested.

Some months after that memorable Sunday the high altar of Saint-Leu saw two weddings. Augustine and Theodore looked radiant; Virginie, who had come in a hired fly with the rest of the family, followed humbly, simply dressed and almost a shadow beside Augustine. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to marry Virginie before Augustine, but the priests addressed the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s prudence in making a substantial match and staying nearby, while others, envious of Augustine’s marrying an artist of rank, taunted the Guillaumes for ambition. An old fan-maker prophesied prodigality for the new husband; father Guillaume privately took pride in prudent settlements. After a splendid ball and substantial supper, the Guillaumes remained at the Rue du Colombier house; the Lebas returned to the Rue Saint-Denis to mind the Cat and Racket. The artist, intoxicated with happiness, carried Augustine from the carriage in the Rue des Trois-Frères to an apartment embellished by the arts.

Theodore’s feverish passion made the first year unclouded. He lavished transports of feeling and languor; when souls soar, bodily union seemed secondary. Augustine, too happy for reflection, surrendered to sanctioned married love. Simple and artless, she had no coquetry; she loved without imagining so exquisite a life could end. As her husband’s sole delight, she believed her devotion and obedience would always charm him. Love made her brilliantly lovely; she took pride in reigning over a man so easily kindled as Monsieur de Sommervieux. Thus marriage taught her only lessons of love.

Still a simple child of Rue Saint-Denis, she never sought the world’s manners. Her speech, shaped by love, was pliant and sweet—the language of women in passion. When she expressed an idea at odds with Theodore’s, he laughed as at a foreigner’s mistake—a laugh that could irritate if left unchecked.

By the end of that year Sommervieux resumed work and habits. His wife was expecting their first child; during tedious nursing he worked zealously but sometimes sought diversion in society, frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s house, which drew celebrated artists to her parties. When Augustine was well and the boy needed less care, Theodore desired the vanity of displaying a handsome wife in society and provoking women’s envy.

Appearing in drawing-rooms under his reflected fame pleased Augustine, but it proved the last gleam of conjugal happiness. She wounded her husband’s vanity by revealing her ignorance, inelegant language, and narrow ideas. The painter’s nature, subdued for two and a half years, regained its old habits: poetry, painting, and imagination reclaimed their claims. Once the meadows of love were ransacked and his hands had held all they could, the scene changed. When he showed her his finest sketches she exclaimed, as her father had, “How pretty!”—a tepid admiration born of love, not feeling.

Augustine cared more for a look than a picture; the only sublime she knew was of the heart. Theodore could not deny the truth—his wife was insensible to poetry; she did not inhabit his sphere and could not follow his vagaries and joys; she trod reality while his head was in the skies. Common minds cannot grasp the continual suffering of one who, though affectionate, must suppress his soul’s flights and the images a magic power compels him to create. More intolerable, his feeling demanded transparency: they should share aspirations as fully as emotion. He retreated to his studio, hoping that living among artists would shape his wife and awaken intelligence. But Augustine was too sincerely religious to be at ease among them. At a dinner Theodore gave, a young painter said—unintelligibly to her—“Madame, your Paradise cannot be more beautiful than Raphael’s Transfiguration!—Well, and I got tired of looking at that.” She entered this set with visible distrust. She restrained their freedom; artists who feel restraint are pitiless—they stay away or mock. Madame Guillaume held extreme notions of married dignity; Augustine, though she had mocked them, could not wholly avoid imitating her mother’s primness. This propriety provoked epigrams and jests among guests—reprisals her husband could not entirely ignore—but to Theodore, open to impressions, nothing seemed trivial. A coldness crept and spread.

To attain conjugal happiness one must tread a narrow ridge: the painter’s love was slipping. He saw his wife as incapable of grasping moral considerations that, for him, justified his singular behavior, and felt innocent in hiding thoughts and peccadilloes beyond bourgeois jurisdiction. Augustine wrapped herself in sullen grief. These unconfessed feelings laid a shroud between them. Though considerate, he reserved for the world the wit and grace he had once lavished on her. She interpreted jocular speeches about men’s inconstancy in the worst light. She made no complaints, but her manner conveyed reproach.

Three years after marriage this young woman, once envied in her carriage and in a sphere of glory, became prey to intense grief. Her color faded; she reflected and compared; sorrow taught her experience. She resolved to confine herself to duty, hoping to regain her husband’s affection. It did not return. When Sommervieux came from the studio fired with work, Augustine did not put away her knitting more quickly; she might be found mending household linen and his with the care of a housewife. She supplied, without murmur, the money needed for his lavishness, economized strictly for herself, and managed domestic details with thrift. Such conduct clashes with artists’ easy habits, who rarely probe the causes of ruin.

The chronic shadows need not all be chronicled. One evening Augustine, long hearing her husband praise the Duchesse de Carigliano, received malign warnings about his attachment to that celebrated court flirt. At twenty-one, in youth and beauty, she saw herself deserted for a woman of thirty-six. Feeling wretched in a glittering world that was blank to her, she could no longer understand the admiration she excited or the envy she provoked. Melancholy gave her features the sweetness of resignation and the pallor of scorned love. Soon she too was courted by fascinating men, but she remained lonely and virtuous. Some contemptuous words from her husband filled her with indescribable despair. A sinister flash showed breaches—due to her humble education—that hindered a perfect union of souls with Theodore; she loved him enough to absolve him and condemn herself. She shed tears and perceived too late that there are mesalliances of spirit as well as rank. As she recalled their early raptures she understood lost happiness was a rich harvest paid for by sorrow. Yet she loved too truly to lose hope. At twenty-one she dared to educate herself and make her imagination worthy, at least in understanding, of what she admired. “If I am not a poet,” she thought, “at least I will understand poetry.”

With a will passion inspires, Madame de Sommervieux set about altering her character, devouring books to become less ignorant; but lightness of wit and conversational grace are gifts of nature or cradle education. She could appreciate music and literature, but could not sing or converse with true taste; her stubborn memory resisted cultivation. Home-bred religious notions opposed full emancipation. A prejudice against her had taken hold in Theodore’s mind and she could not dispel it. The artist laughed at those who flattered him about his wife; his irony overawed her so that she trembled in his presence. Hampered by the desire to please, her wits and knowledge vanished into one feeling. Even her fidelity irritated the unfaithful husband, who seemed to stigmatize her virtue as insensibility. Augustine tried in vain to abdicate reason, yield to caprice, and devote herself to his vanity. Her sacrifices bore no fruit. Perhaps they had both missed the critical moment when souls meet in comprehension. One day her too sensitive heart received a blow that stretched their bonds; she withdrew into solitude. Before long, a fatal thought suggested seeking counsel and comfort in her family’s bosom.

One morning she went to the plain, grotesque façade of the humble home of her childhood. She sighed looking up at the sash-window from which she had sent her first kiss to the man who now brought as much sorrow as glory. Nothing had changed in the cavern, though the drapery business had restarted. Augustine’s sister sat at the desk in their mother’s place. The brother-in-law, pen behind his ear and engrossed in stock-taking, begged her excuse. She was received coldly by her sister, who still bore a grudge—Augustine in finery had rarely visited. Madame Lebas, suspecting money motives, maintained a reserve that made Augustine smile. The painter’s wife noted that, beyond cap and lappets, her mother had found in Virginie a successor who upheld the Cat and Racket. At breakfast she observed management changes crediting Lebas’ good sense: assistants did not rise before dessert, they were allowed to talk, and the abundant meal spoke of ease without luxury. She found a ticket for a box at the Français where she remembered seeing her sister; Madame Lebas had a cashmere shawl showing her husband’s generosity. In short, the couple kept pace with the times. During the two-thirds of the day she spent there, Augustine was moved by the equable happiness—without storm yet free from passion—enjoyed by this well-matched pair. Virginie had worked to create love; having learned to esteem and care for his wife, time assured Joseph Lebas of its durability. Hence, when Augustine plaintively described her situation, she encountered the flood of commonplace morality of Rue Saint-Denis tradition.

“The mischief is done, wife,” said Joseph Lebas; “we must try to give our sister good advice.” The clever tradesman ponderously analyzed legal and customary remedies like merchandise, and concluded by urging violent steps that could not satisfy the love she still bore. Augustine thanked them and returned home more undecided. She then went to the Rue du Colombier to confide in her father and mother, like a sick person trying every prescription and even old wives’ remedies.

The old people received their daughter with an effusiveness that touched her deeply. Her visit was a diversion worth a fortune to them. For four years they had moved like aimless navigators, endlessly rehearsing their disasters: lawsuits, vanishing investments in cloth, bankruptcies, and above all Monsieur Guillaume’s humiliation in Lecocq’s failure — his “battle of Marengo.” When legal tales ran out they revisited profitable stocktakings and old Saint-Denis stories. At two each afternoon Guillaume inspected the Cat and Racket; on the way home he called on former rivals who tried to inveigle him into risky discounts, which he never flatly refused. Two fine Normandy horses fattened unused in the stables; Madame Guillaume rode them only to high Mass. Three times a week the couple entertained. Through the influence of their son-in-law Sommervieux, Monsieur Guillaume sat on the consulting board for Army clothing, and Madame Guillaume, sensing a rise in standing, filled her rooms with tasteless but valuable furniture and showy gold and silver so that every room looked like a chapel. Economy and ostentation battled in every detail, as if even a candlestick must be a sound investment. In this bazaar of display, the famous Sommervieux picture held pride of place; the Guillaumes, spectacles on nose, would turn to it twenty times a day as a consolation and reminder of their daughter’s better past.

This house and its occupants — cast upon a rock far from the currents of the world — startled Augustine. She saw here the sequel to the scene that had struck her at Lebas’s: a life of stir without movement, mechanical and instinctive. She felt an indefinable pride in her sufferings, because they had sprung from eighteen months of happiness that dwarfed this existence; the household’s vacuity seemed horrible. She hid the not‑very‑charitable thought and, with the modest accomplishments love had taught her, poured out the details of her married life to her parents — old people being especially receptive to such confidences. Madame Guillaume wanted the smallest particulars of a life that seemed fabulous to her; the Duchess de la Houtan’s printed travels were no more astonishing to her than the stories Augustine told.

Madame Guillaume’s reaction was outraged moralism. “Your husband shuts himself in a room with naked women!” she cried, laying aside her spectacles. Augustine explained that artists needed models. “He took good care not to tell us that when he asked to marry you,” the mother replied. “If I had known it, I would never have given my daughter to a man who followed such a trade. Religion forbids such horrors.” When Augustine said Théodore often came home late, her parents exchanged amazement. “Then he gambles?” Monsieur Guillaume asked. Augustine scorned the suggestion. Madame Guillaume fretted over nights kept waiting, oddities like Théodore’s eating habits, his sudden disappearances, his rides into the woods at odd hours — all signs, to her, of instability bordering on madness. She denounced his lack of churchgoing and the whims that made him curl his hair, cover statues with muslin, work by lamp-light, shut shutters in day to paint — signs, she insisted, of being morally unfit. The old draper, prompted by paternal love and the excitement of legal strife, took up the word “divorce” with gusto: he would lead the application, pay costs, see lawyers, plead the cause. Madame de Sommervieux refused him, frightened at the thought of such proceedings and declaring she would not be separated, even if ten times more unhappy. She learned that a wife must hide from everyone, even loving parents, those sorrows for which sympathy is rare. The storms of the upper spheres are understood only by those who live in them; only equals can judge.

Cast back into the cold atmosphere of her parents’ house, Augustine found study unavailing: it had not restored her husband’s heart. Having seen souls of fire but lacking their strength, she shared their sorrows without their pleasures and grew disgusted with a world that seemed mean compared to the incidents of passion. Her life felt like a failure. Then an idea struck her like a beam of light: she would go to the Duchesse de Carigliano — not to plead for her husband’s return, but to learn the arts by which the Duchess had captured him. Augustine resolved to engage the fine lady’s interest for the mother of her lover’s children and to make the Duchess an instrument of her happiness, since the Duchess had been the cause of her wretchedness.

One afternoon Augustine, timid but steady with a desperate courage, drove to the Faubourg Saint‑Germain to present herself at the Duchess’s boudoir. As she passed stately corridors, handsome staircases, and vast, flowered drawing rooms, Augustine felt a clutch at her heart; she coveted the secrets of elegance that explained Théodore’s attraction. In the Duchess’s private rooms she was overawed: luxurious furniture and draperies suggested a disorder that was itself a kind of grace; luxury affected a contempt for splendor. The air smelled warm and discreetly perfumed; everything harmonized with the view of evergreen lawns beyond plate glass. The atmosphere was deftly poised between display and disdain. Augustine sensed an entirely different world and feared that a simple, loving heart might not be enough to match an artist brought up in that air.

She was shown in. The Duchess lounged like a Greek statue on an ottoman in shadowed velvet, rare flowers in Sèvres vases accenting her presence. A young, dashing colonel sat twirling his whip until dismissed with cool politeness. The Duchess turned to Augustine with the languid interest of a practiced coquette.

Augustine, trembling, spoke her purpose: she understood why Théodore preferred the Duchess’s house and why her mind had power over him; she still loved her husband and had come to ask how to win him back. The Duchess, touched in spite of herself by this homage from virtue, gently reproved her tears — “we must deal firmly with sorrows, for love does not linger by a sick-bed”— and warned that melancholia, however graceful at first, ruined its victim. Then she offered practical counsel. She maintained she had enjoyed Sommervieux as a spectacle rather than as an accomplice, and she insisted women should admire great men but not live with them. Wives of such men who governed well, whether by possessing or feigning qualities the husband lacked, could keep him. These lofty souls had a speck of madness that women must use. Augustine must aim always to have the upper hand: never be despised, never show herself defeated, and never allow her husband to see her as conquered. Power was factitious; to reclaim a man one might need odious maneuvers. Then, with a touch of shrewd mischief, the Duchess proposed a practical step.

She led Augustine, who followed like an apprentice, through a back staircase into a gallery where Théodore’s portrait of Mademoiselle Guillaume hung. On seeing it Augustine cried out. “I knew it was no longer in my house,” she said. “But—here!” The Duchess confessed she had taken it to see what pitch of idiocy a man of genius might reach; she told Augustine she meant to return it, but at that moment, with caressing irony, she declared she would have it carried to Augustine’s carriage. “If, armed with such a talisman, you are not your husband’s mistress for a hundred years, you are not a woman,” she added. Augustine, grateful and dazzled, kissed the Duchess’s hand; the Duchess warmed at the thought that by morning Augustine would be forgotten.

Returning home, Augustine was aflame with new plans: coquettish stratagems, feigned aloofness, rehearsed speeches that seemed effective in the abstract. Yet confronted with her husband, her courage faltered. She placed the portrait in her room and awaited Théodore, hoping the talisman would do its work. At midnight the groom announced the artist’s carriage.

Théodore entered, startled by the illumination. At the sight of the portrait he asked coldly where she had found it. Augustine, in a voice of sweetness that might have melted anyone but a jealous, wounded artist, said the Duchess had returned it and that she had not known she possessed it. Théodore’s face clouded. His reaction was violent and possessive: “It is worthy of her!” he thundered. “I will be avenged! She shall die of shame; I will paint her! I will paint her as Messalina stealing out at night from the palace of Claudius.” He raged that he would kill her, and accused her of loving the cavalry colonel because he rode well. The scene escalated into frenzy; Théodore, torn between furious artistry and wounded vanity, rained threats and raving denunciations on the woman who sought him.

The whole scene was bitter and destructive. Théodore’s frenzy prompted acts and words that any woman older and less innocent than Augustine could have named madness. He attacked the portrait and wrecked it, perhaps symbolically destroying the last bridge between her and him. That night Augustine was left in ruins; at dawn Madame Guillaume found her daughter pale, eyes red, hair disordered, clutching a handkerchief soaked with tears, surrounded by ruins: torn fragments of a dress and the gilt frame broken in pieces. Augustine stood almost senseless with grief pointing to the wreck. Madame Guillaume, misinterpreting the scene, dismissed the loss as trivial and comforted her child with commonplace consolations, declaring the man a monster and repeating the old refrain that he was mad. Augustine implored silence; during that dreadful night she had found a patient resignation, a yielding that in mothers and devoted wives transcends ordinary human energy and perhaps touches chords God has withheld from men.

An inscription on a broken column in Montmartre cemetery records that Madame de Sommervieux died at twenty-seven. In this laconic phrasing one of her timid friends reads the last scene of a tragedy. Each year on All Souls’ Day he passes the youthful monument and wonders whether Augustine might not have needed a stronger nature to endure the violent embrace of genius. “The humble and modest flowers of the valley,” he reflects, “perish perhaps when transplanted too near the skies, to the region where storms gather and the sun is scorching.”
