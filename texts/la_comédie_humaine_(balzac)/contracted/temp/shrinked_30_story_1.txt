Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls exposed timber patterns and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. Overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood opposite studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows and inspected the third floor’s modest sash windows, clouded with green-tinted glass, occasionally glancing at the shop below where comic details amused him.

A large wooden beam, propped on four pillars, bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket and absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” recalling the shop’s old charm.

Dressed for a ball in a stylish cloak, white silk stockings and gloves, his disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried by, but the street was unusually still; his pale face, dazzling cravat and expressive eyes drew shopkeepers’ attention.

Three apprentices at the dormer laughed and sprinkled him with shaving cream, which he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face—like a Raphael Virgin—appeared and fled as the shutter slammed. Then the “Cat and Racket” shutters opened, a manservant hung the cloth sign, and behind iron bars the shop revealed many brown-linen-wrapped packages. Monsieur Guillaume, old-fashioned but reputed Paris’s best-stocked, was honest and profitable, though fellow traders grumbled about his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, awakening from a long sleep. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional dress, watched a stranger loitering by the shop. His shrewd, weathered face marked him a staunch traditionalist who lamented lost customs and imposed a strict routine on his three fearful apprentices.

At work Guillaume noticed the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest apprentice, seeing Mademoiselle Augustine hasten away, hailed a cab and averted a prank aimed at her.

He reproved the youths for idleness, recalling his own youthful industry. Though the apprentices came from comfortable homes and paid board, he demanded rigorous labor, unlike softer new shops. The shop was meticulous; Madame Guillaume’s frugality even excluded apprentices from the family dessert.

They kept strict routine—attending Mass together—and Joseph Lebas, the eldest apprentice, after years of service earned a modest wage. Guillaume eyed Joseph as a possible husband for his elder daughter Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger Augustine. Virginie was devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, delicate and charming, was stifled by maternal expectations. Amid the shop’s monotony quiet feelings grew, and Guillaume worried about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

The old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie; marrying off his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of a fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted and reserved, he kept his love hidden.

Months earlier a young painter had paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The shop, cave-like, opened on a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, a chubby maid—formed a peaceful, modest tableau. Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, the painter left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture the moment, he retreated to his studio and returned repeatedly to the Cat and Racket, even disguising himself to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet interrupted his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet, recognizing deep feeling in them, embraced him and suggested love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand, the paintings were exhibited and created a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre, and the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

These public successes contrasted with Augustine’s monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition. With her cousin’s help Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait, she recognized herself and felt terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Fleeing the crowd, she tried to leave but was drawn back, wept, and exchanged a brief nod with the artist. That connection ignited guilt and exhilaration; the Salon’s chaos and the painter’s gaze revealed unfamiliar emotions. She feigned a headache to avoid talk, longed that night for the elegance she had always admired, and took comfort in the idea of being a genius’s wife. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. The paintings’ disappearance, however, had awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

The morning after the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had captured Augustine's heart, found it hard to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume under her mother's watch, while Theodore, fearing rivals among the assistants, bribed a maid to arrange discreet exchanges; they agreed to meet at Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family concentrated on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume’s supervision, tallying inventory and balancing accounts, indifferent to Augustine’s longing. When work ended, they relaxed: attended a performance of Cendrillon and the apprentices enjoyed a boisterous night out.

The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review profitable results, offering partnership in recognition of his work. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once clarified, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame later chastised Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her to her room, prompting a heated discussion about their daughter’s future. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore, insisting on his respectability. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match. Her report swayed the parents. By evening Guillaume informed Joseph, the family celebrated, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and Monsieur consented: the lovers and household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar witnessed two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore shone with happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had wanted Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance might bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated in his cautious approach to settlements. After a splendid ball and supper the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. Naïve at times, she remained happily unaware of much around her. As pregnancy approached, Theodore felt the pull of work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the social proof of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew between them. Theodore hoped studio life would draw her in, but she could not adapt; at gatherings her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and her discomfort showed.

Gradually Theodore’s warmth cooled and Augustine keenly felt his indifference. Three years into marriage, she sank into sorrow as his involvement with the Duchesse intensified her fear of being supplanted. Determined, she tried to educate herself in poetry and art to reconnect, but only highlighted her inadequacy. She briefly returned to her childhood home—where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness—but Joseph’s practical, commercial view offered no comfort. Augustine ultimately went back to her husband’s house, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter, whose visit interrupted years of monotonous reminiscence about past business ups and downs—most notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume fussed over his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors; Madame Guillaume, placed on the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law’s influence, filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation contrasted sharply with Augustine’s turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Augustine displayed her achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents received with keen interest. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter. When Augustine described his late nights and eccentric habits, her mother disapproved of his profession and lifestyle. Augustine defended him, and the family’s skepticism turned into a debate about genius versus marital responsibility.

At the mention of divorce her parents were shocked; Monsieur Guillaume offered practical support. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine recoiled, recognizing an isolation only those who had suffered similarly could know.

Determined to change course, she sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. In the Duchess’s opulent home, Augustine felt envy and rivalry. The Duchess listened sympathetically and advised a strategy: with men of genius, show strength and self-control, cultivate mystery, and never reveal the whole measure of one’s affection. Though uneasy, Augustine accepted these lessons as necessary.

The Duchess lent Augustine a portrait Theodore had painted to rekindle his interest. Hopeful, Augustine returned and tried to become that image; Theodore’s fury and jealousy erupted into a violent quarrel that shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine dead among the wreckage. She died young, her tragic end a poignant reminder of the struggle women endure seeking love and fulfillment amid the complexities of artistic genius; the inscription on her grave bears that sorrow.
