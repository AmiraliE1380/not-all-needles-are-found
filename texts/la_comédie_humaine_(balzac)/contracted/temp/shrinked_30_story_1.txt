Halfway down Rue Saint-Denis, near Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls revealed timber frames and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer, while overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood opposite studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows, noted the third floor’s modest sash windows—crude, clouded with green-tinted glass—and glanced at the shop below, where small comic details amused him. A large timber on four pillars bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket and absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a trace of the shop’s old charm.

Dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, he looked fresh from a wedding or ball; white gloves and dishevelled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried by, yet the street was unusually still, his pale face and dazzling cravat drawing attention. Three apprentices at the dormer laughed as they sprinkled him with shaving cream, which he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face, recalling a Raphael Virgin, flashed and fled as the shutter slammed. The “Cat and Racket” shutters opened; a manservant hung the cloth sign. Behind iron bars the shop displayed many brown-linen-wrapped packages. Though old-fashioned, Monsieur Guillaume was reputed Paris’s best-stocked cloth merchant—honest and profitable—while fellow traders grumbled about the notary’s heavy discounts.

Guillaume kept a strict routine and lamented changing customs. He noticed a stranger; when Augustine hurried off an apprentice averted a prank. Guillaume condemned idleness and insisted on hard work, unlike newer shops; apprentices came from comfortable homes and Madame denied dessert. The routine included Mass; Joseph Lebas earned a wage. Guillaume thought Joseph suited elder Virginie, though Joseph loved Augustine. Virginie was devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, stifled by her mother, fed on romances. As feelings deepened, Guillaume fretted about his daughters' prospects.

From that day the old merchant, mourning his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie’s: marrying his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, conscious of a fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted but reserved, he kept his love hidden.

Months earlier a young painter had paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The cave-like shop opened on a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, with a chubby maid—formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Fresh from Rome and tired of grand art, the painter craved real life. Augustine, pensive and hardly eating, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

Resolved to capture it, he returned to the Cat and Racket, even in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his seclusion to ask about the Salon submission. The artist unveiled two masterpieces; Girodet, sensing deep feeling, embraced him and declared love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand them, the paintings were shown and caused a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre; the unforgettable portrait won acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These successes contrasted with Augustine’s monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s help Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself, torn between terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned. Fleeing the crowd she tried to leave but was drawn back, wept, and exchanged a quiet nod with him. That brief contact ignited guilt and exhilaration; she feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, dreading her mother’s planned visit. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the paintings’ disappearance had awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

After the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, hesitated to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat under her mother's watch in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume; fearing rivals among the assistants, Theodore bribed a maid to arrange discreet meetings and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family concentrated on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, indifferent to Augustine’s longing. When work ended they relaxed: they saw Cendrillon and the apprentices caroused.

The next morning Guillaume resumed routine, summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results, and offered him a partnership. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once corrected, Guillaume said he would consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tensions rose: Madame scolded Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her away. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore and insisted on his respectability. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match. Her report swayed the parents. By evening Guillaume informed Joseph, the family celebrated, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and though Monsieur remained cautious about money, he consented and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant while Virginie, simply dressed, followed. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married first, but the priests favored the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated in his cautious settlements. After a splendid ball the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection and Augustine, lost in love, believed she would always charm him. Naïve, she remained largely unaware of the world around her. As pregnancy neared, Theodore felt the pull of work, reconnected with friends, and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the prestige of being seen with a handsome, accomplished wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew. Theodore hoped studio life would engage her, but she could not adapt; her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and made her uncomfortable.

Gradually his warmth cooled and, three years in, Augustine sank into sorrow as his intimacy with the Duchesse deepened. Attempts to learn poetry and art only highlighted her inadequacy. She briefly sought solace at her childhood home, where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness, but Joseph’s practical consolations helped little. Augustine returned to her husband’s house, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal their fractured marriage.

The elderly Guillaumes welcomed their daughter Augustine, whose visit interrupted years of monotonous reminiscence—especially about the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume inspected his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, appointed through her son‑in‑law to the Army’s clothing consulting board, filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation contrasted sharply with Augustine’s turbulent life, leaving them both proud and appalled.

Augustine, despite her turmoil, showed achievements and aired marital grievances, which they received eagerly. Madame was particularly curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter; learning of his late nights and eccentric habits she disapproved of his lifestyle and profession. Augustine defended him, and the family’s incredulity became a debate about genius versus marital responsibility. When divorce was mentioned the parents were shocked; Monsieur even offered practical support. Overwhelmed and unable to convey her suffering, Augustine recoiled from separation and felt isolated.

Determined to change course, she sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. At the Duchess’s opulent home she felt envy and rivalry. The Duchess advised projecting strength and mystery, never full affection, and gave Augustine a portrait by Theodore to rekindle him. Returning to embody the image, Augustine provoked Theodore’s fury; a violent quarrel shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine despairing amid the wreckage; she died young. Her tragic end remains a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love and fulfillment beside artistic genius; the inscription on her grave reflects this sorrow.
