Halfway down Rue Saint-Denis, near Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls exposed timber frames and cracked plaster; a warped triangular roof overhung the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. Overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood opposite studying its peculiarities. He ignored the narrow boarded first-floor windows, noted the third floor’s modest sash windows—crude, clouded with green-tinted glass—and glanced at the shop below, where small comic details amused him.

A large timber on four pillars bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket and an absurd ball. A faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of the shop’s old charm.

Dressed in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, he looked fresh from a wedding or ball; white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent revelry. Market gardeners hurried by, yet the street was unusually still. His pale face, dazzling cravat and expressive eyes drew shopkeepers’ attention.

Three apprentices then appeared at the dormer, laughing as they sprinkled him with shaving cream, which he brushed off indifferently. A young girl’s face, recalling a Raphael Virgin, flashed and fled as the shutter slammed. Then the “Cat and Racket” shutters opened; a manservant hung the cloth sign. Behind iron bars the shop displayed many packages wrapped in brown linen. Though old-fashioned, Monsieur Guillaume was reputed Paris’s best-stocked cloth merchant—honest and profitable—while fellow traders grumbled about the notary’s heavy discounts.

Monsieur Guillaume, an old merchant on Rue Saint‑Denis, stood at his shop threshold lamenting lost customs and enforcing a strict routine on his three apprentices. At work he noticed a stranger’s anxious glances; when Mademoiselle Augustine hurried away the eldest apprentice hailed a cab, averting a prank. Guillaume reproved the youths for idleness and demanded rigorous labor, unlike softer new shops; the apprentices came from comfortable homes. Madame Guillaume’s frugality even denied them the family dessert; the shop ran with strict discipline and a steady routine—attending Mass—and Joseph Lebas earned only a modest wage. Guillaume regarded Joseph as a fit match for his elder daughter Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger Augustine. Virginie was devout and severe; eighteen‑year‑old Augustine, delicate and charming, was stifled by maternal expectations and fed on romantic novels. Amid the shop’s monotony feelings deepened and Guillaume worried about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like those of Joseph Lebas and Virginie: marrying his daughter would repay a sacred debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, mindful of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted but reserved, he kept his love hidden.

Months earlier a young painter paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy. The cave-like shop opened onto a dining room lit by a hanging lamp that recalled Dutch interiors. The family—Augustine among them, with a chubby maid—formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Fresh from Rome and weary of grand art, the painter craved real life. Augustine, pensive and hardly eating, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture it, he returned to the Cat and Racket, even in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. One morning his friend Girodet broke his isolation to ask about the Salon submission. The artist unveiled two masterpieces; Girodet, sensing deep feeling, embraced him and said love had made the work great. Despite warnings that the public might not understand them, the paintings were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior launched a new genre; the portrait was unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These successes contrasted with Augustine’s monastic life in Rue Saint-Denis until Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s help Augustine obtained permission to see the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself, caught between terror and excitement. The artist, nearby, whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Fleeing the crowd, Augustine tried to leave but was drawn back to the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she wept and exchanged a quiet nod with the artist. That brief contact ignited guilt and exhilaration; she feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, dreading her mother’s planned visit. The next day her parents returned disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Yet the paintings’ disappearance had awakened in Augustine a woman’s instinctive sensitivity.

After the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine's heart, found it hard to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume under her mother's watch, while Theodore, fearing rivals among the assistants, bribed a maid to arrange discreet meetings and they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family concentrated on stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, tallying inventory and closing accounts, indifferent to Augustine’s longing. When the work ended they relaxed: they attended a performance of Cendrillon and the apprentices enjoyed a boisterous night out.

The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results, offering to make him a partner. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted that he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard and thought he meant Virginie. Once corrected, Guillaume said he would consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tensions rose; Madame scolded Augustine for inattentiveness in church and sent her away. Augustine confessed her love for Theodore and insisted on his respectability. Monsieur, wary of artists, protested until Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune and advocated the match. Her report swayed the parents. By evening Guillaume informed Joseph, the family celebrated, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and though Monsieur remained cautious about money, he consented and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt vindicated in his cautious settlements. After a splendid ball and supper the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. Naïve at times, she remained happily unaware of much around her. As pregnancy approached, Theodore felt the pull of work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, enjoying the social proof of being seen with a handsome, accomplished wife.

Augustine liked society but soon felt out of place among Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew. Theodore hoped studio life would draw her in, but she could not adapt; at gatherings her innocence clashed with the freer manners of artists and her discomfort showed.

Gradually Theodore’s warmth cooled and Augustine keenly felt his indifference. Three years into marriage she sank into sorrow as his intimacy with the Duchesse deepened. She tried to learn poetry and art to bridge the gap, but her efforts only highlighted her inadequacy. Seeking solace, she briefly returned to her childhood home, where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran their drapery with steady, ordinary happiness. Joseph’s practical view offered no comfort. Augustine returned to her husband’s home, burdened by uncertainty and longing, trying every remedy to heal their fractured marriage.

The elderly Guillaumes welcomed their daughter Augustine; her visit interrupted years of monotonous reminiscence about past business successes and failures, notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume—appointed to the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son‑in‑law—filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation contrasted sharply with Augustine’s turbulent life, leaving them both proud and appalled.

Augustine, despite her turmoil, displayed achievements and aired marital grievances, which they received with keen interest. Madame Guillaume was particularly curious about Augustine’s husband, Theodore, a painter; on hearing of his late nights and eccentric habits she disapproved of both his lifestyle and profession. Augustine defended him, and the family’s incredulity turned into a debate about genius versus marital responsibility. When divorce was mentioned her parents were shocked; Monsieur Guillaume even offered practical support. Overwhelmed and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine recoiled from separation, realizing she was isolated in a way only those who had endured similar trials could understand.

Determined to change course, Augustine sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to force Theodore back but to learn how to captivate him. At the Duchess’s opulent home she felt envy and sensed rivalry. The Duchess counseled shrewdly: with men of genius one must project strength and mystery and never reveal one’s full affection. Offered a portrait of Augustine painted by Theodore to rekindle his interest, she returned resolved to embody the image. Theodore’s fury and jealousy erupted into a violent quarrel that shattered her hopes. The next morning her mother found Augustine in despair amid the wreckage; overwhelmed by her tumultuous life, she died young. Her tragic end remains a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love and fulfillment beside artistic genius; the inscription on her grave reflects this sorrow.
