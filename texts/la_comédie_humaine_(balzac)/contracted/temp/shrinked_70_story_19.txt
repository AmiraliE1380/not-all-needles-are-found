In the winter of 1829-30, the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu worried about her daughter Camille's feelings for the Comte de Restaud, cautioning her about his mother, Mlle. Goriot, whose past could harm Camille's future. Derville, a family friend, noted Camille's affection for Restaud and reflected on wealth's influence, contrasting Gobseck, a wealthy usurer, with struggling individuals.

Derville, now a licentiate in law, had gained Gobseck's respect and became head-clerk. He sought Gobseck's help for a loan, which he secured at high interest. After becoming an attorney and marrying Fanny, he repaid Gobseck with her inheritance.

At a bachelor breakfast, Derville introduced M. de Trailles to Gobseck, who refused him a loan due to debts. When a distressed Countess sought to pawn her diamonds, Gobseck's obsession with wealth surfaced. Following the Count's death, chaos ensued as the Countess searched for hidden documents, ultimately leading to Gobseck contemplating the consequences of her actions and reflecting on greed and morality.
