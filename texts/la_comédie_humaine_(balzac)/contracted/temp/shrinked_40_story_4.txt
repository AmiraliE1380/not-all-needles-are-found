In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and daughter, their sadness stemming from shared hardship. Bartolomeo, an older man in tattered clothes, had a serious demeanor, while his distressed wife tried to calm their weary daughter, who still exhibited Italian grace.

Bartolomeo's intense gaze attracted attention, but his fierce demeanor deterred sympathy. After contemplation, he made a desperate decision, handing a dagger to his wife and declaring his intention to confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier, leading to a tense standoff.

Fortunately, Lucien Bonaparte recognized Bartolomeo and took him to see Napoleon, who was meeting with Murat, Lannes, and Rapp. After dismissing the others, Napoleon asked Bartolomeo why he had come. Bartolomeo revealed that he had killed the Portas, a family responsible for his son’s death and the destruction of his home after they set fire to his vineyard.

Surprised, Napoleon questioned Bartolomeo's motives. Bartolomeo expressed despair over the Bonaparte brothers' lack of support, feeling they had forgotten their Corsican roots. Napoleon explained he could not protect Bartolomeo but offered him a chance to live in Paris, away from vendetta culture.

Bartolomeo accepted, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte family and expressing gratitude for their recognition of his plight. Napoleon promised financial assistance, emphasizing the importance of adhering to the law in Paris, where personal justice was not tolerated. Fifteen years later, Bartolomeo's life in Paris set the stage for future events intertwined with themes of vengeance and familial loyalty.

Servin, a distinguished artist, established a studio for young girls interested in painting. At around forty, he was dedicated to his art and married to a general's daughter. Initially, mothers brought their daughters to the studio, but soon they felt comfortable sending them alone, reassured by Servin's integrity and quality instruction.

To maintain a harmonious class composition, Servin only accepted pupils from wealthy families, even refusing aspiring professional artists. His careful approach earned him a stellar reputation, making him synonymous with feminine art education.

The studio was meticulously arranged, with a walled-off entrance leading to a spacious attic filled with light from large glass panes. The walls were adorned with caricatures and models, creating an atmosphere that blended artistic chaos with elegance. On a bright July day, the studio buzzed with activity as young girls painted, laughed, and formed distinct cliques based on social status.

Among them was Ginevra Piombo, the most beautiful and modest girl, who sat apart from her peers. The aristocratic group, led by Amelie Thirion, often looked down on the wealthier but less refined girls from the banking sector. Tensions simmered as Amelie orchestrated a slight against Ginevra, moving her easel away from its usual spot, which sparked whispers of jealousy and rivalry.

Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced ostracism from the royalist girls, influenced by the political climate following the Bourbons' return. Despite her beauty and talent, she became a target of jealousy, particularly from Amelie, who sought to undermine her.

When Ginevra arrived, she was met with silence, a stark contrast to the usual camaraderie. Her presence commanded attention, yet she remained oblivious to the tension. As she settled into her work, she sensed a mysterious presence behind a partition—a man hidden away, breathing quietly. This revelation filled her with curiosity and concern for his safety.

Ginevra's indifference to the slight against her only fueled her peers' animosity. Meanwhile, Amelie and her group attempted to humiliate Ginevra further, but their efforts were thwarted by her apparent calm.

Madame Servin's arrival interrupted the brewing conflict, announcing that her husband could not attend that day. Ginevra and Madame Servin exchanged glances, both aware of the hidden man. As the session continued, Ginevra focused on sketching the officer's head, inspired by the urgency of the moment.

Servin praised her work, drawing the attention of the other girls, especially Amelie, who was determined to uncover Ginevra's secret. The atmosphere shifted as Ginevra's skill became evident, and Servin's admiration heightened the tension.

As the day progressed, Ginevra and Servin shared a private conversation about the hidden officer, revealing the political stakes involved. Ginevra offered her assistance, demonstrating her commitment to protecting the man and her loyalty to Servin. The dynamics in the studio shifted as the girls became increasingly aware of Ginevra's focus and the mystery surrounding her. Amelie, driven by jealousy, attempted to spy on Ginevra, but the latter remained resolute in her purpose.

In a dim garret, painter Servin and Ginevra were startled by a knock, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, Luigi, who was wounded and shaken by Labedoyere's death sentence. He recoiled upon seeing Ginevra, but Servin introduced her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor. Luigi expressed despair over his cowardice during Labedoyere's execution, and Ginevra offered her savings, inspiring hope in him.

As they spoke, Ginevra felt a deep connection to Luigi, and their bond grew. Servin asked if Luigi could save Labedoyere, but he could only seek revenge. The next day, Luigi shared his harrowing experiences from the retreats at Moscow and Waterloo, captivating Ginevra. They developed a secret communication system through Corsican songs.

However, rumors about their relationship spread among Ginevra’s classmates, prompting their departure from the studio. Ginevra remained focused on her feelings for Luigi, urging him to submit to royal authority for safety, but he refused, staying loyal to his beliefs.

One evening, Ginevra learned from Laure that the other girls had left due to gossip. Shocked, she confided in Servin, who dismissed the rumors, valuing their bond. Luigi, feeling guilty, overheard their conversation. Ginevra proposed to help him secure a pardon through her father’s influence, while her parents grew anxious over her late return home, highlighting themes of loyalty, love, and societal judgment.

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, embracing his daughter. Ginevra reassured her mother, and Piombo's joy was evident as he rubbed his hands, a habit from his court days. “Now, to dinner!” he called, referring to Ginevra as “Signora Piombellina.” During dinner, he noted her long hours at the studio, suggesting painting had become more important than family. Ginevra, preoccupied, eventually confessed, “I love a young man.”

Her parents were taken aback. When asked about him, she revealed he was without fortune and a comrade of Labedoyere, who had been proscribed. Piombo reacted with disbelief, insisting that Ginevra should love no one but him. She countered that love was not a choice.

“I thought you would remain faithful to me,” Piombo lamented, but Ginevra reminded him of his own absences for political missions. “Life has necessities,” she defended her feelings.

Piombo’s anger flared at the thought of losing his daughter. Ginevra pleaded for understanding, promising her love would not diminish her affection for him. “You will learn to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she insisted.

Piombo could not accept this. “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me,” he declared, his rage palpable. Ginevra maintained that her love was pure and sincere.

The tension escalated, with Piombo declaring he would never allow the marriage. Ginevra stood firm, stating, “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta.” Piombo’s fury intensified, warning her of the consequences of marrying a Porta, the family responsible for her brother’s death. Ginevra argued that Luigi was innocent, having been just a child during the vendetta.

Despite her father’s threats, Ginevra remained resolute. “I will be his wife,” she asserted, leading to a confrontation where Piombo nearly attacked her but ultimately hesitated.

The next day, Ginevra found herself barred from leaving the house. She communicated with Luigi through a chambermaid, and their love deepened despite the growing rift with her father.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother attempted to mediate, but the arrival of two notaries to discuss her marriage to Luigi escalated tensions. Piombo confronted the notaries, who were merely doing their duty.

In desperation, Ginevra knelt before her father, pleading for his consent. “Give me Luigi, or I die!” she cried, but Piombo, overwhelmed, rejected her. “You have no father,” he declared, casting her out.

Ginevra entered Luigi’s modest lodging, declaring, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” Luigi promised to love her in place of her parents, and Ginevra resolved to find happiness with him. “Then let us be happy,” she said, her spirit unbroken.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin, who coldly refused to help. Hurt yet proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a hopeful future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning, Ginevra received trunks filled with essentials from her mother, including a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter moved Ginevra, but just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived, and her resolve to abandon him faded.

On their wedding day, Ginevra had no friends present. Luigi brought two witnesses, both dressed simply, reflecting the somber nature of the occasion. Ginevra, though beautiful, wore modest attire, and the atmosphere was grave.

As they walked to the mayor’s office, they encountered other wedding parties filled with happiness, contrasting sharply with their own situation. The bureaucratic ceremony felt cold, overshadowing their love with familial disapproval.

After a brief ceremony, Ginevra and Luigi were married, but they left feeling the weight of their families' absence. Outside, Luigi expressed gratitude, and Ginevra felt relief despite the challenges ahead. They hurried to their modest apartment, where Luigi embraced her, declaring their true wedding had begun.

Inside their new home, Ginevra was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements Luigi had made, including a studio for her art. Their initial days together were consumed by passion, often forgetting the outside world.

However, reality soon set in, and they realized they needed to work to sustain themselves. Ginevra began copying paintings, quickly gaining clients, while Luigi struggled until he decided to use his handwriting for copying services. His efforts paid off, allowing them to support themselves comfortably.

This period marked the happiest time in their lives, filled with love and shared labor. They enjoyed evenings together, filled with music and laughter, and neither expressed regret about their choices. Yet, Ginevra occasionally felt sorrow for her parents, imagining their loneliness over her absence.

To commemorate their anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing not just her likeness but the essence of their love. This masterpiece symbolized their happiness and commitment to one another. Another year passed, and their life together continued in blissful harmony, marked by their enduring love.

In early 1819, Ginevra's picture-dealers encouraged her to create original works due to rising competition. Acknowledging her neglect of genre painting, she attempted portraiture but faced fierce competition from struggling artists. Despite some savings, Ginevra and Luigi's future remained uncertain.

As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly, facing declining writing fees that forced him to labor longer hours. Ginevra produced several commendable paintings, but the market was unkind, leaving her efforts largely unsold. Their financial situation worsened, yet they masked their anxieties with love, fearing poverty would strip away their happiness.

One night, Ginevra found Luigi working late and felt guilty for his sacrifices. She resolved to help by coloring lithographs. They both pretended to sleep, driven by love and desperation. One evening, Luigi noticed Ginevra’s lamp and realized she was also working. They shared a tender moment, hopeful despite their hardships.

Their situation worsened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Initially joyful, they soon faced financial strain, selling possessions and jewels. Ginevra, now frail, struggled to care for her sickly child. In despair, Luigi sold Ginevra’s remaining paintings and furniture, but their situation continued to deteriorate. 

One evening, Luigi realized Ginevra was starving and decided to sell himself as a military substitute. He rushed home, only to find Ginevra in despair, holding their lifeless child. She expressed her love and regret before succumbing to exhaustion in Luigi's arms. Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents were haunted by memories of her, and when Luigi appeared at their door, he revealed the tragic fate of their families, shattering them with grief.
