In October 1800 a foreigner, Bartolomeo di Piombo, stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and young daughter. Over sixty, grave and troubled, his wife tried to stay calm for their child; their despair drew attention, though his fierce gaze discouraged sympathy.

After a moment’s thought Bartolomeo handed a dagger to his wife and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. A soldier stopped him at the palace, demanding a written request. Following a tense standoff, Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, recognized him and brought him to meet his brother Napoleon.

Napoleon dismissed his aides and spoke privately. Bartolomeo sought asylum and confessed he had killed the Portas—seven members of a family who had burned his vineyard and murdered his son—saying he had avenged his child. He complained the Bonapartes had been indifferent despite his earlier protection of their family.

Now a national leader, Napoleon felt bound by law and could not endorse personal vengeance, yet he acknowledged the Corsican vendetta. He offered Bartolomeo a chance to live in Paris with financial assistance, warning the law would not tolerate private justice. Bartolomeo accepted, pledged loyalty, and appeared hopeful. Lucien, learning they had arrived with nothing, gave them money and promised further help, noting their Corsican property wouldn’t sustain them in Paris. Fifteen years later events made this context necessary.

Servin, an upright, respected artist of about forty married to a general’s dowerless daughter, was the first to open a studio for young girls who wished to paint. Mothers at first accompanied their daughters, then trusted Servin’s methods and sent them alone. He admitted only young ladies from wealthy families—refusing aspiring professionals who would demand more thorough instruction—to avoid class disputes. Careful and exacting, he gained a stellar reputation among the aristocracy; when a girl showed aptitude, mothers recommended him without hesitation. He became synonymous with feminine art as Herbault was with bonnets or Leroy with gowns, and though well connected to Parisian elites he kept an independent, patriotic wit.

The studio, arranged for pupils, sat above his apartment; the attic entrance was sealed and reached by a small spiral staircase. The large room, lit by broad panes veiled with green linen shades, had dark-gray walls hung with caricatures and plaster models—a controlled disorder suited to study.

On a bright July day the studio hummed with young women at easels, each with distinct expressions and styles. They split into two cliques: daughters of wealthy merchants and the aristocratic set led by Amelie Thirion, vain and pretentious. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modestly dressed and somewhat apart, appearing anxious. Though the girls laughed and chatted, Ginevra’s talent and beauty inspired jealousy; to ostracize her, Amelie slid Ginevra’s easel away from the group, provoking whispers that the slight carried political overtones as well.

The political climate after the Bourbons’ second return sharpened such tensions. Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, met disdain from royalist peers; Amelie’s rebuke was both personal and political. Yet Ginevra remained absorbed in her work.

A mysterious sound behind a partition revealed that Servin was sheltering a political fugitive, an officer of the Imperial Guard. Excitement and concern followed; Ginevra chose to protect him, sitting near the hiding place rather than risk his exposure. Madame Servin’s calm arrival suggested she knew of the guest. To mask the faint breathing, Ginevra hummed a Corsican song and sketched the officer’s head with fervor. Servin praised her; curious classmates, including Amelie, were drawn to her sheet, but Ginevra deftly deflected attention.

Servin moved among his pupils watching Ginevra. Their shared knowledge created a tacit understanding; her resolve to keep the secret and aid the officer’s escape grew firmer. Outside, political turmoil pressed on, but within the studio the young women navigated rivalries and friendships, divided and united by art, ambition, and allegiance.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra believed themselves alone, Servin opened the door to a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and his face pale with pain. He started at Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. Recognizing her patriotism, the soldier visibly relaxed.

"You are wounded," Ginevra observed.

"It's nothing, mademoiselle," he said. "The wound is healing."

Cries in the street announced a death sentence—"Condemned to death!"—and at the name "Labedoyere" the officer turned ashen and sank onto a stool, despairing that Labedoyere faced execution for their cause while he hid. Ginevra barred his exit and asked bluntly whether he expected to restore the Emperor and could raise again the giant who had failed to maintain himself. Overwhelmed by shame after losing his protector, he declared himself ready to die, insisting an honest man's life was worth more than two traitors', even speaking of selling his life to the executioner.

Alarmed, Ginevra offered her savings, explaining her father's wealth and saying he would not blame her for aiding a soldier. She urged patience and escape until the political storm passed, suggesting he serve elsewhere rather than desert. Her practical kindness, framed with maternal grace and hope, moved him. He admired her beauty and called her "an angel of goodness," though thoughts of Labedoyere and the cause returned to torment him. In the ensuing silence their acquaintance deepened into an unspoken bond. When Servin asked if the officer could save Labedoyere, the soldier said he could only avenge him. Ginevra, stirred by the idea of vengeance and recognizing his passionate devotion to Napoleon, felt a magical connection as the sun set on his face and left smiling, a spark of hope kindled between them.

The next day she returned to the studio. The officer, now called Luigi, spoke freely of the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo; Ginevra listened with growing admiration. Discovering they both spoke Corsican tightened their closeness—shared language and memory reinforcing their bond. Days passed, and Ginevra’s absences from gatherings provoked gossip. Mademoiselle Thirion withdrew from class, attendance declined, and Laure confided that her mother insisted she leave because of the scandal. Ginevra promised they would meet again; Laure departed, and Ginevra felt the loss.

Ginevra and Luigi’s intimacy quietly grew into mutual love. When Servin, absorbed in an exhibition, dismissed rumors, Luigi entered and half-jokingly claimed responsibility for the lost students; the painter playfully suggested marriage. Their shared silence that followed was the first open recognition of their love. Servin encouraged their union, and Ginevra pledged to use her father's influence to seek a pardon for Luigi.

At home, her parents grew anxious over her late returns. Bartolomeo, devoted and politically loyal, worried for his only daughter. When Ginevra finally came home, her father embraced her with relief; the warm reunion confirmed that her parents remained her greatest supporters.

Back in the studio, despite scandal and politics, Ginevra and Luigi’s love, forged in adversity and rooted in shared ideals, became a source of strength that united them against the world.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” the old man cried.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra exclaimed, but was soon set down and reassured that it was a jest. The baroness revived; Piombo beamed; the parents seemed recovered.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo announced, taking Ginevra’s hand. When he teased that she spent more time at the studio than with them, Ginevra protested. Her mother hinted at a surprise—perhaps a portrait—but Ginevra, pale and hesitant, confessed she had been busy for another reason: she loved a young man. Her parents were astonished.

He is no prince, she said; he had no fortune and was a comrade of Labedoyere, proscribed and sheltered by Servin. Piombo praised Servin yet scolded her for loving any man but her father. Ginevra answered gently that love was not a choice. She reminded him she had borne his long absences while he served Napoleon without complaint; she did not love him only for favors, and his reproaches revealed selfishness. He flared; she replied she did not blame him—each had motives: he for ego, she because, raised under his protection, she sought a husband who could protect her in turn.

Piombo bristled at talk of owing benefits. “Oh! stay with us! I cannot bear to have you love another man,” he pleaded. Ginevra assured him she need not leave: they could both love her, and he would come to know the man she intended to make part of the family. Piombo recalled an old chance to marry offered when Napoleon had ordered it, which she had declined to spare him being alone. This enraged him: “You don’t wish to leave me alone, yet you marry! I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us.” Elisa sat stunned. “We have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry,” he moaned, half in despair, half in theatrical grief.

Ginevra expected fury but found him gentler; she implored him to love her for herself and insisted the man loved her truly and would never make her unhappy. Any comparison set him ablaze: “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me; if he does not, I would kill him,” he raged. She met his fury calmly: she loved him, though she felt unworthy; love for her father and for her lover were different feelings that couldn't simply be ranked. Piombo vowed she would not marry him; she vowed she would.

The baroness feared ruin. Piombo begged her to say nothing that night and let him think, even kneeling; she refused: “Ginevra Piombo does not break her word.” Elisa urged compromise—“We are sent into the world to marry”—but Piombo insisted no paternal order could be unjust. Ginevra argued a refusal to obey an unjust order was not disobedience, and that forbidding her marriage would only make her hate them. He accused her of not loving them; she denied it, yet said honoring them only while they lived would be empty. Anger cooled; she clung to him, weeping that she could not outlive him. His rage melted into affection; he played with her like a child but still refused any introduction to Luigi.

Days passed. Ginevra grew tender, thanking her father as if for consent, singing and playing. A week later her mother whispered she had persuaded Piombo to receive the man. Ginevra, elated, returned home with Luigi on her arm. Luigi, an officer who had fought beside the Emperor, had emerged from hiding after Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre restored his name; he was anxious to meet Piombo. His pallor and fear deepened Ginevra’s love.

At home Piombo sat stern. Ginevra announced she had brought a soldier who fought at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo sneered that the man bore no decoration; Luigi, modest and shy, said he no longer wore such honors. His simple, respectful manner seemed to appease Piombo. Madame Piombo, noting the softening, observed that Monsieur resembled someone known in Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi confirmed Nina was his sister. Then Piombo asked bluntly, “Are you Luigi Porta?”

“Yes,” Luigi answered.

The name staggered Piombo and Elisa; they left the room in silence, abandoning Ginevra in shock. The revelation implied a vendetta. Ginevra, too young to have been told the family’s tragic past, listened as her mother revealed that the Portas had once killed Ginevra’s brother and burned their house, and that Piombo had retaliated, massacring their family. Luigi had been tied to a bed before the house was set on fire. He had survived, taken to Genoa at six, raised by an old man named Colonna, warned of enemies, and told to take his present name to elude them; he had no other memory.

Ginevra warned Luigi to leave: he was safe only under her father’s roof; once he left, Piombo’s Corsicans would pursue him. She realized their love must be purer and stronger because of the peril. Luigi, shaken, squeezed her hand; she led him to his lodging and saw him safely housed. Returning, she greeted her parents with calm resolve and observed her mother’s tears. They dined in silence; in the dim salon Piombo tried to speak but doubted himself, requesting the fire be lit because he felt cold. Both suffered, pride and pain masking their love.

On Ginevra’s birthday the baroness, desperate to heal the rupture, gathered them in Bartolomeo’s study and summoned two notaries with witnesses to perform the legal formalities for Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta. Bartolomeo watched them with foreboding. When the older notary announced their business—to observe the law relative to the daughter’s marriage—Piombo erupted, denouncing any law that would destroy paternal authority or rob an old man of his last consolation.

In fury he seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses intervened, but he shoved them aside with the force of a man whose world had toppled. Seeing him advance, Ginevra knelt and implored him. Overcome, he threw the dagger aside. She begged his mercy, kneeling and asking for consent with a trembling voice. Bartolomeo, unable to accept the union, pushed her away: the wife of Luigi Porta could not be a Piombo; she was, in effect, dead to him. “Leave my house, unhappy girl,” he declared, turning her out.

Ginevra fled to Luigi’s humble lodging. “My Luigi, we have no fortune but our love,” she said. “Then I am richer than kings!” he replied. She told him her parents had cast her off; he vowed to love her in their place. They resolved to be happy despite the shadow over them—joy and dread mingling as she declared, “Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!”

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father's house she sought refuge with Madame Servin, who received her coldly, resentful of the trouble Ginevra had caused her husband. Proud but stunned, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi. He devoted himself to her and painted a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father’s harshness.

One morning Ginevra received trunks of essentials from her mother and a loving, sorrowful letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. Deeply moved and tempted to return, she nonetheless abandoned the thought when Luigi arrived; she resolved to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was essentially alone. Luigi brought two simple witnesses in ordinary clothes; their quiet presence and Ginevra’s beauty made the mood somber. Passing other celebratory parties only highlighted their difference. At the mayor’s office the ceremony felt like a legal formality: the clerk noted the absence of her parents and confirmed a protest, an oppressive reminder of their disapproval. The ritual was reduced to signatures on a register.

After signing, they left with mixed relief and sadness. Luigi expressed gratitude, and Ginevra felt some happiness. In a modest chapel an old priest married them; his remarks about their parents’ absence stung, but a sergeant’s simple blessing comforted her.

They moved into a modest apartment Luigi called their true wedding. He had thoughtfully arranged artistic supplies for her; she delighted in the details, and together they found a small paradise in their compact home.

At first they indulged in love, but soon had to work. Ginevra copied paintings and found clients; Luigi, after initial struggle, offered copying and handwriting services, then secured enough work to hire assistants. This period, filled with mutual labor and quiet evenings, became the happiest of their lives. Ginevra rarely complained though she sometimes felt pangs of remorse for the family she had left; to mark an anniversary she painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, and another year passed in contentment.

At the beginning of 1819, picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition intensified. Madame Porta regretted not taking up genre painting earlier, which might have improved her standing. Ginevra tried portraiture but faced stiff competition from established artists. With only modest savings, she and Luigi grew anxious about their future.

As winter progressed, Luigi labored relentlessly against a shrinking market for writing. Unable to afford help, he worked longer hours for the same pay. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists, and even low prices failed to sell her paintings.

Their household deteriorated: love and happiness were eclipsed by looming poverty. They concealed worries from each other—Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears—clinging to the hope that love would sustain them. One night Ginevra woke to find Luigi gone and discovered him working late to avoid burdening her. Moved, she took up coloring lithographs, laboring through the night while Luigi toiled in his garret. Both pretended to sleep to spare the other their anxieties.

On another sleepless night, feverish and exhausted, Luigi opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. Confronted, she said she could not be idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment, watching the dawn together and feeling hopeful.

Their struggles deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. The joy of parenthood briefly masked their financial burdens, but soon they began selling possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride, escalating their desperation. Months later, frail and worn, Ginevra struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, feeling helpless, sought aid but found none; he sold Ginevra’s paintings and remaining furniture for a meager sum. Ginevra showed remarkable strength, managing the household and often starving herself to provide for Luigi.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread; realizing her sacrifice, he left to find help and decided to sell himself as a military substitute. After securing an advance, he hurried home, only to find Ginevra asleep with their child. When he roused her, Bartolomeo was cold and lifeless. In delirium Ginevra spoke her love and sorrow, then died in Luigi’s arms.

As Luigi mourned, Ginevra’s parents—who had not seen her in years—reflected on her absence. Elisa Piombo hoped to rekindle Bartolomeo’s love for Ginevra, but a messenger soon revealed the tragedy. The old couple was shattered; the cycle of suffering had claimed another victim, leaving only memories and grief.
