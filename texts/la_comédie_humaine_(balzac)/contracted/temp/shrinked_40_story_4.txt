In October 1800 Bartolomeo di Piombo, a distraught sixty-something, stood outside the Tuileries with his wife and daughter, handed his dagger to his wife, and declared he would confront the Bonapartes. A soldier barred him until Lucien Bonaparte recognized him and brought him to Napoleon. Bartolomeo sought asylum, confessing he had avenged his family by killing the Portas—the clan that burned his vineyard and murdered his son—admitting he had slain all seven Portas. Bitter at perceived Bonaparte indifference, he reminded them he had protected their family.

Napoleon, unwilling to condone private vengeance, acknowledged the Corsican vendetta tradition, offered lodging in Paris and financial aid, but warned that the city would not tolerate private justice. Bartolomeo accepted and pledged loyalty. Lucien found they had arrived with nothing, gave them money and promised help, noting their Corsican property could not sustain them in Paris. Fifteen years later, events would make Bartolomeo’s choices significant.

Servin, a respected artist of about forty, was the first to open a studio specifically for young girls who wished to paint. Morally strict and devoted to art, married to a general’s daughter who brought no dowry, he initially had mothers stay with their daughters but, reassured by his principles, they soon left. To avoid social disputes and because professional training required greater rigor, he limited pupils to young ladies from wealthy families and refused those intent on becoming professionals. His exacting method won a stellar reputation among the aristocracy: Servin became to feminine art what Herbault was to bonnets and Leroy to gowns, and any mother who suspected talent in her daughter recommended him.

The studio—an attic above his apartment reached by a narrow spiral stair and sealed from below—was arranged with care: a light-filled room with green-shaded panes, dark-gray walls hung with caricatures and plaster models, a blend of disciplined disorder and harmony.

On a bright July day the attic hummed with girls at their easels. Two cliques had formed: daughters of wealthy merchants and aristocrats led by the vain Amelie Thirion, and the rest. Among them sat Ginevra Piombo, modest and anxious. Her talent and beauty provoked the aristocrats’ jealousy; Amelie, intent on ostracizing her, shoved Ginevra’s easel amid whispers. The Bourbons’ second return had sharpened political divides: Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, drew royalist disdain.

A faint sound behind a partition revealed Servin was sheltering a fugitive officer of the Imperial Guard. To protect him, Ginevra chose proximity rather than risk exposure. When Madame Servin’s arrival hinted at the hidden man, Ginevra quietly hummed a Corsican song to mask his breathing while sketching his head with surprising fervor. Servin praised her likeness; the other girls crowded round, inflaming Amelie’s envy. The shared awareness of the fugitive forged a tacit bond: Ginevra chose to keep the secret and aid his escape. Outside, political turmoil loomed; inside, rivalries and friendships negotiated themselves around their art.

When Servin and Ginevra believed themselves alone, Servin admitted a young Imperial officer with his arm in a sling. He at first recoiled from Ginevra until Servin soothed him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo; on recognizing her evident patriotism the soldier relaxed.

Ginevra noticed his wound; he dismissed it, but cries from the street—“Condemned to death!”—made him go pale at the name “Labedoyere” and collapse. In despair he lamented that while Labedoyere faced death for their cause he had been hiding; stripped of his protector, he felt helpless and tormented by the thought that though ready to die he had not, convinced an honest life could not atone for traitors’ deeds. When he tried to leave, Ginevra stopped him and asked whether he truly believed he could restore the Emperor; moved by her calm conviction, he spoke of being ready to give his life.

Alarmed and compassionate, Ginevra offered her savings and noted her father’s wealth would excuse her aid; she urged patience, escape, and service elsewhere. Her maternal grace and hopeful insistence touched him—he admired her, calling her “an angel of goodness,” though his thoughts kept returning to Labedoyere. A tacit understanding formed. When Servin asked if the officer could save Labedoyere, he replied he could only avenge him; Ginevra felt a surge at the thought of vengeance and at his devotion to Napoleon.

As the sun set over his face, she felt a near-mystical connection and left smiling, newly hopeful. The next day the officer—now called Luigi—recounted the retreats from Moscow and Waterloo; speaking Corsican together and sharing memories deepened their intimacy as Ginevra listened with growing admiration.

Days passed. Her absence from gatherings sparked rumors; Mademoiselle Thirion withdrew and attendance dwindled. Laure, urged away by her mother, left saddened. One evening Luigi signaled but retreated on seeing Laure; later, when classmates had gone, Ginevra confronted Servin, who dismissed the gossip. Luigi joked he was to blame and Servin playfully suggested marriage; their silent exchange acknowledged mutual love, and Ginevra offered to secure a pardon with her father’s influence.

Her parents grew anxious at her late returns; Bartolomeo paced until she came home, then embraced her with relief. In the studio, amid hardship and scandal, Ginevra and Luigi’s love, forged by shared danger and ideals, became a sustaining strength that united them and embodied their cause’s resilience.

“Here she is, my Ginevra… mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old baron, embracing his daughter. Ginevra laughed and protested, relieving the moment; the baroness revived, Piombo smoothed his hands with courtly ease, and the parents seemed renewed.

At dinner Piombo teased Ginevra about her hours in the studio and hinted at jealousy of painting’s hold on her. The mood shifted when she, eyes downcast, confessed she loved a young man. Her parents were stunned: he was no prince but a poor comrade of Labedoyere, proscribed and hidden by Servin. Piombo, offended, declared she should love only her father; Ginevra answered that love was not a matter of will.

Piombo asserted his right to expect her fidelity; she replied that she had not reproached him for devotion to Napoleon during long absences and that she wanted a husband to protect her. Accusations ensued—his possessive pride versus her plea to choose—and she insisted she would not break her promise: she loved this man truly. The baroness tried to soften things, but Piombo could not accept obedience to what he called an unjust order.

The quarrel escalated: Piombo raged that if the man loved Ginevra he would kill him; if not, Piombo himself would. Ginevra maintained one cannot compare filial and romantic love and admitted the latter might be stronger. He thundered he would not allow the marriage; she calmly replied she would marry him. The baroness entreated peace. Shaken, Piombo asked to think. Ginevra implored him to love her for herself and promised never to abandon him. Their tenderness cooled his anger; he forgave, and they played as in earlier days.

In the following week Ginevra grew more affectionate, as if thanking her parents for an ungranted consent. She sang and suggested their nocturnes needed a man’s voice. At last the baroness signaled triumph: she had persuaded Piombo to receive the suitor. That same day Ginevra arrived home on Luigi’s arm.

Luigi, an officer who had reappeared after hiding, had been restored to the officer’s list through Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre. Warned of Piombo’s possible disapproval, Luigi was anxious; his pallor deepened Ginevra’s love. Piombo sat cold and stern. Ginevra presented Luigi as a soldier who had fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo inspected him and noted he wore no decoration; Luigi said he no longer bore the Legion of Honor.

The baroness, seeking to bridge the moment, remarked that Luigi resembled someone they knew in Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi answered that Nina had been his sister and, when pressed, confirmed he was Luigi Porta.

At that name Piombo reeled. He and the baroness left the room in stunned silence; Ginevra stood pale and speechless. The revelation plunged the household into dread. The baroness had once said the Portas had killed Ginevra’s brother and burned their house; Piombo had then massacred the Portas in revenge. Luigi, bewildered, asked how he had survived. He told how, bound to a bed before the house was set alight, he had been taken to Genoa at six and raised by an old man named Colonna, who warned him of enemies and advised he adopt the name Luigi to hide his identity. How he escaped death remained unclear.

Horrified at the vendetta revealed in a single word, Ginevra urged Luigi to stay for the moment—he was safe in her father’s house—but warned that once he left he would be hunted by Corsicans in Piombo’s service. Luigi, shaken, asked if the vendetta existed between them; she smiled sadly and nodded. Their love, she said, must be pure and resolute to endure what lay ahead. He squeezed her hand and smiled; they resolved their destiny was sealed. Ginevra escorted him to his modest lodging herself, determined rather than cowed.

Back home the atmosphere chilled. Piombo fumbled for words, rang for a servant, and ordered a fire kindled though his voice trembled. Both father and daughter nursed private torment their household could not bridge.

On Ginevra’s birthday, hoping to reconcile them, the baroness gathered father and daughter in Piombo’s study. At that moment two notaries arrived with witnesses to fulfill the law regarding Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta. Piombo bristled, furious at what he saw as an assault on paternal authority, demanding whether French laws could tear a daughter from her father. When the notary continued calmly, Piombo suddenly seized a dagger from the wall and rushed toward Ginevra. Witnesses lunged to restrain him; seeing him advance, Ginevra knelt before her father and begged mercy. The sight unnerved him; he flung the dagger away and, struck by shame, pushed her aside.

Then Piombo pronounced his cruelest judgment: “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here.” He pressed his hand to his heart and bade her leave his house. The baroness could not prevent him; he had renounced Ginevra publicly and irreversibly.

Ginevra fled to Luigi’s humble abode. She confessed the loss of parental blessing and their lack of fortune, but Luigi flung aside material concerns. “Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed, vowing to love her in her parents’ place. They resolved to face the long ordeal together. Thus remained the hard facts: Ginevra’s candid declaration of love; Piombo’s possessive claim, violent attempt, and final renunciation; Luigi’s return thanks to Ginevra; and the catastrophic discovery that Luigi was a Porta tied to the vendetta that had cost Ginevra’s brother and destroyed her family’s house, leaving her only Luigi as shelter.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house she took refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. Madame Servin received her coldly—her husband blamed Ginevra—and the woman’s selfishness left Ginevra proud but shaken. She rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a hopeful future that buoyed her spirits.

One morning trunks arrived from her mother with a loving, sorrowful letter pleading that she reconsider. For an instant Ginevra thought of returning home, but when Luigi appeared her resolve collapsed; she chose to bury her grief for the sake of their love.

On the wedding day Ginevra was essentially alone; Luigi brought ordinary men as witnesses, and passing joyful processions emphasized their contrast. In the mayor’s office the clerk noted her parents’ absence and the mayor learned they had protested, making the formalities feel oppressive and legal. An old priest’s remark about their parents stung, but a passing sergeant’s simple blessing comforted her.

They moved into a modest apartment Luigi called their true wedding, arranged with artistic supplies. Ginevra copied paintings and won clients; Luigi did likewise and hired assistants. Evenings were shared, occasional regrets surfaced, but an anniversary portrait she painted symbolized their contented year together.

At the beginning of 1819 picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta regretted not turning sooner to genre painting; Ginevra tried portraiture but faced stiff rivalry. Despite modest savings, Luigi and Ginevra grew anxious about their future.

That winter Luigi labored relentlessly in a crowded, low-paying writing market and could no longer afford help. Ginevra painted several commendable works, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists and low prices brought few sales.

Their household became dire; love remained, but poverty overshadowed happiness. Each concealed worries: Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears. One night Ginevra found Luigi working late to avoid burdening her; she began coloring lithographs, working into the night while he toiled in his garret. At dawn they pretended to sleep, each shielding the other.

Exhausted, Luigi later saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard and confronted her; she could not idle while he suffered. They shared a tender moment watching the dawn and felt tentative hope.

Their struggle deepened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Joy briefly masked their burdens, but soon they sold possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride. Months later, frail Ginevra tended a sickly child as Luigi, feeling helpless, sold her paintings and remaining furniture for a pittance. She often starved herself to feed him.

One evening she offered Luigi her last piece of bread; he left to seek aid and, desperate, sold himself as a military substitute for an advance. Returning with the money, he found Bartolomeo cold and lifeless. Ginevra, delirious, professed her love and died in his arms. As Luigi mourned, Ginevra’s parents were shattered when a messenger brought the fatal news—another victim of the cycle of suffering.
