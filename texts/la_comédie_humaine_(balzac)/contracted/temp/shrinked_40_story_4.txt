In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and daughter, burdened by hardship. An older man in tattered clothes, Bartolomeo's serious demeanor contrasted with his distressed wife, who tried to calm their weary daughter. Desperate, he handed a dagger to his wife, declaring his intention to confront the Bonapartes. A soldier stopped him at the palace, refusing entry without an audience request, leading to a tense standoff.

Lucien Bonaparte recognized Bartolomeo and took him to see Napoleon, who was meeting with Murat, Lannes, and Rapp. After dismissing the others, Napoleon asked Bartolomeo why he had come. Bartolomeo revealed he had killed the Portas, responsible for his son’s death and the destruction of his home. Surprised, Napoleon questioned Bartolomeo's motives, who expressed despair over the Bonaparte brothers' lack of support, feeling they had forgotten their Corsican roots. Napoleon explained he could not protect Bartolomeo but offered him a chance to live in Paris, away from vendetta culture. Bartolomeo accepted, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte family and expressing gratitude for their recognition of his plight. Napoleon promised financial assistance, emphasizing the importance of adhering to the law in Paris.

Fifteen years later, Bartolomeo's life in Paris set the stage for future events intertwined with themes of vengeance and familial loyalty. Meanwhile, Servin, a distinguished artist, established a studio for young girls interested in painting. At around forty, he was dedicated to his art and married to a general's daughter. Initially, mothers brought their daughters to the studio, but soon they felt comfortable sending them alone, reassured by Servin's integrity.

To maintain class composition, Servin only accepted pupils from wealthy families, refusing aspiring professional artists. His careful approach earned him a stellar reputation in feminine art education. The studio buzzed with activity as young girls painted and formed cliques based on social status. Among them was Ginevra Piombo, the most beautiful and modest girl, who faced ostracism from royalist peers influenced by the political climate.

Ginevra's indifference to slights fueled animosity, particularly from Amelie, who sought to undermine her. When Ginevra sensed a mysterious presence behind a partition—a hidden man—her curiosity grew. Despite Amelie's attempts to humiliate her, Ginevra remained focused on her work. Madame Servin's arrival interrupted the brewing conflict, and Ginevra exchanged glances with her, both aware of the hidden man.

As the session continued, Ginevra sketched the officer's head, drawing praise from Servin, which heightened tension among the girls. Ginevra and Servin later discussed the hidden officer, revealing the political stakes involved. Ginevra offered her assistance, demonstrating her commitment to protecting the man and her loyalty to Servin. The dynamics in the studio shifted as the girls became increasingly aware of Ginevra's focus and the mystery surrounding her, while Amelie, driven by jealousy, attempted to spy on her.

In a dim garret, painter Servin and Ginevra were surprised by a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling. He recoiled upon seeing Ginevra, but Servin introduced her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, Baron di Piombo. The officer, Luigi, was shaken by Labedoyere's death sentence and expressed despair over his cowardice for hiding during the execution. Ginevra offered her savings, inspiring hope in him, and they quickly formed a deep connection.

The next day, Luigi shared his harrowing experiences from the retreat from Moscow and Waterloo, captivating Ginevra. Their friendship grew, and they devised a secret communication system through Corsican songs. However, rumors about their relationship began to circulate among Ginevra’s classmates, leading to their departure from the studio. Ginevra remained focused on her feelings for Luigi, while he refused to submit to royal authority, staying loyal to his beliefs.

One evening, Ginevra learned from her friend Laure that the other girls had left due to gossip. Shocked, she confided in Servin, who dismissed the rumors, valuing their bond. Luigi, overhearing, felt guilty for the trouble he caused. Their unspoken love became evident, and Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon through her father’s influence. Meanwhile, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious over her late return home. Her father, Bartolomeo di Piombo, was a stern yet loving figure known for his integrity. This chapter explores themes of loyalty, love, and societal judgment through the relationships between Ginevra, Luigi, and their families.

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, embracing his daughter. He noted her long hours at the studio, suggesting painting had become more important than family. Ginevra shocked her parents by confessing, “I love a young man,” revealing he was a comrade of Labedoyere, who had been proscribed. Piombo insisted she should love no one but him, but Ginevra countered that love was not a choice.

Piombo lamented her lack of faithfulness, but Ginevra reminded him of his own absences for political missions. As Piombo's anger flared at the thought of losing her, Ginevra pleaded for understanding, promising her love would not diminish her affection for him. “You will learn to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she insisted.

Piombo declared he would never allow the marriage, but Ginevra stood firm, stating, “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta.” Piombo's fury intensified as he warned her of the consequences of marrying a Porta, the family responsible for her brother’s death. Ginevra argued that Luigi was innocent, having been just a child during the vendetta.

Despite her father’s threats, Ginevra remained resolute. “I will be his wife,” she asserted, leading to a confrontation where Piombo nearly attacked her but ultimately hesitated. The next day, Ginevra found herself barred from leaving the house, communicating with Luigi through a chambermaid, deepening their love despite the rift with her father.

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother attempted to mediate, but the arrival of two notaries to discuss her marriage escalated tensions. Ginevra knelt before her father, pleading for his consent. “Give me Luigi, or I die!” she cried, but Piombo, overwhelmed, rejected her. “You have no father,” he declared, casting her out.

Ginevra entered Luigi’s modest lodging, declaring, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” Luigi promised to love her in place of her parents, and Ginevra resolved to find happiness with him. “Then let us be happy,” she said, her spirit unbroken.

The day after her expulsion, Ginevra sought refuge with Madame Servin, who coldly refused to help. Hurt yet proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a hopeful future. One morning, Ginevra received trunks filled with essentials from her mother, including a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived, and her resolve to abandon him faded.

On their wedding day, Ginevra had no friends present, and the atmosphere was grave. After a brief, cold ceremony, they left feeling the weight of their families' absence. Outside, Luigi expressed gratitude, and Ginevra felt relief despite the challenges ahead.

Inside their new home, Ginevra was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements Luigi had made, including a studio for her art. Their initial days were filled with passion, but reality soon set in, and they needed to work to sustain themselves. Ginevra began copying paintings, gaining clients, while Luigi established a successful business using his handwriting.

This period marked their happiest time, filled with love and shared labor. They enjoyed evenings of music and laughter, with no regrets about their choices. Yet, Ginevra occasionally felt sorrow for her parents, imagining their loneliness. To commemorate their anniversary, she painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, symbolizing their happiness and commitment.

In early 1819, Ginevra's picture-dealers encouraged her to create original works due to increasing competition. Despite some savings, Ginevra and Luigi's future remained uncertain. As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly, facing declining writing fees that forced him to work longer hours. Ginevra produced several commendable paintings, but the market was unkind, leaving her efforts largely unsold.

Their financial situation worsened, yet they masked their anxieties with love, fearing poverty would strip away their happiness. One night, Ginevra found Luigi working late and, feeling guilty, resolved to help by coloring lithographs. They both pretended to sleep, only to rise again to work, driven by love and desperation.

Their situation worsened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Initially joyful, they soon faced financial strain, selling possessions and jewels. Months later, Ginevra, now frail, struggled to care for her sickly child while Luigi sold her remaining paintings for meager sums.

One evening, Ginevra saved a piece of bread for Luigi, who was horrified to realize she was starving. In despair, he decided to sell himself as a military substitute for money. Rushing home, he found Ginevra in despair, holding their lifeless child. Despite his pleas, Ginevra succumbed to exhaustion and died in his arms, leaving him devastated.

Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents were haunted by memories of their daughter. When Luigi appeared at their door, broken and revealing Ginevra’s fate, it shattered them, leading to profound grief over their loss and estrangement.
