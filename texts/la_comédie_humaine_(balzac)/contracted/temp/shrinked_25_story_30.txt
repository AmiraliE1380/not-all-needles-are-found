To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,—You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to you a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827 a sixteen-year-old boy in modest clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins, taking in working mills and simple bourgeois houses framed by linden trees. He recognized the house he sought: a recently restored white-stone dwelling with gray blinds and yellow iron balconies, its neatness betraying the pride of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As the sun lit a calico curtain in a garret window, the boy began a Breton ditty for newlyweds, a song that stirred memories of Brittany and life’s bittersweet turns. A girl, Pierrette, signaled from the window; he offered a yellow broom-flower. He was Brigaut, her childhood companion, and he spoke of settling nearby. Pierrette, alarmed, urged him to flee when a grotesque old maid below—sharp-eyed and ready to imagine scandal—appeared at a window.

Overwhelmed by Brigaut’s visit, Pierrette reflected on her past. Seated in a shabby armchair, frail and thoughtful, she recalled childhood joys now tinged by illness and uncertainty. Her mother, born a Demoiselle Auffray, came from a tangled family—her father had married twice and left a complicated inheritance. Her mother later married Major Lorrain, a Breton officer who died in battle, leaving the family impoverished; the Lorrains could not care for Pierrette.

Ultimately she was sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins. Unaware of the full intricacies of her lineage and the consequences of her parents’ fortunes and losses, Pierrette finds her fate increasingly shaped by these relatives. The Rogrons’ control sets the stage for the drama to follow.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Père Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, was a ruddy-faced, indulgent short man. He and his unattractive, selfish wife lived roughly on modest proceeds from their land and inn, then moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, were neglected and ill-bred, their features coarsened and voices harsh. Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops with little support.

Sylvie apprenticed on rue Saint-Denis and became a forewoman; Jérôme rose to head-clerk. They met rarely and pooled savings to buy the retail “Family Sister.” They had some success, but competition and severity made them notorious.

Sylvie, shrewd though plain, ran the business effectively; Jérôme was unsuited to broader trade. Their lives revolved around the shop, leaving them oblivious beyond it. Their stinginess and poor treatment drove high clerk turnover. Sylvie was a pleasant saleswoman to customers and a sour spinster at home.

While mourning their father, they received a letter about cousin Pierrette’s inheritance. They considered selling and returning to Provins but refused to take Pierrette in to protect their wealth, delayed replying, and left for Provins four years before Brigaut arrived.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A petty shopkeeper returns from Paris to his provincial town with a few Parisian airs that soon fade as provincial life reasserts itself. The transition to the bourgeois feels like an illness: Paris’s vibrancy yields to local stillness. With newfound wealth, the Rogrons—brother and sister—indulge desires like travel, politics, and hunting, and remodel their house to mimic wealthier families.

Their renovations advertise ambition; the contractor even consults them on elite details. Their first year is consumed by talk of the house and efforts to join the town’s social circle, headed by the established Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées. Madame Tiphaine, the local belle, advances her husband toward deputyship by hosting influential gatherings. The Rogrons lack polish: Sylvie studies card games while Rogron stays awkward and silent.

Lavish dinners fail to win acceptance; invitations dwindle and ridicule spreads. Sylvie’s faith that money guarantees status collapses; she goes frugal, abandons entertainments, and the siblings sink into ennui, nostalgic for business days and resentful of isolation. Bringing in cousin Pierrette Lorrain backfires as neglect becomes public. Bitter and sidelined, the Rogrons court the lower bourgeoisie—figures like Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet who exploit their wealth and naiveté. The story shows provincial hierarchies and subtle social codes trump money.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette—ashamed to live on charity—felt relieved when wealthy relatives were found. Brigaut, a childhood friend and her mother’s friend’s son, gave her sixty francs for the trip to Paris; she accepted without thanks, a sign of their close bond. He often visited to console her and ran after the coach in tears when she left. Penniless in Paris, a conductor briefly paid for her hotel and treated her like a package; four days later a kinder conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with a small bundle, dressed in Breton clothes. Sylvie and Rogron were startled and questioned her, but Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, spoke little. Sylvie and the maid Adèle showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette, and Sylvie immediately fretted over her lack of clothing, resolving to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found coldness and criticism. Exploring the house and garden the next morning, she was reproved for dirty shoes. Her cousins’ concern for possessions over her well‑being and their constant fault‑finding sharply contrasted with the love she’d known. Pierrette’s innocence made their disdain more painful; she began to suppress her joy and became passive for fear of scorn.

The Rogrons obsessed over her appearance, treating her as a burden and stifling her spirit. When she tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, expecting stern rebukes. Life in Provins worsened as Sylvie forbade visits to friends and pursued social ambitions encouraged by Vinet and the colonel. As her cousins plotted for status, Pierrette was increasingly confined—transformed from a lively child into a shadow of her former self, enduring harsh, self‑interested care that was not love.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the start of a new phase with her wealthy cousins, Pierrette went to say goodnight and Sylvie turned away coldly, making her cry. When asked why, Pierrette said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother; Sylvie retorted the grandmother had taken money while the cousin would leave her some—Pierrette wanted affection, not wealth.

Sylvie then accused Pierrette of mischief—breaking a watch, disobeying orders—and Pierrette left ashamed. Rogron demanded punishment, Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school, and the colonel thought instruction might keep her quiet. Vinet called Pierrette a rough diamond needing polishing and a writing master was hired. His lessons produced ink stains and scattered papers, blamed on Pierrette, and she was sharply told she must earn her keep. Rogron’s severity and Sylvie’s indifference deepened her isolation; her curiosity and spirit were stifled and she began to feel ill, complaining of pains the cousins dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; the colonel and Vinet attributed Pierrette’s behavior to a stubborn Breton nature. Meanwhile Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper to boost their standing, while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

She considered escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian prevented it. Vinet hinted at an inheritance; Rogron secured from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs upon the old woman’s death. Preparations for Pierrette’s first communion shifted household dynamics. Sylvie insisted on the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, who profoundly influenced Pierrette. Finding solace in faith, she abandoned escape plans and embraced the Church’s teachings, a change that even made Sylvie adopt new piety.

Habert’s sister offered to take Pierrette to her school, but the Rogrons refused, fearing a convent education. Later Vinet introduced the Chargeboeufs; Bathilde attracted Rogron, and Sylvie forced Pierrette into domestic tasks. Without Adèle’s protection she was treated worse than staff—scolded for trifles, denied praise—and over the years her health and spirit faded into a silent shadow while her cousins remained oblivious.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role in the events.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing change; her once-vibrant spirit had become pale and sorrowful. From a high point in the Upper town he watched her home, consumed by questions about her welfare and the depth of his feelings. Their rare, deep bond led him to try to improve his prospects: he briefly went to Paris to learn a trade, then returned to Provins and found work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette.

He buried his feelings while learning more about the Rogrons. Madame Frappier revealed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. Brigaut often saw Pierrette at the market, where her frailty distressed him. Sundays at church brought them near, though she signaled him to remain hidden, sensing danger. Determined to discover the cause of her pallor, he feared for her health.

Pierrette, meanwhile, fretted about changes since Brigaut’s return but was unaware of the Rogrons’ internal strife, especially between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for advantageous marriage. Sylvie, forty-two, feared growing old alone; Céleste, ten years younger and encouraged by her brother the priest, actively sought a husband. A recent political shift emboldened local Liberals and intensified competition for social standing in Provins.

Sylvie’s anxiety drove her to consult a doctor about marrying at her age; his discouraging prognosis heightened her fear. Céleste, seeing an opening, urged delay to remove a rival. Sylvie’s unease turned to jealousy when she noticed the colonel’s increasing attention to Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, exploited Sylvie’s vulnerability: he plotted to separate the colonel from Pierrette and steer the Rogron fortune toward Bathilde.

Vinet persuaded the colonel to court Pierrette, painting a picture of happiness while secretly maneuvering to benefit Bathilde. The colonel, a mix of affection and reserve, began seeking Pierrette more, which inflamed Sylvie’s fears. Her suspicions sharpened after overhearing Brigaut serenade Pierrette; she concluded the colonel was involved. On a tense morning she accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer, mocked her innocence, and demanded answers. Pierrette, frightened and bewildered, stayed silent, which intensified Sylvie’s wrath and threats.

As Pierrette endured the oppressive Rogron household, her health continued to decline. Her innocence sheltered her from some realities, but emotional strain and heavy labor eroded her strength. Brigaut’s presence offered brief comfort, yet Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny deepened Pierrette’s isolation. Amid this turmoil, love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for a tragic sequence that would affect everyone involved.

################################################
For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, so Gouraud came himself and lingered to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see him, Sylvie dressed in her best; when Gouraud arrived Rogron excused himself, leaving them alone.

Noting her solemnity, Gouraud asked after her cousin; she replied the cousin was unwell. He grew uneasy: Vinet’s rising influence with the Rogrons made him feel sidelined. A half-pay officer, he feared Vinet’s control of their political plans and Rogron’s infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which threatened his prospects. He even suspected Vinet might be manoeuvring to steer him toward Pierrette as a ploy to favor Vinet and Bathilde.

Alone with Sylvie he sensed her unease; when she tentatively mentioned Pierrette he dismissed any affection for the girl, saying he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and hinted her brother had once considered Pierrette a match. Sensing her interest, Gouraud declared he would marry Sylvie, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their home but remained wary of Pierrette’s interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared Sylvie scolded Pierrette for a stain yet ordered her to dress nicely—suspecting collusion. The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde, spectacularly dressed under Vinet’s guidance, treated marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, using beauty and connections for independence.

The evening grew tense: Bathilde’s condescension and Vinet’s remarks sharpened rivalries. At cards Pierrette’s innocent comments provoked Sylvie, and Pierrette left in tears. That night Sylvie brooded on men’s duplicity and her precarious position. The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall; Sylvie blamed disobedience and showed no sympathy, revealing a widening rift between the cousins.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended in relentless torment for Pierrette under Sylvie’s escalating cruelty. She concealed Brigaut’s arrival to avoid anger, but her longing and fear for him endured. Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged a nightly note exchange: one morning he slipped a message to her while pretending to buy fruit and asked her to lower a string from her window.

Pierrette returned with his heartfelt letter, full of longing and plans to escape. Despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie, she resolved to reply, pouring out her love and describing her relatives’ abuse. At midnight she sent the letter down; their correspondence continued, hopeful and intimate, while Sylvie’s jealousy and vigilance grew.

Under Sylvie’s scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined; one evening she fainted, drawing concern from the colonel and others, though Sylvie dismissed it as a ruse. Desperate, Pierrette cried for help, provoking a violent confrontation when Sylvie tried to seize her letters. In the struggle Pierrette was injured, including a hurt hand, and left distressed.

At the height of Sylvie’s fury Madame Lorrain, Pierrette’s grandmother, arrived after Brigaut’s urgent appeals to come to Provins. She witnessed the violence, rushed to Pierrette’s aid, and sought medical care. As Pierrette recovered, her bond with Madame Lorrain deepened; the promise of protection and a future with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a hopeful glimmer. Pierrette remained determined to reclaim her life.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine o’clock Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about Pierrette’s mistreatment by the Rogrons. Tiphaine brought in Auffray, a notary and relative, and long‑standing enmities between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions—inflamed by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family—gave the dispute political force.

Seeing an opportunity to weaken the influential royalist Rogrons, Tiphaine consulted the public prosecutor. He, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts to make Auffray Pierrette’s guardian so a Family Council, backed by medical testimony, could remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries and rumors spread. Doctors Bianchon and Brigaut examined her and confirmed abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, warned of criminal exposure; Sylvie, initially evasive, later admitted jealousy and the truth. Exploiting fear, Vinet proposed a marriage announcement to quiet the scandal. Under pressure the Rogrons signed a marriage contract that furthered their political aims. Vinet then filed a petition accusing Brigaut and the widow Lorrain of abducting Pierrette, attempting to recast his clients as victims.

The hearing was postponed, heightening interest. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who, unaware of the turmoil, was cared for by her grandmother and Brigaut. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

When the court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report to justify protecting Pierrette. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered she be kept from the Rogrons until the Family Council could meet—a public rebuke. The Council of relatives and allies voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray. Despite Vinet’s defense, the evidence led to a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health declined. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; her grandmother’s care and Brigaut’s visits comforted her. A critical operation in March 1828 offered little hope; she forgave her cousins and soon died. Her funeral filled the town; the Rogrons, fearing outrage, stayed away. Brigaut made her coffin. Though community support hampered the Rogrons’ maneuvers and Vinet faced backlash, the Rogrons and Vinet largely retained political standing; only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and like‑minded friends—remembered the reality of Pierrette’s suffering. The account closes on justice, accountability, and the lasting impact of her life and death.
