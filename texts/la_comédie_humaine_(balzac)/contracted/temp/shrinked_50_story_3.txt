At a particular twilight that inspires reflection, a young painter finishing a large canvas falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness. He is awakened by a beautiful young woman and her marquise-like mother, who tend him with gentle, reserved care and withdraw without seeking acquaintance. The porter’s wife later identifies them as Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, long-time residents of the building.

The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, has just moved into the attic studio and is gaining recognition, though he keeps his affairs private to secure a better life for his own mother. During convalescence he replays the encounter, especially the girl’s image, and soon visits them, feeling a kinship born of imagined shared misfortune.

Their modest home shows poverty but also an elegant bearing: worn finery kept meticulously clean and traces of past luxury. After awkward conversation turns to art, Madame Leseigneur reveals a decaying portrait of her late husband, a naval officer; Hippolyte offers to repaint it, hoping to deepen their acquaintance. Adelaide returns with candles to brighten the room, and a visitor arrives—an older, aristocratic-looking gentleman whose presence stirs Hippolyte’s jealousy and hints at complex social histories. He leaves torn between admiration and the fragile boundary between their worlds.

A remarkable, lean, aged man stood among the ragged remnants of the Monarchy—“Louis XIV’s light infantry”—a faded echo of his former self: worn coat, powdered hair dulled, intellect diminished. Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz seemed apt. He puzzled the painter Chevalier; no one addressed him. His relation to the gallant man nearby was unclear, and his presence hinted at a possible past link to the gallant’s deceased wife without answering anything.

One of the least dilapidated men kissed Madame de Rouville’s hand and sat beside her; another bowed and took a place nearby. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a pose recalling a famous painting, though his familiarity irritated her. “Are you sulky with me?” he asked, glancing shrewdly at the painter Schinner.

Madame de Rouville introduced Hippolyte, a celebrated painter. The old man noticed Hippolyte’s red ribbon and contrasted it with his own honors. Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, was briefly blind to the squalor around them; the old gentleman lamented his losing streak at cards. Adelaide offered help; he insisted she stay where he could see her. When the game ended, the gentleman complained of losses and the silent figure only echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to take his leave, already smitten.

The next day Hippolyte returned for the Baron’s portrait Adelaide sent him to fetch, their exchange charged with modesty and promise. Over the following days their relationship deepened—laughter, shared thoughts, and quiet mutual trust. Schinner learned piquet and often lost; Hippolyte grew jealous when the old Count showed familiarity with Adelaide. Within two months Hippolyte visited daily, the old gentleman’s visits faded, and Hippolyte took his place at the card table.

Worried about Madame de Rouville’s finances, Hippolyte had spent two months on the Baron’s portrait and puzzled over the Baroness’s silence about payment. When Adelaide visited his studio, awed by the work, Hippolyte declared his desire to marry her. That evening, after a night of turmoil, his purse was missing and he suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft. Ashamed by her pallor and sadness when confronted, his doubt began to waver. During a tender card game Adelaide quietly placed a new purse before him; overwhelmed, he vowed to marry her. The old vice-admiral soon returned, congratulated the portrait, announced a promotion, and offered financial support, dispelling remaining doubts. The story follows these characters through love, mistrust, and reconciliation toward renewed unity.
