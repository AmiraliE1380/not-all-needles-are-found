DEDICATION

To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,--If fortune allows these lines to endure, they will repay a little for the trouble you have taken—you, the Hozier, the Cherin, the King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life; you to whom the Navarreins, Cadignans, Langeais, Blamont-Chauvrys, Chaulieus, Arthez, Esgrignons, Mortsaufs, Valois—the great names of the “Human Comedy”—owe their mottoes and bearings. The Armorial of the Etudes, devised by Ferdinand de Gramont, is a complete manual of French Heraldry, forgetting nothing, even the arms of the Empire, and I shall preserve it as a monument of friendship and patience. What knowledge of the feudal spirit is shown in the Beauseants’ Pulchre sedens, melius agens; the Espards’ Des partem leonis; the Vandenesses’ Ne se vend. Your poetic touch brings elegance and accuracy to countless learned details.

Your old friend,
DE BALZAC.

THE MUSE OF THE DEPARTMENT

On the skirts of Le Berry stands a town on the Loire that attracts the traveler’s eye. Sancerre crowns the highest hill of a chain ending the Nivernais range. The Loire floods the flats at the slopes’ feet, leaving fertile yellow alluvium except where sand from its violent risings has spoiled the land.

Because the town’s hill lies well above the river, the little port Saint-Thibault handles Sancerre’s traffic: wine shipments and oak staves, produce from the upper and lower Loire. By the time this story begins, suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault had been built, so travelers from Paris by the southern road no longer needed ferries. This marks that the great cross-shuffle of 1830 was past; the House of Orleans had long favored substantial improvements, though somewhat like a husband spending his wife’s marriage portion.

Except on a small plateau, the streets are steep; the town is girdled by slopes called the Great Ramparts, the place’s highroads. Outside them lies a belt of vineyards. Wine is the chief industry, producing aromatic vintages close to Burgundy’s in character, deceiving the common palate. Sancerre finds in Paris wine-shops the quick market necessary for wines that will not keep more than seven or eight years. Below the town are a few villages—Fontenoy and Saint-Satur—almost suburbs, recalling the smiling vineyards about Neuchâtel in Switzerland.

The town retains much of its ancient look: narrow pebble-paved streets and some old houses remain. The citadel, a relic of feudal power, endured one of the most terrible sieges of our religious wars, when French Calvinists surpassed even the ferocity of Walter Scott’s Cameronians.

Richer in a greater past but divested of military importance, Sancerre is destined for a less glorious future, since trade favors the Loire’s right bank. The sketch suggests it will grow lonelier despite two bridges to Cosne.

Sancerre, the pride of the left bank, numbers at most three thousand five hundred inhabitants, while Cosne now exceeds six thousand. Within half a century the roles of these opposite towns have reversed. The advantage of situation still belongs to the historic town: the view is enchanting, the air pure, vegetation splendid, and the inhabitants—friendly and in harmony with nature—are not Puritans, though two-thirds are Calvinists. Under these conditions, despite small-town inconveniences and an officious gaze that makes private life almost public, a local spirit—a kind of patriotism, though not a substitute for love of home—flourishes.

Sancerre prides itself on producing Horace Bianchon, a glory of modern medicine, and Etienne Lousteau, an author of secondary rank and one of our most successful journalists. The district, however, suffers from domination by seven or eight large landowners—the wire-pullers of elections. A small conspiracy to shake off the electoral yoke imposed by a creed that had reduced it to a rotten borough was attempted by a handful whose vanity was provoked, but it failed: jealousy of one man’s inevitable elevation roused the others. This revealed the scheme’s fatal defect; the proposed remedy was to rally round a champion at the next election—one of the two men who had so gloriously represented Sancerre in Paris.

This idea was advanced for provincial politics; since 1830 the nomination of parochial dignitaries has so increased that genuine statesmen have become rare in the lower chamber. In fact, the plan was hatched by the Superior Woman of the borough, dux feminae fasti, from personal interest. It was so rooted in her past and crucial to her future prospects that it can scarcely be explained without a sketch of her antecedent career.

Sancerre at that time could boast a Superior Woman, long misprized but now, about 1836, enjoying an extensive local reputation. This was also the period when two Sancerrois in Paris were attaining distinction and fashion: Etienne Lousteau wrote reviews for a paper with eight thousand subscribers; and Bianchon, already chief physician to a hospital, Officer of the Legion of Honor, and member of the Academy of Sciences, had just been made a professor.

If the word did not to many readers imply blame, one might say George Sand created “Sandism,” so true is it that morally every good has a reverse. This leprosy of sentimentality might have been charming. Still, Sandism has a good side: the woman it attacks bases her superiority on scorned feelings; she is a blue-stocking of sentiment and somewhat less of a bore, love partly neutralizing literature. The chief result of George Sand’s celebrity was to reveal that France has an enormous number of superior women who, until now, generously left the field to the Maréchal de Saxe’s granddaughter.

The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town- and country-house within ten minutes of the town, in the village or suburb of Saint-Satur. The present La Baudrayes, as often happens, have thrust themselves in and substituted for those La Baudrayes whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in Le Berry’s history.

The story must be told.

In the time of Louis XIV a certain sheriff named Milaud, whose forefathers had been fervent Calvinists, was converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. To encourage conversions in a Calvinist stronghold, the King gave Milaud an appointment in the “Waters and Forests,” granted him arms and the title Sire de la Baudraye, with the fief of the old La Baudrayes. The descendants of the famous Captain la Baudraye later fell into one of the snares for heretics in the new decrees and were hanged—an unworthy deed of the great King.

Under Louis XV Milaud de la Baudraye rose from squire to Chevalier and had influence enough to obtain for his son a cornet’s commission in the Musketeers. This officer perished at Fontenoy, leaving a child to whom Louis XVI subsequently granted, by patent, the privileges of a farmer-general in remembrance of his father’s death.

This financier, a fashionable wit great at charades, capping verses and posies to Chloris, lived in society, clung to the Duc de Nivernais, and fancied himself obliged to follow the nobility into exile; but he took care to carry his money with him. Thus the rich émigré was able to assist more than one family of high rank.

In 1800, tired of hoping and perhaps of lending, he returned to Sancerre, bought back La Baudraye out of vanity and imaginary pride—quite intelligible in a sheriff’s grandson—though under the Consulate his prospects were slender; all the more so because the ex-farmer-general had small hopes that his heir would perpetuate the new race of La Baudraye.

Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, his only son, delicate from birth, was plainly the child of a man whose constitution had been exhausted by excesses and late marriage, bringing degeneracy into high society. During the emigration Madame de la Baudraye, a girl of no fortune chosen for her birth, patiently reared this sallow, sickly boy with the devoted love mothers show such changelings. Her death—she was a Casteran de la Tour—contributed to Monsieur de la Baudraye’s return to France.

This Lucullus of the Milauds, when he died, left his son the fief, stripped indeed of its fines and dues, but graced with weathercocks bearing his coat-of-arms, a thousand louis-d’or (in 1802 a considerable sum), and certain receipts for claims on very distinguished émigrés enclosed in a pocketbook full of verses, the wrapper inscribed Vanitas vanitatum et omnia vanitas.

Young La Baudraye did not die; he owed his life to habits of monastic strictness, to the economy of action Fontenelle preached as the religion of the invalid, and, above all, to the air of Sancerre and the influence of its fine elevation, whence a panorama over the Loire valley extends for forty leagues.

From 1802 to 1815 young La Baudraye added several plots to his vineyards and devoted himself to the culture of the vine. The Restoration at first seemed so insecure that he dared not go to Paris to claim his debts; but after Napoleon’s death he tried to turn his father’s collection of autographs into money, though he did not understand the philosophy that mixed IOUs and copies of verses. But the winegrower wasted time trying to impress his identity on the Duke of Nivernais “and others,” and returned to Sancerre to his vintage without having obtained anything but offers of service.

The Restoration raised the nobility to such lustre that La Baudraye wished to justify his ambitions by having an heir. This result of matrimony he considered doubtful, or he would not have so long postponed the step; however, finding himself still above ground in 1823 at forty-three—a length of years which no doctor, astrologer, or midwife would have dared promise—he hoped to earn the reward of his sober life. Yet his choice showed such lack of prudence regarding his frail constitution that the malicious wit of a country town suspected some deep calculation.

At that time Monseigneur the Archbishop of Bourges had converted to Catholicism a young woman, the daughter of one of the citizen families that had upheld Calvinism and, thanks to obscurity or compromise, escaped persecution under Louis XIV. The Piedefers—whose name suggested the champions of the Reformation—had become respectable cloth merchants. In Louis XVI’s reign Abraham Piedefer fell into difficulties and at his death in 1786 left his two children in extreme poverty. One, Tobie Piedefer, went to the Indies, leaving the meagre inheritance to his elder brother. During the Revolution Moise Piedefer bought nationalized land, pulled down abbeys and churches with ancestral zeal, and married a Catholic, the only daughter of a member of the Convention who had perished on the scaffold. This ambitious Piedefer died in 1819, leaving a little girl of remarkable beauty. Brought up in the Calvinist faith, she was named Dinah, following the sect’s custom of choosing Biblical rather than Roman Catholic saint-names.

Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer was placed by her mother in the Demoiselles Chamarolles’ school in Bourges and was soon distinguished for mind as well as beauty; but she was snubbed by girls of birth and fortune destined to play greater parts in the world than a plebeian’s daughter dependent on the settlement of the Piedefer estate. Having raised herself above her companions, Dinah aimed to remain on a level with them for life. She determined, therefore, to renounce Calvinism in the hope that the Cardinal would favor his proselyte and interest himself in her prospects. You may judge of Mademoiselle Dinah’s superiority, since at seventeen she converted solely from ambition.

The Archbishop, convinced Dinah would adorn society, was anxious to see her married. But every family to whom he made advances recoiled from a damsel with the looks of a princess, reputed the cleverest of Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ pupils and who always shone at the prize-giving. A thousand crowns a year, the share she might hope from La Hautoy when divided between mother and daughter, would be trifling compared to the expenses a husband would incur by the personal advantages of so brilliant a creature.

As soon as these facts reached little Polydore de la Baudraye—they were the talk of every circle in the Cher—he went to Bourges just when Madame Piedefer, a devotee at high services, had almost made up her mind and her daughter’s to take the first comer with well-lined pockets—the first chien coiffé, as they say in Le Berry. If the Cardinal was delighted to receive Monsieur de la Baudraye, Monsieur de la Baudraye was even more pleased to receive a wife from the Cardinal’s hands. The little gentleman only demanded a formal promise that His Eminence would support his claims with the President of the Council to enable him to recover his debts from the Duc de Navarreins “and others” by a lien on their indemnities. This method, however, seemed to the Minister then occupying the Pavillon Marsan too sharp, and he gave the vine-owner to understand his business would be attended to in good time.

It is easy to imagine the excitement produced in the Sancerre district by news of Monsieur de la Baudraye’s imprudent marriage.

“It is quite intelligible,” said President Boirouge; “the little man was very much startled, I am told, at hearing handsome young Milaud, the Attorney-General’s deputy at Nevers, say to Monsieur de Clagny as they looked at La Baudraye’s turrets, ‘That will be mine some day.’—‘But,’ says Clagny, ‘he may marry and have children.’—‘Impossible!’—So you may imagine how such a changeling as little La Baudraye must hate that colossal Milaud.”

At Nevers there was a plebeian branch of the Milauds, grown rich in the cutlery trade, that had brought its representative up to the civil service under Marchangy, now dead.

It will be useful to set aside the baser material interests that occupied Monsieur de la Baudraye, by briefly relating his negotiations in Paris. This will also shed light on certain mysterious contemporary political phenomena that hampered the Ministry during the Restoration.

The Ministers’ promises were so illusory that Monsieur de la Baudraye decided to go to Paris when the Cardinal’s presence was required there by the Chambers’ sitting.

This is how the Duc de Navarreins, the principal debtor threatened by Monsieur de la Baudraye, got out of the scrape.

The country gentleman, lodging at the Hotel de Mayence, Rue Saint-Honoré near the Place Vendôme, one morning received a visit from a confidential agent of the Ministry, an expert in “winding up” business. This elegant person, stepping from an elegant cab and neatly dressed, was shown to the third floor, to a small room where he found his provincial concocting coffee over a bedroom fire.

“Is it to Monsieur Milaud de la Baudraye that I have the honor—”

“Yes,” said the little man, draping himself in his dressing-gown.

After examining the garment—an illicit offspring of an old china wrapper of Madame Piedefer’s and a gown of the late Madame de la Baudraye—the emissary considered the man, the dressing-gown, and the tin saucepan on the little stove so characteristic that he deemed it needless to beat about the bush.

“I will lay a wager, monsieur,” said he audaciously, “that you dine for forty sous at Hurbain’s in the Palais-Royal.”

“Pray, why?”

“Oh, I know you, having seen you there,” replied the Parisian gravely. “All the princes’ creditors dine there. You know you recover scarcely ten per cent on debts from these fine gentlemen. I would not give you five per cent on a debt to be recovered from the estate of the late Duc d’Orléans—nor even,” he added in a low voice, “from MONSIEUR.”

“So you have come to buy up the bills?” said La Baudraye, thinking himself clever.

“Buy them!” said his visitor. “Why, what do you take me for? I am Monsieur des Lupeaulx, Master of Appeals, Secretary-General to the Ministry, and I have come to propose an arrangement.”

“What is that?”

“Of course, monsieur, you know the position of your debtor—”

“Of my debtors—”

“Well, monsieur, you understand their position; they stand high in the King’s good graces but have no money and must make a good show. Again, you know the political difficulties. The aristocracy must be rehabilitated against a strong third estate. The King’s idea—of which France does him scant justice—is to create a peerage as a national institution analogous to the English peerage. To realize this we need years—and millions. Noblesse oblige. The Duc de Navarreins, who is, as you know, first gentleman of the Bedchamber to the King, does not repudiate his debt, but he cannot—Now, be reasonable—consider politics. We are emerging from the pit of the Revolution—and you yourself are noble—He simply cannot pay—”

“Monsieur—”

“You are hasty,” said des Lupeaulx. “Listen. He cannot pay in money. Well then; you, a clever man, can take payment in favors—royal or ministerial.”

“What! When in 1793 my father put down one hundred thousand—”

“My dear sir, recrimination is useless. Listen to a simple statement in political arithmetic: the collectorship at Sancerre is vacant; a certain paymaster-general of the forces has a claim on it, but he has no chance of getting it; you have the chance—and no claim. You will get the place. You will hold it for three months, then resign, and Monsieur Gravier will give twenty thousand francs for it. In addition, the Order of the Legion of Honor will be conferred on you.”

“Well, that is something,” said the wine-grower, tempted more by the money than the red ribbon.

“But then,” said des Lupeaulx, “you must show your gratitude to His Excellency by restoring to Monseigneur the Duc de Navarreins all your claims on him.”

La Baudraye returned to Sancerre as Collector of Taxes. Six months later he was superseded by Monsieur Gravier, regarded as one of the most agreeable financiers who had served under the Empire, and whom Monsieur de la Baudraye introduced to his wife.

When released from his functions, Monsieur de la Baudraye returned to Paris to settle accounts with his debtors. He became Referendary under the Great Seal, Baron, and Officer of the Legion of Honor. After selling the referendary appointment and collecting from his remaining debtors, he reappeared at Sancerre as Master of Appeals and was named Royal Commissioner to a commercial association in the Nivernais with a salary of six thousand francs—a sinecure. Thus the man who was supposed to have blundered financially had in fact made a shrewd match in marriage.

By thrift and an indemnity from his father’s estate, nationalized and sold in 1793, he realized his dream by 1827. Paying four hundred thousand francs down and committing to further instalments—after six years of strict economy—he bought the estate of Anzy on the Loire, two leagues above Sancerre, and its celebrated castle by Philibert de l’Orme, long the Uxelles family seat. He became a major provincial landowner. An entail created by letters patent in December 1820 included Anzy, La Baudraye, and La Hautoy—though this did not erase Dinah’s long years of unacknowledged parsimony until 1835.

This account of the first Baron de la Baudraye’s finances explains his character. Those familiar with rural manias will recognize in him the land-hunger that becomes a consuming passion—an outward avarice that often brings ruin when mortgage interest outstrips agricultural yield. From 1802 to 1827, those who had laughed at the little man trotting to Saint-Thibault and tending his vineyards saw their error when the ant-lion seized his prey after the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse’s extravagance led to the sale of her splendid property.

Madame Piedefer came to live with her daughter. The combined fortunes of Monsieur de la Baudraye and his mother-in-law—who accepted an annuity of twelve hundred francs on La Hautoy and handed it over to him—yielded an acknowledged income of about fifteen thousand francs.

Early in her married life Dinah made La Baudraye agreeable. She converted a spacious forecourt into a formal garden, removed wine-stores and shabby outhouses, and laid out a second garden behind the small turreted Manor with shrubs, flower-beds, and lawns, screened from the vineyards by a creeper-clad wall. She made the interiors as comfortable as circumstances allowed.

To prevent being ruined by so superior a young woman, Monsieur de la Baudraye prudently kept silent about his Parisian recoveries. That secrecy lent him mystery and gave dignity in his wife’s eyes during their early years—so majestic is silence.

The changes at La Baudraye made everyone eager to see the mistress, especially since Dinah would receive no company until she’d settled at home and studied the neighborhood and her taciturn husband. When, one spring morning in 1825, she was first seen walking on the Mall in a blue velvet dress with her mother in black, Sancerre was excited. Visitors, constrained in her presence, admired fine furnishings and curios—shawl-pattern carpets, a carved gilt writing-table, brocade curtains, a Japanese bowl of flowers among recent books. Hearing Dinah play at sight on the piano without demur confirmed her reputation. To avoid carelessness or bad taste, she kept abreast of fashions and luxury through active correspondence with Anna Grossetete, her schoolfriend at Mademoiselle Chamarolles’. Anna, with a fine fortune, had married the Comte de Fontaine’s third son, so Dinah’s callers were constantly piqued that she led fashion; however they tried, they were always, as turfmen say, distanced.

These trifles stirred malignant envy among Sancerre’s ladies, but Dinah’s conversation and wit provoked absolute aversion. In her ambition to keep her mind at Parisian levels, she tolerated no vacuous small talk, no old-fashioned compliments, no petty backstairs slander. She would not endure tittle-tattle; she liked to hear of discoveries in science and art, the latest theatre pieces, the newest poems, and by airing current cant words she gave the impression of thinking deeply.

The Abbé Duret, cure of Sancerre, an old worldly cleric fond of cards, welcomed this taste and enjoyed her company. The sous-préfet, Vicomte de Chargeboeuf, found in her drawing-room an oasis from provincial life. Monsieur de Clagny, the public prosecutor, so admired Dinah that he refused all promotion and became a devoted worshipper. He was a tall, lean man with a minatory countenance and terrible eyes under enormous eyebrows; his eloquence could be incisive, though his love-making was not.

Monsieur Gravier was a little, round man who had been a charming ballad-singer in the Empire days, an accomplishment that helped him become Paymaster-General of the forces. Having been mixed up in Spanish matters with generals then in opposition, he leveraged connections to secure the Receivership at Sancerre by payment. The easy frivolity of the Empire had turned ponderous in Gravier; he failed, or refused, to grasp the difference in manners under the Restoration. He fancied himself superior to Monsieur de Clagny and prided himself on better taste—buff waistcoat, gray trousers, snug coats, a fashionable silk tie slipped through a diamond ring—while the lawyer wore plain black.

These four men were the first to extol Dinah’s cultivation, taste, and refinement, calling her a woman of superior mind. The women, however, whispered that “Madame de la Baudraye must laugh at us behind our back.”

That belief, more or less correct, kept them from visiting. Dinah, branded a pedant for speaking grammatically, was nicknamed the Sappho of Saint-Satur. At last the neighborhood made insolent sport of the great qualities that had roused their enmity. They denied her relative superiority—an act that only emphasized their own ignorance and could not forgive it. In a community of hunched backs, a straight figure is monstrous; Dinah was deemed dangerous and found herself in a desert.

Astonished by the infrequent, perfunctory calls of the neighborhood women, Dinah asked Monsieur de Clagny the reason. “You are too superior a woman to be liked by other women,” he said. Monsieur Gravier, when pressed by the forlorn Dinah, admitted that she was not content to be merely charming: she was clever, well educated, read every new book, loved poetry, played music, and spoke delightfully—too much for jealous women to forgive.

Men told Monsieur de la Baudraye, “You who have such a Superior Woman for a wife are very fortunate.” He began to echo the sentiment proudly. Madame Piedefer, flattered by her daughter, boasted similarly in society—“My daughter, who is a very Superior Woman, was writing yesterday to Madame de Fontaine...”

Those who know the world, especially Paris, know how such reputations are made.

Two years later, by the end of 1825, Dinah de la Baudraye was accused of preferring only men as visitors; soon it was said she did not care for women—and that became a crime. Not a trifling action escaped criticism or misrepresentation. After making every sacrifice a well-bred woman could make and placing herself entirely in the right, Madame de la Baudraye was so rash as to say to a false friend who condoled with her isolation:

“I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!”

The remark produced a terrible effect in Sancerre and was cruelly retorted on the Sappho of Saint-Satur when, being childless after five years of marriage, little de la Baudraye became a byword for laughter. To explain the provincial witticism, recall the Bailli de Ferrette—some may have known him—of whom it was said he was the bravest man in Europe for daring to walk on his legs and who was accused of putting lead in his shoes to save himself from being blown away. Monsieur de la Baudraye, a sallow, almost diaphanous creature, would have been engaged by the Bailli de Ferrette as first gentleman-in-waiting if that diplomatist had been the Grand Duke of Baden rather than merely his envoy.

Monsieur de la Baudraye’s legs were so thin that, for decency, he wore false calves; his thighs were like the arms of an average man, his body not unlike that of a cockchafer, and he would have made an advantageous foil to the Bailli de Ferrette. As he walked his leg-pads often twisted onto his shins; he thanked anyone who warned him. He wore knee-breeches, black silk stockings, and a white waistcoat until 1824. After the marriage he adopted blue trousers and boots with heels, which made Sancerre declare that he had added two inches to his stature so as to come up to his wife’s chin. For ten years he was always seen in the same little bottle-green coat with large white-metal buttons and a black stock that accentuated his cold, stingy face, lighted by gray-blue eyes as keen and passionless as a cat’s. Gentle, as men with fixed habits are, he seemed to please his wife by never contradicting her; he allowed her to do the talking and moved with the deliberate tenacity of an insect.

Dinah, adored for her unrivaled beauty and admired for her cleverness by the most gentlemanly men of the place, encouraged their admiration by conversations it was later said she prepared beforehand. Finding her listeners rapt, she began to listen to herself, enjoyed haranguing them, and at last regarded her friends as the chorus in a tragedy, there only to give her cues. In fact, she had a fine collection of phrases and ideas, derived either from books or by assimilating the opinions of her companions, and thus became a sort of mechanical instrument, going off on a round of phrases as soon as some chance remark released the spring. To her credit, Dinah was full of knowledge and read everything—medical books, statistics, science, jurisprudence—because she did not know how to spend her days once she had reviewed her flower-beds and given orders to the gardener. Gifted with an excellent memory and the knack some women have for hitting the right word, she could talk on any subject with the lucidity of a studied style. Men came from Cosne, La Charité, Nevers, and from Léré, Vailly, Argent, Blancafort, and Aubigny to be introduced to Madame de la Baudraye, as in Switzerland they were introduced to Madame de Staël. Those who heard the tunes emitted by this musical snuff-box went away amazed and told such wonders of Dinah that all the women ten leagues round grew jealous.

There is an indescribable headiness in the admiration we inspire, or in the effect of playing a part, which keeps criticism from reaching the idol. An atmosphere, perhaps from unceasing nervous tension, forms a halo through which the world below is seen. How otherwise account for the perennial good faith that leads to repeated presentments of the same effects, and the constant ignoring of warnings given by children, so dreaded by parents, or by husbands familiar with the peacock airs of their wives? Monsieur de la Baudraye had the frankness of a man who opens an umbrella at the first drop of rain. When his wife started on Negro emancipation or prison reform, he would take up his little blue cap and vanish without a sound, certain he could get to Saint-Thibault to see off a cargo of puncheons and return an hour later to find the discussion near its close. Or, if he had no business, he would walk on the Mall, which commanded the Loire valley panorama, and take a draught of fresh air while his wife performed a sonata in words or a dialectical duet.

Once established as a Superior Woman, Dinah was eager to prove her devotion to remarkable creations of art. She threw herself into propaganda for the romantic school, extending Art to poetry, painting, literature, sculpture, furniture, and opera. She became a medievalist and prized Renaissance treasures, employing her allies as commission agents. Soon after marriage she acquired the Rougets’ furniture, sold at Issoudun in early 1824. She purchased good things in Nivernais and the Haute-Loire. At New Year and on her birthday her friends never failed to give curiosities. These fancies pleased Monsieur de la Baudraye; they made him seem to sacrifice a few crowns to his wife’s taste. In fact his land mania allowed him to think of nothing but the estate of Anzy.

These “antiquities” then cost much less than modern furniture. In five or six years the anteroom, dining-room, two drawing-rooms, and boudoir Dinah arranged on La Baudraye’s ground floor—every spot, even the staircase—were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments. Called “queer” by neighbors, they suited Dinah. All these marvels, soon to be the rage, struck strangers introduced to her; they came expecting something unusual and found their expectations surpassed when, behind a bower of flowers, they saw catacombs full of old things, piled as Sommerard used to pile them—that “Old Mortality” of furniture. These finds served as springs which, turned by a question, played off essays on Jean Goujon, Michel Colombe, Germain Pilon, Boulle, Van Huysum, and Boucher, the great native painter of Le Berry; on Clodion, the carver of wood; on Venetian mirrors; on Brustolone, an Italian tenor who was the Michael-Angelo of boxwood and holm oak; on the thirteenth through seventeenth centuries, on Bernard de Palissy’s glazes, Petitot’s enamels, Dürer’s engravings—whom she called Dur—on illuminations on vellum, on Gothic architecture, early decorated, flamboyant and pure—enough to rouse both old and young.

Possessed with the idea of waking up Sancerre, Madame de la Baudraye tried to form a so-called literary circle. The Presiding Judge, Monsieur Boirouge, who had a house and garden as part of the Popinot-Chandier property, favored the notion. The wily Judge discussed rules with Madame de la Baudraye; he proposed to figure as one of the founders and to let the house for fifteen years to the literary club. Within a year the members were playing dominoes, billiards, and bouillotte, and drinking mulled wine, punch, and liqueurs. A few elegant suppers were given and some masked balls during Carnival. As to literature—there were the newspapers. Politics and business were discussed. Monsieur de la Baudraye was constantly there—on his wife’s account, as she joked.

This result deeply grieved the Superior Woman, who despaired of Sancerre, and therefore collected the wit of the neighborhood in her own drawing-room. Nevertheless, despite the efforts of Messieurs de Chargeboeuf, Gravier, and de Clagny, of the Abbé Duret and the two chief magistrates, of a young doctor, and a young Assistant Judge—all blind admirers of Dinah—there were occasions when, weary of discussion, they allowed excursions into agreeable frivolity, the common basis of worldly conversation. Monsieur Gravier called this “from grave to gay.” The Abbé Duret’s rubber made another pleasing variety on the oracle’s monologues. The three rivals, tired of keeping their minds at the “high range of discussion,” but not daring to confess it, would sometimes turn with ingratiating hints to the old priest.

“Monsieur le Curé is dying for his game,” they would say.

The wily priest lent himself readily to the little trick. He protested.

“We should lose too much by ceasing to listen to our inspired hostess!” and so he would incite Dinah’s magnanimity to take pity at last on her dear Abbé.

This bold manoeuvre, a device of the Sous-préfet’s, was repeated with such skill that Dinah never suspected her slaves of escaping to the prison yard, so to speak, of the cardtable; they would leave her one of the younger functionaries to harry.

One young landowner, the dandy of Sancerre, fell from Dinah’s favor after some rash demonstrations. Having solicited admission to this circle, where he flattered himself he could snatch the blossom from the constituted authorities who guarded it, he was unfortunate enough to yawn in the middle of an explanation Dinah was favoring him with—for the fourth time, it is true—on the philosophy of Kant. Monsieur de la Thaumassière, grandson of the historian of Le Berry, was thenceforth regarded as a man entirely bereft of soul and brains.

The three devotees en titre each submitted to these exorbitant demands on their mind and attention in hope of a crowning triumph when Dinah should become human; for none dared imagine she would give up her innocence as a wife till she had lost all illusions. In 1826, when she was surrounded by adorers, Dinah completed her twentieth year, and the Abbé Duret kept her in a sort of fervid Catholicism; so her worshipers had to be content to overwhelm her with little attentions and small services, only too happy to be taken for the carpet-knights of this sovereign lady by strangers admitted to spend an evening or two at La Baudraye.

“Madame de la Baudraye is a fruit that must be left to ripen.” This was Monsieur Gravier’s opinion, who was waiting.

As to the lawyer, he wrote four-page letters, to which Dinah replied in soothing words as she walked, leaning on his arm, round and round the lawn after dinner.

Madame de la Baudraye, thus guarded by three passions and always under the eye of her pious mother, escaped the malignity of slander. It was evident to all Sancerre that no two of these three men would ever leave the third alone with Madame de la Baudraye; their jealousy was a comedy to lookers-on.

To reach Saint-Thibault from Caesar’s Gate there is a way much shorter than that by the ramparts, down what in mountainous districts is called a coursière, at Sancerre le Casse-cou, or Break-neck Alley. The name fits a steep, stony path shut in by high banks of vineyards. By the Break-neck the distance from Sancerre to La Baudraye is much abridged. The ladies of the place, jealous of the Sappho of Saint-Satur, were wont to walk on the Mall, looking down this Longchamp of the bigwigs, whom they would stop and engage in conversation—sometimes the Sous-préfet and sometimes the Public Prosecutor—who would listen with every sign of impatience or uncivil absence of mind. As the turrets of La Baudraye are visible from the Mall, many a younger man came to contemplate Dinah’s abode while envying the ten or twelve privileged persons who might spend their afternoons with the Queen of the neighborhood.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was not slow to discover the advantage he, as Dinah’s husband, held over his wife’s adorers, and he used them openly, obtaining remission of taxes and winning two lawsuits. In every litigation he used the Public Prosecutor’s name so effectively that matters were carried no further; like many undersized men, he was contentious and litigious in business, though in the gentlest manner.

At the same time, the more plainly guiltless she appeared, the less conceivable Madame de la Baudraye’s position seemed to prying women. Frequently, at the Présidente de Boirouge’s house, ladies of a certain age would spend whole evenings discussing the La Baudraye household among themselves. They all suspected a mystery, a secret such as always interests women experienced in life. And, in fact, at La Baudraye one of those slow, monotonous conjugal tragedies was being played out which would have remained forever unknown if the merciless scalpel of the nineteenth century, driven by the demand for novelty, had not dissected the darkest corners of the heart, or at least those which the decency of past centuries left unopened. That domestic drama sufficiently accounts for Dinah’s immaculate virtue during her early married life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A young woman whose school triumphs sprang from pride, and whose first scheme in life had succeeded, was unlikely to rest on her laurels. Frail as Monsieur de la Baudraye seemed, he was an unexpectedly good match for Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer. What motive had this country landowner, at forty-four, in marrying a girl of seventeen—and what could his wife make of the bargain? These were Dinah’s first thoughts.

The little man never behaved as his wife expected. He let her take the five precious acres around La Baudraye for pleasure grounds, and paid, almost generously, the seven or eight thousand francs Dinah required for house improvements, enabling her to buy furniture at the Rougets’ sale and redecorate in various styles—Mediaeval, Louis XIV, Pompadour. For a while Dinah believed either he was not so miserly as reputed or that she had great influence over him. The illusion lasted a year and a half.

After his second journey to Paris Dinah discovered in him the coldness of a provincial miser where money was concerned. The first time she asked for supplies and played sweetness, the little man bluntly set strict sums: two hundred francs a month for her personal expenses, twelve hundred francs a year to Madame Piedefer as a charge on La Hautoy—two hundred more than the marriage settlement—and nothing explicitly for housekeeping. He reminded her that as a bachelor he had lived on fifteen hundred francs a year and now spent six thousand including rates and repairs; that a winegrower’s returns could be ruined by weather, by bankrupt clients, or bad speculation; and that small owners must count on the minimum, keep a year’s income in hand, and reckon on no more than two-thirds of returns.

Any resistance is enough to make a woman vow to subdue it; Dinah flung herself against a will of iron masked with gentleness. She tried to fill him with jealousy and alarms, but his confidence was insolent. He left her, when he went to Paris, with Medor’s conviction in Angelique’s fidelity. When she affected cold disdain to nettle him, Monsieur de la Baudraye gazed at her with fixed eyes, like a cat that waits when a blow is threatened. His mute indifference unnerved the young wife of twenty; she could not understand the selfish quiescence of a man who regulated his life with clocklike regularity. So the little man always evaded, while she always struck out—ten feet above his head.

Dinah’s fits of fury at being condemned to La Baudraye and Sancerre can be imagined—she who had dreamed of handling a fortune and managing the dwarf whom she at first humored to command. Hoping to appear on Paris’s greater stage, she accepted the vulgar incense of her admirers with a view to seeing Monsieur de la Baudraye’s name drawn from the electoral urn; she supposed him ambitious after seeing him return thrice from Paris, each time a step higher in social rank. But when she struck at his heart, it was like tapping on marble. The man who had been Receiver-General, Referendary, Master of Appeals, Officer of the Legion of Honor, and Royal Commissioner was but a mole throwing up little hills round a vineyard. Lamentations to the Public Prosecutor, the Sous-préfet, even Monsieur Gravier, increased their devotion to this supposed victim; like many women she never mentioned her speculative schemes, and, finding such speculation vain, she ceased to speculate.

Dinah, tossed by mental storms, was undecided when, in the autumn of 1827, news came of the Baron de la Baudraye’s purchase of the estate of Anzy. Then the little man showed a pride and glee that for months altered Dinah’s ideas; she fancied some hidden greatness when she found him applying for a patent of entail. In his triumph he cried, “Dinah, you shall be a countess yet!” There followed a patched-up reunion between husband and wife, the kind that can never endure and only humiliates a woman whose apparent superiority is unreal while her unseen superiority is genuine. Dinah, ridiculous from the perversity of her cleverness, had real qualities of soul which circumstances did not bring to light; provincial life debased her small change of wit daily. Monsieur de la Baudraye, devoid of soul, strength, and wit, seemed a man of character simply by pursuing a fixed plan he was too feeble to change.

Their lives entered a first phase of six years during which Dinah became utterly provincial. Paris has many kinds of women—the duchess, the financier’s wife, the ambassadress, the minister’s wife, and ladies on both banks of the Seine—but in the country there is but one kind: the provincial woman. This remark points to a sore of modern society. France in the nineteenth century divides into two broad zones—Paris and the provinces. The provinces are jealous of Paris; Paris barely thinks of the provinces but to demand money. Once Paris was the capital of the provinces and the court ruled the capital; now all Paris is the Court, and all the country is the town.

However lofty or clever a girl from any department may be, if she marries in the country and remains there she inevitably becomes the provincial woman. The commonplace of second-rate ideas, indifference to dress, and the culture of vulgar people swamp the sublimer essence hidden in youth; everything decays. How should it be otherwise? Country girls see none but provincials; their choice lies among mediocrities; provincial fathers marry daughters to provincial sons; crossing races isn’t thought of, and thus mind degenerates. Mankind becomes dwarfed in mind and body, for the fatal principle of conformity of fortune governs matrimonial alliances. Men of talent, artists, superior brains—every bird of brilliant plumage—flies to Paris. The provincial woman, inferior in herself, is also inferior through her husband. How can she be happy under this twofold consciousness?

A third element deepens the aridity. One flattering balm for a woman is the assurance of being something in the life of a superior man chosen by herself. But in the country husbands are often inferior beings, and bachelors are no better. When a provincial wife commits her “little sin,” she falls for some local dandy who is at best merely well dressed; she knows her fancy is chasing the commonplace. Dinah was preserved from this danger by her pride and the conviction of her own superiority. Even if not guarded by her mother’s presence, her pride and sense of her destiny would have protected her. Flattered by admirers, she saw no lover among them. No man realized the poetical ideal she and Anna Grossetete had sketched. Stirred by homage, she asked herself who she would choose; she preferred Monsieur de Chargeboeuf, a gentleman of good family, but his cold nature, selfishness, and narrow ambitions repelled her. He had deserted a woman he had loved when his family feared scandal. Monsieur de Clagny, whose mind appealed to hers and whose ambition was founded on love, Dinah thought perfectly odious. When condemned to six years at Sancerre she nearly accepted Chargeboeuf’s devotion, but he was appointed to a prefecture and left. Clagny’s great satisfaction was that the new Sous-préfet was a married man whose wife befriended Dinah. The lawyer’s only rival now was Monsieur Gravier—the typical man of forty used by women while they laugh at him, whose hopes they remorselessly encourage. In six years among men introduced from twenty leagues round, none stirred Dinah’s anticipation of love or superior soul.

Thus none of Dinah’s choicest faculties developed; she swallowed many insults to her pride, constantly bruised by a husband who calmly played supernumerary in the drama of her life. Compelled to bury her wealth of love, she showed only surface to the world. Now and then she tried to rouse herself, to form some manly resolution; but she was kept in leading strings by the need for money. Slowly, despite ambitious protests and grievous recriminations of her mind, she underwent the provincial metamorphosis. Each day took a fragment of her spirited determination. She had rules for her person which she gradually abandoned. Though at first she kept up with fashions and novelties, she had to limit purchases to her allowance: instead of six hats, caps, or gowns she resigned herself to one gown each season; an admired bonnet served two seasons. Often her artistic sense led her to sacrifice personal requirements to obtain some bit of Gothic furniture. By the seventh year she had her morning dresses made at home by the best local needlewoman; her mother, husband, and friends pronounced her charming in these inexpensive costumes which credited her taste. Her ideas were imitated; with no standard of comparison she fell into provincial snares. If a Parisian has a defect she makes it an adornment; the provincial woman accepts the worst. Dinah, extremely slim, showed it to excess; reduced by dull weariness she began to look like a skeleton in clothes; those who saw her daily did not notice the gradual change.

This is a natural result of provincial life. In spite of marriage a young woman preserves her beauty for some time; the town is proud of her, but everyone sees her every day and perception dulled. If she loses color it is scarcely noticed; a little neglect is thought charming. When Dinah ceased to have a new dress each season she seemed to have yielded to the place’s philosophy.

Matters of speech, choice of words and ideas, rust as the body if not rubbed up in Paris; but provincialism most stamps gesture, gait, and movement. The provincial walks as if there were nothing to avoid; being known and always in her place, everyone makes way for her, and she loses the charm of the unforeseen. Life in common induces simian mimicry: gestures, tone, manner, attitudes, and countenance are copied. In six years Dinah sank to the pitch of her society. As she took on Monsieur de Clagny’s ideas she assumed his tone and unconsciously fell into masculine manners from seeing none but men; she thought laughing at their ridiculous traits kept her safe, but some hue of what she laughed at remained. A Parisian woman, seeing many examples of good taste, learns to seize the moment to appear advantageously; Madame de la Baudraye, accustomed to the stage, acquired an indefinable theatrical and domineering manner—the air of a prima donna—ironical smiles would soon have cured her in the capital.

After she had acquired these absurdities, deceived by worshipers and imagining them graces, a moment of terrible awakening came like an avalanche. In one day she was crushed by a frightful comparison.

In 1829, after Chargeboeuf’s departure, she expected the Baronne de Fontaine. Anna’s husband, now Director-General under the Minister of Finance, took advantage of leave on his father’s death to take his wife to Italy. Anna came to Sancerre; the meeting was disastrous. Anna, less handsome than Dinah at school, was now a thousand times handsomer despite fatigue and traveling dress. She stepped from an elegant chaise loaded with Paris milliners’ boxes and had a lady’s maid whose airs frightened Dinah. All the difference between a Paris woman and a provincial was evident: Anna spent six thousand francs a year on herself—the same sum that kept the household at La Baudraye.

In twenty-four hours the friends exchanged confidences; the Parisian, seeing herself so far superior to the phoenix of Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ school, showed kindness and attentions that stabbed Dinah. She understood Anna’s advantages lay on the surface while her own were buried. When Anna left, Madame de la Baudraye, by then twenty-two, fell into despair.

“What ails you?” asked Monsieur de Clagny, seeing her dejected.

“Anna,” she said, “has learned to live, while I have been learning to endure.”

A tragi-comedy was enacting in the house, in harmony with money quarrels and successive transformations—a drama the Abbe Duret and Monsieur de Clagny alone partly understood—when Dinah, in idleness or vanity, revealed the secret of her anonymous fame.

Though mixing verse and prose is odd in French literature, to give a fair idea of her unconfessed struggle it is necessary to analyze a poem born of her despair. Her patience broken by Chargeboeuf’s departure, Dinah took the Abbe’s advice to exhale her thoughts in verse—“You will find relief as those who write epitaphs or elegies,” he said. The strange production stirred the Allier, the Nievre, and the Cher, proud of a poet to rival Paris. Paquita la Sevillane, by Jan Diaz, was published in the Echo du Morvan, a review that for eighteen months survived provincial indifference. Some at Nevers thought Jan Diaz mocked the new school of eccentric verse imitating foreign mannerisms.

The poem began with a ballad praising Spain’s fragrant plains and Sevillian dancers and contrasting them with Northern squalor. Dinah then wrote a magnificent if affected description of Rouen—where she had never been—contrasting a manufacturing town’s life with Spain’s careless life, poetry versus sordid money-making. Jan Diaz made Paquita a Seville native sovereign of her town at thirteen, loved by three toreadors, whose soldier-lover carried her away to Normandy only to leave for Russia. The parting and vows were painted with Byron-like feeling. Much of the poem describes Paquita’s sufferings waiting alone in Rouen; she lives on narcotics and dreams, almost dies, but remains faithful; the soldier returns pale and frozen by Russia, and she, true, is still beautiful. The poem then takes a bitter turn: Paquita, failing to find suitable luxury in Rouen, falls into vice, ending in a house of ill-fame described with stark realism. Finally she is faded and old, but it was she who once sang of Spain.

The poem, some six hundred lines, with seguidillas at beginning and end, had gloomy vigor that alarmed Dinah, who found herself admired in three departments under the cloak of anonymity. While she enjoyed success, she feared provincial malignity: if the secret leaked, other women would trace likenesses between writer and Paquita. Reflection came too late; she shuddered at having made “copy” of some of her woes. “Write no more,” said the Abbe. “You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet.” Moulins, Nevers, Bourges were searched for Jan Diaz; Dinah remained impenetrable.

To remove suspicion, she wrote a charming two-canto poem on the Mass-Oak, a Nivernais legend: foes from Nevers and Saint-Saulge, about to battle in the forest of Faye, are stayed by a priest who, by saying Mass under an oak, makes them friends. This poem, far superior to Paquita, was less admired.

After these attempts Madame de la Baudraye, feeling herself a poet, had a light on her brow and a flash in her eyes that made her handsomer than ever. She cast longing looks at Paris, aspiring to fame—and fell back into her den at La Baudraye, daily squabbles with her husband, and a little circle whose characters had become a bore. Though literature gave relief—poetry echoed in her empty life and provided an outlet—it increased her hatred of the gray, ponderous provincial atmosphere.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When, after the Revolution of 1830, George Sand’s fame revived interest in Le Berry, towns envied La Châtre for producing this rival of Madame de Staël and Camille Maupin, and were ready to acclaim any minor feminine talent. Thus in France arose a host of would-be Muses—young girls or wives drawn from silent lives by the lure of glory. Strange doctrines were proclaimed about women’s role: French common sense was not wholly perverted, but women were allowed to express ideas and opinions they would not have owned a few years earlier.

Monsieur de Clagny took advantage of this outbreak of freedom to collect the works of Jan Diaz in a small volume printed by Desroziers at Moulins. He wrote a short notice amusing to those in the secret, though not novel. Practical jokes are capital while the author is unknown; they fail when the poet’s identity is known.

From this angle, the memoir of Jan Diaz, said to be born at Bourges in 1807, son of a Spanish prisoner, might deceive a future compiler of a Universal Biography. Nothing is overlooked: names of professors at Bourges College, deceased schoolfellows such as Lousteau and Bianchon, who supposedly knew the dreamy boy and his bent toward poetry. An elegy called Tristesse written at school; the two poems Paquita la Sevillane and Le Chene de la Messe; three sonnets, a description of the Cathedral and the House of Jacques Coeur at Bourges, and a tale called Carola, published as his work in hand at death—these remains, and the poet’s last hours of misery near Château-Chinon, wrung the hearts of the feeling public in the Nievre, the Bourbonnais, the Cher, and the Morvan, where he died unknown even to the woman he had loved.

Two hundred copies of the little yellow volume were printed; one hundred and fifty sold—about fifty in each department. This average revives enthusiasm for provincial passion for letters, now more apt to expend itself in business than in books.

After Monsieur de Clagny had given away some copies, Dinah still had seven or eight wrapped in newspapers that published notices. Twenty copies sent to Paris papers were swamped in editors’ piles. Nathan and several fellow-countrymen of Le Berry were taken in; Nathan wrote an article crediting the great man with all the fine qualities we ascribe to the dead.

Lousteau, warned by schoolfellows who could not remember Jan Diaz, sought information from Sancerre and learned Jan Diaz was a pseudonym assumed by a woman.

Then, in and around Sancerre, Madame de la Baudraye became the rage; she was hailed as the future rival of George Sand. From Sancerre to Bourges a poem was praised that at any other time would have been hooted. The provincial public does not love the middle course: it lifts you to the skies or drags you in the mud.

By this time the good Abbé who had counselled Madame de la Baudraye was dead; he would certainly have prevented her rushing into public life. But three years’ work without recognition weighed on Dinah’s soul, and she accepted the clatter of fame as a substitute for disappointed ambitions. Poetry and dreams of celebrity, which had lulled her grief since her meeting with Anna Grossetête, no longer sufficed to exhaust her morbid heart. The Abbé Duret—who had spoken of the world when religion’s voice was impotent and had promised a happy future by assuring her God would compensate brave sufferings—could no longer stand between her and the opening to sin.

The wise old priest had tried more than once to enlighten Dinah about her husband’s character, telling her the man could hate; but women are not ready to believe in such force in weak natures. Dinah, finding her husband incapable of love, denied him the power to hate.

“Do not confound hatred and vengeance,” said the Abbé. “They are different. Hatred is a vice of narrow souls; they feed it with meanness and make it a pretext for sordid tyranny. So beware of offending Monsieur de la Baudraye; he would forgive an infidelity, because he could make capital of it, but he would be doubly implacable if you touched that spot so cruelly wounded by Monsieur Milaud of Nevers, and would make your life unendurable.”

Now, when the whole countryside—Nevers and Sancerre, Le Morvan and Le Berry—prided itself on Madame de la Baudraye and lauded her as Jan Diaz, “little La Baudraye” felt his glory a mortal blow. He alone knew the secret source of Paquita la Sevillane. When this terrible work was spoken of, everybody said of Dinah, “Poor woman! Poor soul!” The women rejoiced to pity her who had so long oppressed them; never had Dinah seemed to stand higher in the neighborhood.

The shriveled old man, more wrinkled, yellower, feebler than ever, gave no sign; but Dinah sometimes detected in his gaze an icy venom that belied his increased politeness. She understood at last that this was not a mere domestic squabble; when she forced an explanation with her “insect,” as Monsieur Gravier called him, she found cold, hard impassibility. She flew into a passion, reproached him for her life over the past eleven years, and made, intentionally, what women call a scene. “Little La Baudraye” sat in an armchair with his eyes shut and listened phlegmatically. As usual, the dwarf got the better of his wife. Dinah saw she had done wrong in writing; she vowed never to write another line, and she kept her vow.

Then there was desolation in the Sancerrois.

“Why did not Madame de la Baudraye compose any more verses?” was the universal cry.

At this time Madame de la Baudraye had no enemies; everyone rushed to see her, not a week passed without fresh introductions. The wife of the presiding judge, a proud bourgeoise née Popinot-Chandier, desired her son Gatien to pay his respects to La Baudraye, flattering herself that he might win favor with this Superior Woman—the epithet replacing the absurd nickname of “The Sappho of Saint-Satur.” Nine years leading the opposition, she praised the Muse of Sancerre lavishly.

After scrambling through so many brambles and setting off on many roads, after dreaming of love in splendor and smelling the darkest dramas, weary of her dreary existence, one day Dinah fell into the pit she had sworn to avoid. Seeing Monsieur de Clagny always sacrificing himself and at last refusing a high appointment in Paris where his family wanted him, she said to herself, “He loves me!” She vanquished her repulsion and seemed willing to reward so much constancy.

This impulse formed a coalition in Sancerre to secure Monsieur de Clagny’s return at the next elections. Madame de la Baudraye dreamed of going to Paris in the wake of the new deputy.

But despite solemn promises, the hundred and fifty votes hoped for this adorer of Dinah—who hoped to see him Keeper of the Seals—figured as an imposing minority of fifty votes. The jealousy of the President de Boirouge and Monsieur Gravier’s hatred, for he believed in the candidate’s supremacy in Dinah’s heart, were worked on by a young sous-préfet; for this deed the allies got the young man made a préfet elsewhere.

“I shall never cease to regret,” he said as he left Sancerre, “that I did not succeed in pleasing Madame de la Baudraye; that would have made my triumph complete!”

The household thus racked by domestic troubles was calm on the surface; two ill-assorted but resigned beings upheld the propriety society insists on, a yoke to Dinah. Why did she long to throw off the mask she had worn for twelve years? Whence this increasing weariness and hope of finding herself a widow?

These illusions deceive many women. After attempting to master Monsieur de la Baudraye, she had hoped to become a mother. Between those miserable disputes and the melancholy conviction as to her fate, time passed. Then, when she looked for consolation, Monsieur de Chargeboeuf left her. Thus the temptation that commonly causes women to sin had hitherto been absent. For while some women make straight for unfaithfulness, many cling to hope and do not fall till long wandering in a labyrinth of secret woes.

Such was Dinah. She had so little impulse to fail in duty that she did not care enough for Monsieur de Clagny to forgive him his defeat.

The move to the Château d’Anzy, the rearrangement of her treasures and curiosities—now set off by Philibert de Lorme’s splendid setting—occupied her for months and gave her leisure to meditate one of those decisive steps that startle the public, ignorant of motives sometimes revealed by gossip.

Madame de la Baudraye had been struck by Lousteau’s reputation as a ladies’ man and wished to know him. She read his books and was fired with enthusiasm, less for his talents than for his successes with women; to attract him to the country, she broached the notion that Sancerre must return one of its great men at the elections. She made Gatien Boirouge write to the great physician Bianchon, whom he claimed as cousin via the Popinots, and persuaded an old friend of the departed Madame Lousteau to stir the journalist’s ambitions by saying certain persons in Sancerre were determined to elect a deputy from among Paris’s distinguished men.

Tired of commonplace neighbors, Madame de la Baudraye would at last meet really illustrious men and might give her fall the lustre of fame.

Neither Lousteau nor Bianchon replied at first; Bianchon, who had won his professorial chair the year before, could not leave his lectures. In September, when the vintage was at its height, the two Parisians arrived in their native province and found it absorbed in the wine-crop of 1836; there could be no public demonstration in their favor. “We have fallen flat,” said Lousteau.

In 1836 Lousteau, worn by sixteen years of struggle in the Capital and aged by pleasure, penury, and disappointments, looked forty-eight though he was thirty-seven. Bald and assuming a Byronic air to match his decay, he blamed the Press for consuming superior talents and lent a grace to his exhaustion. He affected contempt of life and spurious misanthropy, though his eyes could still flash with fire, and he dressed fashionably to make up for lost youth.

Horace Bianchon, wearing the ribbon of the Legion of Honor, was fat and burly, with a patriarchal air, thick hair, a prominent brow, the frame of a hard worker, and the calm expression of a philosopher. This prosaic personality set off his more frivolous companion.

The two great men remained unrecognized at the inn the whole morning, and only by chance Monsieur de Clagny heard of their arrival. Madame de la Baudraye, in despair, despatched Gatien Boirouge—who had no vineyards—to beg them to spend a few days at the Château d’Anzy. For the last year Dinah had played the chatelaine and spent the winter only at La Baudraye. Monsieur Gravier, the public prosecutor, the presiding judge, and Gatien combined to give a banquet for the great men to meet the town’s literary personages.

On hearing that the beautiful Madame de la Baudraye was Jan Diaz, the Parisians went to Anzy, fetched in a wagonette driven by Gatien. The young man, genuinely believing in his heroine, spoke of her as the handsomest woman in those parts, a Superior Woman who might produce a sensation in Paris. Hence the suppressed astonishment of Bianchon and the waggish journalist when they beheld, on the garden steps of Anzy, a lady in thin black cashmere with a deep tucker, a short riding-habit effect, and a black velvet cap; below it her hair fell in thick curls. The attire showed a pretty figure, fine eyes, and lids somewhat faded by her weariful life. In Le Berry the singularity of this artistic costume served as cloak for the romantic affectations of the Superior Woman.

On seeing these affectations—indeed, affectations of soul and mind—the friends put on a serious face to listen to Madame de la Baudraye, who made them a set speech of thanks for coming to cheer her monotony. Dinah walked her guests round the lawn, ornamented with large vases of flowers in front of the château.

“How is it,” said Lousteau, “that so handsome a woman as you, and apparently so superior, should have remained buried in the country? What do you do to make life endurable?”

“That is the crux,” said the lady. “It is unendurable. Utter despair or dull resignation—there is no third alternative; that is the arid soil in which our existence is rooted, and on which a thousand stagnant ideas fall; they cannot fertilize the ground but supply food for the etiolated flowers of our desert souls. Never believe in indifference! Indifference is either despair or resignation. Then each woman takes up the pursuit which, according to her character, seems to promise some amusement. Some rush into jam-making and washing, household management, the vintage or harvest, bottling fruit, embroidering handkerchiefs, the cares of motherhood, the intrigues of a country town. Others torment a much-enduring piano until it sounds like an old kettle. Some pious dames argue the Word of God’s brands; they play cards in the evening, dance with the same partners for twelve years, make solemn walks on the Mall, visits of politeness, and ask each other where they bought their gowns.”

“I admire such courage, madame,” said Bianchon. “When we endure such misfortunes, it is well to have the wit to make a virtue of necessity.”

Amazed at Dinah’s laying provincial life bare in anticipation of sarcasm, Gatien nudged Lousteau: “Well! did I say too much?”

“But, madame,” said Lousteau, “you prove we are still in Paris. I shall steal this gem of description; it will be worth ten thousand francs to me in an article.”

“Oh, monsieur,” she retorted, “never trust provincial women.”

“And why not?” said Lousteau.

Madame de la Baudraye was wily enough—an innocent form of cunning—to show the two Parisians, one of whom she hoped to conquer, the snare into which he would fall, reflecting she would have the upper hand when he ceased to see it.

“When you first come,” she said, “you laugh at us. Then, forgetting Paris’s brilliancy, you pay court to us, if only as a pastime. You who are famous for past passions will be flattered. Then take care!” cried Dinah, with a coquettish gesture. “When a poor little country-bred woman has an eccentric passion for some superior man who has wandered into the provinces, it is to her something more than a sentiment; she makes it her occupation and part of her life. There is nothing more dangerous than such attachment; she compares, studies, reflects, dreams, and will not give up her dream; she thinks of the man when he has ceased to think of her.

“One catastrophe that weighs on a woman in the provinces is that abrupt termination of a passion often seen in England. A life under minute observation compels a woman either to keep on the rails or to start aside like a steam engine wrecked by an obstacle. Parisian strategies of love, the coquetting forming half the composition of a Parisian woman, are unknown here.”

“That is true,” said Lousteau. “There is in a country-bred woman’s heart a store of surprises, as in some toys.”

“A woman will have spoken to you three times in a winter and without your knowing it you will be lodged in her heart. Then comes a picnic or an excursion and all is said—or done! A poet or philosopher, instead of vilifying the provincial woman and believing her depraved, would guess the unrevealed poetry and the sweet romance whose last phrase falls to some happy sub-lieutenant or provincial bigwig.”

“The provincial women I have met in Paris,” said Lousteau, “were rapid in their proceedings—”

“My word, they are strange,” said the lady, with a shrug.

“They are like playgoers who book the second performance, sure the piece will not fail,” replied the journalist.

“And what is the cause of all these woes?” asked Bianchon.

“Paris is the monster that brings us grief,” replied the Superior Woman. “The evil radiates seven leagues round and devastates the land. Provincial life is not self-existent. Only when a nation is divided into many minor states can each have a physiognomy, and a woman reflect the glory of her sphere. In France, as in any country with one capital, centralization produces a dead level of manners.”

“Then you would say manners could recover individuality by a federation of French states into an empire?” said Lousteau.

“That is hardly to be wished; France would have to conquer too many countries,” said Bianchon.

“This misfortune is unknown in England,” exclaimed Dinah. “London does not exert such tyranny; it has a worse disease—vile hypocrisy—which is a far greater evil!”

“The English aristocracy,” said Lousteau, “assimilates every form of superiority; it lives in magnificent parks and is in London for no more than the season. It lives in the country, flourishing there.”

“Yes,” said Madame de la Baudraye, “London is the capital of trade and government. The aristocracy hold a ‘mote’ there for sixty days only, give and take the passwords of the day, look in on legislative cookery, review the girls to marry and the carriages to be sold, exchange greetings, and are away again.”

“Hence,” said Lousteau, in epigram, “in Perfidious Albion you may meet a charming woman anywhere.”

“But charming English women!” replied Madame de la Baudraye with a smile. “Here is my mother,” she added, seeing Madame Piedefer coming.

Having introduced the two Paris lions to the ambitious skeleton that called itself woman under Madame Piedefer’s name—a tall, lean person with a red face—Dinah left her visitors for a few minutes.

“Well,” said Gatien to Lousteau, “what do you think of her?”

“I think the clever woman of Sancerre is simply the greatest chatterbox,” replied the journalist.

“A woman who wants to see you deputy!” cried Gatien. “An angel!”

“Forgive me, I forgot you were in love with her,” said Lousteau. “Forgive the cynicism of an old scamp. Ask Bianchon; I have no illusions left. I see things as they are. The woman has evidently dried up her mother like a partridge roasted at too fierce a fire.”

Gatien de Boirouge contrived to let Madame de la Baudraye know what the journalist had said of her during the dinner, which was copious and splendid, and the lady took care not to talk too much while it proceeded. This lack of conversation betrayed Gatien’s indiscretion. Etienne tried to regain his footing, but Dinah’s advances were directed to Bianchon.

However, half-way through the evening, the Baroness was gracious to Lousteau again. Have you never observed what great meanness may be committed for small ends? Thus the haughty Dinah, who would not sacrifice herself for a fool, who in the country led a wretched life of struggles, suppressed rebellion, and unuttered poetry, suddenly stepped off her peak when she recollected her album.

Madame de la Baudraye had caught the mania for autographs; she possessed an oblong volume truly an album, two-thirds blank. The Baronne de Fontaine, who had kept it three months, had with difficulty obtained a line from Rossini, six bars by Meyerbeer, the four lines Victor Hugo writes in every album, a verse from Lamartine, a few words from Béranger, the first words of Télémaque by George Sand, Scribe’s famous lines on the umbrella, some notes from Berlioz. Monsieur de Clagny had added a song by Lacenaire, two lines from Fieschi, and a short note from Napoleon. Then Monsieur Gravier had persuaded Mademoiselle Mars and other distinguished actors and singers to write their names. This beginning was precious to Dinah because she was the only person for ten leagues round who owned an album.

Within the last two years, several young ladies had acquired such books filled with more or less absurd quotations. You who spend your lives collecting autographs, simple souls like Dutch tulip-fanciers, will excuse Dinah when, fearing to keep her guests only two days, she begged Bianchon to enrich the volume she handed him.

The doctor made Lousteau smile by showing him this sentence on the first page:

“What makes the populace dangerous is that it has in its pocket an absolution for every crime.

“J. B. DE CLAGNY.”

“We will second the man brave enough to plead for the Monarchy,” Desplein’s great pupil whispered to Lousteau, and he wrote below:

“The distinction between Napoleon and a water-carrier is evident only to Society; Nature takes no account of it. Thus Democracy, resisting inequality, constantly appeals to Nature.

“H. BIANCHON.”

“Ah!” cried Dinah, amazed, “you rich men take a gold piece from your purse as poor men bring out a farthing.... I do not know,” she went on, turning to Lousteau, “whether it is taking an unfair advantage of a guest to hope for a few lines—”

“Nay, madame, you flatter me. Bianchon is a great man, but I am too insignificant! Twenty years hence my name will be more difficult to identify than that of the Public Prosecutor whose axiom, written in your album, will designate him as an obscurer Montesquieu. And I should want twenty-four hours to improvise some sufficiently bitter reflections, for I could only describe what I feel.”

“I wish you needed a fortnight,” said Madame de la Baudraye graciously, as she handed him the book.

At five the next morning everyone at the Château d’Anzy was astir. Little La Baudraye had arranged a day’s shooting for his Parisians—less for their pleasure than to show the twelve hundred acres he intended to reclaim, a project costing about a hundred thousand francs and promising an annual increase of thirty to sixty thousand francs to the estate.

“Do you know why the Public Prosecutor has not come out with us?” Gatien Boirouge asked Monsieur Gravier.

“He told us he must sit to-day; the minor cases are before the Court,” Gravier answered.

“And did you believe that?” cried Gatien. “My papa said to me, ‘Monsieur Lebas will not join you early, for Monsieur de Clagny has begged him as his deputy!’”

Gravier’s face changed. “Indeed! And Monsieur de la Baudraye is gone to La Charité!”

“Why meddle?” Bianchon reproved Gatien.

“Horace is right,” Lousteau added. “Why trouble about each other? You squander time in frivolities.”

At a copse Gravier went off with the two great men and Gatien under a keeper’s guidance through a ravine. When they reached a clearing Bianchon suggested waiting for Gravier.

“You may be a great physician,” said Gatien, “but ignorant of provincial life. Gravier will be back at the château within twenty minutes.” He checked his watch. “I couldn’t rest if Madame de la Baudraye were alone with Monsieur de Clagny. There are two of them now; Dinah will be guarded.”

“Ah ha! Then Dinah hasn’t decided?” Lousteau teased.

“So mamma thinks. I fear Monsieur de Clagny may have enchanted her. If she sees him as Attorney‑General he may appear handsome; eloquence has privileges. Besides, Madame de la Baudraye is ambitious; she dislikes Sancerre and dreams of Paris.”

“You yourself hope to succeed the Public Prosecutor?” Lousteau smiled.

“Paris offers many women,” Gatien pleaded. “But at Sancerre, where women set up for sanctity, the handsomest keeps others at a distance. A young man of twenty‑two may guess at her secrets if she treats him with some consideration.”

Bianchon dismissed Gatien’s alarm. Lousteau proposed a plot: after dinner they would tell lurid stories of murderous wives to watch the faces of Madame de la Baudraye and de Clagny.

That evening Lousteau returned an album to Dinah with an elegy—“Spleen”—that painted himself as a blighted poet. Dinah looked pitying; Clagny, suspicious, scowled at the sham melancholy. He sat down to play backgammon with the cure; Gravier placed a lamp to cast light on Dinah, who embroidered a wicker‑paper basket. The three conspirators sat near.

“For whom the basket?” asked Lousteau.

“For Monsieur de la Baudraye,” she replied.

Lousteau mocked Parisians’ disbelief in women’s virtue; de Clagny complained of their literature’s focus on adultery. Etienne retorted with an extravagant defense: if you exclude all books mentioning illicit love you must burn vast swathes of literature—Homer, Milton, Dante, Rousseau.

“When you take the political attitude,” he went on, “a judge of Tiberius’s day might have called Joseph’s wife an adulteress; Pilate would not recognize miracles. Judges always represent established interests—Pilate, Anytus, modern prosecutors. In their view Socrates and Jesus were criminals. So to call religions or revolutions impostures is to think like a public prosecutor.”

“Your argument is blasphemy,” said de Clagny.

“I grant it,” Etienne answered, “but not maliciously. If there were many men like me, government would be impossible.”

Madame Piedefer made arch faces. Bianchon warned Lousteau not to upset the company with talk of religion. Gravier, if a romancer, said, would favor wronged husbands; he had seen dramatic, even heroic avengers. Bianchon and Lousteau agreed that deceived husbands could be admirable, and Lousteau promised a chilling story of revenge—one told by Charles Nodier about a Chevalier de Beauvoir.

He recounted the tale: after the 18th Brumaire, a Maille envoy, Chevalier de Beauvoir, sent by Chouans, was arrested on landing. Though his papers were in order and he bore himself well, sticking to minute orders meant he was detained and sent to the Castle of l’Escarpe, a fortress on precipitous rocks.

The commandant, a Corsican, treated him well; his pretty wife took a fancy to Beauvoir. Whether imprudence occurred the Chevalier never explained, but the jealous commandant imprisoned him in a dungeon, fettered and fed on black bread. In despair Beauvoir planned escape when a turnkey, secretly paid, offered him a file and a knotted rope and explained how to cut a bar and lower himself by knots. Beauvoir filed the bars, disguised the breach, hid his rope, and one murky night let himself down. At the last knot he found no footing: the distance to the rocks was a hundred feet. Suspecting a trap, he climbed back, feigned an attempted escape by leaving his rope, and lay in wait. When the jailer returned and opened the door, Beauvoir struck him dead with the iron bar he’d filed out, stripped him, donned his clothes, mimicked his gait, and walked out with the unsuspecting warders’ confidence.

The tale did not seem to affect de Clagny or Dinah; the conspirators watched them for signs of guilt but found coolness. Bianchon then told “La Grande Bretèche”—the Spanish grandee walled in a cupboard—ending to dramatic effect. Madame de la Baudraye marvelled at a woman’s courage to risk such things for love; Gravier offered a Spanish anecdote of a party in Madrid when the surgeon‑major told of being kidnapped to deliver a woman’s child in secret.

He described being forced, blindfolded, into a house where the husband snored nearby, the woman in labor veiled and biting a handkerchief, the lover and waiting‑woman attending. Though the infant proved dead, the surgeon cut and saved the mother. The Spaniard lover, distraught, carried off the body, while the surgeon later, haunted by fear, saw the Spaniard’s burning eyes in the garden; the surgeon was later murdered—stabbed by the lady’s husband—after being shown the amputated arm of someone bearing a telltale mole.

Gravier added that during the restoration of Ferdinand VII, he met a Spanish grandee with a motionless right arm and a lovely wife whose past horrors might explain its loss. The company agreed Spain bred strange passions.

De Clagny then related a French crime: a Tours woman, whose husband disappeared during the Loire army’s disbandment and who had been devout, confessed to bleeding him to death, salting the body, packing it into casks, and throwing pieces into the Loire; the head remained pickled, discovered by the examining judge. She was condemned and executed, her earlier repentance having softened public feeling. Bianchon and the others reflected on the hidden tragedies behind private life and the difficulty of judging crimes between spouses.

At bedtime the bachelors gathered in a passage, candle in hand, to discuss their plan. Gravier revealed the scheme: they would seal the doors of Madame de la Baudraye and the Public Prosecutor to catch any nocturnal tryst. Robbing barefoot like thieves, Bianchon, Lousteau, Gravier, and Gatien stuck hairs across the two doors with wax—a trick common in country houses—and agreed to inspect them at five. To their amazement the hairs were still intact on both doors in the morning.

“This alters the matter!” cried Lousteau. If Dinah were virtuous, she was worth the effort. He resolved to press his suit.

In the garden Lousteau found Madame de la Baudraye and engaged her. She invited him to La Baudraye to learn more of provincial life and declared provincials simple but true. Etienne told her he had only teased Gatien into confessing his adoration; her silence at dinner had betrayed an indiscretion, he said, but he assured her her innocence was proven. “If you fancied the magistrate,” he added, “you’d have lost my respect. I love perfection; I cannot believe the Public Prosecutor matches you.”

Madame de la Baudraye’s manner warmed. Lousteau spoke frankly of his life: he had sought pleasure but never loved; perhaps he had defects that made him less lovable. Dinah heard Paris through him—its daring axioms and decadence—and was intrigued. Etienne, acting the Parisian wit, charmed her with tales of the capital; she spent one of her happiest days with him and Bianchon.

Lousteau flattered her by disparaging a great female celebrity of Le Berry, implying Dinah could rival her; this intoxicated Dinah. Clagny, Gravier, and Gatien thought she was warmer to Etienne than before. Their exaggerated praise spread through Sancerre; sixteen people came to Anzy that evening, some in family coaches, others in wagonettes and on hired horses. Dinah, expecting triumph, lit up the huge drawing‑room and unveiled the furniture; the Parisians studied the arrivals’ attitudes and speeches.

Visitors displayed ancestral laces and faded honors; the Présidente Boirouge complained of chest pains to Bianchon, who prescribed “tea after dinner” as an English remedy. Conversation turned to Bianchon’s possible candidacy for elections; Lousteau, recalling the doctor’s fame, proposed him as a candidate, but Bianchon demurred—science and law differ. Madame Chandier and the Mayor’s wife compared incomes and boots; Gatien boasted Lousteau made twenty thousand francs a year.

The footman brought letters and proofs; Lousteau left a packet for Bianchon. Dinah picked up a printers’ wrapper, surprised to find literature pursuing them. Bianchon noticed a used proof folded round the journal proofs: a serial romance, “Olympia, or Roman Revenge.” He handed it to Lousteau, who read a page aloud. From the fragment—brigands in a cavern, a vanished Rinaldo, a Duchess who forgets her glove—the Parisians sat amused while Lousteau mocked the author’s lack of “local color,” and Bianchon defended censored writers. The provincial ladies and men stared at Lousteau, half scandalized, half entertained.

Thus the evening passed—stories, parodies, and flirtations weaving the lives of Parisians and provincials at the Château d’Anzy.

The Public Prosecutor’s wife, who, in Monsieur Gravier’s phrase, might have put a Cossack to flight in 1814, sat up like a rider in his stirrups and made a face at her neighbor as if to say, “They are looking at us; we must smile as if we understood.”

“Charming!” said the Mayoress to Gatien. “Pray go on, Monsieur Lousteau.”

Lousteau glanced at the two women, forced a smile, and prefaced his reading with “Attention!” He then resumed: a rustle of dress, and Cardinal Borborigano confronting the Duchess—his face gloomy, his brow dark, a bitter smile in his wrinkles—saying she was under suspicion and should flee whether guilty or not. The Duchess thanked him, saying the Duke of Bracciano would reappear when she wanted to prove he was alive.

“Cardinal Borborigano!” cried Bianchon, enchanted by the name. Dinah, pitying the eighteen faces watching Lousteau, said she could see the whole plot: Rome, a murdered husband, a wife bold and wicked sleeping over a volcano of crime, always fearing discovery. Lousteau elaborated: a French Adolphe embraced by the Duchess, Rinaldo lurking behind the scenes like some melodramatic conspirator; images of Victor Hugo and Pixerecourt were invoked.

The company gaped. Madame de la Baudraye and others urged Lousteau to continue. He read fragments and stage directions, stopping to explain the printers’ proofs: a waste sheet printed on one side only, a made-up sheet following it, numbers indicating volume and sheet, tailed with a block engraving. From these hints Lousteau inferred the romance’s form—four duodecimo volumes, second edition—and that the narrative was advancing toward a conclusion.

He translated a jumble of quoted lines and stage directions: the Duchess’s key, Rinaldo’s substitution of a similar key, the terror of a lost key leading to death, and a tailpiece by Normand cut by Duplat. He teased the audience with the missing corresponding page and jumped to a later complete sheet labeled IV.; J, which moved the action ahead: Rinaldo steals into the Baths of Vespasian, finds a concealed door, then realizes he is imprisoned. Lousteau pointed out that the made-up sheet jumped pages—what had been 212 led to 217—showing how the story progressed.

He read on: Rinaldo fled to what he thought was the cellar where the Bracciano family’s treasures lay; he found a door and vanished, then sensed an awful fact—he was imprisoned. The narrator described Rinaldo’s panic and his hollow roar “like that of a hyaena,” provoking Lousteau to comment on such expressions in Empire literature—how direct and blunt it was, sketchy rather than ornate, terse where modern work elaborates character. Bianchon supplied a defense of modern criticism: readers now demand invention, style, thought, learning, and feeling; critics, though often envious, chase defects because they cannot create.

Ignoring protests to remain on point, Lousteau continued. A deep groan answered Rinaldo’s cry; a voice called “Santa Maria!” and a dialogue ensued. Rinaldo mocked, then heard a voice claim to be the Duke of Bracciano. The Duke, speaking from darkness, guided Rinaldo through the cell until Rinaldo felt iron bars and realized the voice was in a cage. The Duke was a prisoner, confined thirty months, unable to sit, raised nightly by a counterpoise under the Duchess’s bedroom so she could taunt him when she embraced her lover. He had been tricked in a duel of wits with his wife; while plotting revenge, he had been trapped and left to live on a piece of bread; she showed him the poison he had prepared, withheld it, and displayed her lover in his arms.

The Duke begged for alliance; Rinaldo, the brigand, revealed he had files and pincers and had filed through one bar. The Duke pleaded for a dagger, and Rinaldo lent it; the spring clattered, the Duke shot up, and Rinaldo, astonished at the Duke’s haste to leave, muttered that he had sworn never to injure a woman. Lousteau broke in with printerly observations: another tailpiece, a Cupid on a snail, a blank page 230, then “Conclusion.”

The fragment’s conclusion was brief and passionate: the Duchess, more lovely than ever, freshly bathed and goddess-like, finds Adolphe voluptuously reclining; they exchange professions of love, and she presses the springs, opening the concealed passage. Lousteau lamented that the foreman had torn off the rest of the proof in wrapping, but declared the author full of promise.

The company’s reactions varied. Gatien Boirouge and Monsieur Gravier confessed confusion, while Lousteau insisted the piece was typical of Empire-era romance. Monsieur Gravier suggested the brigand’s graceful talk betrayed an author ignorant of Italy; Madame Gorju disparaged the fiction and praised Bianchon’s prescriptions. Bianchon, looking at Mademoiselle Euphemie Gorju, defended leniency: they had only twenty pages of a thousand.

Lousteau and Bianchon speculated about the author: perhaps not a Councillor of State but a woman—Madame Hadot—citing women’s greater extravagance of invention: Frankenstein, Leone Leoni, Ann Radcliffe. Dinah glared at Monsieur de Clagny when he applied such examples, feeling the slight abroad in his choice.

Little La Baudraye, commenting on revenge, noted that society’s harsher penalties often suffice: a cuckolded woman might be reduced to penury and ostracism rather than killed. The little man, practical as ever, said a woman’s loss of finery and respect was the sum of misery; Madame de la Baudraye added that she might still have happiness because she had a lover. Monsieur de la Baudraye, blowing out his candle, returned that the woman had a lover, and Bianchon and Lousteau exchanged wry asides.

Dinah’s small laugh and knowledge of Louise’s remarks betrayed to Bianchon the inner life of Madame de la Baudraye; he understood, from her premature wrinkles and knowing smile, something of her past. But Dinah did not catch the full innuendo of her husband’s quip; little La Baudraye had seen a swift flash of tenderness in her eyes when she looked at the journalist. Despite his own virtues, Bianchon observed, a man like Lousteau—poet, journalist, libertine—had that tinsel of intelligence and freedom that attracted women who wanted to feel chosen, desired, and remembered.

Lousteau noticed Dinah’s superiority to the provincial visitors—her dress, grace, and complexion shone by candlelight against a background of shy, ill-dressed faces. As the party dwindled and the Sancerre visitors prepared for their hour’s drive, only Monsieur de Clagny, Monsieur Lebas, Gatien, and Monsieur Gravier remained to sleep at Anzy. Lousteau, his opinion of Dinah changing since their meeting, accepted the provincial setting but had his attention drawn by her personality. Madame de la Baudraye’s audacious prophecy—that Lousteau would be the happy man of Dinah’s choice—seemed in play.

The rest of the evening passed pleasantly in small-party epigrams at the expense of the Sancerre visitors. Bianchon, going to bed with Lousteau in a large room, told him frankly: “You will be the happy man of this woman’s choice.” Lousteau demurred, yet the doctor observed how appealing a man reputed to have had many mistresses can be; a woman likes to feel she will be longer remembered than others, to appeal to experience, to have her lover’s reputation confirm his value. Bianchon predicted Dinah's beauty would be enhanced by love, and, pragmatically, that a child would secure her fortune. Lousteau declared making love to her might be an act of virtue and, with Bianchon’s help, hinted at plans for the next day.

Next morning, Madame de la Baudraye decided to take Bianchon as far as Cosne, dropping her mother at La Baudraye, continuing to Cosne with the Parisians, and returning alone with Etienne. She was elegantly dressed; Lousteau had put on his “war-paint” clothes, the studied Parisian look. Monsieur de Clagny and Monsieur Gravier felt out of place as they watched the Parisians climb into the carriage. Monsieur de la Baudraye waved from above, his little hand, and the men joked about escorting them.

Gatien returned from the stables and joined the chaise, a prudent escort. Dinah sighed that in thirteen years of marriage she had never had three hours’ liberty. Lousteau, ever a wit, quipped an anecdote about Michaud setting out for the Holy Land, retorting to those who cited his marriage that he was “so little married.” Even the rigid Madame Piedefer allowed a smile.

Dinah teased that Monsieur de Clagny might mount her pony to complete the escort. Bianchon, to free her from Gatien, suggested a simple plan: ask Gatien to go back to Anzy for some lecture notes Bianchon had left on his table. That little plot delighted Madame de la Baudraye; from the road between Anzy and Sancerre the Loire could be seen like a noble lake, and the drive was agreeable. Dinah, content at being understood, found herself discussing love in theory with the journalist. Lousteau spoke with a tone of refined corruption: love obeys no law; it is varied by character and circumstance; any woman, however firm, might be carried away in a few hours by an internal whirlwind known only to God. He declared this the key to all the adventures they had discussed over the last three days.

Throughout the reading and the subsequent talk, Lousteau’s literary commentaries punctuated the narrative. He mocked the slovenly printers of the Quay and their lack of correctors, concluding they did not care for the refined art of making-up a book. He contrasted the terse, outline-based style of Empire romances—where Lubin loves Toinette, Toinette refuses, Lubin kills and is guillotined—with modern writers who reveal inner life. Bianchon agreed, noting criticism’s evolution: readers demand five literary senses and punish works that lack them; critics, lacking creation, denounce the incomplete. This controversy, Lousteau teased, was the history of contemporary literary quarrels: masters like Walter Scott combined the senses, leaving lesser writers to lament.

As the Sancerre guests took offense at what they deemed a Parisian joke—feeling mocked for not following the proofs—Madame Piedefer urged her daughter to pacify the ladies. Provincial folks are quick to anger when they suspect they have been made to look ridiculous. Dinah’s distinct appeal set her apart in the drawing-room, making Lousteau’s interest natural. He accepted the beauty of the château and the antique splendor, but it was Dinah’s personality that completed the picture. Thus, by the time the visitors left, his mind had shifted from amusement toward attraction.

Bianchon’s assessment of Madame de la Baudraye’s case was succinct: he had seen in a few signs what might otherwise take years to discover. Dinah’s glance at Lousteau—little, luminous, signifying a readiness to be moved—had told him enough. In private he told Lousteau as much, who, though amused, seemed to plan on acting with Bianchon’s assistance.

Their evening ended with a mixture of gossip, literary theory, and the implicit movement of attraction. The reading had unveiled a tragic, melodramatic tale of entrapment and revenge—the Duke in his marble cage, nightly mocked by his wife, the brigand Rinaldo’s reluctant help—while the society around the story displayed small-town temper, critical pretension, and the social mechanics of flirtation. As they prepared to travel the next day, the conversation about love’s unpredictability and power closed the circle: Lousteau’s claim that love is the key to all such adventures left the women and men reflecting on the thin line between plotting in fiction and plotting in life.

For three days Dinah’s imagination bristled with perilous romances, fed by the Parisians’ talk. Lousteau watched, ready to seize the moment when her hidden readiness would overcome irresolution.

Dinah intended to show La Baudraye to her two visitors. Gatien rode off to obey his master; Madame Piedefer went shopping; Dinah went to Cosne alone with Lousteau and Bianchon. Lousteau sat by Dinah while Bianchon rode behind, both men pitying the fate of so fine a nature in vulgar surroundings. Bianchon mocked the Public Prosecutor and Gatien, bolstering Lousteau’s case with scorn for the provincials.

Crossing the Loire, Bianchon told Dinah she had been attracted by Etienne; he loved her, but was frivolous, poor, and tied to Paris, while she was rooted in Sancerre. He urged prudence: make Lousteau a friend, accept three visits a year, and secure beauty, happiness, and fortune. He warned that without love she would grow old and hideous—a blunt, paternal argument that left Dinah defenseless. Though she protested that she would not be a mere passerby’s mistress, Etienne’s approach interrupted her dissembling; he seemed to study Cosne as he came near.

Bianchon gathered his luggage and prepared to leave, giving Dinah a final, almost paternal blessing and allowing Lousteau to take her hand. The touch thrilled Dinah; Lousteau felt triumph. He imagined her managing her frock as a sign of choosing him, worrying over the muslin’s suitability. Bianchon quietly departed, advising Dinah to go home, and Lousteau, indifferent and audacious, shook his hand.

On the return to La Baudraye, Lousteau abruptly declared his love. Dinah, trembling, protested that she would not entrust the best of herself to a man who would see her as a transient pleasure. Gatien’s arrival forced things: Lousteau insisted Gatien go back to the inn, then deliberately messed Dinah’s muslin so she would not be presentable—an outrageous act he called a “trick” to spare her reputation. Gatien, baffled and hurt, was sent away; Dinah, humiliated, fled and burst into tears. Lousteau followed, threw himself at her feet, and promised devotion.

Madame Piedefer arrived; Lousteau suggested the mother find another dress for Dinah. Hearing Gatien’s return, Dinah and her mother withdrew to change into something warmer, as Bianchon had advised. Gatien, bewildered by contradictory explanations—muslin torn on a nail or trampled by Lousteau—felt mocked. Dinah reappeared in velvet, had to mask coldness with Lousteau’s presence, and was escorted to the carriage with him. Lousteau professed to renounce Paris and linger in Sancerre for her sake, a rhetoric that, though insincere to Gatien, struck Dinah and made her laugh—an apology, and a surrender.

That evening gentlemen whispered: she had gone out in muslin and returned in velvet. The muslin story became scandal; the town joked, scandalized, or condemned. The Presidente Boirouge fulminated that Dinah’s poetry proved she must live what she wrote; others muttered that virtue was fine until happiness arrived, then Calumny took over. Monsieur de Clagny defended her, claiming she and Lousteau worked on some great project, but was mocked.

October’s beauty seemed to help Dinah: as Bianchon predicted, genuine passion bloomed. In a month she changed—alive, discovering dormant qualities, feeling Lousteau as an angel and delighting in being loved for herself. The vast château and grounds favored the romance. Lousteau, who appreciated her artlessness and originality, played the role of consummate seducer, supplying every rehearsed flourish of passion. Seeing the affair as temporary, he nonetheless strove to imprint himself on her memory with eloquent, elaborate tenderness. Their long walks and evenings together deepened mutual feeling; when two lovers exhaust every stride of their duet yet remain enamored, their love is real.

Lousteau’s time was limited—he planned to leave Anzy in early November. The day before his departure, the master of the house showed them the château’s front bearing the arms of Uxelles; he wanted to replace them with his own newly created coat of arms, a sign of his newly established entail and pride. Lousteau teased, and the old man’s comment that Dinah was still young—there was time—made Lousteau smile without grasping his meaning.

They postponed farewells; Dinah accompanied Lousteau visibly as far as Cosne, and in the meantime extracted from him many solemn promises and pledges. Back at La Baudraye, she later told Monsieur de Clagny, Gatien, and Gravier—each in turn—that Lousteau had taught her she had not been loved for herself, and she made a noble speech about man and base passions. She accepted Clagny’s steadfast devotion as confidant and lavished on him the marks of friendship a woman can give to a suitor willing to play the part of adoring, submissive companion.

Thus Dinah’s brief, passionate conversion to love—engineered, encouraged, and complicated by Lousteau’s artful audacity and Bianchon’s counsel—transformed her social standing and inner life, provoking scandal in Sancerre even as it led her to embrace a new identity shaped by affection and theatrical devotion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In Paris again, Lousteau in a few weeks had lost the impression of the happy time he had spent at the Chateau d’Anzy. The reason was simple: he lived by his pen.

In this century, especially since the bourgeois triumph—the thrifty citizen who avoids imitating Francis I or Louis XIV—to live by the pen is a kind of penal servitude. To live by the pen means to create daily—or to seem to create; imitation costs as dearly as reality. Besides his weekly newspaper piece, which came every Monday like Sisyphus’s stone on his pen, Etienne worked for three or four literary magazines. Do not be alarmed; he put no artistic conscientiousness into his work. This man of Sancerre had a facility—carelessness, if you will—that made him a mere scrivener, a literary hack. In Paris, hack-work cuts a man off from pretensions to a literary position; when he can do no more or no longer cares for advancement, the man who can write becomes a journalist and a hack.

The life he led was not without pleasure. Blue-stockings, beginners in every walk of life, actresses, publishers and authors all made much of his ready pen. Lousteau, a thorough man about town, lived at little expense beyond paying his rent. He had boxes at all the theatres; the sale of the books he reviewed or left unreviewed paid for his gloves; and he would say to authors who published at their own expense, “I have your book always in my hands!” He took toll from vanity in drawings or pictures. Every day had engagements to dinner, every night its theatre, every morning callers, visits, and lounging. His newspaper serial, two novels a year for weeklies, and miscellaneous articles were the tax for this easy life. Yet to reach this position Etienne had struggled for ten years.

Known in the literary world, liked for mischief and easy good humor, he let himself float with the stream, never planning for the future. He ruled a small circle of newcomers and kept fifteen-year habits with those he supped and dined with, indulging his wit alongside them. He earned seven to eight hundred francs a month, a sum he found insufficient for the prodigality peculiar to the impecunious. Indeed, Lousteau was now as hard up as when, on first appearing in Paris, he had told himself, “If I had but five hundred francs a month, I should be rich!”

The cause was this: Lousteau lived in the Rue des Martyrs in handsome ground-floor rooms with a garden, splendidly furnished. When he settled there in 1833 he had struck a deal with an upholsterer that kept his pocket money low for a long time. These rooms were let for twelve hundred francs. January, April, July, and October were, as he phrased it, his indigent months; the rent and the porter’s account cleaned him out. Lousteau took no fewer hackney cabs, spent a hundred francs on breakfasts, smoked thirty francs’ worth of cigars, and could never refuse the mistress of a day a dinner or a new dress. He dipped so deeply into the fluctuating earnings of following months that he could no more find a hundred francs on his chimney-piece now, making seven or eight hundred francs a month, than he could in 1822, when he was hardly getting two hundred.

Sometimes tired by the incessant vicissitudes of a literary life, and as much bored by amusement as a courtesan, Lousteau would get out of the tideway and sit on the bank, and say to one or another of his intimates—Nathan or Bixiou—who sat smoking in his small garden, looking out on an evergreen lawn as big as a dinner-table:

“What will be the end of us? White hairs are giving us respectful hints!”

“Lord! we shall marry when we choose to give as much thought to the matter as we give to a drama or a novel,” said Nathan.

“And Florine?” retorted Bixiou.

“Oh, we all have a Florine,” said Etienne, flinging away his cigar and thinking of Madame Schontz.

Madame Schontz was pretty enough to put a very high price on the interest of her beauty while reserving absolute ownership for Lousteau, the man of her heart. Like all the women called Lorettes, from the Church of Notre-Dame de Lorette around which they dwell, she lived in the Rue Flechier, a stone’s throw from Lousteau. This lady took pride in teasing friends by boasting of having a Wit for her lover.

These details of Lousteau’s life and fortune are indispensable, for this penury and bohemian existence—where Parisian luxury had become a necessity—was fated to have a cruel influence on Dinah’s life. Those familiar with Parisian bohemia will understand how it was that, by the end of a fortnight, the journalist, deep in his literary environment, could laugh about his Baroness with his friends and even with Madame Schontz. To those who regard such things as mean, it is almost useless to make excuses they will not accept.

“What did you do at Sancerre?” asked Bixiou the first time he met Lousteau.

“I did good service to three worthy provincials—a Receiver-General of Taxes, a little cousin of his, and a Public Prosecutor, who for ten years had been dancing round one of the hundred ‘Tenth Muses’ who adorn the Departments,” said he. “But they had no more dared to touch her than we touch a decorated cream at dessert till some strong-minded person has made a hole in it.”

“Poor boy!” said Bixiou. “I said you had gone to Sancerre to turn Pegasus out to grass.”

“Your joke is as stupid as my Muse is handsome,” retorted Lousteau. “Ask Bianchon, my dear fellow.”

“A Muse and a Poet! A homeopathic cure then!” said Bixiou.

On the tenth day Lousteau received a letter with the Sancerre postmark.

“Good! very good!” said Lousteau.

“‘Beloved friend, idol of my heart and soul----’ twenty pages of it! all at one sitting, and dated midnight! She writes when she finds herself alone. Poor woman! Ah, ha! And a postscript—

“‘I dare not ask you to write to me as I write, every day; still, I hope to have a few lines from my dear one every week, to relieve my mind.’—What a pity to burn it all! it is really well written,” said Lousteau to himself, as he threw the ten sheets of paper into the fire after reading them. “That woman was born to reel off copy!”

Lousteau was not much afraid of Madame Schontz, who really loved him for himself, but he had supplanted a friend in the heart of a Marquise. This Marquise, a lady not at all coy, sometimes dropped in unexpectedly at his rooms in the evening, arriving veiled in a hackney coach; and she, as a literary woman, allowed herself to hunt through all his drawers.

A week later, Lousteau, who hardly remembered Dinah, was startled by another budget from Sancerre—eight leaves, sixteen pages! He heard a woman’s step; he thought it announced a search from the Marquise, and tossed these rapturous proofs of affection into the fire—unread!

“A woman’s letter!” exclaimed Madame Schontz, as she came in. “The paper, the wax, are scented—”

“Here you are, sir,” said a porter from the coach office, setting down two huge hampers in the ante-room. “Carriage paid. Please to sign my book.”

“Carriage paid!” cried Madame Schontz. “It must have come from Sancerre.”

“Yes, madame,” said the porter.

“Your Tenth Muse is a remarkably intelligent woman,” said the courtesan, opening one hamper while Lousteau signed. “I like a Muse who understands housekeeping, and who can make game pies as well as blots. And, oh! what beautiful flowers!” she went on, opening the second hamper. “Why, you could get none finer in Paris!—And here, and here! A hare, partridges, half a roebuck!—We will ask your friends and have a famous dinner, for Athalie has a special talent for dressing venison.”

Lousteau wrote to Dinah; but instead of writing from the heart, he was clever. The letter was insidious; it was like one of Mirabeau’s letters to Sophie. The style of a true lover is transparent. It is a clear stream that shows the bottom of the heart between two banks, bright with trifles of existence, and covered with the flowers of the soul that blossom afresh every day, full of intoxicating beauty—but only for two. As soon as a love letter has any charm for a third reader, it is beyond doubt the product of the head, not of the heart. But a woman will always be beguiled; she always believes herself to be the determining cause of this flow of wit.

By the end of December Lousteau had ceased to read Dinah’s letters; they lay in a heap in a drawer of his chest that was never locked, under his shirts, from which they scented.

Then one of those chances came to Lousteau which such bohemians ought to clutch by every hair. In the middle of December, Madame Schontz, who took a real interest in Etienne, sent to beg him to call one morning on business.

“My dear fellow, you have a chance of marrying.”

“I can marry very often, happily, my dear.”

“When I say marrying, I mean marrying well. You have no prejudices: I need not mince matters. This is the position: A young lady has got into trouble; her mother knows nothing of even a kiss. Her father is an honest notary, a man of honor; he has been wise enough to keep it dark. He wants to get his daughter married within a fortnight, and he will give her a fortune of a hundred and fifty thousand francs—for he has three other children; but—and—it is not a bad idea—he will add a hundred thousand francs, under the rose, hand to hand, to cover the damages. They are an old family of Paris citizens, Rue des Lombards—”

“Well, then, why does not the lover marry her?”

“Dead.”

“What a romance! Such things are nowhere to be heard of but in the Rue des Lombards.”

“But do not take it into your head that a jealous brother murdered the seducer. The young man died in the most commonplace way of a pleurisy caught as he came out of the theatre. A head-clerk and penniless, the man entrapped the daughter to marry into the business—A judgment from heaven, I call it!”

“Where did you hear the story?”

“From Malaga; the notary is her milord.”

“What, Cardot, the son of that little old man in hair-powder, Florentine’s first friend?”

“Just so. Malaga, whose ‘fancy’ is a little tomtit of a fiddler of eighteen, cannot in conscience make such a boy marry the girl. Besides, she has no cause to do him an ill turn.—Indeed, Monsieur Cardot wants a man of thirty at least. Our notary, I feel sure, will be proud to have a famous man for his son-in-law. So just feel yourself all over.—You will pay your debts, you will have twelve thousand francs a year, and be a father without any trouble on your part; what do you say to that? And, after all, you only marry a very consolable widow. There is an income of fifty thousand francs in the house, and the value of the connection, so in due time you may look forward to not less than fifteen thousand francs a year more for your share, and you will enter a family holding a fine political position; Cardot is the brother-in-law of old Camusot, the depute who lived so long with Fanny Beaupre.”

“Yes,” said Lousteau, “old Camusot married little Daddy Cardot’s eldest daughter, and they had high times together!”

“Well!” Madame Schontz went on, “and Madame Cardot, the notary’s wife, was a Chiffreville—manufacturers of chemical products, the aristocracy of these days! Potash, I tell you! Still, this is the unpleasant side. You will have a terrible mother-in-law, a woman capable of killing her daughter if she knew! This Cardot woman is a bigot; she has lips like two faded narrow pink ribbons.

“A man of the town like you would never pass muster with that woman, who, in her well-meaning way, will spy out your bachelor life and know every fact of the past. However, Cardot says he means to exert his paternal authority. The poor man will be obliged to do the civil to his wife for some days; a woman made of wood, my dear fellow; Malaga, who has seen her, calls her a penitential scrubber. Cardot is a man of forty; he will be mayor of his district, and perhaps be elected deputy. He is prepared to give, in lieu of the hundred thousand francs, a nice little house in the Rue Saint-Lazare, with a forecourt and a garden, which cost him no more than sixty thousand at the time of the July overthrow; he would sell, and that would be an opportunity for you to go and come at the house, to see the daughter, and be civil to the mother.—And it would give you a look of property in Madame Cardot’s eyes. You would be housed like a prince in that little mansion. Then, by Camusot’s interest, you may get an appointment as librarian to some public office where there is no library.—Well, and then if you invest your money in backing up a newspaper, you will get ten thousand francs a year on it, you can earn six, your librarianship will bring in four.—Can you do better for yourself?

“If you were to marry a lamb without spot, it might be a light woman by the end of two years. What is the damage?—an anticipated dividend! It is quite the fashion.

“Take my word for it, you can do no better than come to dine with Malaga to-morrow. You will meet your father-in-law; he will know the secret has been let out—by Malaga, with whom he cannot be angry—and then you are master of the situation. As to your wife!—her misconduct leaves you as free as a bachelor—”

“Your language is as blunt as a cannon ball.”

“I love you for your own sake, that is all—and I can reason. Well! why do you stand there like a wax image of Abd-el-Kader? There is nothing to meditate over. Marriage is heads or tails—well, you have tossed heads up.”

“You shall have my reply to-morrow,” said Lousteau.

“I would sooner have it at once; Malaga will write you up to-night.”

“Well, then, yes.”

Lousteau spent the evening writing a long letter to the Marquise, giving the reasons that compelled him to marry: his constant poverty, the torpor of his imagination, his white hairs, his moral and physical exhaustion—in short, four pages of arguments. “As to Dinah, I will send her a circular announcing the marriage,” said he to himself. “As Bixiou says, I have not my match for knowing how to dock the tail of a passion.”

Lousteau, who at first had been on some ceremony with himself, by next day dreaded lest the marriage should not come off. He was pressingly civil to the notary.

“I knew monsieur your father,” said he, “at Florentine’s, so I may well know you here, at Mademoiselle Turquet’s. Like father, like son. A very good fellow and a philosopher was little Daddy Cardot—excuse me, we always called him so. At that time, Florine, Florentine, Tullia, Coralie, and Mariette were the five fingers of your hand, so to speak—it is fifteen years ago. My follies, as you may suppose, are a thing of the past.—In those days it was pleasure that ran away with me; now I am ambitious; but, in our day, to get on a man must be free from debt, have a good income, a wife, and a family. If I pay taxes enough to qualify me, I may be a deputy yet, like any other man.”

Maître Cardot appreciated this profession of faith. Lousteau had laid himself out to please and the notary liked him, feeling himself more at ease, as may be easily imagined, with a man who had known his father’s secrets than he would with another. On the following day Lousteau was introduced to the Cardot family as the purchaser of the house in the Rue Saint-Lazare, and three days later he dined there.

Cardot lived in an old house near the Place du Chatelet. In this house everything was “good.” Economy covered every scrap of gilding with green gauze; all the furniture wore holland covers. Though it was impossible to feel uneasy as to the family’s wealth, at the end of half an hour no one could suppress a yawn. Boredom perched in every nook; the curtains hung dolefully; the dining-room was like Harpagon’s. Even had Lousteau not known Malaga, he could have guessed the notary’s real life was spent elsewhere.

The journalist saw a tall, fair girl with blue eyes, at once shy and languishing. The elder brother took a fancy to him; he was the fourth clerk in the office, but strongly attracted by literary snares, though destined to succeed his father. The younger sister was twelve. Lousteau, assuming a little Jesuitical air, played the Monarchist and Churchman for the mother’s benefit: smooth, deliberate, and complimentary.

Within three weeks of their introduction, at his fourth dinner there, Félicie Cardot, who had been watching Lousteau out of the corner of her eye, carried him a cup of coffee where he stood in the window recess, and said in a low voice, with tears in her eyes:

“I will devote my whole life, monsieur, to thanking you for your sacrifice in favor of a poor girl—”

Lousteau was touched; there was so much expression in her look, her accent, her attitude. “She would make a good man happy,” thought he, pressing her hand in reply.

Madame Cardot looked upon her son-in-law as a man with a future; but, above all, she was delighted by his high tone of morals. Etienne, prompted by the wily notary, had pledged his word that he had no natural children, no tie that could endanger the happiness of her dear Félicie.

“You may perhaps think I go rather too far,” said the bigot to the journalist; “but in giving such a jewel as my Félicie to any man, one must think of the future. I am not one of those mothers who want to be rid of their daughters. Monsieur Cardot hurries matters on, urges forward his daughter’s marriage; he wishes it over. This is the only point on which we differ.—Though with a man like you, monsieur, a literary man whose youth has been preserved by hard work from the moral shipwreck now so prevalent, we may feel quite safe; still, you would be the first to laugh at me if I looked for a husband for my daughter with my eyes shut. I know you are not an innocent, and I should be very sorry for my Félicie if you were” (this in a whisper); “but if you had any liaison—For instance, monsieur, you have heard of Madame Roguin, the wife of a notary who, unhappily for our faculty, was sadly notorious. Madame Roguin has, ever since 1820, been kept by a banker—”

“Yes, du Tillet,” replied Etienne; but he bit his tongue as he recollected how rash it was to confess acquaintance with du Tillet.

“Yes.—Well, monsieur, if you were a mother, would you not quake at the thought that Madame du Tillet’s fate might be your child’s? At her age, and née de Granville! To have as a rival a woman of fifty and more. Sooner would I see my daughter dead than give her to a man who had such a connection with a married woman. A grisette, an actress, you take her and leave her.—There is no danger, in my opinion, from women of that stamp; love is their trade, they care for no one; one down and another to come on!—But a woman who has sinned against duty must hug her sin; her only excuse is constancy, if such a crime can have an excuse. At least, that is the view I hold of a respectable woman’s fall, and that is what makes it so terrible—”

Instead of seeking the meaning of these speeches, Etienne made a jest of them at Malaga’s, whither he went with his father-in-law elect; for the notary and the journalist were the best of friends.

Lousteau had already given himself the airs of importance; his life at last was to have a purpose; he was in luck’s way, and in a few days would be the owner of a delightful little house in the Rue Saint-Lazare; he was going to be married to a charming woman, he would have about twenty thousand francs a year, and could give the reins to his ambition; the young lady loved him, and he would be connected with several respectable families. In short, he was in full sail on the blue waters of hope.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Madame Cardot had asked to see the prints for Gil Blas, an illustrated volume the publishers were issuing, and Lousteau had taken the first numbers for her inspection. But the lawyer’s wife had an intention: she had borrowed the book only as an excuse to call unexpectedly on her future son-in-law. The sight of those bachelor rooms, which her husband had described as charming, would tell her more about Lousteau’s habits than any report. Madame Camusot, ignorant of the fateful secret, was terrified at such a marriage for her niece. Monsieur Camusot, a Councillor of the Supreme Court and old Camusot’s son by his first marriage, had given his stepmother, Cardot’s sister, an unflattering account of the journalist.

Lousteau, clever, did not find it strange that the wife of a rich notary should wish to examine a volume costing fifteen francs before buying. Such clever men seldom study the middle class; while he makes game of them, they are ready to throttle him.

One day in January 1837 Madame Cardot and her daughter took a hackney coach to the Rue des Martyrs to return parts of Gil Blas to Félicie’s betrothed, eager to see Lousteau’s rooms. Such domiciliary visits are common among the old bourgeoisie. The porter was out, but his daughter, on being told they had come, handed over the key—especially after Madame Cardot slipped her a gold piece.

It was about noon, the hour the journalist would return from breakfast at the Café Anglais. Crossing the open space between Notre-Dame de Lorette and the Rue des Martyrs, Lousteau fancied he saw Dinah in a hired coach going up the Rue du Faubourg-Montmartre. He stood rooted; at his door he saw her at the coach.

“What has brought you here?” he asked, in the familiar tu. Formal vous was out of the question to a woman he meant to get rid of.

“Why, my love,” she cried, “have you not read my letters?”

“Certainly I have,” he said.

“Well, then?”

“Well, then?”

“You are a father,” said the country lady.

“Faugh!” he ejaculated. He thought she must be prepared for the blow.

He told the coachman to wait, took Madame de la Baudraye’s hand, and, leaving the chaise full of trunks, vowed he would send her and her luggage back where she had come from.

“Monsieur, monsieur,” called little Pamela. The child had sense; she thought three women must not be allowed to meet in a bachelor’s rooms.

“Well, well!” said Lousteau, dragging Dinah along. Pamela, thinking the lady some relation, added, “The key is in the door; your mother-in-law is there.”

In his agitation Lousteau heard only “mother is there” and went in.

Félicie and her mother, already in the bedroom, crept into a corner on seeing Etienne enter with a woman.

“At last, Etienne, my dearest, I am yours for life!” cried Dinah, flinging her arms around him and clasping him while he turned the key. “Life is perpetual anguish to me in that house at Anzy. I could not bear it longer; and when it came for me to proclaim my happiness—well, I had not courage. Here I am, your wife with your child! And you have not written to me; you have left me two months without a line.”

“But, Dinah, you put me in the greatest difficulty—”

“Do you love me?”

“How can I do otherwise than love you? But would you not have been wiser to remain at Sancerre? I am in the most abject poverty, and I fear to drag you into it—”

“Your misery will be paradise to me. I only ask to live here, never to go out—”

“Good God! that is all very fine in words, but—” Dinah burst into tears at his roughness.

Lousteau could not resist her distress. He clasped the Baroness and kissed her.

“Do not cry, Didine!” he said; and seeing Madame Cardot’s figure in the mirror, he murmured in her ear, “Come, Didine, go with Pamela and get your trunks unloaded. Go; do not cry; we will be happy!” He led her to the door and returned to face the storm.

“Monsieur,” said Madame Cardot, “I congratulate myself on having resolved to see for myself the home of the man who was to be my son-in-law. If my daughter were to die of it, she should never be the wife of such a man as you. You must devote yourself to making your Didine happy, monsieur.” The virtuous lady then left, followed by Félicie, who wept for the loss of Etienne. Madame Cardot stared insolently at Dinah as she drove off; Dinah’s heart smarted at “that is all very fine in words,” yet, like every woman in love, she believed the murmured “Do not cry, Didine!”

Lousteau, practical from a stormy life, reflected: “Didine is high-minded; when she knows of my proposed marriage, she will sacrifice herself for my prospects, and I know how to let her know.” Delighted at this trick’s apparent certainty, he began humming to himself; and, to Dinah’s surprise, she found him dancing.

“Your arrival and our happiness have turned my head with joy,” he said, to explain his mood.

“And I fancied you had ceased to love me!” she cried, dropping her handbag and weeping with joy.

“Make yourself at home, my darling,” said Etienne laughing inwardly; “I must write two lines to excuse myself from a bachelor party, for I mean to devote myself to you. Give your orders; you are at home.”

He wrote to Bixiou:

MY DEAR BOY,—My Baroness has dropped into my arms, and will be fatal to my marriage unless we perform one of the most familiar stratagems of the thousand and one comedies. I rely on you to come here, like one of Moliere’s old men, to scold your nephew Leandre for his folly, while the Tenth Muse lies hidden in my bedroom; you must work on her feelings; strike hard, be brutal, offensive. I, you understand, shall express my blind devotion, and be deaf, so you may have to shout at me.

Come if you can at seven o’clock.

Yours,

E. LOUSTEAU.

Having sent this letter by a commissionaire to the man who delighted in such practical jokes—the artist’s “charge”—Lousteau made a show of settling the Muse of Sancerre in his rooms. He busied himself arranging her luggage and told her about the house with overflowing glee, kind words and caresses, so Dinah believed herself supremely beloved. The fashionable rooms pleased her far more than her old château.

Pamela Migeon, the intelligent fourteen-year-old, was asked if she would like to be waiting-maid to the Baroness. Pamela, delighted, ordered dinner from a boulevard restaurant. Dinah, taking possession of closets, found none of the household necessities—extreme poverty lurked under the superficial elegance of the bachelor home. She indulged in tender dreams: she would alter Étienne’s habits, make him domestic, and fill his cup of happiness.

The novelty hid the disastrous side; Dinah saw reciprocated love as absolution of her sin and did not yet look beyond the walls. Pamela, whose wits matched a lorette’s, went to Madame Schontz to borrow plate, telling her what had happened. Madame Schontz welcomed her and hurried to Malaga to warn Cardot of the catastrophe.

The journalist, not uneasy in respect to his marriage prospects, grew ever more charming to the provincial lady. Dinner was the child’s play of lovers set free. After coffee, Lousteau sat by the fire, Dinah on his knee, when Pamela ran in, scared.

“Here is Monsieur Bixiou!” she cried.

“Go into the bedroom,” said Lousteau to his mistress; “I’ll soon get rid of him. He’s one of my most intimate friends, and I must explain my new start in life.”

“Oh, ho! dinner for two, and a blue velvet bonnet!” cried Bixiou. “I am off.—Ah! that is what comes of marrying—one must go through some partings. How rich one feels when one begins to move one’s sticks, heh?”

“Who talks of marrying?” said Lousteau.

“What! are you not going to be married, then?” cried Bixiou.

“No!”

“No? My word, what next? You, who have come across twenty thousand francs a year, and a house, and a wife connected with all the first families—a wife, in short, out of the Rue des Lombards—”

“That will do, Bixiou, enough. It is at an end. Be off!”

“Be off? I have a friend’s privileges, and I shall take every advantage.—What has come over you?”

“What has come over me is my lady from Sancerre. She is a mother, and we are going to live together happily to the end of our days.—You would have heard it tomorrow, so you may as well be told it now.”

“Many chimney-pots are falling on my head, as Arnal says. But if this woman really loves you, she will go back to the place she came from. Did any provincial woman find her sea-legs in Paris? She will wound all your vanities. Have you forgotten what a provincial is? She will bore you as much when she is happy as when she is sad; she will have a talent for escaping grace as a Parisian has in inventing it.

“Lousteau, listen. A passion may make you forget the times, but I have no mythological bandage over my eyes. For fifteen years you have been tossing in the literary world; you are no longer young, you have padded the hoof till your soles are worn through! You hide your rags like a street urchin with turned socks. In short, the joke is stale. Your excuses are more familiar than a patent medicine—”

“I may say to you, like the Regent to Cardinal Dubois, ‘That is kicking enough!’” said Lousteau.

“Oh, venerable young man,” said Bixiou, “the iron has touched the sore. You are worn out, aren’t you? Well, then; in the heyday of youth, under penury, what have you done? You are not in the front rank, and you have not a thousand francs of your own. Can you, in the decline of your powers, support a family by your pen, when your wife, if honest, won’t have the resources of a woman of the streets, who can extract her thousand-franc note from the depths where milord keeps it safe? You are rushing into the lowest depths of the social theatre.

“And this is only the financial side. Consider the political position. We live in a bourgeois age, where honor and genius are summed up in paying your way, owing nobody, and conducting your affairs with judgment. Be steady, respectable, have a wife and children, pay rent and taxes, serve in the National Guard, and you may aspire to the Ministry! You were preparing to fulfill the conditions for a political personage; you are capable of the mean tricks necessary in office. And just for a woman, who will abandon you—the end of every passion—you throw away the sacred Hearth, the Rue des Lombards, a political career, thirty thousand francs per annum, respectability and respect! Ought that be the end of a man who has done with illusions?

“If you had kept a pot boiling for some actress who gave you fun for it—well; that is a cabinet matter. But to live with another man’s wife is a draft at sight on disaster; it is bolting the bitter pills of vice with none of the gilding.”

“That will do. One word answers it all; I love Madame de la Baudraye, and prefer her to every fortune and every position the world can offer. I may have been carried away by ambition, but everything must give way to the joy of being a father.”

“Ah, ha! you have a fancy for paternity? But, wretched man, we are fathers only of our legitimate children. What is a brat that does not bear your name? The last chapter of the romance.—Your child will be taken from you! We have seen that story in twenty plays these ten years past.

“Society, my dear boy, will drop upon you sooner or later. Read Adolphe.—I fancy I can see you, after you and she are used to each other: dejected, hang-dog, bereft of position and fortune, fighting like shareholders tricked by a director. Your director is happiness.”

“Say no more, Bixiou.”

“But I have only just begun,” continued Bixiou. “Listen. Marriage has been out of favor; but apart from certifying heredity and offering a chance for a penniless young man to make his fortune, it survives. There is not the man living who would not repent, sooner or later, of having lost the chance of marrying thirty thousand francs a year.”

“You won’t understand me,” cried Lousteau, exasperated. “Go away—she is there—”

“I beg your pardon; why did you not tell me sooner?—You are of age, and so is she,” he added, lowering his voice but loud enough for Dinah to hear. “She will make you repent bitterly of your happiness!—”

“If it is a folly, I intend to commit it.—Good-bye.”

“A man gone overboard!” cried Bixiou.

“Devil take those friends who think they have a right to preach,” said Lousteau, opening the bedroom door. He found Madame de la Baudraye sunk in an armchair, dabbing her eyes with an embroidered handkerchief.

“Oh, why did I come here?” she sobbed. “Good Heavens, why indeed? Etienne, I am not so provincial as you think me. You are making a fool of me.”

“Darling angel,” answered Lousteau, taking her in his arms and lifting her half-lifeless into the drawing-room, “we have both pledged our future; it is sacrifice for sacrifice. While loving you at Sancerre, they were engaging me to be married here, but I refused.—Oh! I was extremely distressed—”

“I am going,” cried Dinah, starting up.

“You will stay, my Didine. All is at an end. Is this fortune so lightly earned? Must I marry a gawky, tow-haired creature, with a red nose, the daughter of a notary, and saddle myself with a stepmother who could give Madame de Piedefer points for bigotry—”

Pamela flew in and whispered, “Madame Schontz!”

Lousteau rose, leaving Dinah on the sofa, and went out.

“It is all over with you, my dear,” Madame Schontz said. “Cardot does not mean to quarrel with his wife for the sake of a son-in-law. The lady made a scene—something like a scene! So, to conclude, the head-clerk, who had been the late head-clerk’s deputy for two years, agrees to take the girl with the business.”

“Mean wretch!” exclaimed Lousteau. “What! in two hours he has decided?”

“Bless me, that is simple enough. The rascal, knowing all the dead man’s little secrets, guessed your master’s fix from overhearing a few words of the squabble with Madame Cardot. The notary relies on your honor and good feeling, for the affair is settled. The clerk, whose conduct has been admirable, even went so far as to attend mass! A finished hypocrite—just suits the mamma. You and Cardot will still be friends. He is to be a director in an immense financial concern, and he may be useful to you.—So you have been waked from a sweet dream.”

“I have lost a fortune, a wife, and—”

“And a mistress,” said Madame Schontz, smiling. “Here you are, more than married; you will be insufferable, always wanting to get home, nothing loose about you, neither your clothes nor your habits. And, after all, my Arthur does things in style. I will be faithful to him and cut Malaga’s acquaintance.

“Let me peep at her through the door—your Sancerre Muse,” she went on. “Is there no finer bird than that to be found in the desert? You are cheated! She is dignified, lean, lachrymose; she only needs Lady Dudley’s turban!”

What is it now?” asked Madame de la Baudraye, hearing the rustle of silk and a woman’s murmur.

“It is, my darling, that we are now indissolubly united.—I have had an answer to the letter you saw me write, which was to break off my marriage—”

“So that was the party you gave up?”

“Yes.”

“Oh, I will be more than your wife—I am your slave, I give you my life,” said the deluded creature. “I did not believe I could love you more than I did! Now I shall not be a mere incident, but your whole life.”

“Yes, my beautiful, my generous Didine.”

“Swear to me,” she begged, “that only death shall divide us.”

Lousteau was ready with the most fascinating vows. Between the apartment door where he had taken the lorette’s farewell kiss and the drawing-room where the Muse lay bewildered by shocks, he had remembered little De la Baudraye’s precarious health, his fine fortune, and Bianchon’s remark about Dinah—“She will be a rich widow!”—and he said to himself, “I would a hundred times rather have Madame de la Baudraye for a wife than Félicie!”

His plan was quickly fixed: he determined to play the farce of passion again, and to perfection. His mean self-interest and false vehemence had disastrous effects. Madame de la Baudraye had intended, on coming to Paris, to live in rooms of her own near Lousteau; but the proofs of his devotion—his giving up brilliant prospects—and the perfection of the first days of their illicit union kept her from suggesting a parting. The second day was to be, and was, a high festival; such a proposal then would have been a discord.

Lousteau, anxious to make Dinah feel dependent, kept her in a state of intoxication with incessant amusement. These circumstances prevented two clever people from avoiding the slough they fell into—that of a life in common, a folly common in Parisian literary circles.

Thus the whole programme of a provincial amour, satirically described by Lousteau to Madame de la Baudraye, was enacted—a fact neither he nor she now remembered. Passion is born deaf and mute.

################################################
This winter in Paris was to Madame de la Baudraye all that October had been at Sancerre. Etienne, to initiate “his wife” into Paris life, varied their honeymoon with evenings at the theatre, where Dinah would only occupy the stage box. At first she preserved traces of provincial modesty; afraid of being seen, she hid her happiness and would timidly say, “Monsieur de Clagny or Monsieur Gravier may have followed me to Paris.” Even in the capital she felt fearful, as if still in Sancerre.

Lousteau, excessively vain, educated Dinah, took her to the best dressmakers, and pointed out fashionable models. Her provincial look soon vanished. Lousteau’s friends congratulated him on his conquest.

During that season Etienne wrote little and ran deeply into debt, though Dinah, proud, bought her clothes from her own savings, fancying she had cost him nothing. After three months she was acclimatized to Paris: she revelled in the Italian opera, knew the plays, actors, and jests of the day, and was inured to the constant excitement and forgetfulness of city life. She no longer stood astonished at every novelty; she breathed that quick, witty atmosphere in which clever people thrive.

One morning, reading the papers Lousteau collected, two lines carried her back to Sancerre:

“Monsieur le Baron de Clagny, Public Prosecutor to the Criminal Court at Sancerre, has been appointed Deputy Public Prosecutor to the Supreme Court in Paris.”

“How well that worthy lawyer loves you!” the journalist sneered.

“Poor man!” she answered. “What did I tell you? He is following me.”

Etienne and Dinah were then in that dazzling stage of passion when each is comfortably accustomed to the other yet freshness remains. They knew each other but had not explored every secret; enchantment reigned, with no collisions. Dinah used love’s hyperbole:

“When you cease to love me, kill me.—If you should cease to love me, I believe I could kill you first and myself after.”

Lousteau joked of testing constancy and proposed they each write letters to justify suicide in case of infidelity, but neither ever did. He promised himself he would deceive her when tired of her, while feeling the yoke. Dinah’s consent to their union was a generous act of self-respect. In intimacy she never surrendered her modesty, rectitude, or the ambition that formed her character. Lousteau involuntarily held her in high esteem. As a Parisian she could be superior to the most fascinating courtesan by virtue of information, habits of mind, and reading; her wit generalized while courtesans dazzled a narrow circle.

“There is in Dinah,” Etienne told Bixiou, “the stuff to make both a Ninon and a De Staël.”
“A woman who combines an encyclopaedia and a seraglio is very dangerous,” Bixiou mocked.

When the expected infant became visible, Madame de la Baudraye prepared to shut herself away; but before doing so she insisted on attending the first performance of a new play by Nathan—a literary ceremonial that occupied the two thousand people who constituted “all Paris.” Dinah, never before at a first night, was curious. By then she gloried in her misconduct and wanted to brave society.

She dressed perfectly, matching her delicate features and pallid complexion; but misfortune awaited. The box given to Lousteau was next to that of Anna Grossetete; the two friends did not even bow. At the end of the first act Lousteau left his seat, abandoning Dinah to the scrutiny of opera-glasses, while Anna’s companions received the most distinguished men.

Dinah’s solitude distressed her; she could not put on a studied air, lapsed into provincial awkwardness, and between the second and third acts a man entered her box—Monsieur de Clagny.

“I am happy to see you, to tell you how pleased I am by your promotion,” she said.

“Oh! Madame, for whom should I come to Paris----?”

“What! have I anything to do with your appointment?”

“Everything. Since you left Sancerre it had been intolerable to me; I was dying—”

“Your friendship does me good,” she answered, holding out her hand. “I value my true friends now. I feared I had lost your esteem, but your visit touches me more deeply than ten years’ attachment.”

The lawyer, moved, confessed his unease about Dinah’s position.

“You are an object of curiosity to the whole house,” he said. “Could you not be happy and remain honored? I hear you are Monsieur Etienne Lousteau’s mistress, living as man and wife. You have broken forever with society; even if you should marry your lover later, you will feel the want of respectability you now despise. Ought you not live at home with your mother, who would protect you?”

“I am in the wrong to have come here, that is all.—I have bid farewell to the advantages society confers. My abnegation is so complete I wish I could clear a vast space to make a desert of my love, full of God, of him, and of myself. We have sacrificed much; we are united, indissolubly if you will, by disgrace. I am happy; I can love freely, and confide in you.”

Moved, the magistrate replied in heartrending tones:

“I wanted to see that you were loved: I shall be at ease. But will your lover appreciate your sacrifice? Is there gratitude in his affection?”

“Come to the Rue des Martyrs and you will see!”

“Yes, I will call,” he answered. “There are glorious exceptions in the literary world, but these men of letters drag terrible evils; publicity is one of the greatest. A woman may commit herself with—”

“With a Public Prosecutor?” the Baronne quipped.

“Well! with a more or less famous man the public is informed. Look: you are sitting back-to-back with the Comtesse Marie Vandenesse, who nearly committed folly for a more celebrated man than Lousteau—Nathan—and now they do not even know each other. She was saved; she kept her house; but she was the talk of the town a whole winter. Yet her husband’s fortune and name and his admirable conduct preserved her.”

“And how was Sancerre when you came away?” Dinah asked to change the subject.

“Monsieur de la Baudraye said your confinement made it necessary you should be in Paris, and insisted on the first physicians. So, in spite of the commotion, you still have your legal status.”

“Why!” she exclaimed, “can Monsieur de la Baudraye still hope----”

“Your husband did what he always does—made a little calculation.”

The lawyer left the box when Lousteau returned, bowing with dignity.

“You are a greater hit than the piece,” Etienne said to Dinah.

That brief triumph brought Dinah more happiness than any provincial success, yet she was grave as they left. Three days later she noticed a cloud on Lousteau’s brow and learned their household was penniless, two quarters’ rent owing and on the eve of execution. The reality pierced her; she repented tempting Etienne into extravagance. It is hard to pass from pleasure to work; happiness had wrecked more poems than sorrow ever inspired.

Dinah, used to never owing a sou, was informed through Migeon (Pamela’s father) that she could pawn jewels; her “uncle” advanced nine hundred francs. She kept three hundred for baby clothes and expenses and joyfully gave Lousteau the rest as he wrestled through a novel for a periodical.

“Finish without sacrificing to necessity,” she urged. “Polish the style; work up the subject. I will be housewife and attend to business.”

For months Etienne had taken Dinah every day to the Café Riche; a corner was always kept for them. She was dismayed to find five hundred francs owing for the last fortnight.

“What! have we been drinking wine at six francs a bottle? A sole Normande costs five francs!” she cried.

“Well, it makes little difference whether we are robbed at a restaurant or by a cook,” Lousteau shrugged.

She insisted that for the cost of his dinners he might be frugal. Having obtained a kitchen and two servants’ rooms, she wrote to her mother for linen and a loan of a thousand francs. Her mother sent two trunks of linen, some plate, and two thousand francs by a pious cook.

Ten days after the theatre evening Monsieur de Clagny called at four and found Dinah making a little cap. The sight of this proud woman condescending to needlework moved him; the pricks on one taper finger, which he had often kissed, told him she was not merely playing the maternal part. In that first interview he saw to the depths of her soul: she meant to be the journalist’s guardian spirit, to lead him into a nobler road. Dinah had understood that Lousteau’s practical difficulties sprang from moral defects—indolence and lack of will—habits that obstruct talent. She strove to make him happy by economy and method; she became housekeeper as she had become consort of a poet, by loftiness of soul.

“His happiness will be my absolution,” she told Clagny.

Etienne had flaunted their triumph at Nathan’s first night, revealing that to him Madame de la Baudraye was “a fine feather in his cap.” He preferred public display to shy secrecy. The judge, deceived for a time by Lousteau’s ingratiating attentions—he was a natural actor—gradually saw the truth. Between April and July, when Dinah expected confinement, she discovered why Lousteau had not triumphed over poverty: he was idle and weak of will. Genius requires a strong will; talent without it is unproductive. Men who govern or lead their age conquer indolence, deny themselves, and keep their faculties ready. Lousteau’s cigarette habit lulled him; tobacco numbed energy. Criticism as a profession also stultified him. The newspaper critic often performs mere acrobatics, praising or tearing down as convenience and wit allow; true criticism is a science requiring system and principles. Between dexterity and art lies a gulf Lousteau could not bridge.

Dinah, her intellect quickened, measured her idol. She saw him scamping work, writing at the last minute under compulsion. She excused him, saying, “He is a poet!” and tried to help. Loving him, she attempted through her mother to negotiate an allowance from her husband without Etienne’s knowledge, thinking it would offend him. A few days before July’s end, she crumpled up a letter from her mother conveying Monsieur de la Baudraye’s ultimatum: “Madame cannot need an allowance in Paris when she can live in perfect luxury at her Chateau of Anzy: she may return.” Lousteau picked up this letter and read it.

“I will avenge you!” he said in that ominous tone flattering to a woman whose antipathies are flattered.

Five days later Bianchon and Duriau, the famous ladies’ doctor, were engaged at Lousteau’s; he made a great display over the infant’s arrival. Monsieur de Clagny and Madame Piedefer were to be godparents. Dinah gave birth to a boy that might have filled a queen with envy who hoped for an heir.

Bianchon and Clagny registered the child at the Mayor’s office as the son of Monsieur and Madame de la Baudraye, unknown to Etienne. Lousteau, rushing to a printer, had fifty circulars set up:

“Madame la Baronne de la Baudraye is happily delivered of a son.
Monsieur Etienne Lousteau has the pleasure of informing you of the fact.
The mother and child are doing well.”

Clagny, finding a list of Sancerre names to which Lousteau planned to send the notice, confiscated the list and the remaining circulars, showed proofs to Madame Piedefer, and dashed to another printer for a corrected announcement:

“Madame la Baronne de la Baudraye is happily delivered of a son.
Monsieur le Baron de la Baudraye has the honor of informing you of the fact.
Mother and child are doing well.”

Clagny destroyed proofs and intercepted sent copies, recovering thirty and finally leaving only one of the original notes—sent to Nathan.

Five times Clagny called on Nathan without success; when admitted and the affair explained, Nathan laughed.

“Give up that monument of recklessness?” he cried. “That autograph is a weapon an athlete in the circus cannot afford to lay down. That note proves Lousteau has no heart, taste, or dignity; it insults himself. I have gone through a passion for a great lady and would have died rather than utter her name. A woman’s naming of her passion is supreme offering; a man—he must be a Lousteau! No, I would not give up that paper for a thousand crowns.”

Monsieur de Clagny, after an eloquent half-hour, argued otherwise: it was not for Lousteau but for a woman and child ignorant of the damage to their fortune and honour. At last Nathan reflected on the judge’s influence and gave up the circular.

When the jest was counteracted, Clagny rated Lousteau in Madame Piedefer’s presence and found Etienne fuming.

“I did it with a purpose!” Etienne protested. “Monsieur de la Baudraye has sixty thousand francs a year and refuses an allowance; I wished to make him feel the child is in my power.”

“For that reason I agreed to be godfather,” the lawyer answered. “The child is registered as the son of the Baron and Baronne; if you have father’s feelings you ought to rejoice, for the child is heir to a great entailed estate.”

“And is the mother to die of hunger?”

“Be at ease. I will transact with Monsieur de la Baudraye.”

Clagny left chilled. Dinah, his idol, was loved for her money; would she not, too late, be disillusioned? “Poor woman!” he thought, pitying and devoted.

The care of the infant, its cries, the quiet needed for the mother, and Madame Piedefer’s presence were adverse to writing; Lousteau moved to rooms on the first floor. Obliged to attend first nights without Dinah, living apart, he found charms in liberty. He was dragged to jollifications and to the houses of friends’ mistresses, seeing brilliantly dressed young women in whom economy seemed treason. Dinah, after nursing for three months, could not compare with these perishable blossoms.

Yet home life had strong attraction for Etienne. In three months the mother and daughter with the Sancerre cook and little Pamela had transformed the apartment. Lousteau found breakfasts and dinners served with a kind of luxury. Dinah, handsome and careful, anticipated his wishes; he felt king at home, where everything, even the baby, was subject to his selfishness. Didine’s affection appeared in every trifle; the deceptions of his unreal passion continued.

Dinah was aware that Lousteau’s life threatened ruin to love and household. After ten months she weaned her baby, installed her mother upstairs, and restored family intimacy. The daily intercourse of domestic life gives a woman power; men left too often to themselves become susceptible to release. Dinah intended to be indispensable and to infuse energy into Lousteau, whose weakness she mistook for security. She suggested subjects, sketched treatments, and at times wrote whole chapters. She revived dying talent by transfusing fresh blood; she supplied ideas and opinions. She produced two successful books by collaboration she kept secret from Madame Piedefer.

This galvanism was rewarded by better pay, and they lived comfortably through 1838. Lousteau grew accustomed to seeing Dinah do his work and paid her nothing—“monkey money.” The more she gave, the more she loved him; she felt she could never give him up.

But another child came, and that year was a trial. Despite precautions, Etienne contracted debts; he worked himself nearly to death to pay them while Dinah was ill, and she thought him heroic. After this effort, appalled at supporting two women, children, and two maids, he was incapable of continuing the struggle by his pen after having failed even to maintain himself. So he let things take their chance and exaggerated home-made love to secure liberty abroad.

Dinah endured stoically without support. The belief “He loves me!” gave her superhuman strength. She worked as hard as the most energetic spirits. At the risk of her beauty she sacrificed dress—dyeing her gowns black and wearing nothing else. Malaga mocked the stench of black that she carried, and the thrifty black irritated Lousteau, who reproached her for dowdiness while secretly benefiting from her sacrifices—just like a woman who makes a man debased and then scorns him for dirt.

Dinah then gathered the loose reins by which a clever woman steers a weak man, but by doing so she lost moral lustre; suspicion and reproach draw a woman into quarrels that breed disrespect. She made concessions; Lousteau entertained his friends—Nathan, Bixiou, Blondet, Finot—whose manners and talk were depraving. They mocked her principles as provincial prudishness and preached woman’s superiority.

During Carnival 1840 she disguised herself, attending balls at the Opera-house and suppers to spy on Etienne. The morning after Mid-Lent she came home from a ball in fancy dress to go to bed; she had gone to watch Lousteau, who, believing her ill, had engaged that evening to Fanny Beaupré. Warned by a friend, he behaved so as to deceive her, and she, eager to be deceived, was duped.

Stepping from the hired cab she met Monsieur de la Baudraye, the porter pointing her out. The little old man took his wife by the arm in an icy tone: “So this is you, madame!” The conjugal authority froze her; to escape Etienne’s eye she had chosen a dress he would not detect. Using her mask she slipped away, changed, and went to her mother’s rooms, where she found her husband waiting. Despite her assumed dignity she blushed.

“What do you want of me, monsieur? Are we not separated forever?” she asked.

“Actually, yes. Legally, no,” he answered. Madame Piedefer signalled; Dinah understood his visit was prompted by interest.

“Nothing could have brought you here but your own interests,” she said bitterly.

“Our interests,” he replied coldly, “for we have two children. Your Uncle Silas Piedefer is dead in New York; after making and losing fortunes, he left some seven or eight hundred thousand francs—some say a million. I am chief in our common interests and act for you.”

“In everything that relates to business I trust Monsieur de Clagny,” she retorted. “He knows the law; come to terms with him.”

“I have no occasion for Monsieur de Clagny to take my children from you,” Monsieur de la Baudraye said.

“Your children!” Dinah laughed. “Your children to whom you have sent no sou!” Her outburst froze under his coolness.

“Your mother brought them to show me,” he continued. “They are charming boys. I do not intend to part from them. I shall take them to Anzy, if only to save them from seeing their mother disguised like a—”

“Silence!” Dinah cried. “What do you want of me?”

“A power of attorney to receive our Uncle Silas’ property.”

Dinah wrote two lines to Monsieur de Clagny and asked her husband to return in the afternoon.

At five o’clock Clagny—now Attorney-General—explained her position and undertook to bargain with the old man. Monsieur de la Baudraye, needing her power of attorney to deal as he wished, purchased it by concessions: he agreed to allow her ten thousand francs a year so long as she chose to reside in Paris; the children were to be placed in his custody at six; and Clagny extracted payment in advance.

Little La Baudraye came jaunty to say good-bye in a white india-rubber overcoat. He looked so like the La Baudraye of 1836 that Dinah despaired. From the garden, where he smoked, Lousteau watched him cross the forecourt. This short scene changed the writer’s secret scheming. As he smoked a second cigar, he reviewed the situation.

Their life together had cost both sides equally; in moral accounting Lousteau considered himself the creditor. It was a favorable moment to desert her. Tired after three years of a comedy that could not be habit, he was hiding his weariness; he forced a smile at home like a debtor before a creditor. The advantages he had expected in future had sustained him; but when Monsieur de la Baudraye embarked briskly for America, Lousteau ceased to believe in the future.

Entering the drawing-room where Dinah had just left her husband, he found her grave.

“Etienne,” she said, “do you know my lord proposed that if I wish to return to Anzy during his absence, he hopes my mother’s advice will weigh with me and that I shall go back with my children.”

“It is very good advice,” Lousteau replied dryly, knowing she expected strong denial.

His tone, words, and cold look struck her who lived only in love; two tears slipped down her cheeks, unseen until she wiped them. He asked tenderly, “What is it, Didine?”

“Just as I was priding myself on having won our freedom at the cost of my fortune—by selling what is most precious to a mother’s heart—I have sold my children! He will have them at six and I cannot see them unless I go to Sancerre; that is torture! Ah, dear God! What have I done—?”

Lousteau knelt, kissed her hands, coaxing.

“You do not understand me,” he said. “I blame myself; I am not worth such sacrifices. I am second-rate in literature. If I can no longer cut a figure at the bottom of the newspaper, the editors will throw me aside. We tight-rope dancers have no retiring pension! I am forty-two, idle as a marmot. My love can only be fatal to you. At twenty-two I lived on Florine; what was excusable in youth is a disgrace to a man of forty. We have shared existence’s burden; it has not been lovely. Out of devotion you wear black and that does me no credit.” Dinah shrugged magnanimously. “Yes,” he went on, “you sacrifice everything to my whims, even your beauty. I, with a heart worn from past struggles, a soul full of dark presentiments, cannot repay your exquisite love equally. I cannot bear to see a sweet poem end badly. Am I wrong?”

Dinah loved him so truly that this prudence, measured like Clagny’s, gratified and staunched her tears. “He loves me for myself alone!” she thought, smiling.

After four years of intimacy her love now combined every shade modern society creates; Beyle (Stendhal) had first delineated such complex affections. Lousteau could magnetize Dinah—one look or touch reduced her to obedience. A kind word wreathed her soul with flowers; a cold look depressed her. Walking at his arm she lost all sense of herself. Fascinated by his wit and magnetized by his airs, his vices seemed trivial. She even loved the cigar-smoke drifting into her room and hid to inhale it. She hated editors who refused Lousteau money and deluded herself that he was writing a remunerative novel rather than working off a debt.

This is true love: heart and head, passion, caprice, and taste. Dinah argued, “If I have put myself outside the pale, why should you not sacrifice some of a man’s honor for me? We live outside social convention; Nathan can accept homage from Florine; why not from me? We will square accounts when we part; only death can part us. We shall be married some day—time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.”

“I never thought of that,” said Lousteau simply, adding to himself, “Time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
From that day forth Etienne lived in luxury; and Dinah, on first nights, could hold her own with the best dressed women in Paris. Lousteau affected among his friends the posture of a man overborne, bored to extinction, ruined by Madame de la Baudraye.

“Oh, what would I not give to the friend who would deliver me from Dinah! But no one ever can!” he said. “She loves me enough to throw herself out of the window if I told her.”

He was pitied; he took precautions against Dinah’s jealousy when accepting invitations. Yet he was shamelessly unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, really in despair at seeing Dinah in such disgraceful circumstances when she might have been rich and in the position she once sought, came to warn her—“You are betrayed”—and she only replied, “I know it.”

The lawyer was silenced; still he found his tongue to say one thing.

Madame de la Baudraye interrupted him when he had scarcely spoken.

“Do you still love me?” she asked.

“I would lose my soul for you!” he exclaimed, starting to his feet.

The hapless man’s eyes flashed, he trembled like a leaf; he believed himself blessed to be accepted as his idol’s avenger, and this poor joy filled him with rapture.

“Why are you so startled?” said she, making him sit again. “That is how I love him.”

The lawyer understood this ad hominem. Tears shone in the Judge’s eyes, who had just condemned a man to death.

Lousteau’s satiety, that odious end of illicit relations, betrayed itself in a thousand little things—grains of sand against the panes of the little magical hut where lovers dwell and dream. These grains, growing to pebbles, had never been discerned by Dinah till they were as big as rocks. Madame de la Baudraye had at last thoroughly understood Lousteau’s character.

“He is,” she said to her mother, “a poet, defenseless against disaster, mean from laziness, not for want of heart, rather too prone to pleasure; in short, a great cat, whom it is impossible to hate. What would become of him without me? I hindered his marriage; he has no prospects. His talent would perish in privations.”

“Oh, my Dinah!” Madame Piedefer exclaimed, “what a hell you live in! What is the feeling that gives you strength enough to persist?”

“I will be a mother to him!” she replied.

There are horrible situations in which we decide nothing until friends disclose our dishonor. We accept compromises with ourselves so long as we escape the prosecutor’s scrutiny. Monsieur de Clagny, clumsy as a tortured man, had been tormenting Dinah.

“To preserve my love I will be all that Madame de Pompadour was to preserve her power,” she thought when Clagny left. This phrase showed that her love was becoming a burden and would become toil rather than pleasure.

The part she assumed was horribly painful, and Lousteau made it no easier. When he wanted to go out after dinner he played tender farces of affection, addressing Dinah in words full of devotion; he would take her by the chain, and when he had bruised her would say, “Did I wound you?”

These false caresses degraded Dinah, who believed in a revival of his love. The mother yielded to the mistress with shameful readiness. She felt herself a mere plaything, and at last confessed to herself: “Well, then, I will be his plaything!”—finding joy in it—the rapture of damnation.

A woman of really manly spirit, picturing solitude, felt courage fail. She preferred the miseries of fierce intimacy to the absence of joys that are more exquisite because they spring from remorse, from a terrible struggle of a No persuaded to be Yes. At every moment she seemed to meet the bitter pool one finds in a desert, and drank with more relish than the traveler sips the finest wines at a prince’s table.

When Dinah wondered at midnight, “Will he come home, or will he not?” she was not alive again till she heard Lousteau’s boots and ring at the bell.

She often tried to restrain him by giving him pleasure; she hoped to match her rivals, and leave them no hold on that agitated heart. How many times a day she rehearsed the tragedy of Le Dernier Jour d’un condamne, telling herself, “To-morrow we part.” And how often would a word, a look, a kiss apparently artless bring her back to the depths of her love!

It was terrible. More than once had she meditated suicide as she paced the little town garden where a few pale flowers bloomed. In fact, she had not yet exhausted the vast treasure of devotion a woman bears in her heart.

The romance of Adolphe was her Bible; above all, she would not be an Ellenore. She allowed herself no tears, avoided the bitterness so cleverly described by the critic to whom we owe an analysis of that striking work; his comments seemed to Dinah almost superior to the book. She read again this fine essay in the Revue des Deux Mondes.

“No,” she said repeating the author’s words, “no, I will not ‘give my requests the form of an order,’ I will not ‘fly to tears as a means of revenge,’ I will not ‘condemn the things I once approved without reservation,’ I will not ‘dog his footsteps with a prying eye’; if he plays truant, he shall not on his return ‘see a scornful lip, whose kiss is an unanswerable command.’ No, ‘my silence shall not be a reproach nor my first word a quarrel.’—I will not be like every other woman!” she went on, laying on her table the little yellow paper volume which had already drawn Lousteau’s remark, “What! are you studying Adolphe?”—“If for one day only he should recognize my merits and say, ‘That victim never uttered a cry!’—it will be all I ask. And besides, the others have him for an hour; I have him for life!”

Thinking himself justified by his private tribunal in punishing his wife, Monsieur de la Baudraye robbed her to pursue his cherished enterprise of reclaiming three thousand acres of moorland, to which he had devoted himself since 1836, living like a mouse. He manipulated Monsieur Silas Piedefer’s property so ingeniously that he contrived to reduce the proved value to eight hundred thousand francs while pocketing twelve hundred thousand. He did not announce his return; while his wife endured unspeakable woes, he was building farms, digging trenches, and ploughing rough ground with courage that ranked him among the most remarkable provincial agriculturists.

The four hundred thousand francs he had filched from his wife were spent in three years on this undertaking, and Anzy was expected to return seventy-two thousand francs a year net after taxes. The eight hundred thousand he invested at four and a half per cent, buying at eighty francs during the financial crisis of the First of March. By securing his wife an income of forty-eight thousand francs he considered himself no longer in her debt. Could he not restore the odd twelve hundred thousand as soon as the four and a half per cents rose above a hundred? He fancied himself nearly the greatest man in Sancerre besides one—the richest proprietor in France—whose rival he considered himself. He saw himself with an income of a hundred and forty thousand francs, of which ninety thousand came from entailed lands. Calculating his payments—ten thousand in taxes, three thousand in working expenses, ten thousand to his wife, and twelve hundred to his mother-in-law—he would say in Sancerre’s literary circles:

“I am reputed miserly, and said to spend nothing; but my outlay amounts to twenty-six thousand five hundred francs a year. And I have still to pay for the education of my two children! I daresay it is not a pleasing fact to the Milauds of Nevers, but the second house of La Baudraye may yet have as noble a center as the first.—I shall most likely go to Paris and petition the King of the French to grant me the title of Count—Monsieur Roy is a Count—and my wife would be pleased to be Madame la Comtesse.”

And this was said with such coolness that no one dared laugh. Only Monsieur Boirouge, the Presiding Judge, remarked: “In your place, I should not be happy unless I had a daughter.”

“Well, I shall go to Paris before long—” said the Baron.

In early 1842 Madame de la Baudraye, feeling she was to Lousteau no more than a reserve, had again sacrificed herself absolutely to secure his comfort; she had resumed black raiment, but now as mourning, for her pleasure was turning to remorse. She was too often shamed not to feel the chain; her mother found her sunk in moods of meditation into which visions of the future cast unhappy souls in a torpor.

Madame Piedefer, by her spiritual director’s advice, watched for exhaustion, which the priest said would inevitably come, and then she pleaded for the children. She urged Dinah and Lousteau to live apart, not asking her to give him up. In real life violent situations do not close as in books—by death or contrived catastrophes; they end far less poetically—in disgust, the blighting of every flower of the soul, the commonplace of habit, or in another passion which robs a wife of interest. When common sense, social propriety, family interest, a sense of insults too offensive, the fatigue of constant self-sacrifice near exhaustion, and a too cruel blow—one of those mean acts a man never lets a woman know unless he believes himself master—put the crowning touch to her revulsion, the moment comes for intervention by the friend who undertakes the cure. Madame Piedefer had no difficulty now in removing the film from her daughter’s eyes.

She sent for Monsieur de Clagny, who completed the work by assuring Madame de la Baudraye that if she gave up Etienne, her husband would allow her to keep the children, live in Paris, and restore her to the command of her fortune.

“And what a life you are leading!” he said. “With care and judgment, the support of pious persons, you may have a salon and conquer a position. Paris is not Sancerre.”

Dinah left it to Monsieur de Clagny to negotiate reconciliation with the old man.

Monsieur de la Baudraye had sold his wine, wool, felled timber, and without telling his wife had come to Paris to invest two hundred thousand francs in a delightful residence in the Rue de l’Arcade being sold in liquidation. He had been a member of the Departmental Council since 1826, and now, paying ten thousand francs in taxes, was doubly qualified for a peerage under the new legislation.

Some time before the elections of 1842 he had put himself forward as candidate unless called to the Upper House as Peer of France. He asked for the title of Count and promotion in the Legion of Honor. In the event of his being won over to the Government, Sancerre would be more of a royalist rotten borough. Monsieur de Clagny, whose talents and modesty were appreciated by the authorities, supported him, pointing out that raising this enterprising agriculturist to the peerage would guarantee such undertakings.

Monsieur de la Baudraye, Count, Peer of France, and Commander of the Legion of Honor, was vain enough to wish to cut a figure with a wife and handsomely appointed house.—“He wanted to enjoy life,” he said.

He therefore addressed a letter to his wife, dictated by Monsieur de Clagny, begging her to live under his roof and to furnish the house, giving play to the taste of which the evidences had charmed him at the Chateau d’Anzy. The newly made Count told his wife that while their property’s interests forbade his leaving Sancerre, the education of their boys required her presence in Paris. He asked Monsieur de Clagny to place sixty thousand francs at Madame la Comtesse’s disposal for interior decoration, requesting a marble tablet over the gateway with the inscription: Hotel de la Baudraye.

He then accounted for Silas Piedefer’s money, told her of the investment at four and a half per cent of the eight hundred thousand francs he had brought from New York, and allowed her that income for expenses, including the children’s education. As he would be compelled to stay in Paris during the House of Peers sessions, he requested his wife reserve for him a suite of rooms in an entresol over the kitchens.

“Bless me! why, he is growing young again—a gentleman—a magnifico—What will he become next? It is quite alarming,” said Madame de la Baudraye.

“He now fulfils all your wishes at the age of twenty,” replied the lawyer.

Comparing future prospects with present position was unendurable to Dinah. Only the day before, Anna de Fontaine had turned away to avoid seeing her at Chamarolles’ school.

“I am a countess,” said Dinah to herself. “I shall have the peer’s blue hammer-cloth on my carriage, and the leaders of the literary world in my drawing-room—and I will look at her!”—This little triumph weighed heavily at the moment of her rehabilitation, as the world’s contempt had once weighed on her happiness.

One fine day in May 1842 Madame de la Baudraye paid all small household debts and left a thousand crowns atop the packet of receipted bills. After sending her mother and the children to the Hotel de la Baudraye, she awaited Lousteau, dressed to leave. When Etienne came to dinner, she said:

“I have upset the pot, my dear. Madame de la Baudraye requests the pleasure of your company at the Rocher de Cancale.”

She carried off Lousteau, bewildering him with the light and easy manners assumed by the woman who till that morning had been his slave; she too had been acting a farce for two months.

“Madame de la Baudraye is figged out as if for a first night,” said he.

“Do not forget the respect you owe to Madame de la Baudraye,” said Dinah gravely. “I do not mean to understand such a word as figged out.”

“Didine a rebel!” said he, putting his arm round her waist.

“There is no such person as Didine; you have killed her, my dear,” she replied, releasing herself. “I am taking you to the first performance of Madame la Comtesse de la Baudraye.”

“It is true, then, that our insect is a peer of France?”

“The nomination is to be gazetted in this evening’s Moniteur, as Monsieur de Clagny tells me, who is promoted to the Court of Appeal.”

“Well, it is right,” said the journalist. “The entomology of society ought to be represented in the Upper House.”

“My friend, we are parting for ever,” said Madame de la Baudraye, trying to control trembling of voice. “I have dismissed the two servants. When you go in, you will find the house in order, and no debts. I shall always feel a mother’s affection for you, but in secret. Let us part calmly, without a fuss, like decent people.

“Have you had a fault to find with my conduct during the past six years?”

“None, but that you have spoiled my life, and wrecked my prospects,” he answered hard. “You have read Benjamin Constant very diligently; you have even studied the last critique; but you have read with a woman’s eyes. Though you have one of those superior intellects which would make a fortune of a poet, you have never dared to take the man’s point of view.

“That book, my dear, is of both sexes.—We agreed that books were male or female, dark or fair. In Adolphe women see nothing but Ellenore; young men see only Adolphe; men of experience see Ellenore and Adolphe; political men see the whole of social existence. You did not think it necessary to read the soul of Adolphe—any more than your critic, who saw only Ellenore. What kills that poor fellow, my dear, is that he has sacrificed his future for a woman; that he never can be what he might have been—an ambassador, a minister, a chamberlain, a poet—and rich. He gives up six years of his energy when a man is ready to submit to apprenticeship—to a petticoat, which he outstrips in ingratitude, for the woman who has thrown over her first lover is certain sooner or later to desert the second. Adolphe is, in fact, a tow-haired German, who has not spirit enough to be false to Ellenore. There are Adolphes who spare their Ellenores all ignominious quarreling and reproaches, who say to themselves, ‘I will not talk of what I have sacrificed; I will not for ever be showing the stump of my wrist to let that incarnate selfishness I have made my queen,’ as Ramorny does in The Fair Maid of Perth. But men like that get cast aside.

“Adolphe is a man of birth, an aristocratic nature, who wants to get back into the highroad to honors and recover his social birthright.—You, at this moment, are playing both parts. You are suffering from losing your position, and think yourself justified in throwing over a hapless lover whose misfortune it has been that he fancied you so far superior as to understand that, though a man’s heart ought to be true, his sex may be allowed to indulge its caprices.”

“And do you suppose that I shall not make it my business to restore to you all you have lost by me? Be quite easy,” said Madame de la Baudraye, astounded by this attack. “Your Ellenore is not dying; and if God gives her life, if you amend your ways, if you give up courtesans and actresses, we will find you a better match than a Felicie Cardot.”

The two lovers were sullen. Lousteau affected dejection; Dinah, really distressed, listened to her heart’s reproaches.

“Why,” said Lousteau presently, “why not end as we ought to have begun—hide our love from all eyes, and see each other in secret?”

“Never!” cried the new-made Countess, with an icy look. “Do you not comprehend that we are, after all, finite creatures? Our feelings seem infinite by reason of our anticipation of heaven, but here on earth they are limited by our physical strength. There are some feeble, mean natures which may receive an endless number of wounds and live on; but some highly-tempered souls snap at last under repeated blows. You have—”

“Oh! enough!” cried he. “No more copy! Your dissertation is unnecessary, since you can justify yourself by merely saying—‘I have ceased to love!’”

“What!” she exclaimed. “Is it I who have ceased to love?”

“Certainly. You have calculated that I gave you more trouble, more vexation than pleasure, and you desert your partner—”

“I desert!” cried she, clasping her hands.

“Have not you yourself just said ‘Never’?”

“Well, then, yes! Never,” she repeated vehemently.

This final Never, spoken in fear of falling under Lousteau’s influence, he took as the death-warrant of his power, since Dinah remained insensible to his sarcastic scorn.

The journalist could not suppress a tear. He was losing sincere affection. He had found in Dinah the gentlest La Valliere, the most delightful Pompadour that any egoist short of a king could hope for; and, like a boy who has killed a cockchafer by tormenting it, Lousteau shed a tear.

Madame de la Baudraye rushed out of the dining room, paid the bill, and fled home to the Rue de l’Arcade, scolding herself and thinking herself a brute.

Dinah, who had made her house a model of comfort, now metamorphosed herself. This cost thirty thousand francs more than her husband had anticipated.

The fatal accident depriving the House of Orleans of the heir-presumptive in 1842 required the Chambers to meet in August; La Baudraye presented his titles to the Upper House sooner than expected, and then saw what his wife had done. He was so delighted that he paid the thirty thousand francs without a word, as he had formerly paid eight thousand for decorating La Baudraye.

On his return from the Luxembourg, where he had been presented by two peers—the Baron de Nucingen and the Marquis de Montriveau—the new Count met the old Duc de Chaulieu walking along; the contrast filled his heart with a draught of the balm on which the middle class had been getting drunk since 1840.

Madame de la Baudraye was shocked to see her husband improved and looking better than on the day of their marriage. The little dwarf, full of rapturous delight at sixty-four, triumphed in the life long denied him; in the family his handsome cousin Milaud of Nevers had said he would never have; and in his wife—who had asked Monsieur and Madame de Clagny to dinner to meet the parish cure and his two sponsors to the Chamber of Peers. He petted the children with fatuous delight.

The handsome display on the table met his approval.

“These are the fleeces of the Berry sheep,” he said, showing Monsieur de Nucingen the dish-covers surmounted by his coronet. “They are of silver, you see!”

Though consumed by melancholy, which she concealed with the determination of a superior woman, Dinah was charming, witty, and young again in her court mourning.

“You might declare,” cried La Baudraye to Monsieur de Nucingen with a wave to his wife, “that the Countess was not yet thirty.”

“Ah, ha! Matame is a voman of dirty!” replied the baron, prone to time-honored remarks.

“In every sense,” replied the Countess. “I am, in fact, five-and-thirty, and mean to set up a little passion—”

“Oh, yes, my wife ruins me in curiosities and china images—”

“She started that mania,” said the Marquis with a smile.

“Yes,” said La Baudraye, cold at the Marquis, “you know that in ‘25, ‘26, and ‘27, she picked a million francs’ worth of treasures. Anzy is a perfect museum.”

“What a cool hand!” thought Monsieur de Clagny, seeing the country miser on the level of his position.

But misers have savings for use.

On the day after the Regency vote passed, the little Count went back to Sancerre for the vintage and resumed old habits.

That winter the Comtesse, supported by the Attorney-General to the Court of Appeals, tried to form a little circle. She had an “at home” day, selected men of mark, receiving those of serious purpose and ripe years. She went to the Opera twice a week with her mother and Madame de Clagny, who visited Dinah at her husband’s insistence. Still, despite cleverness, charming manners, and stylishness, she was never really happy but with her children, on whom she lavished disappointed affection.

Worthy Monsieur de Clagny tried to recruit women for the Countess’ circle, and he succeeded among advocates of piety more than women of fashion.

“And they bore her!” said he to himself with horror, seeing his idol matured by grief, pale from remorse, then restored by luxury and care for her boys. This devoted friend, encouraged by her mother and the cure, was full of expedients. Every Wednesday he introduced some celebrity from Germany, England, Italy, or Prussia to the dear Countess; he spoke of her as an exceptional woman to people she hardly addressed two words; but she listened with such deep attention that they went away convinced of her superiority. In Paris, Dinah conquered by silence as at Sancerre she had conquered by loquacity. Now and then a smart saying or a sarcasm betrayed a woman used to deal with ideas—the one who, four years since, had given new life to Lousteau’s articles.

This phase was to the hapless lawyer like the Indian summer after a sunless year. He affected to be older than he was, to have the right to befriend Dinah without injury, and kept at a distance as though he were young, handsome, and compromising, like a man who has happiness to conceal. He tried to keep attentions secret, the trifling gifts Dinah showed to everyone; he suggested a dangerous meaning for his little services.

“He plays at passion,” said the Countess, laughing. She made fun of Monsieur de Clagny to his face, and he thought, “She notices me.”

One evening Monsieur de Clagny and his wife were taking Dinah home from the theatre; she was pensive. They had seen Leon Gozlan’s La Main Droite et la Main Gauche.

“What are you thinking about?” asked the lawyer, alarmed at her dejection.

This deep melancholy, though disguised, was a perilous malady for which Clagny knew no remedy; for true love is often clumsy, especially when not reciprocated. True love takes expression from character. Now, this good man loved like Alceste, when Madame de la Baudraye wanted love like Philinte. The meaner side of love can never suit the Misanthrope’s loyalty. Thus Dinah had taken care never to open her heart to him. How could she confess that sometimes she regretted the slough she had left?

She felt a void in fashionable life; she had no one for whom to dress, or whom to tell of her successes. Sometimes memory of wretchedness came, mingled with memories of consuming joys. She would hate Lousteau for not following her; she wished for tender or furious letters from him.

Dinah made no reply, so Clagny repeated the question, taking her hand and pressing it with devout respect.

“Will you have the right hand or the left?” said she, smiling.

“The left,” said he, “for I suppose you mean the truth or a fib.”

“Well, then, I saw him,” she said into the lawyer’s ear. “And as I saw him looking so sad, so out of heart, I said to myself, Has he a cigar? Has he any money?”

“If you wish the truth, I can tell it,” said he. “He is living as a husband with Fanny Beaupre. You have forced me to tell you this secret; I should never have told you, for you might have suspected me perhaps of an ungenerous motive.”

Madame de la Baudraye grasped his hand.

“Your husband,” she said to her chaperon, “is one of the rarest souls!—Ah! Why—”

She shrank into her corner, looking out, but did not finish the sentence Clagny could guess: “Why had not Lousteau a little of your husband’s generosity?”

This information cured Dinah of melancholy; she threw herself into the whirl of fashion. She wished for success, and she achieved it; still she did not make much way with women and found introductions difficult.

In March, Madame Piedefer’s friends—the priests and Monsieur de Clagny—got Madame de la Baudraye appointed receiver of subscriptions for Madame de Carcado’s charitable work. She was commissioned to collect Royal Family donations for Guadeloupe earthquake sufferers. The Marquise d’Espard, hearing of the Countess’s commission, said: “I have lived a long time in the world, and I can remember nothing finer than the manoeuvres undertaken for the rehabilitation of Madame de la Baudraye.”

In early spring, by whim of our planets, Paris smiled in March 1843, making the Champs-Elysees green before Longchamp. Fanny Beaupre’s attache had seen Madame de la Baudraye several times without being seen by her. Stung to the heart by prompts of jealousy and envy familiar to provincials, he beheld his former mistress in a handsome carriage, well dressed, with dreamy eyes, and his two little boys, one at each window. He accused himself the more because he was warring with sharp poverty—poverty unconfessed. Like light, frivolous natures, he cherished a point of honor: never to derogate in the eyes of one’s little public, which drives some men on the Bourse to crime to avoid expulsion from Per-cent’s temple, and has made some criminals perform acts of virtue.

Lousteau dined and smoked as if rich. Not for an inheritance would he buy anything but the dearest cigars; he took walks in patent-leather boots; but he feared an execution on goods that had already received the last sacrament. Fanny had nothing left to pawn; her salary was pledged. After exhausting advances from newspapers and publishers, Etienne knew not what ink could churn gold. Gambling houses, suppressed, no longer cashed IOUs. The journalist was reduced to such extremity that he had borrowed a hundred francs of poorest friend Bixiou, whom he’d never asked for a franc. What distressed him was not owing five thousand francs but seeing himself bereft of elegance, of furniture bought by privation and added to by Madame de la Baudraye.

On April 3 a yellow poster announced the sale of a handsome suite of furniture the following Saturday. Lousteau was walking, smoking, seeking ideas—for in Paris ideas are in the air—yet he found nothing but friends who carried him to dinner and intoxicating champagne.

“Beware,” said the virulent Bixiou one night—the man who would give a hundred francs and stab a comrade with sarcasm—“if you go to sleep drunk every night, one day you will wake up mad.”

On the Friday the unhappy wretch felt condemned. He might once have said, “The furniture is old! I will buy new,” but he was incapable now of literary legerdemain. Publishers paid poorly; newspapers bargained hard; Opera managers did with tenors as they pleased.

He walked with a cigar and affected smile. Then he saw Madame de la Baudraye pass in a carriage; she was going to drive in the Bois.

“There is nothing else left!” he said to himself, and went home to smarten.

That evening, at seven, he arrived in a hackney at Madame de la Baudraye’s door, and begged the porter to send a note up—a few lines:

“Would Madame la Comtesse do Monsieur Lousteau the favor of receiving him for a moment, and at once?”

This note bore the seal they both had used. The Countess had the word Parce que engraved on a carnelian—a potent, accounting word.

She had just finished dressing for the Opera; at the sight of the seal she turned pale.

“I will come,” she said, tucking the note into her dress.

Firm to conceal agitation, she begged her mother to put the children to bed and sent for Lousteau, receiving him in a boudoir with open doors. She wore brocaded brocade in pale blue stripes; gloves trimmed with tassels set off white arms; hair a la Sevigne; pearls lay on her bosom like bubbles.

“What is the matter, monsieur?” she asked, putting out a foot to rest on a velvet cushion. “I thought, I hoped, I was quite forgotten.”

“If I should reply Never, you would refuse to believe me,” said Lousteau, standing, chewing flowers he plucked.

For a moment silence reigned. Dinah saw he was dressed as a fastidious dandy.

“You are the only person in the world who can help me, or hold out a plank—I am drowning and have swallowed more than one mouthful,” he said. “Since you see me here, it is because my affairs are going to the devil.”

“That is enough,” said she; “I understand.”

He turned away, took out his handkerchief, seemed to wipe a tear.

“How much do you want, Etienne?” she went on in motherly tones. “We are at this moment old comrades; speak to me as you would to—Bixiou.”

“To save my furniture from vanishing to-morrow at the auction, eighteen hundred francs! To repay my friends, as much again! Three quarters’ rent—My ‘uncle’ wants five hundred francs—”

“And you—to live on?”

“Oh! I have my pen—”

“It is heavier to lift than anyone who reads your articles believes,” she said with a subtle smile.—“I have not such a sum, but come to-morrow at eight; the bailiff will surely wait till nine, especially if you bring him away to pay him.”

She must dismiss Lousteau, who affected inability to look; she felt pity that might cut every social Gordian knot.

“Thank you,” she added, rising and offering her hand. “Your confidence has done me good! It is long since my heart has known such joy—”

Lousteau took her hand and pressed it to his heart.

“A drop of water in the desert—and sent by the hand of an angel! God always does things handsomely!”

He spoke half jest, half pathos; as acting it was as fine as Talma’s Leicester. Dinah felt his heart beat through his coat; it throbbed with satisfaction and fire at seeing her rejuvenescent restored by wealth.

Stealing a glance, Dinah saw that his expression matched flowers of love which had blossomed in his heart; she tried to look into his eyes, but blood rushed to her face. Their eyes met with the same fiery glow as when Lousteau had encouraged himself on the Quay to crumple Dinah’s muslin. The Bohemian put his arm round her waist; she yielded; their cheeks touched.

“Here comes my mother, hide!” cried Dinah and hurried to intercept Madame Piedefer.

“Mamma,” said she—this coaxing word always enthralled the stern old lady—“will you do me a great favor? Take the carriage and go to my banker, Monsieur Mongenod, with a note I will give you, and bring back six thousand francs. Come, come—it is an act of charity; come into my room.”

She dragged away her mother, anxious to see who was in the boudoir.

Two days afterwards Madame Piedefer consulted the parish cure. After hearing the old mother’s lamentations, the priest said gravely:

“Any moral regeneration not based on strong religious sentiment and carried out in the Church is built on sand.—The many means of grace enjoined by Catholicism are so many dams necessary to restrain evil promptings. Persuade your daughter to perform all her duties, and we shall save her yet.”

Within ten days the Hotel de la Baudraye was shut. The Countess, the children, her mother, and household, including a tutor, had gone to Sancerre, where Dinah intended to spend the summer. She was everything that was nice to the Count, people said.

And so the Muse of Sancerre had simply come back to family and married life; but evil tongues declared she had been compelled to come back, for the little peer’s wishes would no doubt be fulfilled—he hoped for a little girl.

Gatien and Monsieur Gravier lavished care and servile attention on the handsome Countess. Gatien, who had been to Paris to learn lionnerie, was supposed to have a good chance of favor. Others bet on the tutor; Madame Piedefer urged religion.

In June 1844, as the Comte took a walk on the Mall at Sancerre with the two fine little boys, he met Monsieur Milaud the Public Prosecutor and said, “These are my children, cousin.”

“Ah, ha! so these are our children!” replied the lawyer with a twinkle.

PARIS, June 1843–August 1844.
