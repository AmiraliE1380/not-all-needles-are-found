DEDICATION

To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,--If fortune allows these lines to endure, they will repay a little the trouble you have taken—you, the Hozier, the Cherin, the King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life; you to whom the Navarreins, Cadignans, Langeais, Blamont-Chauvrys, Chaulieus, Arthez, Esgrignons, Mortsaufs, Valois—the great names of the “Human Comedy”—owe their mottoes and bearings. The “Armorial of the Etudes, devised by Ferdinand de Gramont, gentleman,” is a complete manual of French Heraldry; I shall keep it as a monument of friendship and Benedictine patience. What knowledge of the feudal spirit in the Beauseants’ motto, Pulchre sedens, melius agens; in the Espards’, Des partem leonis; in the Vandenesses’, Ne se vend; and what elegance in the learned symbolism throughout my work, to which you, the poet, contributed.

Your old friend,
DE BALZAC.

THE MUSE OF THE DEPARTMENT

On the skirts of Le Berry stands a town by the Loire that draws the traveler’s eye. Sancerre crowns the last height of a hill-chain extending into the Nivernais. The Loire floods the flats at the foot of these slopes, leaving yellow alluvium fertile except where sand from terrible risings—like those of the Vistula—has spoiled them.

The hill where Sancerre’s houses cluster lies away from the river; so the little port of Saint-Thibault thrives on the town’s life. There wine is shipped and oak staves landed, trading produce from the upper and lower Loire. By the time this tale begins, suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault had been built, ending ferrying on the southern road—evidence that the great 1830 upheaval was past. The House of Orléans had favored practical improvements, though somewhat like a husband spending his wife’s marriage portion.

Except on a small plateau, the streets are steep; the town is ringed by slopes called the Great Ramparts, its highroads. Outside these lies a belt of vineyards. Wine is the chief industry, producing aromatic vintages close to Burgundy’s so as to deceive the vulgar palate. Sancerre finds in Paris wine-shops the quick market necessary for a liquor that will not keep more than seven or eight years. Below the town a few villages—Fontenoy and Saint-Satur—are almost suburbs, recalling the smiling vineyards about Neuchâtel in Switzerland.

The town still shows much of its ancient face: narrow streets paved with pebbles hauled from the Loire, and some old houses survive. The citadel, relic of feudal power, endured one of the fiercest sieges of our religious wars, when French Calvinists rivaled the ferocity of Walter Scott’s Cameronians.

Sancerre, rich in a greater past but bereft of military importance, is destined to a less glorious future, for trade runs on the Loire’s right bank. The sketch suggests it will grow lonelier despite two bridges to Cosne.

Sancerre, the left-bank pride, counts at most three thousand five hundred inhabitants, while Cosne now has over six thousand. Within half a century the roles of these opposite towns have reversed. The advantage of situation remains with the historic town: the view is enchanting, the air pure, vegetation splendid, and the people—friendly, in harmony with nature, free from Puritanism, though two-thirds are Calvinists. Under such conditions, despite small-town drawbacks and an officious eye that makes private life almost public, a township spirit—a kind of patriotism, though not a substitute for love of home—flourishes.

Thus Sancerre proudly claims Horace Bianchon, a glory of modern medicine, and Étienne Lousteau, an author of secondary rank and one of our most successful journalists. The district, distressed to be ruled by seven or eight large landowners—the wire-pullers of elections—tried to shake off this electoral yoke, a rotten-borough condition. A little conspiracy, plotted by a handful whose vanity was aroused, failed because jealousy at one man’s inevitable elevation split them. That exposed the plan’s fatal defect; the suggested remedy was to rally about a champion at the next election—one of the two men who gloriously represented Sancerre in Paris.

This idea was urged for the provinces; since 1830 the nomination of parochial dignitaries has grown so common that true statesmen in the lower chamber are rare. In fact, the doubtful scheme was hatched by the Superior Woman of the borough, dux femina fasti, for personal ends. It was so rooted in her past and so crucial to her future prospects that it can scarcely be understood without a sketch of her antecedent career.

Sancerre at that time could boast of a Superior Woman, long undervalued but by about 1836 enjoying a wide local reputation. Two Sancerrois in Paris were also rising to distinction. Etienne Lousteau wrote reviews for a paper of eight thousand subscribers; Bianchon, already chief physician to a hospital, Officer of the Legion of Honor, and a member of the Academy of Sciences, had just been made a professor.

If the word did not to many imply blame, one might say George Sand created “Sandism,” so true is it that morally every good has a reverse. Sand’s sentimentality can be trying, yet it has a useful side: the woman it attacks bases her superiority on scorned feelings; she is a blue-stocking of sentiment, somewhat less of a bore, love partly neutralizing literature. George Sand’s celebrity chiefly revealed that France had an enormous number of superior women who, until then, had generously left the field to the Maréchal de Saxe’s granddaughter.

The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town-and-country house within ten minutes of the town, in the village or suburb of Saint-Satur. Present La Baudrayes, like many modern namesakes, have supplanted the original La Baudrayes whose name, glorious in the Crusades, figured in Le Berry’s history.

The story must be told.

In Louis XIV’s time a sheriff named Milaud, whose forebears had been fervent Calvinists, converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. To foster conversions in a Calvinist stronghold, the King gave Milaud an appointment in the “Waters and Forests,” granted him arms, the title Sire de la Baudraye, and the fief of the old La Baudrayes. Later descendants of the famous Captain la Baudraye fell into one of the snares laid for heretics by the new decrees and were hanged—an unworthy deed of the great King.

Under Louis XV, Milaud de la Baudraye rose from squire to Chevalier and had influence enough to obtain a cornet’s commission in the Musketeers for his son. This officer perished at Fontenoy, leaving a child to whom Louis XVI subsequently granted, by patent, the privileges of a farmer-general in remembrance of his father’s death.

This financier, a fashionable wit adept at charades, capping verses and posies to Chloris, lived in society, clung to the Duc de Nivernais, and apparently intended to follow the nobility into exile; but he took care to carry his money with him. Thus the rich émigré was able to assist more than one family of high rank.

In 1800, tired of hoping and perhaps of lending, he returned to Sancerre and bought back La Baudraye out of vanity and imagined pride—quite intelligible in a sheriff’s grandson—though under the Consulate his prospects were slender; all the more so because the ex-farmer-general had little hope that his heir would perpetuate the new race of La Baudraye.

Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, his only son, delicate from birth, plainly showed the result of a constitution exhausted by excesses and late marriage, introducing degeneracy into high society. During the emigration Madame de la Baudraye, a girl of no fortune chosen for her birth, patiently reared this sallow, sickly boy with the devoted love mothers show such changelings. Her death—she was a Casteran de la Tour—contributed to Monsieur de la Baudraye’s return to France.

This Lucullus of the Milauds, when he died, left his son the fief, stripped indeed of its fines and dues, but graced with weathercocks bearing his coat-of-arms, a thousand louis-d’or, and certain receipts for claims on very distinguished émigrés enclosed in a pocketbook full of verses, the wrapper inscribed Vanitas vanitatum et omnia vanitas.

Young La Baudraye did not die; he owed his life to habits of monastic strictness, to the economy of action Fontenelle preached as the religion of the invalid, and, above all, to the air of Sancerre and the benefit of its elevation, whence a panorama over the Loire valley extends for forty leagues.

From 1802 to 1815 young La Baudraye added several plots to his vineyards and devoted himself to vine culture. The Restoration at first seemed so insecure that he dared not go to Paris to claim his debts; but after Napoleon’s death he tried to turn his father’s collection of autographs into money, though he did not understand the philosophy that mixed IOUs and copies of verses. The winegrower wasted time trying to impress his identity on the Duke of Nivernais “and others,” and returned to Sancerre to his vintage without having obtained anything but offers of service.

The Restoration raised the nobility to such lustre that La Baudraye wished to justify his ambitions by having an heir. This result of matrimony he considered doubtful, or he would not have postponed the step so long; however, finding himself still above ground in 1823 at forty-three—a length of years which no doctor, astrologer, or midwife would have dared promise—he hoped to earn the reward of his sober life. Yet his choice showed such lack of prudence regarding his frail constitution that the malicious wit of a country town suspected some deep calculation.

At that time Monseigneur the Archbishop of Bourges had converted a young woman, the daughter of one of the citizen families that had upheld Calvinism and, thanks to obscurity or compromise, escaped persecution under Louis XIV. The Piedefers—respectable cloth merchants who had once supported the Reformation—had suffered reverses. In Louis XVI’s reign Abraham Piedefer fell into difficulties and at his death in 1786 left his two children in extreme poverty. One, Tobie Piedefer, went to the Indies, leaving the meagre inheritance to his elder brother. During the Revolution Moise Piedefer bought nationalized land, pulled down abbeys and churches with ancestral zeal, and married a Catholic, the only daughter of a member of the Convention who had perished on the scaffold. This ambitious Piedefer died in 1819, leaving a little girl of remarkable beauty. Brought up in the Calvinist faith, she was named Dinah, following the sect’s custom of choosing Biblical rather than Roman Catholic names.

Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer was placed by her mother in the Demoiselles Chamarolles’ school in Bourges and was soon distinguished for mind as well as beauty; but she was snubbed by girls of birth and fortune destined to play greater parts in the world than a plebeian’s daughter dependent on the settlement of the Piedefer estate. Having raised herself above her companions, Dinah aimed to remain on a level with them for life. She determined, therefore, to renounce Calvinism in the hope that the Cardinal would favor his proselyte and interest himself in her prospects. You may judge of Mademoiselle Dinah’s superiority, since at seventeen she converted solely from ambition.

The Archbishop, convinced Dinah would adorn society, was anxious to see her married. But every family to whom he made advances recoiled from a damsel with the looks of a princess, reputed the cleverest of Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ pupils and who always shone at the prize-giving. A thousand crowns a year, the share she might hope from La Hautoy when divided between mother and daughter, would be trifling compared to the expenses a husband would incur by the personal advantages of so brilliant a creature.

As soon as these facts reached little Polydore de la Baudraye—they were the talk of every circle in the Cher—he went to Bourges just when Madame Piedefer, a devotee at high services, had almost made up her mind and her daughter’s to take the first comer with well-lined pockets—the first chien coiffé, as they say in Le Berry. If the Cardinal was delighted to receive Monsieur de la Baudraye, Monsieur de la Baudraye was even more pleased to receive a wife from the Cardinal’s hands. The little gentleman only demanded a formal promise that His Eminence would support his claims with the President of the Council to enable him to recover his debts from the Duc de Navarreins “and others” by a lien on their indemnities. This method, however, seemed to the Minister then occupying the Pavillon Marsan too sharp, and he gave the vine-owner to understand his business would be attended to in good time.

It is easy to imagine the excitement in the Sancerre district at news of Monsieur de la Baudraye’s imprudent marriage.

“It is quite intelligible,” said President Boirouge; “the little man was very much startled, I am told, at hearing handsome young Milaud, the Attorney-General’s deputy at Nevers, say to Monsieur de Clagny as they looked at La Baudraye’s turrets, ‘That will be mine some day.’—‘But,’ says Clagny, ‘he may marry and have children.’—‘Impossible!’—So you may imagine how such a changeling as little La Baudraye must hate that colossal Milaud.”

At Nevers there was a plebeian branch of the Milauds, grown rich in the cutlery trade, that had brought its representative up to the civil service under Marchangy, now dead.

It will be useful to set aside the baser material interests that occupied Monsieur de la Baudraye, by briefly relating his negotiations in Paris. This will also shed light on certain mysterious contemporary political phenomena that hampered the Ministry during the Restoration.

The Ministers’ promises were so illusory that Monsieur de la Baudraye decided to go to Paris when the Cardinal’s presence was required there by the Chambers’ sitting.

This is how the Duc de Navarreins, the principal debtor threatened by Monsieur de la Baudraye, got out of the scrape.

The country gentleman, lodging at the Hotel de Mayence, Rue Saint-Honoré near the Place Vendôme, one morning received a visit from a confidential agent of the Ministry, an expert in “winding up” business. This elegant person, stepping from an elegant cab and neatly dressed, was shown to the third floor, to a small room where he found his provincial concocting coffee over a bedroom fire.

“Is it to Monsieur Milaud de la Baudraye that I have the honor—”

“Yes,” said the little man, draping himself in his dressing-gown.

After examining the garment—an illicit offspring of an old china wrapper of Madame Piedefer’s and a gown of the late Madame de la Baudraye—the emissary considered the man, the dressing-gown, and the tin saucepan on the little stove so characteristic that he deemed it needless to beat about the bush.

“I will lay a wager, monsieur,” said he audaciously, “that you dine for forty sous at Hurbain’s in the Palais-Royal.”

“Pray, why?”

“Oh, I know you, having seen you there,” replied the Parisian gravely. “All the princes’ creditors dine there. You know you recover scarcely ten per cent on debts from these fine gentlemen. I would not give you five per cent on a debt to be recovered from the estate of the late Duc d’Orléans—nor even,” he added in a low voice, “from MONSIEUR.”

“So you have come to buy up the bills?” said La Baudraye, thinking himself clever.

“Buy them!” said his visitor. “Why, what do you take me for? I am Monsieur des Lupeaulx, Master of Appeals, Secretary-General to the Ministry, and I have come to propose an arrangement.”

“What is that?”

“Of course, monsieur, you know the position of your debtor—”

“Of my debtors—”

“Well, monsieur, you understand their position; they stand high in the King’s good graces but have no money and must make a good show. Again, you know the political difficulties. The aristocracy must be rehabilitated against a strong third estate. The King’s idea—of which France does him scant justice—is to create a peerage as a national institution analogous to the English peerage. To realize this we need years—and millions. Noblesse oblige. The Duc de Navarreins, who is, as you know, first gentleman of the Bedchamber to the King, does not repudiate his debt, but he cannot—Now, be reasonable—consider politics. We are emerging from the pit of the Revolution—and you yourself are noble—He simply cannot pay—”

“Monsieur—”

“You are hasty,” said des Lupeaulx. “Listen. He cannot pay in money. Well then; you, a clever man, can take payment in favors—royal or ministerial.”

“What! When in 1793 my father put down one hundred thousand—”

“My dear sir, recrimination is useless. Listen to a simple statement in political arithmetic: the collectorship at Sancerre is vacant; a certain paymaster-general of the forces has a claim on it, but he has no chance of getting it; you have the chance—and no claim. You will get the place. You will hold it for three months, then resign, and Monsieur Gravier will give twenty thousand francs for it. In addition, the Order of the Legion of Honor will be conferred on you.”

“Well, that is something,” said the wine-grower, tempted more by the money than the red ribbon.

“But then,” said des Lupeaulx, “you must show your gratitude to His Excellency by restoring to Monseigneur the Duc de Navarreins all your claims on him.”

La Baudraye returned to Sancerre as Collector of Taxes. Six months later he was superseded by Monsieur Gravier, regarded as one of the most agreeable financiers who had served under the Empire, and whom Monsieur de la Baudraye introduced to his wife.

As soon as he was released from his functions, Monsieur de la Baudraye returned to Paris to settle with some debtors. He became referendary under the Great Seal, a baron, and an Officer of the Legion of Honor. He sold the referendary post, visited his last debtors, and reappeared at Sancerre as Master of Appeals, appointed Royal Commissioner to a Nivernais commercial association with a six-thousand-franc sinecure. Thus the worthy La Baudraye, who was supposed to have committed a financial blunder, had in fact done very good business in the choice of a wife.

Thanks to frugal economy and an indemnity for his father’s estate, nationalized and sold in 1793, by 1827 he could realize his dream. By paying four hundred thousand francs down and binding himself to further instalments, after six years of tight living he purchased the estate of Anzy on the Loire, about two leagues above Sancerre, and its magnificent castle built by Philibert de l’Orme, admired by connoisseurs and for five centuries the property of the Uxelles family. At last he was one of the great landowners of the province. An entail had been created by letters patent dated December 1820, including the estates of Anzy, La Baudraye, and La Hautoy—though this may not have fully compensated Dinah for living in unconfessed penuriousness until 1835.

This sketch of the first Baron de la Baudraye’s financial policy explains the man. Those familiar with rural manias will recognize in him the land-hunger that becomes a consuming passion to the exclusion of every other—a sort of avarice displayed in the open air, which often leads to ruin when mortgage interest outstrips the produce of the soil. Those who, from 1802 to 1827, had laughed at the little man trotting to Saint-Thibault and attending to his business like a merchant living on his vineyards found their answer when the ant-lion seized his prey after the extravagance of the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse culminated in the sale of that splendid property.

Madame Piedefer came to live with her daughter. The combined fortunes of Monsieur de la Baudraye and his mother-in-law—who accepted an annuity of twelve hundred francs on the lands of La Hautoy, which she handed over to him—amounted to an acknowledged income of about fifteen thousand francs.

Early in her married life Dinah made alterations that turned La Baudraye into a pleasant residence. She converted a spacious forecourt into a formal garden, pulled down wine-stores, presses, and shabby outhouses. Behind the small but stylish manor-house, with its turrets and gables, she laid out a second garden with shrubs, flower-beds, and lawns, screening it from the vineyards by a wall hidden under creepers. She also made the interiors as comfortable as circumstances allowed.

To avoid being ruined by a young lady so superior as Dinah seemed, Monsieur de la Baudraye was shrewd enough to say nothing about the recovery of debts in Paris. This secrecy about his finances lent a touch of mystery to his character and dignity in his wife’s eyes during the first years of their married life.

The alterations at La Baudraye made everybody eager to see the mistress, all the more since Dinah would not receive company until she felt settled at home and had thoroughly studied the inhabitants and, above all, her taciturn husband. When, one spring morning in 1825, Madame de la Baudraye was first seen walking on the Mall in a blue velvet dress, with her mother in black velvet, there was excitement in Sancerre. The dress confirmed the young woman’s reputation for superiority, educated as she had been in the capital of Le Berry. Visitors, constrained in her presence, admired an Indian-shawl-pattern carpet in the drawing-room, a Pompadour writing-table carved and gilt, brocade curtains, a Japanese bowl of flowers on a round table among the newest books. When they heard the fair Dinah play at sight, without the smallest demur before seating herself at the piano, her supposed superiority seemed vast. To avoid carelessness or bad taste, Dinah resolved to keep up with fashions and the developments of luxury by an active correspondence with Anna Grossetete, her bosom friend from Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ school.

Anna, thanks to a fine fortune, had married the Comte de Fontaine’s third son. Thus those who visited La Baudraye were perpetually piqued by Dinah’s success in leading fashion; do what they would, they were always behind, or, as they say on the turf, distanced.

While these trifles aroused malignant envy among Sancerre’s ladies, Dinah’s conversation and wit bred absolute aversion. In her ambition to keep her mind at Parisian levels of brilliancy, Madame de la Baudraye allowed no vacuous small talk, no old-fashioned compliments, no pointless remarks; she would never endure the yelping of tittle-tattle, the backstairs slander that forms provincial talk. She liked to hear of discoveries in science and art, the latest pieces at the theatres, the newest poems, and by airing the cant words of the day she made a show of uttering thoughts.

The Abbé Duret, cure of Sancerre, an old man of a lost type of clergy and a man of the world with a liking for cards, was delighted at Madame de la Baudraye’s coming, and they got on admirably. The sous-préfet, one Vicomte de Chargeboeuf, was pleased to find in her drawing-room an oasis where there was a truce to provincial life. As to Monsieur de Clagny, the public prosecutor, his admiration for the fair Dinah kept him tied to Sancerre. The enthusiastic lawyer refused all promotion and became a quite pious adorer of this angel of grace and beauty. He was a tall, lean man, with a minatory countenance set off by terrible eyes in deep black circles under enormous eyebrows; and his eloquence, unlike his love-making, could be incisive.

Monsieur Gravier was a little, round man who in the days of the Empire had been a charming ballad-singer; it was this accomplishment that had won him the high position of Paymaster-General of the forces. Having mixed himself up in certain matters in Spain with generals then in opposition, he had used these connections with the Minister, who, in consideration of the place he had lost, promised him the Receivership at Sancerre, and then allowed him to pay for the appointment. The frivolous spirit and light tone of the Empire had become ponderous in Monsieur Gravier; he did not, or would not, understand the wide difference between manners under the Restoration and under the Empire. Still, he conceived himself far superior to Monsieur de Clagny; his style was in better taste—buff waistcoat, gray trousers, neat, tightly fitting coats; he wore a fashionable silk tie slipped through a diamond ring—while the lawyer dressed only in black—coat, trousers, and waistcoat alike, often shabby.

These four men were the first to go into ecstasies over Dinah’s cultivation, good taste, and refinement, and pronounced her a woman of most superior mind. Then the women said to each other, “Madame de la Baudraye must laugh at us behind our back.”

This view, more or less correct, kept them from visiting at La Baudraye. Dinah, attainted and convicted of pedantry because she spoke grammatically, was nicknamed the Sappho of Saint-Satur. At last everybody made insolent game of the great qualities of the woman who had thus roused the ladies’ enmity. They ended by denying a superiority—after all merely comparative—which emphasized their ignorance and would not forgive it. Where the whole population is hunch-backed, a straight shape is the monstrosity; Dinah was regarded as monstrous and dangerous, and she found herself in a desert.

Astonished at seeing the neighborhood women only at long intervals and for visits of a few minutes, Dinah asked Monsieur de Clagny the reason. “You are too superior a woman to be liked by other women,” said the lawyer. Monsieur Gravier, when questioned by the forlorn fair, only after much entreaty replied: “Well, lady fair, you are not satisfied to be merely charming. You are clever and well educated, you know every book that comes out, you love poetry, you are a musician, and you talk delightfully. Women cannot forgive so much superiority.”

Men said to Monsieur de la Baudraye, “You who have such a Superior Woman for a wife are very fortunate,” and at last he himself would say, “I who have a Superior Woman for a wife am very fortunate,” etc. Madame Piedefer, flattered through her daughter, also allowed herself to say such things—“My daughter, who is a very Superior Woman, was writing yesterday to Madame de Fontaine such and such a thing.” Those who know the world—France, Paris—know how true it is that many celebrities are thus created.

Two years later, by the end of 1825, Dinah de la Baudraye was accused of preferring only men as visitors; soon it was said she did not care for women—and that became a crime. Not a trifling action escaped criticism or misrepresentation. After making every sacrifice a well-bred woman could make and placing herself entirely in the right, Madame de la Baudraye was so rash as to say to a false friend who condoled with her isolation:

“I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!”

The remark produced a terrible effect in Sancerre and was cruelly retorted on the Sappho of Saint-Satur when, being childless after five years of marriage, little de la Baudraye became a byword for laughter. To explain the provincial witticism, recall the Bailli de Ferrette—some may have known him—of whom it was said he was the bravest man in Europe for daring to walk on his legs and who was accused of putting lead in his shoes to save himself from being blown away. Monsieur de la Baudraye, a sallow, almost diaphanous creature, would have been engaged by the Bailli de Ferrette as first gentleman-in-waiting if that diplomatist had been the Grand Duke of Baden rather than merely his envoy.

Monsieur de la Baudraye’s legs were so thin that, for decency, he wore false calves; his thighs were like the arms of an average man, his body not unlike that of a cockchafer, and he would have made an advantageous foil to the Bailli de Ferrette. As he walked his leg-pads often twisted onto his shins; he thanked anyone who warned him. He wore knee-breeches, black silk stockings, and a white waistcoat until 1824. After the marriage he adopted blue trousers and boots with heels, which made Sancerre declare that he had added two inches to his stature so as to come up to his wife’s chin. For ten years he was always seen in the same little bottle-green coat with large white-metal buttons and a black stock that accentuated his cold, stingy face, lighted by gray-blue eyes as keen and passionless as a cat’s. Gentle, as men with fixed habits are, he seemed to please his wife by never contradicting her; he allowed her to do the talking and moved with the deliberate tenacity of an insect.

Dinah, adored for her unrivaled beauty and admired for her cleverness by the most gentlemanly men of the place, encouraged their admiration by conversations it was later said she prepared beforehand. Finding her listeners rapt, she began to listen to herself, enjoyed haranguing them, and at last regarded her friends as the chorus in a tragedy, there only to give her cues. In fact, she had a fine collection of phrases and ideas, derived either from books or by assimilating the opinions of her companions, and thus became a sort of mechanical instrument, going off on a round of phrases as soon as some chance remark released the spring. To her credit, Dinah was full of knowledge and read everything—medical books, statistics, science, jurisprudence—because she did not know how to spend her days once she had reviewed her flower-beds and given orders to the gardener. Gifted with an excellent memory and the knack some women have for hitting the right word, she could talk on any subject with the lucidity of a studied style. Men came from Cosne, La Charité, Nevers, and from Léré, Vailly, Argent, Blancafort, and Aubigny to be introduced to Madame de la Baudraye, as in Switzerland they were introduced to Madame de Staël. Those who heard the tunes emitted by this musical snuff-box went away amazed and told such wonders of Dinah that all the women ten leagues round grew jealous.

There is an indescribable headiness in the admiration we inspire, or in the effect of playing a part, which keeps criticism from reaching the idol. An atmosphere, perhaps from unceasing nervous tension, forms a halo through which the world below is seen. How otherwise account for the perennial good faith that leads to repeated presentments of the same effects, and the constant ignoring of warnings given by children, so dreaded by parents, or by husbands familiar with the peacock airs of their wives? Monsieur de la Baudraye had the frankness of a man who opens an umbrella at the first drop of rain. When his wife started on Negro emancipation or prison reform, he would take up his little blue cap and vanish without a sound, certain he could get to Saint-Thibault to see off a cargo of puncheons and return an hour later to find the discussion near its close. Or, if he had no business, he would walk on the Mall, which commanded the Loire valley panorama, and take a draught of fresh air while his wife performed a sonata in words or a dialectical duet.

Once established as a Superior Woman, Dinah was eager to prove her devotion to remarkable creations of art. She threw herself into propaganda for the romantic school, extending Art to poetry, painting, literature, sculpture, furniture, and opera. She became a medievalist and prized Renaissance treasures, employing her allies as commission agents. Soon after marriage she acquired the Rougets’ furniture, sold at Issoudun in early 1824. She purchased good things in Nivernais and the Haute-Loire. At New Year and on her birthday her friends never failed to give curiosities. These fancies pleased Monsieur de la Baudraye; they made him seem to sacrifice a few crowns to his wife’s taste. In fact his land mania allowed him to think of nothing but the estate of Anzy.

These “antiquities” then cost much less than modern furniture. In five or six years the anteroom, dining-room, two drawing-rooms, and boudoir Dinah arranged on La Baudraye’s ground floor—every spot, even the staircase—were crammed with masterpieces collected in the four adjacent departments. Called “queer” by neighbors, they suited Dinah. All these marvels, soon to be the rage, struck strangers introduced to her; they came expecting something unusual and found their expectations surpassed when, behind a bower of flowers, they saw catacombs full of old things, piled as Sommerard used to pile them—that “Old Mortality” of furniture. These finds served as springs which, turned by a question, played off essays on Jean Goujon, Michel Colombe, Germain Pilon, Boulle, Van Huysum, and Boucher, the great native painter of Le Berry; on Clodion, the carver of wood; on Venetian mirrors; on Brustolone, an Italian tenor who was the Michael-Angelo of boxwood and holm oak; on the thirteenth through seventeenth centuries, on Bernard de Palissy’s glazes, Petitot’s enamels, Dürer’s engravings—whom she called Dur—on illuminations on vellum, on Gothic architecture, early decorated, flamboyant and pure—enough to rouse both old and young.

Possessed with the idea of waking up Sancerre, Madame de la Baudraye tried to form a so-called literary circle. The Presiding Judge, Monsieur Boirouge, who had a house and garden as part of the Popinot-Chandier property, favored the notion. The wily Judge discussed rules with Madame de la Baudraye; he proposed to figure as one of the founders and to let the house for fifteen years to the literary club. Within a year the members were playing dominoes, billiards, and bouillotte, and drinking mulled wine, punch, and liqueurs. A few elegant suppers were given and some masked balls during Carnival. As to literature—there were the newspapers. Politics and business were discussed. Monsieur de la Baudraye was constantly there—on his wife’s account, as she joked.

This result deeply grieved the Superior Woman, who despaired of Sancerre, and therefore collected the wit of the neighborhood in her own drawing-room. Nevertheless, despite the efforts of Messieurs de Chargeboeuf, Gravier, and de Clagny, of the Abbé Duret and the two chief magistrates, of a young doctor, and a young Assistant Judge—all blind admirers of Dinah—there were occasions when, weary of discussion, they allowed excursions into agreeable frivolity, the common basis of worldly conversation. Monsieur Gravier called this “from grave to gay.” The Abbé Duret’s rubber made another pleasing variety on the oracle’s monologues. The three rivals, tired of keeping their minds at the “high range of discussion,” but not daring to confess it, would sometimes turn with ingratiating hints to the old priest.

“Monsieur le Curé is dying for his game,” they would say.

The wily priest lent himself readily to the little trick. He protested.

“We should lose too much by ceasing to listen to our inspired hostess!” and so he would incite Dinah’s magnanimity to take pity at last on her dear Abbé.

This bold manoeuvre, a device of the Sous-préfet’s, was repeated with such skill that Dinah never suspected her slaves of escaping to the prison yard, so to speak, of the cardtable; they would leave her one of the younger functionaries to harry.

One young landowner, the dandy of Sancerre, fell from Dinah’s favor after some rash demonstrations. Having solicited admission to this circle, where he flattered himself he could snatch the blossom from the constituted authorities who guarded it, he was unfortunate enough to yawn in the middle of an explanation Dinah was favoring him with—for the fourth time, it is true—on the philosophy of Kant. Monsieur de la Thaumassière, grandson of the historian of Le Berry, was thenceforth regarded as a man entirely bereft of soul and brains.

The three devotees en titre each submitted to these exorbitant demands on their mind and attention in hope of a crowning triumph when Dinah should become human; for none dared imagine she would give up her innocence as a wife till she had lost all illusions. In 1826, when she was surrounded by adorers, Dinah completed her twentieth year, and the Abbé Duret kept her in a sort of fervid Catholicism; so her worshipers had to be content to overwhelm her with little attentions and small services, only too happy to be taken for the carpet-knights of this sovereign lady by strangers admitted to spend an evening or two at La Baudraye.

“Madame de la Baudraye is a fruit that must be left to ripen.” This was Monsieur Gravier’s opinion, who was waiting.

As to the lawyer, he wrote four-page letters, to which Dinah replied in soothing words as she walked, leaning on his arm, round and round the lawn after dinner.

Madame de la Baudraye, thus guarded by three passions and always under the eye of her pious mother, escaped the malignity of slander. It was evident to all Sancerre that no two of these three men would ever leave the third alone with Madame de la Baudraye; their jealousy was a comedy to lookers-on.

To reach Saint-Thibault from Caesar’s Gate there is a way much shorter than that by the ramparts, down what in mountainous districts is called a coursière, at Sancerre le Casse-cou, or Break-neck Alley. The name fits a steep, stony path shut in by high banks of vineyards. By the Break-neck the distance from Sancerre to La Baudraye is much abridged. The ladies of the place, jealous of the Sappho of Saint-Satur, were wont to walk on the Mall, looking down this Longchamp of the bigwigs, whom they would stop and engage in conversation—sometimes the Sous-préfet and sometimes the Public Prosecutor—who would listen with every sign of impatience or uncivil absence of mind. As the turrets of La Baudraye are visible from the Mall, many a younger man came to contemplate Dinah’s abode while envying the ten or twelve privileged persons who might spend their afternoons with the Queen of the neighborhood.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was not slow to discover the advantage he, as Dinah’s husband, held over his wife’s adorers, and he used them openly, obtaining remission of taxes and winning two lawsuits. In every litigation he used the Public Prosecutor’s name so effectively that matters were carried no further; like many undersized men, he was contentious and litigious in business, though in the gentlest manner.

At the same time, the more plainly guiltless she appeared, the less conceivable Madame de la Baudraye’s position seemed to prying women. Frequently, at the Présidente de Boirouge’s house, ladies of a certain age would spend whole evenings discussing the La Baudraye household among themselves. They all suspected a mystery, a secret such as always interests women experienced in life. And, in fact, at La Baudraye one of those slow, monotonous conjugal tragedies was being played out which would have remained forever unknown if the merciless scalpel of the nineteenth century, driven by the demand for novelty, had not dissected the darkest corners of the heart, or at least those which the decency of past centuries left unopened. That domestic drama sufficiently accounts for Dinah’s immaculate virtue during her early married life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A young woman whose school triumphs sprang from pride, and whose first scheme in life had succeeded, was unlikely to rest on her laurels. Frail as Monsieur de la Baudraye seemed, he was an unexpectedly good match for Mademoiselle Dinah Piedefer. What motive had this country landowner, at forty-four, in marrying a girl of seventeen—and what could his wife make of the bargain? These were Dinah’s first thoughts.

The little man never behaved as his wife expected. He let her take the five precious acres around La Baudraye for pleasure grounds, and paid, almost generously, the seven or eight thousand francs Dinah required for house improvements, enabling her to buy furniture at the Rougets’ sale and redecorate in varied styles—Mediaeval, Louis XIV, Pompadour. For a while Dinah believed either he was not so miserly as reputed or that she had great influence over him. The illusion lasted a year and a half.

After his second journey to Paris Dinah discovered in him the coldness of a provincial miser where money was concerned. The first time she asked for supplies and played sweetness, the little man bluntly set strict sums: two hundred francs a month for her personal expenses, twelve hundred francs a year to Madame Piedefer as a charge on La Hautoy—two hundred more than the marriage settlement—and nothing explicitly for housekeeping. He reminded her that as a bachelor he had lived on fifteen hundred francs a year and now spent six thousand including rates and repairs; that a winegrower’s returns could be ruined by weather, by bankrupt clients, or bad speculation; and that small owners must count on the minimum, keep a year’s income in hand, and reckon on no more than two-thirds of returns.

Any resistance is enough to make a woman vow to subdue it; Dinah flung herself against a will of iron masked with gentleness. She tried to fill him with jealousy and alarms, but his confidence was insolent. He left her, when he went to Paris, with Medor’s conviction in Angelique’s fidelity. When she affected cold disdain to nettle him, Monsieur de la Baudraye gazed at her with fixed eyes, like a cat that waits when a blow is threatened. His mute indifference unnerved the young wife of twenty; she could not understand the selfish quiescence of a man who regulated his life with clocklike regularity. So the little man always evaded, while she always struck out—ten feet above his head.

Dinah’s fits of fury at being condemned to La Baudraye and Sancerre can be imagined—she who had dreamed of handling a fortune and managing the dwarf whom she at first humored to command. Hoping to appear on Paris’s greater stage, she accepted the vulgar incense of her admirers with a view to seeing Monsieur de la Baudraye’s name drawn from the electoral urn; she supposed him ambitious after seeing him return thrice from Paris, each time a step higher in social rank. But when she struck at his heart, it was like tapping on marble. The man who had been Receiver-General, Referendary, Master of Appeals, Officer of the Legion of Honor, and Royal Commissioner was but a mole throwing up little hills round a vineyard. Lamentations to the Public Prosecutor, the Sous-préfet, even Monsieur Gravier, increased their devotion to this supposed victim; like many women she never mentioned her speculative schemes, and, finding such speculation vain, she ceased to speculate.

Dinah, tossed by mental storms, was undecided when, in the autumn of 1827, news came of the Baron de la Baudraye’s purchase of the estate of Anzy. Then the little man showed a pride and glee that for months altered Dinah’s ideas; she fancied some hidden greatness when she found him applying for a patent of entail. In his triumph he cried, “Dinah, you shall be a countess yet!” There followed a patched-up reunion between husband and wife, the kind that can never endure and only humiliates a woman whose apparent superiority is unreal while her unseen superiority is genuine. Dinah, ridiculous from the perversity of her cleverness, had real qualities of soul which circumstances did not bring to light; provincial life debased her small change of wit daily. Monsieur de la Baudraye, devoid of soul, strength, and wit, seemed a man of character simply by pursuing a fixed plan he was too feeble to change.

Their lives entered a first phase of six years during which Dinah became utterly provincial. Paris has many kinds of women—the duchess, the financier’s wife, the ambassadress, the minister’s wife, and ladies on both banks of the Seine—but in the country there is but one kind: the provincial woman. This remark points to a sore of modern society. France in the nineteenth century divides into two broad zones—Paris and the provinces. The provinces are jealous of Paris; Paris barely thinks of the provinces but to demand money. Once Paris was the capital of the provinces and the court ruled the capital; now all Paris is the Court, and all the country is the town.

However lofty or clever a girl from any department may be, if she marries in the country and remains there she inevitably becomes the provincial woman. The commonplace of second-rate ideas, indifference to dress, and the culture of vulgar people swamp the sublimer essence hidden in youth; everything decays. How should it be otherwise? Country girls see none but provincials; their choice lies among mediocrities; provincial fathers marry daughters to provincial sons; crossing races isn’t thought of, and thus mind degenerates. Mankind becomes dwarfed in mind and body, for the fatal principle of conformity of fortune governs matrimonial alliances. Men of talent, artists, superior brains—every bird of brilliant plumage—flies to Paris. The provincial woman, inferior in herself, is also inferior through her husband. How can she be happy under this twofold consciousness?

A third element deepens the aridity. One flattering balm for a woman is the assurance of being something in the life of a superior man chosen by herself. But in the country husbands are often inferior beings, and bachelors are no better. When a provincial wife commits her “little sin,” she falls for some local dandy who is at best merely well dressed; she knows her fancy is chasing the commonplace. Dinah was preserved from this danger by her pride and the conviction of her own superiority. Even if not guarded by her mother’s presence, her pride and sense of her destiny would have protected her. Flattered by admirers, she saw no lover among them. No man realized the poetical ideal she and Anna Grossetete had sketched. Stirred by homage, she asked herself who she would choose; she preferred Monsieur de Chargeboeuf, a gentleman of good family, but his cold nature, selfishness, and narrow ambitions repelled her. He had deserted a woman he had loved when his family feared scandal. Monsieur de Clagny, whose mind appealed to hers and whose ambition was founded on love, Dinah thought perfectly odious. When condemned to six years at Sancerre she nearly accepted Chargeboeuf’s devotion, but he was appointed to a prefecture and left. Clagny’s great satisfaction was that the new Sous-préfet was a married man whose wife befriended Dinah. The lawyer’s only rival now was Monsieur Gravier—the typical man of forty used by women while they laugh at him, whose hopes they remorselessly encourage. In six years among men introduced from twenty leagues round, none stirred Dinah’s anticipation of love or superior soul.

Thus none of Dinah’s choicest faculties developed; she swallowed many insults to her pride, constantly bruised by a husband who calmly played supernumerary in the drama of her life. Compelled to bury her wealth of love, she showed only surface to the world. Now and then she tried to rouse herself, to form some manly resolution; but she was kept in leading strings by the need for money. Slowly, despite ambitious protests and grievous recriminations of her mind, she underwent the provincial metamorphosis. Each day took a fragment of her spirited determination. She had rules for her person which she gradually abandoned. Though at first she kept up with fashions and novelties, she had to limit purchases to her allowance: instead of six hats, caps, or gowns she resigned herself to one gown each season; an admired bonnet served two seasons. Often her artistic sense led her to sacrifice personal requirements to obtain some bit of Gothic furniture. By the seventh year she had her morning dresses made at home by the best local needlewoman; her mother, husband, and friends pronounced her charming in these inexpensive costumes which credited her taste. Her ideas were imitated; with no standard of comparison she fell into provincial snares. If a Parisian has a defect she makes it an adornment; the provincial woman accepts the worst. Dinah, extremely slim, showed it to excess; reduced by dull weariness she began to look like a skeleton in clothes; those who saw her daily did not notice the gradual change.

This is a natural result of provincial life. In spite of marriage a young woman preserves her beauty for some time; the town is proud of her, but everyone sees her every day and perception dulled. If she loses color it is scarcely noticed; a little neglect is thought charming. When Dinah ceased to have a new dress each season she seemed to have yielded to the place’s philosophy.

Matters of speech, choice of words and ideas, rust as the body if not rubbed up in Paris; but provincialism most stamps gesture, gait, and movement. The provincial walks as if there were nothing to avoid; being known and always in her place, everyone makes way for her, and she loses the charm of the unforeseen. Life in common induces simian mimicry: gestures, tone, manner, attitudes, and countenance are copied. In six years Dinah sank to the pitch of her society. As she took on Monsieur de Clagny’s ideas she assumed his tone and unconsciously fell into masculine manners from seeing none but men; she thought laughing at their ridiculous traits kept her safe, but some hue of what she laughed at remained. A Parisian woman, seeing many examples of good taste, learns to seize the moment to appear advantageously; Madame de la Baudraye, accustomed to the stage, acquired an indefinable theatrical and domineering manner—the air of a prima donna—ironical smiles would soon have cured her in the capital.

After she had acquired these absurdities, deceived by worshipers and imagining them graces, a moment of terrible awakening came like an avalanche. In one day she was crushed by a frightful comparison.

In 1829, after Chargeboeuf’s departure, she expected the Baronne de Fontaine. Anna’s husband, now Director-General under the Minister of Finance, took advantage of leave on his father’s death to take his wife to Italy. Anna came to Sancerre; the meeting was disastrous. Anna, less handsome than Dinah at school, was now a thousand times handsomer despite fatigue and traveling dress. She stepped from an elegant chaise loaded with Paris milliners’ boxes and had a lady’s maid whose airs frightened Dinah. All the difference between a Paris woman and a provincial was evident: Anna spent six thousand francs a year on herself—the same sum that kept the household at La Baudraye.

In twenty-four hours the friends exchanged confidences; the Parisian, seeing herself so far superior to the phoenix of Mademoiselle Chamarolles’ school, showed kindness and attentions that stabbed Dinah. She understood Anna’s advantages lay on the surface while her own were buried. When Anna left, Madame de la Baudraye, by then twenty-two, fell into despair.

“What ails you?” asked Monsieur de Clagny, seeing her dejected.

“Anna,” she said, “has learned to live, while I have been learning to endure.”

A tragi-comedy was enacting in the house, in harmony with money quarrels and successive transformations—a drama the Abbe Duret and Monsieur de Clagny alone partly understood—when Dinah, in idleness or vanity, revealed the secret of her anonymous fame.

Though mixing verse and prose is odd in French literature, to give a fair idea of her unconfessed struggle it is necessary to analyze a poem born of her despair. Her patience broken by Chargeboeuf’s departure, Dinah took the Abbe’s advice to exhale her thoughts in verse—“You will find relief as those who write epitaphs or elegies,” he said. The strange production stirred the Allier, the Nievre, and the Cher, proud of a poet to rival Paris. Paquita la Sevillane, by Jan Diaz, was published in the Echo du Morvan, a review that for eighteen months survived provincial indifference. Some at Nevers thought Jan Diaz mocked the new school of eccentric verse imitating foreign mannerisms.

The poem began with a ballad praising Spain’s fragrant plains and Sevillian dancers and contrasting them with Northern squalor. Dinah then wrote a magnificent if affected description of Rouen—where she had never been—contrasting a manufacturing town’s life with Spain’s careless life, poetry versus sordid money-making. Jan Diaz made Paquita a Seville native sovereign of her town at thirteen, loved by three toreadors, whose soldier-lover carried her away to Normandy only to leave for Russia. The parting and vows were painted with Byron-like feeling. Much of the poem describes Paquita’s sufferings waiting alone in Rouen; she lives on narcotics and dreams, almost dies, but remains faithful; the soldier returns pale and frozen by Russia, and she, true, is still beautiful. The poem then takes a bitter turn: Paquita, failing to find suitable luxury in Rouen, falls into vice, ending in a house of ill-fame described with stark realism. Finally she is faded and old, but it was she who once sang of Spain.

The poem, some six hundred lines, with seguidillas at beginning and end, had gloomy vigor that alarmed Dinah, who found herself admired in three departments under the cloak of anonymity. While she enjoyed success, she feared provincial malignity: if the secret leaked, other women would trace likenesses between writer and Paquita. Reflection came too late; she shuddered at having made “copy” of some of her woes. “Write no more,” said the Abbe. “You will cease to be a woman; you will be a poet.” Moulins, Nevers, Bourges were searched for Jan Diaz; Dinah remained impenetrable.

To remove suspicion, she wrote a charming two-canto poem on the Mass-Oak, a Nivernais legend: foes from Nevers and Saint-Saulge, about to battle in the forest of Faye, are stayed by a priest who, by saying Mass under an oak, makes them friends. This poem, far superior to Paquita, was less admired.

After these attempts Madame de la Baudraye, feeling herself a poet, had a light on her brow and a flash in her eyes that made her handsomer than ever. She cast longing looks at Paris, aspiring to fame—and fell back into her den at La Baudraye, daily squabbles with her husband, and a little circle whose characters had become a bore. Though literature gave relief—poetry echoed in her empty life and provided an outlet—it increased her hatred of the gray, ponderous provincial atmosphere.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After the Revolution of 1830, George Sand’s renewed fame revived interest in Le Berry, and La Châtre was envied as the birthplace of this rival to Madame de Staël and Camille Maupin. A provincial appetite for female authors emerged: towns eager to claim their own “Muses” celebrated any modest feminine talent. New, sometimes odd, doctrines about women’s social roles circulated; though French common sense remained, women were permitted to voice opinions they would not have a few years earlier.

Monsieur de Clagny made use of this atmosphere of permissiveness to collect the poems of Jan Diaz in a small volume printed by Desroziers at Moulins and wrote a short, mildly amusing notice for insiders. Practical jokes work while the author is unknown; they unravel once identity is revealed. The memoir presented as Jan Diaz’s—born at Bourges in 1807, son of a Spanish prisoner—was complete with names of professors at Bourges College and schoolfellows like Lousteau and Bianchon said to have known the dreamy, precocious boy. It included a school elegy called Tristesse; the poems Paquita la Sevillane and Le Chene de la Messe; three sonnets; descriptions of the Cathedral and Jacques Coeur’s house at Bourges; a tale called Carola; and an account of the poet’s miserable death near Château-Chinon, unknown even to the woman he had loved. These remains, published as a young man’s last work, were meant to move the provincial public.

Of the little yellow paper volume two hundred copies were printed; one hundred and fifty sold—about fifty per department—revealing how literary sentiment persisted in provinces more inclined to commerce than letters. Monsieur de Clagny gave away some copies; Dinah still had several wrapped in newspapers that had reviewed the book. Twenty copies sent to Paris papers were lost among editors. Nathan and several Southerners of Berry were deceived; Nathan wrote an article extolling the supposed great man.

Lousteau, alerted by schoolfellows who could not recall Jan Diaz, inquired at Sancerre and discovered Jan Diaz was a pseudonym adopted by a woman. In and around Sancerre, Madame de la Baudraye became the rage, hailed as the next George Sand. From Sancerre to Bourges a poem was praised that, at any other time, would have been hooted; provincial publics seldom choose the middle course, either exalting or denigrating extremes.

By then the Abbé who had counseled Madame de la Baudraye was dead; he would have opposed her plunge into public life. Three years of unrecognized effort had weighed on Dinah’s spirit, and she welcomed the clatter of fame as a consolation for thwarted ambitions. Poetry and dreams of celebrity, which had soothed her grief since meeting Anna Grossetête, no longer exhausted her morbid heart. Abbé Duret, who had comforted her with religion’s promise that God would reward brave suffering, could no longer restrain her from the opening to sin.

The wise old priest had once warned Dinah about her husband’s character, telling her the man was capable of hatred; women often refuse to believe such intensity in weak natures. Dinah, convinced her husband could not love, denied he could hate. The Abbé pointed out the difference: hatred and vengeance are distinct; narrow souls feed hatred with meanness and use it as a pretext for tyranny. He warned Dinah that Monsieur de la Baudraye might forgive an infidelity if he could profit by it, but would be implacable if she struck at the wound left by Monsieur Milaud of Nevers, making her life unbearable.

When the countryside—Nevers, Sancerre, Le Morvan, and Le Berry—prided itself on Madame de la Baudraye and lauded her as Jan Diaz, “little La Baudraye” felt humiliated. He alone knew the true source of Paquita la Sevillane. The work’s fame softened public judgment: people pitied Dinah, women delighted in a woman’s triumph over oppression, and never had she seemed higher in the neighborhood.

The withered old man showed no overt sign at first, but Dinah sometimes detected an icy venom in his gaze that belied his increased politeness. She finally perceived this was no mere domestic quarrel. Forcing an explanation produced the cold, impassive steel of his temperament. Dinah exploded, reproached him for the past eleven years, and made an intentional scene. “Little La Baudraye” listened phlegmatically in his chair and, as usual, outmaneuvered his wife. Dinah saw she had erred in writing; she vowed never to write another line—and kept the vow.

Sancerre fell into desolation. “Why did not Madame de la Baudraye compose any more verses?” became the universal cry. Meanwhile, Dinah had no enemies; guests pressed to visit her, introductions multiplied, and every week brought new callers. The wife of the presiding judge, a proud bourgeoise née Popinot-Chandier, hoped her son Gatien, twenty-two, might gain the favor of this “Superior Woman”—the title had replaced the earlier jesting nickname, “The Sappho of Saint-Satur.” For nine years the lady of the opposition praised her lavishly.

After many false starts and romantic reveries, Dinah succumbed. Seeing Monsieur de Clagny repeatedly sacrifice himself and finally decline a high appointment in Paris, she convinced herself he loved her. She overcame her repugnance and seemed willing to reward his constancy. This impulse formed a coalition in Sancerre to secure Monsieur de Clagny’s return in the next elections; Dinah dreamed of going to Paris in the wake of the new deputy.

Promises notwithstanding, the anticipated one hundred and fifty votes condensed into a disappointing fifty. Jealousy from President de Boirouge and Monsieur Gravier, who presumed the candidate’s influence over Dinah, and intrigues engineered by a young sous-préfet who was later made a préfet elsewhere, sabotaged the campaign. The young man who plotted against Clagny lamented leaving without having pleased Madame de la Baudraye.

The household, racked by troubles, preserved a calm surface; two ill-matched, resigned figures upheld social propriety—a yoke to Dinah. Why did she yearn to cast off the mask of the past twelve years? What made her daily more eager for widowhood? These illusions often deceive women: after trying to master Monsieur de la Baudraye, she had dreamed of motherhood; time passed in miserable disputes and melancholic resignation. Monsieur de Chargeboeuf had left her; thus the usual temptation to sin had been absent. Many women cling to hope and are led into infidelity only after long wandering in a labyrinth of secret woes. Dinah, with little impulse to abandon duty, did not even feel enough for Monsieur de Clagny to forgive his electoral defeat.

The move to the Château d’Anzy, the rearrangement of her treasures and curiosities now enriched by Philibert de Lorme’s setting, occupied her for months and gave her time to contemplate decisive steps. Struck by Lousteau’s reputation as a seducer, Dinah wished to meet him. She read his works and, less for his talent than for his conquests, she strove to lure him to the country. She suggested Sancerre should return one of its great men as deputy, had Gatien write to Bianchon as a supposed cousin, and persuaded a friend of the late Madame Lousteau to inflame the journalist’s ambitions by implying Sancerre was determined to elect a Parisian luminary.

Her motive was to encounter truly illustrious men and, if she fell, to have the fall gilded by fame. Neither Lousteau nor Bianchon replied at first; Bianchon, having won a professorial chair, could not leave his lectures. In September, during the 1836 vintage, both Parisians arrived in the province and found it absorbed in wine; no public demonstration was possible. “We have fallen flat,” said Lousteau.

By 1836 Lousteau, after sixteen years of struggle, aged by pleasure and penury, looked forty-eight rather than thirty-seven: balding, affectingly Byronic, with contrived misanthropy but still capable of flashes of fire. He dressed smartly to compensate for lost youth. Horace Bianchon, ribboned with the Legion of Honor, was fat, burly, patriarchal, with thick hair and a frame of a hard worker; his calm, prosaic air contrasted Lousteau’s theatrical decay.

The two men spent the morning unrecognized at the inn until Monsieur de Clagny learned of their presence. Madame de la Baudraye, desperate, sent Gatien—who had no vineyards—to beg them to spend days at Château d’Anzy. For a year Dinah had played chatelaine, wintering mostly at La Baudraye. Monsieur Gravier, the public prosecutor, the presiding judge, and Gatien arranged a banquet to present the great men to the town’s literati.

When told the beautiful Madame de la Baudraye was Jan Diaz, the Parisians went to Anzy in Gatien’s wagonette. The youth, sincerely believing in his heroine, described her as the handsomest woman locally, a Superior Woman who might stir Paris—hence Bianchon’s and Lousteau’s suppressed astonishment at finding, on Anzy’s steps, a lady in thin black cashmere, a short riding-habit effect, and a black velvet cap; thick curls framed a pretty face, with eyes faded by weary life. In Le Berry the costume masked the romantic affectations of the Superior Woman.

Seeing her affectations—indeed affectations of soul and mind—the visitors adopted serious faces to listen to Madame de la Baudraye’s set speech of thanks. Dinah led them round the lawn, decorated with flower vases. Lousteau asked why so handsome and apparently superior a woman had remained buried in the country and what she did to make life endurable. She answered in a declamatory portrait of provincial female existence: either utter despair or dull resignation; a desert feeding only etiolated ideas that cannot fertilize the soil. Indifference, she declared, is either despair or resignation, and women adopt pursuits—jam-making, washing, harvest work, embroidering, piano practice, pious argument, card-playing, dancing the same dances for years, polite visits, discussions of gowns—that pass the time.

Conversation, she said, revolves around hidden intrigues, proposed marriages, jealousies, and sour remarks; aging before thirty is common; ruined complexions and attempts to justify condition lead some to denounce Parisian passion and glorify provincial squalor. Bianchon praised her courage. Gatien nudged Lousteau, who promised to steal her phrasing for an article. Dinah, seeing Lousteau’s irony, warned that provincial women can be dangerous in attachment: a fleeting acquaintance might lodge in a woman’s heart and become an occupation; she studies, compares, dreams, and refuses to relinquish her dream. Country-bred women, she said, can surprise like toys, and their sudden, close, intense attachments can wreck reputations.

Lousteau, cynical, called provincial women quick in proceedings; Dinah countered that Paris corrupted with its centralizing influence—Paris as monster radiating seven leagues of mischief—destroyed provincial individuality. Bianchon and Lousteau debated English aristocracy’s country-season life; Dinah praised English women as charming, and in that moment spotted her mother, Madame Piedefer, a tall, lean woman with a red face, arriving. She introduced her visitors to this ambitious figure. After a private moment, Gatien reported Lousteau’s remark that Dinah was “the greatest chatterbox.” Lousteau, who mocked illusions, told Bianchon he had no illusions left: he saw things as they were; Dinah had dried her mother like a partridge overcooked.

Gatien told Dinah what Lousteau had said during dinner; she curbed her loquacity for a while and directed her advances to Bianchon. Midway through the evening she again treated Lousteau kindly. Small mean actions for trifling ends are often committed; thus Dinah, who would not sacrifice herself for a fool and who led a life of unuttered poetry, remembered her album and stepped down from her peak.

Madame de la Baudraye had acquired the autograph mania—an oblong album, two-thirds blank, where the Baroness de Fontaine had secured lines from Rossini, bars by Meyerbeer, formulaic verses from Victor Hugo, a verse from Lamartine, a few words from Béranger, the beginning of Télémaque by George Sand, Scribe’s lines on an umbrella, and notes from Berlioz. Monsieur de Clagny had added a song by Lacenaire, two lines from Fieschi, and a short note from Napoleon; Monsieur Gravier had procured signatures from Mademoiselle Mars and other celebrated actors and singers. This album was precious to Dinah; she was the only person for ten leagues with such a thing.

Autograph mania had spread among many young ladies, who filled albums with trivial quotations. Dinah, fearing to detain her guests only two days, begged Bianchon to enrich her volume. The doctor amused Lousteau by showing the first page’s inscriptions: De Clagny’s aphorism—“What makes the populace dangerous is that it has in its pocket an absolution for every crime”—and Bianchon’s own axiom below—about the distinction between Napoleon and a water-carrier being a social one, Nature taking no account of it, and Democracy appealing to Nature. Lousteau, indulgent and flippant, promised to contribute something sharp but modest, pleading his insignificance and saying he would need time to improvise a suitably bitter reflection. Dinah expressed hopes he might take a fortnight, and handed him the book.

Thus the evening closed with autographs added to the album—a small, human proof of Dinah’s social triumph and of the provincial hunger for recognition, flattery, and the trappings of literary fame.

At five the next morning the Chateau d’Anzy was astir. Little La Baudraye had arranged a day’s shooting for his Parisians—less for sport than to exhibit the twelve hundred acres he planned to reclaim, a project costing about a hundred thousand francs and promising an annual increase of thirty to sixty thousand francs to the estate.

“Do you know why the Public Prosecutor has not come out with us?” Gatien Boirouge asked Monsieur Gravier.

“He told us he must sit to-day; the minor cases are before the Court,” Gravier answered.

“And did you believe that?” cried Gatien. “My papa said, ‘Monsieur Lebas will not join you early, for Monsieur de Clagny has begged him as his deputy!’”

Gravier’s face changed. “Indeed! And Monsieur de la Baudraye is gone to La Charite!”

“Why meddle?” Bianchon reproved Gatien.

“Horace is right,” Lousteau added. “Why trouble about each other? You squander time in frivolities.”

At a copse Gravier went off with the two great men and Gatien under a keeper’s guidance through a ravine. When they reached a clearing Bianchon suggested waiting for Gravier.

“You may be a great physician,” said Gatien, “but ignorant of provincial life. Gravier will be back at the chateau within twenty minutes.” He checked his watch. “I couldn’t rest if Madame de la Baudraye were alone with Monsieur de Clagny. There are two of them now; Dinah will be guarded.”

“Ah ha! Then Dinah hasn’t decided?” Lousteau teased.

“So mamma thinks. I fear Monsieur de Clagny may have enchanted her. If she sees him as Attorney-General he may appear handsome; eloquence has privileges. Besides, Madame de la Baudraye is ambitious; she dislikes Sancerre and dreams of Paris.”

“You yourself hope to succeed the Public Prosecutor?” Lousteau smiled.

“Paris offers many women,” Gatien pleaded. “But at Sancerre, where women set up for sanctity, the handsomest keeps others at a distance. A young man of twenty-two may guess at her secrets if she treats him with some consideration.”

Bianchon dismissed Gatien’s alarm. Lousteau proposed a plot: after dinner they would tell lurid stories of murderous wives to watch the faces of Madame de la Baudraye and de Clagny.

That evening Lousteau returned an album to Dinah with an elegy—“Spleen”—portraying himself as a blighted poet. Dinah looked pitying; Clagny, suspicious, scowled at the sham melancholy. He sat down to play backgammon with the cure; Gravier placed a lamp to cast light on Dinah, who embroidered a wicker-paper basket. The three conspirators sat near.

“For whom the basket?” asked Lousteau.

“For Monsieur de la Baudraye,” she replied.

Lousteau mocked Parisians’ disbelief in women’s virtue; de Clagny complained of their literature’s focus on adultery. Étienne retorted: if you exclude all books that mention illicit love you must burn vast swathes of literature—Homer, Milton, Dante, Rousseau. “When you take the political attitude,” he added, “a judge of Tiberius’s day might have called Joseph’s wife an adulteress; Pilate would not recognize miracles. Judges always represent established interests—Pilate, Anytus, modern prosecutors. In their view Socrates and Jesus were criminals. So to call religions or revolutions impostures is to think like a public prosecutor.”

“Your argument is blasphemy,” said de Clagny.

“I grant it,” Etienne answered, “but not maliciously. If there were many men like me, government would be impossible.”

Madame Piedefer made arch faces. Bianchon warned Lousteau not to upset the company with talk of religion. Gravier, if a romancer, said, would favor wronged husbands; he had seen dramatic, even heroic avengers. Bianchon and Lousteau agreed that deceived husbands could be admirable, and Lousteau promised a chilling story of revenge—one told by Charles Nodier about a Chevalier de Beauvoir.

He recounted the tale: after the 18th Brumaire, a Maille envoy, Chevalier de Beauvoir, sent by Chouans, was arrested on landing. Though his papers were in order and he bore himself well, adhering to minute orders meant he was detained and sent to the Castle of l’Escarpe, a fortress on precipitous rocks.

The commandant, a Corsican, treated him well; his pretty wife took a fancy to Beauvoir. Whether imprudence occurred the Chevalier never explained, but the jealous commandant imprisoned him in a dungeon, fettered and fed on black bread. In despair Beauvoir planned escape when a turnkey, secretly paid, offered him a file and a knotted rope and explained how to cut a bar and lower himself by knots. Beauvoir filed the bars, disguised the breach, hid his rope, and one murky night let himself down. At the last knot he found no footing: the distance to the rocks was a hundred feet. Suspecting a trap, he climbed back, feigned an attempted escape by leaving his rope, and lay in wait. When the jailer returned and opened the door, Beauvoir struck him dead with the iron bar he’d filed out, stripped him, donned his clothes, mimicked his gait, and walked out with the unsuspecting warders’ confidence.

The tale did not move de Clagny or Dinah; the conspirators watched them but found coolness. Bianchon then told “La Grande Bretèche”—the Spanish grandee walled in a cupboard—ending to dramatic effect. Madame de la Baudraye marvelled at a woman’s courage to risk such things for love; Gravier offered a Spanish anecdote of a party in Madrid when the surgeon-major told of being kidnapped to deliver a woman’s child in secret.

He described being forced, blindfolded, into a house where the husband snored nearby, the woman in labor veiled and biting a handkerchief, the lover and waiting-woman attending. Though the infant proved dead, the surgeon cut and saved the mother. The Spaniard lover, distraught, carried off the body, while the surgeon later, haunted by fear, saw the Spaniard’s burning eyes in the garden; the surgeon was later murdered—stabbed by the lady’s husband—after being shown the amputated arm of someone bearing a telltale mole.

Gravier added that during the restoration of Ferdinand VII, he met a Spanish grandee with a motionless right arm and a lovely wife whose past horrors might explain its loss. The company agreed Spain bred strange passions.

De Clagny then related a French crime: a Tours woman, whose husband disappeared during the Loire army’s disbandment and who had been devout, confessed to bleeding him to death, salting the body, packing it into casks, and throwing pieces into the Loire; the head remained pickled, discovered by the examining judge. She was condemned and executed, her earlier repentance having softened public feeling. Bianchon and the others reflected on the hidden tragedies behind private life and the difficulty of judging crimes within marriage.

At bedtime the bachelors gathered in a passage, candle in hand, to discuss their plan. Gravier revealed the scheme: they would seal the doors of Madame de la Baudraye and the Public Prosecutor to catch any nocturnal tryst. Robbing barefoot like thieves, Bianchon, Lousteau, Gravier, and Gatien stuck hairs across the two doors with wax—a trick common in country houses—and agreed to inspect them at five. To their amazement the hairs were still intact on both doors in the morning.

“This alters the matter!” cried Lousteau. If Dinah were virtuous, she was worth the effort. He resolved to press his suit.

In the garden Lousteau found Madame de la Baudraye and engaged her. She invited him to La Baudraye to learn more of provincial life and declared provincials simple but true. Étienne told her he had only teased Gatien into confessing his adoration; her silence at dinner had betrayed an indiscretion, he said, but he assured her her innocence was proven. “If you fancied the magistrate,” he added, “you’d have lost my respect. I love perfection; I cannot believe the Public Prosecutor matches you.”

Madame de la Baudraye’s manner warmed. Lousteau spoke bluntly of his life: he had sought pleasure but never loved; perhaps he had defects that made him less lovable. Dinah heard Paris through him—its daring axioms and decadence—and was intrigued. Étienne, acting the Parisian wit, charmed her with tales of the capital; she spent one of her happiest days with him and Bianchon.

Lousteau flattered her by disparaging a great female celebrity of Le Berry, implying Dinah could rival her; this intoxicated Dinah. Clagny, Gravier, and Gatien thought she was warmer to Étienne than before. Their exaggerated praise spread through Sancerre; sixteen people came to Anzy that evening, some in family coaches, others in wagonettes and on hired horses. Dinah, expecting triumph, lit up the huge drawing-room and unveiled the furniture; the Parisians studied the arrivals’ attitudes and speeches.

Visitors displayed ancestral laces and faded honors; the Presidente Boirouge complained of chest pains to Bianchon, who prescribed “tea after dinner” as an English remedy. Conversation turned to Bianchon’s possible candidacy for elections; Lousteau, recalling the doctor’s fame, proposed him as a candidate, but Bianchon demurred—science and law differ. Madame Chandier and the Mayor’s wife compared incomes and boots; Gatien boasted Étienne made twenty thousand francs a year.

The footman brought letters and proofs; Lousteau left a packet for Bianchon. Dinah picked up a printers’ wrapper, surprised to find literature pursuing them. Bianchon noticed a used proof folded round the journal proofs: a serial romance, “Olympia, or Roman Revenge.” He handed it to Lousteau, who read a page aloud. From the fragment—brigands in a cavern, a vanished Rinaldo, a Duchess who forgets her glove—the Parisians were amused while Lousteau mocked the author’s lack of “local color,” and Bianchon defended censored writers. The provincial ladies and men stared at Lousteau, half scandalized, half entertained.

Thus the evening passed—stories, parodies, and flirtations weaving together the lives of Parisians and provincials at the Chateau d’Anzy.

“And yet it is a novel of the time of the Empire,” said Lousteau.

“By the way the brigand is made to speak,” said Monsieur Gravier, “it is evident the author knew nothing of Italy. Banditti do not allow themselves such graceful conceits.”

Madame Gorju, seeing Bianchon pensive and glancing at her daughter Mademoiselle Euphemie Gorju, owner of a fair fortune—“What a rhodomontade!” said she. “The prescriptions you write are worth more than all that rubbish.”

The Mayoress had worked up this speech, thinking it evidence of strong judgment.

“Well, madame, we must be lenient, we have but twenty pages out of a thousand,” said Bianchon, looking at Mademoiselle Gorju, whose figure threatened trouble after her first child.

“Well, Monsieur de Clagny,” said Lousteau, “we were talking yesterday of the forms of revenge invented by husbands. What do you say to those invented by wives?”

“I say,” replied the Public Prosecutor, “that the romance is not by a Councillor of State, but by a woman. For extravagant inventions the imagination of women far outdoes that of men; witness Frankenstein by Mrs. Shelley, Leone Leoni by George Sand, the works of Anne Radcliffe, and the Nouveau Prométhée of Camille de Maupin.”

Dinah looked steadily at Monsieur de Clagny, giving him a chill, showing she regarded his examples as a reflection on Paquita la Sevillane.

“Pooh!” said little Baudraye, “the Duke of Bracciano, whom his wife puts into a cage and to whom she shows herself every night in the arms of her lover, will kill her—and you call that revenge?—Our laws and society are far more cruel.”

“Why, little La Baudraye is talking!” said Monsieur Boirouge to his wife.

“Why, the woman is left to live on a small allowance, the world turns its back on her; she has no more finery, and no respect—the two things, in my opinion, that make a woman,” said the little old man.

“But she has happiness!” said Madame de la Baudraye sententiously.

“No,” said the master of the house, lighting his candle to go to bed, “for she has a lover.”

“For a man who thinks of nothing but his vine-stocks and poles, he has some spunk,” said Lousteau.

“Well, he must have something!” replied Bianchon.

Madame de la Baudraye, the only one who could hear Bianchon’s remark, laughed knowingly and bitterly; the physician guessed the mystery of this woman’s life from her premature wrinkles.

But Dinah did not guess the ominous prophecy contained for her in her husband’s little speech, which Abbé Duret, if alive, would have elucidated. Little La Baudraye had detected in Dinah’s eyes, when she glanced at the journalist returning the ball of his jests, that swift and luminous flash of tenderness which gilds a woman’s eye when prudence is cast to the winds. Dinah paid no heed to her husband’s hint to observe proprieties any more than Lousteau had to her significant warnings on his arrival.

Any other man than Bianchon would have been surprised at Lousteau’s immediate success; but Bianchon, the doctor, was not nettled at Dinah’s marked preference for the newspaper rather than the prescriber. Dinah, famous and naturally more alive to wit than to fame, preferred contrast to similarity. Everything was against the physician—his frankness, simplicity, and his profession. Women who want to love—and Dinah wanted to love as much as to be loved—have an instinctive aversion to men absorbed in a calling; in spite of superiority, they are all women in the matter of encroachment. Lousteau, a poet, journalist, and libertine with a veneer of misanthropy, had that tinsel of intellect and led the half-idle life that attracts women. The blunt good sense and keen insight of the really great man weighed upon Dinah, who would not confess her own smallness even to herself. She thought—“The doctor is perhaps the better man, but I do not like him.”

She reflected on his professional duties, wondering whether a woman could ever be anything but a subject to a medical man who sees many subjects a day. Bianchon’s aphorism written in her album struck so directly at woman that Dinah could not fail to be hit. And Bianchon was leaving on the morrow; his practice required his return. What woman, short of Cupid’s dart in her heart, could decide in so short a time?

These little things, which lead to great catastrophes—having been seen in a mass by Bianchon—he pronounced the verdict he had come to as to Madame de la Baudraye in a few words to Lousteau, to the journalist’s great amazement.

While the two friends stood talking, a storm gathered in the Sancerre circle, who could not understand Lousteau’s paraphrases and vented it on their hostess. Far from finding in his talk the romance the Public Prosecutor, the Sous-préfet, the Presiding Judge, and his deputy had discovered—let alone Monsieur de la Baudraye and Dinah—the ladies by the tea-table took it as a practical joke and accused the Muse of Sancerre of having a finger in it. They had all looked forward to a delightful evening, and felt themselves mocked. Nothing makes provincial folks so angry as the notion of being made a laughing-stock by Paris folks.

Madame Piedefer left the table to say to her daughter, “Do go and talk to the ladies; they are quite annoyed by your behavior.”

Lousteau could not fail to see Dinah’s superiority over the best women of Sancerre: better dressed, graceful movements, complexion exquisitely white by candlelight—she stood out against old faces and shy girls like a queen in her court. Visions of Paris faded; Lousteau accepted the provincial setting; while impressed by the castle’s carved beauty and antique rooms, he valued the personality that completed this Renaissance gem. By the time the visitors from Sancerre had taken their leave one by one—each with an hour’s drive—when only Monsieur de Clagny, Monsieur Lebas, Gatien, and Monsieur Gravier remained who were to sleep at Anzy, the journalist had already changed his mind about Dinah. His opinion evolved as Madame de la Baudraye had audaciously prophesied at their first meeting.

“Ah, what things they will say about us on the drive home!” cried the mistress, returning after seeing the President and the Presidente to their carriage.

The rest of the evening had its pleasant side. In the intimacy of a small party each brought epigrams on the figure the Sancerre visitors had cut during Lousteau’s comments on the wrappers round the proofs.

“My dear fellow,” said Bianchon to Lousteau as they went to bed—they had an enormous room with two beds—“you will be the happy man of this woman’s choice—née Piedefer!”

“Do you think so?”

“It is quite natural. You are supposed here to have had many mistresses in Paris; and to a woman there is something indescribably inviting in a man whom other women favor—something attractive; is it that she prides herself on being remembered longer than the rest? that she appeals to his experience, as a sick man will pay more to a famous physician? or that she is flattered by reviving a world-worn heart?”

“Vanity and the senses count for so much in love affairs,” said Lousteau, “that there may be truth in all those hypotheses. However, if I remain, it will be because of the certificate of innocence, without ignorance, you have given Dinah. She is handsome, is she not?”

“Love will make her beautiful,” said the doctor. “And, after all, she will be a rich widow some day! And a child would secure her the life-interest in the Master of La Baudraye’s fortune—”

“Why, it is quite an act of virtue to make love to her,” said Lousteau, rolling up in the bed-clothes, “and tomorrow, with your help—yes, to-morrow, I—well, good-night.”

On the following day, Madame de la Baudraye, whom her husband had six months since given a pair of horses and an old carriage, decided to take Bianchon as far as Cosne, where he would take the Lyons diligence. She also took her mother and Lousteau, intending to drop her mother at La Baudraye, go on to Cosne with the two Parisians, and return alone with Etienne. She was elegantly dressed—bronze kid boots, gray silk stockings, a muslin dress, a green silk scarf with shaded fringe, and a pretty black lace bonnet with flowers. Lousteau assumed his war-paint—patent leather boots, English kerseymere trousers, a very open waistcoat showing a fine shirt and black brocade cravat, and a thin, short black riding-coat.

Monsieur de Clagny and Monsieur Gravier felt rather silly as they beheld the two Parisians in the carriage while they stood at the foot of the steps. Monsieur de la Baudraye, at the top waving his little hand in farewell to the doctor, smiled as Monsieur de Clagny said to Monsieur Gravier: “You should have escorted them on horseback.”

At this juncture, Gatien, riding Monsieur de la Baudraye’s quiet little mare, came out of the side road from the stables and joined the chaise.

“Ah, good,” said the Receiver-General, “the boy has mounted guard.”

“What a bore!” cried Dinah as she saw Gatien. “In thirteen years—for I have been married nearly thirteen years—I have never had three hours’ liberty.”

“Married, madame?” said the journalist with a smile. “You remind me of a saying of Michaud’s—he was witty! He was setting out for the Holy Land, and his friends remonstrated with him, urging his age, and the perils. ‘And then,’ said one, ‘you are married.’—‘Married!’ said he, ‘so little married.’”

Even rigid Madame Piedefer could not repress a smile.

“I should not be surprised to see Monsieur de Clagny mounted on my pony to complete the escort,” said Dinah.

“Well, if the Public Prosecutor does not pursue us, you can get rid of this little fellow at Sancerre. Bianchon must, of course, have left something on his table—the notes for the first lecture—and you can ask Gatien to go back to Anzy to fetch it.”

This plot put Madame de la Baudraye in high spirits. From the road between Anzy and Sancerre a glorious landscape of the Loire frequently appears, and the drive passed pleasantly, for Dinah was happy in finding herself understood. Love was discussed in theory—a subject allowing lovers in petto to take the measure, as it were, of each other’s heart. The journalist took a tone of refined corruption to prove that love obeys no law: character and social circumstance give infinite variety to its incidents; in love all is possible and true, and any woman, after resisting every temptation, may be carried off her feet in a few hours by a fancy, an internal whirlwind known only to God.

“Why,” he said, “is not that the key to all the adventures we have talked over these three days past?”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For three days Dinah’s imagination teemed with dangerous romances; the Parisians’ talk affected her like mischievous reading. Lousteau watched the effect of his manoeuvre, waiting to seize the moment when his prey, whose readiness lay hidden under irresolution, should grow quite dizzy.

Dinah wished to show La Baudraye to her two visitors, and the farce about the papers Bianchon had left at Anzy was played out. Gatien rode off to obey his sovereign; Madame Piedefer went shopping in Sancerre; and Dinah proceeded to Cosne with the two friends. Lousteau sat by the lady, Bianchon riding backwards. The two men talked with deep compassion for so fine a nature, ill understood in vulgar surroundings. Bianchon aided Lousteau by making fun of the Public Prosecutor, Monsieur Gravier, and Gatien; his genuine contempt silenced Madame de la Baudraye’s partisans.

“I perfectly understand the position you have maintained,” the doctor said as they crossed the Loire. “You were inaccessible except to that brain-love which often leads to heart-love; none of those men can disguise what, early in life, is disgusting to a refined woman's senses. To you, now, love is indispensable.”

“Indispensable!” cried Dinah, looking curiously at him. “Do you prescribe love to me?”

“If you live as you do now, in three years you will be hideous,” Bianchon replied, dictatorial.

“Monsieur!” Madame de la Baudraye was almost frightened.

“Forgive my friend,” said Lousteau lightly. “He is always the medical man, and to him love is hygiene. But he is disinterested—it is for your sake he speaks—as is evident, since he is starting in an hour.”

At Cosne a crowd gathered round the old repainted chaise, with arms granted by Louis XIV to the new La Baudraye—bearing scales and cross-crosslets, with two greyhounds as supporters—and the ironical motto, Deo sic patet fides et hominibus, had been inflicted on the converted Calvinist by Hozier.

“Let us get out; they will come and find us,” the Baroness said, telling her coachman to keep watch.

Dinah took Bianchon’s arm, and the doctor set off along the Loire at a pace that left the journalist behind. The physician had winked to indicate he meant to help Lousteau.

“You have been attracted by Etienne,” Bianchon said to Dinah; “he has appealed to your imagination; last night we were talking about you. He loves you. But he is frivolous and difficult to hold; his poverty compels him to live in Paris, while everything condemns you to Sancerre. Take a lofty view. Make Lousteau your friend; do not ask too much of him; he will come three times a year, and you will owe him your beauty, happiness and fortune. Monsieur de la Baudraye may live to be a hundred; but he might die if he left off the flannel swaddling he wears. Run no risks—be prudent. Say nothing—I have read your heart.”

Madame de la Baudraye was defenseless under this serried attack, before a man who spoke at once as doctor, confessor, and friend.

“Indeed!” she said. “Can you suppose any woman would care to compete with a journalist’s mistresses? Monsieur Lousteau strikes me agreeable and witty, but he is blasé, etc., etc.—”

Dinah checked the words by which she tried to disguise her intentions; for Etienne, who seemed studying Cosne, was coming to meet them.

“Believe me,” said Bianchon, “what he wants is to be truly loved; and if he alters his course, it will be to the benefit of his talent.”

The coachman hurried up breathlessly to say the diligence had come in, and they walked quickly, Madame de la Baudraye between the two men.

“Good-bye, my children!” said Bianchon before they entered the town; “you have my blessing!”

He withdrew Madame de la Baudraye’s hand and allowed Lousteau to take it, giving it a tender look as he pressed it to his heart. What a contrast to Dinah! Etienne’s arm thrilled her deeply; Bianchon’s did not. She and the journalist exchanged one of those glowing looks that are more than an avowal.

“Only provincial women wear muslin gowns these days,” Lousteau thought; “this woman, who has chosen me, will make a fuss over her frock! If she had but put on a foulard skirt, I should be happy.—What is the meaning of these difficulties—”

While Lousteau wondered whether Dinah had worn muslin to protect herself, Bianchon, with the coachman’s help, had his luggage piled on the diligence. Finally he came to take leave of Dinah, who was excessively friendly.

“Go home, Madame la Baronne, leave me here—Gatien will be coming,” he whispered. “It is getting late,” he said aloud. “Good-bye!”

“Good-bye—great man!” cried Lousteau, shaking Bianchon’s hand.

When the journalist and Madame de la Baudraye, side by side in the rickety chaise, had recrossed the Loire, they were both unready to speak. In such moments the first words that break the silence are full of meaning.

“Do you know how much I love you?” said the journalist flatly.

Victory might gratify Lousteau, but defeat could not harm him. This indifference was the secret of his audacity. He took Dinah’s hand in both his; she gently released it.

“Yes, I am as good as an actress or a grisette,” she said in a trembling voice though she spoke lightly. “But can you suppose that a woman, who in spite of her absurdities has some intelligence, reserved the best treasures of her heart for a man who will regard her merely as a transient pleasure? I am not surprised to hear from your lips the words so many men have said to me—but—”

The coachman turned.

“Here comes Monsieur Gatien,” he said.

“I love you, I will have you, you shall be mine, for I have never felt for any woman the passion I have for you!” whispered Lousteau.

“In spite of my will, perhaps?” she smiled.

“At least you must seem to have been assaulted to save my honor,” said the Parisian, whose notion of the muslin's fatal immaculateness was ridiculous.

Before Gatien reached the bridge end, the outrageous journalist had crumpled Madame de la Baudraye’s muslin so she was not presentable.

“Oh, monsieur!” she exclaimed in dignified reproof.

“You defied me,” said Lousteau.

Gatien now rode up like a duped lover. To regain some esteem, Lousteau did his best to hide the tumbled dress by leaning out to speak to Gatien from Dinah’s side.

“Go back to our inn,” he said; “there is still time; the diligence does not start for half an hour. The papers are on the table of the room Bianchon used; he wants them for his lecture.”

“Pray go, Gatien,” Dinah said with an imperious glance. The boy obeyed, turning his horse away.

“Go quickly to La Baudraye,” cried Lousteau to the coachman. “Madame is not well—Your mother only will know the secret of my trick,” he added, taking his seat beside Dinah.

“You call such infamous conduct a trick?” cried Madame de la Baudraye, swallowing tears that dried with indignation.

She leaned back, crossed her arms, and stared at the Loire and landscape rather than at Lousteau. The journalist put on his most ingratiating tone and talked till they reached La Baudraye, where Dinah fled indoors, trying not to be seen. In agitation she threw herself on a sofa and burst into tears.

“If I am an object of horror to you, of aversion or scorn, I will go,” said Lousteau, who had followed, and threw himself at her feet.

At that crisis Madame Piedefer entered, asking her daughter:

“What is the matter? What has happened?”

“Give your daughter another dress at once,” said the audacious Parisian into the prim mother’s ear.

Hearing Gatien’s mad gallop, Madame de la Baudraye fled to her bedroom, followed by her mother.

“There are no papers at the inn,” Gatien told Lousteau, who went to meet him.

“And none at the Château d’Anzy either?” replied Lousteau.

“You have been making a fool of me,” said Gatien coldly.

“Quite so,” said Lousteau. “Madame was annoyed by your following her uninvited. To bore a woman is a bad way of courting. Dinah has played you a trick, and you have given her a laugh; it is more than any of you has done in thirteen years past. You owe that success to Bianchon, for your cousin was the author of the Farce of the ‘Manuscript.’—Will the horse get over it?” he laughed, while Gatien hesitated between anger and bewilderment.

At that moment Madame de la Baudraye entered, dressed in a velvet gown, accompanied by her mother, who shot angry looks at Lousteau. It would have been too rash for Dinah to seem cold to Lousteau in Gatien’s presence; Etienne, seizing the moment, offered his arm to the supposed Lucretia; she declined.

“Do you mean to cast off a man who has vowed to live for you?” he said, walking close beside her. “I shall stop at Sancerre and go home tomorrow.”

“Are you coming, mamma?” Dinah asked, avoiding Lousteau’s direct challenge.

Lousteau helped the mother into the chaise, assisted Madame de la Baudraye by taking her arm, and he and Gatien took the front seat, leaving the saddle horse at La Baudraye.

“You have changed your gown,” Gatien blundered.

“Madame la Baronne was chilled by the river air,” Lousteau replied. “Bianchon advised a warm dress.”

Dinah blushed; Madame Piedefer assumed a stern expression.

“Poor Bianchon! he is on the road to Paris. A noble soul!” said Lousteau.

“Oh, yes!” cried Dinah. “He is high-minded, full of delicate feeling—”

“We were in such good spirits when we set out,” Lousteau said; “now you are overdone, and you speak to me so bitterly—why? Are you not used to being told how handsome and clever you are? For my part, I say boldly, before Gatien, I give up Paris; I mean to stay at Sancerre and swell the number of your cavalieri serventi. I feel so young again in my native district; I have forgotten Paris and its wickedness, its bores, and its wearisome pleasures.—Yes, my life seems purified.”

Dinah let Lousteau talk without looking at him; yet at last his rhetoric, meant to feign passion in phrases whose meaning for Gatien was hidden but which struck Dinah’s heart, made her raise her eyes. This look crowned Lousteau’s joy; his wit flowed more freely, and at last he made her laugh. When a woman compromised in pride is made to laugh, she is finally committed.

As they drove into the graveled forecourt, with its central lawn and large vases that set off Anzy’s facade, the journalist said:

“When women love, they forgive everything, even our crimes; when they do not love, they cannot forgive anything—not even our virtues.—Do you forgive me,” he added in her ear, pressing her arm to his heart. Dinah could not help smiling.

All through dinner and the evening Etienne was in the most delightful spirits, inexhaustibly cheerful, though he now and then fell into dreamy abstraction, rapt in his own happiness.

After coffee, Madame de la Baudraye and her mother left the men to wander the gardens. Monsieur Gravier then remarked to Monsieur de Clagny:

“Did you observe that Madame de la Baudraye went out in a muslin gown and came home in a velvet?”

“As she got into the carriage at Cosne, the muslin caught on a brass nail and was torn all the way down,” replied Lousteau.

“Oh!” exclaimed Gatien, stricken to the heart by two such different explanations.

The journalist, understanding, took Gatien’s arm as a hint to be silent. A few minutes later Etienne left Dinah’s three adorers and took possession of little La Baudraye. Gatien was then cross-questioned about the day’s events. Gravier and Clagny were dismayed to learn Lousteau had been alone with Dinah on the return from Cosne, and more so on hearing the two versions of the dress change. The three discomfited gentlemen were awkward for the rest of the evening.

Next day each man had business and left Anzy; Dinah stayed with her mother, Lousteau, and her husband. The annoyance of the three victims caused an organized rebellion in Sancerre. The surrender of the Muse of Le Berry, of the Nivernais, and of Morvan provoked a perfect hue and cry of slander, evil report, and guesses in which the muslin gown story figured largely. No dress Dinah had worn had been so much commented on; the girls, unable to conceive the connection between love and muslin, were fascinated; the married women laughed.

The Presidente Boirouge, furious at her son’s discomfiture, forgot her praise of the poem of Paquita and fulminated terrible condemnation on the woman who could produce such a disgraceful work.

“The wretched woman commits every crime she writes about,” said she. “Perhaps she will come to the same end as her heroine!”

Dinah’s fate among Sancerre’s good folks resembled Marshal Soult’s in opposition papers: while minister he lost the battle of Toulouse; out of office he won it! While virtuous, Dinah matched Camille de Maupin; as soon as happy, she was an unhappy creature.

Monsieur de Clagny became her valiant champion; he went often to Anzy to rebut the rumors and maintained she and Lousteau were engaged in some great work. But the lawyer was laughed at.

October was lovely; autumn in the Loire valley was unusually glorious in 1836. Nature seemed to aid Dinah, who as Bianchon predicted, gradually developed genuine passion. In a month she was altered. She discovered many dormant qualities. To her, Lousteau seemed an angel; heart-love, the crowning need of a great nature, had made a new woman of her. Dinah was alive: she had found an outlet for her powers, saw undreamed vistas in the future—in short, she was happy, without alarms or hindrances. The vast castle, gardens, park, and forest favored love.

Lousteau found in Madame de la Baudraye an artlessness and originality; there was more unexpected and winning about her than a girl. He appreciated a form of flattery genuine in Dinah: she really learned from him the ways of love; he truly was the first to reign in her heart. He took care to be exceedingly amiable.

Men like women possess a repertoire of recitatives, cantabile, nocturnes, airs and refrains—recipes of love each believes exclusively his own. Men of Lousteau’s age try to distribute the “movements” through the whole opera of a passion. Lousteau, seeing this adventure as temporary, was eager to stamp himself on her memory in indelible lines; during that October he lavished his most entrancing melodies and elaborate barcarolles. He exhausted every resource of the stage-management of love.

“If that woman ever forgets me!” he sometimes said as they returned from long walks, “I will owe her no grudge—”

When two have sung all the duets of that score and still love each other, they truly love.

Lousteau had little time; he was to leave Anzy in early November—his paper needed him in Paris. Before breakfast, the day before he left, the journalist and Dinah saw the master of the house arrive with a Nevers artist who restored carvings.

“What are you going to do?” Lousteau asked. “What is to be done to the château?”

“This,” said the little man, leading Lousteau, the local artist, and Dinah onto the terrace.

He pointed to a shield on the front, supported by two sirens, like one on the arcade once leading from the Quai des Tuileries to the old Louvre’s courtyard. It bore the arms of Uxelles: gold and red party per fess, with two lions as supporters; above, a knight’s helm mantled to the shield's tincture, surmounted by a ducal coronet. Motto, Cy paroist! A proud device.

“I want to put my own coat of arms where that of the Uxelles is; they are repeated six times on the two fronts and wings, so it is no trifle.”

“Your arms, so new, and since 1830!” exclaimed Dinah.

“Have I not created an entail?”

“I could understand it if you had children,” said Lousteau.

“Oh!” said the old man, “Madame de la Baudraye is still young; there is time.”

This remark made Lousteau smile; he did not understand Monsieur de la Baudraye.

“There, Didine!” he said in Dinah’s ear, “what a waste of remorse!”

Dinah begged him to give one more day, and the lovers parted as certain theatres part—giving ten last performances of a paying piece. How many promises they made! How many solemn pledges did Dinah exact and the unblushing journalist give!

Dinah, with the superiority of the Superior Woman, accompanied Lousteau publicly as far as Cosne, with her mother and little La Baudraye. Ten days later, in her drawing-room at La Baudraye, she received Monsieur de Clagny, Gatien, and Gravier, and addressed each in turn:

“I owe it to Monsieur Lousteau that I discovered I had not been loved for my own sake.”

What noble speeches she made on man, on the nature of his feelings, on the end of base passions. Of Dinah’s three worshipers, Monsieur de Clagny alone said to her: “I love you, come what may”—and Dinah accepted him as confidant, lavishing on him all the marks of friendship women can devise for the Gurths who are ready to wear the collar of gilded slavery.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In Paris again, Lousteau soon lost the memory of his happy weeks at the Chateau d’Anzy. The reason was simple: he lived by his pen.

In this century, particularly since the rise of the bourgeois—thrifty, non-imitative citizens—to live by the pen is almost a form of penal servitude. To live by the pen means to produce—or to seem to produce—constantly; imitation costs as dearly as genuine invention. Besides his weekly newspaper piece, which arrived like Sisyphus’s stone every Monday, Etienne wrote for three or four literary magazines. He put no high artistic conscience into this work. A man from Sancerre by temperament and gift, he had a facility—carelessness, perhaps—that made him a mere scrivener, a literary hack. In Paris, hack-work cuts a man off from pretensions to literature; when a man can do no more or no longer cares to advance, he becomes a journalist and a hack.

The life he led was not unpleasant. Blue-stockings, novices in every sphere, actresses, publishers and authors all flattered his ready pen. Lousteau, a thorough man about town, lived cheaply aside from rent. He had theatre boxes; the sale of reviewed or unreviewed books paid for his gloves; he accepted vanity commissions for drawings and pictures. Every day had dinners, every night theatre, every morning callers and visits. His newspaper serial, two novels a year for weeklies, and miscellaneous articles were the tax for this easy life. Yet to reach it he had struggled for ten years.

Known in the literary world and liked for his facile good humor, he floated with the current, careless about the future. He presided over a small circle of newcomers and kept habits of fifteen years’ standing with men he supped and dined with, indulging his wit. He earned seven to eight hundred francs a month, a sum inadequate for the prodigality typical of the impecunious. At his first arrival in Paris he had said, “If I had but five hundred francs a month, I should be rich!” He was as hard up now as then.

The reason: Lousteau lived in ground-floor rooms with a garden in the Rue des Martyrs, splendidly furnished. When he settled there in 1833 he struck a deal with an upholsterer that kept his pocket money low for a while, but these rooms were let for twelve hundred francs. January, April, July, and October were, as he put it, his indigent months; rent and the porter’s account cleaned him out. He took many hackney cabs, spent a hundred francs on breakfasts, smoked thirty francs’ worth of cigars, and could never refuse the mistress of a day a dinner or a new dress. He drew so deeply on his fluctuating future earnings that he could no more find a hundred francs on his chimney-piece now, making seven or eight hundred francs a month, than he could in 1822, when he hardly made two hundred.

Sometimes weary of the ceaseless vicissitudes of literary life and as jaded by amusement as a courtesan, Lousteau would step aside from the tide, sit on the bank of his little garden and say to intimates—Nathan or Bixiou—who smoked there with him, looking at an evergreen lawn as big as a dinner-table: “What will be the end of us? White hairs are giving us respectful hints!”

“Lord! we shall marry when we give as much thought to the matter as we give to a drama or a novel,” said Nathan.

“And Florine?” retorted Bixiou.

“Oh, we all have a Florine,” Etienne answered, flinging away his cigar and thinking of Madame Schontz.

Madame Schontz was pretty enough to charge high prices for the interest on her beauty while reserving Lousteau’s devotion as absolute. Like other Lorettes—women clustered around Notre Dame de Lorette—she lived nearby in the Rue Fléchier and took pride in teasing friends by boasting of having a Wit for her lover.

These details of Lousteau’s life and fortune are essential: his penury and bohemian existence, married to a taste for Parisian luxury, were destined to cruelly affect Dinah’s life. Those who know Parisian bohemia now understand how, after a fortnight in the literary whirl, the journalist could laugh at his Baroness with friends and even with Madame Schontz. For those who find such things mean, excuses are futile.

“What did you do at Sancerre?” Bixiou asked on first meeting Lousteau.

“I did good service to three worthy provincials—a Receiver-General, a little cousin, and a Public Prosecutor, who for ten years had been dancing round one of the hundred ‘Tenth Muses’ who adorn the Departments,” said he. “But they had dared not touch her.”

“Poor boy!” said Bixiou. “I said you had gone to Sancerre to turn Pegasus out to grass.”

“Your joke is as stupid as my Muse is handsome,” retorted Lousteau. “Ask Bianchon, my dear fellow.”

On the tenth day Lousteau received a letter postmarked Sancerre.

“Good! very good!” he said, and read: “‘Beloved friend, idol of my heart and soul----’ twenty pages, all at once, dated midnight! She writes when alone. Poor woman!—and a postscript: ‘I dare not ask you to write to me as I write; still, I hope for a line or two every week, to relieve my mind.’ What a pity to burn it; it is really well written,” he told himself, tossing the ten sheets into the fire. “That woman was born to reel off copy!”

Lousteau feared neither Madame Schontz—who truly loved him—nor the Marquise whose friend he had supplanted in her heart. This Marquise sometimes dropped in unexpectedly, veiled in a coach, and allowed herself, as a literary woman, to hunt through all his drawers.

A week later, more letters from Sancerre arrived—eight leaves, sixteen pages. He heard a woman’s step, thought the Marquise had come, and threw these rapturous proofs into the fire—unread!

“A woman’s letter!” exclaimed Madame Schontz when she came in. “The paper, the wax, are scented—”

Then the coach-office porter placed two huge hampers in the ante-room: “Carriage paid,” he announced. “It must have come from Sancerre,” said Madame Schontz, and opened them. “Your Tenth Muse is an intelligent woman,” she said, delighted by meats and flowers; “I like a Muse who understands housekeeping and can make game pies.”

Lousteau wrote to Dinah, but his letter was clever, insidious—like one of Mirabeau’s to Sophie—rather than of the heart. A true love letter is transparent; it pleases only the beloved. As soon as a love letter charms a third reader, it’s clearly the invention of the head. Still, women are beguiled and believe themselves the cause of a flow of wit.

By December’s end Lousteau had ceased to read Dinah’s letters; they lay in a heap in a drawer never locked, under his shirts, which they scented.

Then chance came, the kind bohemians ought to clutch. In mid-December Madame Schontz, interested in Etienne’s future, begged him to call one morning on business.

“My dear fellow, you have a chance of marrying,” she said.

“I can marry very often, happily, my dear.”

“When I say marrying, I mean marrying well. A young lady has got into trouble; her mother knows nothing of a kiss. Her father is an honest notary, a man of honor; he has kept it dark. He wants to get his daughter married within a fortnight, and will give her a fortune of a hundred and fifty thousand francs—for he has three other children—but he will add, under the rose, a hundred thousand francs hand to hand, to cover the damages. They are an old family of Paris citizens, Rue des Lombards—”

“Well, then, why does the lover not marry her?”

“Dead.”

“What a romance! Such things are only in the Rue des Lombards.”

“But do not think a jealous brother murdered him. The young man died of pleurisy caught as he came out of the theatre. A head-clerk and penniless, he had entrapped the daughter to marry into business—a judgment from heaven, I call it!”

“Where did you hear the story?”

“From Malaga; the notary is her milord.”

“Cardot? the son of that little old man in hair-powder, Florentine’s first friend?”

“Just so. Malaga cannot in conscience marry her off to a tomtit fiddler of eighteen; besides Monsieur Cardot wants a man of thirty at least. Our notary will be proud to have a famous man for his son-in-law. You will pay your debts, get twelve thousand francs a year, and be a father without trouble; what do you say? You marry a very consolable widow. There is an income of fifty thousand francs and the value of the connection; later you may expect fifteen thousand more from your share, and enter a family with political position; Cardot is brother-in-law to old Camusot, the depute who lived so long with Fanny Beaupré.”

“Yes,” said Lousteau, “old Camusot married Daddy Cardot’s eldest daughter!”

“Well! and Madame Cardot was a Chiffreville—manufacturers of chemicals, the aristocracy of these days! Potash, I tell you! But this is the unpleasant side. You will have a terrible mother-in-law, a woman capable of killing her daughter if she knew! This Cardot woman is a bigot; she has lips like two faded narrow pink ribbons.

“A man of the town like you would never pass muster with her, who will spy out your bachelor life. Cardot says he will exert paternal authority. The poor man will be obliged to do the civil to his wife for some days; a woman made of wood, my dear; Malaga calls her a penitential scrubber. Cardot is forty; he will be mayor and perhaps deputy. He proposes, instead of the hundred thousand francs, a little house in the Rue Saint-Lazare with a forecourt and garden—sixty thousand at the July overthrow; he would sell it: you would have a home and a look of property in Madame Cardot’s eyes. You would be housed like a prince in that little mansion. Then, by Camusot’s interest, you may get an appointment as librarian of some public office where there is no library.—If you invest in backing a newspaper, you will get ten thousand a year on it, you can earn six; your librarianship will bring in four.—Can you do better?”

“If you marry a lamb without spot, she might be a light woman in two years. What is the damage? an anticipated dividend! It is quite the fashion.

“Take my word, dine with Malaga tomorrow. You will meet your father-in-law; he will know the secret has been let out—by Malaga—and then you are master of the situation. As to your wife!—her misconduct leaves you as free as a bachelor—”

“Your language is as blunt as a cannon ball.”

“I love you for your own sake, that is all—and I can reason. Well! why stand there like a wax image? There is nothing to meditate over. Marriage is heads or tails—well, you have tossed heads up.”

“You shall have my reply tomorrow,” said Lousteau.

“I would sooner have it at once; Malaga will write you up tonight.”

“Well, then, yes.”

Lousteau spent the evening writing a long letter to the Marquise, giving reasons for marrying: constant poverty, the torpor of his imagination, white hairs, moral and physical exhaustion—four pages of argument. “As to Dinah, I will send her a circular announcing the marriage,” he told himself. “As Bixiou says, I have not my match for knowing how to dock the tail of a passion.”

At first on ceremony with himself, next day Lousteau dreaded lest the marriage fail. He was assiduously civil to the notary.

“I knew your father at Florentine’s, so I may well know you at Mademoiselle Turquet’s. Like father, like son. A very good fellow and a philosopher was little Daddy Cardot—excuse me, we always called him so. In our day, pleasure ran away with me; now I am ambitious; to get on one must be free from debt, have a good income, a wife, and a family. If I pay enough taxes to qualify, I may be a deputy yet.”

Maître Cardot appreciated this profession of faith. Lousteau had laid himself out to please, and the notary felt more at ease with a man who had known his father’s secrets than he would with another. The next day Lousteau was introduced to the Cardots as purchaser of the Rue Saint-Lazare house, and three days later he dined there.

Cardot lived in an old house near the Place du Châtelet. Everything was “good”; economy covered gilding with green gauze; furniture wore holland covers. Though it was impossible to doubt the family’s wealth, boredom clung to every corner; curtains hung dolefully; the dining-room resembled Harpagon’s. Even had Lousteau not known Malaga, he could have guessed the notary’s real life was spent elsewhere.

The journalist saw a tall, fair girl with blue eyes, shy and languishing. The elder brother liked him; he was the fourth clerk in the office but attracted by literary snares, destined to succeed his father. The younger sister was twelve. Lousteau, adopting a Jesuitical air, played Monarchist and Churchman for the mother’s benefit: smooth, deliberate, and complimentary.

Within three weeks, at his fourth dinner, Félicie Cardot—who had watched him—carried a cup of coffee to him in the window recess and whispered through tears: “I will devote my whole life, monsieur, to thanking you for your sacrifice in favor of a poor girl—”

Lousteau was touched; there was expression in her look and tone. “She would make a good man happy,” he thought, pressing her hand.

Madame Cardot saw her future son-in-law as a man with prospects; she was delighted by his moral tone. Etienne, prompted by the wily notary, had pledged he had no natural children, no tie that could endanger Félicie’s happiness.

“You may think I go too far,” the mother said to the journalist. “But in giving such a jewel as my Félicie to any man, one must think of the future. I am not one of those mothers who want to be rid of their daughters. Monsieur Cardot hurries matters; this is the only point on which we differ.—Though with a man like you, a literary man whose youth has been preserved by hard work from the moral shipwreck so prevalent, we may feel safe; still, you would be the first to laugh at me if I looked for a husband for my daughter with my eyes shut. I know you are not an innocent, and I should be sorry for my Félicie if you were” (this in a whisper); “but if you had any liaison—For instance, monsieur, you have heard of Madame Roguin, the wife of a notary who was sadly notorious. Madame Roguin has, since 1820, been kept by a banker—”

“Yes, du Tillet,” Etienne began, and bit his tongue, recollecting how rash it would be to confess acquaintance with du Tillet.

“Yes.—Well, monsieur, if you were a mother, would you not quake at the thought that Madame du Tillet’s fate might be your child’s? At her age, and née de Granville! To have a rival of fifty and more. Sooner would I see my daughter dead than give her to a man who had such a connection with a married woman. A grisette or an actress you take and leave—there is no danger, in my view, from women of that stamp; love is their trade. But a respectable woman’s fall is terrible—her only excuse might be constancy.”

Instead of pondering these speeches, Etienne jested about them at Malaga’s, where he went with his notary father-in-law elect; for Cardot and the journalist were the best of friends.

Lousteau already adopted airs of importance; his life at last seemed purposeful. In a few days he expected to own a delightful little house in the Rue Saint-Lazare; marry a charming woman; have about twenty thousand francs a year; and give reins to ambition. The young lady loved him, he would be connected with respectable families, and in short he sailed full on the blue waters of hope.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Madame Cardot had asked to see the prints for Gil Blas, an illustrated volume the publishers were issuing, and Lousteau had taken the first numbers for her inspection. But she had an intention: she borrowed the book as an excuse to call unexpectedly on her future son-in-law. The sight of those bachelor rooms, which her husband had described as charming, would tell her more about Lousteau’s habits than any report. Madame Camusot, ignorant of the secret, was terrified at such a marriage for her niece. Monsieur Camusot, a Councillor of the Supreme Court and old Camusot’s son by his first marriage, had given his stepmother, Cardot’s sister, an unflattering account of the journalist.

Lousteau, clever, did not find it strange that the wife of a rich notary should wish to examine a volume costing fifteen francs before buying. Such clever men seldom study the middle class; while he made game of them, they were ready to throttle him.

One day in January 1837 Madame Cardot and her daughter took a hackney coach to the Rue des Martyrs to return parts of Gil Blas to Félicie’s betrothed, eager to see Lousteau’s rooms. Such domiciliary visits are common among the old bourgeoisie. The porter was out, but his daughter, on being told they had come, handed over the key—especially after Madame Cardot slipped her a gold piece.

It was about noon, the hour the journalist would return from breakfast at the Café Anglais. Crossing the open space between Notre-Dame de Lorette and the Rue des Martyrs, Lousteau fancied he saw Dinah in a hired coach going up the Rue du Faubourg-Montmartre. He stood rooted; at his door he saw her at the coach.

“What has brought you here?” he asked in the familiar tu. Formal vous was out of the question to a woman he meant to get rid of.

“Why, my love,” she cried, “have you not read my letters?”

“Certainly I have,” he said.

“Well, then?”

“You are a father,” said the country lady.

“Faugh!” he ejaculated. He thought she must be prepared for the blow.

He told the coachman to wait, took Madame de la Baudraye’s hand, and, leaving the chaise full of trunks, vowed he would send her and her luggage back where she had come from.

“Monsieur, monsieur,” called little Pamela. The child had sense; she thought three women must not be allowed to meet in a bachelor’s rooms.

“Well, well!” said Lousteau, dragging Dinah along. Pamela, thinking the lady some relation, added, “The key is in the door; your mother-in-law is there.”

In his agitation Lousteau heard only “mother is there” and went in.

Félicie and her mother, already in the bedroom, crept into a corner on seeing Etienne enter with a woman.

“At last, Etienne, my dearest, I am yours for life!” cried Dinah, flinging her arms around him while he turned the key. “Life is perpetual anguish to me in that house at Anzy. I could not bear it longer; and when it came for me to proclaim my happiness—well, I had not courage. Here I am, your wife with your child! And you have not written to me; you have left me two months without a line.”

“But, Dinah, you put me in the greatest difficulty—”

“Do you love me?”

“How can I do otherwise than love you? But would you not have been wiser to remain at Sancerre? I am in the most abject poverty, and I fear to drag you into it—”

“Your misery will be paradise to me. I only ask to live here, never to go out—”

“Good God! that is all very fine in words, but—” Dinah burst into tears at his roughness.

Lousteau could not resist her distress. He clasped the Baroness and kissed her.

“Do not cry, Didine!” he said; and seeing Madame Cardot’s figure in the mirror, he murmured in her ear, “Come, Didine, go with Pamela and get your trunks unloaded. Go; do not cry; we will be happy!” He led her to the door and returned to face the storm.

“Monsieur,” said Madame Cardot, “I congratulate myself on having resolved to see for myself the home of the man who was to be my son-in-law. If my daughter were to die of it, she should never be the wife of such a man as you. You must devote yourself to making your Didine happy, monsieur.” The virtuous lady then left, followed by Félicie, who wept for the loss of Etienne. Madame Cardot stared insolently at Dinah as she drove off; Dinah’s heart smarted at “that is all very fine in words,” yet, like every woman in love, she believed the murmured “Do not cry, Didine!”

Lousteau, practical from a stormy life, reflected: “Didine is high-minded; when she knows of my proposed marriage, she will sacrifice herself for my prospects, and I know how to let her know.” Delighted at this trick’s apparent certainty, he began humming: “Larifla, fla, fla!... And Didine once out of the way,” he soliloquized, “I will treat Maman Cardot to a call: I have seduced her Félicie—Félicie bears in her bosom the pledge of our affection—and larifla, fla, fla! the father Ergo, the notary, his wife and daughter are caught—” and, to Dinah’s surprise, she found him dancing.

“Your arrival and our happiness have turned my head with joy,” he said, to explain his mood.

“And I fancied you had ceased to love me!” she cried, dropping her handbag and weeping with joy.

“Make yourself at home, my darling,” said Etienne laughing inwardly; “I must write two lines to excuse myself from a bachelor party, for I mean to devote myself to you. Give your orders; you are at home.”

He wrote to Bixiou:

MY DEAR BOY,—My Baroness has dropped into my arms, and will be fatal to my marriage unless we perform one of the most familiar stratagems of the thousand and one comedies. I rely on you to come here, like one of Moliere’s old men, to scold your nephew Leandre for his folly, while the Tenth Muse lies hidden in my bedroom; you must work on her feelings; strike hard, be brutal, offensive. I, you understand, shall express my blind devotion, and be deaf, so you may have to shout at me.

Come if you can at seven o’clock.

Yours,

E. LOUSTEAU.

Having sent this letter by a commissionaire to the man who delighted in such practical jokes—the artist’s “charge”—Lousteau made a show of settling the Muse of Sancerre in his rooms. He busied himself arranging her luggage and told her about the house with overflowing glee, kind words and caresses, so Dinah believed herself supremely beloved. The fashionable rooms pleased her far more than her old château.

Pamela Migeon, the intelligent fourteen-year-old, was asked if she would like to be waiting-maid to the Baroness. Pamela, delighted, ordered dinner from a boulevard restaurant. Dinah, taking possession of closets, found none of the household necessities—extreme poverty lurked under the superficial elegance of the bachelor home. She indulged in tender dreams: she would alter Étienne’s habits, make him domestic, and fill his cup of happiness.

The novelty hid the disastrous side; Dinah saw reciprocated love as absolution of her sin and did not yet look beyond the walls. Pamela, whose wits matched a lorette’s, went to Madame Schontz to borrow plate, telling her what had happened. Madame Schontz welcomed her and hurried to Malaga to warn Cardot of the catastrophe.

The journalist, not uneasy in respect to his marriage prospects, grew ever more charming to the provincial lady. Dinner was the child’s play of lovers set free. After coffee, Lousteau sat by the fire, Dinah on his knee, when Pamela ran in, scared.

“Here is Monsieur Bixiou!” she cried.

“Go into the bedroom,” said Lousteau to his mistress; “I’ll soon get rid of him. He’s one of my most intimate friends, and I must explain my new start in life.”

“Oh, ho! dinner for two, and a blue velvet bonnet!” cried Bixiou. “I am off.—Ah! that is what comes of marrying—one must go through some partings. How rich one feels when one begins to move one’s sticks, heh?”

“Who talks of marrying?” said Lousteau.

“What! are you not going to be married, then?” cried Bixiou.

“No!”

“No? My word, what next? You, who have come across twenty thousand francs a year, and a house, and a wife connected with all the first families—a wife, in short, out of the Rue des Lombards—”

“That will do, Bixiou, enough. It is at an end. Be off!”

“Be off? I have a friend’s privileges, and I shall take every advantage.—What has come over you?”

“What has come over me is my lady from Sancerre. She is a mother, and we are going to live together happily to the end of our days.—You would have heard it tomorrow, so you may as well be told it now.”

“Many chimney-pots are falling on my head, as Arnal says. But if this woman really loves you, she will go back to the place she came from. Did any provincial woman find her sea-legs in Paris? She will wound all your vanities. Have you forgotten what a provincial is? She will bore you as much when she is happy as when she is sad; she will have a talent for escaping grace as a Parisian has in inventing it.

“Lousteau, listen. A passion may make you forget the times, but I have no mythological bandage over my eyes. For fifteen years you have been tossing in the literary world; you are no longer young, you have padded the hoof till your soles are worn through! You hide your rags like a street urchin with turned socks. In short, the joke is stale. Your excuses are more familiar than a patent medicine—”

“I may say to you, like the Regent to Cardinal Dubois, ‘That is kicking enough!’” said Lousteau.

“Oh, venerable young man,” said Bixiou, “the iron has touched the sore. You are worn out, aren’t you? Well, then; in the heyday of youth, under penury, what have you done? You are not in the front rank, and you have not a thousand francs of your own. Can you, in the decline of your powers, support a family by your pen, when your wife, if honest, won’t have the resources of a woman of the streets, who can extract her thousand-franc note from the depths where milord keeps it safe? You are rushing into the lowest depths of the social theatre.

“And this is only the financial side. Consider the political position. We live in a bourgeois age, where honor and genius are summed up in paying your way, owing nobody, and conducting your affairs with judgment. Be steady, respectable, have a wife and children, pay rent and taxes, serve in the National Guard, and you may aspire to the Ministry! You were preparing to fulfill the conditions for a political personage; you are capable of the mean tricks necessary in office. And just for a woman, who will abandon you—the end of every passion—you throw away the sacred Hearth, the Rue des Lombards, a political career, thirty thousand francs per annum, respectability and respect! Ought that be the end of a man who has done with illusions?

“If you had kept a pot boiling for some actress who gave you fun for it—well; that is a cabinet matter. But to live with another man’s wife is a draft at sight on disaster; it is bolting the bitter pills of vice with none of the gilding.”

“That will do. One word answers it all; I love Madame de la Baudraye, and prefer her to every fortune and every position the world can offer. I may have been carried away by ambition, but everything must give way to the joy of being a father.”

“Ah, ha! you have a fancy for paternity? But, wretched man, we are fathers only of our legitimate children. What is a brat that does not bear your name? The last chapter of the romance.—Your child will be taken from you! We have seen that story in twenty plays these ten years past.

“Society, my dear boy, will drop upon you sooner or later. Read Adolphe.—I fancy I can see you, after you and she are used to each other: dejected, hang-dog, bereft of position and fortune, fighting like shareholders tricked by a director. Your director is happiness.”

“Say no more, Bixiou.”

“But I have only just begun,” continued Bixiou. “Listen. Marriage has been out of favor; but apart from certifying heredity and offering a chance for a penniless young man to make his fortune, it survives. There is not the man living who would not repent, sooner or later, of having lost the chance of marrying thirty thousand francs a year.”

“You won’t understand me,” cried Lousteau, exasperated. “Go away—she is there—”

“I beg your pardon; why did you not tell me sooner?—You are of age, and so is she,” he added, lowering his voice but loud enough for Dinah to hear. “She will make you repent bitterly of your happiness!—”

“If it is a folly, I intend to commit it.—Good-bye.”

“A man gone overboard!” cried Bixiou.

“Devil take those friends who think they have a right to preach,” said Lousteau, opening the bedroom door. He found Madame de la Baudraye sunk in an armchair, dabbing her eyes with an embroidered handkerchief.

“Oh, why did I come here?” she sobbed. “Good Heavens, why indeed? Etienne, I am not so provincial as you think me. You are making a fool of me.”

“Darling angel,” answered Lousteau, taking her in his arms and lifting her half-lifeless into the drawing-room, “we have both pledged our future; it is sacrifice for sacrifice. While loving you at Sancerre, they were engaging me to be married here, but I refused.—Oh! I was extremely distressed—”

“I am going,” cried Dinah, starting up.

“You will stay, my Didine. All is at an end. Is this fortune so lightly earned? Must I marry a gawky, tow-haired creature, with a red nose, the daughter of a notary, and saddle myself with a stepmother who could give Madame de Piedefer points for bigotry—”

Pamela flew in and whispered, “Madame Schontz!”

Lousteau rose, leaving Dinah on the sofa, and went out.

“It is all over with you, my dear,” Madame Schontz said. “Cardot does not mean to quarrel with his wife for the sake of a son-in-law. The lady made a scene—something like a scene! So, to conclude, the head-clerk, who had been the late head-clerk’s deputy for two years, agrees to take the girl with the business.”

“Mean wretch!” exclaimed Lousteau. “What! in two hours he has decided?”

“Bless me, that is simple enough. The rascal, knowing all the dead man’s little secrets, guessed your master’s fix from overhearing a few words of the squabble with Madame Cardot. The notary relies on your honor and good feeling, for the affair is settled. The clerk, whose conduct has been admirable, even went so far as to attend mass! A finished hypocrite—just suits the mamma. You and Cardot will still be friends. He is to be a director in an immense financial concern, and he may be useful to you.—So you have been waked from a sweet dream.”

“I have lost a fortune, a wife, and—”

“And a mistress,” said Madame Schontz, smiling. “Here you are, more than married; you will be insufferable, always wanting to get home, nothing loose about you, neither your clothes nor your habits. And, after all, my Arthur does things in style. I will be faithful to him and cut Malaga’s acquaintance.

“Let me peep at her through the door—your Sancerre Muse,” she went on. “Is there no finer bird than that to be found in the desert? You are cheated! She is dignified, lean, lachrymose; she only needs Lady Dudley’s turban!”

What is it now?” asked Madame de la Baudraye, hearing the rustle of silk and a woman’s murmur.

“It is, my darling, that we are now indissolubly united.—I have had an answer to the letter you saw me write, which was to break off my marriage—”

“So that was the party you gave up?”

“Yes.”

“Oh, I will be more than your wife—I am your slave, I give you my life,” said the deluded creature. “I did not believe I could love you more than I did! Now I shall not be a mere incident, but your whole life.”

“Yes, my beautiful, my generous Didine.”

“Swear to me,” she begged, “that only death shall divide us.”

Lousteau was ready with the most fascinating vows. Between the apartment door where he had taken the lorette’s farewell kiss and the drawing-room where the Muse lay bewildered by shocks, he had remembered little De la Baudraye’s precarious health, his fine fortune, and Bianchon’s remark about Dinah—“She will be a rich widow!”—and he said to himself, “I would a hundred times rather have Madame de la Baudraye for a wife than Félicie!”

His plan was quickly fixed: he determined to play the farce of passion again, and to perfection. His mean self-interest and false vehemence had disastrous effects. Madame de la Baudraye had intended, on coming to Paris, to live in rooms of her own near Lousteau; but the proofs of his devotion—his giving up brilliant prospects—and the perfection of the first days of their illicit union kept her from suggesting a parting. The second day was to be, and was, a high festival; such a proposal then would have been a discord.

Lousteau, anxious to make Dinah feel dependent, kept her in a state of intoxication with incessant amusement. These circumstances prevented two clever people from avoiding the slough they fell into—that of a life in common, a folly common in Parisian literary circles.

Thus the whole programme of a provincial amour, satirically described by Lousteau to Madame de la Baudraye, was enacted—a fact neither he nor she now remembered. Passion is born deaf and mute.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
This winter in Paris to Madame de la Baudraye was what October had been at Sancerre. Etienne, to initiate “his wife” into Paris life, varied their honeymoon with evenings at the theatre, where Dinah would only occupy the stage box. At first she preserved traces of provincial modesty; afraid of being seen, she hid her happiness and would timidly say, “Monsieur de Clagny or Monsieur Gravier may have followed me to Paris.” Even in the capital she felt fearful, as if still in Sancerre.

Lousteau, excessively vain, educated Dinah, took her to the best dressmakers, and pointed out fashionable models. Her provincial look soon vanished. Lousteau’s friends congratulated him on his conquest.

During that season Etienne wrote little and ran deeply into debt, though Dinah, proud, bought her clothes from her own savings, fancying she had cost him nothing. After three months she was acclimatized to Paris: she revelled in the Italian opera, knew the plays, actors, and jests of the day, and was inured to the constant excitement and forgetfulness of city life. She no longer stood astonished at every novelty; she breathed that quick, witty atmosphere in which clever people thrive.

One morning, reading the papers Lousteau collected, two lines carried her back to Sancerre:

“Monsieur le Baron de Clagny, Public Prosecutor to the Criminal Court at Sancerre, has been appointed Deputy Public Prosecutor to the Supreme Court in Paris.”

“How well that worthy lawyer loves you!” the journalist sneered.

“Poor man!” she answered. “What did I tell you? He is following me.”

Etienne and Dinah were then in that dazzling stage of passion when each is comfortably accustomed to the other yet freshness remains. They knew each other but had not explored every secret; enchantment reigned, with no collisions. Dinah used love’s hyperbole:

“When you cease to love me, kill me.—If you should cease to love me, I believe I could kill you first and myself after.”

Lousteau joked of testing constancy and proposed they each write letters to justify suicide in case of infidelity, but neither ever did. He promised himself he would deceive her when tired of her, while feeling the yoke. Dinah’s consent to their union was a generous act of self-respect. In intimacy she never surrendered her modesty, rectitude, or the ambition that formed her character. Lousteau involuntarily held her in high esteem. As a Parisian she could be superior to the most fascinating courtesan by virtue of information, habits of mind, and reading; her wit generalized while courtesans dazzled a narrow circle.

“There is in Dinah,” Etienne told Bixiou, “the stuff to make both a Ninon and a De Staël.”
“A woman who combines an encyclopaedia and a seraglio is very dangerous,” Bixiou mocked.

When the expected infant became visible, Madame de la Baudraye prepared to shut herself away; but before doing so she insisted on attending the first performance of a new play by Nathan—a literary ceremonial that occupied the two thousand people who constituted “all Paris.” Dinah, never before at a first night, was curious. By then she gloried in her misconduct and wanted to brave society.

She dressed perfectly, matching her delicate features and pallid complexion; but misfortune awaited. The box given to Lousteau was next to that of Anna Grossetete; the two friends did not even bow. At the end of the first act Lousteau left his seat, abandoning Dinah to the scrutiny of opera-glasses, while Anna’s companions received the most distinguished men.

Dinah’s solitude distressed her; she could not put on a studied air, lapsed into provincial awkwardness, and between the second and third acts a man entered her box—Monsieur de Clagny.

“I am happy to see you, to tell you how pleased I am by your promotion,” she said.

“Oh! Madame, for whom should I come to Paris----?”

“What! have I anything to do with your appointment?”

“Everything. Since you left Sancerre it had been intolerable to me; I was dying—”

“Your friendship does me good,” she answered, holding out her hand. “I value my true friends now. I feared I had lost your esteem, but your visit touches me more deeply than ten years’ attachment.”

The lawyer, moved, confessed his unease about Dinah’s position.

“You are an object of curiosity to the whole house,” he said. “Could you not be happy and remain honored? I hear you are Monsieur Etienne Lousteau’s mistress, living as man and wife. You have broken forever with society; even if you should marry your lover later, you will feel the want of respectability you now despise. Ought you not live at home with your mother, who would protect you?”

“I am in the wrong to have come here, that is all.—I have bid farewell to the advantages society confers. My abnegation is so complete I wish I could clear a vast space to make a desert of my love, full of God, of him, and of myself. We have sacrificed much; we are united, indissolubly if you will, by disgrace. I am happy; I can love freely, and confide in you.”

Moved, the magistrate replied in heartrending tones:

“I wanted to see that you were loved: I shall be at ease. But will your lover appreciate your sacrifice? Is there gratitude in his affection?”

“Come to the Rue des Martyrs and you will see!”

“Yes, I will call,” he answered. “There are glorious exceptions in the literary world, but these men of letters drag terrible evils; publicity is one of the greatest. A woman may commit herself with—”

“With a Public Prosecutor?” the Baronne quipped.

“Well! with a more or less famous man the public is informed. Look: you are sitting back-to-back with the Comtesse Marie Vandenesse, who nearly committed folly for a more celebrated man than Lousteau—Nathan—and now they do not even know each other. She was saved; she kept her house; but she was the talk of the town a whole winter. Yet her husband’s fortune and name and his admirable conduct preserved her.”

“And how was Sancerre when you came away?” Dinah asked to change the subject.

“Monsieur de la Baudraye said your confinement made it necessary you should be in Paris, and insisted on the first physicians. So, in spite of the commotion, you still have your legal status.”

“Why!” she exclaimed, “can Monsieur de la Baudraye still hope----”

“Your husband did what he always does—made a little calculation.”

The lawyer left the box when Lousteau returned, bowing with dignity.

“You are a greater hit than the piece,” Etienne said to Dinah.

That brief triumph brought Dinah more happiness than any provincial success, yet she was grave as they left. Three days later she noticed a cloud on Lousteau’s brow and learned their household was penniless, two quarters’ rent owing and on the eve of execution. The reality pierced her; she repented tempting Etienne into extravagance. It is hard to pass from pleasure to work; happiness had wrecked more poems than sorrow ever inspired.

Dinah, used to never owing a sou, was informed through Migeon (Pamela’s father) that she could pawn jewels; her “uncle” advanced nine hundred francs. She kept three hundred for baby clothes and expenses and joyfully gave Lousteau the rest as he wrestled through a novel for a periodical.

“Finish without sacrificing to necessity,” she urged. “Polish the style; work up the subject. I will be housewife and attend to business.”

For months Etienne had taken Dinah every day to the Café Riche; a corner was always kept for them. She was dismayed to find five hundred francs owing for the last fortnight.

“What! have we been drinking wine at six francs a bottle? A sole Normande costs five francs!” she cried.

“Well, it makes little difference whether we are robbed at a restaurant or by a cook,” Lousteau shrugged.

She insisted that for the cost of his dinners he might be frugal. Having obtained a kitchen and two servants’ rooms, she wrote to her mother for linen and a loan of a thousand francs. Her mother sent two trunks of linen, some plate, and two thousand francs by a pious cook.

Ten days after the theatre evening Monsieur de Clagny called at four and found Dinah making a little cap. The sight of this proud woman condescending to needlework moved him; the pricks on one taper finger, which he had often kissed, told him she was not merely playing the maternal part. In that first interview he saw to the depths of her soul: she meant to be the journalist’s guardian spirit, to lead him into a nobler road. Dinah had understood that Lousteau’s practical difficulties sprang from moral defects—indolence and lack of will—habits that obstruct talent. She strove to make him happy by economy and method; she became housekeeper as she had become consort of a poet, by loftiness of soul.

“His happiness will be my absolution,” she told Clagny.

Etienne had flaunted their triumph at Nathan’s first night, revealing that to him Madame de la Baudraye was “a fine feather in his cap.” He preferred public display to shy secrecy. The judge, deceived for a time by Lousteau’s ingratiating attentions—he was a natural actor—gradually saw the truth. Between April and July, when Dinah expected confinement, she discovered why Lousteau had not triumphed over poverty: he was idle and weak of will. Genius requires a strong will; talent without it is unproductive. Men who govern or lead their age conquer indolence, deny themselves, and keep their faculties ready. Lousteau’s cigarette habit lulled him; tobacco numbed energy. Criticism as a profession also stultified him. The newspaper critic often performs mere acrobatics, praising or tearing down as convenience and wit allow. True criticism is a science requiring system, principles, and a fixed jurisprudence of ideas. Between Claude Vignon and Lousteau lay the gulf separating dexterity from art.

Dinah, her intellect quickened, measured her idol. She saw him scamping work, writing at the last minute under compulsion. She excused him, saying, “He is a poet!” and tried to help. Loving him, she attempted through her mother to negotiate an allowance from her husband without Etienne’s knowledge, thinking it would offend him. A few days before July’s end, she crumpled up a letter from her mother conveying Monsieur de la Baudraye’s ultimatum: “Madame cannot need an allowance in Paris when she can live in perfect luxury at her Chateau of Anzy: she may return.” Lousteau picked up this letter and read it.

“I will avenge you!” he said in that ominous tone flattering to a woman whose antipathies are flattered.

Five days later Bianchon and Duriau, the famous ladies’ doctor, were engaged at Lousteau’s; he made a great display over the infant’s arrival. Monsieur de Clagny and Madame Piedefer were to be godparents. Dinah gave birth to a boy that might have filled a queen with envy who hoped for an heir.

Bianchon and Clagny registered the child at the Mayor’s office as the son of Monsieur and Madame de la Baudraye, unknown to Etienne. Lousteau, rushing to a printer, had fifty circulars set up:

“Madame la Baronne de la Baudraye is happily delivered of a son.
Monsieur Etienne Lousteau has the pleasure of informing you of the fact.
The mother and child are doing well.”

Clagny, finding a list of Sancerre names to which Lousteau planned to send the notice, confiscated the list and the remaining circulars, showed proofs to Madame Piedefer, and dashed to another printer for a corrected announcement:

“Madame la Baronne de la Baudraye is happily delivered of a son.
Monsieur le Baron de la Baudraye has the honor of informing you of the fact.
Mother and child are doing well.”

Clagny destroyed proofs and intercepted sent copies, recovering thirty and finally leaving only one of the original notes—sent to Nathan.

Five times Clagny called on Nathan without success; when admitted and the affair explained, Nathan laughed.

“Give up that monument of recklessness?” he cried. “That autograph is a weapon an athlete in the circus cannot afford to lay down. That note proves Lousteau has no heart, taste, or dignity; it insults himself. I have gone through a passion for a great lady and would have died rather than utter her name. A woman’s naming of her passion is supreme offering; a man—he must be a Lousteau! No, I would not give up that paper for a thousand crowns.”

Monsieur de Clagny, after an eloquent half-hour, argued otherwise: it was not for Lousteau but for a woman and child ignorant of the damage to their fortune and honour. At last Nathan reflected on the judge’s influence and gave up the circular.

When the jest was counteracted, Clagny rated Lousteau in Madame Piedefer’s presence and found Etienne fuming.

“I did it with a purpose!” Etienne protested. “Monsieur de la Baudraye has sixty thousand francs a year and refuses an allowance; I wished to make him feel the child is in my power.”

“For that reason I agreed to be godfather,” the lawyer answered. “The child is registered as the son of the Baron and Baronne; if you have father’s feelings you ought to rejoice, for the child is heir to a great entailed estate.”

“And is the mother to die of hunger?”

“Be at ease. I will transact with Monsieur de la Baudraye.”

Clagny left chilled. Dinah, his idol, was loved for her money; would she not, too late, be disillusioned? “Poor woman!” he thought, pitying and devoted.

The care of the infant, its cries, the quiet needed for the mother, and Madame Piedefer’s presence were adverse to writing; Lousteau moved to rooms on the first floor. Obliged to attend first nights without Dinah, living apart, he found charms in liberty. He was dragged to jollifications and to the houses of friends’ mistresses, seeing brilliantly dressed young women in whom economy seemed treason. Dinah, after nursing for three months, could not compare with these perishable blossoms.

Yet home life had strong attraction for Etienne. In three months the mother and daughter with the Sancerre cook and little Pamela had transformed the apartment. Lousteau found breakfasts and dinners served with a kind of luxury. Dinah, handsome and careful, anticipated his wishes; he felt king at home, where everything, even the baby, was subject to his selfishness. Didine’s affection appeared in every trifle; the deceptions of his unreal passion continued.

Dinah was aware that Lousteau’s life threatened ruin to love and household. After ten months she weaned her baby, installed her mother upstairs, and restored family intimacy. The daily intercourse of domestic life gives a woman power; men left too often to themselves become susceptible to release. Dinah intended to be indispensable and to infuse energy into Lousteau, whose weakness she mistook for security. She suggested subjects, sketched treatments, and at times wrote whole chapters. She revived dying talent by transfusing fresh blood; she supplied ideas and opinions. She produced two successful books by collaboration she kept secret from Madame Piedefer.

This galvanism was rewarded by better pay, and they lived comfortably through 1838. Lousteau grew accustomed to seeing Dinah do his work and paid her nothing—“monkey money.” The more she gave, the more she loved him; she felt she could never give him up.

But another child came, and that year was a trial. Despite precautions, Etienne contracted debts; he worked himself nearly to death to pay them while Dinah was ill, and she thought him heroic. After this effort, appalled at supporting two women, children, and two maids, he was incapable of continuing the struggle by his pen after having failed even to maintain himself. So he let things take their chance and exaggerated home-made love to secure liberty abroad.

Dinah endured stoically without support. The belief “He loves me!” gave her superhuman strength. She worked as hard as the most energetic spirits. At the risk of her beauty she sacrificed dress—dyeing her gowns black and wearing nothing else. Malaga mocked the stench of black that she carried, and the thrifty black irritated Lousteau, who reproached her for dowdiness while secretly benefiting from her sacrifices—just like a woman who makes a man debased and then scorns him for dirt.

Dinah then gathered the loose reins by which a clever woman steers a weak man, but by doing so she lost moral lustre; suspicion and reproach draw a woman into quarrels that breed disrespect. She made concessions; Lousteau entertained his friends—Nathan, Bixiou, Blondet, Finot—whose manners and talk were depraving. They mocked her principles as provincial prudishness and preached woman’s superiority.

During Carnival 1840 she disguised herself, attending balls at the Opera-house and suppers to spy on Etienne. The morning after Mid-Lent she came home from a ball in fancy dress to go to bed; she had gone to watch Lousteau, who, believing her ill, had engaged that evening to Fanny Beaupré. Warned by a friend, he behaved so as to deceive her, and she, eager to be deceived, was duped.

Stepping from the hired cab she met Monsieur de la Baudraye, the porter pointing her out. The little old man took his wife by the arm in an icy tone: “So this is you, madame!” The conjugal authority froze her; to escape Etienne’s eye she had chosen a dress he would not detect. Using her mask she slipped away, changed, and went to her mother’s rooms, where she found her husband waiting. Despite her assumed dignity she blushed.

“What do you want of me, monsieur? Are we not separated forever?” she asked.

“Actually, yes. Legally, no,” he answered. Madame Piedefer signalled; Dinah understood his visit was prompted by interest.

“Nothing could have brought you here but your own interests,” she said bitterly.

“Our interests,” he replied coldly, “for we have two children. Your Uncle Silas Piedefer is dead in New York; after making and losing fortunes, he left some seven or eight hundred thousand francs—some say a million. I am chief in our common interests and act for you.”

“In everything that relates to business I trust Monsieur de Clagny,” she retorted. “He knows the law; come to terms with him.”

“I have no occasion for Monsieur de Clagny to take my children from you,” Monsieur de la Baudraye said.

“Your children!” Dinah laughed. “Your children to whom you have sent no sou!” Her outburst froze under his coolness.

“Your mother brought them to show me,” he continued. “They are charming boys. I do not intend to part from them. I shall take them to Anzy, if only to save them from seeing their mother disguised like a—”

“Silence!” Dinah cried. “What do you want of me?”

“A power of attorney to receive our Uncle Silas’ property.”

Dinah wrote two lines to Monsieur de Clagny and asked her husband to return in the afternoon.

At five o’clock Clagny—now Attorney-General—explained her position and undertook to bargain with the old man. Monsieur de la Baudraye, needing her power of attorney to deal as he wished, purchased it by concessions: he agreed to allow her ten thousand francs a year so long as she chose to reside in Paris; the children were to be placed in his custody at six; and Clagny extracted payment in advance.

Little La Baudraye came jaunty to say good-bye in a white india-rubber overcoat. He looked so like the La Baudraye of 1836 that Dinah despaired. From the garden, where he smoked, Lousteau watched him cross the forecourt. This short scene changed the writer’s secret scheming. As he smoked a second cigar, he reviewed the situation.

Their life together had cost both sides equally; in moral accounting Lousteau considered himself the creditor. It was a favorable moment to desert her. Tired after three years of a comedy that could not be habit, he was hiding his weariness; he forced a smile at home like a debtor before a creditor. The advantages he had expected in future had sustained him; but when Monsieur de la Baudraye embarked briskly for America, Lousteau ceased to believe in the future.

Entering the drawing-room where Dinah had just left her husband, he found her grave.

“Etienne,” she said, “do you know my lord proposed that if I wish to return to Anzy during his absence, he hopes my mother’s advice will weigh with me and that I shall go back with my children.”

“It is very good advice,” Lousteau replied dryly, knowing she expected strong denial.

His tone, words, and cold look struck her who lived only in love; two tears slipped down her cheeks, unseen until she wiped them. He asked tenderly, “What is it, Didine?”

“Just as I was priding myself on having won our freedom at the cost of my fortune—by selling what is most precious to a mother’s heart—I have sold my children! He will have them at six and I cannot see them unless I go to Sancerre; that is torture! Ah, dear God! What have I done—?”

Lousteau knelt, kissed her hands, coaxing.

“You do not understand me,” he said. “I blame myself; I am not worth such sacrifices. I am second-rate in literature. If I can no longer cut a figure at the bottom of the newspaper, the editors will throw me aside. We tight-rope dancers have no retiring pension! I am forty-two, idle as a marmot. My love can only be fatal to you. At twenty-two I lived on Florine; what was excusable in youth is a disgrace to a man of forty. We have shared existence’s burden; it has not been lovely. Out of devotion you wear black and that does me no credit.” Dinah shrugged magnanimously. “Yes,” he went on, “you sacrifice everything to my whims, even your beauty. I, with a heart worn from past struggles, a soul full of dark presentiments, cannot repay your exquisite love equally. I cannot bear to see a sweet poem end badly. Am I wrong?”

Dinah loved him so truly that this prudence, measured like Clagny’s, gratified and staunched her tears. “He loves me for myself alone!” she thought, smiling.

After four years of intimacy her love now combined every shade modern society creates; Beyle (Stendhal) had first delineated such complex affections. Lousteau could magnetize Dinah—one look or touch reduced her to obedience. A kind word wreathed her soul with flowers; a cold look depressed her. Walking at his arm she lost all sense of herself. Fascinated by his wit and magnetized by his airs, his vices seemed trivial. She even loved the cigar-smoke drifting into her room and hid to inhale it. She hated editors who refused Lousteau money and deluded herself that he was writing a remunerative novel rather than working off a debt.

This is true love: heart and head, passion, caprice, and taste. Dinah argued, “If I have put myself outside the pale, why should you not sacrifice some of a man’s honor for me? We live outside social convention; Nathan can accept homage from Florine; why not from me? We will square accounts when we part; only death can part us. We shall be married some day—time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.”

“I never thought of that,” said Lousteau simply, adding to himself, “Time enough to part when little La Baudraye is safe back again.”

From that day forth Etienne lived in luxury; and Dinah, on first nights, could hold her own with the best dressed women in Paris. Lousteau, affectingly tragic among his friends, pretended to be ruined by Madame de la Baudraye.

“Oh, what would I not give to the friend who would deliver me from Dinah! But no one ever can!” he said. “She loves me enough to throw herself out of the window if I told her.”

He was pitied; he took precautions against Dinah’s jealousy when accepting invitations, yet remained unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, in despair at seeing Dinah degraded when she might have been rich and had the position she once sought, told her bluntly—“You are betrayed”—and she replied only, “I know it.”

The lawyer was silenced; yet he found his tongue to say one thing. Madame de la Baudraye interrupted him.

“Do you still love me?” she asked.

“I would lose my soul for you!” he cried, starting up. The hapless man’s eyes flashed; he trembled; he believed himself blessed to be accepted as his idol’s avenger, and this poor joy filled him with rapture. “Why are you so startled?” she said as she made him sit. “That is how I love him.” The lawyer understood the ad hominem; tears shone in the judge’s eyes, who had just condemned a man to death.

Lousteau’s satiety, that odious end of illicit relations, betrayed itself in a thousand little ways—grains of sand in lovers’ paradise which Dinah had not seen until they were pebbles. Madame de la Baudraye had at last understood Lousteau’s character.

“He is,” she told her mother, “a poet, defenseless against disaster, mean from laziness, not for want of heart, rather too prone to pleasure; in short, a great cat, whom it is impossible to hate. What would become of him without me? I hindered his marriage; he has no prospects. His talent would perish in privations.”

“Oh, my Dinah!” Madame Piedefer exclaimed, “what a hell you live in! What is the feeling that gives you strength enough to persist?”

“I will be a mother to him!” she replied.

There are horrible situations in which we decide nothing until friends disclose our dishonor. We make compromises so long as we escape the prosecutor’s eye. Clagny, clumsy as a tortured man, tormented Dinah. “To preserve my love I will be all that Madame de Pompadour was to preserve her power,” she thought when Clagny left—an expression showing that love had become burden and toil rather than pleasure.

The part she assumed was horribly painful, and Lousteau made it no easier. When he wanted to go out after dinner he played tender farces of affection, addressing her in words of devotion; he would take her by the chain, and when he had bruised her would say, “Did I wound you?” These false caresses degraded Dinah, who believed in a revival of his love. The mother yielded to the mistress with shameful readiness. She felt herself a mere plaything, and at last confessed to herself: “Well, then, I will be his plaything!”—and found joy in it—the rapture of damnation.

A woman of resolute spirit, picturing solitude, might have found the courage to quit. Dinah preferred the miseries of fierce intimacy to the absence of the remorse-sweetened joys; she chose the bitter draught of love as the traveller prefers an oasis to princely wine. When she wondered at midnight, “Will he come home, or will he not?” she was not alive again till she heard Lousteau’s boots and the bell.

She often tried to restrain him by giving him pleasure; she hoped to match his rivals and leave them no hold on his agitated heart. How many times a day she rehearsed the tragedy of Le Dernier Jour d’un condamné, telling herself, “To-morrow we part.” And how often would a word, a look, a seemingly artless kiss bring her back to the depths of her love! It was terrible. She had often meditated suicide as she paced the little town garden. She had not exhausted the vast treasure of devotion a woman bears.

The romance of Adolphe was her Bible; above all, she would not be an Ellenore. She allowed herself no tear-storms, avoiding the bitterness so cleverly described by the critic whose analysis she admired, and who seemed almost superior to the book. She repeated the critic’s counsel to herself as rules of behavior—no reproachful silence, no scornful lip on the lover’s return. “I will not be like every other woman,” she said. “If for one day only he should recognize my merits and say, ‘That victim never uttered a cry!’—it will be all I ask. And besides, the others have him for an hour; I have him for life!”

Her husband Monsieur de la Baudraye, thinking himself justified by his private tribunal in punishing his wife, had robbed her to pursue his cherished enterprise of reclaiming three thousand acres of moorland—Anzy—a project begun in 1836. He manipulated Silas Piedefer’s property so that he reduced its proved value to eight hundred thousand francs while pocketing twelve hundred thousand. He did not announce his return; while his wife endured unspeakable woes, he built farms, dug trenches, and ploughed rough ground with the courage that made him one of the most remarkable provincial agriculturists.

The four hundred thousand francs he had filched from his wife were spent in three years on the undertaking, and Anzy was expected to return seventy-two thousand francs a year net after taxes. The eight hundred thousand he invested at four and a half percent, bought at eighty francs during the financial crisis. By securing his wife an income of forty-eight thousand francs he considered himself no longer in her debt. Could he not restore the odd twelve hundred thousand as soon as the four-and-a-half-per-cents rose? He fancied himself the greatest man in Sancerre besides one—the richest proprietor in France—whose rival he considered himself. He saw himself with an income of a hundred and forty thousand francs, of which ninety thousand came from entailed lands. Calculating payments—ten thousand in taxes, three thousand in working expenses, ten thousand to his wife, and twelve hundred to his mother-in-law—he would boast in Sancerre’s literary circles of his moderation: “I am reputed miserly, but my outlay amounts to twenty-six thousand five hundred francs a year. And I have the education of my two children! The second house of La Baudraye may yet have as noble a center as the first.—I shall most likely go to Paris and petition the King for the title of Count—Monsieur Roy is a Count—and my wife would be pleased to be Madame la Comtesse.”

This was said with such coolness that no one laughed; only Monsieur Boirouge remarked, “In your place, I should not be happy unless I had a daughter.” “Well, I shall go to Paris before long,” said the Baron.

In early 1842 Madame de la Baudraye, feeling she was for Lousteau no more than a reserve, had again sacrificed herself to secure his comfort; she resumed black raiment, now as mourning, for her pleasure was turning to remorse. She was too often shamed not to feel the chain; her mother found her sunk in meditative moods into which visions of the future cast unhappy souls.

Madame Piedefer, by her spiritual director’s advice, watched for exhaustion, which the priest said would inevitably come, and then she pleaded for the children. She urged Dinah and Lousteau to live apart, not asking her to give him up. In real life such violent situations do not end as in books—by death or contrived catastrophe—but far less poetically: in disgust, the blighting of every flower of the soul, in habit, or in another passion. When common sense, social propriety, family interest, sense of insults too offensive, the fatigue of constant self-sacrifice, and a too cruel blow—one of those mean acts a man never lets a woman know unless he believes himself master—put the crowning touch to her revulsion, the moment comes for intervention by the friend who undertakes the cure. Madame Piedefer had no difficulty removing the film from her daughter’s eyes.

She sent for Monsieur de Clagny, who completed the work by assuring Madame de la Baudraye that if she gave up Etienne, her husband would allow her to keep the children, live in Paris, and restore her to command of her fortune. “And what a life you are leading!” he said. “With care and judgment, the support of pious persons, you may have a salon and conquer a position. Paris is not Sancerre.”

Dinah left it to Monsieur de Clagny to negotiate reconciliation with the old man. Monsieur de la Baudraye had sold wine, wool, felled timber, and without telling his wife had come to Paris to invest two hundred thousand francs in a residence in the Rue de l’Arcade being sold in liquidation. He had been a member of the Departmental Council since 1826; now, paying ten thousand francs in taxes, he was doubly qualified for a peerage under new legislation.

Some time before the elections of 1842 he put himself forward as candidate unless called to the Upper House as Peer. He asked for the title of Count and promotion in the Legion of Honor. Monsieur de Clagny, whose talents and modesty were appreciated by the authorities, supported him, pointing out that raising this enterprising agriculturist to the peerage would guarantee such undertakings.

Monsieur de la Baudraye, Count, Peer of France, and Commander of the Legion of Honor, was vain enough to wish to cut a figure with a wife and handsomely appointed house. “He wanted to enjoy life,” he said. He therefore addressed a letter to his wife, dictated by Clagny, begging her to live under his roof and to furnish the house, giving play to the taste whose evidences had charmed him at the Château d’Anzy. The newly made Count told his wife that while their property’s interests forbade his leaving Sancerre, the education of their boys required her presence in Paris. He asked Clagny to place sixty thousand francs at Madame la Comtesse’s disposal for interior decoration, requesting a marble tablet over the gateway with the inscription: Hotel de la Baudraye.

He then accounted for Silas Piedefer’s money, told her of the investment at four and a half percent of the eight hundred thousand francs he had brought from New York, and allowed her that income for expenses, including the children’s education. As he would be compelled to stay in Paris during sessions, he requested his wife reserve for him a suite of rooms in an entresol over the kitchens.

“Bless me! why, he is growing young again—a gentleman—a magnifico—What will he become next? It is quite alarming,” said Madame de la Baudraye.

“He now fulfils all your wishes at the age of twenty,” replied the lawyer.

Comparing future prospects with present position was unendurable to Dinah. Only the day before, Anna de Fontaine had turned away to avoid seeing her at Chamarolles’ school. “I am a countess,” she said to herself. “I shall have the peer’s blue hammer-cloth on my carriage, and the leaders of the literary world in my drawing-room—and I will look at her!”—This little triumph weighed heavily at the moment of her rehabilitation, as the world’s contempt had once weighed on her happiness.

One fine day in May 1842 Madame de la Baudraye paid all small household debts and left a thousand crowns atop the packet of receipted bills. After sending her mother and the children to the Hôtel de la Baudraye, she awaited Lousteau, dressed to leave. When Etienne came to dinner, she said: “I have upset the pot, my dear. Madame de la Baudraye requests the pleasure of your company at the Rocher de Cancale.” She bewildered him with the light and easy manners assumed by the woman who till that morning had been his slave; she too had been acting a farce for two months.

“Madame de la Baudraye is figged out as if for a first night,” said he.

“Do not forget the respect you owe to Madame de la Baudraye,” said Dinah gravely. “I do not mean to understand such a word as figged out.”

“Didine a rebel!” said he, putting his arm round her waist.

“There is no such person as Didine; you have killed her, my dear,” she replied, releasing herself. “I am taking you to the first performance of Madame la Comtesse de la Baudraye.”

“It is true, then, that our insect is a peer of France?” he sneered.

“The nomination is to be gazetted in this evening’s Moniteur, as Monsieur de Clagny tells me, who is promoted to the Court of Appeal.”

“Well, it is right,” said the journalist. “The entomology of society ought to be represented in the Upper House.”

“My friend, we are parting for ever,” said Madame de la Baudraye, trying to control trembling. “I have dismissed the two servants. When you go in, you will find the house in order, and no debts. I shall always feel a mother’s affection for you, but in secret. Let us part calmly, without a fuss, like decent people.”

“Have you had a fault to find with my conduct during the past six years?”

“None, but that you have spoiled my life, and wrecked my prospects,” he answered hard. “You have read Benjamin Constant very diligently; you have even studied the last critique; but you have read with a woman’s eyes. Though you have one of those superior intellects which would make a fortune of a poet, you have never dared to take the man’s point of view.

“That book, my dear, is of both sexes.—We agreed that books were male or female, dark or fair. In Adolphe women see nothing but Ellenore; young men see only Adolphe; men of experience see Ellenore and Adolphe; political men see the whole of social existence. You did not think it necessary to read the soul of Adolphe—any more than your critic, who saw only Ellenore. What kills that poor fellow, my dear, is that he has sacrificed his future for a woman; that he never can be what he might have been—an ambassador, a minister, a chamberlain, a poet—and rich. He gives up six years of his energy when a man is ready to submit to apprenticeship—to a petticoat, which he outstrips in ingratitude, for the woman who has thrown over her first lover is certain sooner or later to desert the second. Adolphe is, in fact, a tow-haired German, who has not spirit enough to be false to Ellenore. There are Adolphes who spare their Ellenores all ignominious quarreling and reproaches, who say to themselves, ‘I will not talk of what I have sacrificed; I will not for ever be showing the stump of my wrist to let that incarnate selfishness I have made my queen,’ as Ramorny does in The Fair Maid of Perth. But men like that get cast aside.

“You, at this moment, are playing both parts. You are suffering from losing your position, and think yourself justified in throwing over a hapless lover whose misfortune it has been that he fancied you so far superior as to understand that, though a man’s heart ought to be true, his sex may be allowed to indulge its caprices.”

“And do you suppose that I shall not make it my business to restore to you all you have lost by me? Be quite easy,” said Madame de la Baudraye, astounded. “Your Ellenore is not dying; and if God gives her life, if you amend your ways, if you give up courtesans and actresses, we will find you a better match than a Felicie Cardot.”

The two lovers were sullen. Lousteau affected dejection; Dinah, really distressed, listened to her heart’s reproaches.

“Why,” said Lousteau presently, “why not end as we ought to have begun—hide our love from all eyes, and see each other in secret?”

“Never!” cried the new-made Countess, with an icy look. “Do you not comprehend that we are, after all, finite creatures? Our feelings seem infinite by reason of our anticipation of heaven, but here on earth they are limited by our physical strength. There are some feeble, mean natures which may receive an endless number of wounds and live on; but some highly-tempered souls snap at last under repeated blows. You have—”

“Oh! enough!” cried he. “No more copy! Your dissertation is unnecessary, since you can justify yourself by merely saying—‘I have ceased to love!’”

“What!” she exclaimed. “Is it I who have ceased to love?”

“Certainly. You have calculated that I gave you more trouble, more vexation than pleasure, and you desert your partner—”

“I desert!” cried she, clasping her hands. “Have not you yourself just said ‘Never’?”

“Well, then, yes! Never,” she repeated vehemently.

This final Never, spoken in fear of falling under Lousteau’s influence, he took as the death-warrant of his power; Dinah remained insensible to his sarcastic scorn.

The journalist could not suppress a tear. He had found in Dinah the gentlest La Valliere, the most delightful Pompadour that any egoist short of a king could hope for; and, like a boy who has killed a cockchafer by tormenting it, Lousteau shed a tear.

Madame de la Baudraye rushed out of the dining room, paid the bill, and fled home to the Rue de l’Arcade, scolding herself and thinking herself a brute.

Dinah, who had made her house a model of comfort, now metamorphosed herself. This cost thirty thousand francs more than her husband had anticipated. The fatal accident depriving the House of Orleans of the heir-presumptive in 1842 required the Chambers to meet in August; La Baudraye presented his titles sooner than expected, and then saw what his wife had done. He was so delighted that he paid the thirty thousand francs without a word, as he had formerly paid eight thousand for decorating La Baudraye.

On his return from the Luxembourg, where he had been presented by two peers—the Baron de Nucingen and the Marquis de Montriveau—the new Count met the Duc de Chaulieu walking along; the contrast filled his heart with a draught of the balm on which the middle class had been getting drunk since 1840.

Madame de la Baudraye was shocked to see her husband improved and looking better than on the day of their marriage. The little dwarf, full of rapturous delight at sixty-four, triumphed in the life long denied him; in the family his handsome cousin Milaud of Nevers had said he would never have; and in his wife—who had asked Monsieur and Madame de Clagny to dinner to meet the parish cure and his two sponsors to the Chamber of Peers—he petted the children with fatuous delight. The handsome display on the table met his approval.

“These are the fleeces of the Berry sheep,” he said, showing Baron de Nucingen the dish-covers surmounted by his coronet. “They are of silver, you see!”

Though consumed by melancholy, which she concealed with the determination of a superior woman, Dinah was charming, witty, and young again in her court mourning.

“You might declare,” cried La Baudraye to Monsieur de Nucingen with a wave to his wife, “that the Countess was not yet thirty.”

“Ah, ha! Matame is a voman of dirty!” replied the baron.

“In every sense,” replied the Countess. “I am, in fact, five-and-thirty, and mean to set up a little passion—”

“Oh, yes, my wife ruins me in curiosities and china images—”

“She started that mania,” said the Marquis with a smile.

“Yes,” said La Baudraye, cold at the Marquis, “you know that in ‘25, ‘26, and ‘27, she picked a million francs’ worth of treasures. Anzy is a perfect museum.”

“What a cool hand!” thought Monsieur de Clagny.

But misers have savings for use. On the day after the Regency vote passed, the little Count went back to Sancerre for the vintage and resumed old habits.

That winter the Comtesse, supported by the Attorney-General to the Court of Appeals, tried to form a small circle. She had an “at home” day, selected men of mark, receiving those of serious purpose and ripe years. She went to the Opera twice a week with her mother and Madame de Clagny, who visited Dinah at her husband’s insistence. Still, despite cleverness, charming manners, and stylishness, she was only really happy with her children, on whom she lavished disappointed affection.

Worthy Monsieur de Clagny tried to recruit women for the Countess’ circle, and he succeeded among advocates of piety more than women of fashion. “And they bore her!” he said to himself with horror, seeing his idol matured by grief, pale from remorse, then restored by luxury and care for her boys. This devoted friend, encouraged by her mother and the cure, was full of expedients. Every Wednesday he introduced some celebrity from Germany, England, Italy, or Prussia to the dear Countess; he spoke of her as an exceptional woman to people she hardly addressed two words; but she listened with such deep attention that they went away convinced of her superiority. In Paris, Dinah conquered by silence as at Sancerre she had conquered by loquacity. Now and then a smart saying or a sarcasm betrayed a woman used to dealing with ideas—the one who, four years since, had given new life to Lousteau’s articles.

This phase was to the hapless lawyer like the Indian summer after a sunless year. He affected to be older than he was, to have the right to befriend Dinah without injury, and kept at a distance as though he were young, handsome, and compromising, like a man who has happiness to conceal. He tried to keep attentions secret, the trifling gifts Dinah showed to everyone; he suggested a dangerous meaning for his little services. “He plays at passion,” said the Countess, laughing. She made fun of Monsieur de Clagny to his face, and he thought, “She notices me.”

One evening Clagny and his wife were taking Dinah home from the theatre; she was pensive. They had seen Leon Gozlan’s La Main Droite et la Main Gauche.

“What are you thinking about?” asked the lawyer, alarmed at her dejection. This deep melancholy, though disguised, was a perilous malady for which Clagny had no remedy; for true love is often clumsy, especially when not reciprocated. He loved like Alceste, when Madame de la Baudraye wanted love like Philinte. The meaner side of love cannot suit the Misanthrope’s loyalty. Thus Dinah had taken care never to open her heart to him. How could she confess that sometimes she regretted the slough she had left?

She felt a void in fashionable life; she had no one for whom to dress, or whom to tell of her successes. Sometimes memory of wretchedness came, mingled with memories of consuming joys. She would hate Lousteau for not following her; she wished for tender or furious letters from him.

Dinah made no reply, so Clagny repeated the question, taking her hand. “Will you have the right hand or the left?” she said, smiling.

“The left,” said he, “for I suppose you mean the truth or a fib.”

“Well, then, I saw him,” she said into the lawyer’s ear. “And as I saw him looking so sad, so out of heart, I said to myself, Has he a cigar? Has he any money?”

“If you wish the truth, I can tell it,” said he. “He is living as a husband with Fanny Beaupre. You have forced me to tell you this secret; I should never have told you, for you might have suspected me perhaps of an ungenerous motive.”

Madame de la Baudraye grasped his hand. “Your husband,” she said to her chaperon, “is one of the rarest souls!—Ah! Why—” She shrank into her corner, looking out, but did not finish the sentence Clagny could guess: “Why had not Lousteau a little of your husband’s generosity?”

This information cured Dinah of melancholy; she threw herself into the whirl of fashion. She wished for success, and she achieved it; still she did not make much way with women and found introductions difficult.

In March, Madame Piedefer’s friends—the priests and Monsieur de Clagny—got Madame de la Baudraye appointed receiver of subscriptions for Madame de Carcado’s charitable work. She was commissioned to collect Royal Family donations for Guadeloupe earthquake sufferers. The Marquise d’Espard, hearing of the Countess's commission, said: “I have lived a long time in the world, and I can remember nothing finer than the manoeuvres undertaken for the rehabilitation of Madame de la Baudraye.”

In early spring, Paris smiled in March 1843, and Fanny Beaupre’s attache had seen Madame de la Baudraye several times without being seen by her. Stung by jealousy and provincial pride, he beheld his former mistress in a handsome carriage, well dressed, with dreamy eyes, and her two little boys, one at each window. He accused himself the more because he was warring with sharp poverty—poverty unconfessed. Like frivolous natures, he kept a point of honor: never to derogate in the eyes of his little public, which drives some men to crime to avoid expulsion from Per-cent’s temple, and has made some criminals perform acts of virtue.

Lousteau dined and smoked as if rich. Not for an inheritance would he buy anything but the dearest cigars; he took walks in patent-leather boots; but he feared execution on goods that had already received the last sacrament. Fanny had nothing left to pawn; her salary was pledged. After exhausting advances from newspapers and publishers, Etienne knew not what ink could churn gold. Gambling houses, suppressed, no longer cashed IOUs. The journalist was reduced to such extremity that he had borrowed a hundred francs of the poorest friend Bixiou, whom he’d never asked before. What distressed him was not owing five thousand francs but seeing himself bereft of elegance, of furniture bought by privation and added to by Madame de la Baudraye.

On April 3 a yellow poster announced the sale of a handsome suite of furniture the following Saturday. Lousteau walked smoking, seeking ideas—for in Paris ideas are in the air—yet found nothing but friends who carried him to dinner and intoxicating champagne. “Beware,” said the virulent Bixiou one night—the man who would give a hundred francs and stab a comrade with sarcasm—“if you go to sleep drunk every night, one day you will wake up mad.”

On Friday the unhappy wretch felt condemned. He might once have said, “The furniture is old! I will buy new,” but he was incapable now of literary legerdemain. Publishers paid poorly; newspapers bargained hard; opera managers did with tenors as they pleased.

He walked with a cigar and an affected smile. Then he saw Madame de la Baudraye pass in a carriage; she was going to drive in the Bois. “There is nothing else left!” he said to himself, and went home to smarten.

That evening, at seven, he arrived in a hackney at Madame de la Baudraye’s door, and begged the porter to send a note up—a few lines: “Would Madame la Comtesse do Monsieur Lousteau the favor of receiving him for a moment, and at once?” The note bore the seal they both had used. The Countess had the word Parce que engraved on a carnelian—a potent word.

She had just finished dressing for the Opera; at the sight of the seal she turned pale. “I will come,” she said, tucking the note into her dress. Firm to conceal agitation, she begged her mother to put the children to bed and sent for Lousteau, receiving him in a boudoir with open doors. She wore brocaded brocade in pale blue stripes; gloves trimmed with tassels set off white arms; hair à la Sévigné; pearls lay on her bosom like bubbles.

“What is the matter, monsieur?” she asked, putting out a foot on a velvet cushion. “I thought, I hoped, I was quite forgotten.”

“If I should reply Never, you would refuse to believe me,” said Lousteau, standing, chewing flowers he plucked. For a moment silence reigned. Dinah saw he was dressed as a fastidious dandy. “You are the only person in the world who can help me, or hold out a plank—I am drowning and have swallowed more than one mouthful,” he said. “Since you see me here, it is because my affairs are going to the devil.”

“That is enough,” said she; “I understand.” He turned away, seemed to wipe a tear. “How much do you want, Etienne?” she went on in motherly tones. “We are at this moment old comrades; speak to me as you would to—Bixiou.”

“To save my furniture from vanishing to-morrow at the auction, eighteen hundred francs! To repay my friends, as much again! Three quarters’ rent—My ‘uncle’ wants five hundred francs—”

“And you—to live on?”

“Oh! I have my pen—”

“It is heavier to lift than anyone who reads your articles believes,” she said with a subtle smile. “I have not such a sum, but come to-morrow at eight; the bailiff will surely wait till nine, especially if you bring him away to pay him.”

She must dismiss Lousteau; she felt pity that might cut every social Gordian knot. “Thank you,” she added, rising and offering her hand. “Your confidence has done me good! It is long since my heart has known such joy—” Lousteau took her hand and pressed it to his heart. “A drop of water in the desert—and sent by the hand of an angel! God always does things handsomely!” He spoke half jest, half pathos; acting it was as fine as Talma’s Leicester. Dinah felt his heart beat through his coat; it throbbed with satisfaction and fire at seeing her rejuvenescent restored by wealth.

Stealing a glance, Dinah saw that his expression matched flowers of love that had blossomed in his heart; she tried to look into his eyes, but blood rushed to her face. Their eyes met with the same fiery glow as when Lousteau had encouraged himself on the Quay to crumple Dinah’s muslin. The Bohemian put his arm round her waist; she yielded; their cheeks touched.

“Here comes my mother, hide!” cried Dinah and hurried to intercept Madame Piedefer. “Mamma,” said she—this coaxing word always enthralled the stern old lady—“will you do me a great favor? Take the carriage and go to my banker, Monsieur Mongenod, with a note I will give you, and bring back six thousand francs. Come, come—it is an act of charity; come into my room.” She dragged away her mother, anxious to see who was in the boudoir.

Two days afterwards Madame Piedefer consulted the parish cure. After hearing the old mother’s lamentations, the priest said gravely: “Any moral regeneration not based on strong religious sentiment and carried out in the Church is built on sand.—The many means of grace enjoined by Catholicism are so many dams necessary to restrain evil promptings. Persuade your daughter to perform all her duties, and we shall save her yet.”

Within ten days the Hôtel de la Baudraye was shut. The Countess, the children, her mother, and household, including a tutor, had gone to Sancerre, where Dinah intended to spend the summer. She was everything that was nice to the Count, people said.

And so the Muse of Sancerre had simply come back to family and married life; but evil tongues declared she had been compelled to come back, for the little peer’s wishes would no doubt be fulfilled—he hoped for a little girl.

Gatien and Monsieur Gravier lavished care and servile attention on the handsome Countess. Gatien, who had been to Paris to learn lionnerie, was supposed to have a good chance of favor. Others bet on the tutor; Madame Piedefer urged religion.

In June 1844, as the Comte took a walk on the Mall at Sancerre with the two fine little boys, he met Monsieur Milaud the Public Prosecutor and said, “These are my children, cousin.”

“Ah, ha! so these are our children!” replied the lawyer with a twinkle.

PARIS, June 1843–August 1844.
