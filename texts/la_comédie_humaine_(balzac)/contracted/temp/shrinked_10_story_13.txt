**By Honoré de Balzac**  
**Translated by R. S. Scott**  

**DEDICATION**  
To George Sand, whose friendship has been a source of joy amidst my literary challenges. May this work reflect the esteemed company of great names and noble hearts that accompany my writings.

**LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES**

**FIRST PART**

**I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.**  
My dearest Renee, I am free! I hope you have escaped from the convent as well. My release was quite ordinary; my aunt, fearing for my health, allowed me to leave after your departure left me despondent. Now in Paris, I feel our souls are intertwined after our shared dreams. Without you, I felt like a Carmelite, trapped in monotony. My aunt cannot understand my longing for life outside the convent.

I remember the day I left; my aunt's parting words were both ominous and affectionate. She believes I possess a noble spirit that will guide my passions. Arriving in Paris, I found no one waiting for me, and my heart sank at the emptiness of my new home, which belonged to my late grandmother. The memories overwhelmed me, and I felt a deep connection to her presence in the rooms.

Philippe, my grandmother's servant, informed me that my inheritance was intact, but my father had not yet restored the family home. This realization filled me with despair, as I feared I would be left to languish without a marriage arrangement. Yet, as I settled into my rooms, I found comfort in my grandmother's memories and resolved to make the space my own.

I began to write to you, longing for our conversations and the details of our lives. I hope you are well and will share everything about your surroundings with me.

**October 19th.**  
I met my mother for the first time since my return. She greeted me warmly, treating me as an equal rather than a daughter. Her beauty is striking, and I was charmed by her demeanor. She expressed her desire to be a friend rather than a strict mother. My father was equally affectionate, and I felt a sense of belonging for the first time.

I am now preparing for my debut into society, and my father has provided me with a generous allowance. I will have a governess, Miss Griffith, to help me navigate this new world. I am excited yet anxious about the changes ahead.

**II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.**  
I am beginning to feel at home in my new life. My father has given me a substantial allowance, and I am acquiring new clothes for my social debut. I am eager to shed my convent attire and embrace the elegance of society.

Miss Griffith is proving to be a good companion, and I appreciate her guidance. My father has declined a ministerial position, preferring the quieter life of an ambassador. I sense that my family is busy with their own lives, leaving me to discover my own path.

I have not yet ventured into the streets of Paris, but I am eager to experience the city. My mother is often out, and I find myself alone with Miss Griffith, which allows me time to reflect on my new circumstances.

**III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.**  
I am ready to enter society! After much preparation, I feel confident in my appearance and demeanor. I have acquired a beautiful wardrobe and am excited for my debut. I plan to present myself as charming and innocent, hoping to win the favor of those around me.

Despite my excitement, I feel a twinge of regret for my past life in the convent. The transition is overwhelming, and I am aware of the separations that come with social life. My family remains distant, absorbed in their own affairs, while I navigate this new world.

I am curious about the allure of society and how it captivates people, keeping them from truly connecting with one another. I hope to uncover the mysteries of my family and the society I am about to enter.

On December 15th, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day reminiscent of the Loire. Despite my carefully curated appearance, I received little attention from passersby, except for my father, who approved, and my mother, who greeted me with a kiss. Griffith, indifferent, scanned the crowd while I grew frustrated that one man inspected my carriage without noticing me, realizing that beauty in Paris is more common than I expected.

The men I observed were generally unattractive, their fashion lacking elegance and comfort, particularly their hats, which resembled truncated columns. I commented to Miss Griffith about their looks, but she defended them as cultivated.

At an evening ball, I stayed close to my mother, who attracted all the attention while I was merely a pretext for compliments. My dress, which I thought stunning, paled in comparison to the married women’s finery. The men at the ball did not impress me; they appeared worn and lacked the proud bearing of their ancestors. One man stood out for his beauty and intellect, but he seemed self-absorbed, suggesting that intellectuals often prioritize ambition over genuine connections.

Determined not to let society's indifference affect me, I focused on enjoying the dance. I overheard gossip, but my ignorance of the subjects made it less engaging. Upon returning home, I shared my experiences with my mother, who advised me to be discreet about my feelings. This advice highlighted the complexities of feminine duplicity, leaving me shy and uncertain about expressing my joy in dancing, troubled by the clash between my natural exuberance and societal expectations.

**Summary of V. Renee de Maucombe to Louise de Chaulieu**

Your letter moved me deeply as I reflect on our contrasting lives. You are entering a vibrant world, while I will find peace in my modest retreat at the Castle of Maucombe. Upon my return, my room remained unchanged, now with a lovely view of the Gemenos valley.

A fortnight after my arrival, my family dined with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy neighbor whose only son was lost to the army during Bonaparte's reign. After evading conscription, he joined the Emperor's bodyguard in 1813 but disappeared after the Battle of Leipzig. His father, clinging to hope, believed he might still be alive, even dreaming of him. Eventually, the son returned from travels through Russia, Poland, and Germany, aided by the French Minister in Berlin. M. de l'Estorade, a small landowner in Provence, has amassed a fortune of about 250,000 livres, excluding his estate.

My parents support a match between me and Louis de l'Estorade, especially since I would be acceptable without a dowry. They devised a plan to navigate the Civil Code, which complicates marriages for noble families. The dinner marked my first meeting with Louis, who, despite his humble background, is now my intended. His home is modest, lacking the grandeur of my own, reflecting years of hardship.

The dinner was simple, served on old silverware, and Louis appeared worn and silent, bearing the marks of his struggles. Despite this, I have agreed to marry him, contingent upon my ability to improve his estate and create a park. I have requested water from Maucombe to support my plans. In a month, I will be Mrs. de l'Estorade, and I believe I made a good impression on him.

While you prepare for the excitement of Parisian life, I embrace a simpler existence. I am determined to bring beauty to my new home, planting trees and creating a garden for my future children. I envision a life filled with nature and family, free from past turmoil.

My future seems straightforward, with no more Bonaparte to disrupt it. If I have children, they will be mine to raise, and I will find joy in their growth. You, dear Louise, will continue to live the romance I once dreamed of, and I ask you to share your adventures with me. Describe your balls, attire, and the men you encounter, as I will be there in spirit.

I jest about my new life, but I must embrace this role. My lips remain untouched, and our propriety is amusing. I must conclude my thoughts here.

P.S. I have about 1,000 livres to spend; please buy me lovely things unavailable here or in Marseilles. While you pursue your interests, remember the recluse at La Crampade. I will respond to your letter soon.

**Summary of Correspondence Between Don Felipe Henarez and Don Fernand Paris, and Letters from Louise de Chaulieu to Renee de Maucombe**

**I. Don Felipe Henarez to Don Fernand Paris (September)**

In this letter, Don Felipe reassures his brother, Don Fernand, of his safety amid Spain's political turmoil. He reflects on the massacre of their ancestors that forced them into a life of cunning survival. Despite Ferdinand's assurances of protection, Felipe remains skeptical, believing true Spaniards do not need to repeat promises.

Felipe recounts his escape from Spain, aided by brigands who once feared him and now protect him from the King’s vendetta. He laments the disrepair of his estates, which require significant investment to be profitable. The sight of Spain’s decline brings him sorrow, and he expresses a sense of martyrdom, feeling trapped between life and death, unable to fight for his country or pursue love.

He outlines his last wishes, urging Fernand to claim his titles and properties from the King, using his love for Marie Heredia as leverage. Felipe acknowledges the mutual affection between Fernand and Marie, encouraging their union while resigning himself to a life of obscurity. He plans to keep his mother’s diamonds for independence and offers Fernand a wedding gift from his meager savings. Felipe concludes with a sense of finality, preparing to embrace a life as the Baron de Macumer, away from Spain's chaos.

**II. Don Felipe Henarez to Don Fernand Paris (October)**

Felipe reflects on his current state, having arrived in France with little money. He describes his modest life, teaching Spanish and living simply. Despite his resignation, he finds solace in his new routine, enjoying nature and solitude. He expresses no regrets about his abdication, feeling detached from his former status and wealth.

Felipe opens up about his emotional state, revealing unexpressed tenderness and devotion, which he channels into his love for Spain. He questions his existence and laments the loss of his heritage. He finds joy in Fernand and Marie’s love, hoping they will cherish each other and fulfill the dreams he could not.

He reflects on their mother’s hopes for Fernand, believing she would be pleased with the union. Felipe urges Fernand to comply with his wishes and keep his identity as Henarez a secret from Marie, emphasizing the importance of preserving their family’s legacy.

**III. Louise de Chaulieu to Renee de Maucombe (First Letter)**

Louise expresses disbelief at Renee’s quick engagement to a stranger, warning her against the constraints of marriage and urging her to embrace life in Paris instead. She describes her own experiences in society, filled with excitement but also disillusionment with the men she encounters, finding them lacking in depth.

Louise shares her father’s plans to represent the King in Madrid, hinting at potential marriage prospects for herself. She critiques the superficiality of social interactions, noting the brutality underlying polite behavior. Despite her observations, she remains hopeful for love, longing for a connection that transcends the mundane.

**IV. Louise de Chaulieu to Renee de Maucombe (Second Letter, January)**

Louise writes about a refugee, Don Henarez, who has captured her interest. She describes his dignified yet humble demeanor, contrasting it with the superficiality of her social circle. Despite his taciturn nature, she senses a depth of character in him. Their interactions reveal a complex dynamic as she navigates her feelings and societal expectations.

Louise reflects on her father’s plans to return to Spain and considers the implications for her future. She grapples with her emotions, recognizing the rarity of genuine affection and the challenges of finding true love. Her correspondence reveals her internal struggle between societal norms and her desire for a meaningful connection.

**Summary of Louise de Chaulieu's Letter to Mme. de L'Estorade**

Dear Renee, you are truly wonderful, and I see that we can only be virtuous through deception. The man who loves us is ours to shape, though we often make fools of them. You aim to inspire ambition in yours while keeping him in love, but isn't love alone enough?

If there were no afterlife, your self-sacrifice would be tragic. You want to keep him ambitious and in love, but where is the line between virtue and calculation? Despite my deceit, I confess my love for Felipe, which I hide behind a mask of hypocrisy. I long for him to enter my life, yet I would scorn him if he did.

What holds me back from revealing my happiness to Felipe? His love is pure and overwhelming, yet I feel a strange force pulling me toward him, despite my better judgment. I find myself more attuned to your feelings as I navigate this emotional turmoil.

Felipe's happiness seems to stem from his devotion to me, but his respectful distance frustrates me. I want to shout to him that if he loves me as a mere image, he will be even more captivated by the real me.

Oh, Renee, please burn my letters, as I will yours. If anyone else were to read our intimate thoughts, I would have Felipe silence them permanently.

On Monday, I ponder the complexities of the human heart. I wish my father would introduce me to M. Bonald, for I seek answers. Does Felipe still worship me? If I sense any change in his respect, I would have the strength to walk away.

My father had my portrait painted by Mme. de Mirbel, and I sent the original to Felipe with a note suggesting both promise and challenge. If he addresses me affectionately in his reply, he is lost.

On Tuesday, I received his response, and it was perfect. He described how every moment away from me was filled with thoughts of my image, expressing his deep, unyielding happiness. He placed me on a pedestal, equating my presence with divine grace. His words revealed his vulnerability and longing, and I felt a wave of happiness wash over me.

The weather has been dreadful, preventing me from going out without raising suspicions.

On Wednesday evening, I saw Felipe at the Opera, and he seemed transformed. He approached our box, and his nervousness was palpable. His eyes sparkled with newfound light, and I felt a rush of joy. Others noticed his change, commenting on his love, which made me feel elated.

I yearn for him to overcome obstacles and boldly ask for my hand, so I can gauge the depth of his love. As I wrote, I saw him riskily climbing along the wall outside my window. When he leaped down and ran to show me he was unharmed, I was overwhelmed with emotion.

I am left wondering what he wanted to say. I will go to bed joyful, dreaming of our conversations. Farewell, dear friend. I have not scolded you for your silence, but it has been over a month since I heard from you. Are you finally happy? Have you given up your cherished independence?

In her letter, Renee expresses discontent with marriage, questioning why love is often overlooked by society and philosophers. She feels that marriage imposes a discordant obligation on women, forcing them into subservience for family. Renee reflects on her transformation from an individual to a possession within this framework, lamenting the emotional sacrifices she has made. She longs for a love that transcends societal expectations and hopes for fulfillment through motherhood.

Renee observes that her husband, Louis, remains blissfully unaware of her struggles, enjoying the benefits of her sacrifices without recognizing the effort behind them. She yearns for a child to channel her energy and emotions, believing motherhood could bring her joy.

In response, Louise shares her turmoil as she contemplates a secret meeting with Felipe, a man she desires. Torn between societal norms and her instincts, she wants to test Felipe's character and commitment. Driven by a longing for adventure, Louise plans a clandestine encounter under the lime trees in her garden, hoping to explore their feelings away from prying eyes.

As she prepares for the meeting, Louise reflects on the thrill and anxiety of the situation, wishing for Renee's company to share in the excitement. When the moment arrives, she meets Felipe, and they engage in a charged conversation filled with unspoken emotions. Their exchange reveals a deepening connection, with Louise acknowledging the complexities of love and the balance of power within relationships. She contemplates love's dual nature—one that commands and one that obeys. Despite her affection for Felipe, she maintains control, pondering whether their dynamic will shift over time.

Louise's reflections lead her to question pleasure and desire, wondering if the anticipation of love is more fulfilling than its actual experience. She grapples with the idea of pursuing another secret meeting, contemplating the boundaries of her desires and the societal constraints governing her actions.

**Summary of "The Same to the Same"**

Dear friend, it has been nearly eight months since our marriage, and your silence is inexcusable.

We traveled to the Castle of Chantepleurs, which Macumer purchased in Nivernais, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants arrived ahead of us, and we reached the castle the following evening. I slept throughout the journey, comforted by Felipe's tender presence. His affectionate gestures deeply touched me, and I awoke to find his passionate gaze unchanged. Despite his calm demeanor, he later revealed the intense emotions he had kept hidden.

As we settled into our new life, I found that marriage was not as daunting as I expected. Felipe reassured me of his fidelity, and I felt a mix of joy and the complexities of love. I began to understand the depth of my feelings and the nature of our bond.

I realized that true happiness in marriage is often unspoken, a sacred experience that cannot be easily shared. Duty seems to overshadow the natural expression of love, which flourishes freely. I admire your strength, Renee, and see that those who marry for love, like me, experience a different reality than those who marry for convenience, like you.

In my reflections, I discerned that only truly great men know how to love deeply. Felipe embodies this greatness; he is my other half, and our connection grows stronger with time. My joy is profound, and I feel as if I am living in a dream, far removed from the fears my mother instilled in me about marriage.

We spent seven and a half blissful months at Chantepleurs, akin to lovers escaping the world. Yet, I often thought of you and your more pragmatic marriage. Our experiences feel worlds apart; I am elevated by love, while you navigate the earthly realm of duty.

Now, we are in Paris, enjoying a lovely home in the Rue du Bac. I feel more beautiful and content than ever, relishing the music of Rossini that once soothed my restless spirit.

Renee, my happiness compels me to reach out to you. I see you as noble and admirable, and I wish to understand your heart. I suspect you hide your own wounds and sufferings. I once thought your marriage was merely an experiment, but I now recognize that true happiness transcends societal expectations.

I long for you to find joy with Louis, who adores you. I hope that love has illuminated your life as it has mine. Remember our youthful vows to share each other's burdens? I now pray for your happiness as fervently as you once prayed for mine.

I have an idea: encourage Louis to pursue a political career. If he becomes a deputy, we can be together in Paris, and perhaps he will gain a title. This could be a new beginning for both of us.
