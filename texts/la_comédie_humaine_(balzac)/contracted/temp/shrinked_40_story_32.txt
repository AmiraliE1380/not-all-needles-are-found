**The Two Brothers**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Monsieur Charles Nodier, a member of the French Academy. This book explores the hidden injustices of domestic life, where divine chance often replaces human justice, reflecting on the decline of paternal authority and the consequences of feminine weakness, urging a return to the moral foundations of marriage and family.

---

**Chapter I**  
In 1792, the townspeople of Issoudun viewed Dr. Rouget as malicious, believing he made his beautiful wife, Madame Rouget, unhappy. Their son, Jean-Jacques, was born first, followed by their daughter Agathe a decade later, whose arrival strained family dynamics. The Rougets were connected to the wealthy Descoings family, who profited from the wool trade. Descoings’ brother sought fortune in Paris but ultimately failed.

Descoings was imprisoned during the Revolution and executed, shocking the town. After his death, Madame Rouget’s health deteriorated due to her husband’s neglect and their son’s indifference. Agathe was sent to Paris to live with her uncle, but her arrival coincided with Descoings’ imprisonment, leaving him unable to care for her.

Fearing she would never see Agathe again, Madame Rouget fell ill. Agathe’s beauty attracted Bridau, a secretary in the ministry, who married her with Dr. Rouget’s approval. After inheriting the Descoings’ wealth, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life following his wife’s death in 1799, leaving Jean-Jacques unprepared for adulthood.

Agathe, resembling her mother, was a devoted wife and mother, but her ignorance of life’s complexities led to misfortunes. Bridau, loyal to Napoleon, worked tirelessly in the ministry until his death in 1808, just before a significant promotion.

After Bridau’s death, Agathe received a pension and scholarships for her sons but remained estranged from her family in Issoudun. Despite warnings from her godmother, Madame Hochon, Agathe did not contest her father’s will, which deprived her of her rightful inheritance. Instead, she and Madame Descoings pooled their resources, living modestly.

Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, maintained her youthful appearance and sought to cheer Agathe, often taking her to the theater. However, she harbored a secret about her grandson, Bixiou, whom she raised in secrecy. Agathe, devoted to her children, declined to remarry, but her lack of authority as a mother led to significant challenges.

Madame Descoings’ gambling addiction worsened their financial situation, as she used household funds to support her lottery habit. As debts mounted, Agathe, unaware of the extent of the problem, continued to trust her aunt. Eventually, Madame Descoings confessed her financial troubles to Agathe, who took drastic measures to pay off the debts, selling possessions and cutting expenses.

This chapter sets the stage for the unfolding drama of familial relationships, financial struggles, and the consequences of choices made within societal expectations and personal ambitions, highlighting the complexities of love, loyalty, and the often-hidden burdens within families.

The rue Mazarin, particularly between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, is one of Paris's dreariest areas, dominated by Cardinal Mazarin's college and library. Here, Madame Bridau, a widow, lives on the third floor of a dimly lit building. Opposite her, the Institute trains aspiring artists, known as "rapins," who undergo rigorous examinations for prestigious prizes.

Madame Bridau's modest apartment reflects her frugal lifestyle. Since her husband's death, she has become increasingly disheveled and consumed by grief, neglecting her appearance and home. With financial struggles looming, she worries about her children, Philippe and Joseph, despite receiving some support from the Emperor.

Nearby, her aunt Madame Descoings also faces financial difficulties but provides assistance to Madame Bridau. The two widows share a close bond, often playing cards with friends from the ministry. Madame Descoings dotes on Philippe and Joseph, while Madame Bridau restricts her spending to save for her children's future.

Philippe, the elder son, resembles his mother and exhibits a bold demeanor, preferring physical activities over academics. In contrast, Joseph is more introverted and artistic, struggling with cleanliness. Despite their differences, both boys are loved by their mother, though she tends to favor Philippe.

Joseph's interest in art is ignited when he sees a student drawing at the Institute. He becomes enamored with the artistic environment and begins visiting sculptor Chaudet's studio, who recognizes his potential. However, Madame Bridau is distressed by her son's aspirations, fearing hardship as an artist.

Despite her objections, Joseph pursues art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Meanwhile, Philippe, inspired by military grandeur, secretly petitions the Emperor to join the army. He is accepted into the Imperial Lyceum and quickly rises through the ranks.

As the political landscape shifts following Napoleon's return from exile, both brothers face challenges. Joseph's scholarship is revoked, and he is determined to make a living as an artist, while Philippe, now a captain, returns home to find his family in financial ruin. Their contrasting paths highlight their differing personalities and the complexities of their mother's affections.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In 1816, Joseph Bridau established an art studio in his attic with support from his mother, Agathe, and financial backing from Madame Descoings. Both widows viewed painting as a respectable profession. While Joseph studied under Gros and Schinner, political unrest in France led to conspiracies and riots. His brother Philippe became involved, was arrested, and later released, but his military career was jeopardized when he was placed back on active duty.

Agathe worried for Philippe's safety as he frequented disreputable cafes, while the Grecian sages advised sending him abroad. Despite their concerns, Philippe joined General Lallemand in the U.S., leading to the failed Champ d’Asile venture. Agathe invested ten thousand francs to support him, but upon his return in 1819, Philippe was changed, hardened by his experiences and struggling with alcoholism.

Eager to help, Agathe bought him new clothes and indulged his drinking, despite her suspicions about his character. Philippe's tales of woe masked his selfishness, which had developed during his time away. Back in Paris, his behavior alarmed Agathe and Madame Descoings, who faced financial struggles. Agathe suggested selling furniture to support him, but Philippe continued gambling and drinking, worsening their situation.

Philippe's social life revolved around old army comrades and gambling, becoming reckless. He formed a bond with Giroudeau, who introduced him to Paris nightlife. Philippe's ambitions shifted towards pursuing Mariette, a young dancer, while still dreaming of political influence. Despite his failures, he sought to leverage his past for personal gain, planning to demand accountability for the funds raised for Champ d’Asile. His mother and Madame Descoings secured him a cashier position at a new newspaper, believing it would help financially. However, Philippe lived extravagantly without supporting them, while Mariette gained fame through his connections.

Bixiou informed Agathe and Madame Descoings of Philippe's infatuation with Mariette, shocking them as they viewed stage women as morally corrupt. Philippe proposed marriage, but she declined, seeking wealthier prospects. As Philippe distanced himself from his family, he indulged in a hedonistic lifestyle, leading to embezzlement from his job.

After Mariette left for London, Philippe returned home, grappling with despair over his financial situation. He confided in Joseph about his suicidal thoughts stemming from his embezzlement. Alarmed, Joseph urged him to reconsider, suggesting repayment could avert disgrace. Philippe fled, leaving his family distressed. Agathe and Madame Descoings were devastated, fearing for his life.

Philippe visited a gambling house, initially winning but ultimately losing everything. He returned home, feigning distress, and Agathe begged him not to harm himself. In the following days, Agathe aged visibly from worry, realizing their dire financial situation. Madame Descoings suggested consulting a lawyer, Desroches, to manage the fallout from Philippe's actions. Desroches devised a plan to protect Philippe’s reputation, framing his theft as a result of misplaced trust.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure, while Joseph focused on his art, gaining recognition. Agathe remained preoccupied with Philippe, oscillating between hope and fear. Desroches assured them Philippe would soon be reinstated in the army, but warned that his gambling habits could jeopardize this opportunity. As Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, Joseph suspected him of stealing, and they resolved to confront him. Despite their efforts, Philippe's charm allowed him to evade accountability, deepening their struggles and leaving Joseph worried about their future.

Despite Philippe's cautious gambling, he occasionally found himself "cleaned out." Desperate for his nightly stake of ten francs, he resorted to stealing from his family, including Agathe and Madame Descoings. Agathe, heartbroken, recognized that his thefts indicated a deeper moral failing. After a troubling dream where Philippe robbed her, she urged him to ask for money instead of stealing. However, within two weeks, Agathe was left with nothing and considered finding work to support herself.

As Agathe's health deteriorated from worry, Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, neglecting his appearance and becoming increasingly debauched. Madame Descoings urged him to buy new clothes, but Philippe dismissed her concerns, claiming he had no money. He had even mortgaged his military pension to pay off gambling debts.

On Christmas Eve, Philippe planned to steal from Madame Descoings, who had saved money for lottery tickets. He broke into her apartment while she was out, found twenty napoleons hidden in her mattress, and left without raising suspicion. With this money, he gambled and experienced a lucky streak, winning substantial amounts at the gaming house. However, his greed led him to ignore his own gambling rules, and he ultimately lost everything.

Meanwhile, Agathe and Madame Descoings awaited Philippe's return for Christmas dinner. When he finally arrived, he was drunk, and tensions escalated as Agathe confronted him about his behavior. Philippe, in a fit of rage, dismissed their concerns, even as Madame Descoings suffered from the shock of losing her savings.

The next day, Madame Descoings learned that her lottery ticket had won three million francs, but she was devastated to discover that Philippe had stolen her money before she could pay for her stake. This revelation caused her to suffer a stroke, leaving her incapacitated. Agathe and Joseph were left to deal with the fallout of Philippe's actions, which had brought ruin upon their family.

As Madame Descoings lay ill, Philippe returned home, oblivious to the chaos he had caused. Agathe confronted him about his theft and the pain inflicted on their family. Philippe, however, remained indifferent, believing he was justified. The confrontation escalated into violence, leading to Philippe being restrained due to his delirium.

In the aftermath, Madame Descoings passed away, leaving Agathe with a meager annuity. Philippe, now estranged from his family, continued his reckless lifestyle, showing no remorse. Agathe, heartbroken and defeated, prayed for her son, realizing that her motherhood had become a source of deep sorrow.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into a modest attic room above her former apartment, where Joseph aimed to make her comfortable after Philippe's departure. Joseph decorated the room and took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hote, costing nearly a hundred francs a month. Concerned about costs, Agathe secured a job as an assistant in a lottery office, earning seven hundred francs a year, which required long hours.

Despite her new routine, Agathe found solace in its stability. She prepared breakfast for Joseph and kept his studio tidy, though she struggled with the artistic discussions around her. Their bond deepened through small acts of care, even as they remained distant regarding art. One day, Agathe expressed concern for Philippe, prompting Joseph to visit him. Philippe, now a cashier for a newspaper, invited Joseph to dinner, but Joseph declined, prioritizing his mother.

As Philippe posed for a portrait, Agathe secretly observed, filled with hope for their family. However, Philippe's behavior raised Joseph's suspicions, particularly regarding his finances. After a lavish dinner, Agathe's joy turned to worry when Philippe's reckless spending became evident. During another sitting, Philippe boasted of his ambitions but was evasive about his finances.

One evening, Philippe stole a valuable painting from Joseph's studio, believing it to be a copy. When Joseph discovered the theft, he was devastated, and Agathe was heartbroken, realizing the extent of Philippe's betrayal. Her despair deepened upon learning of Philippe's deteriorating condition and need for medical care. Despite Giroudeau's pleas for financial assistance, Agathe refused to help Philippe, feeling betrayed and exhausted.

Months later, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, now a shadow of his former self, recently released from a hospital. Overcome with maternal instinct, she gave him money, despite knowing it would likely be squandered. Her health declined as she grappled with the pain of her son's choices.

When news broke of Philippe's involvement in a conspiracy, Agathe fainted, fearing for his life. Joseph sought legal advice from Desroches, who advised them to secure their family's inheritance from Philippe's influence over their wealthy uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget. Agathe wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, seeking help, but learned that Rouget was unlikely to assist. Desroches urged Agathe to travel to Issoudun to protect her family's interests, prompting her to reflect on the changes in Issoudun and her estrangement from her brother.

Issoudun, one of France's oldest cities, has a rich history and significant commerce, highlighted by archaeological finds like a fifth-century basilica on a Roman temple site. Its name, derived from "Dun," suggests Celtic origins and a long-standing settlement. Historically a wool trade center, Issoudun had a population of around 16,000 in the fourteenth century, but this has declined to about 12,000 today, including vine-growers from surrounding areas.

This decline is largely due to residents' resistance to change, particularly in viticulture, where outdated practices hinder quality wine production. The local bourgeoisie, clinging to medieval traditions, resisted military presence that could have revitalized the economy, reflecting broader regional trends seen in towns like Bourges.

Issoudun's topography features a canal and fertile meadows, but the social fabric has deteriorated, lacking the class distinctions that once enriched community life. The absence of nobility and military presence has led to a homogenous society, with a local government of familiar faces becoming ineffective, further entrenching the town's inertia.

In the early 19th century, young men in Issoudun formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet, a charismatic figure with a tumultuous past. The group engaged in mischievous pranks that terrorized the town, with Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, endearing himself to the common folk despite bourgeois contempt. Under Max's leadership, the Knights executed elaborate pranks, often involving townspeople in humorous chaos, leading to the resignation of the sub-prefect due to relentless harassment. This chapter illustrates the interplay between social stagnation, youthful rebellion, and the decline of a once-thriving community, highlighting how the past influences Issoudun's present dynamics.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orleans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank on Pere Fario by hoisting his cart onto an embankment, returning to Mere Cognette’s for food and laughter, eager to see Fario's reaction.

The Knights enjoyed leisure activities, with Max particularly admired by Madame Hochon's grandsons, Francois and Baruch, who relied on him for financial support. During a gathering, Baruch warned Max about the arrival of Madame Bridau and her son, which could threaten Max's interests in the property of Jean-Jacques Rouget, an old bachelor connected to Flore Brazier, Rouget's servant-mistress. Although Max initially dismissed the concern, he felt uneasy, knowing Rouget's wealth was tied to Flore, and the legitimate heirs could jeopardize his plans.

Francois speculated that if Rouget revoked his will in favor of Flore, it could impact Max's future. However, Max expressed confidence in his relationship with Flore and proposed that the Knights assist him in dealing with the Bridaus, clarifying he did not intend to harm them but wanted to secure his position. The Knights pledged their loyalty, toasting to Flore and dismissing the Bridaus, reflecting their relaxed morals. Max aimed to cultivate their loyalty for future support in reclaiming his status.

As the night ended, Max contemplated the implications of the Bridaus' arrival. He realized Flore had not informed him about her family, which could threaten their financial future. His affection for Flore was intertwined with her potential inheritance from Rouget, prompting him to act before the Bridaus could secure their claim.

The narrative shifts to Flore's backstory. Discovered by Doctor Rouget as a child, she was taken in due to her beauty and innocence, receiving a better life than she would have had with her uncle. Under Rouget's care, Flore enjoyed education and luxuries that distinguished her from other girls in Issoudun. Despite gossip surrounding their relationship, Flore thrived, though townspeople misinterpreted Rouget's intentions. Her life changed when Rouget, nearing death, refused to leave her any inheritance, believing her beauty would ensure her future wealth.

Jean-Jacques Rouget, at thirty-seven, was timid and socially awkward, particularly around women. He did not mourn his father as Flore did, having gained materially from him. His father had hoped to marry him off but hesitated, fearing a wife's influence. Instead, he instilled avarice in Jean-Jacques, who secretly loved Flore, the only woman in his life.

Flore began to see Jean-Jacques differently after noticing his silence. Their relationship evolved, culminating in a conversation where he expressed his desire for her to stay. Intrigued by his affection, Flore found him less unattractive. As time passed, Flore took charge of the household, surpassing Jean-Jacques in management and influence. However, her beauty attracted attention, and she eventually fell for Maxence Gilet, straining her relationship with Jean-Jacques.

Flore's passion for Max led to tension, as she expressed frustrations with Jean-Jacques's perceived stinginess. Despite his reluctance, Jean-Jacques agreed to let Max move in, hoping to maintain her affection. Once Max moved in, dynamics shifted again, complicating the interplay of love, power, and dependency among the three.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to safeguard her inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had lapsed into a semi-vegetative state, motivated solely by his affection for Flore. One morning, Flore found Max asleep after a late night and learned that Rouget's family was coming to claim their inheritance. Concerned, Max advised Flore to act as if she hadn’t heard the news.

A commotion erupted when Fario reported his cart missing. Max feigned indifference but gathered a crowd, humorously suggesting the cart had magically ascended the tower, leading to laughter at Fario's expense. Eventually, Max and his friends destroyed the cart, further complicating his situation with Flore.

As tensions rose, Francois informed Max of the Bridaus' imminent arrival. Max opted for manipulation rather than open opposition. Meanwhile, Flore, upset by the news, took her frustration out on Rouget, withholding affection. Flore's harsh treatment stemmed from her fear of losing her position. Max advised Rouget to welcome his family to avoid scandal, emphasizing the importance of appearances.

After breakfast, Max and Flore discussed financial strategies to secure Rouget's wealth, plotting to ensure the Bridaus would not interfere with their plans. Meanwhile, the Hochon household prepared for the Bridaus' arrival, with Madame Hochon urging her husband to treat the visitors kindly, while Hochon remained skeptical about their intentions. As the day progressed, news of the Bridaus' arrival divided the town, escalating tensions over Rouget's inheritance and shifting power dynamics within the community.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun, where Agathe felt nostalgic. Madame Hochon welcomed her warmly, having developed a maternal affection for Agathe. The family gathered in the salon, scrutinizing Joseph, whose appearance led to unflattering comments about his health and character.

Joseph noted the stark contrast between his artistic life in Paris and the austere provincial lifestyle. Dinner revealed the Hochons' frugality, which disheartened him, though Madame Hochon expressed her hopes for his success. The evening was filled with gossip about local elites, particularly Maxence Gilet and the influence of the Rabouilleuse over Joseph's uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget.

After dinner, the Hochons and the Bridau family discussed reclaiming the Rouget property from Max and Flore. Joseph expressed skepticism about their chances against such formidable opposition. The conversation shifted to the upcoming visit to Rouget, where Joseph was encouraged to make a good impression.

The next morning, Joseph saw his uncle, now unrecognizable under Flore's influence. Agathe was distressed, while Joseph noted Flore's beauty despite her role in their family's troubles. The Hochons advised Joseph to visit Rouget alone to discuss family matters without Flore's interference.

A letter from Rouget invited Joseph to dinner, raising suspicions among the Hochons about Flore's manipulations. They crafted a response emphasizing Agathe's struggles and Joseph's artistic aspirations to secure a favorable position in the family dynamics.

As dinner preparations unfolded, Gritte, the servant, reported on Flore's extravagant display. The Hochons recognized the need for caution, understanding the complexities of the situation. Joseph was urged to remain observant during his visit, as the stakes were high.

At four o’clock, Joseph arrived at Rouget's house, meeting Kouski, Flore, and Gilet. Joseph admired Flore, which pleased Rouget. Flore suggested a walk to see the tower of Issoudun, and Joseph eagerly agreed. He explored Rouget's home, discovering significant paintings. Flore encouraged Rouget to pay Joseph four thousand francs for copies, much to his surprise.

News of Joseph's acquisition spread quickly, enhancing Flore's status. Dinner was lavish, attended by local dignitaries, and Joseph left feeling satisfied. Meanwhile, Rouget indulged heavily in wine.

Max and Flore strategized to keep the Bridaus at bay while benefiting from Rouget's wealth. The next day, Joseph found the paintings had been moved, and Agathe visited Rouget to express gratitude for Flore's care. Old Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate a relationship with Flore, but she struggled to speak privately with her brother.

As the Bridaus struggled for Rouget's favor, Joseph received letters from painter Schinner and lawyer Desroches. Frustrated, he decided to return to Paris, bidding farewell to Rouget, who seemed oblivious to the value of his paintings. Insulted by Flore and Max's manipulation, Joseph returned the paintings, asserting his independence.

The incident became gossip in Issoudun, with exaggerated tales about Joseph's departure. Old Hochon warned Agathe that Joseph's actions might jeopardize their inheritance chances, leaving the Bridaus' future uncertain amid growing tension with Maxence and Flore.

################################################
Between midnight and one o’clock, the Knights of Idleness fed the town's dogs, wrapping up their activities around three in the morning. Max was attacked by Fario, who stabbed him before escaping. Max's cries brought Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet to his side, but the townspeople, used to false alarms, ignored the commotion. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario, prompting Lousteau-Prangin to inform his father, the judge.

Max was taken home, where Goddet assessed the knife wound as serious but not fatal. Seizing the opportunity, Max plotted to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter, for the attack. He instructed Flore to spread rumors of his impending death, hoping this would lead to Joseph's arrest and force his mother to flee to Paris.

Flore, pretending to be distraught, accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit from his uncle. The townspeople, stirred by her claims, began searching for Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn after a restless night. Alarmed by the crowd's anger, Hochon bolted his door, fearing for his gold. Meanwhile, Agathe, Joseph's mother, fainted upon hearing the news.

As the crowd grew, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, who were convinced of his guilt. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety. He recounted his whereabouts that morning, providing an alibi that could be verified by witnesses.

Initially skeptical, the magistrates agreed to investigate further but decided to detain Joseph for protection. He wrote a reassuring letter to his mother, calming her fears. Meanwhile, Max, recovering from his wound, plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, believing it would rid them of the Parisians.

As the investigation progressed, Max's deception unraveled. Witnesses confirmed Joseph's alibi, and Max's own letter to the judge, detailing the assailant's shoes, further implicated him. Eventually, Joseph was released, warmly greeted by his relieved mother.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph's spirits lifted despite his brother Philippe's ongoing trial for actions during the political upheaval. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and advising caution with Max, who was clever and dangerous. Ultimately, Philippe was released and prepared to confront Max and reclaim his family's legacy.

On November 2nd, All-Souls’ Day, Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun, registering with the local police and lodging on rue l’Avenier. His return as a banished officer linked to a military conspiracy stirred excitement, especially since he was the brother of a wrongfully accused painter. Maxence Gilet, now recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, raising concerns in town. Monsieur Hochon sought information from notary Monsieur Heron about Gilet's actions, worried for the Bridau family.

Gilet and Flore Brazier felt secure with Philippe's arrival, knowing they could manipulate old Rouget for his wealth. Philippe visited his uncle in worn clothing, reflecting his hardships, yet he maintained dignity, refusing financial help from Rouget due to past grievances. Flore, sensing Philippe's hostility, advised Rouget to avoid him.

Philippe later visited the Hochons, introducing himself and expressing his intent to live modestly on his limited income. He engaged in polite conversation, winning over Madame Hochon by discussing her nephew, journalist Lousteau. Aware of the town's gossip, Philippe suggested a private discussion with Hochon to avoid suspicion, aiming to connect with other officers of the old army who opposed Gilet.

Philippe soon acquired a new wardrobe on credit and befriended Mignonnet and Carpentier, fellow officers disapproving of Gilet. He shared insights about the failed conspiracy to overthrow the Bourbons, earning respect among the officers while plotting to undermine Gilet and secure his uncle's fortune.

As Philippe ingratiated himself with the local elite, he maintained a facade of respectability, contrasting with Gilet's dubious reputation. He frequently dined with Mignonnet and Carpentier, solidifying his community position. Meanwhile, Gilet viewed Philippe as a rival.

Philippe discovered that Gilet's associates, including Rouget's grandsons, were involved in dubious activities, which he reported to Hochon. This revelation heightened tensions, as Gilet and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth. Philippe confronted his uncle about Flore's influence, insisting on a private discussion.

After a tense exchange, Philippe met Hochon, who informed him that Rouget had not signed any power of attorney, preventing Gilet from accessing his funds. Philippe planned to monitor his uncle and Gilet closely, determined to protect his family's interests. Alarmed by Philippe's boldness, Flore relayed the details to Gilet, who proposed a risky plan to manipulate Rouget into signing a power of attorney.

As Philippe walked with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he contemplated the impending clash between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet, both vying for Rouget's affections and fortune. Philippe expressed disdain for Gilet, revealing his plan to eliminate him to ensure Rouget's happiness with Flore. Despite Rouget's infatuation with Flore, Philippe urged him not to sign a power of attorney that would grant Gilet access to his wealth.

Rouget, easily swayed by Flore's charms, proved difficult for Philippe to convince. Philippe insisted he would deal with Gilet if necessary, promising to make Flore obedient to Rouget. Their conversation shifted when they encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, who were aware of Rouget's precarious situation. Philippe concluded that either Gilet must die or he would have to kill Gilet himself to save his uncle.

Meanwhile, Gilet recognized the growing animosity against him in Issoudun due to his reckless behavior. He met with Captain Potel, who urged him to confront Bridau to restore his honor. However, Gilet was preoccupied with securing Rouget's wealth for himself and Flore.

At Rouget's home, Kouski, under Gilet's orders, feigned sorrow to inform Rouget that Flore had left with Vedie. This devastated Rouget, who searched for Flore in vain. Gilet attempted to maintain his reputation while plotting to keep Rouget under his control.

As rumors of Gilet's misdeeds spread, the townspeople began to turn against him. Potel expressed dismay over the division within the Imperial Guard, now pitted against itself due to Gilet's actions. Feeling cornered, Gilet resolved to confront Bridau, believing he could outmaneuver him in a duel.

Philippe, anticipating Gilet's next move, prepared to counteract his plans, suspecting Gilet would further manipulate Flore and Rouget. When Rouget returned home, he began drafting a letter to Flore, threatening to revoke his will if she did not return immediately.

Seeing an opportunity, Philippe convinced Rouget to send the letter with Carpentier for personal delivery to Flore, believing this would bring her back and restore Rouget's authority.

As evening approached, Philippe strategized to ensure Flore's compliance and Rouget's happiness. He planned to take Gilet's place in Rouget's life, promising to keep Flore in line. When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting his dominance and warning her of the consequences if she did not comply with Rouget's needs.

The next day, gossip about the impending duel between Philippe and Gilet spread throughout Issoudun, with many speculating on the outcome. Flore, realizing the stakes, began to act more affectionately towards Rouget to secure her position. However, Gilet remained defiant, unwilling to back down from the challenge posed by Philippe. As tensions escalated, the townspeople rallied around Philippe, hoping for a resolution that would restore order and honor to the Imperial Guard, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation that would determine their fates.

The day after the coronation banquet, officers of the old army met at Lacroix's restaurant in Issoudun to discuss Max Gilet's dismissal from old Rouget's house. Tensions escalated when Max arrived, receiving a colder reception than Colonel Philippe Bridau. During the banquet, Philippe and Max exchanged barbs, aware of their duel scheduled for the next morning. Philippe was visited by Monsieur and Madame Hochon, who urged him to pray and offered a relic for protection, which he accepted despite skepticism.

On the duel day, both men appeared calm. Philippe quickly disarmed Max, but a desperate counterattack resulted in serious injuries for both. Ultimately, Philippe delivered a fatal blow, ending Max's ambitions and the influence of the Order of Idleness in Issoudun.

As Philippe recovered, he wrote letters about his plans regarding Flore Brazier, Max's former lover, aiming to secure his inheritance and manipulate her circumstances after she fell ill following Max's death. Philippe planned to marry Flore, believing it would solidify his position. He encouraged his mother, Agathe, to support this union, framing it as a means to restore Flore's respectability. Eventually, Flore agreed to marry Rouget, influenced by Philippe's manipulations, and the marriage contract was signed, securing her a substantial dowry.

Philippe returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, facilitating the transfer of Rouget's income to himself. He indulged them in a lavish lifestyle, leading to Rouget's demise after a night out, attributed to indigestion. Philippe's aunt was monitored to prevent excessive indulgence.

In October 1823, Philippe returned to Issoudun to settle his uncle's estate, netting 1.6 million francs, which he invested. By March 1824, he married Madame Rouget, securing her fortune. With Charles X's accession, Philippe gained favor, becoming Comte de Brambourg and seeking to marry into high society.

By January 1827, Philippe had risen to the Royal Guard and sought further honors, distancing himself from his past. Meanwhile, Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, overshadowed by Philippe's success. After Agathe's death, Joseph, heartbroken, refrained from mentioning Philippe during her illness. He later learned of Philippe's indifference and Flore's suffering, seeking to help her but ultimately finding she succumbed to her hardships. Philippe faced societal scrutiny and financial ruin during the July Revolution of 1830, returning to military service, where he met a tragic end in battle in 1839. Joseph, having married well, inherited Philippe's estate and continued to paint, gaining recognition but never achieving the fame he desired.
