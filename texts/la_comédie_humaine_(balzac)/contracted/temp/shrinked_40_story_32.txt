PREPARER’S NOTE

Two Poets is part one of a trilogy and begins the story of Lucien, his sister Eve, and his friend David in the provincial town of Angoulême. Part two, A Distinguished Provincial at Paris, follows Lucien in Paris. Part three, Eve and David, returns to Angoulême. Often parts one and three are combined under the title Lost Illusions, while A Distinguished Provincial at Paris appears separately. Lucien’s story continues later in Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life.

DEDICATION

To Monsieur Victor Hugo,

It was your birthright to be, like a Rafael or a Pitt, a great poet at an age when others are children; it was your fate, as with Chateaubriand and every man of genius, to struggle against jealousy lurking in newspapers and the subterranean places of journalism. For this reason I desired that your victorious name should help to win a victory for this work, which some say is an act of courage as well as a veracious history. If there had been journalists in Molière’s time, who can doubt they would have been within the province of the playwright? Why should Comedy, qui castigat ridendo mores, exempt one power when the Parisian press spares none? I am happy to subscribe myself your sincere admirer and friend,

DE BALZAC.

TWO POETS

When this story opens, the Stanhope press and the modern ink roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. Even in Angoulême, connected to paper-mills and Parisian typography, the machinery was primitive: wooden presses, leather ink-balls, and a heavy movable marble “impression-stone.” This obsolete equipment, to which Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard clung with near-superstitious affection, figures in this chronicle.

Séchard had been a journeyman pressman, nicknamed a “bear” in compositors’ slang—so called for the slow pacing of the pressman from ink-table to press. “Bears” called compositors “monkeys” for their nimble fingers. In the disastrous year 1793, Séchard, fifty and married, escaped the great Requisition that swept most workmen into the army. He was the only hand left in the printing-house; when the master died, leaving a widow but no children, the business seemed doomed because the solitary “bear” could not become a “monkey” and, as a pressman, could neither read nor write. A Representative of the People, hurried to publish Convention decrees, bestowed a master printer’s license on Séchard and requisitioned the establishment. Jérôme-Nicolas accepted the dangerous patent, bought the business from the widow with his wife’s savings, and took over the plant at half value—but he still had to print the Republic’s decrees without mistakes.

At this crisis Séchard was fortunate to find a noble Marseillais unwilling to emigrate or risk his head, who agreed to work disguised in a provincial printer’s jacket. M. le Comte de Maucombe set up, read, and corrected the decrees that forbade citizens to harbor aristocrats; the “bear” printed and posted them, and both remained safe.

In 1795, when the Terror had passed, Séchard needed a new jack-of-all-trades to be compositor, reader, and foreman; an Abbé who declined the oath succeeded the Count after the First Consul restored public worship. The Abbé later became a Bishop at the Restoration; in time the Count and the Abbé met again on the same bench in the House of Peers.

By 1795 Jérôme-Nicolas had not learned to read or write; by 1802 he still had not. Yet by allowing a large margin for “wear and tear” in his estimates, he managed to pay a foreman’s wages. Once an easy-going journeyman, he became a terror to his “bears” and “monkeys.” Where poverty ceases, avarice begins. On glimpsing the possibility of making a fortune, covetousness sharpened a practical faculty for business—greedy, suspicious, and keen-eyed. He scorned theory; he could estimate at a glance the cost of printing per page or sheet for every kind of type. He proved to unlettered customers that large type costs more to move; if small type was discussed, he argued it was more difficult to handle. Setting up type was the one part of the craft he did not know; afraid of undercharging, he always made a heavy profit. He never took his eyes off compositors paid by the hour. If a paper manufacturer was in trouble, he bought up stock cheap and warehoused it. So he became his own landlord and owned the old house that had been a printing office for ages.

He had every sort of luck. Left a widower with one son, he sent the boy to grammar school not so much for the child’s sake as to train a successor; and Séchard treated the lad harshly to prolong parental rule, making him work at case on holidays and telling him he must learn to earn his living to repay his father’s toil.

The Abbé left and Séchard promoted one of his four compositors to foreman, chosen on the future bishop’s recommendation as an honest, intelligent man. These measures, Séchard thought, would tide him over until his son could take over what he expected to be a flourishing business.

David Séchard’s school career was brilliant. Old Séchard, as a self-made man without education, entertained considerable contempt for book learning; yet when he sent his son to Paris to study higher typography, he urged him to save money in the “working man’s paradise” and made clear he was not to draw on the paternal purse. David learned his trade and completed his education without costing his father a farthing; Didot’s foreman became a scholar. At the end of 1819 he was summoned home to take the helm.

Until then Nicolas Séchard’s establishment had enjoyed a monopoly of all regional official printing plus the prefecture and diocese—connections that should have been profitable. But at that moment the firm of Cointet Brothers, paper manufacturers, applied for the second printer’s license in Angoulême. Séchard had kept the license dormant during war-induced commercial atrophy but neglected to buy it outright; this parsimony threatened ruin. Séchard rejoiced when he heard the coming struggle would be fought by his son and not by himself—“a young fellow from the Didots will pull through,” he thought.

The septuagenarian sighed for an easy life. Though his knowledge of higher typography was slight, he was master of “tipple-ography,” an art in which he indulged. Bound by the laws of etymology to be a dry subject, he suffered from an inextinguishable thirst. The habit altered his appearance: a large nose, veined cheeks like vine-leaves, and bloated purple patches gave his face a truffled look. His small gray eyes, under thick brows, glimmered with the cunning of avarice that had supplanted fatherly instinct. Those eyes retained their craftiness even in drink. Séchard resembled one of La Fontaine’s friars with a fringe of grizzled hair about a bald pate—short, corpulent, like an old-fashioned lamp burning much oil for a small wick; excess confirmed his body’s condition.

For thirty years he had worn the municipal three-cornered hat still seen on provincial towncriers. His breeches and waistcoat were greenish velveteen, with an old brown greatcoat, gray cotton stockings, and shoes with silver buckles. This costume suited the man so completely it seemed part of his nature. If the old printer had been given to blind greed, it showed plainly in his manner of abdicating.

Knowing his son had learned the business thoroughly at Didot’s, Jérôme-Nicolas had long plotted the bargain he meant to drive with David. All that the father made, the son was to lose, but in business Jérôme-Nicolas knew nothing of father or son. At first he regarded David as his only child; later he saw him as the natural purchaser of the business and therefore an antagonist whose duty it was to get the better of him. The transformation of sentiment into self-seeking, slow and veiled in more educated people, was swift and direct in the old “bear,” who showed the superiority of shrewd tipple-ography over book-learned typography.

David returned home and the old man greeted him with the cordiality that cunning folk assume for business. He fussed over him—giving his arm, cautioning where to set his foot, warming his bed, making supper. After a sumptuous dinner and much potation, Jérôme-Nicolas began, between hiccoughs, with “Now for business.” David begged to postpone; the old “bear” would not. He had dragged the chain fifty years and would not wait another hour; his son would be “gaffer” tomorrow.

A word about the premises: the printing-house had stood since Louis XIV’s reign at the angle of Rue de Beaulieu and Place du Mûrier. The ground floor was a single large room lit by a street casement and a sash window to the yard; a side passage led to the private office. In the provinces, typography excited curiosity, so customers entered through the street glass door and descended three steps because the workshop floor lay below street level. Newcomers, staring at sheets strung across the ceiling, often ran into cases or tie-bars; they watched compositors pick out type from the hundred and fifty-two compartments, read copy, and assemble lines, only to trip over a ream of damp paper or bump against a bench—delighting the boys, “bears,” and “monkeys.” No one passed through the shop without mishap. Two glass-windowed cages extended into the yard for foreman and master. The yard walls were decorated with trellised vines—tempting, given the owner’s reputation. One side held the kitchen, the other the woodshed, and a ramshackle penthouse against the hall housed paper trimming and damping; there forms—masses of set-up type—were washed. Inky streams from there mixed with kitchen waste and ran into the kennel in the street, so peasants thought the Devil was taking a wash inside the establishment.

Above the shop, the house had three rooms on the first floor and two attic rooms. The first room served as dining-room and lobby; it was the same length as the passage below, minus the old wooden staircase, and lit by a narrow casement and a bull’s-eye into the yard. Its chief trait was a cynical simplicity born of money-making greed: bare whitewashed walls, a dirty brick floor never scoured, three rickety chairs, a round table, and a sideboard between bedroom and sitting-room doors. Windows and doors were dingy. Reams of paper or printed matter cluttered the floor; remains of Séchard’s dinners, empty bottles, and plates lay among packages.

The bedroom, lit to the yard by a leaded window, was hung with old-world tapestry typical in provincial houses on Corpus Christi Day. Its furniture was the old Seigneur Rouzeau’s: a vast four-post bed with canopy, valances, and crimson serge quilt; a couple of worm-eaten armchairs, two tapestry-covered walnut chairs, an aged bureau, and a mantel clock. The room remained as Rouzeau had left it.

The sitting-room had been half-modernized by the late Mme. Séchard—powder-blue wainscot, sepia panels, six lyre-backed chairs—yet unfinished when she died; Jérôme-Nicolas, seeing no profit in completing it, left the mantel bare.

With an air both timid and proud, Jérôme-Nicolas brought his son into that room and pointed to a foreman’s valuation of the printing plant. David read the inventory: three wooden presses, implements and accessories, valued at sixteen hundred francs. The old man interrupted, boasting of his presses as superior to the new cast-iron “Stanhopes” that David admired in Paris. He demonstrated their soundness by briskly operating each one, polishing a cheek with his coat like a dealer showing a horse, and declared them worth many more than the foreman’s estimate. He dismissed cast-iron presses as type-mauling, praised M. Vaflard’s heavy metal type as nearly new after long use, and held up packets of unused sorts to vindicate his claim. The inventory, in his view, undervalued every old tool and ornament—presses, chases, paste-pots, ropes across the ceiling—because “old tools are always the best tools” and provincial customers would not pay for fashionable innovations.

David, trained at Didot’s and conscious of modern economy, found the valuation exorbitant—thirty thousand francs including license and goodwill, for premises his father had bought years before for ten thousand in assignats. The father, who equated bargaining with vigilance, pressed him to accept: the license and regular income from announcements and placards were real, he argued, and these provincial tastes would sustain the business. Attached to the valuation was a deed of partnership: Jérôme-Nicolas would let house and premises to the firm for twelve hundred francs a year, reserve a garret room for himself, divide profits equally until David paid the purchase money in full, then leave David as sole proprietor. David worked out the value of the license, goodwill, and paper stock and, thinking the debt possible to clear, accepted the conditions.

Jérôme-Nicolas, at once elated and anxiously speculating about payment, tried to get some cash up front; David produced none. The old man moved his household items to the attic and, on the day David took possession of the three bare rooms of the printing-house, refused to contribute working capital, claiming he had nothing left after buying the place and that David’s education—paid for by his father’s sweat—should now be put to use. David, humiliated by the father’s niggardliness and contempt for books, endured and resolved to work, encouraged by the father’s reminder that Marion, a sturdy country girl, would handle dampening, cutting, cooking, and other indispensable tasks.

Back in his country vineyard at Marsac, the old man’s mood swung from intoxication at the imagined thirty thousand francs to anxieties about its recovery. He paced his vines, counted his imagined coin, and then returned to Angoulême to inspect his presses and peek into the shop where he saw his son and the foreman reading books—mistaken by him for proof-sheets. Avarice made him rueful, but he was convinced his son’s education and honor would bring payment. Yet unease grew into real fear that the Cointet brothers, rival printers who cultivated conservative, clerical patronage, would steal the prefectural and diocesan work and ruin the Séchards.

The Cointet brothers were deliberate in courting the clergy and monarchical customers: fasting on Fridays, frequenting the cathedral, publishing devotional books. They slandered David as a Liberal and immoral because of Jérôme-Nicolas’s reputation, gradually capturing the monopoly of official work. David’s circulation dwindled and rivals started a competing sheet of announcements. The brothers offered to buy the printing-paper and the departmental announcements; when David and his father balked, Jérôme-Nicolas proposed selling the Charente Chronicle for fear of complete ruin. After bargaining, the Cointets paid twenty-two thousand francs for the paper but insisted David pledge never to print a newspaper under penalty of thirty thousand francs in damages. The sale, while tempting immediate cash, was in effect fatal to the Séchard business: it ceded the crucial paper connection that fed much of their trade.

Séchard senior, content to pocket money, still retained the premises and kept the rent at twelve hundred francs. He largely withdrew from town life thereafter, though his arrangement left openings for further maneuvering—he expected to appear in any bankruptcy as a privileged creditor for arrears of rent. The foreman, who had joined the rival concern, knew how much the father’s “generosity” was really worth.

David’s own mistakes contributed to the downfall. Soon after taking over, he met Lucien Chardon, a former school companion now reduced to poverty. Lucien, about twenty-one and trained in scientific habits by a chemist father who had died unfulfilled, came from a once-comfortable family reduced to modest rentes. His mother, having sold the druggist’s shop, worked as a monthly nurse under the name Madame Charlotte; his sister Eve worked for a laundress. Facing destitution, Lucien accepted David’s generous offer of forty francs a month to learn the printer’s reader’s work—a post David did not really need but offered to save Lucien from despair.

The two young men quickly bonded. Both were intelligent, literary, and drawn to natural science. David was moved by Eve’s beauty and by the family’s plight and promised support, convinced Lucien’s genius would yield fortune. Lucien recalled his father’s abandoned ideas: a chemical method to refine sugar and the notion of an American vegetable fibre to make paper à la chinoise, which could cut raw material costs. David seized on the paper-fibre scheme as a potential fortune and treated Lucien as a benefactor, diverting attention and capital to speculative experiments rather than practical improvements in the printing business.

Their intellectual preoccupations left the printing-house neglected. Where a shrewd businessman would have invested in new type and machinery, hunted cheaper Paris orders, and modernized production, master and foreman were content with trickling business: while the Cointets were turning fifteen to twenty thousand francs, Séchard & Son scraped together barely three hundred francs a month—barely enough to pay the foreman, Marion, rent, and taxes, leaving David scarcely twelve hundred francs a year. The rivals, seeing David’s habits, did not crush the business outright but propped it up strategically, sending prospectuses and job-work as a way to keep the trade from falling into stronger hands—like running a sham opposition to deter better competition.

Everything about the establishment spoke of Jérôme-Nicolas’s stinginess. The house had been worn by sun and rain, a sagging, worm-eaten roof, dingy casements, heavy shutters and iron bars; the workshop was lit at either end and dark in the middle, plastered with handbills, hung with cords across the ceiling, and crammed with stacks of paper, old presses, weighting slabs, rows of cases, and two dens where the master and foreman retired. It was the life of two friends who, devoted to thought more than trade, let their business decline while dreaming of scientific discoveries and literary fame.

One day in early May, 1821, David and Lucien stood at the shop window looking into the yard. It was nearly two; a few men went out to dinner; the apprentice had shut the street door and fastened the bell. As if the smells of paper, ink and presses had grown intolerable, David led Lucien into the yard and they sat beneath the trellised vines, keeping the office and door in view. Sunbeams braided the shoots, making an aureole about their heads and throwing their faces—and their characters—into sharp relief.

David’s frame belonged to a fighter: broad shoulders, a well-developed chest, thick black hair and a high-colored, swarthy face. Yet his features, the lines about his mouth, the dimpled chin and steady, intent eyes, revealed a different truth: a thoughtful, strenuous melancholy. He was quick to see distant horizons and the end of winding ways; he could analyze joys still only ideas and then recoil from them. Behind the rough solidity showed flashes of genius smothered by the sense of social annihilation to which low birth and lack of fortune condemn many lofty minds. He loathed the handicraft of printing as ill-suited to his intellectual instincts, and his spirit retained the brooding seriousness of one who feels himself inexorably held down.

Beside him stood Lucien, as graceful as a sculpted Bacchus. His profile was antique, his skin velvety white, blue eyes set in pearly whites, long lashes, pencilled brows; his damp, curling hair shone golden in the sunlight. A heavenly graciousness transfigured his temples; his small raised chin bore a matchless nobility; his lips curved in a sorrowful angel’s smile. His hands were shapely, his figure slender; a feminine impression might suggest disguise—an effect often found in keen-witted men. But beneath the beauty pulsed a restless, ambitious mind: the temperament of a Gascon—rash, brave, adventurous, ready to seize advantage and to excuse means by ends. He understood both good and evil, and this knowledge made him capable of stooping if success required it. For the moment, his baser impulses were tempered by youthful illusions and the enthusiasm of a man in love with glory; he struggled less with others’ baseness than with his own power.

Both judged society by a lofty standard because their station lay so low; unrecognized power avenged itself by viewing the world from on high. Such flights left them more bitter and hopeless. Lucien had read much and compared; David had thought much and inwardly suffered. Despite David’s robust appearance, his mind was melancholic and shy; yet he had Northern persistence—seeing difficulties, vowing to conquer them, and never to yield. His virtue was softened by pity and inexhaustible indulgence. In their friendship David was the worshiper; Lucien ruled him with the assurance of a beloved. David gladly gave way, thinking of himself as the ox to Lucien’s eagle: “The ox for patient labor in the fields, the free life for the bird,” he resolved.

For three years they had shared a destiny bright with promise. Together they devoured recent works—Schiller, Goethe, Byron, Scott, Jean-Paul—and warmed themselves at those fires. They tried their powers in many abortive creations, laying some aside and taking others up with renewed enthusiasm. Comrades in poverty and in love for art and science, they forgot the hard present while laying the foundations of future fame.

“Lucien,” David said, producing a tiny volume he had just received from Paris, “do you know what I have just received?” He read André de Chénier’s Idyll Néere, then Le Malade, the Elegy on a Suicide, another elegy in the classic taste, and the last two Iambes. Lucien, seized with the discovery, cried out that it filled one with despair—a poet rediscovered by a poet. David told him that after Chénier had written these poems he thought he had written nothing worth publishing. Lucien read aloud the fragment L’Aveugle and two Elegies; on encountering the line, “If they know not bliss, is there happiness on earth?” he pressed the book to his lips and, overcome, both men wept—lovers and fellow-worshipers before the same altar.

The vine-stems were changing; they covered the old house’s rifted walls—fluted columns, knots, bas-reliefs and little grotesques—fairy-work in a gloomy yard. Fancy had strewn flowers and crimson gems over the place; Chénier’s Camille became for David an Eve to worship, for Lucien a great lady to whom he paid homage. Poetry had shaken out her starry robe above the workshop where “monkeys” and “bears” were grotesquely busy among types and presses. Time slipped by unnoticed; five o’clock struck, yet neither hunger nor thirst disturbed them. Life had turned to a golden dream; the world’s treasures seemed at their feet. On the horizon lay the thin blue streak toward which Hope points in storm and stress; a siren voice called, “Come—spread your wings. Through that strip of gold or azure lies the way of escape from evil fortune!”

At that moment the low glass door opened and Cérizet, an apprentice David had brought from Paris, emerged with a stranger—an author with a huge package of manuscript. “This, sir, is a monograph I desire to print,” he said to David. “Will you oblige me with an estimate?” David, without looking, replied that they did not take work on such a scale and advised him to see the Messieurs Cointet. Lucien picked up the roll and found a pretty type that might suit it; he invited the man to leave his commission and call again. The author then asked, “Have I the pleasure of addressing M. Lucien Chardon?” and pronounced himself fortunate to meet a young poet destined to greatness—adding that Mme. de Bargeton had sent him.

Lucien flushed at the name and stammered thanks. David observed his friend’s embarrassment and left him talking with the country gentleman—a vain author preparing a monograph on silk-culture to be printed for his agricultural society. When the man had gone, David asked bluntly, “Lucien, are you in love with Mme. de Bargeton?” Lucien answered that he was passionately so. David warned that social prejudices set them as far apart as if she were in Pekin and he in Greenland. Lucien, eyes lowered, replied: “The will of two lovers can rise victorious over all things.”

But then he declared he had perhaps sacrificed his love to David. He had told Mme. de Bargeton that he would never go to her house again unless another—“a man whose gifts are greater than mine”—were made welcome too: David Séchard, his brother and friend. He expected an answer when he went home; if negative, he would never set foot in Mme. de Bargeton’s house again. David, moved beyond his years, brushed away tears and wrung Lucien’s hand as the clock struck six. “Eve must be anxious; good-bye,” Lucien added abruptly, and hurried away. David watched him cross the workshop, exclaiming silently, “Heart of gold!”

Lucien took the long route to L’Houmeau—along the Promenade de Beaulieu, the Rue du Minage, and Saint-Peter’s Gate—partly because Mme. de Bargeton’s house lay on the way. For two months he had taken this detour daily, delighted to pass beneath her windows though she knew nothing of it. Under Beaulieu’s trees he saw how far the suburb lay from the city. Custom had raised barriers harder to surmount than the descent to the river. Youth and ambition could throw a flying-bridge of glory across the gulf between the city and suburb, yet Lucien felt uneasy and feared his lady’s answer like any favorite who dreads a fall. To understand why a visit to Mme. de Bargeton was fraught required a sketch of Angoulême’s topography and manners.

The old city perches on a crag like a sugar-loaf, overlooking the plain where the Charente winds. That crag—an outlying ridge—ends abruptly above the Paris-to-Bordeaux road; its ramparts, gates and ruined fortress recall it as a stronghold. But the rock’s very strength became modern weakness: Angoulême could not spread down to the river; shut in between ramparts and a steep slope, the old town stagnated. Government efforts—erecting a Prefecture, a Naval School, barracks—opened roads, but private enterprise had already extended life below. The suburb of L’Houmeau grew at the crag’s foot and along the riverside, where the river’s fall served paper-mills and industries established centuries before. Carriers, wheelwrights, posthouses, inns and trades that live by road or river clustered there to avoid the ascent. Tanneries, laundries, warehouses and brandy stores lined the quays. Thus L’Houmeau became a busy, prosperous second city, rivaling the upper town as residence of industry and wealth, though it remained an appendage of the city above.

The noblesse and officialdom dwelt on the crag; trade and wealth flowed below. No love was lost between them. The upper town’s houses belonged to old families who lived on incomes, an autochthonous nation tolerant of no strangers; after two centuries a family from elsewhere might be adopted, but newcomers remained newcomers. Prefects and officials could invite these families to dinners, but were sternly kept from their hearths. Jealous and niggardly, marrying only among themselves and ignorant of modern luxury, they distrusted Parisian ambition such as sending a boy to the capital. Their thick-headed Royalism smacked of bigotry and stagnation; yet Angoulême boasted educational repute, and neighbors sent daughters to its schools and convents.

Class sentiment held Angoulême’s two sections apart. The merchant classes below were rich while the nobles were usually poor; each derided the other. Under the Restoration the feud sharpened: the better families, devoted to the Government, grew more exclusive; “a man of L’Houmeau” became little better than a pariah. Thus a druggist’s son in Mme. de Bargeton’s salon was nothing less than a small revolution. The culpability for such mingling of classes spread over many literary and political figures—Lamartine, Victor Hugo, Béranger, Chateaubriand—because Mme. de Bargeton loved art and letters, an eccentric taste much deplored in Angoulême. To understand her influence on Lucien it is necessary, the narrator says, to sketch her earlier history: she was a woman born to shine, left by unlucky circumstances in the shade, whose taste for letters and art decisively shaped Lucien’s career.

M. de Bargeton descended from Mirault, an alderman of Bordeaux ennobled under Louis XIII. His son served in Louis XIV’s household and married wealth; by Louis XV’s time his grandson dropped Mirault and was simply M. de Bargeton, and that branch later squandered the family fortune. Two brothers returned to trade, so the Mirault name lingered among Bordeaux merchants. The Bargeton lands in Angoumois and the town house in Angoulême were entailed and passed to the prodigal’s grandson. The Revolution stripped seignorial rights but left rents of about ten thousand francs a year. Had prudence prevailed earlier, the family might have risen higher; by 1805 the then M. de Bargeton judged himself fortunate to marry Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, daughter of a reduced provincial noble.

The Nègrepelisses were an old southern family—one had been among St. Louis’s hostages—and an elder branch became d’Espard by marriage. M. de Nègrepelisse, a younger son, lived off his wife’s property near Barbezieux, farming shrewdly, distilling brandy, and accumulating small savings. In the remote countryside his daughter Naïs’s taste for music and reading was shaped by circumstance and by the presence of Abbé Niollant, the Abbé Roze’s best pupil, who took refuge at the manor-house of Escarbas during the Revolution and brought his compositions. Hospitably received, he undertook Naïs’s education. Fluent in Italian and German, a musician and man of letters, he taught her languages, counterpoint, and explained masterpieces of French, German, and Italian literature; in enforced seclusion he also taught Latin, Greek, and touched on natural science.

The Abbé’s artistic temperament and independent judgment, admirable in society, were a mixed blessing in private: his pride in his pupil flattered Naïs and encouraged fearless criticism and ready judgment—qualities useful in men but ill-suited to a woman destined for domestic life. Isolated from peers, she acquired a bold, cavalier air, a high opinion of herself, and a robust contempt for ordinary people. Though the Abbé urged modesty and graciousness, without social intercourse to enforce restraint her originality showed in manner and expression.

M. de Nègrepelisse was miserly; he would have sacrificed books to save a bullock and refused to spend beyond his daughter’s allowance even for education. The Abbé died in 1802 before her marriage—he would likely not have approved of it—leaving her father anxious to manage her. Naïs held strong opinions about marriage: she wished to rule rather than obey, preferring the liberty of a consenting lover to the subjection she expected from ordinary provincial husbands.

Fearing an unsuitable match, M. de Nègrepelisse resolved to marry his daughter for his own peace and sought a son-in-law who would be neither meddlesome nor demanding: a gentleman of breeding but limited cleverness, dull enough to let Naïs have her way and able to take her without a dowry. The only available candidate seemed M. de Bargeton: older, much worn by amorous dissipations, regarded as weak intellectually but with enough common sense to manage affairs and enough breeding to avoid social blunders in Angoulême. Naïs was instructed how to govern such a husband and welcomed the prospect: under the guise of conventional marriage she hoped for liberty to cultivate wit, beauty, and Parisian connections. M. de Bargeton saw the match as brilliant, expecting to profit from the Nègrepelisse estates; a dispassionate observer might have judged the elder M. de Nègrepelisse likely to outlive his son-in-law.

At marriage Mme. de Bargeton was thirty-six; her husband fifty-eight and looking much older. She still appeared young and retained a taste for finery. Their income—about twelve thousand francs—placed them among the town’s few great fortunes. They had to remain in Angoulême until Mme. de Nègrepelisse’s inheritance arrived so they could go to Paris. The elder M. de Nègrepelisse kept them waiting so long that the son-in-law predeceased him, and the anticipated Paris life never came. Naïs’s intellectual gifts and inner resources profited her little in provincial isolation; deprived of stimulating society, her strong faculties hardened into affectations. Pride and enthusiasm, when untempered by greater minds, became exaggeration and petty pretension: knowledge stagnated, taste corrupted, and noble instincts were stifled by provincial meanness.

Mme. de Bargeton began to overstate every feeling and to make spectacles of her emotions. She used inflated language and neologisms, indulged in ceaseless admiration, and reacted theatrically to trifles. Her enthusiasm was indiscriminate: she wept and rhapsodized over each sensation, magnified minor events, and exhausted herself in chronic admiration or dislike. She fancied herself heroic and picturesque, envied dramatic lives, and longed for any destiny more striking than her clear, simple existence. She adored Byron and Rousseau and any life that seemed romantic. Many called her a harmless lunatic; a shrewder observer might see her extravagances as fragments of grandeur broken off before fulfillment—love without a lover.

The first eighteen years of her marriage were spent living on herself and distant hopes. Finding the income too narrow for a promised Paris life, she faced days among men who inspired neither passion nor intellectual companionship; no chance encounters relieved her fate until the Empire’s height roused new hopes. At a military ridotto she fell in love with an officer—a sub-lieutenant of good family—who, dreaming of glory, wore a miniature portrait she gave him at Wagram. On that battlefield a shell destroyed the portrait, and the soldier was killed, leaving her with a lasting veil of sadness. This grief deepened the sense of missed youth’s joys and reinforced an inner austerity. Pride saved her from suburban intrigues; she would have perished of degradation had she succumbed to the petty men of her town. She lived by poetry and by foreign essayists that reached France after 1815; they delighted but could not broaden her. Dignity curdled into stilted manner and affectation, and she ruled her circle with foibles that provincial courtiers adored.

Into this stifled scene came Lucien—Baron Sixte du Châtelet—introduced by a newcomer who disturbed Mme. de Bargeton’s monotony. Born Sixte Châtelet, he took the particle after 1806 and belonged to the adaptable young men who skirted conscription by proximity to the Imperial court. He had been private secretary to an Imperial Highness—an office suited to his outward qualifications: handsome, adept at billiards and dance, passable as an amateur actor, ready with a ballad or quatrain, superficial in many arts. He could accompany a singer, improvise verse, sketch, copy landscapes, and color costumes—talents that served well where women held social influence. He claimed diplomacy as his forte; it fitted a man who could say little and keep mystery.

Created a Baron and sent to Cassel as envoy-extraordinary, he was used by Napoleon as a courier in a European crisis; promised a ministerial post in Westphalia, he lost it when the Empire fell and went to Egypt with General de Montriveau. Misadventures followed—separation from his patron, wandering among Arab tribes, even being sold and resold as a captive—before he returned via an English vessel to Paris. His connections recommended him to the President of the Council, who placed him in M. de Barante’s department until a controllership appeared, which brought him to Angoulême.

Baron Sixte arrived with the manners of a man of the world, habitually assuming the air of a man who had suffered travel and romantic experience. Socially on good terms with local authorities, he remained polite and slightly supercilious, like a man awaiting favors. He judged Angoulême’s men worthless and saw Mme. de Bargeton as the principal person to cultivate: she presided over a salon that seldom admitted strangers. He found music the key to her favor, procured a Mass by Miroir, learned it at the piano, and one Sunday played the organ while the town attended cathedral; his performance sent the ignorant into ecstasies and stirred Mme. de Bargeton’s admiration. By that pretext he gained entrée he could not have obtained by direct request.

Thus Châtelet entered the queen of Angoulême’s circle and paid her marked attention. At forty-five, he saw her dormant youth ready to revive and possibilities to exploit—maybe a rich widow, maybe political connections through the Nègrepelisse family, perhaps even marriage. High-born Angoulême gasped: Mme. de Bargeton’s salon was a sacred preserve of gentility, usually only the bishop intimate there; a mere controller of excise in her circle shocked local notions of precedence and propriety. Those who understand petty vanities will see how the bourgeoisie revered the Hôtel de Bargeton: outwardly a miniature Louvre, inwardly it gathered the intellectual poverty and decayed gentility of the countryside, making the house both a spectacle and a prize to be courted.

Political opinion in the provinces came out as loud commonplaces: the Quotidienne was tepidly loyal, Louis XVIII was denounced as a Jacobin, and the women were judged awkward and spiritless. Yet the ruined nobility, with old manners and knowledge of tradition, masked many shortcomings; their antiquated exclusiveness was a form of social weight, a token of status to townsmen. Châtelet could hardly have tolerated this society but for his designs on Mme. de Bargeton. Some women, flattered by M. du Châtelet, saw in him what their men lacked and were soothed; purists thought he might be seen at Mme. de Bargeton’s but nowhere else.

Du Châtelet cultivated the clergy and the queen of Angoulême, bringing books and reading poetry aloud, and tolerated Romantic enthusiasms with a patience unusual in an Imperial man. Mme. de Bargeton welcomed the literary renaissance, rejoiced in the Bourbon restoration, and pined for Paris and its great men. Du Châtelet, seeing his chance, told her that besides Victor Hugo there was “another sublime child” in Angoulême: a brilliant young poet from L’Houmeau named Lucien, whose verses, he claimed, surpassed the Parisian galaxy. The headmaster had shown the Baron some admirable lines; Mme. de Bargeton, starved for genius in her provincial circle, demanded to see the lad, and du Châtelet negotiated his appearance.

For poor provincials the social distances were vast; Lucien’s heart leapt when his headmaster announced that the doors of the Hôtel de Bargeton would open for him. Only his sister Eve knew. Thrifty housekeeper and laundress, she devoted savings to thin shoes, a new suit, and a frilled shirt, and warned him about manners—no elbows on aristocratic tables. She watched him depart and waited until he reached the Promenade where du Châtelet awaited. For Eve, Lucien’s entry into Mme. de Bargeton’s house meant the dawn of success; she did not foresee that where ambition begins ingenuous feeling ends.

The Hôtel was less grand than imagined: mellow country stone, plain rooms, a neat courtyard, and an interior that was sober and almost convent-like. Lucien climbed the old staircase, crossed an antechamber, and entered a wainscoted drawing-room lit by two wax candles where Mme. de Bargeton sat on a sofa. She did not rise but smiled on the new poet; he found her dignified and was enchanted by her exaltation. She wore a medieval coif of slashed black velvet; red-gold curls framed a white throat and an ardent face. Her refined fingers summoned him to sit close; M. du Châtelet ensconced himself nearby, and Lucien realized he was alone with her.

Those three hours passed to Lucien like a dream. He adored her feminine exaltation and energy, and was blind to faded cheeks or paint; his imagination clung to dazzling particulars. He had brought no verses; she did not ask for them, meaning him to come again to read. M. Sixte du Châtelet, perceiving a rival and mortified, tried to assert himself—walking Lucien down to Beaulieu with tales and advice, bluster intended to destroy a rival—but the young poet was barely intimidated.

Lucien returned often and grew used to condescension. When nobles called on Naïs and found him present, they greeted him with the graciousness of the well-bred toward inferiors; Lucien mistook civilities for kindness until their tone stirred his gall and a bitter Republicanism—a stage in many aspirants—began to ferment. Nevertheless, he would endure anything for Naïs.

Naïs prophesied boundless fame for him. She flattered and exalted him, holding him near as reader and secretary, more attached than she expected. Her conduct mixed intimacy with capricious pride: sometimes lofty patroness, sometimes tender. The oscillation of dread and hope tormented Lucien—the torture of first love. After two months of patronage, confidences came: she softened address to “dear,” and he, bolder, called her Naïs; she rebuked him, and he then called her Louise—a privileged intimacy that delighted him.

One evening she hid a portrait and told the sad tale of Cante-Croix’s stainless but cut-short love, provoking his jealousy and despair. When she raised scruples of duty or appearances he could not analyze; too boyish to reason, he vowed to live for her rather than die for her. Louise demanded verses for her album—pretext for quarrel in case of tardiness—but when she read his stanzas she was moved and changed. She praised the verses as evidence of genius and kissed him on the forehead. She spoke of perfecting him—teaching languages, refining manners—reasons to keep him perpetually in her orbit. She resumed music for his sake, played Beethoven to send him into ecstasy, and implied that such happiness might suffice; he answered “Yes.”

Townsfolk buzzed. The week before, she had asked Lucien to dine with the Bargetons as a third, and the town reacted with outrage; some blamed liberal doctrines for moral decay. Du Châtelet, haughty, joked about Lucien’s mother being Madame Charlotte the monthly nurse—an apparent slur on the poet’s origins. Mme. de Chandour remonstrated with Mme. de Bargeton, who met gossip with composure: genius is often born of humble stock; if they had no money, what would they do? She turned misfortune into virtue with wit that disarmed critics.

That evening she used caustic reasoning before a crowded salon, arguing that because nobles could not themselves produce Molières or Voltaires, they must accept that great men might be upholsterers’ or clockmakers’ sons; genius was not the preserve of birth. Her eccentric defense quelled the storm. When Lucien appeared before the whist-tables he was brought forward like a prince she meant to protect, and he faced the cold hostility of those who vowed to expel him with insolence.

Naïs had won a victory but weakened her supremacy. Rumor spread; Amélie de Chandour, following du Châtelet’s counsel, set up a rival salon on Wednesdays. Habit prevailed with the habitués and the mistress of the staircase. The president of the agricultural society saved face by remarking that pre-Revolution nobles admitted men like Dulcos and Grimm—nobodies—but they never received tax-collectors; Châtelet, a tax-collector, remained unacceptable. Du Châtelet, affronted by the slur and envious of Lucien, declared he would possess Mme. de Bargeton; yet in a twist he sided with her and declared himself Lucien’s friend, feting the poet with a grand dinner. He invited the prefect, receiver-general, colonel, and other dignitaries and had Lucien recite “The Dying Sardanapalus.” Applause was plentiful; the headmaster extolled the lines as superior to Jean-Baptiste Rousseau. Sixte du Châtelet watched with the calculating interest of a rake waiting to strike.

From then on vague reports of a great man in Angoumois circulated. Mme. de Bargeton was praised for her patronage, and she sought general sanction by arranging a soirée with ices, tea, and cakes—novelties in a town where tea was mostly medicine. She summoned the flower of aristocracy to hear Lucien read, warned him of the social cabal, and counselled that only the strong could survive. She proposed he abandon his patronymic for the noble name Rubempré, offering to secure royal sanction through her relation, the Marquise d’Espard. The words “King” and “Marquise” dazzled Lucien; baptism into a noble name seemed requisite.

Mme. de Bargeton crossed social strata to show him that such a change would elevate him. She urged Lucien to renounce the equality rhetoric of ’89, fed his taste for social distinction, and tamed David’s commonsense. The once-rabid Liberal turned into a secret Monarchist: he longed for rank, luxury, and fame and swore to win laurels for his lady, even if blood must be spilled. To prove his courage he confessed his present hardships—the shackles of poverty borne with pride, long days of work for David, nights of study. His ardor recalled the colonel of twenty-six; Louise’s eyes softened; Lucien seized a hand and kissed it with the frenzy of lover and poet; she allowed him to press his lips to her forehead.

Mme. de Bargeton’s arguments—men of genius must sacrifice family and law to grow—fell on Lucien’s worst inclinations and spread corruption in his heart. She invoked Palissy, Louis XI, Fox, Napoleon, Columbus, Caesar—praising the right of genius to set itself above law and take what it needed. For Lucien, with desires hot, all means seemed admissible; unconsciously he stood between prophetic promise and shameful ruin.

Louise loosened his provincial bonds so effectively that he resolved on an experiment to secure her love without risk of rebuff. The forthcoming soirée offered a chance. Ambition and love intertwined: society’s banquet early aroused ambition, robbing youth of its original charm and corrupting generous thoughts into mercenary scheming. Lucien persuaded himself his plotting was for David’s sake.

He wrote a long, impassioned letter to Louise—copied thrice—detailing his father’s thwarted genius, his own grinding poverty, praising his sister as an angel and David as a future Cuvier, friend, and father-figure. He implored her to do for David what she had done for him, to lift David into his deserved place. The letter was a blend of boyish casuistry, naive eloquence, and romantic pleading—the sort of unconscious revelation women love.

Lucien left the letter with the housemaid and spent the rest of the day at the office reading proofs, supervising orders, and attending to the printing-house, saying nothing to David. Youth retains its reticence; perhaps he feared David’s clear eyes. Later, when he read Chénier’s poems with David, the secret rose in his heart under the sting of reproach, like an unhealed wound probed.

Try to follow Lucien’s troubled thoughts as he left Angoulême. Had Mme. de Bargeton been offended? Would she receive David? Lucien measured the distance between a queen and her favorite; he knew how far he had climbed in five months, and though David might leap the same ground at once, Lucien did not yet grasp how completely the lower orders were excluded from that upper world. A second imprudence would ruin Mme. de Bargeton: once suspected of consorting with canaille, she would be shunned. Naïs might have broken moral law and been defended, but admitting humble people to her house was an unforgivable sin. The powerful are forgiven their faults; once they abdicate their rank they pay. And what was it but abdication to receive David?

Lucien’s aristocratic instinct found other obstacles. Manner and bearing can be learned—Racine had a noble air, Corneille looked cumbersome—but David lacked the trifles Nature had given Lucien: a graceful figure, refined features, a certain ring to the voice. Lucien, of gentle blood on his mother’s side and with a high-arched foot, imagined Mme. de Bargeton’s repressed smile at David’s appearance. Not ashamed of his friend, he resolved to be prudent and risk no repeat of such experiments.

So, after an hour of poetry and self-sacrifice came one of worldly wisdom and scheming. Back in L’Houmeau he wished he had not written the letter; he would take it back if he could. Down the vista of the future he glimpsed the inexorable law that nothing succeeds like success, and it cost him to step down from the first rung of the ladder he meant to climb. Images of his simple life rose—David generous and self-sacrificing; his mother proud in her lowly lot; his sister submissive and gracious; his own unspoiled conscience—and the past bloomed.

He told himself it was nobler to hew his way through hostile society by repeated strokes than to reach the goal through a woman’s favor. Sooner or later genius would shine; others had tamed society; women would love him when that day came. He flung his schemes to the wind and blamed himself. Lucien was easily moved between extremes.

He had none of the scholar’s taste for retreat; for a month he had felt shame at seeing POSTEL (LATE CHARDON), PHARMACEUTICAL CHEMIST in yellow letters on green over the shop. It offended him that his father’s name should be posted where every carriage passed. Every evening, after closing the ugly iron gate and going up to Beaulieu to give his arm to Mme. de Bargeton among the dandies, he chafed at the disparity between his lodging and his fortune.

“I love Mme. de Bargeton; perhaps soon she will be mine, yet here I live in this rat-hole!” he thought as he descended into the little yard behind the shop. Bundles of boiled herbs lay along the wall, an apprentice scoured a caldron, and M. Postel, in a laboratory apron, inspected some chemical product while watching the door.

A strong scent of camomile and peppermint pervaded the yard and the poor dwelling reached by a short ladder with ropes for handrails. Lucien’s room was an attic under the roof.

“Good-day, sonny,” said Postel, the provincial tradesman. “Are you pretty middling? I have been experimenting on treacle, but it would take a man like your father to find what I am looking for. Ah! he was a famous chemist, he was! If I had only known his gout specific, you and I should be rolling along in our carriage this day.”

Postel, thick-headed but kind, never failed to allude to Chardon senior’s unlucky secrecy—words that stabbed Lucien. He began to find Postel vulgar, though he blessed him for his kindness; honest Postel had helped the widow and children more than once.

“Is there a letter for me?”

“Yes, a letter that smells like balm! It is lying on the corner near my desk.”

Mme. de Bargeton’s letter among physic bottles in a druggist’s shop! Lucien sprang to rescue it.

“Be quick, Lucien! your dinner has been waiting an hour!” a sweet voice called through a half-opened window; but Lucien did not hear.

“That brother of yours has gone crazy, mademoiselle,” said Postel, lifting his face.

The old bachelor, a miniature brandy-cask of a man with a ruddy, pitted face, became ceremonious and amiable at the sight of Eve, showing thoughts of espousing his predecessor’s daughter. “Your sister is uncommonly pretty, and you are not so bad looking neither! Your father did everything well,” he often said.

Eve was tall, dark-haired, blue-eyed; though vigorous she was gentle, tender, and devoted. Her frank innocence, simplicity, and spotless character won David’s heart; since they first met a repressed, single-hearted love had grown between them in the German fashion—quietly, without fervid protestations. In secret they thought of each other as if barred by something jealous; they hid feelings as if it wronged Lucien. David, lacking confidence, could not believe Eve could care for him; Eve, penniless and proud, was shy. Rumors said Marsac was worth more than eighty thousand francs; old Séchard had savings and bought bits of land, but perhaps David alone knew nothing of his father’s means—he cared little for the estate, seeing Marsac only at vintage time when his father boasted.

David was a solitary student; his timid love sought encouragement. He stood in awe of Eve and was awkward, eager to hurry away. He repressed his passion; often he would reach the green iron railings of L’Houmeau and turn back. Eve read the little he showed and was proud of his reverence, but it was his faith in Lucien that drew her most. Their love’s mute delights were like wildflowers: glances like water-flowers, words vanishing like briar scents—tender blossoms of two rare natures. Eve saw strength beneath David’s weakness; a single word might bind them closer.

Eve opened the door, and Lucien sat at the little trestle table. There was no tablecloth; the household owned three silver spoons and forks, and Eve laid them all for her dearly loved brother.

“What have you there?” she asked when she set a dish and put the extinguisher on the portable stove.

Lucien did not answer. Eve garnished a little plate with vine-leaves and set a jug of cream.

“There, Lucien, I have had strawberries for you.”

But Lucien was absorbed in his letter. Eve sat beside him without murmur; in a sister’s love there is pleasure to serve without ceremony.

“Oh! what is it?” she cried as she saw tears in his eyes.

“Nothing, nothing, Eve,” he said, and putting his arm about her drew her close and kissed her forehead and throat with surprising warmth.

“You are keeping something from me.”

“Well, then—she loves me.”

“I knew very well that you kissed me for somebody else,” Eve pouted, flushing.

“We shall all be happy,” cried Lucien, swallowing great spoonfuls of soup.

“We?” echoed Eve, with the presentiment that had crossed David’s mind. “You will not care so much about us now.”

“How can you think that, if you know me?”

Eve clasped his hand, carried off the empty plate and brought the dish she had made. Lucien read his letter again instead of eating; Eve discreetly respected his silence. Here is the letter in essence: Mme. de Bargeton told Lucien she would not refuse David help in science; she believed merit had rights. Yet she warned Lucien of her circle’s prejudices and, fearing to commit herself rashly, attached a condition—she wanted to see his friend and judge him herself before accepting him into her company, as a mother might rightly watch over her protege.

Lucien had no suspicion of the arts by which polite society frames a “Yes” on the way to a “No.” He took the note for victory. David should go to Mme. de Bargeton’s house! David would shine in the majesty of his genius! He raised his head proudly; his face radiated hope, and Eve told him he looked handsome.

“If that woman has any sense, she must love you! And if so, to-night she will be vexed, for all the ladies will try all sorts of coquetries on you. How handsome you will look when you read your Saint John in Patmos! If only I were a mouse, and could just slip in and see it! Come, I have put your clothes out in mother’s room.”

The mother’s room testified to self-respecting poverty: white curtains, a strip of cheap green carpet, a chest of drawers, a looking-glass, a few walnut chairs. A clock on the chimney-piece told of vanished prosperity. Eve’s adjoining closet held a narrow bed, a low chair, and a work-table; the door always stood open for air. Though poor, the atmosphere was sedate and studious.

Lucien was tying his cravat when David’s step sounded in the little yard. The young printer appeared in a hurry.

“Well, David!” cried Lucien, “we have gained the day! She loves me! You shall come too.”

“No,” David said with confusion. “I came to thank you, but I have been thinking seriously. My life is cut out for me, Lucien. I am David Séchard, printer to His Majesty in Angoulême, my name on bills posted on every wall. For people of that class, I am an artisan, a craftsman who lives over a shop in the Rue de Beaulieu at the corner of the Place du Mûrier. I have not the wealth of a Keller nor the name of a Desplein—powers the nobles ignore. How can I prove my claim to this sudden elevation? I should only make myself a laughing-stock for nobles and bourgeoisie alike. As for you, your position is different. A foreman is not committed. You are gaining knowledge indispensable for later; you can explain your present work by your future. You can leave your place tomorrow and begin something else; nobody has pigeon-holed you. Take advantage of your social maiden fame to walk alone and grasp honors. Enjoy all pleasures. I wish you luck; I shall enjoy your success; you will be like a second self. Yes, in my mind I shall live your life. You shall have the holiday life; I will lead the tradesman’s life of sober toil, patient scientific research.

“You shall be our aristocracy,” he continued, looking at Eve. “If you totter, I will steady you. If you suffer treachery, find refuge in our hearts; love there will never change. Influence and favor might fail us if we were two; we should stand in each other’s way; go forward, you can tow me after you if it comes to that. I shall dedicate my life to yours. The thing you did—risked losing your benefactress, your love perhaps, rather than forsake me—that little thing was so great—ah, well, Lucien, that alone would bind me to you forever if we were not brothers already. Have no remorse; be not concerned about seeming to take the larger share. This one-sided bargain is exactly to my taste. Suppose you give me a pang now and again, who knows that I shall not be your debtor all my life?”

He glanced timidly at Eve; her eyes were full of tears; she saw all beneath the surface.

“In fact,” he added, “you are well made, you have a graceful figure, you wear your clothes with an air, you look like a gentleman in that blue coat with yellow buttons and plain nankeen trousers; I should look like a workingman among those people, awkward and out of my element, or say foolish things—or say nothing. But you can take your mother’s name; call yourself Lucien de Rubempré; I am and shall be David Séchard. Everything tells for you there and against me. Women will worship that angel face of yours; won’t they, Eve?”

Lucien sprang up and flung his arms about David. David’s humility had dispelled many doubts. It was one of those rare moments when forces in us are sweetly strung and every chord gives full resonance.

Yet this noble goodness increased Lucien’s human tendency to take himself as center. He had all the faults of a spoiled eldest son: his mother and sister concentrated tenderness on him, David was devoted; Mme. de Bargeton was doing her best to foster the same tendency by inducing him to forget his family and friend. He was far from doing so yet; but there was reason to fear that as his sphere widened his thought would be how to maintain himself in it.

When emotion subsided, David suggested Lucien’s Saint John in Patmos might be too biblical for such an audience. For his first appearance before the exacting Charente public, Lucien seemed nervous. David advised taking André de Chénier and substituting certain pleasure for dubious delight. Lucien was a perfect reader; listeners would enjoy him, and his modesty would serve him. Like most young people, they endowed the world with their own intelligence and virtues; youth not yet gone astray is pitiless for others’ sins and magnanimous in faith.

The two lovers were left alone. David, embarrassed and timid, half wished, half feared Eve’s praise; he longed to run away, for modesty is not exempt from coquetry. He was afraid to utter a word that might seem to beg for thanks; everything put him in a false position, so he held his tongue and looked guilty. Eve guessed his agony and enjoyed the pause; when David wrung his hat as if to go, she smiled.

“Monsieur David,” she said, “if you are not going to Mme. de Bargeton’s, we can spend the time together. It is fine; shall we take a walk along the Charente? We will have a talk about Lucien.”

David longed to fling himself at her feet. Eve’s tone solved his difficulties: her suggestion was better than praise; it was the first grace offered by love.

“But give me time to dress!” she said as David made to go.

David went out; he, who had never known one tune from another, hummed. Honest Postel heard him and suspected Eve’s feelings for the printer.

Small things that evening made a great impression on Lucien; his character was peculiarly susceptible to first impressions. Like all inexperienced lovers, he arrived so early that Louise was not in the drawing-room; M. de Bargeton was there alone. Lucien had begun to practice the small deceits lovers pay for married happiness—deceits through which a woman learns her power—but he had not met the lady’s husband.

M. de Bargeton’s intellect was limited, poised between harmless vacancy and excessive stupidity. To be agreeable he adopted an opera-dancer’s smile as his sole expression. Satisfied, he smiled; dissatisfied, he smiled again. He smiled at good and evil alike; when obliged to express approval a complacent laugh reinforced the smile; otherwise he hardly spoke. A tête-à-tête embarrassed him, and he rescued himself by returning to the artless ways of childhood—thinking aloud about the smallest personal sensations. He never talked weather or commonplaces; he plunged you into intimate trivialities—“I took veal this morning to please Mme. de Bargeton, and my stomach has been uneasy since.” Such remarks permitted no discussion; a “Yes” or “No” and conversation fell dead. Then he implored help with a gaze that said, “You were saying?” He loved bores who talked about themselves and listened with delicate interest, so twaddlers credited him with more understanding than he showed. When others could find nobody to listen they went to him, sure of his eulogistic smile. Mme. de Bargeton’s rooms were always crowded, and generally her husband felt at ease. He watched arrivals, helped his wife bring guests, laid in wait for departures to take them to the door with that eternal smile. When conversation grew lively, he stood happy and mute, planted like a swan, listening to political talk or watching card-players without notion of the game; or he walked about taking snuff to promote digestion. Anaïs was the bright side of his life; she made it unspeakably pleasant. Stretched in his armchair he watched admiringly while she played hostess. He tried to see the point in her remarks, and often succeeded only after delay; his smiles appeared like a delayed explosion. His respect for his wife almost amounted to adoration. She had drilled him into military subordination; he yielded passive obedience. “Go and call on Monsieur So-and-So,” she would say, and he went forthwith, like a soldier at command. He stood at attention in her presence and waited motionless for orders.

There was talk of naming the mute gentleman as a deputy. Lucien, who had not been long at the house, had scarcely begun to fathom M. de Bargeton. The old man, stretched in his great chair, seemed to see and understand everything; his silence gave him dignity, and Lucien, imaginative and uneasy, regarded him more as a sphinx than a host and wished to conciliate him.

“I am the first comer,” Lucien said with extra respect.

“That is natural enough,” M. de Bargeton replied in a tone Lucien took for an epigram and which made him imagine a jealous husband. When Lucien asked why people who live far off come earlier than those near by, Bargeton only returned to his immobility, snatching at a joke about “final causes” with an amused “Eh! eh!...” The conversation died; Lucien fumbled for topics—Mme. de Bargeton, the chandelier, his boots—trying to please the inscrutable man with small talk about a Japanese jar and the fact that Bargeton seldom left the city. The old man watched him like a suspicious cat, and each was afraid of the other.

Relief came when the old man-servant announced “M. du Châtelet.” Baron Sixte du Châtelet entered with the ease of an inbred beau, dressed in dazzling white trousers, pumps, an eyeglass, and the air of faded elegance. He inspected Lucien coolly, his look saying, “I am satisfied,” and by his presence Lucien felt his own poverty eclipsed by the weight of fortune. M. de Bargeton, sparing of speech, kept a question in reserve and asked Châtelet if there was any news. Châtelet suggested M. Chardon—and, with malicious adjustment of his curl, invited Lucien to produce “some charming poet.” Lucien answered with deference, admitting Châtelet had preceded him in verse. The Baron dismissed his own work—“a few vaudevilles” and an “Epistle to a Sister of Bonaparte”—with affected indifference.

Mme. de Bargeton appeared in full toilette: a theatrical Jewess’ turban, oriental clasp, cameos at her shoulders and bracelets. M. du Châtelet lavished fulsome compliments; she received them with gratification and only a civil nod for Lucien.

Guests began to arrive. First the Bishop and his Vicar-General—one tall and attenuated, the other short and fat—grave and impassive, their silence taken for great intellect. Next came Mme. de Chandour and her husband Stanislas, a ci-devant slim man of forty-five who posed as a dandy: cravat points, awry coat-tails, starched shirt, massive studs—an eighteenth-century caricature whose grossness charmed women and provoked challenge from Châtelet. Mme. de Chandour, short and plump, loud-voiced and restless, talked without depth and wheezed with asthma.

M. de Saintot, Astolphe, President of the Agricultural Society, arrived with his wife Élisa—Lili—solemn, pious, and card-playing at heart. Astolphe posed as a learned man: he compiled agricultural articles by pilfering others’ work, locked himself away to appear industrious, and in conversation dredged Cicero for apt quotations. His reputation rested on rumor and theatrical management of appearances.

M. de Bartas, Adrien, boomed arrival with his bass voice and musical self-conceit. Obsessed with solfeggi, he longed to sing and, when praised, strutted from group to group for more compliments. M. Alexandre de Brébian produced heroic sepia sketches and together with de Bartas they arrived with wives in a cross-cornered arrangement that gossip exaggerated into impropriety. Their wives, Mesdames Charlotte de Brébian and Joséphine de Bartas—Lolotte and Fifine—were obsessed with fashion and Parisian airs, neglecting domestic matters. The husbands, dressed with an artist’s liberty, seemed provincial beside their wives’ doll-like finery.

One curious pair were the Comte and Comtesse de Senonches: Jacques, a mighty hunter, lean, sunburned, jealous and suspicious; his wife Zéphirine, tall and fine but with a sallow complexion and affected languor masking passion. Francis du Hautoy, a friend of the house, had forsaken a consulship to be near her, managing the household and finances with devoted attention. The three formed a conjugal trinity so intimate that Du Hautoy’s role as a quasi-servant, confidant, and companion seemed indispensable; his mincing affectations—fussing over coughs and waistcoats—made him a pampered idol attended to by Zéphirine. Rumor fancied a closer tie between Françoise de la Haye, Zéphirine’s companion, and Du Hautoy, suggesting delicate mysteries despite chronological discrepancies.

The Marquis de Pimentel, the largest landowner, arrived with his wife and, in their calèche, neighbors the Baron and Baroness de Rastignac, the Baroness’ aunt, and two charming, penniless daughters. These families, near the Court and immune to provincial gossip, received ostentatious yet jealous respect; Mme. de Bargeton attended to them most, and they remained dignifiedly aloof from Angoulême’s cliques.

The Prefect and the General arrived last, accompanied by a country gentleman who had brought a treatise on silkworms. This man, secure in his estate but unused to polite society, quivered with awkward servility—shifting feet, half-rises, eager laughter at jokes, and anxious attempts to speak about silkworms—only finding a sympathetic ear late in the evening with Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard.

Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard were a touching study in genteel poverty. Poor yet noble, their dress betrayed their pinched means. The daughter, Camille, a plump twenty-seven, was loudly praised by her mother for piano playing in the most awkward way; Mme. du Brossard plainly sought a husband for her daughter, boasting adaptability to any suitor. Their strained dignity and reliance on social formulas repaid them only with benevolent condescension.

M. de Séverac, fifty-nine and a childless widower interested in silkworm nurseries, conversed with Mme. du Brossard, who absurdly claimed her daughter once understood the inverse ratio of the squares of distances—an anecdote that, in Lucien’s later reading, achieved comedic success.

A few habitués and some eldest sons filtered in—shy, mute young men decked in jewelry, honored by invitation to the literary solemnity; a bold one ventured to chat with Mlle. de la Haye. The women settled in a circle and the men stood behind them. It was a quaint, heterogeneous assembly of wrinkled faces and varied costumes that made Lucien’s heart beat fast under their gaze.

Mme. de Bargeton welcomed the illustrious Angoulême personages with ostentatious courtesy, but a petty sting awaited Lucien: apart from Louise, M. de Bargeton, the Bishop, and a few who wished to please the hostess, nobody called him M. de Rubempré; to the formidable audience he was simply M. Chardon. Lucien read his plebeian name in their movements and anticipatory provincial criticisms cut him deeply. He had not expected such pin-pricks and grew more out of humour with himself.

Lucien, eager to begin and end his torment, waited for a chance, but the room buzzed with trivialities: Jacques told Mme. de Pimentel of his shooting; Adrien lectured Mlle. Laure de Rastignac on Rossini; Astolphe read a newspaper paragraph on a patent plow to the Baron. Unluckily for the poet, few in the room understood or wanted poetry. Many had come through misapprehension; certain words—“beauty,” “glory,” “poetry”—bewitched even coarse minds into attendance.

When everyone had arrived and the low hum subsided—M. de Bargeton, obedient to his wife, made a slow circuit like a beadle tapping the pavement with his wand until silence—Lucien moved to the round table near Mme. de Bargeton. He felt a fierce thrill and announced in an uncertain voice that, to avoid disappointment, he would read the masterpieces of a great poet recently discovered—though André de Chénier’s poems had been published in 1819, no one in Angoulême had heard of him. The audience took the announcement according to convenience; it appeared to many a saving flourish of the hostess to soothe the poet’s self-regard and put the assembly at ease.

Lucien began with Le Malade and won a murmur of applause; but when he followed with L’Aveugle the strain proved too great for most present. Only artists or temperamentally inclined listeners can follow such readings; to render poetry by voice so that an audience grasps it requires intense attention and a close rapport between reader and hearers. That sympathy was missing; Lucien seemed like an angel singing of heaven amid the chucklings of hell. A sensitive artist senses at once whether his audience listens, like a plant reviving or drooping to conditions; here the men who had come with their wives were absorbed in their own affairs, and every murmur registered in Lucien’s ear. He noticed gaps made by spasmodic jaw-work and teeth that seemed to grin defiance at him.

Searching, like a dove for a resting place, he saw only rows of impatient faces. Their owners had come to discuss practical matters; except for Laure de Rastignac, the Bishop, and two or three young men, they looked bored. Those who understand poetry try to develop its quickened seeds; this glacial audience, so far from attaining the spirit, did not even hear the letter.

Discouraged and damp with chill sweat, Lucien was steadied by a glowing glance from Louise, but his poet’s heart bled. Lili asked Fifine if she found it amusing. “Don’t ask me; I cannot keep my eyes open when anyone reads aloud,” Fifine answered. “I hope Naïs will not give us poetry often in the evenings,” said Francis. “If I must attend someone reading after dinner, it upsets my digestion.” “It was very well declaimed,” Alexandre observed, “but I like whist better.” After that quip some card-players decided the reader’s voice needed a rest, and a couple of couples slipped away to the card-room. But Louise, the Bishop, and pretty Laure begged Lucien to continue, and he caught his audience again with Chénier’s spirited reactionary iambes. Several, carried away by his impassioned delivery, applauded without understanding the sense; people of that sort mistake vociferation for meaning, as a coarse palate is tickled by strong spirits.

During the interval, while they had ices, Zéphirine sent Francis to examine the volume and told Amélie that the poetry was in print. Amélie brightened. “Why, that is easily explained,” she said. “M. de Rubempré works for a printer. It is as if a pretty woman should make her own dresses,” she added, looking at Lolotte. “He printed his poetry himself!” the women murmured. “Then why does he call himself M. de Rubempré?” asked Jacques. “If a noble takes a handicraft, he ought to lay his name aside.” “So he did,” said Zizine, “but his name was plebeian; he took his mother’s, which is noble.” “Well, if his verses are printed, we can read them for ourselves,” said Astolphe.

This stupidity complicated matters until Sixte du Châtelet condescended to inform these unlettered folk that the prefatory announcement was not oratorical flourish but fact: the poems had been written by a Royalist brother of Marie-Joseph Chénier, the Revolutionary leader. All Angoulême, except Mme. de Rastignac, her two daughters, and the Bishop—who had genuinely felt the grandeur of the poetry—were mystified and took offense at the hoax. There was a smothered murmur, but Lucien did not heed it. The intoxication of the poetry had seized him; far from the hateful world, he strove to render in speech the music that filled his soul, seeing the faces about him through a cloudy haze. He read the sombre Elegy on the Suicide, lines of a bygone taste pervaded by sublime melancholy; then he turned to the page where the line occurs, “Thy songs are sweet, I love to say them over,” and ended with the delicate idyll Néere.

Mme. de Bargeton sat with a hand buried in her curls, heedless of the havoc she wrought, gazing with unseeing eyes, alone in her drawing-room, lost in delicious dreaming; for the first time in her life she had been transported to the sphere that was hers by nature. Judge, therefore, how unpleasantly she was disturbed by Amélie, who took it upon herself to express the general wish. “Naïs,” she broke in, “we came to hear M. Chardon’s poetry, and you are giving us poetry out of a book. The extracts are very nice, but the ladies feel a patriotic preference for the wine of the country; they would rather have it.” Astolphe added lightly to Châtelet, “The French language does not lend itself very readily to poetry, does it? Cicero’s prose is a thousand times more poetical to my way of thinking.” “The true poetry of France is song, lyric verse,” Châtelet answered. “Which proves that our language is eminently adapted for music,” said Adrien.

“I should very much like to hear the poetry that has cost Naïs her reputation,” said Zéphirine; “but after receiving Amélie’s request in such a way, it is not likely she will give us a specimen.” “She ought to have them recited in justice to herself,” said Francis. “The little fellow’s genius is his sole justification.” “You have been in the diplomatic service,” said Amélie to M. du Châtelet, “go and manage it somehow.” “Nothing easier,” said the Baron.

The Princess’ private secretary, used to petty manoeuvres, went to the Bishop and contrived to bring him forward. At the Bishop’s entreaty Naïs had no choice but to ask Lucien to recite his own verses for them, and the Baron received a languishing smile from Amélie as the reward of his prompt success. “Decidedly, the Baron is a very clever man,” she observed to Lolotte. But Amélie’s previous acid remark about women who made their own dresses rankled in Lolotte’s mind. “Since when have you begun to recognize the Emperor’s barons?” she asked, smiling.

Lucien had proudly recited an ode, “TO HER!”, under Mme. de Bargeton’s protection, but the reception in the salon was chilly and calculating. His poem pictured an angel visiting earth, Love dazzled by light, and a luminous path marking the angel’s flight. Amélie coquetted; the Baron, judging taste, sneered it was a Christianized Ossian—full of swelling words and strained rhymes. Zéphirine called it obscure; Francis mocked the archangel’s armor as thin gauze. The women, hoping to be flattered like angels, pretended pleasure while remaining inwardly cold.

Lolotte warned Adrien not to congratulate Lucien; Adrien obeyed. Zéphirine told Francis that love was a poem to be lived, not written; Stanislas agreed, flattering himself. Amélie, eager to humble Naïs, attacked Lucien’s humble origins—mocking his family’s trades—and the company joined in. Francis aimed a subtler blow by planting an idea with the Bishop: that Lucien’s mother, a modest woman of rare talents, supplied subjects for his verses and that he idolized her. Francis left the Bishop to make the intended allusion.

Interest sharpened when the Bishop rejoined. Lucien, shy and unused to their manners, was embarrassed by being called alternately M. Chardon and M. de Rubempré. He misnamed M. de Senonches and provoked rude laughter and Mme. de Bargeton’s shame. Mme. de Bargeton philosophized about why women are drawn to greatness; the Marquise laughed and sided with Naïs. The Marquise and Marquis spoke lightly; Lolotte insolently asked Lucien if he worked quickly, as one asks a joiner about a box. Stung, Lucien found courage in Mme. de Bargeton’s defense: poetry did not grow in his head like grass in a courtyard. The Bishop, sanctifying poetry, laid a hand on Lucien’s head and called him a poet marked by Fate.

Grateful, Lucien thanked the Bishop with a look—unaware the prelate would wound him next. When Lucien spoke of the poet’s suffering and the need for experience and deep feeling to make true characters, he hinted his work was only beginning. The Bishop’s joke—“Your excellent mother might assist you”—provoked delighted smiles of caste and M. de Bargeton’s laugh. Mme. de Bargeton retorted that a poet who turned to the Bible had a mother in the Church, mockingly challenging Lucien to declaim Saint John in Patmos or Belshazzar’s Feast; the women smiled at the Latin. Lucien faltered, sank, rose resolved to conquer this world, and prepared to declaim Saint John—but the card-tables called the company back. They returned to play, showing contempt, leaving Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton alone.

Angoulême’s social world, conscious of its ignorance of poetry, clustered about the Pimentels and Rastignacs for guidance; the Marquis and Marquise’s verdict—“not bad for provincial poetry”—spread and became malicious. Music followed; duets and schoolroom songs filled the evening. Hurt by the company’s contempt, Mme. de Bargeton withdrew to her boudoir with the Bishop and Mlle. de Rastignac, who was enchanted by the verses. There, free of the salon’s eye, Mme. de Bargeton murmured that they had not understood him and repeated his lines with feeling. She urged Lucien to endure, insisting suffering is the price of immortality and that struggle gives the poet wings. She begged him, when he reached greatness, to remember those disinherited by fate, to sing of lives choked and unblossomed—allegories of souls that remember Heaven. The Bishop compared such a theme to the Song of Songs; Laure de Rastignac suggested it. The Vicar-General declared France’s sacred poem unwritten and prophesied success for the man who should write for religion. Mme. de Bargeton claimed the task for Lucien, seeing vision in his eyes.

That quiet encouragement was short-lived. Amélie, Fifine, Adrien, and Francis reentered with Mme. de Rastignac to summon Naïs for music; they gleefully told the drawing-room M. de Rubempré would recite Saint John—“biblical” became mockery. Lucien excused a lapse of memory; when he reappeared, no one noticed—the poet’s halo had been plucked away. The landowners and pretentious set had no use for him; the women, jealous of Mme. de Bargeton, looked on with icy scorn.

“So this is society!” Lucien thought as he walked the long way home to L’Houmeau, pacing to ease anger and muse on too-late answers. Far from disheartened, his ambition burned: like many who reach a higher sphere before belonging, he vowed any sacrifice to remain. As he strolled toward the Bordeaux road, he fancied Eve and David by the river and hurried to meet them.

Eve had dressed simply; David, shy at her beauty, planned to speak of himself and their future. They walked across the Bridge of Saint Anne along the Charente until Eve broke the silence. David, thinking of Lucien, confessed anxiety: he feared Mme. de Bargeton would spoil Lucien, making him her plaything or, if indifferent, ruin him—Lucien loved pleasure and might not endure the privations required for great literary work. Eve calmly promised to work with her mother to support Lucien until success. Then David proposed marriage: he would marry Eve, give up his present life, and devote himself and any future fortune to supporting Lucien. He argued Lucien should not be a foreman nor live in L’Houmeau; together they could keep Lucien until he reached Paris.

To justify this sacrifice, David revealed a plan born of his study of chemistry and the paper trade. Since the Empire’s fall, linen rags used for durable paper had grown scarce and dear, while cotton made soft, inferior sheets. If a cheap, durable paper could be made from a common vegetable fiber—like the Chinese use of bamboo—books could be far lighter and cheaper, widening the diffusion of knowledge. David sketched the paper-making process: rag-pickers gather rags; merchants sort them; mills wash and reduce them to pulp; the pulp is strained through a mould to set sheet size, then pressed and dried. European experiments toward substituting vegetable fibers and machine production had been slow; innovations such as web-paper and various inventors had begun exploring alternatives. David thought a local plant—reeds or other fibers—could be processed mechanically to replace costly linen rags. Though Chinese handwork gave a surface quality hard to imitate, an industrial solution could revolutionize European paper-making and thus literature’s reach.

As David explained, Lucien arrived, distraught from his humiliation: the sneers, the Bishop’s epigram, the ridicule of his origins, and his fear Mme. de Bargeton might be both consolation and ruin. He believed she loved him as he loved her; he thought she would not abandon him. David and Eve, absorbed in their engagement and David’s plan, first did not grasp Lucien’s shock, but David’s warm description of a household—first floor for his wife, second for Lucien, rooms above the shed for Mme. Chardon—reassured him. The glow of aristocratic ambition faded; the burgher comforts and the brothers’ shared project appealed. They walked back together, reconciled, Lucien joining in the lovers’ joy.

They returned late; Postel, uneasy, saw light in Eve’s room and asked Lucien if anything was wrong. Lucien said his mother had consented to Eve’s engagement; Postel slammed his window in despair that he had not earlier asked for Mlle. Chardon.

David did not sleep in Angoulême; he walked overnight to his father’s house at Marsac. At sunrise, after brief domestic exchange, he declared his purpose: to marry Eve and asked his father’s consent and permission to add a second floor and rooms above the shed for their household. The practical vine-grower thought first of money, grumbled about taxes, and scoffed at book-learning. He consented to the marriage but had no money and assumed Eve must be rich. When David revealed she had nothing, the father proposed other, wealthier matches and railed at David’s education for leading him away from profitable advantage. He refused to fund building, though he would consent to the union and not press the rent; any improvements would be David’s responsibility.

David left Marsac disappointed but resolved: he could not count on his father’s help; his plans must be achieved by his own means.

In Angoulême everyone talked about the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s witty retort; by evening the story had swelled into a triumph for Lucien. The rumor leaked into the bourgeois quarters: young men glanced after him, and phrases of admiration fed his vanity. Petit-Claud and others murmured that he was a lucky, good-looking fellow whom Mme. de Bargeton adored.

Lucien, eager to see Louise, hoped that news of his sister’s forthcoming marriage would win him more favor after last night’s soirée. Mme. de Bargeton greeted him with passionate vehemence that overwhelmed the inexperienced lover: she yielded to his kisses, wiped his brow, called him a crowned poet, and declared she would make an oasis for him—busy and languid by turns—reminding him that he owed his laurels to her. Tears and raptures followed; Lucien kissed her hand, and the household—his mother, sister, David, and Louise—encouraged his self-regard. Compliments from friends and malice from enemies fixed him in an unreal world: a promising young man eager to believe flattering estimates.

Louise, sweet and strategic, encouraged him but kept reserve. When Lucien told her that David and Eve were to be married, Louise brushed off any domestic scruples: lovers are each other’s whole family; the world outside did not matter. Her counsel—that he should be great and seek fame—made Lucien ecstatically happy. M. de Bargeton invited him to stay for dinner and read André Chénier, and under his and Louise’s coaxing Lucien lingered at the Hôtel de Bargeton. Cared for like a favored guest, he began to consider the luxuries he enjoyed as rightful for Lucien de Rubempré. Louise’s love and M. de Bargeton’s indulgence made him feel secure; he assumed a lordly air, tasted despotic sway, and liked being the town’s lion.

Gossip turned his airs into apparent courtship. Amélie, with M. du Châtelet, and other jealous or envious observers concluded he had reached final terms with Mme. de Bargeton. They mocked the idea of a druggist’s son presuming to conquer a woman of Naïs’s rank, but some defended that love knows no class. By the next day every house in Angoulême discussed the intimacy between M. Chardon (Lucien) and Mme. de Bargeton. Though their real fault may have been a few kisses, society chose to believe scandal. Mme. de Bargeton paid for her social sovereignty: the town’s erratic judgments punished her more harshly than long toleration of other indiscretions.

David had come with some savings from Paris, reserved to build an upper story on his father’s house and to marry Eve. He put up a timbered second floor for Lucien rather than strain the old walls, taking pleasure in making preparations for Eve’s household. The friends were blithely happy. Lucien, former printer’s foreman, now called himself M. de Rubempré, lodged sumptuously in contrast to his former attic, dined often with Mme. de Bargeton, and frequented the Bishop’s palace. He believed his name would be one of France’s great names, consoling himself for his meager stipend with the thought that his romance and verse—An Archer of Charles IX. and Marguerites—would yet bring fame and repay sacrifices.

Eve and David deferred their wedding plans to accommodate Lucien: they painted rooms and bought furniture for him first. Lucien’s charm and gracious impatience made his cause so appealing that his friends and family centered comforts around him. This indulgence—common for youthful favorites—tends to ruin some, making them presume on a world predisposed to give to pleasant young things. Eve had meant to be pragmatic, but who could refuse a brother who appeared to long to sew for domestic life? David, sober and observant, watched Lucien with misgiving after his triumph; he feared Lucien would change. When he tested him, however, Lucien chose home pleasures over the great world, and David justified himself: “They will not spoil him for us!” They contrived modest country pleasures—picnics, walks, inexpensive dinners—and praised Lucien as the great man of Angoulême.

The new home nearly complete, David went to Marsac to entreat his father to attend the wedding and perhaps contribute. But events in town then changed everything. Lucien and Louise had a spy in Châtelet, an envious man who watched with hatred and greed for scandal. Sixte—Châtelet—presented himself as Mme. de Bargeton’s humble admirer and confidant; he admired Lucien where expedient and disparaged him elsewhere. Naïs (Mme. de Bargeton) had gradually given him small attentions, for she no longer mistrusted her elderly suitor; but Châtelet had overreached. Love often flounders in provincial life: gestures are delayed, words substituted for action, and desire is forced into intellectual skirmishing. A system of petty espionage governs such towns—every house is transparent, every innocent act is ripe for scandal.

Mme. de Bargeton’s situation was constrained by public life. She could not deny callers without arousing suspicion; doors stood open, servants came and went, the husband pervaded the house without suspecting any impropriety; visitors multiplied as curiosity increased. Any alteration in household routines would be treated as confession. To walk alone with Lucien would have been dangerous; to shut herself up with him would have been worse. Living so visibly, she could not satisfy a passion without exposing herself.

Châtelet lay in wait. He watched Lucien into the de Bargeton house, followed with M. de Chandour, and contrived to feign neutrality while prompting others. He allied with jealous Amélie to lull suspicion in the pair and to manufacture a scene. Their polemics—arguments staged to draw attention—kept Mme. de Bargeton and Lucien in sight. Châtelet later claimed nothing suspicious was visible because the boudoir door stood open and servants came and went, but Stanislas vowed to cross the room on tiptoe the next day and, egged on by Amélie, did so.

For Lucien, the next day he resolved to declare himself extravagantly: six months had elevated him in his own eyes to parity with Louise, and he intended to be her master. Mme. de Bargeton, however, resolved to baffle him. She valued herself as a sovereign, intending Lucien to win her by prowess; she wanted her love to be a reward for prolonged constancy and an incentive to his literary greatness. So when Lucien began vehemently, Louise responded in a lofty, didactic speech: do not sow regrets; my heart is already yours; the beloved should silence the clamor of the senses; if you cannot understand my elevated notion of love, you will never be worthy. Lucien, frantic, accused her of using words to avoid responding and flung himself at her feet, crying he had never loved anyone like her and begging proof that she was his.

At that moment Stanislas came up unseen and found Lucien with his head on Louise’s knee—an appearance ripe for scandal. He turned on Châtelet, and the spies beat a precipitate retreat. Mme. de Bargeton, pale and trembling, learned that M. de Chandour and M. du Châtelet had been in the house; she told Lucien that if they had seen them as Stanislas said, she was ruined. Lucien, selfish in love, only exulted: “So much the better!”—a reply she received with a smile.

In the provinces a story grows as it is told. M. de Chandour, elated, announced his part at the club and then house to house; Châtelet, claiming he had seen nothing, prodded Stanislas to gossip. Stanislas, thinking himself witty, embroidered the tale. The Angoulême nobility flocked to Amélie’s that evening, where exaggerated versions circulated among women who brandished accusations. Some expressed pity for Naïs yet agreed she had made herself ridiculous; others mocked the age difference. Châtelet defended her clumsily, doing more to stir than to quell rumor. Different voices created variations until the town buzzed.

When the scandal seemed in full bloom, Châtelet went to Mme. de Bargeton with a professed offer of help. He suggested Stanislas must have been mistaken and urged Naïs not to let the dolt trifle with her life and honor. He presented himself as her friend and champion, ready to be her public defender; if necessary, he said, M. de Bargeton should press Stanislas for a formal retraction. He hinted that if the young man had behaved foolishly, a woman’s character ought not to hang on the word of a hare-brained boy. His obsequiousness allowed him to push Naïs toward Paris thoughts, and he begged her to dispose of him as she pleased.

Naïs listened, weary of provincial life and drawn by the thought of Paris, but she did not disclose her intentions. Châtelet could not read the “high heart” folded in her silence.

Later, after a whist game, Mme. de Bargeton spoke to her husband solemnly. She explained what had happened that morning: Lucien had thrown himself at her feet in passion, and Stanislas had come in just then. She described Stanislas’ claim that he had seen them in a compromising position, and she urged M. de Bargeton to go to Stanislas and demand satisfaction. She argued that public honor required a public retraction; that a gentleman should not vindicate his wife by fighting the offender, because that would be to publicize the scandal and the man defending her would himself be dishonored; but it would be right for M. de Bargeton to compel Stanislas to retract his insult publicly before witnesses. She planned to send Gentil to Escarbas to fetch her father as his second, and suggested pistols, for her husband was a good shot. Her aim was to restore respectability and reassert their standing.

Touched and proud, M. de Bargeton took his hat and cane and set out that night for Stanislas’ house. Though inwardly anxious—worried what he should say—he found that, like many men of modest habitual reserve, he acted at crisis with a concise dignity that suited the occasion. He arrived at nine, saw Amélie and others gathered, and greeted them with a simple, ironic smile that suggested a grave purpose. The room fell silent, the pause before a storm; Châtelet looked from M. de Bargeton to Stanislas, who was accosted politely by the injured gentleman.

Thus the scandal that springed from a few kisses and a momentary collapse of discretion had already mobilized gossip, jealousies, and schemes. Lucien’s vanity and Mme. de Bargeton’s open indulgence—combined with the petty malice of provincial society—made the pair vulnerable. Châtelet, eager to be her champion and in the bargain to profit by her fall, maneuvered to shape the narrative; others embellished for the sport of scandal. Mme. de Bargeton, refusing to renounce her dignity, insisted on public vindication; M. de Bargeton, though timid, accepted the role of a man of honor, and prepared to force the tongue of rumor into a public retraction. The impending confrontation at Stanislas’ house, with the town assembled, held consequences not only for reputations but for the fragile hopes Lucien and his friends had just begun to entertain.

Châtelet knew a late visit meant old M. de Bargeton would be in bed. Naïs had set that feeble arm in motion, and Châtelet, intimate with the household, took M. de Bargeton aside.

“Do you wish to speak to Stanislas?”

“Yes,” replied the old man, pleased to find a go‑between.

“Very well; go into Amélie’s bedroom,” said the controller of excise, privately delighted at the prospect of a duel that might make Mme. de Bargeton a widow and cut off Lucien’s access to her. Châtelet then addressed M. de Chandour.

“Stanislas,” he said, “Bargeton has come to call you to account, no doubt for the things you have been saying about Naïs. Go into your wife’s room and behave like gentlemen. Keep it quiet, be politely phlegmatic—act with British dignity.”

A minute later Stanislas and Châtelet confronted Bargeton.

Bargeton accused Stanislas of asserting he had found “Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré in an equivocal position.” Stanislas, flippant in tone, corrected the name and was not taken seriously. Bargeton, rehearsing his speech, demanded satisfaction. He announced his second, M. de Nègrepelisse, set pistols as the weapons, and named four o’clock the next morning for the duel. Stanislas, pale with conflicting shame and fear, yielded to the demand, hoping something would avert the meeting: “All right. Tomorrow morning.”

They rejoined the drawing room where whispers spread and people sized up the participants. Some believed Stanislas’s pallor proof of deceit; others admired Bargeton’s dignity. Châtelet maintained a solemn air and lingered to reassure Stanislas and his wife that the younger man would have the advantage in a fight between ages. Amélie fainted and was taken upstairs. Bargeton, savoring his role, eventually left.

Early next morning Angoulême soon echoed with news. Lucien sat at breakfast with David when Mme. Chardon arrived, frightened and breathless with gossip. A countryman had seen the duel from his cart: M. de Nègrepelisse had come to be Bargeton’s second; an officer lent service pistols and tested them; despite Châtelet’s opposition, the officer insisted on serviceable weapons. The seconds set twenty‑five paces. Bargeton fired first; the ball struck Chandour in the neck, who fell before he could return fire. The house surgeon predicted a permanent stiffness. Mme. Chardon warned Lucien to stay away from Angoulême, as friends of Chandour might call Lucien out.

As she spoke, Gentil, Bargeton’s footman, arrived with a note for Lucien from Louise. She begged him to avoid the house that day, asking him not to show himself and urging him to spend the melancholy day with “your Beatrice,” who had been shaken by the event and had much to say. Lucien insisted Mme. de Bargeton was innocent.

David, meanwhile, was engrossed in his own anxieties and satisfactions: his marriage was fixed for the day after tomorrow, and his first floor had been transformed into a fresh, white, bride‑like suite, furnished by the savings and efforts of mother and fiancée. Mme. Chardon and Eve had labored over linens, draperies, and even mattress piping—small vanities and homely comforts reflecting the couple’s love, but David had overspent. The cost exceeded his means; he had not enough for the planned house beyond the shed. He concealed his distress to spare Lucien, fearing his friend’s natural impulsiveness might collapse under the sacrifices.

Lucien slipped away to Mme. de Bargeton’s. He found Naïs at table with her husband; Bargeton’s morning walk had sharpened his appetite, and he breakfasted with unaffected complacency after the duel. Lucien saw M. de Nègrepelisse, an old provincial noble who inspected him keenly. The father seemed to treat Lucien’s attachment as a flirtation; nevertheless, he gave Lucien an approving look.

Louise left her father and husband and drew Lucien aside. Half glad and half melancholy, she told him she was going to Paris immediately; her father would take Bargeton back to his estate and keep him there during her absence. She planned to cultivate Mme. d’Espard (a Blamont‑Chauvry) — influential, connected to court — hoping such acquaintance could secure a place for Bargeton or otherwise elevate their prospects. If Bargeton could represent the Charente or receive some patronage, it would be a step toward the Paris life Louise craved: summers at the Escarbas, winters in Paris, surrounded by society worthy of a woman of quality.

She then outlined to Lucien the glittering advantages Paris offered a young man of talent: salons where ministers, ambassadors, orators, and men of influence gathered; opportunities for sinecures or pensions; the way the monarchy rewarded letters and art. She urged him to follow her there at once, to put himself among great men, to keep his ideas from growing “rancid in the provinces” and to let his genius be seen. Mme. d’Espard’s salon, she promised, would give him introductions to those who could launch a career; the right patronage could secure distinction. She counselled a cautious strategy — being a religious, Royalist poet might be the way to court favor — and implored him to prepare for departure: “Would you rather not go?”

To Lucien, who had always felt intellectually cramped in Angoulême, the suggestion was intoxicating. He imagined Paris as the Eldorado of provincial dreams, where publishers would greet genius with open purses and fame would follow instantly. He understood also that traveling with Louise would make their liaison irreversible; they would live together. Overcome, he embraced her passionately. But then he remembered with a pang his sister’s coming wedding — a domestic tie that represented his last duties at home. His outcry — “Great heavens! my sister is to be married the day after tomorrow!” — marked a last stir of boyish loyalty.

The proud Nègrepelisse, appearing, rebuked Lucien with scorn: what had a tradespeople’s wedding to do with the “progress of our love”? He mocked Lucien’s supposed sentimental attachment to a wedding dinner and scolded him for wanting to be the best man over more exalted pleasures. Louise, wounded, fainted in the course of the argument. Lucien, contrite and desperate, cursed his family and clung to Louise, who, though she had been offended, forgave him but insisted he prove his resolve. She arranged for him to set out at midnight, to meet her on the road a short distance from Mansle, and to travel to Paris together.

Lucien returned to David’s furnished rooms, his mind alight with ambition and misgivings. The riddle of finance loomed: where would the money come from? His departure would make the newly bought furnishings wasted, but perhaps his mother could take the rooms and relieve David. He made many convenient arguments to justify sudden flight. Desire, his guiding Jesuit, pressed him forward.

He hurried to seek counsel from his sister and found the women in a painful position. Eve and Mme. Chardon had spent all their savings to furnish David’s first floor, and to prepare wedding clothes and linens. A ten‑thousand‑franc marriage‑settlement had been arranged for Eve, but the household’s ready money had been drained in doing honor to the match. Lucien proposed borrowing a thousand francs; Mme. Chardon promised to ask M. Postel for the loan.

Eve, distressed, argued he ought not to leave for the wedding, begging him to delay his departure a few days; she feared the bad luck of him missing the wedding. She worried as well about Lucien’s slender wardrobe: only one good coat, two fine shirts, a handful of inadequate ties and waistcoats; Paris would necessitate new, costly clothes, forcing him to need more money. “Take two thousand francs,” she urged.

Postel agreed to lend a thousand francs for six months but demanded a bill endorsed by David: Lucien offered no sufficient security. At that moment David, who had overheard, emerged quietly and, after a pause, offered to sign for the bill and to give Lucien the two thousand francs he would need. The Chardons felt shame for having assumed David’s resources, and David’s eyes were wet as he placed small wedding trinkets on the table he had recently bought for Eve. He declared he would put his name to the bill. Lucien, deeply moved and mindful of the sacrifice, embraced the young couple and implored them not to think ill of him. He professed that his destiny required entering Paris and that distance could not break their spiritual union.

Behind the sentimental scene, practical anxiousness prevailed. David explained to Eve that if he endorsed the loan and surrendered two thousand francs, it would nearly exhaust his capital; half of his remaining funds were necessary to carry on the printing business. He argued that if it were only himself, he would be willing, but they were beginning married life and could not live in constant anxiety. Eve, distraught but resolute, told David to act as if he were alone: she would work to earn whatever was needed.

Lucien prepared to depart with appalling haste. He took only a few garments and his manuscript — the waistcoat, the best overcoat, the famous coat, a couple of shirts — and arranged with David to send the rest by coach to a paper merchant in Paris to be collected there, so as to conceal his travel plans from Mme. de Bargeton. Mme. de Bargeton herself planned to leave Angoulême, and Châtelet, interested in exposing any impropriety, sent his man to Ruffec to watch every carriage change to learn whether Lucien accompanied her. Châtelet thought that if the poet went with her, he’d have proof of their intimacy.

Lucien set out before dawn with David, who had hired a cabriolet and pretended business in Marsac to conceal their true journey. They spent part of the day with the old “bear” at Marsac, and then resumed their road. At dawn beyond Mansle they waited for Mme. de Bargeton. When the seventy‑year‑old carriage she commonly traveled in appeared, Lucien felt the fierce intensity of his decision. He sprang into David’s arms, and they watched Naïs’s carriage approach. David, whose heart was heavy with foreboding about Lucien’s prospects in Paris and his friend’s susceptibility to seduction and ruin, wished Lucien well with a prayer: “God grant that this may be for your good!” They parted as the carriage stopped and Mme. de Bargeton entered for the journey. David drove away, dread clutching him; he had terrible presentiments about Lucien’s fate in Paris.
