DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

Dear Nodier, this book exposes actions hidden behind domestic doors where divine intervention often replaces human justice. Its moral lessons center on family and motherhood and on the harm that follows when paternal authority—the traditional tribunal for domestic wrongs—erodes. A loving mother cannot substitute for patriarchal power. The story defends indissoluble marriage and warns against unchecked selfishness in a society that exalts wealth and success while the law is impotent. I hope education might steer such a society back to Catholic moral guidance.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will show noble self-sacrifice and war’s corrupting effect on those at home who live as if on a battlefield. You have observed our times keenly; I entrust this tale to your name as a shield from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,  
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget; gossip blamed him for his beautiful wife’s unhappiness, though the household’s true nature remained private. Madame Rouget, née Descoings, had been frail since youth. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

Rouget’s belief that men must display strength caused much female suffering. The Descoings family—wealthy wool merchants turned investors in forfeited estates—were rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and later was driven by passion for a beautiful widow. Expecting his wife’s death, Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to secure Descoings’ favor and a share of the inheritance.

Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun and like her mother in looks, provoked a quarrel between Rouget and their friend Lousteau, who then left town. Madame Rouget, learning her daughter had gone, predicted she would never see Agathe again—a prophecy fulfilled as her health declined, worsened by her son’s neglect; Jean-Jacques offered little comfort.

Agathe arrived in Paris as Descoings was imprisoned for imprudent political remarks during the famine and then executed alongside the poet André Chénier—an event that stunned their community. Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Rouget’s approval; Bridau, captivated by her beauty, supported Napoleon, earned promotion in 1804, and labored relentlessly. In 1808 he died of overwork just as another promotion was imminent—an honor the Emperor meant to bestow but belatedly.

After Bridau’s death Agathe received no letters from Issoudun. Her mother died shortly before the birth of her second son; her father died around the Emperor’s coronation; Jean-Jacques remained absent. Madame Hochon warned that Agathe would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother; Agathe hesitated. Bridau’s prudent investments left only a modest income; Agathe and Madame Descoings lived on a combined twelve thousand francs. By 1809 Madame Descoings, sixty-five but fond of theater and fine food, was secretly gambling and accumulating debts. Devoted and refusing to remarry, Agathe took on household duties; her blind trust in Madame Descoings proved disastrous when the lottery debts were revealed, forcing Agathe to sell possessions and liquidate investments—a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches of Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, shaded by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold house, lived a poor widow, Madame Bridau (Agathe). Opposite were the Institute buildings, where aspiring artists worked amid the tumult of examinations; from her windows Agathe could see their cages and the students’ uproar. To brighten her room she defied police regulations and set boxes of earth on the sill to make little gardens.

The shallow house, reached by a winding staircase, contained three rooms: a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom, plus a kitchen and two single rooms. Agathe had chosen the flat because it was cheap—four hundred francs a year—near her sons’ school and in a neighborhood she knew. The interior matched the building’s dreariness: yellowed wallpaper, unglazed tiles, and only essential furniture. The salon held an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; a vivid Napoleon hung above a card table.

After Bridau’s death Agathe’s life narrowed. She filled the room with two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—a hobby that left her space littered with millet and chickweed, visible signs of grief and neglected personal care. She preserved everything belonging to her husband, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Grief seemed to strip away her sense of womanhood and redirect her entirely toward her children.

Agathe lived for her sons and worried ceaselessly over their finances. The Emperor provided three hundred francs a year for each child, and scholarships supplemented this. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, on the second floor, assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe; the Descoings themselves now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores, and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums. Distrustful of the future, Agathe scrimped and saved—she set aside three thousand francs from her pension—hoping to secure some inheritance for her boys. Thus both widows had passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The setting—house, students’ uproar, and her husband’s portrait—would shape her youngest son, Joseph Bridau, into a notable painter of the modern French school. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother in appearance but had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage; Agathe hoped his looks promised similar moral qualities. At fifteen his charm reinforced her hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways: untidy, often scolded, and plainly less favored by his mother.

Joseph’s artistic bent was sparked by a visit to the Tuileries, where a caricature on an Institute wall captivated him and made him want to draw. He began sneaking into the Institute courtyard, enduring teasing from older students until persistence won their respect. He later visited the sculptor Chaudet’s atelier; Chaudet recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared art meant hardship and tried to dissuade him, but Chaudet defended the vocation as a divine calling, insisting true talent must be followed.

Meanwhile Philippe, enchanted by military life, secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. He was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen. Joseph, after his scholarship was withdrawn, tried to earn a living by art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. In 1816 Joseph persuaded his mother to let him convert the adjoining garret into an atelier—partly financed by Madame Descoings—with a skylight and dark walls; he continued studies under Gros and Schinner.

Political unrest simmered in France; Philippe was implicated in conspiracies, briefly arrested, then released for lack of evidence, though his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. Friends urged the widows to send him abroad; Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile—an enterprise that failed. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs, and by late 1817 the household had adjusted to reduced means. Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his talent developed.

In 1818 the notary Roguin absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite her own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support them. Desperate, Joseph appealed to Gérard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII; Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt.

When Philippe returned, Agathe welcomed him warmly but failed to notice how America had hardened him. He became self-centered, drinking, gambling, and swaggering. Agathe excused his faults as results of circumstance. The family’s finances deteriorated; friends discussed selling possessions to help him. Philippe refused to serve the Bourbons, lived off others, disappeared for days in cafés and gaming houses, and neglected family duties. A cashier’s post at a newspaper, secured by Agathe and Madame Descoings, brought temporary relief but did little to reform him. Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated. Philippe’s return and altered behavior strained relationships, exposing the consequences of choices, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between ambition and family duty.

Three months on, the colonel—who ate enough for four—kept complaining about the food while the widows, unwilling to confront him, entertained him lavishly though he paid nothing. His mother and Madame Descoings avoided reminding him of his promise; months passed without a cent moving from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel soothed his conscience by seldom dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother said, “he has a place.”

Propelled by a feuilleton, Mariette made a triumphant debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin with Begrand. A wealthy general, acquainted with Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario. Bixiou soon told his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, their cashier, was in love with Mariette. The widows were appalled: Agathe’s piety condemned all stage women, and they feared such women demanded extravagance. “Do you think my brother will spend on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph protested. Bixiou countered that Mariette might draw higher admirers; he’d even heard an ambassador was infatuated.

Partly from vanity and partly from a desire to be near her, Philippe proposed; Mariette refused—perhaps suspecting his intent or hoping for the Grand Opera. Over the next year Philippe visited his mother rarely, spending his days at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette, enjoying pleasures and mixing with Tullia and other entertainers.

The carnival of indulgence lasted eighteen months but grew stormy. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, maddened by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed—taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette left for London to seek patrons while a new opera house was prepared. Philippe, despite her infidelities, remained deeply in love; to her he was merely a stepping-stone.

Forced to relinquish Mariette, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, unable to imagine life without the recent pleasures. Suicide crossed his mind—not only because of the deficit but because he could not bear losing Mariette’s company. He entered Joseph’s studio faint and subdued, asked how long a portrait would take, then, when pressed, confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph urged that repayment would restore his honor, but Philippe fled. Joseph told Madame Descoings and Agathe; they were horrified and devastated.

That day Philippe calmly submitted accounts, inventing a safekeeping story. He then went to a gambling house—winning at first, then losing everything. Returning home, the family’s concern briefly comforted him. “Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe feigned gratitude and affection; for a moment he felt relieved.

The next day Agathe seemed older and more anxious. Madame Descoings proposed consulting Desroches, who advised Agathe to sign a transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement; he promised Philippe would soon regain his rank, easing some fear. Yet Agathe’s faith was shaken.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, treating women with contempt. Joseph, concentrating on painting, gained recognition and Madame Descoings’s support, who pinned hopes on his future. Noticing financial discrepancies, Joseph suspected Philippe and confided in Madame Descoings; they planned to catch him. When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed; despite anger, he struggled to confront his brother. Madame Descoings urged sympathy, but Joseph remained wary of Philippe’s hold over the family’s future.

Despite careful nightly gambling, Philippe was often “cleaned out.” Needing his usual ten francs, he began stealing from family—taking from his brother and scavenging whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After dreaming that Philippe had robbed her, Agathe begged him to ask for money instead of stealing; within two weeks her savings were gone. She took worsted work for twenty sous a day, spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, and watched his drinking and mood swings worsen.

When Madame Descoings suggested new clothes, Philippe replied that his mother had no money and that he’d mortgaged his pension. He admitted borrowing from Giroudeau as a “debt of honor.” Agathe excused him; Joseph criticized his neglect of duty and appearance. Philippe sank further into debauchery and gambling, losing the steadiness and looks he once had.

Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then guessed where Madame hid her savings, and, expecting she’d be out, broke into her room. He found twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress and took them to the gambling house. Initially he won substantial sums—at one point amassing over 150,000 francs in a night—but greed drove him on and he lost everything. He awoke the next morning in despair, knowing he had ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe at Christmas dinner; when he did not come they worried. Madame had pinned hopes on a lottery stake; finding her hidden money gone, she fainted. The concierge confirmed Philippe had been in the apartment. Confronted, Philippe dismissed accusations, claiming he’d taken what was owed him. A fight between Joseph and Philippe erupted, and Madame Descoings suffered a stroke amid the chaos. Joseph offered her money afterward, but she clung to lottery hope; when the winning numbers were announced she’d missed her chance and died shortly after.

Recovering from illness, Philippe was told by Agathe that their finances had collapsed and urged to rejoin the army. Indifferent and feeling abandoned, he accepted the money she reluctantly offered and left to seek the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for the son she felt she had lost.

By February 1822 Madame Bridau had been moved into the attic above the kitchen of the apartment Philippe once occupied. Joseph set out to make her comfortable: he refurnished the garret, and took Agathe daily to a table d’hôte costing nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by expense and urged by her confessor, Agathe accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at the lottery office, working long hours.

She adapted to routine—preparing Joseph’s breakfasts, mending his clothes, keeping the studio tidy, and aiding his work despite no artistic training—and their bond deepened through these small acts. They spoke often of Philippe; Agathe feared his reckless life, while Joseph, convinced Philippe prospered in New York, was less alarmed.

Joseph visited Philippe at a newspaper; Philippe invited him to dine and to sit for a portrait. Agathe, overwhelmed, watched the brothers together. Philippe’s circle of journalists and actresses and boastful plans surfaced, but money details were vague. Later, thinking a Rubens copy an original, Philippe sold it cheaply; discovering the theft devastated Joseph and broke Agathe’s heart.

Two months on, an old officer told Agathe Philippe was ill and needed money. Working at the lottery, she first refused but Joseph insisted Philippe go to the hospital. Finding him destitute on the street, she gave what she could. She then learned Philippe had been implicated in a conspiracy and arrested; she fainted. Desroches advised securing the family inheritance and gathering evidence of undue influence; he planned to visit Issoudun at the holidays to help. Agathe’s letter to her godmother met only discouragement. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences and Agathe to confront their deep estrangement.

Issoudun, one of France’s oldest cities noted by Caesar and Rigord, yielded in recent excavations a fifth-century basilica—likely unique in France—built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name, from “Isis” with “dun” indicating a religious and military settlement, suggests ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was a center of power: Richard Coeur-de-lion built a tower above the basilica, and the town later served as a stronghold for Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Its prominence has since faded. After the 1830 insurrection vine-growers resisted government intervention, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy—about eight thousand strong.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun held sixteen to seventeen thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves; by Louis XIV’s time it showed some elegance. Today the population is about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from nearby suburbs. The local economy, especially wine, suffers from outdated practices: growers resist modernization and produce poor-quality wine that undercuts potential prosperity.

This decline reflects a broader stagnation: locals opposed a new Paris–Toulouse road fearing higher food prices; the vanished nobility removed competition, the bourgeoisie grew complacent, administration is dominated by intermarried families, and the clergy wields little influence. Against this backdrop the town’s youth formed the “Knights of Idleness,” led by Maxence Gilet—rumored an illegitimate son who rose from poverty and military service—whose disruptive but creative pranks, sheltered by a tavern run by Père and La Cognette, express youthful frustration.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower. Maxence arrived, had the cart hauled up the embankment, and within an hour the youths had disassembled it, carried the pieces aloft, reassembled the cart on the embankment, then, hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the Spaniard’s surprise.

The Knights’ amusements varied; they did not prank every night. The twenty youths hunted, harvested grapes and skated in winter. Maxence, thirty-four and soldierly, was their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, Madame Hochon’s grandsons, treated him like kin and relied on him for money and the adventures their grandfather would not permit.

One wine-filled evening François and Baruch warned Max he was threatened in his stronghold: Madame Bridau, her son, and other relatives were arriving and two rooms were being prepared for them. Max masked unease with bluster. When someone joked that Rouget’s sister would inherit and undo Max’s harvest, he snapped, defending Flore Brazier—Rouget’s servant-mistress, disparaged by society—and forbade contemptuous nicknames. His friends learned to tread carefully on the subject.

For the first time Max spoke openly about Flore. He acknowledged rumors that he spent on the household because of her and that she aimed for Rouget’s property. François suggested he might marry Flore once Rouget died; Max admitted it, but insisted he would not resort to murder or poison, only “fooleries” within the Order’s limits. He declared he loved Flore even more than Mademoiselle Fichet, Issoudun’s richest heiress, courted by Goddet. The Knights cheered; Max toasted them and ordered watchfulness—“be in the market early” and signal when the old Spaniard came for his cart—while assuring he would deal with direct threats. At half-past three they slipped away; Max returned to Place Saint-Jean, concealing the worry he had learned to hide during his time in Cabrera.

Rouget’s estate, producing forty thousand francs a year, anchored Max’s claim on Flore and the household. But legitimate heirs unsettled him: Rouget’s savings—accumulated over seventeen years and said to total large sums kept with various notaries (seven hundred and fifty thousand francs was mentioned)—were in the doctor’s name; even with a will those funds might be secured elsewhere. “That fool of a girl never mentioned her sister and nephews in all these seven years!” Max muttered, resolving to rid himself of these relatives and then secure the property. Exhausted, he used a pass-key to enter Père Rouget’s house and went to bed, promising to think clearly in the morning.

The narrative then traces how Flore Brazier—later called the “Rabouilleuse”—became Jean-Jacques Rouget’s quasi-mistress. As Doctor Rouget aged he tried to impose order on his ineffectual son Jean-Jacques, who yielded to the first strong personality. In September 1799 the doctor found a captivating little girl by a brook, emerging from the water in a tattered petticoat and makeshift hat. She claimed to be from Vatan and to be catching crabs for her uncle Brazier, who joked about republican liberty.

Struck by her looks, the doctor took her in, promising wages and a better life. Flore’s condition improved—fed, clothed, given gifts to encourage study—but her peasant habits made her dislike books and the doctor stopped her schooling. Town gossip hinted at impropriety, yet for five years no scandalous complications arose; Rouget merely enjoyed educating her. Born in 1787 amid Revolutionary upheaval, Flore developed pragmatic self-interest rather than strict virtue. As Rouget neared death, his notary urged provisions for her; his refusal to leave her anything revealed bitterness and set the stage for the inheritance struggle that now threatens Max’s position and Flore’s hopes.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father’s death, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son miserable after Jean-Jacques came of age, yet had provided Flore with the country comforts she enjoyed. At the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her now that Monsieur was dead, Jean-Jacques showed animation for the first time.

When his father died Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven; Flore was seventeen, still beautiful though growing plump like other countrywomen. Timid, submissive, and childlike, Jean-Jacques had long been awkward and solitary. His father had belatedly tried to teach him greed and a sense of property—seeking a daughter-in-law to secure his estate—without changing his son’s temperament.

Secretly in love with Flore, Jean-Jacques took comfort in the instruction his father had given her, believing her education would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for a declaration that never came. Three weeks of uncertainty followed; one night she woke to find him crouched outside her door and, seeing this as proof of affection, began to regard him less repulsively. Their tentative conversations—about work and honesty—showed his growing but inarticulate love.

After ten months Jean-Jacques brightened and paid attention to his appearance; the town noticed. Fanchette, the disapproving cook, left; Flore anticipated this, learned to cook, and took pride in managing the house, surpassing her predecessor. Over the next nine years she gained complete control over Jean-Jacques, treating him with a mix of familiarity and respect that made her the dominant partner while he remained dependent and childlike.

By 1815 Flore was at her peak beauty; in 1816 she fell for Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Pressing Jean-Jacques about his wealth and empty rooms, she persuaded him to invite Max. Jean-Jacques found in Max the conversation and companionship he lacked, while Flore neglected household duties, hired a new cook, refined her appearance to impress Max, bought him a horse, and took a servant. The household shifted: Flore and Max became central, and Jean-Jacques receded.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become semi‑vegetative. After Max’s financial help, Flore improved Rouget’s care, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen; yet he grew weaker and utterly submissive, his devotion to Flore the only force sustaining the man who seemed far older than fifty‑seven.

Max’s modest, elegant apartment reflected his devotion: provincial comforts and stair matting so he could come home late without waking anyone — not even Rouget, unaware of Max’s nocturnal affairs. One morning Flore found Max asleep; he told her Rouget’s family were coming, likely to try to evict them. Flore wanted confrontation; Max urged caution. Baruch then interrupted: Fario’s cart was missing.

Max led the crowd to the tower embankment and, to everyone’s surprise, the cart was found at the top. Amid laughter Max theatrically tossed it down, humiliating Fario and creating town gossip. François warned the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max decided they should not appear hostile and planned to let the Hochons do the driving-out. Flore, furious, punished Rouget by withdrawing affection; Vedie urged him to placate her. Rouget denied inviting kin and blamed Madame Hochon. Max advised him to welcome his relatives for appearances, preserve his will, and enlist the Hochons against the Bridaus.

Opposite Rouget’s, the frugal Hochon household readied to receive goddaughter Agathe; granddaughter Adolphine secretly hoped to win Joseph Bridau. The town split between cautious bourgeoisie and Max’s loyal “Knights of Idleness,” showing how fragile reputations and a few calculated acts could decide the inheritance conflict.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights. News spread quickly; Madame Hochon greeted Agathe like a daughter, clinging to that affection after years of loss.

Agathe met the reserved Monsieur Hochon and the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. Monsieur Hochon mentioned Joseph’s recent hospital stay and hunger; the youths were struck by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said proudly. Joseph’s blunt remark about resembling his father was misread by Madame Hochon as affection for Agathe. Baruch hurried off to spread the news, fuelling gossip that cast Joseph as a brigand and expressed the town’s disdain for artists; many planned to call on the Hochons that evening.

Installed in modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his Parisian artistic life with provincial drabness. Dinner revealed the Hochons’ frugality—plain bouilli, hard eggs, meager accompaniments—and Joseph, used to better fare, tried to adapt. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches; Madame Hochon braced for an influx of callers. Joseph noted the family’s strict ways and limited hospitality.

In the salon, Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged hardships and hard-won advice, repeatedly interrupted by callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph kept to himself, studying visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy; later the women resumed their heartfelt talk.

Elsewhere the Knights of Idleness plotted mischief against the Parisians. Max proposed a rat prank to sabotage a disliked merchant, Fario, aiming to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

Next morning Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget—unrecognizable and disheveled—with the conspicuous Flore Brazier. Joseph admired Flore; Agathe feared her influence. A letter inviting Joseph to dine arrived, met by the Hochons’ mixed feelings and suspicion. Monsieur Hochon warned Joseph to observe carefully and speak frankly about his nephews. With rival schemes and simmering tensions, a fraught reunion loomed.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where he found the table set in the large hall and greeted his uncle Rouget, Flore, and Maxence Gilet. After light banter he admired the house and paintings, which Rouget said came from the Descoings and had been acquired during the Revolution. Ecstatic, Joseph inspected more upstairs with Max. Flore urged Rouget to give the pictures to Joseph, arguing Rouget did not care for them.

Rouget offered the paintings—without frames—and Flore proposed paying him four thousand francs so Joseph could make copies. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. The news spread through Issoudun and enhanced Flore’s standing. Dinner that night was splendid, and Rouget, having drunk heavily, was carried to bed. Max and Flore agreed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them and planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The morning after, the paintings were propped against Rouget’s wall. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, visited Rouget to thank him after Hochon urged her—he feared the Bridaus’ influence and warned Joseph had been fooled. Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate Flore to gain access to Rouget. Agathe was warmly received; Rouget was ill from the previous night. Flore feigned servitude and hinted Rouget should marry her, though she insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

Meanwhile the Knights of Idleness released rats and mice into Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. The Bridaus’ hold on Rouget seemed to strengthen: Flore claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage; they bought a shabby vehicle that drew attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney granting Max authority over his investments while Flore handled local transactions. Fario, finding his store devastated, suspected Max and began gathering evidence.

Joseph, sensing the paintings’ real value, sent them to Desroches in Paris for appraisal, convinced they were worth far more than the 4,000-franc copying job. Agathe warned him to keep their worth quiet, but word leaked. Letters from Desroches and Schinner advised keeping Agathe in town, warned about Max, and suggested Rouget might already have a will—so the Bridaus’ prospects depended on a struggle between Flore and the Church. Disturbed, Joseph decided to return to Paris, resolve to rely on his talent, and later return the pictures. The Hochons suspected Max and Flore had known the paintings’ value and manipulated Joseph; Agathe defended her son, but Hochon warned that returning the paintings might jeopardize any chance at inheritance.

Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness fed the town’s dogs; their expedition lasted until three. After supper at Cognette’s, Max, turning from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue at half-past four, was stabbed by Fario, who had lurked in a recess, fled toward Vilatte, wiped the knife on his handkerchief, washed it in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out and alarming Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet, who ran up the street calling for help; the townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. Max had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and seeing the wound was not fatal, decided to exploit the incident: “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin hurried to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet and others. Goddet, suspicious of Max’s words, asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she said Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. The revelation shocked the group, and they found Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how people would view him, fearing they might think he had robbed his uncle. He longed to flee to Paris and confront Max; at dawn he walked to the poplar avenue toward Tivoli to steady his nerves.

After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, news spread and a crowd gathered at the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore that they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians: pretend he was dying and have Joseph arrested. Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. Incited by her, the crowd demanded Joseph’s capture; the mob grew hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, warned by Gritte, locked himself in, fearing for his safety and wealth. Agathe woke, learned of the accusations, and fainted at the sight of the gendarmes. The lieutenant reported a large crowd demanding justice.

Joseph was spotted returning home and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened and, despite his protests of innocence, took him to the Palais for his safety. There Joseph calmly insisted on his innocence and provided an alibi, recounting his morning’s movements. The magistrates kept him in custody while they verified his claims. Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free.

Max, recovering, recalled a crucial detail: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. He wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release. Witnesses then confirmed Joseph’s alibi and he was freed. He returned to his mother’s relief; the ordeal strengthened their bond and Joseph felt grateful for the support.

The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory; Max and Flore were pleased. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph’s spirits improved, while Agathe fretted about her son Philippe, who was sentenced to five years’ surveillance and exiled (later transferred to Issoudun). Motivated to restore the family’s fortunes and confront Max and the Rabouilleuse, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun and, after a brief dinner, departed to face the challenge.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On All-Souls’ Day Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun and registered at the commissary, unsettling the town already worried by the arrival of Maxence Gilet, a banished officer tied to a painter wrongly accused in the affair. Gilet had recently converted all of Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. Monsieur Hochon, after consulting notary Héron—who thought Rouget’s heirs should be grateful that Gilet hadn’t profited more—resolved to protect the Bridaus, long resentful of Gilet and fearful of losing property.

Gilet and Flore Brazier received Philippe, expecting to manipulate Rouget to secure his funds. Philippe’s blunt pride and refusal to beg, appearing in tattered clothes, impressed Flore and Gilet; Gilet sensed a threat but avoided confrontation. Philippe then visited the Hochons, candid about living on little, and won some household members with charm and honesty. Requesting secrecy, he asked Monsieur Hochon for names of officers hostile to Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier—and befriended them, impressing them with talk of the conspiracy against the Bourbons and carefully integrating into the local military circle while disguising his aims.

He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, cultivated a respectable image, took fencing lessons, and practiced shooting. His disdain for Gilet was evident. His inquiries led him to Fario, who informed him about the Knights of Idleness and their schemes. Suspecting Gilet and Flore planned to secure Rouget’s income and flee, Philippe urged Rouget to take walks to loosen Flore’s influence and told Hochon that Gilet sought power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds; they agreed to watch Rouget’s movements.

When Gilet proposed going to Vatan to secure the money and marry, Flore—fearful of Philippe’s influence—reluctantly consented. Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore honor hardened, setting the stage for a dramatic showdown. Walking with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, Philippe contemplated the looming confrontation and outlined a ruthless plan: he told Rouget that Flore loved Maxence and wanted Rouget out of the way so she could marry him, and insisted he could make her obedient again—by killing Maxence—if Rouget refused to sign a power of attorney before December third that would let them sell Rouget’s assets and flee. Rouget recoiled.

Philippe instructed Mignonnet and Carpentier to arrange banquet seating to force a public confrontation with Gilet, though they warned of Gilet’s dueling skill. Meanwhile Flore briefly left with Vedie—sending Kouski into tears—prompting Rouget’s frantic search. Gilet, urged by Captain Potel to face Bridau, focused instead on transferring assets to Flore to secure control. Aware of these moves, Philippe planned to use Baruch to obtain the power of attorney while distracting Gilet. Rouget drafted a letter threatening to revoke his will in Flore’s favor unless she returned; Philippe encouraged it to force her back. Town gossip swelled about the impending duel and the estate’s fate. When Flore returned, Philippe asserted control and warned her; by morning the town speculated on the duel, and Philippe, confident in his banquet plan, believed it would decide both Rouget’s fortune and his own.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Around four the next afternoon officers of the old army gathered at the place du Marché in Issoudun for a coronation banquet, awaiting Colonel Philippe Bridau. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him. When Max arrived he was coldly received, a sharp contrast to Philippe’s warm welcome, stiffening Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe toasted the Grand Army; Mignonnet toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son. Max, whose reputation carried weight, rose with an ironic wish to meet again next year. Tension rose as Philippe and Max exchanged barbs: Philippe remained composed while Max struggled for control. The duel was set for the next morning with sabres, Goddet, a former army surgeon, to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon urged Philippe to pray; Madame Hochon offered a relic, which he declined. The next morning both men appeared composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance. The duel began: Max was quickly disarmed, Philippe paused to let him recover his weapon, and when they resumed they exchanged fierce blows. Philippe was wounded but delivered a fatal stroke. Max died, ending his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation, noting her illness prevented her from fleeing and intending to use her to his advantage. By February 1823, pressed by others and Philippe’s suggestion that marriage would secure her future, Flore was persuaded to accept a match he had arranged. The contract granted her a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, rejoiced at his apparent reform, though the family lawyer Desroches warned Philippe’s cunning would likely keep Rouget’s property from Joseph and Agathe. Once married, Philippe asserted control: he demanded a power of attorney from Rouget, used Flore to manage her uncle’s affairs while watching her closely, and—backed by local endorsements and letters to the minister of war—was reinstated as lieutenant‑colonel.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he escorted to the Treasury to claim his uncle’s income. Rouget and the Rabouilleuse were absorbed into Parisian extravagance; after a lavish supper with the dancer Lolotte, Rouget overindulged and died.

Madame Rouget prospered while Philippe kept Mariette on guard. In October 1823 he settled the Issoudun estate, securing 1,600,000 francs (excluding paintings held by Monsieur Hochon) and deposited the funds with Mongenod and Sons. He married, protected his wife’s fortune, bought her an apartment, and secretly acquired and renovated a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy.

With Charles X’s rise and by informing on past conspiracies, Philippe was promoted lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment and ennobled Comte de Brambourg. Ambitious for further honors—an aide-de-camp to the Dauphin and a general’s rank—he cut ties with old friends and planned to wed Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges to cement his position.

His brother Joseph, a struggling artist, endured poverty and criticism while their mother Agathe supported him from a lottery office. Philippe’s cold refusal to aid their needy mother hastened her death; Joseph buried her alone. Joseph found solace in friends; when Flore later begged for help, he and Bixiou arranged care and plotted to expose Philippe. Philippe’s fortunes collapsed in the July 1830 Revolution, and Joseph ultimately inherited his estate and achieved success in art, becoming Comte de Brambourg while remaining humble and mindful of the family’s tragedy.
