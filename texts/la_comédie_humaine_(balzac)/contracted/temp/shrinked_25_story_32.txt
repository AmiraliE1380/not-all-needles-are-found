**The Two Brothers**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Monsieur Charles Nodier, a member of the French Academy. This book explores the hidden injustices of domestic life, where divine chance often replaces human justice. It reflects on the decline of paternal authority and the consequences of feminine weakness, urging society to return to the moral foundations of marriage and family.

---

**Chapter I**  
In 1792, the townspeople of Issoudun viewed Dr. Rouget as malicious, believing he made his beautiful wife, Madame Rouget, unhappy. Their son, Jean-Jacques, was born first, followed by their daughter Agathe a decade later, whose unexpected arrival strained family dynamics. The Rougets were connected to the wealthy Descoings family, who profited from the wool trade. Descoings’ brother sought fortune in Paris as a grocer but ultimately failed.

In 1792, Descoings was imprisoned due to political missteps during the Revolution, leading to his execution, which shocked the town and overshadowed the death of poet André Chenier. After Descoings’ demise, Madame Rouget’s health deteriorated, worsened by her husband’s neglect and their son’s indifference. Agathe was sent to Paris to live with her uncle, but her arrival coincided with Descoings’ imprisonment, leaving him unable to care for her.

Fearing she would never see Agathe again, Madame Rouget fell ill. Meanwhile, Agathe’s beauty attracted Bridau, a secretary in the ministry, who married her with Dr. Rouget’s approval. After inheriting the Descoings’ wealth, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life following his wife’s death in 1799, leaving Jean-Jacques unprepared for adulthood.

Agathe, resembling her mother, was a devoted wife and mother, but her ignorance of life’s complexities led to misfortunes. Bridau, loyal to Napoleon, worked tirelessly in the ministry, earning a substantial salary. Their life in Paris was marked by domestic bliss until Bridau’s overwork led to his death in 1808, just before a significant promotion.

After Bridau’s death, Agathe received a pension and scholarships for her sons but remained estranged from her family in Issoudun. Despite warnings from her godmother, Madame Hochon, Agathe did not contest her father’s will, which deprived her of her rightful inheritance. Instead, she and Madame Descoings pooled their resources, living modestly on a combined income.

Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, maintained her youthful appearance and sought to cheer Agathe, often taking her to the theater. However, she harbored a secret about her grandson, Bixiou, whom she raised in secrecy. Agathe, devoted to her children, declined to remarry, but her lack of authority as a mother led to significant challenges.

Madame Descoings’ gambling addiction worsened their financial situation, as she used household funds to support her lottery habit. As debts mounted, Agathe, unaware of the extent of the problem, continued to trust her aunt. Eventually, Madame Descoings confessed her financial troubles to Agathe, who, without reproach, took drastic measures to pay off the debts, selling possessions and cutting expenses.

This chapter sets the stage for the unfolding drama of familial relationships, financial struggles, and the consequences of choices made within societal expectations and personal ambitions, highlighting the complexities of love, loyalty, and the often-hidden burdens within families.

The rue Mazarin, particularly between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, is one of Paris's dreariest areas, dominated by Cardinal Mazarin's college and library. Here, Madame Bridau, a widow, lives on the third floor of a dimly lit building. Opposite her, the Institute trains aspiring artists, known as "rapins," who undergo rigorous examinations for prestigious prizes, though this artistic hub has since moved to the Palais des Beaux-Arts.

Madame Bridau's modest apartment, furnished with only essentials, features small gardens despite police regulations. She chose this location for its affordability (400 francs a year), proximity to her sons' school, and familiarity. The interior reflects her frugal lifestyle, with a sparsely decorated dining room and salon, save for a portrait of her late husband and a vibrant painting of Napoleon.

Since her husband's death, Madame Bridau has become increasingly disheveled and consumed by grief, neglecting her appearance and home. Her devotion to her late husband is evident in her preservation of his belongings, including a pen labeled as his last. With financial struggles looming, she worries about her children, Philippe and Joseph, despite receiving some support from the Emperor.

Nearby, her aunt Madame Descoings also faces financial difficulties but provides assistance to Madame Bridau. The two widows share a close bond, often playing cards with friends from the ministry. Madame Descoings dotes on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small gifts, while Madame Bridau restricts her spending to save for her children's future.

Philippe, the elder son, resembles his mother and exhibits a bold demeanor, preferring physical activities over academics. In contrast, Joseph is more introverted and artistic, struggling with cleanliness and often scolded by his mother. Despite their differences, both boys are loved by their mother, though she tends to favor Philippe.

Joseph's interest in art is ignited when he sees a student drawing a caricature at the Institute. He becomes enamored with the artistic environment and begins visiting sculptor Chaudet's studio, who recognizes his potential. However, Madame Bridau is distressed by her son's aspirations, fearing he will face hardship as an artist.

Despite her objections, Joseph pursues art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Meanwhile, Philippe, inspired by military grandeur, secretly petitions the Emperor to join the army. He is accepted into the Imperial Lyceum and quickly rises through the ranks, earning accolades for his bravery.

As the political landscape shifts following Napoleon's return from exile, both brothers face challenges. Joseph's scholarship is revoked, and he is determined to make a living as an artist, while Philippe, now a captain, returns home to find his family in financial ruin. Their contrasting paths highlight their differing personalities and the complexities of their mother's affections.

Madame Bridau's preference for Philippe grows as he embodies the heroic ideals she admires, while Joseph's artistic pursuits cause her anxiety. Despite this, she remains a loving mother, struggling to understand her younger son’s aspirations. The family dynamics reflect broader themes of ambition, sacrifice, and the pursuit of identity amidst societal expectations.

In 1816, Joseph Bridau transformed his attic into an art studio with support from his mother, Agathe, and financial backing from Madame Descoings. Both widows viewed painting as a legitimate profession. Joseph decorated the studio and continued his studies under Gros and Schinner. Meanwhile, political unrest in France led to conspiracies and riots near the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, Joseph's brother, became involved in these conspiracies, was arrested, and later released, but his military career was jeopardized when he was placed back on active duty.

Agathe worried for Philippe's safety as he frequented disreputable cafes, while the Grecian sages, regular visitors to her home, advised sending him abroad. Despite their concerns, Philippe was determined to serve France and eventually joined General Lallemand in the United States, leading to the disastrous Champ d’Asile venture. Agathe invested ten thousand francs to support him, but upon his return in 1819, Philippe was a changed man, hardened by his experiences and struggling with alcoholism.

Eager to support her son, Agathe bought him new clothes and indulged his drinking habits, despite her suspicions about his character. Philippe's tales of woe masked his selfishness and brutality, traits that had developed during his time away. Agathe remained blind to his flaws, believing he was a victim of circumstance.

Back in Paris, Philippe's behavior alarmed Agathe and Madame Descoings. They faced financial struggles, and Agathe suggested selling their furniture to support him. Philippe assured them he would find work, but he continued gambling and drinking, worsening their financial strain.

Philippe's social life revolved around old army comrades and gambling, becoming increasingly reckless. He formed a bond with Giroudeau, a former dragoon, who introduced him to Paris nightlife. Philippe's ambitions shifted towards pursuing a relationship with Mariette, a young dancer, while still dreaming of political influence.

Despite his failures, Philippe sought to leverage his past for personal gain, planning to demand accountability for the funds raised for the Champ d’Asile. His mother and Madame Descoings, desperate for stability, agreed to secure him a cashier position at a new newspaper, believing it would alleviate their financial woes. As Philippe's situation improved, he promised to contribute to the household, renewing Agathe's faith in him. However, Joseph, feeling overshadowed by Philippe's return, resolved to support himself independently, highlighting the contrasting paths of the two brothers amidst their family's struggles.

Three months later, Colonel Philippe lived extravagantly without supporting his mother and Madame Descoings, who hesitated to remind him of his obligations. His mother believed he was content. Meanwhile, Mariette, a ballet dancer at the Porte-Saint-Martin, gained fame through Philippe and his friends, who were captivated by her.

Bixiou informed Agathe and Madame Descoings of Philippe's infatuation with Mariette, shocking them as they viewed stage women as morally corrupt. Joseph dismissed the idea that Philippe would waste money on her. Bixiou warned that Mariette might attract wealthier suitors, including an ambassador.

Driven by greed, Philippe proposed marriage to Mariette, but she declined, seeking more lucrative prospects at the Grand Opera. As Philippe distanced himself from his family, he indulged in a hedonistic lifestyle, mingling with performers and enjoying Parisian nightlife. However, when Mariette found success, Philippe's finances worsened, leading him to embezzle funds from his job.

After Mariette left for London, Philippe returned home, grappling with despair over his inability to maintain his lifestyle. He confided in Joseph about his suicidal thoughts stemming from his embezzlement and impending disgrace. Alarmed, Joseph urged him to reconsider, suggesting that repayment could avert dishonor.

Philippe fled, leaving his family distressed. Agathe and Madame Descoings were devastated upon learning of his actions, fearing for his life. Despite their anguish, Philippe concealed his theft by claiming he had kept the money for safekeeping.

He then visited a gambling house, initially winning but ultimately losing everything. Returning home, he feigned distress, and his family welcomed him back with relief. Agathe, still shaken, begged him to promise not to harm himself, and Philippe exploited their concern.

In the following days, Agathe visibly aged from worry, realizing their financial situation was dire. Madame Descoings suggested consulting Desroches, a lawyer, to manage the fallout from Philippe's actions. Desroches devised a plan to protect Philippe’s reputation, framing his theft as a result of misplaced trust.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure, gambling, and drinking, while Joseph focused on his art, gaining recognition. Agathe remained preoccupied with Philippe, oscillating between hope and fear regarding his future. Desroches assured them that Philippe would soon be reinstated in the army, but warned that his gambling habits could jeopardize this opportunity.

As Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, Joseph noticed discrepancies in his finances, suspecting Philippe was stealing from him. Madame Descoings confirmed similar suspicions. They resolved to catch Philippe in the act, believing he needed to be confronted about his behavior. Despite their efforts, Philippe's charm allowed him to evade accountability, deepening their struggles. Joseph worried about the impact on their mother and their future, closing the chapter with foreboding about Philippe's potential downfall.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Despite Philippe's cautious gambling, he sometimes found himself "cleaned out." Desperate for his nightly stake of ten francs, he resorted to stealing from his family, including his mother Agathe and Madame Descoings. Agathe, heartbroken, recognized that his thefts indicated a deeper moral failing. After a troubling dream where Philippe robbed her, she urged him to ask for money instead of stealing. However, within two weeks, Agathe was left with nothing and considered finding work to support herself.

As Agathe's health deteriorated from worry, Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, neglecting his appearance and becoming increasingly debauched. Madame Descoings urged him to buy new clothes, but Philippe dismissed her concerns, claiming he had no money. He had even mortgaged his military pension to pay off gambling debts.

On Christmas Eve, Philippe planned to steal from Madame Descoings, who had saved money for lottery tickets. He broke into her apartment while she was out, found twenty napoleons hidden in her mattress, and left without raising suspicion. With this money, he gambled and experienced a lucky streak, winning substantial amounts at the gaming house. However, his greed led him to ignore his own gambling rules, and he ultimately lost everything.

Meanwhile, Agathe and Madame Descoings awaited Philippe's return for Christmas dinner. When he finally arrived, he was drunk, and tensions escalated as Agathe confronted him about his behavior. Philippe, in a fit of rage, dismissed their concerns, even as Madame Descoings suffered from the shock of losing her savings.

The next day, Madame Descoings learned that her lottery ticket had won three million francs, but she was devastated to discover that Philippe had stolen her money before she could pay for her stake. This revelation caused her to suffer a stroke, leaving her incapacitated. Agathe and Joseph were left to deal with the fallout of Philippe's actions, which had brought ruin upon their family.

As Madame Descoings lay ill, Philippe returned home, oblivious to the chaos he had caused. Agathe confronted him about his theft and the pain inflicted on their family. Philippe, however, remained indifferent, believing he was justified. The confrontation escalated into violence, leading to Philippe being restrained due to his delirium.

In the aftermath, Madame Descoings passed away, leaving Agathe with a meager annuity. Philippe, now estranged from his family, continued his reckless lifestyle, showing no remorse. Agathe, heartbroken and defeated, prayed for her son, realizing that her motherhood had become a source of deep sorrow.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into a modest attic room above her former apartment, where her son Joseph aimed to make her comfortable after Philippe's departure. Joseph decorated the room and added a double door for elegance. To manage expenses, he took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hote frequented by respectable patrons, costing nearly a hundred francs a month. Concerned about costs, Agathe secured a job as an assistant in a lottery office, earning seven hundred francs a year, which required long hours.

Despite her new routine, Agathe found solace in its stability. She prepared breakfast for Joseph and kept his studio tidy, though she struggled with the artistic discussions around her. Their bond deepened through small acts of care, even as they remained distant regarding art. One day, Agathe expressed concern for Philippe, prompting Joseph to visit him. Philippe, now a cashier for a newspaper, invited Joseph to dinner, but Joseph declined, prioritizing his mother.

As Philippe posed for a portrait, Agathe secretly observed, filled with hope for their family. However, Philippe's behavior raised Joseph's suspicions, particularly regarding his finances. After a lavish dinner, Agathe's joy turned to worry when Philippe's reckless spending became evident. During another sitting, Philippe boasted of his ambitions but was evasive about his finances.

One evening, Philippe stole a valuable painting from Joseph's studio, believing it to be a copy. When Joseph discovered the theft, he was devastated, and Agathe was heartbroken, realizing the extent of Philippe's betrayal. Her despair deepened upon learning of Philippe's deteriorating condition and need for medical care. Despite Giroudeau's pleas for financial assistance, Agathe refused to help Philippe, feeling betrayed and exhausted.

Months later, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, now a shadow of his former self, recently released from a hospital. Overcome with maternal instinct, she gave him money, despite knowing it would likely be squandered. Her health declined as she grappled with the pain of her son's choices.

When news broke of Philippe's involvement in a conspiracy, Agathe fainted, fearing for his life. Joseph sought legal advice from Desroches, who advised them to secure their family's inheritance from Philippe's influence over their wealthy uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget. Agathe wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, seeking help, but learned that Rouget was unlikely to assist. Desroches urged Agathe to travel to Issoudun to protect her family's interests, prompting her to reflect on the changes in Issoudun and her estrangement from her brother.

Issoudun, one of France's oldest cities, has a rich history and significant commerce, highlighted by archaeological finds like a fifth-century basilica on a Roman temple site. Its name, derived from "Dun," suggests Celtic origins and a long-standing settlement. Historically a wool trade center, Issoudun had a population of around 16,000 in the fourteenth century, but this has declined to about 12,000 today, including vine-growers from surrounding areas.

This decline is largely due to residents' resistance to change, particularly in viticulture, where outdated practices hinder quality wine production. The local bourgeoisie, clinging to medieval traditions, resisted military presence that could have revitalized the economy, reflecting broader regional trends seen in towns like Bourges.

Issoudun's topography features a canal and fertile meadows, but the social fabric has deteriorated, lacking the class distinctions that once enriched community life. The absence of nobility and military presence has led to a homogenous society, with a local government of familiar faces becoming ineffective, further entrenching the town's inertia.

In the early 19th century, young men in Issoudun formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet, a charismatic figure with a tumultuous past. The group engaged in mischievous pranks that terrorized the town, with Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, endearing himself to the common folk despite bourgeois contempt.

Under Max's leadership, the Knights executed elaborate pranks, often involving townspeople in humorous chaos, leading to the resignation of the sub-prefect due to relentless harassment. This chapter illustrates the interplay between social stagnation, youthful rebellion, and the decline of a once-thriving community, highlighting how the past influences Issoudun's present dynamics.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orleans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank on Pere Fario, an old Spaniard, by hoisting his cart onto the embankment. They later returned to Mere Cognette’s for food and laughter, eager to see Fario's reaction.

The Knights enjoyed leisure activities together, though not every night involved mischief. Max was particularly admired by Madame Hochon's grandsons, Francois and Baruch, who viewed him as a cousin and relied on him for financial support and social experiences.

During a gathering at Mere Cognette’s, Baruch informed Max that Madame Bridau and her son were coming to Issoudun, which could threaten Max's interests in the property of Jean-Jacques Rouget, an old bachelor connected to Flore Brazier, Rouget's servant-mistress. Although Max initially dismissed the concern, he felt unsettled, knowing Rouget's wealth was tied to Flore, and the arrival of the legitimate heirs could jeopardize his plans.

Francois and another friend speculated that if Rouget revoked his will in favor of Flore, it could impact Max's future. However, Max expressed confidence in his relationship with Flore and his ability to manage the situation. He proposed that the Knights assist him in dealing with the Bridaus, clarifying that he did not intend to harm them but wanted to secure his position.

The Knights rallied around Max, pledging their support and loyalty. They toasted to Flore and dismissed the Bridaus, reflecting their relaxed morals. Max's leadership among the Knights was strategic; he aimed to cultivate their loyalty for future support in reclaiming his status.

As the night ended, Max returned home, contemplating the implications of the Bridaus' arrival. He realized Flore had not informed him about her family, which could threaten their financial future. His affection for Flore was intertwined with her potential inheritance from Rouget, and he felt an urgency to act before the Bridaus could secure their claim.

The narrative then shifts to Flore's backstory. Discovered by Doctor Rouget as a child, she was taken in due to her beauty and innocence, receiving a better life than she would have had with her uncle. Under Rouget's care, Flore enjoyed education and luxuries that distinguished her from other girls in Issoudun.

Despite gossip surrounding their relationship, Flore thrived, though townspeople often misinterpreted Rouget's intentions. Her life changed dramatically when Rouget, nearing death, refused to leave her any inheritance, believing her beauty would ensure her future wealth.

This chapter highlights the intertwined lives of Max, Flore, and the Rouget family, setting the stage for the conflicts and dynamics that will unfold as they navigate their relationships and ambitions in Issoudun.

Jean-Jacques Rouget, at thirty-seven, was timid and socially awkward, particularly around women, and did not mourn his father as Flore Brazier did, having gained materially from him. His father had hoped to marry him off but hesitated, fearing a wife's influence. Instead, he instilled avarice in Jean-Jacques, who secretly loved Flore, the only woman in his life.

Flore began to see Jean-Jacques differently after noticing his silence. Over three weeks, their relationship evolved, culminating in a conversation where he expressed his desire for her to stay. Intrigued by his affection, Flore found him less unattractive. Their dynamic shifted as Jean-Jacques, emboldened by his feelings, offered her a place in his life, leading to mutual understanding.

As time passed, Flore took charge of the household, surpassing Jean-Jacques in management and influence. She learned to cook and manage finances, becoming indispensable. Their lives settled into a routine, with Jean-Jacques growing happier under her care. However, Flore's beauty attracted attention, and she eventually fell for Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer, straining her relationship with Jean-Jacques.

Flore's passion for Max led to tension, as she expressed frustrations with Jean-Jacques's perceived stinginess and argued for Max to move in. Despite his reluctance, Jean-Jacques agreed, hoping to maintain her affection. Once Max moved in, dynamics shifted again. Flore adopted a more refined lifestyle, complicating the interplay of love, power, and dependency among the three. Jean-Jacques found companionship in Max, while Flore reveled in her newfound status and influence, illustrating the evolution of relationships shaped by love and social expectations in a provincial setting.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to safeguard her inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had lapsed into a semi-vegetative state, motivated solely by his affection for Flore, which led him to comply with her every demand.

Max, Rouget's companion, had adorned his apartment with provincial luxuries. One morning, Flore found Max asleep after a late night and learned that Rouget's family was coming to claim their inheritance. Concerned, Max advised Flore to act as if she hadn’t heard the news.

A commotion erupted when Fario reported his cart missing. Max feigned indifference but soon gathered a crowd, humorously suggesting the cart had magically ascended the tower, which led to laughter at Fario's expense. Eventually, Max and his friends destroyed the cart, further enraging Fario and complicating Max's situation with Flore.

As tensions rose, Francois informed Max of the Bridaus' imminent arrival. Max opted for manipulation rather than open opposition. Meanwhile, Flore, upset by the news, took her frustration out on Rouget, withholding affection and leaving him confused.

Flore's harsh treatment stemmed from her fear of losing her position. She berated Rouget about his sister's visit, claiming it would disrupt their lives. Rouget, bewildered, insisted he knew nothing of their arrival, blaming Madame Hochon for the news.

Max advised Rouget to welcome his family to avoid scandal, emphasizing the importance of appearances. Flore, recognizing Max's authority, softened and helped prepare breakfast, while Rouget expressed gratitude for Max's support.

After breakfast, Max and Flore discussed financial strategies to secure Rouget's wealth, planning wise investments. Flore admired Max's cleverness, and they plotted to ensure the Bridaus would not interfere with their plans.

Meanwhile, the Hochon household prepared for the Bridaus' arrival. Madame Hochon urged her husband to treat the visitors kindly, fearing repercussions, while Hochon remained skeptical about the Bridaus' intentions. As the day progressed, news of the Bridaus' arrival divided the town, escalating tensions over Rouget's inheritance and shifting power dynamics within the community.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun, where Agathe felt nostalgic. News of their arrival spread quickly, and Madame Hochon welcomed Agathe warmly, having developed a maternal affection for her. The family gathered in the salon for introductions, scrutinizing Joseph, whose appearance sparked unflattering comments about his health and character.

As they settled in, Joseph noted the stark contrast between his artistic life in Paris and the austere provincial lifestyle. Dinner revealed the Hochons' frugality, which Joseph found disheartening. Despite the meager meal, Madame Hochon expressed her affection for Joseph and her hopes for his success as an artist. The evening was filled with gossip about local elites, particularly regarding Maxence Gilet and the Rabouilleuse's influence over Joseph's uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget.

After dinner, the Hochons and the Bridau family discussed the challenges of reclaiming the Rouget property from Max and Flore. Joseph expressed skepticism about their chances against such formidable opposition. The conversation shifted to the upcoming visit to Rouget, where Joseph was encouraged to make a good impression.

The next morning, Joseph spotted his uncle outside, now unrecognizable and under Flore's influence. Agathe was distressed, while Joseph remarked on Flore's beauty despite her role in their family's troubles. The Hochons advised Joseph to visit Rouget alone to discuss family matters without Flore's interference.

A letter from Rouget invited Joseph to dinner, eliciting mixed feelings from the Hochons, who suspected Flore's manipulations. They crafted a response emphasizing Agathe's struggles and Joseph's artistic aspirations, hoping to secure a favorable position in the family dynamics.

As preparations for dinner unfolded, Gritte, the servant, reported on Flore's extravagant display, indicating her desire to impress. The Hochons recognized the need for caution, understanding the complexities of the situation with Max and Flore. Joseph was urged to remain observant and strategic during his visit, as the stakes were high in their quest to reclaim the family legacy.

At four o’clock, Joseph arrived at his uncle Rouget's house, where he met Kouski, Flore, and Maxence Gilet. As a painter, Joseph admired Flore, which pleased Rouget. He and Max exchanged pleasantries about their fashionable appearances.

Flore suggested a walk to see the tower of Issoudun before dinner, and Joseph eagerly agreed. While Flore prepared, Joseph admired the significant paintings in Rouget's home. Gilet mentioned more paintings in the attic, prompting Joseph to explore. Upon returning, Joseph was thrilled about the art, and Flore encouraged Rouget to give the paintings to Joseph, suggesting he could make copies for a fee. Rouget agreed to pay Joseph four thousand francs for the copies, much to Joseph's surprise and delight.

News of Joseph's acquisition spread quickly through Issoudun, enhancing Flore's status. Dinner that evening was lavish, attended by local dignitaries, and Joseph left feeling tipsy and satisfied. Meanwhile, Rouget was carried to bed after indulging heavily in wine.

Max, alone with Flore, discussed their strategy to keep the Bridaus at bay while benefiting from Rouget's wealth. They planned to send the paintings to Joseph while redecorating Rouget's home to distract him. The next day, Joseph awoke to find the paintings had been moved, and his mother, Agathe, visited Rouget to express gratitude for Flore's care.

Old Hochon advised Agathe to cultivate a relationship with Flore to gain influence over Rouget. Despite her efforts, Agathe struggled to speak privately with her brother, now under Flore's influence. Meanwhile, the Knights of Idleness plotted mischief, including releasing rats into Fario's grain store, causing him significant losses.

As the Bridaus struggled to gain Rouget's favor, Joseph received letters from painter Schinner and lawyer Desroches. Schinner invited him to work on a project, while Desroches warned Agathe about Maxence's manipulations, hinting that Rouget might still be swayed by remorse.

Frustrated, Joseph decided to return to Paris. He bid farewell to Rouget, who seemed oblivious to the value of his paintings. Feeling insulted by Flore and Max's manipulation, Joseph returned the paintings to Rouget, asserting his independence as an artist.

The incident quickly became gossip in Issoudun, with exaggerated tales about Joseph's departure. Old Hochon warned Agathe that Joseph's actions might jeopardize their chances of inheriting from Rouget, as Max and Flore were aware of the paintings' true worth. The chapter ends with uncertainty about the Bridaus' future and growing tension with Maxence and Flore.

Between midnight and one o’clock, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs, concluding their escapade around three in the morning. As Max turned a corner, Fario ambushed him, stabbing him before fleeing. Max cried out, prompting Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet to rush to his aid, but the townspeople, accustomed to false alarms, remained indifferent. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario as his assailant, leading Lousteau-Prangin to inform his father, the judge.

Max was taken home, where Goddet, the surgeon, found the knife wound serious but not fatal. Seizing the moment, Max plotted to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter, for the attack. He instructed Flore to spread rumors of his impending death, hoping this would lead to Joseph's arrest and prompt his mother to flee to Paris.

Flore, feigning tears, accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit from his uncle. The townspeople, stirred by her claims, began searching for Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn after a restless night. Alarmed by the crowd's anger, Hochon bolted his door, fearing for his gold. Meanwhile, Agathe, Joseph's mother, fainted upon hearing the news.

As the crowd grew, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, who were convinced of his guilt. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety. He recounted his whereabouts that morning, providing an alibi that could be verified by witnesses.

Initially skeptical, the magistrates agreed to investigate further but decided to detain Joseph for protection. He wrote a reassuring letter to his mother, calming her fears. Meanwhile, Max, recovering from his wound, plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, believing it would rid them of the Parisians.

As the investigation progressed, Max's deception unraveled. Witnesses confirmed Joseph's alibi, and Max's own letter to the judge, detailing the assailant's shoes, further implicated him. Eventually, Joseph was released, warmly greeted by his relieved mother.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph's spirits lifted despite his brother Philippe's ongoing trial for actions during the political upheaval. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and advising caution with Max, who was clever and dangerous. Ultimately, Philippe was released and prepared to confront Max and reclaim his family's legacy.

On November 2nd, All-Souls’ Day, Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun, registering with the local police and lodging on rue l’Avenier. His return as a banished officer linked to a military conspiracy stirred excitement, especially since he was the brother of a wrongfully accused painter. Maxence Gilet, now recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, raising concerns in town. Monsieur Hochon sought information from notary Monsieur Heron about Gilet's actions, worried for the Bridau family.

Gilet and Flore Brazier felt secure with Philippe's arrival, knowing they could manipulate old Rouget for his wealth. Philippe visited his uncle in worn clothing, reflecting his hardships, yet he maintained dignity, refusing financial help from Rouget due to past grievances. Flore, sensing Philippe's hostility, advised Rouget to avoid him.

Philippe later visited the Hochons, introducing himself and expressing his intent to live modestly on his limited income. He engaged in polite conversation, winning over Madame Hochon by discussing her nephew, journalist Lousteau. Aware of the town's gossip, Philippe suggested a private discussion with Hochon to avoid suspicion, aiming to connect with other officers of the old army who opposed Gilet.

Philippe soon acquired a new wardrobe on credit and befriended Mignonnet and Carpentier, fellow officers disapproving of Gilet. He shared insights about the failed conspiracy to overthrow the Bourbons, earning respect among the officers while plotting to undermine Gilet and secure his uncle's fortune.

As Philippe ingratiated himself with the local elite, he maintained a facade of respectability, contrasting with Gilet's dubious reputation. He frequently dined with Mignonnet and Carpentier, solidifying his community position. Meanwhile, Gilet viewed Philippe as a rival.

Philippe discovered that Gilet's associates, including Rouget's grandsons, were involved in dubious activities, which he reported to Hochon. This revelation heightened tensions, as Gilet and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth. Philippe confronted his uncle about Flore's influence, insisting on a private discussion.

After a tense exchange, Philippe met Hochon, who informed him that Rouget had not signed any power of attorney, preventing Gilet from accessing his funds. Philippe planned to monitor his uncle and Gilet closely, determined to protect his family's interests. Alarmed by Philippe's boldness, Flore relayed the details to Gilet, who proposed a risky plan to manipulate Rouget into signing a power of attorney, setting the stage for a confrontation over Rouget's fortune.

As Philippe walked with his uncle Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he contemplated the impending clash between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet, both vying for Rouget's affections and fortune. Philippe expressed disdain for Gilet, revealing his plan to eliminate him to ensure Rouget's happiness with Flore, the woman they both loved. Despite Rouget's infatuation with Flore, Philippe urged him not to sign a power of attorney that would grant Gilet access to his wealth, warning that it was a scheme for Gilet and Flore to marry and leave Rouget destitute.

Rouget, easily swayed by Flore's charms, proved difficult for Philippe to convince. Philippe insisted he would deal with Gilet if necessary, promising to make Flore obedient and devoted to Rouget. Their conversation shifted when they encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, who were aware of Rouget's precarious situation. Philippe concluded that either Gilet must die or he would have to kill Gilet himself to save his uncle.

Meanwhile, Gilet recognized the growing animosity against him in Issoudun due to his reckless behavior. He met with Captain Potel, who urged him to confront Bridau to restore his honor. However, Gilet was preoccupied with securing Rouget's wealth for himself and Flore, planning to transfer the funds to her name before any confrontation.

At Rouget's home, Kouski, under Gilet's orders, feigned sorrow to inform Rouget that Flore had left with Vedie. This devastated Rouget, who searched for Flore in vain. Gilet attempted to maintain his reputation while plotting to keep Rouget under his control.

As rumors of Gilet's misdeeds spread, the townspeople began to turn against him. Potel expressed dismay over the division within the Imperial Guard, now pitted against itself due to Gilet's actions. Feeling cornered, Gilet resolved to confront Bridau, believing he could outmaneuver him in a duel.

Philippe, anticipating Gilet's next move, prepared to counteract his plans, suspecting Gilet would further manipulate Flore and Rouget. When Rouget returned home, he began drafting a letter to Flore, threatening to revoke his will in her favor if she did not return immediately.

Seeing an opportunity, Philippe convinced Rouget to send the letter with Carpentier for personal delivery to Flore, believing this would bring her back and restore Rouget's authority.

As evening approached, Philippe strategized to ensure Flore's compliance and Rouget's happiness. He planned to take Gilet's place in Rouget's life, promising to keep Flore in line. When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting his dominance and warning her of the consequences if she did not comply with Rouget's needs.

The next day, gossip about the impending duel between Philippe and Gilet spread throughout Issoudun, with many speculating on the outcome. Flore, realizing the stakes, began to act more affectionately towards Rouget to secure her position. However, Gilet remained defiant, unwilling to back down from the challenge posed by Philippe. As tensions escalated, the townspeople rallied around Philippe, hoping for a resolution that would restore order and honor to the Imperial Guard, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation that would determine their fates.

The day after the coronation banquet, officers of the old army gathered at Lacroix's restaurant in Issoudun, awaiting Colonel Philippe Bridau. Discussions focused on Max Gilet's recent dismissal from old Rouget's house, with few officers defending him. Tensions rose when Max joined the group, receiving a cold reception compared to Philippe's warm welcome.

During the banquet, Philippe and Max exchanged barbs, aware of their impending duel. Philippe proposed a toast to the Grand Army, which was met with enthusiasm, while Max feigned joviality. After dessert, the duel was confirmed for the next morning, with sabres as weapons. Philippe was visited by Monsieur and Madame Hochon, who urged him to pray and offered a relic for protection. Despite his skepticism, Philippe accepted it, feeling hopeful.

The next morning, Max arrived at the duel site with his seconds, where Philippe awaited him. Both men displayed calmness as they prepared to fight. The duel began with a tense standoff, but Philippe quickly gained the upper hand, disarming Max. In a desperate move, Max attacked fiercely, resulting in both sustaining serious injuries. Ultimately, Philippe delivered a fatal blow to Max, ending the duel.

Max's death marked the end of his ambitions and the influence of the Order of Idleness in Issoudun. As Philippe recovered, he wrote letters detailing his situation and plans regarding Flore Brazier, Max's former lover. He aimed to secure his inheritance and manipulate circumstances surrounding Flore, who fell ill after Max's death.

As Flore recovered, Philippe devised a plan to marry her, believing it would solidify his position and inheritance. He encouraged his mother, Agathe, to support this union, framing it as a means to restore Flore's respectability. Eventually, Flore agreed to marry Rouget, influenced by Philippe's manipulations. The marriage contract was signed, securing Flore a substantial dowry and life annuity. Agathe, pleased with her son's apparent transformation, remained unaware of his true intentions, as Philippe's ambition allowed him to solidify control over Rouget's estate.

Lieutenant-Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, facilitating the transfer of Rouget's income to himself. Philippe indulged them in a lavish lifestyle filled with actresses and artists, leading to Rouget's demise after a night out, attributed to indigestion. Philippe's aunt, Madame Rouget, was monitored by Mariette to prevent excessive indulgence.

In October 1823, Philippe returned to Issoudun to settle his uncle's estate, netting 1.6 million francs, which he invested with Mongenod and Sons. In March 1824, he married Madame Rouget, securing her fortune in case of her death without children. He purchased a furnished apartment for her and a grand hotel on rue de Clichy, investing heavily in renovations and art.

With Charles X's accession, Philippe gained favor and was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse's regiment. He leveraged his connections to secure a noble title, becoming Comte de Brambourg, and sought to marry Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges. His ambition led him to request ennoblement, which was granted due to his social standing.

By January 1827, Philippe had risen to the Royal Guard and sought further honors, including a position as aide-de-camp to the Dauphin. He distanced himself from his past, refusing to associate with old friends who might tarnish his reputation. Philippe aspired to marry into high society and become a general.

Meanwhile, Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who managed a lottery office. Despite Joseph's talent, he faced financial difficulties and criticism, overshadowed by Philippe's success. Agathe, proud of Philippe's status, remained unaware of Joseph's struggles.

Agathe eventually wrote to Philippe for financial help for Joseph but received a cold response that devastated her. This led to her declining health, and she sought solace from her confessor, realizing her misplaced affections for Philippe over Joseph. As she lay dying, she expressed regret for not loving Joseph as he deserved.

Joseph, heartbroken by his mother's condition, refrained from mentioning Philippe during her illness. Agathe's death left him alone, and he wrote a bitter letter to Philippe, blaming him for their mother's demise. Philippe, feeling the weight of his actions, distanced himself further from the family.

After Agathe's funeral, Joseph learned of Philippe's indifference and the dire state of his sister-in-law, Flore, who was suffering from neglect and illness. Joseph, along with friends, sought to help Flore, uncovering the extent of Philippe's cruelty towards her. They arranged for her care in a hospital, but she ultimately succumbed to her hardships.

Philippe, now wealthy, faced societal scrutiny and rumors about his past. His ambition led to financial ruin during the July Revolution of 1830, where he lost most of his fortune. He returned to military service, hoping for glory but met a tragic end in battle in 1839.

Joseph, having married well, inherited Philippe's estate and continued to paint, gaining recognition but never achieving the fame he desired. Despite his struggles, he found solace in his art and friendships, ultimately becoming Comte de Brambourg, a title he found ironic given his brother's legacy.
