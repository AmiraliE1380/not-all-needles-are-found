**Summary of "Two Poets" by Honoré De Balzac**

*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy that follows Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in the provincial town of Angoulême. The narrative begins during a time when traditional printing methods were still in use, with Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman, now running a small printing establishment. Despite lacking formal education, Séchard acquired a printing license during the French Revolution, enabling him to operate his business.

Séchard inherited the printing house after the previous owner's death and navigated the political landscape by collaborating with M. le Comte de Maucombe to print decrees while avoiding the era's dangers. Over time, Séchard transformed from a struggling pressman into a shrewd businessman, motivated by greed and a desire to secure a future for his only son, David.

David Séchard excelled in his studies and was sent to Paris to learn the printing trade. However, his father held a deep contempt for book learning, believing practical experience was more valuable. David returned home in 1819, ready to take over the family business, which had enjoyed a monopoly on official printing in the region. Competition arose when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's established business.

Now aging and increasingly greedy, Jérôme-Nicolas was eager to pass the business to David but also wanted to profit from the transaction. He viewed his son not just as an heir but as a rival, straining their relationship. Séchard's cunning overshadowed paternal affection as he prepared to negotiate the sale, intending to sell high while David aimed to buy low.

The printing house mirrored Séchard's character—chaotic yet functional, with a layout that often led to accidents for visitors. It included a workshop filled with tools, a kitchen, and stark living quarters, reflecting Séchard's avarice.

As the story unfolds, the dynamics between father and son grow increasingly complex, highlighting themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of business. Séchard's obsession with profit complicates their relationship, setting the stage for conflict as David seeks independence and navigates the challenges of the printing industry.

In summary, *Two Poets* explores the struggles of a father-son relationship against a changing society, emphasizing the tension between personal ambition and familial loyalty. The characters are shaped by their environment, and their interactions reflect broader societal themes of competition, greed, and the pursuit of success.

The sitting-room of the late Mme. Séchard was only partially modernized, featuring powder blue wainscoting and Oriental wallpaper but lacking essential decor. Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, inebriated, brought his son David to review a valuation of the family printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted the presses were reliable and had served him well for twenty years, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery.

In the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, boasting about their durability and quality. However, David recognized the equipment's outdatedness and questioned the valuation, particularly the inflated worth of the type. Despite Séchard's insistence on the tools' quality and potential profits, David felt trapped in an unfair negotiation.

Sensing David's hesitation, Séchard grew anxious, believing a lack of argument indicated acceptance. He highlighted the value of the business license and income from advertisements, urging David to see the profit potential. Ultimately, David agreed to terms that included a partnership allowing Séchard to retain a room in the building and share profits until the purchase was fully paid.

After moving in, David faced financial strain as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, insisting David manage alone. This led to a painful realization for David about his father's greed and lack of support, overshadowing any paternal concern.

Meanwhile, Séchard, now living in the countryside, became increasingly anxious about the business's success. He frequently visited David, who struggled to keep the printing house afloat amidst competition from the Cointet Brothers, who were gaining market share by slandering David and monopolizing local contracts.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, blossomed as they shared aspirations. Lucien's family had fallen into poverty after his father's death, and he was eager to escape his circumstances. David offered him a position in the printing house, hoping to help him find a path to success.

As their friendship deepened, they discussed innovative ideas for the printing business, but their intellectual pursuits distracted them from practicalities. The Cointets, recognizing David's lack of business acumen, allowed his operation to continue in a diminished capacity, ensuring it wouldn't threaten their growing enterprise.

The physical state of the printing house reflected Séchard's avarice, as he neglected repairs and maintenance. The building was dilapidated, and the workshop was cluttered and grimy, indicative of the struggles faced by David and Lucien as they navigated challenges while contending with familial and financial pressures.

In summary, the narrative explores themes of familial obligation, the clash between tradition and modernity, and the struggles of young men trying to forge their paths in a competitive, economically challenging world. David's relationship with his father is fraught with tension as he grapples with inherited expectations and the desire for independence, while his friendship with Lucien offers a glimmer of hope amidst adversity.

In May 1821, David and Lucien, two aspiring poets, sought refuge in a yard from the oppressive atmosphere of their printing office. David, a robust yet melancholic figure, contrasted with Lucien, whose delicate beauty resembled that of a youthful Bacchus. While David represented the struggles of obscurity, Lucien, with his Gascon temperament, was ambitious and adventurous, often reflecting on society's darker aspects.

Their friendship flourished over three years as they immersed themselves in literature and art, sharing dreams of future fame. David admired Lucien's brilliance, while Lucien aimed to elevate both of their social standings. One day, David excitedly presented a book of poetry by André de Chénier, which deeply inspired them and reignited their aspirations.

As they pursued their artistic ambitions, a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, introducing himself as an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Although Lucien's feelings for her were intense, he recognized the social barriers between them and vowed not to pursue her unless David was welcomed too, demonstrating his loyalty.

After Lucien departed, David reflected on their bond, feeling admiration mixed with concern for Lucien's romantic ambitions. Lucien wandered through Angoulême, contemplating the societal divides between the upper town, home to the nobility, and L’Houmeau, the bustling suburb where he lived. The old city, perched on a crag, felt stagnant, while L’Houmeau thrived with commerce, creating a rivalry between the two areas.

The upper town's old families maintained strict social boundaries, viewing outsiders with disdain. This exclusivity intensified during the Restoration, further alienating the bourgeoisie from the nobility. Lucien's aspirations to rise above his humble origins were complicated by entrenched class prejudices in Angoulême, where even a connection to a woman like Mme. de Bargeton could be revolutionary.

Mme. de Bargeton, an admirer of literature and the arts, significantly influenced Lucien's ambitions. Her interest in him was both a blessing and a curse, placing him at the center of a social conflict that could either elevate or destroy him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David remains a steadfast friend, embodying loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, a descendant of a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, saw his family's fortunes decline due to the extravagance of his grandfather, M. de Bargeton the Waster. By 1789, the family retained only seignorial rights to rents from their Angoumois lands, totaling ten thousand francs annually. M. de Bargeton married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an old but now obscure family. Her father, M. de Nègrepelisse, was a miser who relied on his wife’s small estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who instilled in her a love for music and literature.

Anaïs, or Naïs, developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for those around her, leading to a haughty demeanor. Her enriching education fostered a sense of superiority that made her ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, Naïs confronted her father's miserly nature and her own rebellious spirit against marriage constraints. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a suitable husband for her, ultimately choosing M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect but manageable.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. Over time, Naïs's once-vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. She grew increasingly isolated, longing for excitement beyond her provincial existence.

Her disappointing marriage left her yearning for love and connection. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow. This loss cast a shadow over her life, and as she faced aging without fulfillment, her pride kept her from engaging in the mundane affairs of provincial men.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her intellectual gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, introduced a new dynamic to her life. He ingratiated himself into her circle, recognizing her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of his presence in their elite society.

M. du Châtelet’s background made him an intriguing figure, and he sought to cultivate a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing in her both a source of personal gain and a means to elevate his own status. His arrival disrupted the social order, as he was an outsider welcomed into a previously exclusive space. The contrast between his ambitions and provincial norms highlighted the tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with remnants of decayed gentility.

In summary, Mme. de Bargeton’s life reflects the struggles of a woman caught between her aspirations and the limitations imposed by her environment, ultimately leading to profound isolation and unfulfilled potential.

Political discourse was often verbose and insincere, with Louis XVIII viewed as a Jacobin. Women in society were generally awkward and poorly presented, diminishing their appeal. Châtelet found some solace in this environment due to his interest in Mme. de Bargeton. Despite their flaws, the nobility possessed a charm and tradition that set them apart from the bourgeoisie. Their loyalty to the Bourbon dynasty resembled a form of faithfulness, and their exclusivity added to their perceived value.

Some women, flattered by Châtelet, admired qualities in him that were lacking in their own men, which boosted their egos. They aspired to be close to the Imperial Highness. Châtelet endured rudeness but maintained his position by engaging with the clergy and indulging the queen of Angoulême's whims, sharing literature and poetry with her. Mme. de Bargeton, enamored with the Bourbons' resurgence, longed for Paris's artistic brilliance. Châtelet, believing he had found a local talent, introduced her to Lucien, a young poet from L’Houmeau, whose potential he exaggerated.

Lucien, unaware of social barriers, was excited about visiting the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Eve, supportive and resourceful, prepared him for this significant occasion with new clothes and advice on aristocratic behavior. As Lucien approached the house, he felt a mix of excitement and trepidation, envisioning a world far removed from his humble beginnings.

Upon entering, Lucien found the Hôtel de Bargeton less grand than expected, yet charming. He met Mme. de Bargeton, who was captivated by his youthful beauty and diffidence. Their initial meeting was enchanting, and Lucien was intoxicated by her presence. Mme. de Bargeton, impressed by Lucien, saw him as a poetic embodiment of her aspirations.

Châtelet, sensing a rival in Lucien, attempted to assert dominance through condescension. Despite this, Lucien continued to visit Mme. de Bargeton, growing more comfortable in her presence. Initially grateful for the nobles' attention, he soon recognized their patronizing attitudes, fueling his resentment towards the aristocracy.

Lucien's infatuation with Mme. de Bargeton deepened as she treated him with a mix of affection and aloofness. She encouraged his ambitions, presenting herself as a mentor while grappling with her own feelings. Their relationship oscillated between admiration and jealousy, with Lucien striving to prove himself worthy of her affection.

As their bond grew, Lucien began to express his feelings more openly, leading to moments of intimacy that thrilled and terrified him. Mme. de Bargeton, torn between her social standing and feelings for Lucien, struggled with the implications of their relationship. Societal scrutiny intensified, leading to gossip about their connection.

Despite challenges, Mme. de Bargeton defended Lucien against societal backlash, arguing that genius transcends social class. She hosted gatherings to showcase his talent, drawing both admiration and criticism from the local aristocracy. Lucien's rise in prominence elicited mixed reactions, with some nobles resenting his presence while others celebrated his potential.

Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while trying to win over Mme. de Bargeton. He organized a dinner to elevate Lucien's status, hoping to manipulate the situation. However, Lucien's talent shone through, garnering praise and complicating the dynamics among the three.

As Lucien navigated this new world, he became increasingly aware of the sacrifices required for success. Mme. de Bargeton encouraged him to embrace his ambitions, suggesting he adopt a noble name to solidify his status. This idea ignited Lucien's desire for recognition and prestige, prompting him to reconsider his values and aspirations.

In a moment of vulnerability, Lucien penned a heartfelt letter to Mme. de Bargeton, expressing his hopes and dreams while seeking her support for his friend David. This letter revealed his inner turmoil and desire for validation, showcasing the complexities of his character as he balanced ambition with loyalty.

Ultimately, Lucien's journey was marked by the tension between his aspirations and the realities of social hierarchy. His relationship with Mme. de Bargeton served as both inspiration and a reminder of the obstacles he faced in his pursuit of greatness. As he grappled with his identity and ambitions, Lucien's story unfolded against a backdrop of societal contradictions and challenges.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by thoughts of whether the esteemed Mme. de Bargeton was angry with him and if she would receive his friend David. He recognized the vast social distance between himself and the aristocracy, realizing that David could not easily bridge that gap. Unbeknownst to Lucien, accepting David into their circle could tarnish Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation, as mingling with lower classes was frowned upon in their society.

Despite his ignorance of these social dynamics, Lucien's aristocratic instincts warned him of potential ridicule directed at David, who lacked the refined appearance and manners that Lucien possessed. This realization led him to reconsider his initial impulse to introduce David to Mme. de Bargeton, and he began to regret having invited David to meet her, envisioning the consequences of such a move.

Lucien reminisced about his simple life, his family, and his love for Mme. de Bargeton. He believed it would be more admirable to achieve success through his own efforts rather than relying on a woman’s favor, inspired by Napoleon’s rise to greatness through merit. Lucien resolved to pursue his ambitions independently, feeling ashamed of his previous thoughts.

Returning to L’Houmeau, Lucien was discontented with his humble surroundings, especially as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton. He longed to escape the confines of his father's pharmacy, which reminded him of his family's modest status. His father, M. Postel, often reminisced about Lucien’s late father, a chemist with a secret remedy for gout, deepening Lucien's sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton, he was ecstatic. The letter expressed her willingness to help David but also revealed her prejudices against those of lower social standing. Lucien interpreted this as a victory, believing that David would shine in her company. His sister Eve noticed his joy and teased him about his apparent happiness, sensing it was connected to a romantic interest.

Meanwhile, David, deeply in love with Eve, struggled with his feelings. He admired her from afar, believing his humble background made him unworthy of her affection. Despite his talent and potential, he felt trapped by societal expectations and his insecurities. David was determined to support Lucien’s ambitions, even at the cost of his own desires.

As Lucien prepared for his public reading, David advised him to choose a different poem, fearing that his biblical work might not resonate with the audience. Lucien, filled with confidence, was eager to impress both the audience and Mme. de Bargeton.

Eve and David shared a tender moment, both feeling a deep connection but hesitant to express their feelings openly. Their love blossomed quietly, marked by mutual respect and admiration. David’s modesty and Eve’s gentle nature created a bond that neither fully understood yet.

At the reading, Lucien was nervous but determined to make an impression. He was aware of the social dynamics at play, particularly the presence of M. de Bargeton, who was oblivious to the subtleties of conversation and social interaction. M. de Bargeton’s limited intellect and reliance on his wife for social navigation highlighted the disparities between the characters.

As Lucien navigated the evening, he felt torn between his ambitions and the loyalty he owed to his family and friends. David’s selflessness and Eve’s quiet strength reminded him of the love and support surrounding him, even as he sought to carve out his identity in a world that often judged based on social standing.

Ultimately, Lucien’s journey reflects the complexities of ambition, love, and societal constraints that shape individual destinies. His relationships with David and Eve illustrate the tension between personal aspirations and the bonds of family and friendship, setting the stage for the challenges he would face in his pursuit of success.

Despite the lack of engagement, Lucien pressed on, driven by a desire to connect with his audience. The evening culminated in a request for Lucien to recite his own verses, a moment that felt both daunting and necessary for him to reclaim his dignity.

The poem, rich in imagery of angels and divine love, was met with indifference and sarcasm from the aristocratic crowd, who dismissed it as typical youthful verse.
