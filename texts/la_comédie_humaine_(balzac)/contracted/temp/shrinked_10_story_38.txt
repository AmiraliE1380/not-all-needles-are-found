"Two Poets" is the first part of a trilogy about Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême. Set in the era of traditional printing, it centers on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman who runs a small printing business. He obtained a license during the French Revolution, allowing him to operate without formal education.

After inheriting the press, Séchard partnered with M. le Comte de Maucombe to print decrees, transforming from a struggling workman into a shrewd businessman driven by greed and a desire to secure his son David's future. David, academically gifted, was sent to Paris to learn the trade; his father valued practical experience over formal study. Returning in 1819, David was set to inherit the business, which enjoyed a monopoly on official printing. That dominance was threatened when the Cointet Brothers sought a second license.

As Séchard aged, his avarice deepened, complicating his relationship with David, whom he saw as both heir and rival. This tension underscores themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of commerce amid social change.

In the late Mme. Séchard's sitting room, a drunken Séchard presented David with a valuation of the printing house. David was shocked to see the equipment inflated at sixteen hundred francs; the presses were antiquated and unfit for modern work. Séchard insisted on their reliability and dismissed David's preference for newer machinery.

In the workshop, Séchard proved the presses could run, but David recognized their obsolescence and felt trapped in an unfair bargain. Séchard, sensing David's reluctance, grew anxious and extolled the business's profit potential. Reluctantly, David agreed to terms that let Séchard keep a room and share in profits until the purchase was complete.

After moving in, David strained under finances as Séchard refused to help with operating costs, revealing the depth of his father's greed. Séchard became increasingly anxious about business success, pestering David, who also faced pressure from the rival Cointet Brothers.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, deepened as they shared aspirations. David offered Lucien work in the printing house to help him along. Their bond grew through discussions of art and invention, though often at the expense of practical concerns. The decrepit press mirrored Séchard's avarice and the struggles of both young men.

In May 1821, seeking escape, David and Lucien immersed themselves in literature and dreams of fame. Their artistic focus shifted with the arrival of a stranger bearing a manuscript, claiming to be sent by Mme. de Bargeton, the woman Lucien admired. Despite his passion, Lucien understood the social barrier separating them and vowed not to pursue her unless David would be accepted too.

Wandering Angoulême, Lucien contemplated social divides and the risks of ambition. Influenced by Mme. de Bargeton, his talents put him at the center of a conflict that might either elevate or ruin him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against society's constraints, while David represents loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, from a once-prominent Bordeaux family, suffered decline after his grandfather’s extravagance, left only seignorial rights in Angoumois yielding ten thousand francs a year. He married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman of obscure lineage. Her father, miserly and dependent on his wife’s estate, had Anaïs educated broadly by Abbé Niollant, who fostered her love of music and literature.

Anaïs developed strong self-regard and contempt for others, a haughtiness ill-suited to provincial life. After the Abbé’s death in 1802 she resisted her father’s parsimony and the marriage he imposed. M. de Nègrepelisse chose M. de Bargeton, older and considered dull, as a suitable husband. Hoping marriage would bring freedom and Parisian connections, Naïs wed him; instead the age gap and limited income confined them to Angoulême. Her vivacity became exaggerated affectation, her artistic talents turned into overwrought displays, and isolation left her craving excitement and true affection. The rise of the Empire briefly revived her hopes; she fell for a young officer whose death in battle deepened her sorrow.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life cycled through unfulfilled dreams and artistic longing. Though gifted, she remained trapped in a narrow existence where dignity slid into pretension. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial social climber, disrupted her routine. He saw her as a useful social asset; the local bourgeoisie distrusted him. Châtelet cultivated a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton to bolster his status, exposing the tensions of Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty and decayed gentility coexisted.

Enamored with the Bourbons’ resurgence and Parisian brilliance, Mme. de Bargeton welcomed Châtelet’s introduction of Lucien, a young poet whose promise he exaggerated. Initially grateful for noble attention, Lucien soon perceived their patronizing attitude. His growing closeness to Mme. de Bargeton brought gratitude, peril, and moments of intimacy that both thrilled and frightened him. She defended him against social censure, arguing that genius transcends class, and hosted salons to showcase his talent, provoking mixed reactions from the local aristocracy.

Feeling threatened, Châtelet sought to undermine Lucien while currying favor with Mme. de Bargeton. Lucien’s emerging talent complicated their triangle and made him acutely aware of the sacrifices and compromises success demanded. Encouraged to adopt a noble name, he found his ambition inflamed by the promise of recognition. Ultimately, Lucien’s path was shaped by the tension between aspiration and rigid social hierarchies: his relationship with Mme. de Bargeton inspired him but also underscored the obstacles to achieving greatness.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he worried about Mme. de Bargeton’s possible anger and whether she would accept his friend David. He recognized the social gulf between himself and the aristocracy, fearing David’s lower status would tarnish Mme. de Bargeton’s reputation. Lucien dreaded ridicule for David, who lacked refinement, and reconsidered the introduction.

Reflecting on his simple life and love for Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien resolved to pursue success on his own, inspired by Napoleon’s merit-based rise. Returning to L’Houmeau, he felt discontent with his humble surroundings as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton; his father’s reminders of their modest status deepened his sense of inadequacy.

When Lucien received a letter from Mme. de Bargeton offering to help David, he was ecstatic, seeing it as a triumph despite her prejudices. His sister Eve teased him, sensing a romantic connection. Meanwhile David, in love with Eve, struggled with feelings of unworthiness because of his background. Despite his talent, he felt trapped by social expectations but remained determined to support Lucien’s ambitions.

Before a public reading, David advised Lucien to choose a different poem, fearing Lucien’s biblical piece might not resonate. Eager to impress, especially with M. de Bargeton present, Lucien felt the strain between ambition and loyalty to his family and friends, aware of their love even as he sought his identity.

At the reading, Lucien faced an indifferent audience. His initial poem received mild applause but little interest, undermining his confidence. He pressed on, driven to connect, but his passionate reading met indifference, leaving him isolated. Infatuated with Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien wrote an ode he believed rivaled Chénier's, yet the aristocratic crowd dismissed it as typical youthful verse. Critics called his work obscure; despite polite replies, the atmosphere was hostile. Stung by rejection, Lucien vowed to prove himself worthy of high society.

In a parallel thread, Eve prepared to meet David, who declared his feelings during a walk and voiced concerns about Lucien’s ambitions and the risks of his liaison with Mme. de Bargeton. Eve remained optimistic, believing they could sustain Lucien until he succeeded. David proposed to Eve, arguing their union would enable him to help Lucien; Eve hesitated because of social differences, but David pledged to work hard for their future.

That evening Lucien returned humiliated and shared his frustrations with Eve and David, asserting his determination to win Mme. de Bargeton. Unaware that Eve’s engagement could complicate his aims, he feared her marriage would hinder his social climb. David’s father opposed the match, deeming Eve unsuitable without wealth, yet David remained committed to her and determined to build a life together.

In Angoulême Lucien’s status rose after the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s response made him a local hero. He eagerly told Louise about Eve’s engagement; she supported his ambitions. Buoyed by Louise’s affection, Lucien began to see himself as significant and asked her to be his Beatrice. Frequenting the Hôtel de Bargeton, he relished new comforts, but rumors about his intimacy with Louise spread. The disparity in societal expectations became evident: some moved freely while others faced scrutiny.

David prepared for marriage to Eve, improving their home with his savings. Lucien, now styling himself M. de Rubempré, felt optimistic about his writing, believing his family’s sacrifices earned him success. However, rival Châtelet began spying on Lucien and Louise, hoping to catch them compromising themselves.

Châtelet’s schemes culminated in a scandal when he and Stanislas claimed to have seen Lucien and Louise in a compromising position. The rumor spread quickly as townspeople dissected the situation. Mme. de Bargeton urged her husband to confront Stanislas for slander, and M. de Bargeton, defending his wife’s honor, prepared to challenge Stanislas to a duel.

The next morning news of the duel spread. Mme. Chardon informed Lucien that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, who would suffer lasting injury. Public sentiment turned against Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton. A note from Louise urged Lucien to avoid public appearances, hinting at her distress after the duel.

Preparations for Eve’s wedding went on, but Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire to be with Louise. Captivated by her plans to move to Paris, where they could thrive socially and artistically, she urged him to seize opportunities in the capital. Initially hesitant because of his sister’s marriage and his obligations, Lucien was moved by Louise’s passionate argument about their potential future.

Despite excitement, Lucien grappled with guilt about abandoning his family. Louise’s disdain for his hesitation intensified his internal conflict, leading to a heated exchange that left him distressed. As he prepared to leave, he sought financial support from his family and was met with heartbreak; they recognized the necessity of his ambitions. David offered financial help despite the strain it would cause.

Ultimately Lucien’s departure was bittersweet. He felt the weight of his family’s sacrifices and the uncertainty of his future in Paris, but he set out with David, aware of the challenges ahead and the potential for greatness.
