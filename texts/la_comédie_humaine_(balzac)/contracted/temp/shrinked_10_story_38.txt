At the story’s outset the Stanhope press and ink-distributing roller were not yet common in provincial shops. In Angoulême, tied to Parisian typography by its paper mills, only wooden presses were used: “the press groans” is literal—leather ink-balls still applied the ink and the pressman inked by hand. This old mechanism, cherished by Jérôme‑Nicolas Séchard, is central to the tale.

Séchard, once a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793 and became sole worker after his master’s death. Illiterate, he nonetheless obtained a master‑printer’s license from a Representative of the People and bought the business cheaply. He partnered with the noble M. le Comte de Maucombe, who helped print decrees while avoiding official attention. By 1795, after the Terror, Séchard needed a compositor and hired an Abbé who later became a bishop. Though uneducated, he learned to run the business, mastering costs and profits; his avarice grew as he bought paper when prices fell, became his own landlord, and acquired the old printing house.

He had one son, David, sent to grammar school as a hoped‑for successor. David excelled; despite Séchard’s scorn for book learning, he economized while studying in Paris and finished without burdening his father. The printing house held a regional monopoly on official printing, but the Cointet Brothers sought a second license, threatening Séchard’s position. Confident in David, Séchard anticipated retirement.

Now in his seventies and drinking since his wife’s death, Séchard’s bloated face, cunning gaze, three‑cornered hat and old‑fashioned clothes matched his habits. When David returned, Séchard feigned warmth but pushed business matters, seeing him as both heir and rival. The long‑established shop—a large room with a glass door to the workshop—drew customers who watched the process. The vine‑grown yard, kitchen where inky runoff mixed with household waste, and the cramped, unchanged living quarters above reflect the old printer’s stinginess and relentless pursuit of profit.

The sitting‑room, partly modernized by the late Mme. Séchard with powder‑blue wainscoting and Oriental paper, was sparsely furnished—lyre‑backed chairs, bare arched windows, and unfinished plans: no clock, sconces, or mirror, since Mme. Séchard had died before completing them. Jérôme‑Nicolas, practical and profit‑minded, left it as it was.

He brought his son David in, pointed to a valuation of the printing plant, and had him read the inventory aloud. David scoffed at the old presses and tossed the paper; Séchard, affronted, led him downstairs and briskly demonstrated the presses’ efficiency. He challenged David to name an English press to match his, bragged they could earn enough without a foreman, and dismissed newer machinery as ruinous to type. David doubted the high price placed on the type, which Séchard defended—insisting it had been cast at M. Vaflard’s foundry and, after twelve years, was nearly new. The inventory totaled thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill. David, remembering his father had once bought the business for ten thousand francs, found the asking price exorbitant and hesitated about whether he could afford it.

Séchard, who preferred argument to silence, pressed the point: the license alone brought income from advertisements and work, and attached to the valuation was a partnership deed—Séchard would rent the premises to David for twelve hundred francs a year and reserve a room for himself. Cornered, David calculated the license and goodwill’s worth and, thinking he might manage the debt, accepted the deal, to his father’s surprise.

The next day Séchard had the apprentice move David’s belongings to the attic, leaving him three empty rooms and no funds to pay wages. When David asked for help, Séchard refused, saying he had already paid his share. Humiliated, David saw himself alone; his father intended to profit. He mentioned his mother’s fortune, but Séchard dismissed it as merely beauty and intelligence and warned any legal claim would be long and costly. Resolving to work hard, David accepted his situation.

Séchard spoke then of a “treasure” he left behind—Marion, a capable country girl who ran many tasks in the shop—and departed for his Marsac vineyard, fretting over the thirty thousand francs and making periodic visits, suspecting David’s mismanagement as the Cointet Brothers gained ground.

David, indifferent to politics, struggled for work. The Cointets, aligned with the clergy, monopolized the market while David’s business dwindled; they smeared him as a Liberal and untrustworthy because of his father. As they prospered, the Cointets offered to buy the printing house. Séchard, fearing the loss of their link to Liberal clientele, at first urged David to keep the paper and even negotiated what he framed as a protective price of sixty thousand francs. Ultimately David was forced to sell, marking the Séchard establishment’s decline. Séchard grew more detached, visiting less often though still attached to the old shop.

David’s fortunes worsened when Lucien Chardon, an old school friend reduced to poverty after his father’s death, sought work. David made him a printer’s reader. Their friendship deepened: Lucien dreamed of being a poet; David, of studying natural science. Lucien recounted his father’s schemes for refining sugar and making paper, which intrigued David and suggested other ventures.

But these ambitions distracted them from the shop’s immediate needs. While they indulged plans and conversation, the Cointet Brothers consolidated their advantage. Séchard & Son scraped by, the building and workshop fell into disrepair, and the household’s strained fortunes showed in the neglected, grimy premises.

One day in May 1821, David and Lucien waited by the window until the men went to dinner. Uncomfortable in the office, David led Lucien outside to sit under the vines where sunlight heightened their differences. David was robust, fighter-built, swarthy and weathered—shaped by humble origins and a melancholy reserve. Lucien was graceful and beautiful, with a refined profile and striking blue eyes; delicate in appearance but ambitious and shrewd, willing to view society pragmatically and even consider dubious means to rise.

Both men were low in social standing but nourished higher ideals, which made them bitter when reality intruded. Lucien, impetuous and adventurous, usually led; David, stronger but more introspective and cautious, admired Lucien and often yielded to his charm, content to be the more ordinary companion. For three years their friendship thrived on literature and art: they read voraciously, worked hard, and dreamed of fame as escape from hardship.

One day David brought a new volume of André de Chénier’s poetry. The verses moved them, offering solace and kinship with the poet’s struggles and hope of transcendence. Their reverie was broken by Cérizet, an apprentice who brought a stranger seeking to print a monograph. Lucien eagerly engaged the author, who mentioned Mme. de Bargeton—a woman Lucien admired. He blushed, thanked the author, and confessed to David his passionate love. David cautioned him about social barriers, but Lucien hoped love could bridge them and vowed not to visit Mme. de Bargeton without David’s welcome—a pledge showing both loyalty and pride.

Walking through Angoulême, Lucien reflected on the gulf between his origin and Mme. de Bargeton’s world. The city, perched on a crag, was split between the upper town of nobility and the lower town of commerce. The upper class clung to old values, resisted outsiders and reform, and looked down on merchants; the bourgeoisie resented this disdain. Despite modernization efforts, the hierarchy remained rigid, breeding tension and the prospect of social upheaval as the middle class pressed for recognition.

In that climate, Lucien’s ambition to enter Mme. de Bargeton’s circle was daunting. His background as a druggist’s son marked him an outsider, yet his literary talent, charm, and Mme. de Bargeton’s artistic tastes gave him hope. David’s mixture of admiration and concern captured their friendship: he understood Lucien’s depth but saw the obstacles. Lucien’s determination and his pledge to include David in any future visits encapsulate their bond as love and ambition unfold against entrenched divisions and shifting fortunes.

M. de Bargeton descended from a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, but the family declined after his grandfather, “the Waster,” squandered its wealth. By 1789 he possessed only entailed lands and a house in Angoulême with modest rents. In 1805 he married Marie-Louise-Anaïs (Naïs), from a once-prominent family whose father lived frugally on his wife’s estate, farming and distilling brandy.

During the Revolution the Abbé Niollant taught Naïs languages and music, fostering independence and a certain haughty superiority. Her father’s thrift and provincial seclusion reinforced this outlook; she resisted marriage, valuing autonomy over submission. Seeking a husband who would not challenge her intellect, her father chose M. de Bargeton: well bred but limited in mind. Naïs accepted, intending to pursue social ambitions within the marriage despite their age gap—she thirty-six, he fifty-eight.

Settled in Angoulême, they awaited Naïs’s inheritance and a hoped move to Paris. Instead a modest income and petty provincial life stifled her intellectual yearnings and produced theatrical affectations, turning noble feelings into exaggeration. Longing for love, she had been moved by the Empire’s heroes and loved a young officer whose death left her heartbroken.

Years of solitude hardened dignity into affectation—until M. du Châtelet arrived. Charming, ambitious, and disruptive, he used music to ingratiate himself and eyed Naïs as a potential wealthy widow. His presence scandalized the local elite, who defended her salon; she welcomed the excitement, which exposed tensions of ambition, love, and provincial expectation.

Political opinion was loud and exaggerated: the Quotidienne showed only tepid loyalty and Louis XVIII was mocked as a Jacobin. Society women were awkward and ill-spoken; in the countryside a lingering nobility and old breeding masked many defects, and loyalty to the Bourbons remained a proud provincial badge.

Châtelet, seeking solace in society because of his interest in Mme. de Bargeton, flattered many women and embodied what their husbands lacked; all coveted his proximity. To keep his place he endured insolence, courted the clergy, catered to the queen of Angoulême’s petty tastes, and shared new literature. Mme. de Bargeton—Naïs—enchanted by the Bourbon revival yet craving Parisian genius, learned from Châtelet of a promising young poet in Angoulême.

The young poet Lucien was unaware of the stir. Praised by his headmaster, he expected a warm reception at the Hôtel de Bargeton. His sister Éve secretly supported him—saving for shoes and clothes and coaching him in aristocratic manners—leaving him proud and anxious about his prospects.

At the Hôtel de Bargeton Lucien found the house less grand than imagined, but Naïs embodied the poetry he loved. She admired his beauty and diffidence; their meeting brimmed with mutual attraction, and Lucien overlooked her faded airs, sensing intimacy.

Sensing a rival, Châtelet asserted influence over Lucien, offering unsolicited advice about society and claiming Naïs. Despite Châtelet’s intimidation, Lucien continued to visit—first flattered by noble condescension, later resentful.

Naïs cultivated Lucien as a project—patroness and confidante. His first love deepened as she addressed him intimately, encouraged his literary ambitions, introduced him to music and books, and revealed vulnerabilities. A portrait of a dead lover provoked jealousy, yet Lucien declared his feelings and their relationship advanced.

When their liaison became public the town buzzed. Châtelet tried to undermine Lucien; Naïs defended him, arguing genius can rise from humble origins. To launch him she hosted salons and urged him to adopt the noble name Rubempré, promising courtly patronage. Exhilarated, Lucien’s aspirations widened; earlier egalitarian ideals gave way to social ambition. In a passionate letter he asked Naïs’s help for a friend, David, revealing his inner conflict between love, artistic sincerity, and the pursuit of recognition.

As Lucien descended from Angoulême he worried that Mme. de Bargeton might be angry and whether she would receive David. He perceived the vast social gulf between a queen and her favorite and knew David could not easily cross it; he did not yet see that receiving David could ruin her by associating her with commoners, an unforgivable breach of social law even for the powerful.

Lucien’s aristocratic instinct sensed danger: noble bearing did not equal noble feeling, and many notable men lacked social polish. David lacked the graces Lucien possessed, and Lucien feared the ridicule his friend would suffer. After a poetic reverie he returned to worldly wisdom, regretting his impulsive letter to Mme. de Bargeton and anticipating the sacrifices success demanded. He preferred to win by his own efforts, inspired by Napoleon’s rise from humble origins, though he felt ashamed of his father’s shop as an obstacle to advancement.

At home M. Postel greeted him, praising Lucien’s grandfather and pricking Lucien’s resentment. Searching the shop, Lucien found Mme. de Bargeton’s reply: she offered to help David but revealed prejudice toward lower social standing. Lucien read it as a triumph, convinced David would shine in her circle.

Éve noticed his elation and hoped for his happiness; Lucien reassured her, though Éve sensed his priorities shifting. David, feeling unworthy of the elevation Lucien sought, decided to decline Mme. de Bargeton’s invitation. He affirmed his commitment to being a printer and emphasized the social distance between them. His humility and loyalty moved Lucien, yet ambition began to eclipse gratitude, drawing Lucien toward advancement more than friendship.

Preparing for his first public reading, Lucien and David agreed to choose a different poem. David and Éve shared a quiet moment hinting at mutual affection. At the reading Lucien met the awkward, ineffectual M. de Bargeton, whose limited intellect and shallow pleasantries underscored the social dynamics Lucien must navigate. The evening, marked by Lucien’s ambition and strained relationships, set the stage for future conflicts and aspirations.

There was talk of nominating the mute gentleman for deputy, but Lucien, who had only briefly visited, had not yet fathomed him. M. de Bargeton, reclining and silent, observed everything with a cool reserve that awed Lucien; imaginative, Lucien fancied him a sphinx and treated him deferentially.

“I am the first comer,” Lucien said, bowing more than usual.

“That is natural enough,” Bargeton replied; Lucien took the remark as a sly barb and became self-conscious. When Lucien asked why those from farther away arrived earlier, Bargeton shrugged and fell silent. Lucien tried to converse—guessing Mme. de Bargeton was dressing, fretting over the room’s preparations and his own attire, and lingering by a Japanese jar for a topic—but Bargeton’s curt answers and intent scrutiny unnerved him.

The old servant announced “M. du Châtelet.” The Baron entered with easy confidence, inspecting Lucien’s clothes and deepening his discomfort. Sixte du Châtelet, with the faded air of a once-stylish beau, dismissed Lucien’s poetry as vaudeville. Mme. de Bargeton soon appeared, elaborately dressed and theatrically charming; Châtelet’s compliments delighted her, while she scarcely acknowledged Lucien.

Guests arrived in a parade of types: the dignified Bishop and his Vicar-General; Mme. de Chandour with her eccentric husband—Amélie posing like a rival queen; the solemn M. de Saintot (Astolphe) and his austere wife Élisa, who affected scientific airs; M. de Bartas, craving praise for his singing; the artist Alexandre de Brébian and his wife; fashion-obsessed Lolotte and Fifine with their dowdy husbands. The Comte de Senonches came with Zéphirine, tall and striking despite ill health; Francis, a devoted family friend who ran their household, appeared unusually attentive to Zéphirine and provoked speculation. The wealthy Marquis de Pimentel and his wife arrived with the Baron and Baroness de Rastignac, drawing envy as Mme. de Bargeton lavished them with attention.

The Prefect and the General came last, followed by a nervous country gentleman who awkwardly brought a treatise on silkworms seeking listeners. Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard, noble but impoverished, hovered about, pressing social connections with thinly veiled desperation; M. de Séverac, a childless widower, spoke of silkworm nurseries while Mme. du Brossard pushed her daughter’s prospects.

Throughout the evening Lucien felt increasingly scrutinized and diminished. Most called him M. Chardon rather than M. de Rubempré, a slight that stung his pride. The assembly seemed absorbed in its own interplay of flattery and rivalry rather than his art, leaving him isolated.

When he read, excitement mixed with anxiety. Announcing poems by the newly discovered André de Chénier, he was misunderstood as affectation. He began with Le Malade to mild applause, but his reading of L’Aveugle fell flat—impatience and inattention showed. Sweating under pressure, Lucien drew courage from Louise’s supportive glance and proceeded to Chénier’s Iambes, which finally captured some attention though many missed the meaning.

During the break Zéphirine sent Francis to inquire after the poems, and the women whispered about Lucien’s origins. Amélie’s remarks about his noble name and printing background sparked gossip that complicated his social standing. Absorbed in his poet’s beauty, Lucien remained largely unaware of the mounting tension and misunderstanding around his reading.

Lucien tried to immortalize his love for Mme. de Bargeton with an ode he fancied rivaled Chénier’s. Presenting it proudly under her protective gaze, he felt secure while she privately fretted for him. The audience, however, was unimpressed: the Baron dismissed the poem as youthful—ornate but empty—while the women feigned admiration, their indifference and sarcastic whispers about Lucien’s ambitions obvious.

Amid the mockery, Francis, a shrewd observer, worked to undermine him by spreading rumors that Lucien’s mother had shaped his verse. The Bishop, unwittingly complicit, praised Lucien; the compliment only intensified the aristocrats’ scorn. Overwhelmed and alienated, Lucien misread people and bore the full weight of their disdain.

As the evening shifted to games and music, Mme. de Bargeton, stung by her peers, withdrew with Lucien to her boudoir. She consoled him, urging endurance for future greatness—romanticizing suffering as the path to immortality and encouraging him to transform pain into art.

Meanwhile Lucien’s sister Eve and her lover David debated his ambitions and the danger of the liaison. David feared Lucien was unsuited to high society and might be ruined; Eve hoped they could sustain him until success. When David proposed, Eve hesitated over their unequal standings but accepted, moved by love and a wish to help Lucien. David vowed to provide so Lucien wouldn’t be alone. Frustrated, Lucien resolved to win Mme. de Bargeton’s love and rise above the aristocrats, torn between aspiration and harsh social realities.

In Angoulême the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s rejoinder made Lucien a local celebrity. Walking through Beaulieu he was envied and flattered. He told Louise of Eve’s impending marriage; she greeted him passionately, praised his reading, and promised a poetic refuge despite the envy around her.

Buoyed by family and friends, Lucien’s self-regard grew. He teasingly asked Louise to be his Beatrice; she promised future love and urged him toward greatness. He confided about David and Eve’s attachment; Louise reassured him their bond transcended family ties. When M. de Bargeton arrived, Louise invited Lucien to dinner and a reading, reinforcing his sense of belonging.

Calling himself M. de Rubempré, Lucien enjoyed Louise’s intimacies and M. de Bargeton’s patronage and expected a literary breakthrough. Eve and David postponed their wedding to support him—charmed by his persuasion, though David feared Lucien might change with status. Their closeness drew Châtelet’s attention; he watched for anything to exploit, intent on exposing them. In the small town, even innocent interactions sparked gossip, and Louise felt the weight of convention.

Determined to declare his feelings, Lucien was repeatedly thwarted by provincial constraints; Louise considered fleeing. Châtelet manipulated perception: an outburst seen by Stanislas was misread and seeded rumor. Gossip spread, Châtelet seized the moment, and Stanislas reveled in the drama. Alarmed, Mme. de Bargeton defended her honor and urged her husband to act. Though insecure, M. de Bargeton resolved to challenge Stanislas; approaching modestly, he won respect at the tense gathering, setting the stage for a confrontation that would entangle lives and expose the town’s fraught mix of love, honor, and social expectation.

Châtelet understood the seriousness of a late-night visit to the bedbound M. de Bargeton and suspected Naïs’s involvement. As household authority he asked Bargeton if he wanted to see Stanislas; relieved to have a mediator, Bargeton was brought to Amélie’s room, prepared for a confrontation that could make Mme. de Bargeton a widow and implicate Lucien.

Châtelet warned Stanislas that Bargeton would challenge him about remarks concerning Naïs and urged decorum. In the room, Bargeton demanded a retraction of remarks about Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré. Cornered, Stanislas accepted a duel and chose pistols.

News of the duel darkened the guests’ mood and divided blame. Stanislas was shaken; Amélie, sensing the tension, fell ill and was removed. The men debated consequences, some recalling Bargeton’s former skill with firearms.

At breakfast the next morning Mme. Chardon arrived alarmed: Bargeton had nearly killed Stanislas, who’d accused him of compromising behavior with Mme. de Bargeton. The grim fact—Stanislas shot in the neck—left him injured. Lucien, hearing the news, defended Mme. de Bargeton’s innocence.

Lucien then received a note from Louise warning him to avoid her home because of the duel’s fallout and urging him to spend the day with her. David, preparing for his own marriage, suggested Lucien limit his visits to Mme. de Bargeton.

Visiting Mme. de Bargeton, Lucien found her at breakfast with her husband and M. de Nègrepelisse, who watched him closely. Afterwards Louise revealed she planned to go to Paris to secure a political post for Bargeton; leaving Angoulême would contain the duel’s consequences.

Louise pressed Lucien to join her in Paris, praising the capital’s opportunities over provincial stagnation. Drawn to that life and to Louise, Lucien hesitated because of his sister Eve’s imminent wedding and family duties. After a heated exchange she fainted; Lucien apologized and, desperate, promised discretion and to be ready to leave with her the next night.

At home Eve and their mother lamented his likely departure and the wedding costs. David overheard, offered help and agreed to endorse a loan, deepening Lucien’s guilt. They arranged to send his belongings to Paris covertly. Mistrustful of Mme. de Bargeton’s departure, Châtelet sent someone to follow her. Early next morning Lucien left with David, who feigned business, as Mme. de Bargeton’s carriage approached—Lucien torn between anticipation and dread.
