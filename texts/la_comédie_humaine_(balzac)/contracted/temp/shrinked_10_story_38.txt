At the story's outset, the Stanhope press and ink-distributing roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. In Angoulême, linked to Parisian typography by its paper mills, only wooden presses were used. “The press groans” was literal: leather ink-balls were still employed and the pressman inked by hand. This old mechanism, cherished by Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, is central to the tale.

Séchard, formerly a journeyman pressman, survived the Requisition of 1793 and became the sole worker in a printing house after the master’s death. Illiterate, he received a master printer’s license from a Representative of the People, letting him buy the business cheaply. He partnered with a nobleman, M. le Comte de Maucombe, who discreetly helped print decrees while avoiding attention from the authorities.

By 1795, after the Terror, Séchard needed a compositor and hired an Abbé who later became a Bishop. Despite his lack of education, Séchard learned to run the business, developing a keen sense of costs and profits. His avarice grew as he saw potential wealth: he bought paper when prices fell and became his own landlord, acquiring the old printing house.

Séchard had one son, David, whom he sent to grammar school in hopes of a capable successor. David excelled academically, and Séchard—who scorned book learning—insisted he economize while studying in Paris. David completed his education without costing his father anything.

The printing house held a monopoly on official printing in the region, but the Cointet Brothers sought a second printer’s license, threatening Séchard’s position. Confident in David’s abilities, Séchard looked forward to retirement, believing his son would face the competition.

Now in his seventies, Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard had developed a taste for drink, which his late wife had once kept in check. His appearance reflected the habit: a bloated face, cunning gaze, three-cornered hat, and old-fashioned clothing suited to his character.

When David returned home, Séchard welcomed him with feigned warmth, eager to discuss business. Despite David’s plea to postpone serious matters, Séchard pressed on, intent on negotiating the sale of the business—viewing his son not only as offspring but as a potential rival in commercial dealings.

The printing house, established since Louis XIV’s reign, consisted of a large room with a glass door opening to the workshop, where customers often stumbled through, distracted by the printing process. The premises included a yard with vines and a kitchen where inky runoff mixed with household waste, giving the place a notorious reputation.

The living quarters above the office were simple and cluttered, reflecting Séchard’s greed. The dining room was sparsely furnished and the bedroom held old furniture unchanged since the predecessor’s time. The state of the house mirrored the old printer’s character and relentless pursuit of profit.
