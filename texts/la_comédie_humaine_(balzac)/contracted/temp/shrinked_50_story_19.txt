DEDICATION

To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.

Of all the pupils of the Oratorian school at Vendome, we are, I think, the only two who have since met in mid-career of a life of letters. When we met, you were engaged on your works on German philosophy, and I on this study. So neither of us has missed his vocation; and you, seeing your name here, will feel as much pleasure as I do in inscribing my work to you. Your old schoolfellow, 1840 De Balzac.

GOBSECK

It was one o’clock one winter night in 1829–30 in the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon; two guests remained. A young man took his leave; as his carriage died away, the Vicomtesse turned to her daughter Camille, who pretended to watch a fire-screen while listening anxiously.

“Camille,” she said, “if you continue to treat young Comte de Restaud as you have this evening, I shall have to prevent him from visiting. Trust my love and let me guide you. At seventeen one cannot judge the past or the future or social standings. M. de Restaud has a mother, Mlle. Goriot, who squandered her father’s fortune and is of no birth. Though he behaves admirably toward her and his siblings, any family would hesitate to entrust their daughter’s future to him while she lives.”

Derville, a family friend who had overheard, interposed: “I have won, M. le Comte. I shall assist your niece.” On being challenged, he said he had read the Vicomtesse’s looks and settled by the fire. Camille’s uncle approached and the Vicomtesse sat between them.

“It’s time for a story that may change your view of Ernest de Restaud’s prospects,” Derville said.

By birth and fortune the Vicomtesse led the Faubourg Saint-Germain; that an attorney could be intimate with her seemed odd until the facts were known. Reduced after exile to a royal pension and having lost the Hotel de Grandlieu during the Revolution, she owed Derville much. He had discovered defects in the title, pursued legal action, reclaimed the property and obtained judgments and indemnities that raised her income to about sixty thousand francs. Refusing preferment to keep his practice and ties, he became a trusted family confidant.

His conduct won esteem and aristocratic clients; he did not exploit his popularity for office. Since Ernest de Restaud’s arrival—especially as Camille showed interest—Derville had visited more. At a recent ball he teased Camille about Restaud’s lack of millions; she defended him, saying he would have wealth when in power. “And if he were already rich?” he pressed. Her blush and abrupt departure convinced him she loved the young man. Since then Camille was unusually attentive to Derville, who approved; her feeling combined respect and gratitude, a sentiment often burdensome to the next generation.

“This adventure,” Derville begins, “recalls the one romantic episode of my life.” He explains that, though an attorney, he once fell under the spell of a man unlike any other: a usurer named Gobseck.

Gobseck was a small, sallow-faced, lunar-looking old man with sleek iron-gray hair, little yellow eyes under a shabby cap, thin lips, and a gimlet nose. His voice was low, suave and unemotional. His room matched him—clean but threadbare, with a smoldering fire that never flamed. He moved with the steady economy of a life lived like hourglass sands, hoarding every sensation. Yet at day’s end his money made him gloat: he would rub his hands and, with a silent inward laugh, show a trace of satisfaction. He spoke in monosyllables and seldom showed any expression.

Derville lived near him in the Rue de Grès while finishing his legal studies. The house was damp and gloomy, and no one seemed to penetrate the old man’s reticence except Derville, who, through four years of neighborly contact and sober conduct, obtained small courtesies: a light, a book, an occasional invitation to sit in the evenings. Gobseck’s capital was not visible—kept in the Bank—yet he ran about Paris collecting debts personally. He ate cheap dinners, made coffee on a chafing dish, and kept no ostentation in his lodging; he was eighty when Derville later learned more, though Gobseck himself was born about 1740 near Antwerp as Jean-Esther Van Gobseck, son of a Dutch father and Jewish mother. He had one surviving relative, a grandniece Sara, whose death he mentioned only by saying, indifferently, that women in his family “never marry.” The thought of heirs appalled him; the idea of his wealth passing to others was intolerable.

Sent away at ten as a cabin boy, Gobseck spent twenty years abroad. The inscrutable lines on his face concealed adventures: panic, sudden luck, squandered chances, encounters with celebrated names and pirates, treasure hunting, and the American War of Independence. Yet he refused to tell details willingly. Sociability was foreign to him; his beliefs, religion, or attitude toward Gentiles remained unknown—he appeared simply indifferent.

One evening Derville found him motionless by the mantel, a lamp casting his face pale. Gobseck invited him but said nothing for a long time. Derville pitied the old man as one pities a diseased creature, marveling that while millions were at his command he seemed to possess only the idea of the world—having ransacked and appraised it all yet feeling no claim upon it. Gobseck then surprised him by offering a philosophy of life, showing what pleasures remained for a man like him.

Gobseck’s creed was brutal in its clarity: life, after long experience, reduced to one concrete reality—gold. Place, manners, conventions change; the only constant is self-interest, and gold translates into every form of human power. He scoffed at small pleasures and political or literary vanities; only strong emotion or methodical occupation could be called higher happiness—“passion and calm,” or Science and Art. But beyond these, vanity remained and was satisfied only by gold, which can realize dreams and secure time and means for their fulfillment. He professed to have no illusions, having adapted his principles to each man and latitude; he claimed to see the world with the discount taken off, measuring life by utility and profit.

To prove his point, Gobseck described a morning’s work. He had only two bills to present that day; the rest he had let stand. He always deducted small amounts for hiring a brougham when he discounted a bill—an absurdity in his view that clients should make him trudge through Paris for a few francs when no man was his master. The first bill, for a thousand francs, was presented by a flashy dandy and bore the signature of a Countess, one of Paris’s prettiest women. Why would such a woman sign a bill? Fear of scandal; fear of a protested bill; stupidity, imprudence, love, or charity—anything might be behind such an act. The second bill, signed by “Fanny Malvaut,” came from a linen-draper near bankruptcy. Those who came to Gobseck had usually exhausted other credit; the first step to his door meant despair.

Gobseck set out, making conjectures as he walked. The Countess lived in the Rue du Helder; the girl in the Rue Montmartre. He liked to leave muddy footprints on a rich man’s carpet “not spitefully, but as a reminder of Necessity.” At the Countess’s, a maid said the mistress had returned from a ball at three in the morning and could see no one until noon. Gobseck waited. When admitted, he found a scene of lavish disorder: an Indian shawl thrown over bare shoulders, a loose ruffled gown, an unmade bed, slippers on a bearskin, a crumpled dress and scattered trinkets—flowers, diamonds, gloves—an atmosphere of exhausted luxury and faint perfume. Beneath it all he saw Misery: the marks on a young face, dark circles under the eyes, traces of last night’s excesses. Yet Nature had not been wasted—she was vigorous, alive, a luminous, dangerous beauty that awakened desire.

The Countess received him with an ingratiating tone, and Gobseck, feeling the whole power of his role, contemplated the temptation to be lenient; he recalled a past indulgence at Pondicherry where trust had been repaid. But he knew better. At the sight of him drawing the bill, she slipped a diamond into his hand and begged him to go. He took it—the jewel worth twelve hundred francs to him—and left. Outside, flunkeys brushed liveries and groomed splendid vehicles; the contrast between their pomp and the mud on the street struck him as emblematic: the rich stole millions with formalities while scrubbing off a splashed mud to preserve appearances.

As he left, a young man arrived—the one who’d presented the bill. Gobseck advanced two hundred francs to him and asked him to tell the Countess he would hold her pledge for a week. The young man’s ironic smile told Gobseck all he needed: he was a heartless gambler who would ruin himself, the Countess, and perhaps more, devouring portions and bringing greater havoc to Parisian salons than any battery of guns. Gobseck read their futures as plainly as if they were open books.

He then went to Fanny Malvaut’s lodging. She lived in a neat two-room apartment on the fifth floor, where sunlight softened her fresh, honest face. A sempstress by trade, she showed signs of honest toil: a crucifix and a sprig of green box on her bedstead, and a simple, almost sacred air of industry and solitude. Her chestnut hair and clear blue eyes bespoke rectitude. Gobseck had an instant, contrary tenderness for her: he wanted to ease her burden, to charge her no more than twelve percent to help her business. But he checked himself, suspecting that misplaced generosity often ruins the recipient; benevolence must be tempered by prudence. He thought Fanny would make a good wife, and compared her clean, lonely life with the Countess’s dissipated glamour—yet he restrained the impulse to save her with money.

Gobseck asked Derville if he thought it nothing to have the power to peer into human hearts and live many lives by proxy. He described the endless variety of drama that appeared at his door: girls in love, merchants at the point of bankruptcy, mothers hiding sons’ crimes, starving artists, great men whose influence was fading. Their pleas made him shudder; these sublime actors played solely for him. He could read their inmost thoughts without error. Holding the purse-strings meant the power to loose and bind consciences; he could buy the consciences of those who guided ministers and sway men of consequence. He equated his power with Pleasure and Power—the essential engines of social life.

He claimed there were ten men in Paris like him—silent, unknown kings—arbiters of destinies. Money set the social machine in motion; gold was the spiritual basis of society. These ten met at the Café Thémis near the Pont Neuf, keeping a Black Book that recorded important bills and instruments on public credit. They were, in effect, casuists of the Paris Bourse, an Inquisition weighing every man of fortune and forecasting with infallible precision. Each of them watched a different sphere—judicial, financial, administrative, commercial; Gobseck watched the eldest sons, artists, high society, and gamblers. From the secrets people unwittingly revealed, from thwarted passion and mortified vanity—excellent detectives—these men learned everything. Sated with experience, they loved power and money for their own sakes.

In his bare, chilly room, Gobseck rehearsed how the proudest soldier, the most haughty merchant, the most vain woman come to him on their knees—famous artists, men of letters who may be remembered, all humbled before the lender’s balance. He asked whether Derville still thought there were no delights behind his blank mask. He stretched out his livid face that “reeked of money” and described the intoxicating panorama of human frailty he watched daily.

Derville left Gobseck stunned. The wizened man, once merely a neighbor, had become a strange visionary—the power of gold personified. Derville felt a shrinking from life and his kind and could not sleep. The Countess’s image haunted him; ashamedly, he admits she eclipsed Fanny’s gentleness in his thoughts. Yet in the morning, as sleep’s clouds lifted, Fanny’s fresh, innocent face rose before him and remained his thought. He ends uncertain, torn between the glittering ruin of the Countess and the quiet virtue of the sempstress, and troubled by Gobseck’s insistence that, in the end, everything resolves to gold.

“Will you take a glass of eau sucrée?” the Vicomtesse offered, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it.”

“But I see nothing here that concerns us,” Mme. de Grandlieu said.

Derville then told his story. After speaking with the old miser Gobseck, he finished his law studies, became a licentiate and then an advocate. Gobseck’s opinion of him improved; the old man consulted him gratis on delicate matters. Derville rose from clerk to head-clerk, moved into his employer’s house, and at last earned 150 francs a month. He went to bid the usurer goodbye; Gobseck showed no sentiment but a look implying second sight. Even after they parted, Gobseck continued to consult him.

When Derville’s employer—a pleasure-loving man—fell into financial trouble and sold his practice for 150,000 francs, Derville, the seventh child of a small tradesman from Noyon, had neither money nor connections to buy it. Determined not to beg, he went boldly to Gobseck, who now occupied Derville’s old room and watched callers through a grated window. Derville proposed buying the practice: it now produced about 20,000 francs a year and might reach 40,000 under his care; with a ten-year plan he could pay 150,000 if Gobseck lent the sum.

Gobseck weighed him—no guarantees, age under twenty-five—then agreed to think it over. The next morning he consented on his terms: for acquaintances he would take “twelve and a half”—corrected to thirteen percent—interest. He would pay the seller directly, secure the lien, and demanded that Derville give bills for the amount—fifteen bills of ten thousand francs each—payable to a third party unnamed. Puzzled by the need for a birth certificate, Derville heard Gobseck explain, with malicious smile, that youth’s integrity and brains are commodities one can mortgage—after thirty a man’s value becomes uncertain.

Further conditions followed: Derville would give the old man legal advice without fees for life so long as it cost no outlay; permit Gobseck to visit him, not live ostentatiously, and see him for dinner on Wednesdays and Saturdays after the “Change” at five. Gobseck promised to recommend him, send him business, and teach him “to know men, and what is more—women!” The miser delighted in investing capital in Derville’s head and watching it prosper.

Derville became an attorney, won the suit to recover Mme. de Grandlieu’s estates, and his name spread. Despite the usurer’s enormous terms, he repaid the loan in under five years. He married Fanny Malvaut, whom he loved; through work, shared fortune, and a timely inheritance—one of her uncles left her seventy thousand francs—they cleared the debt. Derville summarized his happiness modestly and returned to the company.

About a year after buying the practice he was drawn into a bachelor breakfast lost as the forfeit of a bet. Among the guests was M. de Trailles, the consummate dandy: graceful in clothes and tandem, a master of taste, gambler and gourmand of society, spending perhaps a hundred thousand francs a year with no visible capital. Brilliant but frivolous, Trailles charmed and ruined in equal measure.

Derville had tried to avoid Trailles, but Goriot insisted. He described the bachelor breakfast: a display of sumptuousness dissolving into a chaotic orgy—broken glasses, ruined linen, loud jesting, songs, quarrels, smashed bottles and a cacophony of voices—an exhausted Bacchanal. Amid that pandemonium Trailles, cool and calculating beneath an affected drunken air, engaged Derville and by evening secured his promise to introduce him to Papa Gobseck. Trailles painted an urgent picture: a young woman’s reputation and marriage at stake unless fifty thousand francs could be procured that morning.

Bound by his word, Derville accompanied Trailles to Gobseck. Trailles, outwardly courtly, was inwardly agitated; on the Rue de Grès he grew nervous, glancing about with sweat on his brow. At Gobseck’s, Derville vouched for him; Gobseck said he had no money except for his own clients’ business. Trailles boasted of partners and foreign revenues, quipping that spendthrifts and usurers were interdependent. Gobseck answered coldly: other creditors—Girard, Palma, Werbrust, Gigonnet—were full of Trailles’s paper and offered it at heavy losses; he would not lend a stiver to a man who owed thirty thousand francs and had nothing.

A cab arrived; Trailles left and returned shortly with a woman on his arm—one of Goriot’s daughters, the Countess. Derville watched unseen at the window; he saw the Countess enter Gobseck’s damp room, proud and distressed. She shuddered at the instant she recognized Derville; he felt pity despite her social brilliance and moral failings. Trailles had become her evil genius, ruling her by vanity and other strings.

The Countess asked if she could obtain the value of her diamonds while retaining the right of repurchase, handing over a jewel-case. Derville explained the sale with faculty of redemption—a transfer allowing recovery by paying a set sum. The Countess breathed freer; the Count feared Gobseck would undervalue the stones.

Gobseck, impassive, took his magnifying glass and inspected the jewels. Derville never forgot the look that came over the old man—an unnatural glitter as he rose and went to the light, holding the diamonds close to his toothless mouth, as if to devour them. He handled bracelets, sprays, necklaces and tiaras like a child or doting miser, letting the light draw out every fire. That flash of greed, the only time Gobseck’s heart seemed to open, shocked Derville and left a lasting impression.

Earlier, Gobseck had mocked Trailles’s ruin and refused to be fooled. Now, with the jewels, his mood changed to triumph; he rejoiced at outwitting rivals Werbrust and Gigonnet and at the prospect of laughing at them that night. Yet after his brief ecstasy his face reverted to the pale, impassive mask of the usurer. The sight of the Countess—beautiful, suffering, and controlled by Trailles—registered in him only as the value of her jewels.

Derville reflected that Trailles had become the Countess’s tormentor: an angel-faced youth whose charm masked moral ruin. In Gobseck’s room they stood as before a judge; the usurer peered at them like an inquisitor, measuring guilt and bargaining worth. The bargain Gobseck had struck with Derville—interest, anonymous bills, visits, unpaid counsel—illustrated a system where personal ties and youthful promise were commodified, and financial life depended on shrewd calculation and a cold knowledge of men’s weaknesses. Gobseck’s power came not only from capital but from this almost scientific mastery of human needs, which he used to acquire dominion and to profit emotionally as well as financially from the commodities people brought him—practices, promises, or jewels.

“Fine stones! This set would have fetched three hundred thousand francs before the Revolution. What water! Genuine Asiatic diamonds from Golconda or Visapur. No one in Paris but Gobseck can appreciate them. In the Empire they’d have cost two hundred thousand more.”

Gobseck’s eyes shone as he handled the case, torn between rapture and calculation. The Count wanted a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years, offering the diamonds as security; the Countess insisted they were hers. I warned that, as a married woman, she could not legally alienate property without her husband’s consent, so a deed of purchase would be void. Gobseck shifted tactics—possession being as good as title—and offered eighty thousand francs if they left the stones with him.

The Count’s pride flared; the Countess was stupefied; Maxime, her lover, blustered. Gobseck, unmoved, produced a cheque for fifty thousand francs on the Bank of France and thirty thousand in protested drafts—and even two pistols to underline his temper. Cornered, they accepted. Gobseck exulted at having obtained the diamonds “tolerably cheaply”; his ferocious delight in possession chilled me.

Soon the Count burst into Gobseck’s room, furious. Gobseck replied with implacable logic: if the Countess could sign bills, she could engage in business; whether the jewels were the Count’s could not be proven without dragging the wife into court and exposing her. Our dispute became a legal contest: perhaps in equity the Count had a claim, but legally the buyer could plead good faith. I advised the Count to compromise—pay to redeem or secure a redemption clause—since litigation would be ruinous and humiliating. With my help the Count signed an acknowledgment of receipt of eighty-five thousand francs, including interest, in consideration of Gobseck’s promise to return the stones.

Months later the Count, fearful and agitated, asked me to draw papers to transfer his estate to Gobseck as a simulated sale, Gobseck to hold it in trust and later restore it to the eldest son. He distrusted his son’s loyalty and feared the Countess’s influence. I warned that such a counter-deed risked disinheriting other children; he wept and agreed to provide for them in the instrument. The deed was signed but not delivered to me.

Gobseck dined at my house and hinted the Count was dying and that only he and I were trusted. I resolved to visit the Count, fearing for the counter-deed. At the Count’s lodgings I found the Countess—pale, anxious—playing with her children and watching the death-chamber from an adjoining salon. She feigned devotion; some thought her penitent. Her motives were mixed: maternal anxiety, fear of destitution, some remorse, and a calculating will to recover the family fortune.

Suspecting her of seeking the counter-deed, I took precautions. I sued the Count for an amount ostensibly due Gobseck so that upon his death I could attach the estate and secure the deed; I bribed a servant to warn me when the Count was gravely ill. Money governed these manoeuvres at the bedside.

For two months the Count lay prostrate in squalor, disdainful of care. One morning, in a lucid moment, he summoned Ernest and entrusted the boy with a sealed packet addressed to me, begging him to carry it to the post and keep the secret even from his mother. Ernest promised. Back with his mother, he was pressed until he refused to repeat what his father had said. Furious, the Count confronted her. He accused her of filling his life with trouble and corrupting their son; she begged for mercy and vowed to expiate her sins if the children could be spared. He, exhausted of affection, called her a bad wife and mother; she fainted. At midnight he died.

Gobseck and I arrived soon after. The house was in chaos. In the death-chamber and adjoining salon the Countess had been ransacking drawers—mattress overturned, papers and furniture strewn. A paper bearing the Count’s arms lay at her feet; in panic she had thrust some documents into the grate and was burning them when we entered. I snatched an addressed paper from the hearth: it was to me. Confronted, the Countess was near collapse, torn between shame and fear. Gobseck, cold and triumphant, reminded her that he was the legal owner of what her husband had sold. He asked if I would judge him; I could not deny that under the law he had rights. I left her beside her husband’s corpse while Gobseck declared himself trustee of the estate.

From then on Gobseck managed the property with the hard efficiency of a usurer turned landowner—leasing the mansion, repairing mills, planting timber. He became, in all but name, lord of the manor, showing no softness. When I suggested he help young Ernest, Gobseck scoffed: adversity is the best teacher; let the youth fend for himself. My attempts to move him to honor the Count’s intentions and provide for the children met his icy thrift.

Time passed. Gobseck grew feebler but retained his iron will. He kept his rooms exactly as always, receiving clients even from his bed. During financial reorganizations after France recognized the Haitian Republic, his knowledge of claims made him influential; he devised agencies, took slices of large transactions, and accepted gifts—plates, snuff-boxes, game—without ever parting cash. He remained as thin, his avarice concentrated to a point.

Then Gobseck died. Summoned by his old porter, I found him kneeling by the grate, muttering bequests and naming people—half delirious, half lucid. At the hearth, amid cinders, I dug and found metal: coins, receipts, tucked papers—proof of the vast hoard he had amassed. I ordered the justice of the peace to seal everything at once.

The rooms revealed the scale and eccentricity of his thrift: one chamber reeked of putrid pies, moldy fish, and shells among chests of tea and coffee; others were packed with furniture, silver-plate, vases, pictures, books, and curios—many likely presents or pledged goods. Jewel-cases bore ciphers and coats of arms; linen and weapons lay in confusion. Every item had been argued over, bartered, haggled for. Some bargains remained unsettled; dealers balked at Gobseck’s stingy terms. The stench and clutter bespoke a miser’s final degradation.

There was talk of Gobseck’s intended bequests—an absurd address for an heiress known as “La Torpille”—but the immediate issue was the Restaud estate: the counter-deed, the burnt fragments, the tormented Countess, and young Ernest’s uncertain inheritance. Despite Gobseck’s harshness, Ernest would, in time, inherit a fortune sufficient to marry Mlle. Camille and provide for his mother and siblings—assuming the legal knots could be untangled and the provisions enforced.

Thus closed the arc of transactions, betrayal, and legal scrimmage: Gobseck’s cold mastery of money altered an aristocratic family’s fate; the Count’s plans were thwarted by rage and sickness; the Countess’s frantic attempts to save her children degenerated into crime; and a miser’s death revealed treasures amid squalor. The outcome promised both restitution and new trials as the rightful heirs navigated tangled transfers and the old usurer’s secreted wealth.
