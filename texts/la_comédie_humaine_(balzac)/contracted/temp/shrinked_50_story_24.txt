If the French dislike traveling and the English embrace it, both have reasons: France’s particular charms—wit, conversation, an implicit social understanding—are hard to find elsewhere, so many Frenchmen take pleasure in recognizing home abroad.

In 1836, while the Sardinian Court stayed in Genoa, two notable Parisians lodged in a palazzo, a relic of Genoese nobility. Genoa’s evenings, especially after rain, suited intimate company and Italian fare. At a dinner given by the French Consul-General were Mademoiselle des Touches (Camille Maupin), the painter Léon de Lora, and the critic Claude Vignon. Camille had come from Florence and, despite her fame, avoided formal ceremony, preferring an unpressured evening. Conversation was lively but light until the Consul-General told a personal tale.

He was thirty-four, Byron-like in appearance, and had married Onorina Pedrotti, the wealthy banker's only child—a union influenced by the ambassador and by his own private struggles. Onorina’s grace brought harmony, yet melancholy persisted in him. A table debate on women’s morality revealed gendered divides, so the Consul related a formative youth: at twenty-two his uncle, Abbé Loraux, secured him a secretaryship to Comte Octave. Serving the Count, outwardly pious and composed, he discovered a tender but sorrowful man whose love of flowers and faded beauty masked restless interior sorrow. That tension—duty and public rectitude concealing private unhappiness—shaped both the Count and the Consul’s understanding of human complexity, leaving the guests to ponder hidden struggles beneath seeming perfection.

“On a certain Monday,” he resumed, “M. le Président de Grandville and M. de Serizy (then Vice‑President of the Council of State) met at Comte Octave’s house. They formed a committee of three with me as secretary. The Count had secured my appointment as Auditor to the Council of State. All the necessary documents for their inquiry lay in the library; MM. de Grandville and de Serizy relied on the Count for the preliminary examination. To avoid carrying all the papers to M. de Serizy, they agreed to meet first in the Rue Payenne. The Tuileries valued this work, and it led to my appointment as Master of Appeals that year.

“Though the Comtes de Grandville and de Serizy, who rarely dined out, were still discussing late into the evening, a servant interrupted: ‘MM. the Cures of Saint‑Paul and of the White Friars have been waiting in the drawing‑room for two hours.’

“It was nine o’clock.

“‘Well, gentlemen, you find yourselves compelled to dine with priests,’ said Comte Octave. ‘I do not know whether Grandville can overcome his horror of a priest’s gown—’

“‘It depends on the priest.’

“‘One of them is my uncle, and the other is the Abbe Gaudron,’ I said. ‘Do not be alarmed; the Abbe Fontanon is no longer second priest at Saint‑Paul.’

“‘Well, let us dine,’ replied the President de Grandville. ‘A bigot frightens me, but there is no one so cheerful as a truly pious man.’

“We entered the drawing‑room. The dinner was delightful. My uncle’s wit was refined and elegant, typical of those who conceal thoughts under a black robe. The Abbe Gaudron, as M. de Grandville observed, was more like a Saint Peter than a Saint Paul: a peasant full of faith, whose ignorance of worldly matters enlivened the talk with guileless amazement. They began discussing a social issue—adultery. My uncle pointed out the contradiction between civil and religious law that legislators had left after the Revolution.

“‘In the eyes of the Church,’ he said, ‘adultery is a crime; in your tribunals, it is a misdemeanor. Adultery drives to the police court in a carriage instead of standing at the bar. Napoleon’s Council of State, touched with tenderness towards erring women, was quite inefficient. Should they not have harmonized civil and religious law and sent the guilty wife to a convent, as of old?’

“‘To a convent!’ said M. de Serizy. ‘They must first have created convents; monasteries were being turned into barracks. Besides, think of what you say, M. l’Abbe—give to God what society would have none of?’

“‘Oh!’ said Comte de Grandville, ‘you do not know France. They had to leave the husband free to take proceedings; well, there are not ten cases of adultery brought up in a year.’

“‘M. l’Abbe preaches for his own saint,’ said Comte Octave; ‘in the East, woman was merely a luxury, regarded as a chattel; no virtues were demanded of her but obedience and beauty. By exalting the soul above the body, modern Europe—daughter of Christ—invented indissoluble marriage and made it a sacrament.’

“‘Ah! the Church saw the difficulties,’ exclaimed M. de Grandville.

“‘This institution has given rise to a new world,’ the Count continued with a smile. ‘But its practices will never match a climate where women marry at seven and are old at twenty‑five. The Catholic Church overlooked half the globe. Let us discuss Europe only.

“‘Is woman our superior or inferior? That is the real question. If woman is inferior, placing her on such a high level as the Church does required severe punishments for adultery. Formerly, cloister or death summed up legislation. But practice has since modified the law. The throne served as a hotbed for adultery, and the growth of this inviting crime marks the decline of Catholic dogmas. Nowadays, where the Church demands only sincere repentance from the erring wife, society is satisfied with a brand instead of execution. The law still condemns the guilty, but it no longer terrifies. In short, there are two moral standards: that of the world and that of the Code. Where the Code is weak, the world is audacious and satirical. Few judges would not gladly have committed the faults they condemn. The world punishes a blunder after encouraging hypocrisy. The entire marriage law seems to require reconstruction. The French law would be perfect if it excluded daughters from inheriting.’

“‘We three know the question thoroughly,’ said Comte de Grandville with a laugh. ‘I have a wife I cannot live with. Serizy has a wife who will not live with him. As for you, Octave, yours ran away from you. So we represent every case of the conjugal conscience, and if divorce is ever brought in again, we shall form the committee.’

“Octave’s fork dropped, breaking his glass and plate. He turned pale and shot a thunderous glare at M. de Grandville, hinting at my presence.

“‘Forgive me, my dear fellow. I did not see Maurice,’ the President continued. ‘Serizy and I, after witnessing your marriage, became your accomplices; I did not think I was committing an indiscretion in front of these venerable priests.’

“M. de Serizy changed the subject, recounting his efforts to please his wife without success. The old man concluded that human sympathies and antipathies cannot be regulated; social law is most perfect when closest to natural law. Nature does not consider the affinities of souls; her aim is the propagation of the species. Thus, the Code, in its current form, wisely leaves latitude to chance. The incapacity of daughters to inherit while male heirs exist is an excellent provision, whether to prevent degeneration or to make households happier by abolishing scandalous unions.

“‘But then,’ he exclaimed, lifting his hand in disgust, ‘how are we to perfect legislation in a country that insists on bringing together seven or eight hundred legislators! After all, if I am sacrificed,’ he added, ‘I have a child to succeed me.’

“‘Setting aside the religious question,’ my uncle remarked, ‘Nature only owes us life; society owes us happiness. Are you a father?’ he asked.

“‘And I—do I have any children?’ said Comte Octave in a hollow voice, making such an impression that the topic of wives and marriage was dropped.

“When coffee was served, the two Counts and the two priests quietly left, noticing that poor Octave had fallen into a melancholy fit, oblivious to their departure. My patron sat in an armchair by the fire, crushed.

“‘You now know the secret of my life,’ he said to me when we were alone. ‘After three years of marriage, I came home one evening to find a letter from the Countess announcing her flight. The letter was dignified; women often retain some virtues even when committing such a sin. The story now is that my wife went abroad on a ship that was wrecked; she is presumed dead. I have lived alone for seven years! Enough for tonight, Maurice. We will discuss my situation when I am ready to speak of it. When suffering from a chronic disease, it takes time to adjust to the idea of improvement. That improvement often seems merely another aspect of the complaint.’

“I went to bed greatly agitated; the mystery seemed more obscure than ever. I anticipated a strange drama, realizing that the events leading the Countess to leave such a noble and loving man must have been extraordinary. M. de Grandville’s remark had illuminated the caverns I had been navigating, and though the light was dim, I could perceive their vastness. I could imagine the Count’s suffering without grasping its depths. His sallow face, parched temples, overwhelming studies, and moments of absentmindedness stood out vividly during the twilight of mental questioning before sleep.

“Oh! how I loved my poor master! He seemed sublime. I read a poem of melancholy, seeing perpetual activity in the heart I had accused of being torpid. Must not supreme grief eventually lead to stagnation? Had this judge, with so much power, ever sought revenge? Was he feeding on her long agony? Is it not remarkable to harbor anger for ten years in Paris? What had Octave done since this great misfortune—when the separation of husband and wife has become a social question?

This, as nearly as may be, is his story.

My father had a ward, Honorine, rich and lovely, who was sixteen when I returned from college to our old house. Raised by my mother, she greeted life with an innocent, poetic view of religion and happiness. We lived like siblings until, by the garden basin, I asked her to marry me. We waited eighteen months at my mother’s insistence; when I was twenty-six and Honorine nineteen, we married. Out of respect for my parents we kept the old house unchanged; I introduced her to society more from duty than delight.

I later learned how treacherous such marriages can be: a husband becomes teacher and moralist, and love may wither under authority. I trusted her innocence too much and reproached myself like an anatomist seeking the cause of a disease. After my parents’ deaths, eighteen months later, Honorine left me—she wrote a farewell letter. The shock stunned me; for a month I was numb, then I resolved to live on memory. I loved Honorine and never ceased to worship her; since her departure I have subsisted on recollections of pleasures she perhaps never appreciated.

Do not make a hero of me; I sought no diversion. I was too young or too in love and met no other like her. After struggling to forget, I almost fell into infidelity, but the thought of Honorine restrained me. My life became a relentless agony of fears, joy, and despair; I felt no anger toward her, only compassionate, paternal sorrow. I realized I had idealized her and perhaps exhausted her with my uncritical passion. Too young to understand the unwritten laws of a woman’s heart, I blamed myself and absolved her. Love held me captive.

Society mocks a husband who cannot keep his wife; I had to remain silent. Serizy is happy—able to see and protect his wife—while I have nothing but a love that starves. I loathe prostitution and remain faithful under a kind of spell. Without religion I might have killed myself; instead I threw myself into work, feverish and sleepless.

The interior drama of my life interests no one; one can only vaguely perceive another’s sorrow. I calm despair by contemplating a miniature of Honorine, kissing her brow, breathing her perfume. I suffer fits of collapse and wild hilarity, and terrors like a criminal meeting the police. The public believes I am absorbed in official duties—Council of State, Chamber, Courts, Politics—but seven hours of nightly work suffice for those. Honorine is my true occupation. To recover my wife, to guard her without her knowing, to satisfy small pleasures she allows, to be always near her like a sylph unseen—that is my life. For seven years I have never gone to bed without checking her night-lamp and the shadow on her curtains.

Honorine left my house with only the dress she wore. Eighteen months later she had been deserted by her lover, who recoiled at poverty. She had a private income of sixty thousand francs a year. In November 1820, I secured the services of a skilled accoucheur disguised as a humble suburban apothecary and induced a parish priest to act as charity when she needed help. To hide her, save her from discovery, and find a devoted housekeeper who would be my confidante—this was a task worthy of Figaro. Locating where my wife had taken refuge was not difficult.

After three months of despair, I devoted myself to Honorine with God as my witness. Love needs daily nourishment; should I not protect this child whose guilt sprang from my imprudence against further ruin? At seven months her infant died—mercifully for both of us. For nine months she lay between life and death, deserted when she most needed a man’s support; but my arm was extended over her head. Honorine was cared for as she would have been at home. When she recovered and asked who had assisted her, she was told it was the Sisters of Charity, the Maternity Society, and the parish priest.

Honorine’s pride, almost a vice, has at times become obstinacy in misfortune. She determined to earn her living. For five years I lodged her in the Rue Saint-Maur, in a charming little house where she made artificial flowers. She believes she sells her creations to a shop, earning twenty francs a day and living as if on fifteen thousand francs a year. She adores flowers, pays a gardener handsomely—an expense I secretly meet. I promised the gardener a market-garden with a house near the porter’s lodge in the Rue Saint-Maur, held in the name of a law-clerk; the smallest indiscretion would ruin him. Honorine has her little house, a garden, and a splendid hothouse for a rent of five hundred francs a year. She lives under the name of Madame Gobain, her housekeeper, an old woman of impeccable discretion who has won Honorine’s affection. For three years Honorine has been happy, believing she owes her comfort solely to her own toil.

Yes, I have tried to reconcile with her. Once, believing there was a chance, I wrote her a letter—rewriting it twenty times. I will not describe my agonies. It was dark and foggy; I went to meet Madame Gobain. Honorine, recognizing my handwriting, threw the letter into the fire without reading it, declaring she would leave the next day. It felt like a dagger in my heart. I had just succeeded in deceiving my wife once—sending an Indian shawl presented as a gift from an actress, which I had wrapped myself for a night. My life may be summed up: I love, and I wait.

Madame Gobain is my faithful spy. I visit her every evening for reports of Honorine’s day; a single exclamation might reveal the secrets of a soul that remains willfully deaf and mute where I am concerned. Honorine is pious—she attends church and prays—but she has never confessed or taken Communion, anticipating what a priest would say. She will not return to me; the horror I inspire overwhelms her, though I have never harmed her and have always been kind. Even if I was hasty in teaching her, is that a reason for her implacable determination? Her silence is absolute: she has never told Madame Gobain who she is; the housekeeper cannot advocate for me. The others know nothing; they live in awe of the Prefect of Police and respect the power of a Minister. Thus it is impossible for me to penetrate her heart. I have no means of action; any violence would ruin me forever.

How can I argue against reasons I do not know? Should I write and have my letter copied and delivered, risking a third removal, which cost me fifty thousand francs previously? The last purchase of a house had been made in the name of my second secretary. The poor man, unaware of my vigilance, was caught opening a private box; I intimidated him into selling the house and dismissed him. If I did not feel my noblest faculties satisfied by this divine fatherhood—if I did not drink delight in every pore—I might think myself a monomaniac. Sometimes at night I hear the jingling bells of madness and dread the violent transitions from feeble hope to complete despair. Recently I thought of the tragic ends of Lovelace and Clarissa Harlowe; if Honorine were the mother of my child, she must return under her husband’s roof.

I have such faith in a happy future that ten months ago I bought one of the finest houses in the Faubourg Saint-Honoré. If I win her back I will not let her see the room from which she fled; I will place my idol in a new temple where life will seem entirely new. That house is being transformed into a marvel of elegance and taste. I was inspired by an anecdote of a poet’s extravagant purchase for an actress; great passions share such impulses. I love like a poet—like an emperor!

From the vehemence of my speech you might imagine a burning physical passion, but it is nothing compared to my worship for her soul, mind, and heart. By retrospection I now see the graces of Honorine’s mind and heart I overlooked in happiness. Day by day I appreciate my loss, discovering gifts in that capricious and proud young creature who has grown strong under poverty and the cowardly shock of desertion. That heavenly blossom is fading in solitude and hiding. The law we discussed cannot compel love—seizing my wife by force would be triumph over a corpse. Religion has little hold on her; she craves its poetry but does not heed its commands. I have exhausted mercy, kindness, and love; I am at my wit’s end. Only one chance remains: the cunning and patience of bird-catchers who finally trap the wariest birds.

When M. de Grandville’s indiscretion revealed my secret life to you, I regarded it as fate’s decree. Have you affection for me to show romantic devotion? I replied with a question about danger: my first secretary had tried to open my deed box; my second might fall in love with my wife. Can anyone put his hand in a brazier without burning it? The Count answered that he would send me well-gloved. It would not be his secretary who lodged in the Rue Saint-Maur but his distant cousin, Baron de l’Hostal, a lawyer high in office—an appearance of respectability.

Shortly after, Madame de Courteville and her daughter called; the Count had family ties on his mother’s side. A young woman of twenty might suit the Count, but he did not propose that match. Instead, three weeks later, I moved into the gardener’s cottage, repaired and furnished by Paris workmen and money. I was as much in love as the Count could desire for security. Would a young man of twenty-five play this part faithfully for his friend’s cause? I confess I relied on my uncle’s advice; the Count had authorized me to confide in him if needed. I engaged the garden, devoted myself to horticulture, and worked frantically—obsessing over varieties of flowers as part of my disguise. The Count dictated the tragi-comedy enacted in the Rue Saint-Maur. Each night, after Honorine went to bed, Octave, Madame Gobain, and I held council; Octave lived only for that hour.

For two months I worked in the garden without ever seeing the little house where my fair neighbor lived; I had not even inquired if I had a neighbor. Her garden front was divided from mine by a paling, her cypress trees already four feet high. One morning Gobain warned me that a neighbor intended to build a wall between the gardens. My curiosity burned to see the Countess—my wish nearly extinguished the budding affection I had briefly felt for Amélie de Courteville. Building a wall would close off fresh air from Honorine’s garden and turn her plot into a narrow alley. The cottage, once a summer villa, imitated a trellis with flowers up to the second floor—a charming, fragile place. My wall would cut away three-quarters of it.

When Gobain informed the Countess that the florist wished to be left in peace, she replied she would speak to him herself. The next day as I walked the path I saw the curtain parted and a face peering out. Gobain announced me as the eccentric neighbor. I had dressed as a countryman—gray flannel trousers, wooden shoes, shabby coat, peaked cap, hands soiled with mold—to strengthen the part. The Countess did not shrink.

It was early May and the air was pure; seeing Honorine, I understood Octave’s passion. Her pallor was of a particular white, with pink flushes at emotion. Sunlight through the acacia gave her a pale gold halo. Her brown eyes were tender and lively, framed by long lashes; a glance could enchant. Light-brown hair framed a high brow; her mouth was voluptuous; her head bore a stamp of nobility. Slight but not thin, she embodied mignonne—pliant, ready to be petted, yet proud and imposing. Her walk had a proud carriage, and even humble people made way. Gay and tender, haughty and commanding, she combined contradictions and remained childlike. She could be as implacable as an angel if hurt.

The violet perfume she wore made sense of the Count’s adoration: she inspired devotion that demanded chivalrous sacrifice. Many women study their expressions; in her it flowed from a nature that went straight to the heart. If I sketch her it is because her soul and exquisite heart are central to this tale, and you would blame me had I not done so.

I was about to forget my ridiculous role as half-crazy rustic when I asked her, with assumed gravity, if she loved flowers. She replied she made artificial flowers—after growing them she imitates them, as a mother paints her children—an occupation that proved she was poor. I pretended to be a gardener with an interest in blue dahlias and blue roses; I argued that blue is the color of superior souls. I proposed open railings to unite our gardens. She, who loved solitude and independence, allowed me the right to enter her garden anytime and urged I be treated as a neighbor with whom she would keep good terms. With a leap I cleared the paling.

For a fortnight I seemed indifferent; by late May on a lovely evening we walked on opposite sides of the paling and exchanged civil words. I leaped the fence and joined her. She led me into the house—a charming sanctuary of eighteenth-century taste: frescoed dining-room, tasteful staircase, faded but elegant drawing-room, a bedroom, dressing-room, and workroom used as a library. The kitchen lay below. Everything harmonized with the woman I had come to love.

Exquisite simplicity reigned. Her little house was like a box of 18th-century art; but occasionally a bitter smile hinted at deeper sorrow. In days we reached intimacy born of proximity and her conviction of my indifference to women. A look would have spoiled everything; I kept my feelings masked. Honorine saw me as an old friend and treated me with pity rather than coquetry. She soon allowed me into her pretty workshop, full of books and curiosities—a boudoir where elegance softened the vulgarity of the tools of her trade. She had poetized manufacture.

Perhaps making artificial flowers allows a woman to remain herself: she can chat, laugh, sing and still work. Honorine had an artist’s sense in arranging petals and shades; white china saucers held colors; a cabinet with drawers held molds; a Japanese bowl held the paste; a Venetian glass held the living model she strove to imitate. She pursued difficult tasks—tiny corollas, wildflowers—moving like an accomplished pianist. Her fingers seemed fairy-like in their deft economy of action.

I could not tire of admiring her; she displayed the genius of a painter in bold attempts, copying faded flowers and yellowed leaves to create poetic crowns suitable to women’s moods. She explained a fanciful botany of feelings—flowers for bacchantes, for bigots, pensive flowers for bored women. She employed me to stamp leaves, cut material, and prepare wires. My affected desire for occupation made me useful. We talked as we worked; when idle I read to her to keep my role. My gloomy, misanthropic persona amused and matched her antipathy to love; she was content with a companion on her desert island.

I spared you the shame I felt as I feigned wounds in my heart to excite her compassion. Her renunciation of love, her dread of happiness, she proclaimed with vehemence. These happy days convinced me that a woman’s friendship can be superior to her love. She resisted any trap of sentiment; mulish obstinacy is common among women, and she was stubborn.

One evening, playing the cynic, I argued that women, once they have inspired passion, hold men sacred forever, while women forget. She rebuked my generalization and denied being like those who give themselves to successive loves. She declared herself a nun, seventy-two years old in spirit, not a woman. Her disgust for life made her invulnerable. She often spoke of the angel Gabriel singing in her heart; if rich she would work still to prevent herself from soaring on “many-tinted wings.” She found some solace in her flowers but sometimes they failed to occupy her. She felt some great event impending; her life seemed about to change.

After three months of this diplomatic struggle between my assumed melancholy and her proud indifference, I told the Count I could not coax her out of herself; the shell must be broken. The Count left me free to act.

One Saturday in July, she gleefully showed me five gold pieces she had earned for her flowers and caps. It was late, a moonlit evening. The room smelled of perfumes; the Countess jingled the coins that Octave’s accomplices had provided as part of the ruse. She said she earned her living by amusing herself and felt transports of pride every Saturday. I suggested the image of a pretty child running among the flowers calling her “mother,” and her reaction was immediate and extreme: she suffered an attack—what she called “a cramp of the heart.” Madame Gobain tended her; I walked the path weeping and doubting whether I should abandon the bird-catcher’s role I had assumed. Gobain returned saying I cried like a child, and the Countess, roused by the danger of misinterpretation, wore a wrapper and came down to me.

“You are not the cause of this attack,” she said, and when I asked about her troubles she declared her heart a poem laid before God. I threatened, lightly, that I could know all her secrets by tomorrow. She defiantly challenged me to be curious and told me to reveal what I discovered and the means by which I discovered it, for the preservation of the small happiness she so dearly bought depended on my discretion.

“I defy you,” she said; she wanted me to find out who she really was. I warned that if I learned anything carelessly, others would be informed, and then—she would fly “on wings” to the New World. I interrupted that Paris is a place where those who must work can hide. She persisted: find out who I am. I promised to tell her what I might discover the next day and pleaded she not hate me. She replied coldly, almost imperiously, that only two words would suffice to destroy her peaceful fraud and she must be allowed to keep her carefully arranged retreat. After a few more words, she dismissed me abruptly.

Thus the Count’s scheme had reached a crucial point: I was to be the Count’s instrument, the gardener-spy and companion, someone who could win Honorine’s confidence without revealing his identity. The house’s delicate balance—Honorine’s love of flowers, her pride, her vow of solitude, and the secret charity that sustained her—would be the stage for whether pity, cunning, or patience could redeem what force and authority could not.

“The battle is to-morrow, then,” I said, trying to smile and appear indifferent. Octave shared Honorine’s agitation; the Count and I walked until two in the morning like generals before a decisive engagement, aware that victory might hinge on a single instant. For Octave and Honorine the true drama was interior—located in the spiritual world of lofty souls.

The next evening I called at ten and entered Honorine’s white-and-blue bedroom, the refuge of this wounded dove. The Comtesse, wrapped in grief, seemed ready to speak but fell silent at my respectful manner. I told her plainly she was not living by choice in her house but under Count Octave’s protection: M. Lenormand the usher was only a front; the seclusion, money, and shelter all came from him. Her disappearance had been plausibly explained; her husband had even hoped she had been saved from a shipwreck. He had hidden her as she hid herself, obeying her for seven years.

The Countess trembled with defiance that later yielded helpless tears. She had believed herself free; marriage weighed on her like a prison. She declared she would not return—if forced she would go somewhere none could follow. When I asked if she meant suicide she denied it but insisted she had powerful reasons to refuse Octave. I urged her to confide in my uncle and me—both impartial, devoted advisers. My uncle, a priest but not a pedant, would listen and try to find a solution: if she were a victim a misunderstanding could be cleared; if guilty expiation might be sought. I promised friendship and refuge if she proved she could not and ought not to return, offering peace, solitude, independence, and to see her only by her consent.

She answered that no man could understand her conscience—“like circles of Dante’s hell.” Another man, though unworthy, had had all her love; for her there could never be two loves. The cup of happiness once broken could not be refilled. She refused to be a prostitute to Octave, whose generosity she esteemed and respected but could never love. Expecting tenderness from him would be impossible; every time their eyes met she would see her sin reflected. To return would be a snare: she feared killing him by coldness or provoking suspicion, and could not bear to kneel while he stood. She clung to the memory of a child conceived in rapture and nursed for seven months; other children would draw tears in their milk. For her there was no situation under his roof that did not hide a precipice. Religion offered solace, but she lacked the strength to endure that particular suffering. She had chosen between a hell in which God did not forbid blessing Him and another under Count Octave’s roof.

I asked where she would go. Could a woman at thirty, beautiful and with undiscovered powers, live unprotected in the wilderness where I could hide her? I reassured her the Count—who had not shown himself at her house for nine years—would not go anywhere without her permission; she had his devotion, a guarantee of tranquillity. I urged her to speak with my uncle, whose moral influence equaled a minister’s. I left at midnight; Honorine seemed calm but depressed, harboring a secret purpose I could not divine.

I found Octave a few paces away in the Rue Saint-Maur—drawn by irresistible attraction, he had left our agreed spot. On hearing of my visit he fretted: “What a night my poor child will go through! Suppose I were to go to her now—what if she saw me suddenly?” I warned that she might throw herself from a window; she was one of those Lucretias who could not survive such violence. He countered that passions change like a tempest on a lake; tonight she might as well rush into his arms as she might throw herself out. He joked of opium to sleep away the torment.

Next day Gobain brought a letter: Honorine had gone to bed at six after taking a chemist’s draught and fallen asleep. He read her anguished letter aloud. She acknowledged conscience condemned her: conscience is God’s interpreter to man. If she did not reconcile with Octave she would be damned; civil law condemned her to obey; if her husband did not reject her the world would deem her pure. Legally, religiously, and socially she ought to return; it would be cruel to deprive him of happiness, children, or to blot his name. Her sufferings and repugnance she admitted were selfish and ought to be sacrificed to family. She should be a mother; a child’s caresses might wipe many tears. God, law, and society were in accord.

Yet she declared she could not live false to herself. She would become “white and innocent again” only in death. Not obstinacy, she said, but a nature that could not renew a passion once consummated. “A young bride is like a plucked flower; a guilty wife is a flower trodden underfoot,” she wrote. She could not love Octave: every time their eyes met she would see her sin reflected; his generosity would measure her crime. Neither esteem nor sacrifice could survive. She feared she would by coldness kill him or provoke suspicion. She could not bear to be treated as chattel, to kneel while he stood. She clung to the memory of her first child; religion might offer solace but she lacked energy for that suffering. She had chosen between a hell in which God did not forbid blessing Him and a hell within Octave’s house. She closed asking me to tell her where to find the solitude, peace, and silence I had promised.

After copying the letter I went to the Rue Payenne. Anxiety had conquered the opium’s power; Octave paced his garden like a madman. I gave him Honorine’s letter and urged him to answer; he must reassure modesty with experience, for it is harder to restore conviction than to seduce ignorance. He cried out with joy on reading her plea: “She is mine!” Ecstasy and torment possessed him; he would act. I visited other friends and, comparing different women, reflected that some inspire perfect fidelity while others demand extraordinary generosity. Life teaches that marriage excludes passion and family cannot be founded on tempests of love. Having dreamed impossible passion and tasted its torment, I saw modest reality and took a manful resolution.

When I returned to the Count to announce his relations’ arrival I set a condition: if he succeeded in bringing Honorine back, she must never see me again and must never know Maurice was his secretary. “Never mention my name to her, or all will be undone.” I asked Octave to get me a diplomatic post abroad instead of a home position; I did not want to marry Amélie and sought to be sent away. He understood and pressed my hand. We agreed on evening arrangements at Honorine’s.

That August evening the Countess sat under an arbor in loose white muslin with blue bows. She did not rise but signaled me to sit. “Is not life deadlocked for me?” she asked. I told her it need not be if she chose differently. She accused me of being a traitor sent by her husband. I explained I had forwarded her letter through my uncle and would bring Octave’s reply. I insisted she must read his answer in the presence of my uncle and me; there is a law, divine and human, that even hatred must obey: do not condemn without hearing the defense. She consented.

My uncle’s entrance into the little Pompadour drawing-room at ten calmed the Countess. He handed her Octave’s letter. In it Octave said that if she had trusted him and read a letter he had written five years earlier she would have spared five years of privations; he admitted faults and ignorance at the marriage’s start and confessed he had been punished for audacity. He begged mercy, saying he so valued her susceptibilities he would not bring her back to Rue Payenne where he could not bear to see her. He was decorating another house and hoped to conduct not a wife secured by ignorance but a sister who would allow him to press a fatherly kiss on her brow. He promised not to force passion on her until he felt sure of his strength to leave her free. She could live as under legitimate protection, unapproachable, occupied as she pleased, with fortune for good works. If she longed for absolution it would wait on her pride; he would not compel her by ecclesiastical terrors or legal bidding; he wanted her happiness and would not take peace except by her consent. If she persisted in condemning him to loneliness he would yield.

Honorine folded the letter, pressed it to her bosom, and, soothed, said she would avail herself of the Count’s permission to remain where she was. My involuntary cry betrayed that I was more than a messenger; she looked at me with mischief and pressed my hand at the door. I replied that I would leave tomorrow. Six days later I departed with an appointment as vice-consul in Spain. After I established myself, Count Octave wrote: if I were happy he would not write; he had entered a new life of suffering, grown young again in desire but governed by a diplomat’s prudence.

When I left, Octave had not yet been admitted to the pavilion in Rue Saint-Maur, though a letter promised permission. He waited more than a month and finally called. Gobain stalled with flattery—“Madame had to dress”—raising Octave’s hopes. Once admitted, he spoke tremblingly: he told her he had no crime but being in love with his wife and proposed she no longer work, offering certificates worth twelve thousand francs each—an offer that was freedom more than money. She took them, languidly, and understood the gift. She conceded and said she would be seen as often as he liked.

Two months of resumed habit revealed her true nature; it was a delicate springtime of love giving Octave ineffable bliss. Yet when he proposed she go to England to resume rank and life in their new residence she feared leaving their experiment. “Why not live always as we are?” she asked, and he submitted, wondering if she were making an experiment. She eventually agreed to return as his wife when he wished but warned that submission had dangers: she could be resigned but could not give the love he wanted. She loved his soul enough to die in his service—an expiation—and knelt, sobbing she might not die.

For two months he struggled. His heart was too full; he sought a friend and cried, “What shall I do?” I did not answer that letter. Two months later newspapers announced the Comtesse Octave had returned on an English vessel, restored to her family after contrived adventures. When I moved to Genoa I received formal notice of the birth of a son to the Count and Countess; I held the letter for two hours on the terrace bench. Two months later, urged by Octave and friends and broken by my uncle’s death, I agreed to marry.

Six months after the Revolution of July I received a final letter concluding the story. The Countess wrote that she was dying though already a mother—perhaps because she was a mother. She had played the part of wife well; she had deceived her husband; she had had happiness as genuine as an actress’s tears. She was dying for society, family, and marriage as early Christians died for God. She did not know of what she was dying; she tried to find out but could not. She was not perverse; she suffered from the malady of memory. She could not twice feel that love which identifies a woman with a man. To the last moment she cried in the confessional and to her husband, “Have mercy!” but there was none. She died with stupendous courage, wearing a cheerful mask while the invisible rival—her dead child—came every day to claim her. She was rent and smiled; two children existed, but the elder, the dead one, would triumph.

She told me she had said this before: the dead child calls her. The intimacy of marriage without love degraded her soul every hour. She could only weep or dream when alone. Social duties and care of her child left no moment to renew herself as she could in solitude. The constant need for watchfulness starved her heart. She lacked tender lips to cool her burning eyes; her soul acted a part and so she died. The doctors, who discovered her secret, assigned a plausible complaint as cause. Octave believed in her apparent adoration; she feared he might follow her. She begged me to be her little Count’s guardian; she had placed a codicil expressing her wish and told me not to produce it unless needed. She asked me not to mourn; she had been dead these many days if Saint Bernard was right that where there is no love there is no life.

The Consul closed the reading by locking his pocket-book. Guests asked about the Count. The Consul-General had seen a white-haired man, an old man of forty-five, pass through Genoa in search of health and amusement; the man had placed his will in the Consul’s hands and appointed him guardian of his son. He suspected the truth of Honorine’s death and that suspicion was killing him. At the steamboat Octave had entrusted his will and looked down at the Mediterranean, pondering why human nature sacrifices exquisite beings to fugitive pleasures. He confessed he was consumed by remorse. In the Rue Payenne he had been dying of joys not had; now in Italy he would die of joys he had had. He wondered where the discord lay between two equally noble natures.

A profound silence followed. The Consul then asked the two women present, “Was she virtuous?” Mademoiselle des Touches turned the question on men: are they not equally wrong when they take a young girl to wife while cherishing an angelic image and then compare us to unknown rivals? If marriage were based on passion such criticism might be fair—but such marriages ruin both sexes. Others recalled such passions devastating men and driving women to nunneries or suicide. Camille Maupin and others agreed that such exceptional souls find peace only in solitude or religious life. As the guests dispersed along the seashore, they lingered on the rarity of Honorine: perhaps the most extraordinary intellect, a pearl whose tragedy was that life alternates pain and pleasure and that to ask Dante’s Paradise of life is to demand a luxury nature resists. Those great souls still existed, they agreed, though to live by such intensity often meant sacrifice beyond the world’s comprehension.
