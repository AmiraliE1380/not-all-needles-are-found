In Saumur, a melancholic house stands beside a dilapidated artisan's home, belonging to Monsieur Grandet, a wealthy man who gained his fortune during the Revolution by acquiring church properties and vineyards. By 1816, his estate was valued at nearly four million francs, earning him admiration and fear. Grandet lived frugally, employing only a few trusted individuals, and focused on marrying off his daughter, Eugenie. The competition for her hand involved the Cruchots and the des Grassins, intensified by Grandet's purchase of the estate of Froidfond, which solidified his wealth and raised questions about love, wealth, and duty in their society.

Monsieur Grandet's cold tufa stone house serves the family and local townsfolk, with Grandet tightly controlling resources. His wife, Madame Grandet, and daughter, Eugenie, manage household tasks, while their long-serving servant, La Grande Nanon, saves for her future despite her low wages.

In November 1819, the family prepares for Eugenie's birthday, where Grandet gifts her a gold coin and Madame Grandet a new dress. Local notables arrive with gifts, creating tension as they vie for Grandet's favor. The evening is interrupted by the arrival of Charles Grandet, Monsieur Grandet's nephew from Paris, who contrasts with the provincial guests. Charles's charm captivates Eugenie, who yearns for his refinement.

Grandet receives a letter about his brother's bankruptcy and impending death, complicating his feelings about family duty. Charles feels out of place in his uncle's shabby home, while Eugenie develops feelings for him. However, Grandet dismisses the idea of a match, devastating Eugenie.

Tragedy strikes when Grandet learns of his brother's suicide. Eugenie, feeling compassion for Charles, is urged to keep the news from him. She prepares a special breakfast, but Grandet chastises them for the lavish meal. Charles learns of his father's death, shattering him, while Grandet remains focused on financial implications. Amid their shared sorrow, Eugenie's affection for Charles deepens.

At around four o'clock, Madame Grandet was surprised by her husband, Grandet, who joyfully announced a profitable wine sale. However, when Eugenie suggested helping her grieving cousin Charles, Grandet's mood soured, and he scolded her, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again. He then retreated to his den to calculate his wealth, while Charles mourned in his room, refusing to eat. Grandet dismissed the idea of mourning attire, insisting true mourning was internal. The family dinner was tense, with Madame Grandet suggesting mourning clothes, but Grandet scoffed at the expense.

Eugenie secretly visited Charles, offering comfort and awakening feelings of love. The next morning, Grandet appeared more thoughtful, planning to manipulate the situation for his financial gain. As Eugenie cared for Charles, their bond deepened, and he began to see her differently. Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, creating a charged atmosphere filled with unspoken emotions. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to discuss his brother's financial troubles privately with the Cruchots.

Grandet manipulated the conversation about bankruptcy while assessing how to navigate his brother's debts. The Cruchots suggested liquidating his brother's affairs, but Grandet insisted he was merely a wine-grower. The des Grassins arrived, offering condolences and help, but Grandet preferred to work with them. After they left, he instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey to sell his gold in Angers.

Eugenie, concerned for Charles, found him asleep surrounded by letters indicating he was preparing to leave France. A letter addressed to "My dear Annette" stirred jealousy in her. Despite her resolve not to read it, she learned of Charles's tragic situation: his father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute. Moved by his plight, Eugenie decided to offer him her savings of nearly six thousand francs. Their intimate moment marked a shift in their relationship, with Charles finding hope and Eugenie vowing to support him.

As days passed, their affection grew amidst family struggles. Grandet remained indifferent, focused on his land, while Eugenie and Charles exchanged tender glances. Three days later, Grandet took Charles to renounce his rights to his father's estate. During breakfast, letters from Paris prompted Eugenie to inquire about his affairs, but Grandet insisted on privacy. In the garden, Charles revealed he was preparing to leave for the Indies and expressed concern for Eugenie's future. They shared a passionate kiss, interrupted by Nanon. On the day of Charles's departure, sadness filled the air as the family escorted him to the diligence. Grandet unexpectedly showed generosity, arranging for his belongings to be sent to Nantes.

After Charles's departure, Eugenie faced profound loneliness, mourning his absence while keeping his room unchanged. Her beauty transformed, reflecting her love, as she followed his journey on a world map. Despite maintaining a facade of cheerfulness, her devotion to Charles overshadowed her daily life, with Nanon expressing frustrations about love and the sacrifices women make.

Two months later, Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, were anxious about a secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often looked at her aunt's portrait, searching for Charles's resemblance. When Madame Grandet discovered Eugenie's exchange of gold for Charles's affection, panic set in about facing Grandet on New Year’s Day, when he would expect to see Eugenie's gold.

As the new year approached, dread filled the household. Madame Grandet suggested avoiding a confrontation with her husband. On January 1, 1820, she requested a fire in her room to delay their visit to Grandet, who was in good humor due to a financial success. Oblivious to the family's turmoil, he pressed Eugenie for her gold after breakfast. When she claimed she no longer had it, he erupted in anger, accusing her of deceit.

Desperate to protect her mother, Eugenie pleaded for mercy, but Madame Grandet, weakened by the confrontation, was taken to bed, leaving Eugenie to face Grandet alone. His fury escalated as he demanded to know what she had done with her gold. Eugenie maintained her silence, asserting her independence, which only infuriated Grandet further. He threatened to confine her until she submitted to his authority.

As the evening wore on, Grandet dined alone, while Nanon, the servant, tried to comfort Eugenie. The tension was palpable, and Madame Grandet's health deteriorated. Months passed, and townspeople speculated about Eugenie's treatment, learning she was fed only bread and water. Despite this, Eugenie remained focused on her love for Charles.

With spring nearing, Madame Grandet sought help from notary Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her estate. This revelation shocked Grandet, prompting him to reconcile with Eugenie. However, upon entering their home, he found her and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold from Charles. Driven by greed, Grandet attempted to seize the gold, leading to a confrontation where Eugenie threatened self-harm. Hesitating, Grandet backed down, allowing her to keep the dressing-case.

The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home. M. Grandet showed unexpected tenderness, which soon revealed itself as a façade. During a family dinner, he pressured Eugenie to renounce her rights to the estate, claiming it would spare him taxes. Confused, she agreed, believing it would ease his worries. M. Grandet promised her a monthly allowance, which he never fulfilled.

In the following years, M. Grandet trained Eugenie in managing the household, instilling his miserly habits in her. Their lives became monotonous, marked by daily routines devoid of joy. After his passing, Eugenie learned from Maitre Cruchot that she inherited a vast fortune, yet her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles. Despite her wealth, she felt no happiness, overshadowed by her mother’s suffering and her unfulfilled love for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles Grandet amassed wealth through the slave trade, losing his moral compass and distancing himself from family. In 1827, he returned to Bordeaux with nearly two million francs and met Monsieur d’Aubrion, who sought a wealthy suitor for his daughter, leading Madame d’Aubrion to see potential in Charles.

In Paris, Charles grew close to the d’Aubrion family, overshadowing his thoughts of Eugenie. Meanwhile, Eugenie hesitated to read Charles's letter about his engagement, feeling devastated and recalling her mother’s words about suffering. She avoided memories of him as the parish priest urged her to consider marriage, expressing a desire to retreat from the world.

When informed of Charles's engagement, Eugenie felt betrayed and later asked the priest about remaining a virgin after marriage, revealing her conflicted feelings. She made a surprising declaration to Monsieur de Bonfons, asking him to stay but stipulating he must leave her free and settle her uncle's debts. After severing ties with Charles, she married the president, who died shortly after, leaving her a widow. Despite her wealth, she lived modestly, dedicating her life to charity and embodying resilience amidst societal constraints.
