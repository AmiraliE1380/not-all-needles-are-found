In provincial towns, certain houses evoke melancholy, particularly one in Saumur at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau. This narrow street, lined with ancient homes, reflects a rich history. Next to a dilapidated artisan's home is the mansion of Monsieur Grandet, a wealthy gentleman who gained his fortune during the Revolution by acquiring church properties. By 1816, his estate was valued at nearly four million francs, earning him both admiration and fear due to his financial acumen.

Grandet lived frugally, relying on his land and employing only a few trusted individuals. His demeanor was simple yet commanding, and he rarely spoke, often using short phrases. His stutter, mistaken for a lack of education, was a calculated facade. His primary concern was marrying off his daughter, Eugenie, to secure a favorable match.

The competition for Eugenie's hand involved two families, the Cruchots and the des Grassins, intensified by the sale of the estate of Froidfond. Grandet's purchase of this estate further solidified his wealth and influence, stirring speculation among townspeople about the implications for the Grandet family and the ongoing rivalry for Eugenie’s hand, highlighting the complexities of love, wealth, and familial duty in a changing society.

Monsieur Grandet's house is a cold, tufa stone structure resembling a jail, with a damp garden enclosed by thick walls. The main room serves multiple purposes—antechamber, salon, office, and dining room—where local townsfolk conduct business with Grandet. Adorned with gray wooden panels and faded tapestries, the hall reflects the family's modest lifestyle.

Madame Grandet and their daughter, Eugenie, live under strict conditions, with Grandet tightly controlling their resources. They manage household tasks diligently, often working late into the night. Their servant, La Grande Nanon, has worked for thirty-five years, earning a meager wage but saving for her future. Despite her rough appearance, she is devoted to Grandet, who treats her with disdain yet grudging affection.

In November 1819, the family prepares to celebrate Eugenie's birthday. Grandet gifts her a gold coin, while Madame Grandet presents a new dress. Their dinner is interrupted by local notables, including the Cruchots and the des Grassins, who bring gifts for Eugenie. The des Grassins' son, Adolphe, delights her with a workbox, while Grandet remains focused on wealth and marriage prospects for Eugenie.

As the evening progresses with card games, tensions among the guests emerge, all vying for Grandet's favor. The atmosphere blends familial warmth with cold calculations of social status and financial gain. The night takes a turn when a loud knock at the door interrupts the festivities.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door for Monsieur Grandet, who was called by his alarmed wife while the players exchanged worried glances. When a young man arrived with trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game, leaving the players uneasy.

The newcomer, Charles Grandet from Paris, was handsome and stylish, contrasting sharply with the provincial attire of the others. He greeted the company gracefully, but Grandet's stern demeanor overshadowed the welcome. Charles aimed to impress with his Parisian sophistication, bringing an extravagant collection of clothing and accessories, unaware of the modest lifestyle that awaited him. Eugenie was particularly captivated by him.

As the game of loto concluded, Nanon announced preparations for Charles's room. Eager to impress, Eugenie rushed to help, suggesting improvements for his comfort, even proposing to buy a wax candle, a luxury her father would typically deny.

Meanwhile, Madame des Grassins attempted to charm Charles, hinting at social gatherings to alleviate his boredom in Saumur. She subtly criticized Grandet's miserly nature, positioning herself as a potential matchmaker.

Later, Grandet received a letter revealing a family tragedy: his brother was bankrupt and dying, leaving Charles in a precarious situation. Grandet's reaction was mixed, concealing his thoughts while contemplating the implications of his brother's demise.

After the guests departed, Grandet's demeanor shifted, revealing a man grappling with familial duty and financial responsibility. Charles's arrival disrupts the provincial status quo, igniting curiosity and ambition among the local elite while unveiling the burdens of family legacy.

Monsieur Grandet instructs Charles on household routines, emphasizing the family's hard work and lack of wealth despite rumors of riches. Charles feels out of place in his uncle's shabby home and questions why his father sent him there.

As Charles settles in, he reflects on his visit's purpose. Meanwhile, Eugenie becomes aware of her feelings for Charles while preparing for the day. She admires her appearance but doubts her beauty, feeling unworthy of him.

Eugenie's morning is consumed with thoughts of Charles, and she asks Nanon to prepare a special breakfast to impress him. Grandet engages in a comical argument with Nanon over provisions but reluctantly allows her to make a galette.

Later, Grandet takes Eugenie for a walk, where they meet Cruchot, revealing Grandet's disdain for the idea of marrying Eugenie to Charles. This devastates Eugenie, who has begun to develop feelings for him.

Grandet soon receives news of his brother's suicide, shocking the family. Eugenie is deeply affected, feeling compassion for Charles, who remains unaware of his father's fate. Grandet insists Eugenie should not inform Charles until he returns.

Overwhelmed, Eugenie confides in her mother, who understands but urges her to respect her father's wishes. Despite the tension, Eugenie expresses her affection for Charles and desires to prepare a special breakfast for him.

After two hours of anxious preparation, Eugenie creates an unusual breakfast for Charles. When he arrives, flamboyantly dressed, he greets them cheerfully despite the recent destruction of his family estate.

As they eat, Charles expresses a desire for more extravagant food, making Eugenie wish she could provide him with a partridge. The mood shifts when Nanon brings in coffee, prompting Charles to comment on their outdated brewing method.

The atmosphere darkens with the familiar knock of Monsieur Grandet. The women panic, quickly hiding evidence of the breakfast. Grandet enters, scrutinizing the scene and chastising them for the lavish meal. Charles, oblivious to the tension, inquires about his father's health, only to learn of his father's suicide. The revelation shatters him, prompting sympathy from Eugenie and her mother, while Grandet remains detached, focusing on financial implications. Moved by Charles's grief, Eugenie feels a growing affection for him, indicating her deepening connection amid shared sorrow.

At around four o'clock, a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. She felt relieved when her husband, Grandet, entered joyfully, announcing he had sold his wine to a Belgian for a good profit. However, when Eugenie suggested helping her grieving cousin Charles, Grandet's mood soured. He scolded her for even considering spending money on him, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again.

Consumed by thoughts of investments, Grandet retreated to his secret den to calculate his wealth. Meanwhile, Charles mourned in his room, refusing to eat. Grandet dismissed the idea of mourning attire, insisting true mourning was internal. The family dinner was tense, with Madame Grandet suggesting they wear mourning, but Grandet scoffed at the expense.

Feeling compassion for Charles, Eugenie secretly visited him, offering comfort. She admired his handsome face, now marked by grief, awakening a desire for love she had never known. The next morning, Grandet appeared more thoughtful and less harsh, planning to manipulate the situation to his advantage while safeguarding his financial interests.

Eugenie cared for Charles, deepening their bond as they shared tender moments. Grateful for her kindness, Charles began to see Eugenie in a new light. As the day progressed, Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, signaling a rare moment of generosity. The atmosphere was charged with unspoken emotions as Eugenie and Charles exchanged glances filled with understanding and hope.

The town of Saumur focused on Grandet's dinner rather than the previous night's controversial wine sale. The des Grassins family learned of Guillaume Grandet's death and decided to visit that evening. The dinner atmosphere was somber, with Grandet grave and Charles silent. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to speak privately with the Cruchots.

Grandet used his stutter and feigned deafness to manipulate the conversation, discussing bankruptcy while assessing how to navigate his brother's financial troubles without damaging his reputation. The Cruchots suggested liquidating his brother's affairs to preserve family honor, but Grandet expressed reluctance, insisting he was merely a wine-grower.

As the discussion progressed, the notary cautioned against compromising integrity. The des Grassins arrived, offering condolences, and the notary asserted that Grandet intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably, surprising them. Impressed by Grandet's supposed nobility, the des Grassins offered to help manage the financial situation, but Grandet preferred to work with them rather than the Cruchots.

After the des Grassins left, Grandet instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey to sell his gold in Angers, taking advantage of rising prices. His meticulousness ensured that no one suspected his true intentions as he departed under the cover of night, reflecting a complex interplay of greed, deception, and the desire to uphold a facade of honor amidst personal gain.

Eugenie stood at the top of the stairs, reflecting on her father’s departure when she heard a cry from Charles's room. Concerned, she found him asleep in an armchair, surrounded by letters indicating he was preparing to leave France. Her heart sank upon seeing a letter addressed to "My dear Annette," stirring feelings of jealousy.

Despite her resolve not to read it, curiosity took over. The letter revealed Charles's tragic situation: his father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute. He expressed his determination to seek fortune in the Indies, lamenting that he could not return to Paris due to the shame of being the son of a bankrupt. Eugenie felt compassion and vowed to help.

As she continued reading, she learned that Charles lacked money for his journey and even a proper outfit, contemplating sailing as a common sailor. He also expressed deep love for Annette but decided to part ways for her sake, believing it would be better for both. This filled Eugenie with joy, interpreting it as a sign of his love for her.

Eugenie found another letter addressed to Alphonse, detailing Charles's financial ruin and requesting assistance. Moved by his plight, she decided to offer him her savings of nearly six thousand francs. When she approached Charles, he hesitated to accept, but she insisted, kneeling and imploring him to take the money. Overcome with emotion, he eventually agreed, recognizing their bond. He then revealed a family heirloom, asking her to keep it safe, solidifying their connection.

Their intimate moment marked a shift in their relationship, with Charles finding hope and Eugenie vowing to support him. The next day, they spent time together, their mutual affection growing amidst family struggles. Meanwhile, Eugenie's father, Grandet, returned home with news of his financial dealings, having profited from gold sales, contrasting sharply with Charles's somber reality.

As the day unfolded, tension grew. Charles grappled with responsibilities while Eugenie remained steadfast in her support. Their relationship blossomed against societal expectations, deepening as they navigated love and loss.

The following morning, the Grandet family gathered for breakfast, united in grief while Grandet remained indifferent, focused on his land. Eugenie and Charles exchanged tender glances, their love awakening amidst mourning. Charles, who had known tumultuous love, now embraced a pure affection for Eugenie.

Three days later, Grandet took Charles to the Civil courts to renounce his rights to his father's estate, a painful act. Dressed in mourning clothes, Charles appeared more mature as the family attended a Requiem for Guillaume Grandet. During breakfast, letters from Paris prompted Eugenie to inquire about his affairs, but Grandet insisted on privacy.

In the garden, Charles revealed he was preparing to leave for the Indies and expressed concern for Eugenie's future, suggesting she might find happiness elsewhere. Eugenie declared her love, and they shared a passionate kiss, interrupted by Nanon. As Charles prepared to leave, Grandet unexpectedly showed generosity, arranging for his belongings to be sent to Nantes.

On the day of Charles's departure, sadness filled the air. Nanon shed tears, and the family escorted him to the diligence, where Grandet offered parting words. As the coach departed, Eugenie wished for divine power to keep Charles close.

Meanwhile, Grandet engaged in financial dealings in Paris, acquiring a fortune through investments while managing his brother's debts. Over time, creditors grew impatient, but Grandet skillfully delayed payments, ultimately selling his investments for a substantial profit.

In the aftermath of Charles's departure, Eugenie faced profound loneliness, keeping his room as it was and mourning his absence. Her beauty transformed, reflecting her love, as she followed his journey on a world map. Despite maintaining a facade of cheerfulness, her devotion to Charles overshadowed her daily life, with Nanon expressing frustrations about love and the sacrifices women make.

Two months later, Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, were anxious about a secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often looked at her aunt's portrait, searching for Charles's resemblance. When Madame Grandet discovered Eugenie's secret exchange of gold for Charles's affection, panic set in about facing Grandet on New Year’s Day, when he would expect to see Eugenie's gold.

As the new year approached, dread filled the household. Madame Grandet suggested avoiding a confrontation with her husband. On January 1, 1820, she requested a fire in her room to delay their visit to Grandet, who surprisingly displayed good humor from a successful financial speculation. Oblivious to the family's turmoil, he pressed Eugenie for her gold after breakfast. When she claimed she no longer had it, he erupted in anger, accusing her of deceit.

Desperate to protect her mother, Eugenie pleaded for mercy, but Madame Grandet, weakened by the confrontation, was taken to bed, leaving Eugenie to face Grandet alone. His fury escalated as he demanded to know what she had done with her gold. Eugenie maintained her silence, asserting her independence, which only infuriated Grandet further. He threatened to confine her until she submitted to his authority. Heartbroken, Eugenie sought comfort from her mother, who urged reconciliation, but Grandet remained indifferent.

As the evening wore on, Grandet dined alone for the first time in years, while Nanon, the servant, tried to comfort Eugenie. The tension was palpable, and Madame Grandet's health deteriorated under the strain. Guests arriving for New Year’s festivities heightened suspicions about the family's troubles. In a small act of kindness, Nanon brought Eugenie a hare pâté, providing a glimmer of hope amidst the turmoil.

For months, Grandet visited his wife without mentioning Eugenie, who was kept under harsh conditions. As Madame Grandet's health declined, townspeople speculated about the family's situation, learning that Eugenie was fed only bread and water. Despite the gossip, Eugenie remained focused on her love for Charles and her ailing mother. As her death approached, Madame Grandet urged reconciliation, but Grandet remained indifferent.

With spring nearing, Madame Grandet's condition worsened, and she sought help from notary Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her estate. This revelation shocked Grandet, who had never considered the legal implications. Cruchot advised him to treat Eugenie kindly to avoid conflict.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Grandet decided to reconcile with Eugenie. However, upon entering their home, he found her and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold from Charles. Driven by greed, Grandet attempted to seize the gold, leading to a confrontation where Eugenie threatened self-harm if he did not relent. Hesitating, Grandet backed down, allowing her to keep the dressing-case. He then attempted to show affection, scattering coins on the bed and promising to care for them. Despite his efforts, Madame Grandet passed away peacefully, leaving Eugenie to navigate a world filled with sorrow.

The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home. M. Grandet showed unexpected tenderness, which soon revealed itself as a façade for his self-serving agenda. During a family dinner, he informed Eugenie that she was now her mother’s heiress and pressured her to renounce her rights to the estate, claiming it would spare him taxes. Confused, Eugenie agreed, believing it would ease his worries. M. Grandet promised her a monthly allowance, which he never fulfilled.

In the following years, M. Grandet trained Eugenie in managing the household and finances, instilling his miserly habits in her. Their lives became monotonous, marked by daily routines devoid of joy. Eugenie’s only social interactions were with the Cruchot family. As M. Grandet aged and became frail, she devoted herself to caring for him after he suffered a stroke.

M. Grandet’s death was uneventful, overshadowed by his obsession with hidden wealth. In his final moments, he expressed concern for his treasures rather than for Eugenie. After his passing, she learned from Maitre Cruchot that she inherited a vast fortune, yet her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles.

Eugenie mourned her father and lost love in solitude, confiding only in Nanon. Despite her wealth, she felt no happiness, overshadowed by her mother’s suffering and her unfulfilled love for Charles. As time passed, the Cruchots and other suitors surrounded Eugenie, eager to win her favor, but she remained indifferent, still longing for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles Grandet amassed wealth in the Indies through the slave trade. In the Indies, he adopted the alias "Shepherd" to conceal his identity while accumulating wealth. By 1827, he returned to Bordeaux aboard the "Marie Caroline," bringing gold-dust worth nearly two million francs. There, he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, who was struggling financially after marrying into a fashionable family. The d’Aubrions sought a wealthy son-in-law, and Madame d’Aubrion saw potential in Charles.

In Paris, Charles grew close to the d’Aubrion family, who aimed to marry him to their unattractive daughter. Madame d’Aubrion promised to help him gain a noble title and a lucrative court position, overshadowing his thoughts of Eugenie. Meanwhile, Eugenie in Saumur reflected on her past with Charles and hesitated to open a letter revealing his engagement to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Devastated, she grappled with loss, recalling her mother’s words about suffering.

Eugenie avoided memories of Charles as the parish priest urged her to consider marriage. Overwhelmed, she expressed a desire to retreat from the world. Madame des Grassins brought news of Charles's engagement, and despite her calm demeanor, Eugenie felt turmoil. She later asked the priest if it would be a sin to remain a virgin after marriage, revealing her conflicted feelings.

Eugenie’s marriage to the president was announced, but he died shortly after his election, leaving her a widow. Despite her wealth, she lived modestly, dedicating her life to charitable works. Her story reflects resilience and compassion, as she supported the less fortunate while remaining steadfast in her independence, embodying a spirit that transcended societal expectations.
