**A Woman of Thirty**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**Dedication**  
To Louis Boulanger, Painter.

---

**Summary**

**I. Early Mistakes**  
In April 1813, a father and his beautiful daughter, Julie, arrive at the Tuileries in Paris. The father, anxious and aging, enjoys the attention they receive, as many young men admire Julie's beauty. As they await a military review commanded by Napoleon, Julie expresses impatience, eager to see the Emperor. The father, however, is preoccupied with thoughts of her future happiness amidst the looming dangers of war.

The review is a grand spectacle, showcasing the Old Guard, but it foreshadows the impending disaster of Napoleon's campaign. Julie's youthful excitement contrasts with her father's somber reflections on the future, hinting at the challenges that lie ahead.

**II. A Hidden Grief**  
Years later, Julie, now married to Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, finds herself unhappy. Her marriage, once filled with promise, has become a source of sorrow. She feels trapped in a life devoid of true love and fulfillment, haunted by memories of her first love, who died tragically. The weight of her unfulfilled desires and the constraints of societal expectations weigh heavily on her.

Julie retreats to the countryside, seeking solace in solitude. She struggles with her identity as a mother to her daughter, Helene, feeling disconnected from her child due to the circumstances of her marriage. The narrative explores the complexities of motherhood, love, and the societal pressures that shape a woman's life.

**III. At Thirty Years**  
As time passes, Julie's beauty fades, and she becomes increasingly isolated. Her daughter, Helene, grows up, and the bond between them is strained. Julie's past continues to haunt her, and she grapples with feelings of regret and loss. The arrival of Charles de Vandenesse, a charming young man, reignites feelings within her, but she is torn between her past and her present.

Charles is captivated by Julie's beauty and depth, and their interactions spark a connection that challenges Julie's resolve. However, she remains haunted by her memories and the societal expectations that bind her.

**IV. The Finger of God**  
The story takes a darker turn as Helene's life becomes intertwined with that of a mysterious stranger, a murderer on the run. The tension escalates as the General, Julie's husband, confronts the intruder, leading to a dramatic confrontation that tests the family's bonds. Helene's loyalty to her mother and her burgeoning feelings for the stranger create a complex web of emotions.

The narrative culminates in a tragic climax, where the consequences of past choices and the weight of societal expectations collide. Julie's struggle for autonomy and the desire for love ultimately lead to a heartbreaking resolution.

**V. Two Meetings**  
As the story unfolds, the themes of love, loss, and the search for identity resonate deeply. Julie's journey reflects the struggles of many women of her time, caught between societal norms and personal desires. The relationships she navigates reveal the complexities of human emotion, and the impact of choices made in the name of love.

In the end, Julie's story serves as a poignant exploration of the human condition, highlighting the sacrifices made for love and the enduring quest for happiness amidst the constraints of society.

This summary captures the essence of Balzac's *A Woman of Thirty*, focusing on the key themes and character developments while maintaining the narrative's emotional depth.
