In provincial towns, certain houses evoke melancholy, notably one in Saumur at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau. The narrow lane, lined with ancient homes, reflects a long history. Beside a dilapidated artisan's house stands the mansion of Monsieur Grandet, a wealthy man who amassed his fortune during the Revolution by buying church properties. By 1816 his estate neared four million francs, earning both admiration and fear for his financial shrewdness.

Grandet lived frugally on his land, employed only a few trusted servants, and maintained a simple yet commanding presence. He spoke little, using short phrases; his stutter, mistaken for ignorance, was in fact a calculated facade. His chief concern was marrying off his daughter Eugenie to secure a favorable match.

Rivals for Eugenie's hand included the Cruchot and des Grassins families, a competition intensified by the sale of the Froidfond estate. Grandet's purchase of Froidfond further consolidated his wealth and influence, fueling town speculation about the family's future and the rivalry for Eugenie—revealing the complex interplay of love, money, and familial duty in a changing society.

Monsieur Grandet's house is a cold tufa stone structure like a jail, with a damp garden enclosed by thick walls. The main room serves as antechamber, salon, office, and dining room, where townsfolk conduct business with Grandet. Gray wooden panels and faded tapestries reflect the family's modest, cheerless lifestyle.

Madame Grandet and their daughter, Eugenie, live under strict conditions, tightly controlled by Grandet. They manage household tasks diligently, often working late. Their servant, La Grande Nanon, has worked thirty-five years for a meager wage but has saved for the future. Despite her roughness, she is devoted to Grandet, who treats her with disdain yet grudging affection.

In November 1819 the family prepares to celebrate Eugenie's birthday. Grandet gives her a gold coin; Madame Grandet a new dress. Local notables attend—the Cruchots and the des Grassins—bringing gifts. The des Grassins' son, Adolphe, delights Eugenie with a workbox. Grandet remains focused on wealth and marriage prospects for his daughter.

As the evening proceeds with card games, tensions rise as guests vie for Grandet's favor. The atmosphere blends familial warmth with cold calculations of status and gain. The night changes when a loud knock interrupts the festivities.

Nanon lights a candle and opens the door. Monsieur Grandet, called by his alarmed wife, returns while players exchange worried glances. A young man arrives with trunks; Grandet brusquely tells his wife to return to the game, leaving the guests uneasy.

The newcomer, Charles Grandet from Paris, is handsome and stylish, sharply contrasting the provincial attire. He greets the company gracefully, but Grandet's sternness overshadows the welcome. Charles aims to impress with Parisian sophistication, bringing extravagant clothes and accessories, unaware of the modest lifestyle ahead. Eugenie is particularly captivated.

When the loto ends, Nanon announces preparations for Charles's room. Eager to impress, Eugenie rushes to help, suggesting small comforts—even offering to buy a wax candle, a luxury her father would deny.

Madame des Grassins tries to charm Charles, hinting at social gatherings to alleviate his boredom and subtly criticizing Grandet's stinginess, positioning herself as a potential matchmaker.

Later Grandet receives a letter revealing a family tragedy: his brother is bankrupt and dying, leaving Charles precarious. Grandet's reaction is mixed; he conceals his thoughts while weighing the implications of his brother's demise.

After the guests depart, Grandet shows a more complex side: a man wrestling with familial duty and financial responsibility. Charles's arrival unsettles the provincial balance, sparking curiosity and ambition among the local elite and exposing burdens of family legacy.

Monsieur Grandet instructs Charles on household routines, emphasizing the family's hard work and lack of wealth despite rumors of riches. Charles feels out of place in his uncle's shabby home and wonders why his father sent him there. As he settles in, he contemplates his visit's purpose. Eugenie, meanwhile, becomes aware of her feelings for Charles while preparing for the day. She admires herself yet doubts her beauty, feeling unworthy.

Her morning is consumed by thoughts of Charles; she asks Nanon to prepare a special breakfast to impress him. Grandet engages in a comic argument with Nanon over provisions but reluctantly allows her to make a galette.

Later, Grandet takes Eugenie for a walk where they meet Cruchot, revealing Grandet's disdain for marrying Eugenie to Charles. This devastates Eugenie, who has begun to love him.

Soon Grandet learns his brother has committed suicide, shocking the family. Eugenie is deeply affected, feeling compassion for Charles, who remains unaware of his father's fate. Grandet insists Eugenie not inform Charles until he returns. Overwhelmed, Eugenie confides in her mother, who understands but urges respect for her father's wishes. She still wants to prepare the special breakfast.

After two hours of anxious preparation Eugenie creates an unusual breakfast. Charles arrives flamboyantly dressed, cheerful despite the destruction of his family estate. As they eat, Charles longs for more extravagant food, making Eugenie wish she could provide a partridge. The mood lightens when Nanon brings coffee; Charles comments on their outdated brewing method.

The atmosphere darkens with Grandet's familiar knock. The women panic, hiding evidence of the breakfast. Grandet enters, scrutinizes the scene, and scolds them for the lavish meal. Charles, oblivious to the tension, inquires about his father's health, only to learn of his father's suicide. The revelation shatters him, prompting Eugenie and her mother to show sympathy, while Grandet remains detached, thinking of financial implications.

Moved by Charles's grief, Eugenie’s affection deepens, hinting at a growing bond formed amid shared sorrow and the unsettling interplay of duty, money, and feeling.

At around four o’clock a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. Relief followed when her husband returned joyfully, announcing he had sold wine to a Belgian at a good profit. But when Eugenie suggested helping their grieving cousin Charles, Grandet’s mood soured; he scolded her for even thinking of spending money on him and threatened to send her away if she mentioned it again.

Consumed by investments, Grandet withdrew to his secret den to calculate his wealth. Charles, meanwhile, mourned in his room and refused to eat. Grandet dismissed external signs of mourning as wasteful, insisting true grief was internal. The family dinner was tense: Madame Grandet proposed wearing mourning, Grandet scoffed at the expense, and Eugenie, moved with compassion, secretly visited Charles that evening to comfort him. She admired his handsome face, now marked by grief, and felt a stirring of love she had never known. The next morning Grandet seemed thoughtful and less openly harsh, already plotting how to turn events to his financial advantage while safeguarding his reputation.

Eugenie tended to Charles; their closeness grew through tender, unspoken moments. Grateful, Charles began to see Eugenie anew. Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, an occasion that hinted at a rare show of generosity. The town of Saumur buzzed about Grandet’s dinner rather than the controversial wine sale, and the des Grassins family learned of Guillaume Grandet’s death and planned to visit that evening.

At the meal, the mood was somber; Grandet grave, Charles silent. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, leaving Grandet to converse privately with the Cruchots. Grandet employed a practiced stutter and feigned deafness to steer the talk, pretending to weigh moral questions about bankruptcy while assessing how to handle his brother’s financial mess without sullying his own name. The Cruchots advised liquidating the brother’s affairs to preserve family honor; Grandet pretended reluctance, claiming merely to be a wine-grower.

As discussion continued, the notary warned against compromising integrity. The des Grassins arrived and, hearing the notary’s assertion that Grandet intended to honorably liquidate his brother’s debts, were pleasantly surprised and eager to help manage affairs. Grandet, calculating, preferred dealing with them rather than the Cruchots. After the visitors left, he quietly instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a covert trip to Angers to sell gold—taking advantage of favorable prices. His meticulous secrecy ensured none suspected his true intent as he departed by night, revealing a complex mix of greed, deception, and a desire to keep a facade of honor while profiting.

Later, Eugenie, standing on the stairs after her father’s departure, heard a cry from Charles’s room. Finding him asleep in an armchair, she discovered letters that hinted he was preparing to leave France. Her heart sank when she saw a letter addressed to “My dear Annette,” which stirred jealousy, though she initially resolved not to read it. Curiosity prevailed. The letter disclosed Charles’s tragic circumstances: his father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute; he felt ashamed to return to Paris as the son of a bankrupt and planned to seek fortune in the Indies. He considered sailing as a common sailor because he lacked funds and a proper outfit. He confessed deep love for Annette but chose to part for her sake, believing distance would spare her shame.

Eugenie, moved by his plight, read on and found another letter addressed to Alphonse requesting assistance. Interpreting Charles’s renunciation of Annette as a sign of his nobility and perhaps love, she decided to help materially. She resolved to offer her almost six thousand francs in savings. Approaching Charles, she met his hesitation with insistence: she knelt and implored him to take the money. Overcome, he eventually accepted; he then entrusted her with a family heirloom to keep safe, binding them in a private intimacy. Their shared moment marked a shift—their relationship deepened into hope and mutual reliance.

The following day they spent time together, their affection growing amid family strain. Grandet returned having profited from his gold sales in Angers, a success that contrasted sharply with Charles’s ruin. Tension tightened as Charles wrestled with responsibilities and departure plans while Eugenie remained steadfast.

Three days later Grandet took Charles to the Civil courts to renounce his rights to his father’s estate—an ordeal that made the young man appear matured by sorrow. The family attended a Requiem for Guillaume Grandet. During breakfast, Paris letters arrived; Eugenie asked about Charles’s affairs, but Grandet was curt and private. In the garden Charles confessed plans to sail to the Indies and fretted about Eugenie’s future, suggesting she might find happiness elsewhere. Eugenie declared her love; they shared a passionate kiss interrupted by Nanon. Grandet, in a surprising gesture, arranged to have Charles’s possessions sent to Nantes.

On the day of departure, sorrow was palpable. Nanon wept; the family escorted Charles to the diligence. As the coach left, Eugenie silently prayed for some divine power to keep him near. Meanwhile Grandet was active in Paris, maneuvering investments while managing his brother’s debts—delaying payments to creditors until he could sell at a profit. Creditors grew impatient, but his skillful stalling ultimately yielded substantial gain.

After Charles’s departure, Eugenie faced profound loneliness. She kept his room untouched and mourned in private, her beauty altered by love and loss. She tracked his journey on a world map and maintained an outward cheerfulness to others, though her devotion to Charles dominated her daily life. Nanon, observing, voiced frustrations about the sacrifices of love and the toll it takes on women.

Two months later, Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, were anxious about a secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often studied her aunt’s portrait, searching for Charles’s resemblance. When Madame Grandet discovered Eugenie had given away gold for Charles’s affection, panic set in over facing Grandet on New Year’s Day, when he would expect to see the gold.

As the new year approached, dread filled the household. Madame Grandet suggested avoiding a confrontation with her husband. On January 1, 1820, she had a fire lit in her room to delay visiting Grandet, who, after a successful speculation, was in good humor and unaware of the family turmoil. After breakfast he pressed Eugenie for her gold; when she said she no longer had it, he exploded, accusing her of deceit.

Eugenie pleaded for mercy to protect her mother, but the shock sent Madame Grandet to bed, leaving Eugenie alone with Grandet’s mounting fury. He demanded to know what she had done with the gold; she remained silent, asserting her independence, which only enraged him further. He threatened to confine her until she submitted. Heartbroken, Eugenie sought comfort from her mother, who urged reconciliation, but Grandet remained indifferent.

That evening Grandet dined alone for the first time in years. Nanon, the servant, tried to comfort Eugenie, bringing a small kindness—a hare pâté—that offered a glimmer of hope. Madame Grandet’s health deteriorated under the strain. Guests arriving for New Year’s festivities heightened suspicions about the family’s troubles. Townspeople later learned Eugenie was fed only bread and water; despite gossip, she remained focused on her love for Charles and her ailing mother. As death neared, Madame Grandet again urged reconciliation, but Grandet stayed unmoved.

In spring the widow’s condition worsened; she consulted notary Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her estate. This shocked Grandet, who had never considered legal consequences. Cruchot advised treating Eugenie kindly to avoid conflict. Realizing the stakes, Grandet decided to reconcile.

Soon after, Grandet returned home to find Eugenie and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold from Charles. Driven by greed, he tried to seize it. Eugenie threatened self-harm if he persisted; hesitating, Grandet relented and allowed her to keep the case. He then feigned affection—scattering coins on the bed and promising to care for the treasure—yet his motives were mercenary. Despite his sudden attention, Madame Grandet died peacefully, leaving Eugenie bereft.

After her mother’s death Eugenie felt renewed attachment to her childhood home. M. Grandet’s tenderness proved a façade for self-interest. At a family dinner he announced she was her mother’s heiress and pressured her to renounce her rights to the estate, claiming it would spare him taxes. Confused and trusting, Eugenie agreed, believing it would ease his worries. Grandet promised her a monthly allowance, which he never paid.

In the years that followed, Grandet trained Eugenie in household and financial management, imposing his miserly habits. Their lives settled into monotonous routines devoid of joy. Eugenie’s only social contacts were the Cruchot family. As Grandet aged and became frail, she devoted herself to nursing him after a stroke.

Grandet’s death was uneventful and overshadowed by his obsession with hidden wealth. In his final moments he worried about his treasures rather than Eugenie. After his passing, Maitre Cruchot informed her she had inherited a vast fortune, yet her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles.

Eugenie mourned both her parents and her lost love in solitude, confiding only in Nanon. Despite newfound wealth, she felt no happiness—her mother’s suffering and unfulfilled love haunted her. Over time the Cruchots and other suitors circled, eager to win her favor, but she remained indifferent, still longing for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles Grandet amassed wealth in the Indies through the slave trade. His initial success of six thousand dollars led him to Africa, where he lost his moral compass and grew distant from his family. In the Indies he adopted the alias "Shepherd" to conceal his identity while accumulating wealth. By 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline, bringing gold-dust worth nearly two million francs. There he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, who was financially strained after marrying into a fashionable household. The d’Aubrions sought a wealthy son-in-law, and Madame d’Aubrion saw potential in Charles.

In Paris Charles ingratiated himself with the d’Aubrion family, who intended him for their unattractive daughter. Madame d’Aubrion promised to secure him a noble title and a lucrative court position, eclipsing any thoughts of Eugenie. Back in Saumur, Eugenie reflected on her past with Charles and hesitated to open a letter that revealed his engagement to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Devastated, she wrestled with loss and remembered her mother’s counsel about suffering.

Eugenie avoided memories of Charles as the parish priest urged her to consider marriage; overwhelmed, she longed to withdraw from the world. When Madame des Grassins announced Charles's engagement, Eugenie concealed her turmoil. She later asked the priest whether it would be sinful to remain a virgin after marriage, exposing her inner conflict.

Eugenie’s subsequent marriage to the president was announced, but he died shortly after his election, leaving her a widow. Though wealthy, she lived modestly and devoted herself to charitable works. Her story shows resilience and compassion: she supported the less fortunate, preserved her independence, and embodied a spirit that transcended societal expectations.
