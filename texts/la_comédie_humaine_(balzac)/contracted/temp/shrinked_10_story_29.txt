Produced by John Bickers; translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley. Dedication: To Maria.

In some provincial towns a cloistered melancholy hangs over certain houses; silence seems to freeze life, broken only by a solitary figure at a window. Such an air brooded over a house in Saumur, down a steep, little-used street of pebble pavement and three-century-old buildings. These wooden houses, prized by antiquarians, show great carved oaken beams, slate façades, worn window-sills with neglected flower-pots, and heavy studded doors bearing traces of history—Protestant marks or forgotten bourgeois insignia—so that the history of France seems recorded in wood and stone.

Beside a dilapidated dwelling stands a country gentleman’s mansion; its stone arch faintly shows armorial bearings eroded by revolutions since 1789. The ground floors are not modern shops but plain dark rooms where goods lie exposed; a girl knitting will call her parents to serve customers. The local climate governs commerce: merchants, wine-growers, and coopers watch the weather anxiously, fearing frost or rain that could ruin them. On Saturdays vineyards call their owners away, streets fall silent, and neighbors observe one another’s doings.

The most melancholy mansion belonged to Monsieur Grandet. To understand the house one must know the man. Grandet began life as a master cooper in 1789 and married the daughter of a rich wood merchant. With savings and his wife’s dowry he bought prime vineyard land during the sale of church property and gained a reputation as protector of local nobility and supplier to Republican armies. By shrewd investments in vineyards and estates—and by 1806 serving as mayor—he steadily increased his fortune. That year he also inherited three fortunes, adding vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey to his holdings, making him one of the region’s wealthiest men.

Townspeople speculated about the extent of his riches; he had a knack for timing sales to secure maximum profit, and his financial cunning made him both admired and feared. By 1816 his fortune was estimated at nearly four million francs, and many believed he held as much again in cash. Former ridicule faded as his acts took on the weight of community law. Frugality governed his life; he spent only for necessities.

Grandet’s manner was plain and unpretentious. He spoke little and stuttered in prolonged conversation—an awkwardness that concealed calculation. He never committed without consulting his wife, whom he kept dependent. Stout, round-faced, and crafty in appearance, he dressed simply.

Only a handful entered his house, among them the young magistrate C. de Bonfons, who hoped to win Grandet’s daughter Eugénie. The town’s social life turned on competition for her—chiefly between the powerful Cruchot family, who sought her for their nephew, and the des Grassins, who wanted her for their son Adolphe. In early 1811 the Cruchots gained advantage: when the estate of Froidfond was sold they negotiated its purchase for Grandet, who paid in gold—an ostentatious display that confirmed his wealth and influence throughout Saumur.

It is now clear what is meant by “the house of Monsieur Grandet,” a cold, silent dwelling above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The entrance, with two pillars and an arch of tufa—the soft white stone that barely lasts two centuries—bears weathered holes that give the façade a jail-like look. Above the arch is a crumbling bas-relief of the four seasons, topped by a plinth where plants, even a small cherry tree, have taken root.

The door is solid oak, split and bolted with iron. A small rusted grating in the middle panel lets curious visitors peer into a dark vault leading to a garden enclosed by thick damp walls, remnants of the ramparts that border neighboring plots.

The principal ground-floor room serves as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining room. Entirely wooden with gray panels and exposed beams, it has two street-facing windows, a white stone chimney topped by an old brass clock and a greenish mirror, and antique chairs in faded tapestry. In each corner stand closets or buffets with dirty shelves. Between the windows sits an old card table with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. Red gros de Tours curtains—a rare luxury—contradict Monsieur Grandet’s frugality. A straw chair by the window lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair occupy the rest.

For fifteen years Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet’s lives have been governed by strict economy: fires lit only on specified dates, the foot-warmer used through cold months. La Grande Nanon, the family servant for thirty-five years, earned only sixty francs a year yet managed to invest in an annuity. Unattractive but indispensable, she cooked, washed, and ran domestic affairs with tireless loyalty; Grandet’s rare favors—an old watch, a pair of stockings—were treated as monumental largesse.

During the difficult 1811 grape harvest, Grandet’s attitude toward Nanon combined dependence and domination: he relied on her labor while keeping strict authority, and she, grateful for the employment and meager kindnesses, remained devoted. Their bond was one of power and dependence rather than affection.

Nanon’s domain contrasted with the rest of the house. Her kitchen was a model of cleanliness and order; she worked late and slept in a small closet-like space, while the upper floors remained bare and unadorned compared with the hall’s modest comforts.

On a November evening in 1819, Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The household prepared for a gathering as local notables arrived to pay respects. Grandet preserved the ritual of giving Eugenie a gold coin; Madame Grandet presented a new dress. Grandet urged Eugenie to save for her future marriage, underscoring the family’s thrift and fixed habits.

Guests, including the des Grassins family, arrived. Madame des Grassins, lively and pushy, and her son Adolphe presented gifts that provoked jealousy among others. Compliments and attentions masked rivalry and calculation; beneath laughter and light games, attendees subtly vied for a share of Grandet’s wealth. Grandet watched with pride and possessiveness over his daughter, manipulating relationships for his benefit. The evening blended familial warmth with cold calculation—until a loud knock at the door signaled an unexpected interruption that would change the night.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door; Monsieur Grandet followed. His wife called anxiously, the players exchanged uneasy looks, and Monsieur des Grassins suggested leaving. A young man arrived with a porter and trunks. Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game and went out to speak with the newcomer.

The players sat but did not resume. Madame des Grassins asked if he was from Saumur; her husband said Paris. The notary checked the hour; Abbé Cruchot inquired if the gentleman was young. Eugénie noticed Nanon’s absence; others guessed the newcomer might be a relative. Madame Grandet urged continuation, aware of her husband’s irritation. Adolphe hinted the young man could be a cousin but was silenced.

Grandet returned alone; the traveler entered and received polite bows and curtsies. Madame Grandet offered warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed concern. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugénie but said he was not tired.

Adjusting his eyeglass, Charles surveyed the room and the loto game. At twenty-two, handsome and confident, he intended to impress provincial relatives with Parisian polish. Sent by his father to stay with his uncle in Saumur, he displayed fashionable dress and urban ease: a hunting costume, several waistcoats, a gold toilet set—and, despite instructions to travel modestly, he had taken a private coupé to flaunt his means.

His elegance sharply contrasted with the provincials. The shabby Cruchots were taken aback; locals met his nonchalance with simultaneous admiration and disdain. Eugénie was captivated, longing for the world he represented; enchanted by his appearance and delicate possessions, she yearned to connect.

The loto ended when Nanon returned for sheets for Charles’s bed; Madame Grandet followed. Guests, sensing the evening’s close, began to leave. Eugénie, seized by impulse, excused herself to help prepare his room, suggesting small refinements—a warming pan, a wax candle instead of tallow—using her birthday as an excuse to persuade Nanon.

While Eugénie fussed, Madame des Grassins engaged Charles, vying for his attention and hinting at local prospects. Abbé Cruchot made sly remarks that revealed underlying tensions and ambitions. Grandet, who had read a letter from his brother about impending bankruptcy and asking him to protect his son, masked his emotions. When guests departed, Grandet offered Charles a modest room, downplaying his wealth. The evening closed with speculation: Charles’s presence might unsettle Saumur. The Cruchots and des Grassins, seeing a common rival, resolved to unite against any attachment between Eugénie and her cousin and to manipulate the situation to their advantage.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late to discuss business and set the household schedule: breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, dinner at five. He professed poverty despite appearances, insisting his fortune came from hard work. He told Nanon to bring candles; Charles at first refused but accepted one so worn it looked like tallow.

Grandet led him through the house, avoiding the cold stairwell; as they climbed Charles’s expectations dwindled—the staircase and decor were shabby, and he wondered why his father had sent him. On the landing he noticed the doors, especially one walled up that led to Grandet’s office — the secret room where Grandet did business and hoarded wealth. Across from it, Eugenie, Grandet’s daughter, sat in her room; after a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon if needed because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon returned with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed with a grumble as he left.

Alone, Charles surveyed the shabby surroundings and questioned his father’s intentions; he felt out of place and uncertain of his purpose. Meanwhile Eugenie rose early with a new sense of beauty and longing. Dressing, she admired herself but felt inadequate beside her cousin. The garden and courtyard, once dull, now seemed charming; her heart swelled with unfamiliar emotions toward Charles and she longed to impress him.

Eugenie hurried to Nanon, asking her to prepare cream for Charles’s breakfast. Nanon reminded her they needed Grandet’s permission for provisions. When Grandet entered, he and Nanon quarreled over supplies, exposing his miserly nature; reluctantly he agreed to give some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast Grandet took Eugenie to the fields, where they met Cruchot, the notary. Grandet spoke of his land and the recent sale of poplars, displaying his calculating side. Cruchot mentioned gossip that Charles might be a suitor for Eugenie. Grandet reacted vehemently, declaring he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin — a statement that shattered Eugenie’s hopes.

Back at the house, news arrived of Grandet’s brother’s suicide — a development with dire consequences for Charles. The subsequent meal was heavy with unspoken grief. Eugenie, moved and tearful, felt deep sympathy; Grandet remained indifferent. Despite the risk of provoking him, Eugenie and Nanon conspired to prepare Charles a better breakfast, and her affection for him deepened as she worked.

After two hours’ work Eugenie prepared a simple breakfast that departed from the family’s custom of a standing midday meal of bread, fruit, and wine. She set the table and placed an armchair for her cousin Charles, anxious about her father’s reaction; Madame Grandet promised to take responsibility if he returned.

Charles arrived in Parisian fashion, cheerful despite a difficult journey. He said he seldom ate before midday but welcomed the meal and joked about wanting a partridge. As they sat, his charm captivated Eugenie; she admired his elegance and felt a flutter of affection. Their conversation highlighted the contrast between his life of luxury and her modest upbringing. When Nanon brought eggs, Charles praised Eugenie, making her blush.

He showed Eugenie his ring and their fingers briefly touched, deepening the spark between them. The mood shifted when Nanon brought coffee: Charles called it outdated, and the women exchanged anxious glances, fearing Monsieur Grandet’s return.

When Grandet entered he surveyed the scene with suspicion, noting the breakfast and especially the sugar, and demanded its source. Unaware of the household’s tensions, Charles asked for sugar for his coffee, but Grandet rebuffed him, suggesting more milk instead. Eugenie, quietly defiant, returned the sugar to the table.

Grandet’s demeanor stiffened as he spoke to Charles, then revealed devastating news: Charles’s father had died by suicide, leaving him bankrupt and penniless. The revelation shattered Charles; he broke down in tears. Eugenie and her mother felt deep compassion, while Grandet remained detached and immediately focused on the financial consequences, lecturing Eugenie about the dishonor of bankruptcy.

Grandet declared Charles must go to the West Indies to seek fortune. Eugenie offered to help; Grandet reluctantly agreed to fund the journey, and for a moment a warm connection appeared between father and daughter. The house remained heavy with grief as Charles’s cries echoed. Finding him mourning in his room, Eugenie urged him to preserve his honor and resolved to share his sorrow—her life irrevocably changed by their entwined loss and love.

About four o’clock a sharp knock at the door startled Madame Grandet.

Grandet burst in, rubbing his hands. “Wife,” he announced, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I made a deal for my vintage at two hundred francs a puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; here are six louis for you. In three months, wines will have fallen.”

News of the sale spread panic through Saumur. Eugenie, excited, asked if he had a thousand puncheons. “Yes, little one.” “Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?” “Yes.” “Then, father, you can easily help Charles.” The name of his neglected nephew stung Grandet. He scolded Eugenie for presuming to advise him, insisting any duty to Charles would be discharged on his terms and threatening to send her to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon if she meddled.

When Madame Grandet said Charles had not come down, Grandet asked what he was doing. “He is weeping for his father,” Eugenie answered. Grandet, momentarily bewildered, retreated to his secret room to consider investing the proceeds in public funds. His thinning forest had already brought six hundred thousand francs; with this sale his total reached nine hundred thousand. Tempted by the idea of twenty percent, he rationalized a more modest eight percent, imagining that in two years he might withdraw fifteen hundred thousand in gold.

Nanon’s knock summoned him to dinner. He accepted a plan for eight percent, murmuring that hunger would bring his nephew from grief. At table he learned Charles refused to eat; he commented curtly, and Nanon approved, “So much saved.” When Madame Grandet suggested mourning, Grandet mocked the expense: “What will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not in clothes,” and told her to buy her mourning out of her six louis; a hat-band would suffice. Eugenie sewed in silence while he ran through calculations. The town buzzed about the sale; Nanon’s spinning and Grandet’s repeated maxim “Nothing should be wasted” were the only other sounds as he pursued arithmetic and plans.

That night Grandet, preoccupied with present gain and dismissive of any future life, revealed how money shaped his laws, politics, and morals. He bargained with conscience and religion, ordered Madame Grandet off to her prayers, and finally growled, “The devil take your good God!” He visited Charles’s dark room to berate him gently in his own miserly fashion. “Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural...I am a good uncle to you. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” He noticed a wax candle and scoffed at the theft of so small an item. Madame Grandet and Eugenie, listening from the landing, fled in fear.

Alone afterward, Madame Grandet prayed for him and worried about the threatened punishment for Eugenie should she interfere. Eugenie, meanwhile, lay restless; her concern for Charles swelled into a personal disturbance. In the early hours she rose, found him asleep in an armchair exhausted by grief, and, touched by his quiet face smoothed by sorrow, watched him with a feeling she scarcely understood. He woke, apologized; she counseled him to go to bed and fled, ashamed and secretly happy. This innocent boldness marked a turning point: she feared what he would think of her visit and hoped he would believe she loved him.

The next morning she and her mother waited anxiously for Grandet. He arrived distracted but outwardly affectionate, kissing Eugenie and sitting at breakfast as if forgetting his harsh threats. “What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he remarked; Nanon said he was asleep. “So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” Grandet mocked. His unexpected softness surprised Madame Grandet, but it was only surface. Grandet, a poor sleeper, had spent the night calculating schemes to enlarge his fortune and dominate others, equating imposition with power.

That day he contrived a way to restore his brother’s honor without disbursing a franc: to save Charles by manipulating public opinion in Paris, a sport of prudence for him. In his absence, Eugenie found tender pleasure in caring for her cousin. She prepared his breakfast with self-forgetting devotion, deciding to bring it to his room to spare him the shame of eating before her father. Her anticipation and care lit her day; she set the table, listened for his footsteps, and joined him and her mother. The warmth and attention of the provincial household—so different from the indifference Charles had known in Paris—moved him. Eugenie’s innocence and genuine affection drew him closer; they shared an unspoken understanding.

Life went on around this quiet intimacy: Grandet continued his calculations, Nanon spun, and later Grandet invited the Cruchots to dinner. Yet beneath the household routine, Eugenie’s tenderness and Charles’s gratitude suggested changes that money alone could not govern, and hinted at the complex obligations that would shape their lives.

Saumur was more excited about the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than it had been the night before at the sale of his vintage, which had seemed a betrayal to the wine-growers. Grandet, who considered himself superior to those he tricked, cared little for their judgment.

When the des Grassins learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and death, they came that evening to offer sympathy and to learn why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At five o’clock Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle, the notary, arrived in their Sunday best. The dinner was sombre: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet speaking little—a proper meal of condolence. Afterwards Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

When the women left, Grandet displayed the cunning that earned him the nickname “the old dog.” He used a practiced stutter—an affectation—to control and frustrate his guests while steering the conversation toward bankruptcy. The president of the commercial court explained that a respected man facing insolvency could have a receiver appointed, distinguishing liquidation from outright failure; sometimes liquidation could be managed without the courts: if the merchant did not file his schedule and no creditor appeared, the family or heirs could liquidate the business. Debts could also be bought at a discount so that, if titles to all debts were acquired, the brother or heirs would owe nothing.

The notary urged this course as a way to save Grandet’s name. Grandet, mindful of family reputation and his nephew’s prospects, weighed the advantages and professed ignorance of Parisian tricks, saying he attended only to vineyards and local business. The president offered to go to Paris to deal with creditors and suggested Grandet might provide some assets to facilitate negotiations.

Before Grandet committed, the des Grassins arrived and declared they intended to liquidate the brother’s debts honorably; the banker applauded the sentiment and Madame des Grassins even offered to go to Paris, competing with the president’s offer. Favoring the banker, Grandet pulled him aside to discuss a separate investment in government bonds and asked him to say nothing of these plans to the Cruchots—discretion to avoid annoyance. They agreed to meet again the next day for final instructions.

As word of his supposed generosity spread, the town began to admire Grandet again, quickly forgiving the sale of the vintage. Once the house was closed, he ordered Nanon to keep silent about his plans and prepared a secret trip to sell gold in Angers while the market was favorable. He took precautions to avoid attention. In the early hours, as he and Nanon prepared to leave, Eugenie—worried about Charles—thought she heard a cry and considered checking but was distracted by seeing her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg. Grandet told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried off in the carriage, determined to reach Angers before morning.

“My father has gone,” Eugenie thought as she listened from the top of the stairs. The house lay hushed; the carriage’s rumble faded and a cry from her cousin’s room cut the silence. A thin thread of light from the doorway lit the old staircase.

“He suffers!” she cried, running up. Charles sat in an armchair, head bowed, a pen fallen from his hand; his breathing was labored. On the table she saw sealed letters to tradesmen—signs he was settling affairs to leave France—and two open letters. The address “My dear Annette” struck her to the core. Rooted, she felt shame and curiosity struggle; curiosity won. She adjusted his head, kissed his hair, and read.

Charles’s letter to Annette explained that misfortune had ruined him: his father had killed himself, their fortune was gone, and he was penniless. He could not remain in Paris, where the scorn for a ruined man would be intolerable. He would not ask Annette to sacrifice comforts to keep him; better to part. He planned to try his fortune in the Indies or America—perhaps to go to sea—and might return aged and altered; he urged Annette to treasure their happiness but accepted that he could not demand fidelity, for marriage or new ties might become necessities. The letter ended abruptly, as if his hand had left the page.

Eugenie excused the coldness in the words, convincing herself a girl in love might not see its selfishness. Young and pure, she read passion into his phrases; “My dear Annette” became music to her. She mourned his misfortune and thought, “I have gold; I will give it to him.”

The second letter, to his friend Alphonse, asked him to settle affairs: sell possessions, collect whatever could be recovered, keep his horse Briton if possible, press builders to delay delivery of an unpaid carriage, and pay off debts, including six louis lost at cards. The letter showed a practical, calculating mind intent on avoiding dishonor and salvaging what remained.

Overwhelmed with compassion and admiration, Eugenie ran to her room and opened the old oak cabinet where she had hidden a red velvet purse—a grandmother’s relic. Inside were coins: various foreign gold pieces, rare rupees—about five thousand eight hundred francs in all. Joy and resolve filled her. Secret love for her cousin and pity for his plight dissolved propriety. She returned upstairs at once, candle and purse in hand.

Charles was awake, stunned at seeing her. Eugenie, trembling, confessed she had read the letters and then, without hesitation, offered him her savings. She called herself a poor girl who wanted nothing, a sister needing to help a cousin. Kneeling, she implored him to accept her gold and, by taking it, to show that he respected her and was generous. Tears fell from Charles onto her hands; moved, she poured the coins onto the table. “You will be rich,” she cried; “this gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

Charles could not refuse. He rose, kissed her, and finally revealed he would reciprocate not with money but with an object he held dear: a leather dressing-case containing, in a hidden drawer, two portraits by Madame Mirbel, set with pearls—his mother and his father, who were Eugenie’s aunt and uncle. He declared the case more precious to him than life, and begged Eugenie to be its guardian. If she guarded it and, in the worst event his fortune vanished, the pearls and gold in the case would repay her; yet he demanded that it ultimately be destroyed to prevent its passing into other hands. This exchange of trust and sacred confidence marked a turning point; Eugenie looked at him with her first deep, womanly affection. He kissed her hand, calling her an “angel of purity,” and vowed that feeling and sentiment must be everything between them henceforth.

They exchanged gentle words and good-nights. Eugenie, practical and blushing, suggested Charles keep the treasures in her old cabinet. He sighed in his mourning but kissed her hand and, as she closed the door, he murmured the ruin of his fortunes. “What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she answered naively. He scoffed; she retorted about Froidfond and Noyers, and he dismissed them as poor farms. The exchange was sweet and foolish: from that moment, beneath his black, Charles began to wear roses.

Next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie and Charles together in the garden. He remained sorrowful; the three women of the house, respectful of his grief, allowed him the morning in the hall while they tended chores and awaited tradesmen for neglected business. Around five, Monsieur Grandet returned from Angers, unexpectedly jubilant, having made fourteen thousand francs on an exchange of gold. He had left his groom at Angers to look after the exhausted horses.

“I am hungry,” he proclaimed upon entering. Nanon scolded him for not eating. Shortly Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet barely registered his nephew’s presence, absorbed in his financial success and amused to baffle others. Des Grassins, the banker, informed him that gold fetched high prices in Angers and that people had come from Nantes; he talked of sending theirs. Grandet curtly refused, hinting at giving his old friend respite from wasted exertion. A startled consultation between the two men followed; they glanced often at Charles. Des Grassins learned that Grandet intended to invest a sum that might yield—so it was suggested—an enormous return; talk even floated of producing a hundred thousand francs a year, though the deputies’ figures and Grandet’s actual plans remained shrouded in his dry, cautious talk.

Des Grassins took the moment to offer to go to Paris and asked Charles if he had any commissions. Charles declined stiffly. Des Grassins rebuked the nephew for the curt reply; he reminded him of family ties and honor. Charles, pale, kissed his uncle and withdrew. Eugenie watched her father with secretly swelling admiration for his mastery over affairs.

Grandet dismissed Des Grassins with a jocular “decoy those people; lead them by the nose,” and the two men shook hands. Grandet resumed his armchair and asked Nanon for black-current ratafia. His excitement made him fidget at first and then sing snatches of a ditty, pacing in what Nanon called “his dancing steps.” The women exchanged uneasy glances at his towering contentment; his gaiety frightened them when it rose to a peak. He chose to go to bed early, expecting others to follow his lead. Madame Grandet, caught in his current, lived according to his will.

During the two hours before retiring, Grandet grew more talkative and philosophical about money, offering peculiar maxims and showing the mindset that governed his life: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.” His cheerfulness came with small generosity—offering Nanon a share of ratafia—and an undertone of the calculation that made him formidable. The evening passed under a mood blending domestic routine, latent anxieties about affairs, and the quiet bloom of a new intimacy between Eugenie and Charles, sealed by her sacrifice and his trust.

Thus the house resumed its ordinary hum: tradesmen, household tasks, and the unspoken commerce of feelings and fortunes. Eugenie’s secret gift and Charles’s confided treasures had altered their relation; Grandet’s profit from the Angers exchange had reawakened his habit of managing and tightening the household’s finances. Between these threads—Eugenie’s selfless devotion and Grandet’s thrifty triumph—Balzac sketches the beginnings of passions, calculations, and the mingling of sentiment with the sober arithmetic of money that will shape the family’s future.

The next morning the family breakfasted together in a private, intimate mood. Grief had drawn Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles closer; even Nanon felt sympathy. The old man, absorbed in thrift and intent on sending Charles away to avoid expense, remained aloof, busy with farm work and leaving Madame Grandet to watch over Eugenie and Charles as their relationship quietly deepened.

For Eugenie, love grew from their shared secret. Their bond took on the simplicity and tenderness of childhood: soft glances, gentle words, and small consolations. Eugenie delighted in comforting Charles, soothing his pain with the sweetness of new love; Charles, hardened by worldly passions, found in her a pure, sincere attachment and prized the modest, unpretentious life they shared. While Grandet worked in the fields after breakfast, Charles savored the company of Eugenie and her mother, charmed by virtues he had once thought limited to Germany. Eugenie became his ideal; she surrendered to their love without foreseeing the looming separation.

Three days after the des Grassins left, Grandet took Charles to the civil courts to renounce his claim to his father’s estate—a legal act that felt like a painful betrayal. Charles signed powers of attorney, got a passport, and sold his belongings to prepare for departure. Grandet admired Charles’s new appearance in mourning and regarded him as ready to seek his fortune. When Charles gathered valuables to sell, Grandet offered to buy them, valuing them at nearly a thousand francs; Charles gratefully accepted. He gave his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet, who was deeply moved. Grandet then insisted on paying for Charles’s passage to the Indies, a generosity that paradoxically cost him little.

As Charles prepared to leave, Eugenie confessed her sorrow; he acknowledged his obligations and his coming-of-age. The family wore mourning for a Requiem Mass for Guillaume Grandet, and Charles received letters from Paris about his affairs, which he shared with Eugenie. In the garden he assured her his affairs were in order, that his effects would be sent to Nantes, and urged her not to tie her future to his uncertain fate. Still, their love held firm and they kissed passionately.

On the day of departure Grandet unexpectedly packed Charles’s belongings for Nantes. The family saw him off at the diligence; after he left the house felt empty. Eugenie, determined to preserve his memory, kept his room unchanged and conspired with Nanon. Her beauty grew more radiant and dignified as she followed his voyage on a map and confided her constant love only to Nanon, who, though resigned, deeply empathized.

Two months later, the once-monotonous life of Eugénie and her mother was consumed by the secret that bound them: Charles filled their thoughts, and Eugénie even looked for him in her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugénie had given Charles, and panic followed.

“What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?” Madame cried. They spent a morning in dread, missed high Mass, and feared the coming confrontation. Madame, unprepared for winter and chilled by her husband’s impending wrath, suggested consulting Monsieur des Grassins in Paris, but Eugénie insisted it was too late.

On the cold morning of January 1, 1820, Madame asked for a fire in her room to avoid the confrontation. Grandet, unexpectedly cheerful, agreed and revealed he had received a large sum from investments. Boasting over breakfast and unaware of their anxiety, his polite New Year wishes only deepened their unease.

After breakfast he demanded to see Eugénie’s gold and ordered its return. Cornered, she said she no longer had it. His anger flared; he accused her of deceit and pressed to know what she’d done with the money. Eugénie defended herself and asserted her independence; he threatened confinement to her room on bread and water. Heartbroken, she fled to her mother, who pleaded for her, but Grandet remained obstinate. Eugénie resolved to stand firm in her love for Charles. Nanon, fearing for her, vowed support and that evening secretly brought a hare pâté, deepening their bond as they faced Grandet’s harshness together.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

For months the old wine-grower tended his ailing wife while never mentioning their daughter Eugénie, who remained confined to her room by his orders. Madame Grandet’s health declined as her husband grew harsher and even began to err in business. Townspeople learned Eugénie was given only bread and water, with Nanon secretly bringing extra food; the community condemned Grandet, and Eugénie’s gentle attendance at Mass drew sympathy and curiosity.

Eugénie’s chief sorrow was her mother’s deterioration, for which she often blamed herself. Madame Grandet tried to comfort her and urged reconciliation, begging her husband to restore their daughter to the family, but he remained indifferent as his wife weakened.

In spring Madame confided in the Cruchots, who were appalled and discussed possible legal consequences of Eugénie’s confinement. Eugénie overheard but, fearing scandal, begged them to stay silent and resolved to obey her father for the family’s honor.

One day, torn between obstinacy and a wish to embrace his daughter, Grandet was visited by the notary Cruchot, who warned that if Madame died Eugénie’s inheritance might force the sale of property and threaten the father’s control of his wealth. Pressed by this, Grandet began to soften.

Returning home to reconcile, he found Eugénie and her mother admiring a gold dressing-case from Charles. Possessive at the sight of the gold, Grandet confronted her; Eugénie demanded it be returned to its rightful owner and, in despair, threatened self-harm. Shocked, Grandet relented, allowed her to keep the case, scattered coins on the bed, and promised kindness. Despite this brief gentleness, Madame Grandet died peacefully in October 1822, urging Eugénie to seek happiness in heaven and fearing for her in a selfish world.

The day after her father’s death Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home, where she had endured much suffering, including her mother’s death; familiar objects moved her to tears. Her father’s sudden tenderness—taking her to breakfast and watching her with an almost kind gaze—made the household suspect his mind was failing. But at the mourning dinner he revealed his plan.

“Maitre” Cruchot explained Grandet did not want to divide property or pay taxes on supposed funds; to avoid this he needed Eugenie to renounce her rights so he could retain control. Confused, Eugenie asked for the deed to sign. Grandet, visibly moved, instead proposed she trust him and accept a monthly allowance of a hundred francs. She agreed; he clasped her hand and declared they were quits. The next morning she signed away her inheritance. A year later he had not paid the promised money; when she reminded him he awkwardly offered jewels.

Over the next two years Grandet trained Eugenie in running the household and managing finances, instilling his frugality. By the third year she became mistress of the house, and their monotonous life continued for five years. Eugenie’s sadness was well known though she never spoke of it. Her only company was Nanon and the Cruchots with their circle, who played whist with her father.

In 1827 Grandet, now eighty-two, suffered a stroke. As he declined Eugenie devoted herself to his care, feeling increasingly isolated. His death was uneventful; to the end he remained fixated on hidden wealth. When the priest came for last rites, Grandet’s avarice surfaced in his final words, urging Eugenie to take care of his riches.

After his death Eugenie was left alone with only Nanon, her loyal servant and confidante. Maitre Cruchot then informed her she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite this fortune, her thoughts centered on finding her cousin Charles. In the empty house she and Nanon lamented their isolation while news of her wealth spread through the province.

Eugenie’s first act was to secure an annuity for Nanon, who soon married Antoine Cornoiller and became Eugenie’s housekeeper. Cornoiller managed the estate; life for the farmers continued unchanged. At thirty Eugenie had never known joy; her childhood had been shadowed by her mother’s suffering and an unfulfilled love for Charles, who had left her with only memories. Mourning delayed thoughts of marriage. The Cruchots flattered her; Monsieur de Bonfons ingratiated himself, Madame des Grassins pushed a match with the Marquis de Froidfond, and Eugenie’s indifference was misread as interest—all while Charles had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. His early ventures yielded six thousand dollars, and he soon learned that human trafficking was the most profitable trade. He went to Africa, bought enslaved people, and combined that traffic with other lucrative goods, driven to return to Paris wealthy and regain social standing.

Experience hardened him. He became skeptical and amoral, believing one society’s condemnation might be another’s praise. He grew ruthless—engaging in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and reselling pirate goods bought cheaply. Memories of Eugénie faded; he viewed his family with contempt and regarded Eugénie as little more than a creditor. To conceal his identity while pursuing wealth he used the alias “Shepherd.” His efforts paid off: by 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline with nearly two million francs in gold-dust, planning further profit at the Paris mint.

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman connected to the royal court, returning with his family. The d’Aubrions, once prominent, were now financially strained. Madame d’Aubrion, anxious to secure her family’s future, sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter. She saw Charles as suitable and promised to help him gain a title and a comfortable income through marriage. The match would elevate both families and allow them to live comfortably in Paris.

Intoxicated by ambition, Charles entertained the match. He consulted Annette, who encouraged him, seeing the advantage for her friend of an influential, affluent husband. Newly confident after the Indies, Charles felt ready to embrace this new life.

Des Grassins, aware of Charles’s return and impending marriage, approached him about settling his father’s debts. Newly wealthy, Charles dismissed the appeal, saying his father’s failures were no longer his problem. In August, Eugénie, sitting where they had once pledged their love, received a trembling letter from Charles: he recounted his success, announced the engagement—marriage as a pragmatic step to status—and declared love in marriage a delusion. Devastated but resigned, she recalled her mother’s warning about suffering and resolved to a life of prayer and acceptance.

Eugenie returned from the garden cautiously, avoiding the corridor haunted by memories of her cousin. The day was pivotal: the parish priest—connected to the Cruchots and allied with Monsieur de Bonfons—had been urged to speak to her about marriage. Consumed by unhappiness, Eugenie confessed a desire to withdraw from the world and live only for God. The priest warned against such a drastic step, reminding her of duties to society and the poor.

Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance and despair, to reveal that Charles Grandet had returned from the Indies and was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Eugenie masked her shock. Madame des Grassins produced a letter from her husband outlining Charles’s situation, including his father’s bankruptcy and the marriage’s consequences. Contempt and sorrow filled Eugenie; she returned the letter unread.

Tormented, Eugenie asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be a sin, exposing her inner conflict. She secluded herself in her father’s secret room, skipped dinner, and avoided guests. Later, curiosity charged the evening when she unexpectedly asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay—an act taken as a pledge of marriage.

Privately, Eugenie told the president she could give only friendship and asked him to settle her uncle’s debts in exchange for her hand. The eager president accepted. He hurried to Paris, met creditors, and arranged payment with interest. Carrying a letter from Eugenie to Charles explaining her decision and its conditions, he informed Charles, who was initially excited but then shocked by the implications when he learned of Eugenie’s fortune. Madame d’Aubrion, unaware of the president’s interventions, remained confident of the marriage.

The president returned to Saumur and announced his marriage to Eugenie. Though wealthy, she lived simply and devoted herself to charity. Her husband entered politics but died soon after being elected deputy. Widowed, Eugenie lived piously, dedicating her fortune to the community and rejecting worldly desires. Rumors linked her to the Marquis de Froidfond, but she remained independent, supported by loyal servants Nanon and Cornoiller. Her story reveals the tension between wealth and personal fulfillment, showing her struggle to meet social expectations while staying true to her values.
