**EUGENIE GRANDET**

**I**

In certain provincial towns, some houses evoke profound melancholy, reminiscent of cloisters or ruins. One such house in Saumur, at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau, embodies this sadness. The now seldom-traveled street is narrow and silent, lined with ancient homes over three centuries old. These houses, with their carved oaken beams and worn window-sills, narrate the history of France through their architecture.

Next to a dilapidated artisan's home stands the mansion of Monsieur Grandet, a country gentleman whose door bears remnants of armorial bearings. The ground-floors of merchants here are not shops but dark, deep rooms without glass, where goods are displayed simply. The inhabitants of Saumur, deeply connected to the land, are acutely aware of the weather's impact on their livelihoods, with conversations revolving around the harvest and market conditions.

On Saturdays, merchants leave for their vineyards, leaving the streets empty. Life unfolds outdoors, with neighbors observing each other closely. The old town of Saumur, once home to local nobility, houses the Grandet family, whose story is central to this narrative.

Monsieur Grandet, known as le Pere Grandet, was a master cooper who became wealthy during the Revolution by purchasing church properties. With his wife’s dowry, he acquired prime vineyards and farms, becoming a respected figure in Saumur. His wealth grew through shrewd investments and a keen understanding of the wine trade, making him a significant player in the local economy.

By 1816, Grandet's estate was estimated at nearly four million francs, with many believing he had hidden treasures. His reputation was built on financial acumen and market manipulation, earning him both admiration and fear. He lived frugally, relying on his land for sustenance and employing only a few trusted individuals.

Grandet's demeanor was simple and unassuming, yet he commanded respect. He rarely spoke, preferring short, decisive phrases. His stutter, often mistaken for a lack of education, was a calculated facade. He was a man of routine, dressed consistently in practical clothing, and his life revolved around his daughter, Eugenie, whom he intended to marry off advantageously.

The social dynamics of Saumur were influenced by the competition for Eugenie's hand in marriage. Two families, the Cruchots and the des Grassins, vied for her favor, each believing they could secure a more advantageous match. The Cruchots, led by the ambitious C. de Bonfons, sought to align with the Grandets through marriage, while the des Grassins, represented by the charming Adolphe, aimed to win Eugenie’s heart.

As the story unfolds, the tension between these families escalates, with stakes raised by the potential inheritance of Eugenie’s fortune. The arrival of the estate of Froidfond for sale creates further intrigue, as both factions see it as a means to elevate their status and secure Eugenie’s future.

Monsieur Grandet, seizing the opportunity, purchases the estate, further solidifying his wealth and influence. This acquisition sends ripples through Saumur, as townspeople speculate on the implications for the Grandet family and the ongoing competition for Eugenie’s hand.

In this world of ambition, avarice, and social maneuvering, the fate of Eugenie Grandet hangs in the balance, reflecting the complexities of love, wealth, and familial duty in a changing society.

**Summary:**

Monsieur Grandet's house is a cold, silent tufa stone structure, resembling a jail with its weathered archway and crumbling bas-relief of the seasons. The entrance features a solid oak door with a rusty grating, leading to a dark vault and a damp garden enclosed by thick walls. The main room, a large hall, serves as an antechamber, salon, office, and dining room, where local townsfolk conduct business with Grandet. Adorned with gray wooden panels, an old brass clock, and faded tapestries, the hall reflects the family's modest lifestyle.

Madame Grandet and their daughter, Eugenie, live under strict conditions, with Grandet tightly controlling their resources. They manage household tasks diligently, often working late into the night. La Grande Nanon, their servant for thirty-five years, earns a meager wage but has saved enough to secure her future. Despite her rough appearance, she is devoted to Grandet, who treats her with a mix of disdain and grudging affection.

In November 1819, the family prepares to celebrate Eugenie's birthday. Grandet gifts her a gold coin, while Madame Grandet presents a new dress. Their dinner is interrupted by local notables, including the Cruchots and the des Grassins, who bring gifts for Eugenie. The des Grassins' son, Adolphe, delights her with a workbox, while Grandet remains focused on wealth and potential marriage prospects for Eugenie.

As the evening progresses with card games, underlying tensions and ambitions among the guests emerge, all vying for Grandet's favor and wealth. The atmosphere blends familial warmth with cold calculations of social status and financial gain. Grandet's miserly nature overshadows genuine emotions, leaving the women blissfully unaware of his true wealth and the surrounding machinations.

The night takes a turn when a loud knock at the door interrupts the festivities, signaling an unexpected visitor and heightening the household tension.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door, followed by her master, Monsieur Grandet. His wife, alarmed, called out to him, while the players exchanged worried glances. Monsieur des Grassins suggested they leave, sensing something ominous. When a young man arrived with a porter carrying trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game, leaving the players uneasy.

The newcomer was Charles Grandet, the son of Grandet from Paris. Handsome and dressed in the latest fashion, he sharply contrasted with the provincial attire of the others. As he entered, he greeted the company gracefully, but Grandet's stern demeanor overshadowed the warm welcome. Madame Grandet offered him hospitality, but Grandet interrupted, insisting he needed no refreshment.

Charles, sent to Saumur for the first time, aimed to impress with his Parisian sophistication. He brought an extravagant collection of clothing and accessories, intending to showcase his status, unaware of the modesty of his uncle's home and the provincial lifestyle that awaited him.

The provincial guests, including the Cruchots and des Grassins, were taken aback by Charles's elegance. They scrutinized him with a mix of admiration and disdain, feeling both intrigued and threatened by his presence. Eugenie Grandet, Charles's cousin, was particularly captivated, longing for the refinement he represented, contrasting sharply with her mundane life.

As the game of loto concluded, Nanon returned to announce preparations for Charles's room. Eugenie, eager to impress her cousin, rushed to help with the arrangements, suggesting improvements for his comfort, even proposing to buy a wax candle, a luxury her father would typically deny.

Meanwhile, Madame des Grassins attempted to charm Charles, hinting at social gatherings that could alleviate his boredom in Saumur. She subtly criticized Grandet's miserly nature and suggested that Charles would find more enjoyment outside his uncle's home. The conversation hinted at potential romantic interests, with Madame des Grassins positioning herself as a matchmaker.

As the evening progressed, Grandet received a letter revealing a family tragedy: his brother was bankrupt and dying, leaving his son Charles in a precarious situation. The letter implored Grandet to care for Charles, emphasizing the urgency of the request. Grandet's reaction was mixed, concealing his thoughts while contemplating the implications of his brother's demise.

After the guests departed, Grandet's demeanor shifted, revealing a man grappling with familial duty and financial responsibility. The evening set the stage for a complex interplay of relationships, ambitions, and the looming specter of wealth and poverty that would shape the lives of all involved.

In summary, Charles Grandet's arrival in Saumur disrupts the provincial status quo, igniting curiosity and ambition among the local elite while unveiling the burdens of family legacy and expectations of wealth.

Monsieur Grandet, a miser, instructs his nephew Charles on household routines upon his arrival, emphasizing his hard work and lack of wealth despite rumors of riches. Charles feels out of place in his uncle's shabby home and is warned about the ferocious dog. Disheartened by the dilapidated state of the house, he questions why his father sent him there.

As Charles settles in, he reflects on his visit's purpose. Meanwhile, Eugenie, Grandet's daughter, becomes aware of her feelings as she prepares for the day. She admires her appearance and feels hope regarding her cousin Charles, though she struggles with self-doubt, believing she is not beautiful enough for him.

Eugenie's morning is consumed with thoughts of Charles, and she asks Nanon, the servant, to prepare a special breakfast to impress him. Grandet, however, is stingy and engages in a comical argument with Nanon over provisions. Despite his frugality, he reluctantly allows Nanon to make a galette for the young ones.

Later, Grandet takes Eugenie for a walk, where they meet Cruchot, the notary. Their conversation reveals Grandet's disdain for the idea of marrying Eugenie to Charles, stating he would rather throw her into the Loire than allow such a union. This declaration devastates Eugenie, who has begun to develop feelings for Charles.

Grandet soon receives news of his brother's suicide, shocking the family. Eugenie is deeply affected, feeling compassion for Charles, who remains unaware of his father's fate. Grandet insists Eugenie should not inform Charles until he returns, revealing his cold-heartedness.

Overwhelmed, Eugenie confides in her mother, who understands but urges her to respect her father's wishes. Despite the tension, Eugenie expresses her affection for Charles and desires to prepare a special breakfast for him, showcasing her growing attachment.

The narrative highlights the contrast between Grandet's miserly nature and Eugenie's budding love for Charles, capturing the complexities of familial relationships, societal expectations, and the innocence of first love amid financial hardship and emotional turmoil.

After two hours of anxious preparation, Eugenie created an unusual breakfast for her cousin Charles, deviating from their family's usual meal of bread, fruit, and wine. She set the table carefully, worried about her father's reaction if he returned to find such a spread. Madame Grandet reassured her, promising to take the blame if needed. When Charles arrived, he was flamboyantly dressed and greeted them cheerfully, despite the recent destruction of his family estate.

As they sat down to eat, Charles expressed his hunger and desire for more extravagant food, making Eugenie wish she could provide him with a partridge. Their conversation highlighted the stark contrast between Charles's Parisian lifestyle and Eugenie's simple country life. Charles complimented Eugenie, causing her to blush, and they shared a moment of connection. However, the mood shifted when Nanon brought in coffee, prompting Charles to comment on their outdated brewing method.

The atmosphere darkened with the familiar knock of Monsieur Grandet, Eugenie's father. The women panicked, quickly hiding evidence of the breakfast. Grandet entered, scrutinizing the scene and sensing something was amiss. He chastised them for the lavish meal, unaware of its significance to Charles. As he prepared his own meager breakfast, he noticed the sugar and questioned its source, leading to a tense silence among the women.

Charles, oblivious to the tension, asked about his father's health, only to learn of his father's death by suicide. The revelation shattered him, and he wept openly, prompting sympathy from Eugenie and her mother. Grandet, however, remained detached, focusing on the financial implications of Charles's loss rather than the emotional devastation.

Moved by Charles's grief, Eugenie felt a growing affection for him. She and her mother decided to comfort him, but Charles was consumed by sorrow, lamenting his father's struggles. Eugenie encouraged him to think of his honor, trying to redirect his focus amidst his despair. The two women returned to their sewing, reflecting on the gravity of the situation and the stark contrast between their quiet lives and the turmoil surrounding them.

As they sewed in silence, Eugenie contemplated wearing mourning for her uncle, indicating her deepening connection to Charles and the shared grief that now bound them.

At around four o'clock, a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. Concerned for her husband, she was relieved when Grandet entered joyfully, announcing he had sold his wine to a Belgian for a good price and made a significant profit. However, when Eugenie suggested helping her grieving cousin Charles, Grandet's mood soured. He scolded her for even considering spending money on him, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again.

Consumed by thoughts of investments, Grandet retreated to his secret den to calculate his growing wealth. Meanwhile, Charles mourned in his room, refusing to eat. Grandet dismissed the idea of mourning attire, insisting true mourning was internal. The family dinner was tense and silent, with Madame Grandet suggesting they wear mourning, but Grandet scoffed at the expense.

Feeling compassion for Charles, Eugenie secretly visited him, offering comfort. She admired his handsome face, now marked by grief, and felt a deep connection awakening a desire for love she had never known. 

The next morning, Grandet's demeanor shifted; he appeared more thoughtful and less harsh. He planned to manipulate the situation to his advantage, intending to help Charles while safeguarding his financial interests. He devised a scheme to impress the townspeople without spending his own money, using his brother's failure to gain respect.

Eugenie busied herself caring for Charles, bringing him breakfast and attending to his needs. Their bond deepened as they shared tender moments amidst their sorrows. Grateful for her kindness, Charles began to see Eugenie in a new light, appreciating her innocence and compassion.

As the day progressed, Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, signaling a rare moment of generosity. The household buzzed with activity as they set the table, and Charles, though still grieving, joined them with quiet dignity. The atmosphere was charged with unspoken emotions, as Eugenie and Charles exchanged glances filled with understanding and hope, bridging the gap between their worlds through shared suffering and budding affection.

At this moment, the town of Saumur was more intrigued by Grandet's dinner for the Cruchots than by the previous night's controversial wine sale. Grandet, a shrewd miser, paid little attention to public opinion, believing himself superior to the community he often deceived.

The des Grassins family soon learned of Guillaume Grandet's death and decided to visit him that evening to express their condolences and understand his motives for inviting the Cruchots. The dinner atmosphere was somber, with Grandet grave, Charles silent, and Eugenie and Madame Grandet saying little. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to speak privately with the Cruchots.

Grandet, known for his cunning, employed his stutter and feigned deafness to manipulate the conversation, avoiding responsibility for his actions. He engaged in a discussion about bankruptcy with Monsieur C. de Bonfons, who attempted to guide him through the complexities of insolvency. Grandet, while pretending to be confused, was actually assessing how to navigate his brother's financial troubles without tarnishing his own reputation.

The Cruchots suggested that Grandet could liquidate his brother's affairs to preserve his family's honor. However, Grandet expressed reluctance to engage in matters outside his expertise, insisting he was a simple wine-grower focused on his vines. The conversation turned to the possibility of buying up his brother's debts at a reduced rate, which Grandet found intriguing but complicated.

As the discussion progressed, the notary interrupted to caution against compromising their integrity. Just then, the des Grassins arrived, expressing their condolences for the Grandet family's misfortunes. The notary seized the opportunity to assert that Grandet intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably, surprising the des Grassins, who had expected him to act selfishly.

Impressed by Grandet's supposed nobility, the des Grassins offered to help manage the financial situation, but Grandet preferred to work with them rather than the Cruchots. He confided in the banker about his interest in investing in government bonds, indicating his desire to profit from the situation while maintaining a façade of familial duty.

After the des Grassins left, Grandet instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey. He planned to sell his gold in Angers, taking advantage of rising prices due to military preparations. Grandet's meticulousness ensured that no one in the neighborhood suspected his true intentions as he departed under the cover of night.

In summary, Grandet's dinner served as a backdrop for his cunning manipulation of those around him, as he navigated familial obligations and financial opportunities while maintaining his reputation as a respectable community member. His actions reflected a complex interplay of greed, deception, and the desire to uphold a facade of honor amidst personal gain.

Eugenie stood at the top of the stairs, reflecting on her father’s departure. The house fell silent, and soon she heard a cry from her cousin Charles's room. Concerned, she rushed upstairs to find him asleep in an armchair, exhausted from writing letters. She noticed the addresses of various businesses, realizing he was preparing to leave France. Her heart sank upon spotting a letter addressed to "My dear Annette," igniting feelings of jealousy and despair.

Despite her initial resolve not to read it, curiosity overwhelmed her. As she read, she learned of Charles's tragic situation: his father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute. He expressed his determination to seek fortune in the Indies, lamenting that he could not return to Paris, where he would face scorn as the son of a bankrupt. Eugenie felt compassion for him, recognizing his plight and vowing to help.

She continued reading, discovering that Charles had no money for his journey or even a proper outfit. He contemplated sailing as a common sailor if necessary. The letter revealed his deep love for Annette but also his decision to part ways for her sake, believing it would be better for both. This filled Eugenie with joy, interpreting it as a sign of his love for her.

Eugenie then found another letter addressed to Alphonse, where Charles detailed his financial ruin and requested assistance in settling his affairs. Moved by his situation, Eugenie decided to offer him her savings, which she had carefully accumulated. She retrieved a purse filled with gold coins, worth nearly six thousand francs, and approached Charles, who was initially hesitant to accept her gift.

Eugenie insisted, kneeling before him and imploring him to take the money. Overcome with emotion, Charles eventually agreed, recognizing the depth of their bond. He then revealed a precious family heirloom, a dressing case containing portraits of his parents, asking her to keep it safe. Their exchange solidified their connection, transcending mere familial ties.

As they shared this intimate moment, both felt a shift in their relationship. Charles, despite his misfortunes, began to see hope in their bond, while Eugenie vowed to support him. The next day, they spent time together, their mutual affection growing stronger amidst their family struggles.

Eugenie's father, Grandet, returned home with news of his financial dealings, having profited from the sale of gold. His jovial demeanor contrasted sharply with Charles's somber reality, highlighting the disparities in their lives. Grandet's focus on wealth and status overshadowed the emotional turmoil surrounding his daughter and nephew.

As the day unfolded, the tension between the characters became palpable. Charles grappled with his newfound responsibilities and the weight of his family's legacy, while Eugenie remained steadfast in her support. Their relationship blossomed against societal expectations and familial obligations, setting the stage for future challenges.

In the end, both Eugenie and Charles found solace in each other, their bond deepening as they navigated the complexities of love, loss, and the harsh realities of their world.

The following day, the Grandet family gathered for breakfast, creating a scene of domestic intimacy. Grief united Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles, while the old man, focused on his avarice, remained indifferent to their bond. He busied himself with his land and vineyards, allowing the young couple to express their feelings under Madame Grandet's watchful eye.

Eugenie experienced the awakening of love, having shared a secret with Charles that deepened their connection. Their relationship blossomed amidst mourning, as they found joy in each other's company, exchanging tender glances and words. Charles, who had previously known only tumultuous love, now embraced a pure affection for Eugenie and even the old house, which he had once found ridiculous.

After breakfast, while Grandet worked, Charles enjoyed the simple pleasure of being with Eugenie and her mother, appreciating their unworldly beauty. Eugenie became his ideal, reminiscent of Goethe's Margaret before her fall. Their innocent love was tinged with the awareness of impending separation.

Three days after des Grassins' departure, Grandet took Charles to the Civil courts to renounce his rights to his father's estate, a painful act for Charles. He also arranged for a passport and sold his belongings, which pleased Grandet. Dressed in mourning clothes, Charles appeared more mature, and the family attended a Requiem for Guillaume Grandet.

During breakfast, Charles received letters from Paris, prompting Eugenie to inquire about his affairs. Grandet, irritated by the question, insisted on privacy. Later, in the garden, Charles revealed that his affairs were well-managed and that he was preparing to leave for the Indies. He expressed concern for Eugenie's future, suggesting she might find happiness elsewhere.

Eugenie, however, declared her love for him, and they shared a passionate kiss, interrupted by Nanon. As Charles prepared to leave, Grandet showed unexpected generosity, taking an interest in his nephew's departure and arranging for his belongings to be sent to Nantes.

The day of Charles's departure was filled with sadness. Nanon, despite her usual cheer, shed tears for him. The family escorted Charles to the diligence, where Grandet offered parting words of encouragement. As the coach departed, Eugenie wished for divine power to keep Charles close.

Meanwhile, Grandet engaged in financial dealings in Paris, acquiring a significant fortune through investments. His cunning allowed him to manage his brother's debts while maintaining his reputation. Over time, creditors grew impatient, but Grandet skillfully delayed payments, leveraging their changing moods to his advantage.

As months passed, many creditors forgot their claims, and Grandet continued to manipulate the situation, ultimately selling his investments for a substantial profit. Des Grassins, now living in Paris, faced personal troubles, while Grandet distanced himself from any obligation to him.

In the aftermath of Charles's departure, Eugenie faced profound loneliness. She kept Charles's room as it was, hoping for his return. Her beauty transformed, reflecting the depth of her love. She followed his journey on a world map and mourned his absence while maintaining a facade of cheerfulness around others.

Eugenie's love became a constant presence in her life, overshadowing her daily activities. Nanon, sympathetic to her plight, expressed her frustrations about love, highlighting the sacrifices women make. Despite the pain, Eugenie's devotion to Charles remained unwavering as she navigated the complexities of her emotions in a world that felt increasingly empty without him.

Two months passed, and the once monotonous life of Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, was now filled with anxiety over a shared secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often gazed at her aunt's portrait, searching for traces of Charles in her features. One Sunday, her mother discovered Eugenie's secret exchange of gold for Charles's affection, leading to panic about how they would face Grandet on New Year’s Day when he would expect to see Eugenie's gold.

As the new year approached, dread consumed the women. Madame Grandet, feeling the weight of her husband's anger and their precarious situation, suggested they avoid confronting him directly. On January 1, 1820, the cold winter morning prompted Madame Grandet to request a fire in her room, hoping to delay their visit to Grandet. Surprisingly, he agreed, displaying an unusual good humor that bewildered both women.

Grandet's joy stemmed from a successful financial speculation, having received a substantial sum from Monsieur des Grassins. He reveled in his newfound wealth, oblivious to the turmoil within his family. As he prepared for breakfast, he expressed a desire to see Eugenie's gold, heightening the tension. Eugenie, feeling the weight of her secret, suggested they eat first to postpone the inevitable confrontation.

After breakfast, Grandet pressed Eugenie for her gold, insisting she return it. When she claimed she no longer had it, he erupted in anger, accusing her of deceit and theft. Desperate to protect her mother from Grandet's wrath, Eugenie fell at her mother's feet, pleading for mercy. Madame Grandet, weakened by the confrontation, was taken to bed, leaving Eugenie to face her father alone.

Grandet's fury escalated as he demanded to know what Eugenie had done with her gold. She maintained her silence, asserting her independence. Grandet, unable to comprehend her defiance, threatened to confine her to her room until she submitted to his authority. Heartbroken, Eugenie retreated to her mother, who tried to comfort her despite her own suffering.

The confrontation reached a boiling point, with Grandet cursing Eugenie and accusing her of giving her fortune to Charles. He refused to forgive her, insisting she confess where her gold had gone. Caught between her husband and daughter, Madame Grandet implored him to show compassion, but Grandet remained resolute in his anger.

As the evening wore on, Grandet dined alone for the first time in years, while Nanon, the servant, tried to comfort Eugenie. The tension in the household was palpable, with Madame Grandet's health deteriorating under the strain of the conflict. The arrival of guests for New Year’s festivities only highlighted the absence of the women, raising suspicions about the family's troubles.

In the quiet of the night, Nanon brought Eugenie a special treat, a hare pâté, determined that she would not suffer on dry bread alone. This small act of kindness amidst the turmoil provided a glimmer of hope for Eugenie, who felt the weight of her choices and the love she held for Charles. The family dynamics, marked by love, fear, and the struggle for power, set the stage for a tragic unfolding of events in their lives.

For several months, the old wine-grower, Grandet, visited his wife’s room without mentioning their daughter, Eugenie, who was confined under harsh conditions. Madame Grandet's health deteriorated, and townspeople speculated about the family's situation, eventually discovering that Eugenie was being kept on bread and water by her father's orders. Despite the town's condemnation of Grandet, Eugenie remained unaware of the gossip, focusing on her love for Charles and her ailing mother.

Aware of her impending death, Madame Grandet expressed a desire for reconciliation between father and daughter, urging Grandet to bring Eugenie back into the family. He remained indifferent. As her illness progressed, Madame Grandet confided in the Cruchots about Eugenie's treatment, prompting them to consider legal action against Grandet. However, Eugenie insisted on loyalty to her father, asking them to remain silent to protect the family honor.

As spring approached, Madame Grandet's condition worsened, and she sought help from the notary, Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her mother’s estate, potentially forcing him to sell his property. This revelation shocked Grandet, who had never considered the legal implications of his daughter's inheritance. Cruchot advised him to treat Eugenie kindly to avoid conflict over the estate.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Grandet decided to reconcile with Eugenie. He returned home determined to mend their relationship, but upon entering, he found Eugenie and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold given to Eugenie by Charles. Driven by greed, Grandet attempted to seize the gold, leading to a confrontation where Eugenie threatened to harm herself if he did not relent. 

In a moment of hesitation, Grandet backed down, allowing Eugenie to keep the dressing-case. He then attempted to show affection towards both his wife and daughter, scattering coins on the bed and promising to care for them. Despite his efforts, Madame Grandet's health continued to decline, and she passed away peacefully, leaving Eugenie to navigate a world filled with selfishness and sorrow. In her final moments, Madame Grandet imparted a poignant message to Eugenie about the true nature of happiness, emphasizing that it lies in heaven.

The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie Grandet felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home, where she had suffered greatly. Her father, M. Grandet, showed unexpected tenderness, surprising both her and the household staff, but this behavior soon revealed itself as a façade for his self-serving agenda.

During a family dinner with notary Maitre Cruchot, M. Grandet informed Eugenie that she was now her mother’s heiress. He pressured her to renounce her rights to her mother’s estate, claiming it would spare him taxes and the burden of an inventory. Confused, Eugenie agreed, believing it would ease her father’s worries. M. Grandet promised her a monthly allowance of a hundred francs, which he never fulfilled, instead giving her a few jewels.

In the following years, M. Grandet trained Eugenie in managing the household and his finances, instilling his miserly habits in her. Their lives became monotonous, marked by daily routines devoid of joy. Eugenie’s only social interactions were with the Cruchot family and their friends, who played whist with her father. As M. Grandet aged and became frail, Eugenie devoted herself to caring for him after he suffered a stroke.

M. Grandet’s death was uneventful, overshadowed by his obsession with hidden wealth. In his final moments, he expressed concern for his treasures rather than for Eugenie. After his passing, she learned from Maitre Cruchot that she inherited a vast fortune, including three hundred thousand francs in income and millions in investments and gold. Yet, her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles.

Eugenie mourned her father and lost love in solitude, confiding only in her loyal servant, Nanon. Nanon’s marriage to Antoine Cornoiller brought some joy, but Eugenie remained isolated in her grief. Despite her wealth, she felt no happiness, overshadowed by her mother’s suffering and her unfulfilled love for Charles.

As time passed, the Cruchots and other suitors surrounded Eugenie, eager to win her favor and wealth. They flattered her, but she remained indifferent, still longing for Charles. The Cruchots, led by M. de Bonfons, sought to position themselves favorably in her eyes, but their efforts felt hollow. Madame des Grassins attempted to persuade Eugenie to consider marrying the Marquis de Froidfond, but her heart remained with Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years. The contrast between her wealth and emotional desolation deepened, leaving her trapped in a life devoid of love and joy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles Grandet was amassing wealth in the Indies. His initial success yielded six thousand dollars, and he quickly learned that buying and selling people was the most lucrative business. He ventured to Africa, purchasing enslaved individuals and combining this trade with other profitable ventures. Driven by ambition, he aimed to return to Paris with a substantial fortune and reclaim a more prestigious position.

As he navigated various cultures, Charles's moral compass shifted; he became skeptical and hardened, losing sight of right and wrong. He engaged in exploitative practices, including usury and smuggling, and bought stolen goods at low prices to sell for profit. Initially inspired by his cousin Eugenie, his memories of her faded as he indulged in excess and debauchery, ultimately severing ties with his family.

In the Indies, he adopted the alias "Shepherd" to protect his identity while pursuing wealth through unscrupulous means. By 1827, he returned to Bordeaux aboard the "Marie Caroline," bringing gold-dust worth nearly two million francs. On the ship, he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, whose financial struggles stemmed from marrying into a fashionable family. The d’Aubrions were desperate to secure a wealthy son-in-law, and Madame d’Aubrion saw potential in Charles.

Upon arriving in Paris, Charles became close to the d’Aubrion family, who sought to marry him to their unattractive daughter. Madame d’Aubrion promised to help him gain a noble title and a lucrative position at court, which excited Charles. He envisioned a future filled with wealth and status, overshadowing any thoughts of Eugenie.

Meanwhile, Eugenie remained in Saumur, reflecting on her past with Charles. One day, she received a letter from him, which she initially struggled to open. The letter revealed that Charles had returned wealthy but had moved on from their youthful love.
