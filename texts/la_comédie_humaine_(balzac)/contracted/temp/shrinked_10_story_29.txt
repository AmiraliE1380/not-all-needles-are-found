In some provincial towns a cloistered melancholy hangs over certain houses; life seems frozen except for a solitary figure at a window. Such an air brooded over a house in Saumur, down a steep, little-used street of pebble pavement and three-century-old buildings. These wooden houses, prized by antiquarians, display carved oaken beams, slate façades, worn window-sills with neglected flower-pots, and heavy studded doors bearing Protestant marks or forgotten bourgeois insignia—so that the history of France seems recorded in wood and stone.

Beside a dilapidated dwelling stands a country gentleman’s mansion; its stone arch faintly shows armorial bearings eroded by the revolutions since 1789. Ground floors are not modern shops but plain dark rooms where goods lie exposed and a girl knitting will call her parents to serve customers. The local climate governs commerce: merchants, wine-growers, and coopers watch the weather anxiously; on Saturdays vineyards call their owners away, streets fall silent, and neighbors observe one another.

The most melancholy mansion belonged to Monsieur Grandet. He began life as a master cooper in 1789, married a rich wood-merchant’s daughter, and with savings and her dowry bought prime vineyard land at the sale of church property. He became protector of local nobility and supplier to Republican armies, invested shrewdly in vineyards and estates, and served as mayor in 1806. That year he inherited three fortunes, adding vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him among the region’s wealthiest men.

Townspeople speculated about his riches; his knack for timing sales made him admired and feared. By 1816 his fortune was estimated near four million francs, perhaps as much again in cash. Frugality ruled him—he spent only on necessities. Plain, stout, round-faced, and stuttering in long conversation, he concealed calculation and kept his wife dependent. Few entered his house; among them the young magistrate C. de Bonfons, who hoped to win Grandet’s daughter Eugénie. The town’s social life centered on competition for her, chiefly between the Cruchots and the des Grassins. In early 1811 the Cruchots gained advantage: when Froidfond was sold they negotiated its purchase for Grandet, who paid in gold—an ostentatious display that confirmed his wealth and influence throughout Saumur.

“The house of Monsieur Grandet” is a cold, silent dwelling above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The tufa entrance, with weathered pillars and an arch, looks jail-like; atop a crumbling bas-relief of the four seasons a plinth has taken root for plants, even a small cherry tree. The solid oak door, split and bolted with iron, has a small rusted grating that opens onto a dark vault and a garden enclosed by damp rampart walls.

The principal ground-floor room serves as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining room. Paneled wood, exposed beams, two street-facing windows, and a white stone chimney crowned by an old brass clock and a greenish mirror give modest comfort alongside faded tapestry chairs. Closets and buffets stand in the corners; a card table with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors sits between the windows. Red gros de Tours curtains—an uncommon luxury—contradict Monsieur Grandet’s frugality. A straw chair lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair occupy the rest.

For fifteen years the household has lived by strict economy: fires allowed only on set dates, the foot-warmer reused through cold months. La Grande Nanon, servant for thirty-five years, earned merely sixty francs a year but managed an annuity. Unattractive yet indispensable, she ran domestic affairs with tireless loyalty; Grandet’s rare favors were treated as monumental largesse. During the hard 1811 harvest his relation to Nanon combined dependence and domination; she remained devoted out of gratitude and necessity.

Nanon’s kitchen contrasted with the bare upper floors—immaculately clean though she slept in a tiny closet. On a November evening in 1819, when she lit the season’s first fire for Eugenie’s birthday, local notables arrived. Grandet kept the ritual of giving Eugenie a gold coin while Madame Grandet presented a new dress. Guests, notably Madame des Grassins and her son Adolphe, exchanged gifts and compliments that masked rivalry, each subtly angling for a share of Grandet’s wealth. He watched with possessive pride until a loud knock abruptly interrupted the evening.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door as Monsieur Grandet followed. His wife called anxiously; the players hesitated, and Monsieur des Grassins suggested leaving. A young man arrived with a porter and trunks. Grandet brusquely sent his wife back to the game and stepped out to speak with the newcomer.

The players stayed but did not resume. Madame des Grassins asked if he came from Saumur; her husband said Paris. The notary checked the hour; Abbé Cruchot inquired if the gentleman was young. Eugénie noticed Nanon’s absence; others speculated the newcomer might be kin. Madame Grandet urged them to continue, aware of her husband’s irritation.

Grandet returned alone; the traveler entered with polite bows. Madame Grandet offered warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, showed little interest. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugénie and, at twenty-two, displayed Parisian polish: a hunting costume, several waistcoats, a gold toilet set and—contrary to instructions to travel modestly—a private coupé to flaunt his means.

His elegance contrasted sharply with the provincials. The shabby Cruchots were taken aback; locals responded with mixed admiration and disdain. Eugénie, enchanted, longed for the world he represented and seized the chance to prepare his room, using her birthday as an excuse to persuade Nanon to add small refinements.

Madame des Grassins and Abbé Cruchot vied for Charles’s attention, revealing local ambitions. Grandet, having read a letter about his brother’s impending bankruptcy and charged to protect the son, masked his emotion and offered Charles a modest room. As guests left, the Cruchots and des Grassins resolved to unite against any attachment between Eugénie and her cousin and to manipulate the situation to their advantage.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late for business and set the household schedule: breakfast at eight, light lunch at noon, dinner at five. He professed poverty despite appearances, claiming his fortune came from hard work. He told Nanon to bring candles for Charles; Charles at first refused but accepted one so worn it looked like tallow.

Grandet led him through a shabby house, avoiding the cold stairwell. Charles’s expectations dwindled: the staircase and decor were rundown, and he wondered why his father had sent him here. On the landing he noticed a walled-up door to Grandet’s office — the secret room where Grandet did business and hoarded wealth. Across from it sat Eugenie; after a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon brought a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed.

Alone, Charles surveyed the surroundings and questioned his father’s intentions. Eugenie, meanwhile, woke with a new sense of longing. She admired herself but felt inadequate beside Charles; the garden and courtyard now charmed her and she yearned to impress him.

She asked Nanon to prepare cream for Charles’s breakfast, but Nanon said they needed Grandet’s permission. He quarrelled with Nanon over supplies, revealing his stinginess, and grudgingly gave flour and butter for a galette. Later in the fields Grandet met Cruchot, boasted about land and poplar sales, and when told gossip that Charles might court Eugenie he exploded: he’d sooner throw her into the Loire than let her marry her cousin, shattering Eugenie’s hopes.

Back home came news of Grandet’s brother’s suicide, a disaster for Charles. Breakfast was heavy with grief; Eugenie wept while Grandet remained indifferent. Risking his anger, Eugenie and Nanon conspired to give Charles a better breakfast, and her affection for him deepened.

After two hours’ work Eugenie prepared a simple breakfast, breaking the family custom of a standing midday meal. She set the table and placed an armchair for her cousin Charles, anxious about her father’s reaction; Madame Grandet promised to take responsibility if he returned.

Charles arrived in Parisian fashion, cheerful despite a hard journey. He rarely ate before midday but welcomed the meal and joked about wanting a partridge. His charm captivated Eugenie; she admired his elegance and felt a flutter of affection. Their conversation highlighted the contrast between his luxury and her modest upbringing. When Nanon brought eggs, Charles praised Eugenie and showed her his ring; their fingers briefly touched. The mood shifted over coffee when Charles called it outdated, and the women grew anxious at the thought of Monsieur Grandet’s return.

Grandet entered suspiciously, noticed the breakfast and especially the sugar, and demanded its source. Charles asked for sugar for his coffee; Grandet rebuffed him. Then Grandet revealed devastating news: Charles’s father had killed himself, leaving him bankrupt. Charles broke down; Eugenie and her mother felt deep compassion while Grandet coldly considered the financial fallout, insisting Charles go to the West Indies. Eugenie offered help; Grandet agreed to fund the journey. Alone with her feelings, Eugenie resolved to share Charles’s sorrow—her life irrevocably changed.

About four o’clock a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. Grandet burst in, rubbing his hands. “Wife,” he announced, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I made a deal for my vintage at two hundred francs a puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; here are six louis for you. In three months, wines will have fallen.”

News of the sale spread through Saumur. Eugenie, excited, asked if he had a thousand puncheons. When he confirmed, she calculated the sum and pleaded, “Then, father, you can easily help Charles.” The name of his neglected nephew stung Grandet. He scolded Eugenie for presuming, insisted any duty to Charles would be discharged on his terms, and threatened to send her to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon if she meddled.

Madame Grandet reported that Charles had not come down because he was weeping for his father. Grandet, momentarily bewildered, retreated to his secret room to consider investing the proceeds. His thinning forest had already brought six hundred thousand francs; with this sale his total reached nine hundred thousand. Tempted by visions of twenty percent returns, he settled on a more modest eight, imagining that in two years he might withdraw a fortune in gold.

Nanon’s knock summoned him to dinner. He agreed to the eight percent plan, murmuring that hunger would bring his nephew from grief. At table he learned Charles refused to eat; Nanon approved—“So much saved.” When Madame Grandet suggested mourning, Grandet mocked the expense: “What will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not in clothes,” and told her to buy her mourning out of her six louis; a hat-band would suffice. Eugenie sewed in silence while he ran through calculations. The town buzzed about the sale; Nanon’s spinning and Grandet’s repeated maxim “Nothing should be wasted” filled the house as he lost himself in arithmetic and plans.

That night Grandet, preoccupied with present gain and dismissive of any future life, revealed how money shaped his laws, politics, and morals, bargaining with conscience and religion and finally growling, “The devil take your good God!” He visited Charles’s dark room to berate him gently in his own miserly fashion: “Well, nephew, you are in trouble… I am a good uncle to you. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” He scoffed at a wax candle’s theft. Madame Grandet and Eugenie, listening from the landing, fled in fear.

Alone afterward Madame Grandet prayed for him. Eugenie, meanwhile, lay restless; her concern for Charles swelled into a personal disturbance. In the early hours she rose, found him asleep in an armchair exhausted by grief, and, touched by his quiet, watched him with a feeling she scarcely understood. He woke, apologized; she counseled him to go to bed and fled, ashamed and secretly happy—an innocent boldness that marked a turning point.

Next morning Grandet arrived distracted but outwardly affectionate. Though he seemed softened, he had spent the night calculating schemes to enlarge his fortune and dominate others; that day he contrived to restore his brother’s honor without disbursing a franc, by manipulating public opinion in Paris. In his absence Eugenie found tender pleasure in caring for Charles. She prepared his breakfast with self-forgetting devotion and brought it to his room to spare him the shame of eating before her. The warmth and attention of the provincial household—so different from the indifference Charles had known in Paris—moved him. Eugenie’s innocence and genuine affection drew him closer; they shared an unspoken understanding.

Life went on around this quiet intimacy: Grandet continued his calculations, Nanon spun, and later Grandet invited the Cruchots to dinner. Yet beneath the household routine, Eugenie’s tenderness and Charles’s gratitude suggested changes that money alone could not govern, and hinted at the complex obligations that would shape their lives.

Saumur showed more interest in the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than in the previous night’s sale of his vintage, which had offended the wine‑growers; Grandet, who prided himself on outwitting others, cared little for their judgment.

When the des Grassins learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and death, they came that evening to offer sympathy and to learn why he had invited the Cruchots. At five, Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in their Sunday best. The meal was sombre: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet barely speaking. Afterward Charles went off to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet revealed the cunning that earned him the nickname “the old dog.” Affecting a practiced stutter, he controlled and frustrated his guests while steering talk toward bankruptcy. The president of the commercial court explained that a respected merchant facing insolvency might have a receiver appointed and that liquidation could differ from outright failure; sometimes affairs could be settled without the courts—if the merchant did not file and no creditor appeared, family or heirs could liquidate. Debts could also be bought at a discount so that, if all titles were acquired, heirs would owe nothing. The notary urged this plan to save Grandet’s name.

Mindful of reputation and his nephew’s prospects, Grandet feigned ignorance of Parisian tricks. The president offered to go to Paris to negotiate and suggested Grandet provide some assets. The des Grassins then declared they would honorably liquidate the brother’s debts; their banker and Madame des Grassins volunteered to handle Paris. Favoring the banker, Grandet confided a separate plan to buy government bonds and asked for discretion; they agreed to meet the next day.

As word spread of his supposed generosity, the town forgave the vintage sale. Behind closed doors Grandet ordered Nanon to keep silent and, under cover of night, set out to sell gold in Angers. Eugenie, worried for Charles, thought she heard a cry but was distracted by seeing her father and Nanon carry a heavy keg; Grandet told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried away.

“My father has gone,” Eugenie thought as she listened from the top of the stairs. The house lay hushed; the carriage had gone and a cry from her cousin’s room pierced the silence. A thin thread of light from the doorway lit the old staircase.

“He suffers!” she cried, running up. Charles sat in an armchair, head bowed, a pen fallen from his hand; his breathing was labored. On the table were sealed letters to tradesmen—signs he was settling affairs to leave France—and two open letters. The greeting “My dear Annette” struck her. Rooted by shame and curiosity, she yielded to the latter. She adjusted his head, kissed his hair, and read.

Charles’s letter to Annette explained that misfortune had ruined him: his father had killed himself, their fortune was gone, and he was penniless. He could not remain in Paris, where society would scorn a ruined man. He would not ask Annette to abandon comforts to keep him; better to part forever. He planned to seek fortune in the Indies or America—perhaps as a sailor—and might return aged and altered; he urged Annette to cherish their happy memory yet accepted that he could not demand fidelity, for marriage and new ties might become necessities. The letter ended abruptly, as if his hand had left the page.

Eugenie softened the bluntness, reading passion into the formal words; “My dear Annette” became sweetness to her ear. She mourned his misfortune and thought, “I have gold; I will give it to him.”

The second letter, to his friend Alphonse, asked him to settle affairs: sell possessions, recover what could be had, keep the horse Briton if possible, press builders to delay delivering an unpaid carriage, and pay off debts, including six louis lost at cards. It showed a practical, calculating mind intent on avoiding dishonor and salvaging what remained.

Overcome with compassion and admiration, Eugenie ran to her room and opened the old oak cabinet where she’d hidden a red velvet purse—a grandmother’s relic. Inside were foreign gold pieces, rare rupees—about five thousand eight hundred francs. Joy and resolve filled her. Secret love for her cousin and pity overcame propriety. She returned upstairs at once, candle and purse in hand.

Charles woke, stunned. Eugenie, trembling, confessed she had read the letters and then offered him her savings without hesitation. Calling herself a poor girl who wanted nothing, a sister helping a cousin, she knelt and implored him to accept her gold as proof of his respect and generosity. Tears fell from Charles onto her hands; moved, she poured the coins onto the table. “You will be rich,” she cried; “this gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

He could not refuse. Rising, he kissed her and revealed he would reciprocate not with money but with an object he prized: a leather dressing-case containing in a hidden drawer two portraits by Madame Mirbel, set with pearls—his mother and father, who were Eugenie’s aunt and uncle. He declared the case more precious than life and begged Eugenie to be its guardian. If she kept it, and in the worst event his fortune vanished, the pearls and gold in the case would repay her; yet he demanded it ultimately be destroyed to prevent its passing into other hands. This exchange of trust marked a turning point; Eugenie looked at him with her first deep, womanly affection. He kissed her hand, calling her an “angel of purity,” and vowed that feeling and sentiment must be everything between them henceforth.

They exchanged gentle words and good-nights. Eugenie, blushingly practical in her joy, suggested Charles keep the treasures in her old cabinet. He sighed in his mourning but kissed her hand; as she closed the door he murmured the ruin of his fortunes. “What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she answered, naively. He scoffed; she spoke of family estates, and he dismissed them as poor farms. The exchange was sweet and foolish: beneath his black, Charles began to wear roses.

Next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie and Charles together in the garden. He remained sad; the three women of the house, respectful of his grief, allowed him the morning while they tended chores and awaited tradesmen. Around five, Monsieur Grandet returned from Angers, unexpectedly jubilant, having made fourteen thousand francs on an exchange of gold. He had left his groom at Angers to look after the exhausted horses.

“I am hungry,” he proclaimed. Nanon scolded him for not eating. Shortly Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet barely registered his nephew’s presence, absorbed instead in his financial success and in baffling others. Des Grassins, the banker, told him gold fetched high prices in Angers and spoke of sending theirs; Grandet curtly refused, hinting at giving his old friend respite from wasted exertion. A whispered consultation ensued; they glanced often at Charles. Des Grassins offered to go to Paris and asked Charles if he had commissions. Charles declined stiffly; Des Grassins rebuked him for the curt reply, reminding him of family ties. Charles, pale, kissed his uncle and withdrew. Eugenie watched her father with secret admiration for his mastery over affairs.

Grandet dismissed Des Grassins with a jocular “decoy those people; lead them by the nose,” and resumed his armchair, asking Nanon for black-current ratafia. His excitement made him fidget and sing snatches of a ditty; the women exchanged uneasy glances at his towering contentment. He spoke philosophically about money: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life.” His cheerfulness came with small generosity and an undertone of calculation that made him formidable.

The evening passed with domestic routine, latent anxieties about affairs, and the quiet bloom of a new intimacy between Eugenie and Charles sealed by her sacrifice and his trust. Thus the house resumed its hum: tradesmen, chores, and the unspoken commerce of feelings and fortunes. Eugenie’s selfless devotion and Grandet’s thrifty triumph—passion and calculation—had begun to intertwine, shaping the family’s future.

They breakfasted the next morning in an intimate mood. Grief had drawn Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles together; even Nanon felt sympathy. The old man, absorbed in thrift and intent on sending Charles away to avoid expense, stayed aloof, busy with farm work while Madame watched Eugenie and Charles as the relationship deepened.

For Eugenie, love grew from their secret into simple, childlike tenderness: soft glances, gentle words, and small consolations. She delighted in soothing Charles; he, hardened by the world, found in her a pure attachment and cherished their life. While Grandet worked, Charles savored the company and virtues of mother and daughter. Eugenie surrendered to love without foreseeing the looming separation.

Three days after the des Grassins left, Grandet took Charles to the civil court to renounce any claim to his father’s estate. Charles signed powers of attorney, obtained a passport, and sold his belongings. Grandet bought many items worth nearly a thousand francs and insisted on paying Charles’s passage to the Indies. Charles gave his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet, who was deeply moved.

They wore mourning for Guillaume Grandet’s Requiem Mass. Charles showed letters from Paris and assured Eugenie in the garden his affairs were in order and his effects would be sent to Nantes, urging her not to tie her future to his uncertain fate; they kissed. On departure day Grandet packed his baggage and the family saw him off. After he left, Eugenie kept his room unchanged, plotted with Nanon, followed his voyage on a map, and confided her love only to Nanon.

Two months later Eugénie and her mother were consumed by their secret: Charles filled their thoughts, and Eugénie searched her aunt’s portrait for him. One Sunday Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugénie had given Charles, and panic ensued.

“What will you tell your father on New Year’s when he asks for your gold?” Madame cried. They spent the morning in dread, missed Mass, and feared the confrontation. Madame, ill-prepared for winter and terrified of her husband’s wrath, suggested consulting Monsieur des Grassins, but Eugénie said it was too late.

On the cold morning of January 1, 1820, Grandet—cheerful after a large investment return—boasted at breakfast, then demanded the gold back. Eugénie said she no longer had it; he accused her of deceit and threatened confinement to bread and water. She defended her independence and stood firm in her love for Charles. Nanon, fearing for her, vowed support and that evening secretly brought a hare pâté, deepening their bond against Grandet’s harshness.

For months the old wine-grower tended his ailing wife while never mentioning their daughter Eugénie, who remained confined to her room by his orders. Madame Grandet’s health declined as her husband grew harsher and began to err in business. Townspeople learned Eugénie was given only bread and water, with Nanon secretly bringing extra food; the community condemned Grandet, and Eugénie’s gentle attendance at Mass won sympathy.

Eugénie’s chief sorrow was her mother’s deterioration, which she often blamed on herself. Madame tried to comfort her, urged reconciliation, and begged her husband to restore their daughter, but he remained indifferent as she weakened.

In spring Madame confided in the Cruchots, who were appalled and discussed legal consequences; Eugénie overheard but, fearing scandal, begged for silence and resolved to obey her father. A visiting notary warned Grandet that Madame’s death would make Eugénie’s inheritance a threat to his control, and he began to soften. Returning to reconcile, he found them admiring a gold dressing-case from Charles; after Eugénie threatened self-harm he relented, let her keep the case, scattered coins, and promised kindness. Madame died peacefully in October 1822, urging Eugénie to seek happiness in heaven and warning of the selfish world.

The day after her father’s death Eugenie felt newly attached to her childhood home, where memories of suffering—especially her mother’s death—moved her to tears. Her father’s sudden tenderness before he died—taking her to breakfast and regarding her kindly—made the household suspect senility. At the mourning dinner Maitre Cruchot revealed Grandet’s plan: to avoid dividing property or paying taxes on supposed funds, he needed Eugenie to renounce her rights so he could retain control. Confused, she asked for the deed; Grandet, moved, urged her to trust him and accept a monthly allowance of a hundred francs. She agreed and signed away her inheritance the next morning. A year later he had not paid; when reminded he offered jewels.

Over the next two years Grandet trained Eugenie to run the household and manage finances, instilling his frugality. By the third year she was mistress of the house and a monotonous routine continued for five years. Her sadness was evident though silent; her only companions were the Cruchots and their whist-playing circle.

In 1827 Grandet, eighty-two, suffered a stroke. Eugenie devoted herself to his care until his uneventful death, during which his fixation on hidden wealth persisted—his last words urged her to look after his riches. Afterwards, with only her loyal servant Nanon, she learned from Maitre Cruchot that she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite the fortune, Eugenie thought of finding her cousin Charles. She secured an annuity for Nanon, who married Antoine Cornoiller and remained housekeeper; the estate and farmers stayed the same. At thirty Eugenie had known little joy: a childhood shadowed by her mother’s suffering and an unfulfilled love for Charles, absent seven years. Mourning and others’ matchmaking—Cruchots, Bonfons, Madame des Grassins and the Marquis de Froidfond—met her indifference, which was misread as interest.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. His early ventures brought six thousand dollars, and he discovered that human trafficking was most profitable. He went to Africa to buy enslaved people, combined that trade with other goods, and aimed to return to Paris wealthy and restore his social standing.

Experience hardened him; he lost his moral compass, rationalizing that one society’s crimes might be another’s virtues, and grew ruthless—engaging in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and reselling pirate plunder. Memories of Eugénie faded; he grew contemptuous of his family and treated her like a creditor. To conceal himself he used the alias “Shepherd.” In 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline with gold-dust worth nearly two million francs, planning further profit at the Paris mint.

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a court-connected gentleman whose family had fallen on hard times. Madame d’Aubrion sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter and promised Charles help obtaining a title and income through marriage—an alliance to elevate both families. Ambitious and urged on by Annette, Charles entertained the match.

When Des Grassins asked him to settle his father’s debts, Charles refused. In August Eugénie received Charles’s trembling letter: he recounted his success, announced the pragmatic engagement, and declared conjugal love a delusion. Devastated, she recalled her mother’s warning and resigned herself to a life of prayer.

Eugenie returned from the garden uneasy, avoiding the corridor haunted by memories of her cousin. That day the parish priest, aligned with the Cruchots and Monsieur de Bonfons, had been urged to press her about marriage. Unhappy, Eugenie confessed she wished to withdraw from the world and live only for God; the priest cautioned against abandoning societal duties, especially to the poor.

Madame des Grassins, vengeful, revealed that Charles Grandet had returned from the Indies and was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, producing a letter that detailed Charles’s situation and his father’s bankruptcy. Eugenie, stunned, returned the letter unread. Tormented, she asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be sinful, then secluded herself in her father’s secret room. In a charged evening she asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay—a request taken as a pledge.

Privately, Eugenie told the president she could offer only friendship and asked him to settle her uncle’s debts for her hand. He agreed, went to Paris, paid creditors, and informed Charles—whose initial excitement turned to shock when he learned of Eugenie’s fortune. The president married Eugenie; she lived simply, devoted to charity. Widowed soon after, she remained pious and independent, supported by servants Nanon and Cornoiller, her life illustrating the tension between wealth, duty, and personal fulfillment.
