In September 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, the marquis’s only daughter, married Comte Adam Laginski, a Polish exile. Her father had squandered his fortune; settlements from her mother provided their income. Adam, from an illustrious Polish family and no public charge, nonetheless faced Parisian prejudice: Polish nobility was scorned and often mistaken for exiled students. The émigré community’s split between republican and noble exiles made him a local joke.

After buying a house on rue de la Pépinière in 1834, by 1836 Adam was established in Parisian society; the house’s mixed styles and fine décor reflected bourgeois taste. Clémentine noted Captain Paz, a discreet factotum overseeing finances and stables. Adam said Paz came from a poor branch, had fought for Poland, and refused a generous gift—proof of loyalty and pride. As they prepared to go out, Clémentine’s affection for both men hinted that personal ties could transcend social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. She insisted he dine with her and Adam; he dressed for the occasion but comported himself like a subordinate, answering little and deferring to Clémentine while Adam urged him to be livelier. Outwardly dull, he was painfully conscious of his love for Clémentine and tried to hide it.

At the Opera he remained distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused his disagreeableness. His reserve sprang from unrequited love, heightened after her return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household—aware it would falter without him even as it caused him inward suffering.

Clémentine, newly confident and focused on her Parisian standing, had little room for him. Once, noticing him while driving home, she impulsively invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: he felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance. Routine resumed; he found solace in managing their affairs, reflecting on the depth and pain of his attachment.

When Clémentine hosted a reception, Thaddeus’s absence went unnoticed. He retreated into solitary habits, content in silent devotion yet acutely aware that his love was both joy and torment. He admired her from afar, cherishing small glimpses of her happiness. As Clémentine struggled for attention—distracted by rivals and the strain of maintaining status—she increasingly neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steadfast presence, quietly overseeing things while wrestling with his feelings.

In May, as she prepared to visit her uncle’s estate, she insisted he join them for dinner. He tried to engage despite shyness; her interest in him grew and their conversation hinted at something deeper, but loyalty to Adam restrained him. After dinner she voiced dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for more passion. Torn between love and friendship, Thaddeus withheld confession and invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer—providing for her and visiting as an escape. Malaga’s despairing letter longing for his companionship struck him, underscoring the constancy of his devotion to Clémentine and the sacrifices it demanded.

Clementine returned home more beautiful than before. After dinner the count and countess spoke of Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam lamented Thaddeus’s misfortune and feared that an association with a circus performer would damage his reputation; Clementine openly scorned such women. Adam defended Thaddeus, citing the sacrifices he had made for Malaga.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, was viewed by the countess as a threat to Adam’s stability. Though anxious, Adam trusted that Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess doubted this.

Paz felt Clementine’s chill and contempt and sank into despair, yet he took quiet pleasure in admiring her at gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love ran deep; feeling unworthy he fell ill from anguish before recovering.

After Adam lost heavily gambling, he asked Thaddeus to cover the loss by blaming Malaga. Thaddeus reluctantly agreed, burdened by obligations. When Clementine learned of his generosity toward Paz she protested and questioned his loyalty. Overwhelmed by her expectations and the strain of managing her affairs, Thaddeus resolved to teach her household management so she could be independent.

In spring Adam fell gravely ill. Thaddeus nursed him devotedly while Clementine sought the best care. Thanks to Thaddeus’s devotion Adam slowly improved and praised him. Tormented by unrequited love and fearing Paris would prolong his pain, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after writing Clementine a heartfelt letter confessing his feelings and sacrifices. Clementine and Adam never fully grasped his devotion; years later she wondered about his fate, his memory a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
