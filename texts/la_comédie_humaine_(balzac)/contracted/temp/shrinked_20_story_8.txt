DEDICATION
Dedicated to the Comtesse Clara Maffei.

I

In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre, the only daughter of the dissipated Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a young Polish exile. (These Polish names are rendered as pronounced to ease readers.) Though the Marquis had wasted a large fortune won by marrying a Demoiselle de Ronquerolles, Clémentine du Rouvre became unexpectedly wealthy. On her mother’s side she was niece to the Marquis de Ronquerolles and Madame de Serizy; on her father’s side another uncle was the eccentric, land-speculating Chevalier du Rouvre. Misfortunes and deaths in these families left Clémentine a great heiress; the three relatives agreed to settle portions on her, each promising ten thousand francs a year at her marriage.

Comte Adam Laginski, though an exile, was not dependent on France. His family was ancient and allied to princely houses, but heraldry meant little to the ascending bourgeoisie of Louis-Philippe’s Paris. When Adam first appeared in 1833 on the boulevard and at the Jockey-Club, he lived like a young man bent on pleasure; some thought he was a student. By then Poles had fallen in French estimation because of political reactions after the insurrection; the populace and the press made the once-admired Polish name the target of ridicule. The bourgeoisie treated Polish exiles—divided among republican followers of Lelewel and conservative nobles around Prince Czartoryski—with contempt. Exiles brought their own divisions and hatreds, which Parisian society loved to exploit.

In 1834 Adam Mitgislas Laginski became a butt of Parisian pleasantry. Young dandies mocked the Polish airs and pretensions, though some admitted he paid his gambling debts. The Sarmatian lightness of mind partly justified satire; the French would behave similarly in comparable fortune. Yet Adam’s appearance and ways kept him from being taken seriously: small, sharp face, short nose, fair hair, reddish beard, and oblique, yellow-tinged eyes gave him a slightly goatish look. Despite this, his manners were exquisite—due partly to his dress and partly to the training of his mother, a Radziwill. He had daring courage and a mind suited to ephemeral Parisian discourse, which made him appear only a fashionable trifler rather than a great seigneur.

Then, in 1834, Adam suddenly bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière and, within six months, began living at the highest style in Paris. About the time he took himself seriously he saw Clémentine at the opera and fell in love; a year later they were married. Society at first misjudged him until they discovered his means. His mother had prudently mortgaged her property during the insurrection to raise a large loan from two Jewish bankers; Adam was then found to have an income of eighty thousand francs a year. Once known, people stopped blaming Clémentine’s guardians for imprudence. In winter 1836 Adam and the young Comtesse Laginska became the fashion; she joined the circle of notable young women—Mesdames de Lestorade, de Portenduere, Marie de Vandenesse, du Guénic, and de Maufrigneuse—distinguished from parvenus. This sets the scene for a discreetly noble action, one of many unrecorded virtues hidden in society’s tumult.

Balzac observes that architecture reflects manners and morals, a truth visible after 1830. Old fortunes dwindled, mansions were torn down, and mixed, often incongruous houses rose—upper-class families occupying upper floors over newly rich neighbours. Architects imitated grandeur on small grounds: a garden could conceal a mock-Louvre, a few rooms could be made to appear spacious by clever distribution. Such is the hôtel of the Comtesse Laginska in the rue de la Pépinière: a structure built between court and garden, with offices and stables to the right, coachhouse to the left, and a porter’s lodge between two porte-cochères. Its chief luxury is a greenhouse opening from a ground-floor boudoir into a suite of reception rooms. The house combines many borrowed styles—medieval ceilings, marble veneers, panels by Steinbock and François Souchet, painted ceilings by Schinner—and cost, in a climate of unrest, about eleven hundred thousand francs. All this “princely” luxury occupied the garden of a house once owned by a Revolution-era purveyor who had speculated, fled, and died bankrupt; the English widow who inherited it sold, and Adam bought at a bargain, though such buying cheaply was uncharacteristic of a grand seigneur.

Behind the house a velvety lawn shaded by exotic trees and a Chinese pagoda led to an immense greenhouse perfuming the boudoir; climbing plants and painted poles covered the northern and opposite walls. Within twenty-five square rods—now worth about four hundred thousand francs—Parisian artifice simulated forest, flower beds, and gravelled paths. The boudoir, though large and artistically arranged, displayed an array of pretty nothings—Chinese tables, inlaid ivories, mosaics, Dutch pictures, angels, statuettes, Italian silk draperies, Persian rugs—arranged under half-light filtered through embroidered curtains: an exhibition of shop contents intended to amuse rather than repose. A whip with a handle by Mademoiselle de Fauveau lay among curiosities, a small sign that the countess rode.

One morning Clémentine lay full length on a couch, the greenhouse doors open, the tropical perfume entering. She watched her husband smoking a narghile, the only pipe she permitted, and surveyed the elegant salons—one white and gold, one Renaissance—leading to a medieval-styled dining-gallery and the principal staircase. Married two years and returned to Paris, Clémentine had discovered a secret in her household. Adam habitually deferred to a certain Paz: for any difficulty—carriage, bill, or bitting a horse—she heard, “Paz will settle that.” She never saw Paz, yet his name recurred like a refrain. Who was he?

When she summoned him, the footman answered that Captain Paz was at the stables, and that he had been showing the grooms how to groom her mare, Cora. When Paz entered, Clémentine saw a tall, handsome, robust man. Thick black hair, a broad, luminous forehead, a large Roman nose, jet-black fervent eyes, and a soldier’s visaged bearing marked him. He wore a frogged overcoat (a polonaise) and peasant-pleated trousers—conspicuously Polish. In contrast to the lean, fair, almost goatish count, Paz was muscular, square-headed, and seemed inwardly strong and sorrowful. The count greeted him familiarly; Paz bowed courteously to Clémentine.

She asked why this friend of Adam’s lived in a position of subordination—didn’t he take meals with them? Why had he not accompanied them to Italy and Switzerland? Paz answered simply: he was Adam’s friend and took care of his interests; he desired no social distinction and was indifferent to Parisian opinion. When the count teased him about standing, Paz replied he lived for himself, or if she preferred, for the married pair. He accepted the countess’s invitation to join their ride to the Bois.

Adam then told Clémentine the story of Paz. The captain—named Thaddeus—came of an old family, possibly descended from the Pazzi of Florence who settled in Poland. He had been an orphan, penniless save for his sword, and had served under the Grand Duke Constantine in the Polish revolution. At the decisive affair Paz, believing himself followed by his men, dashed upon a Russian battery and was taken prisoner. Adam had been there: he and his troop charged the battery to recover him, bringing Paz back wounded and exhausted. After Warsaw fell they escaped across the Vistula, evaded Prussian arrest (through Adam’s name and some connections), reached Dantzic, and passed by Dutch vessel to London, where Adam attended his ill mother. These shared hardships made the two like brothers.

On gaining a comfortable income at twenty-two, Adam wanted to secure Paz’s future; he drew a certificate of Funds for a sum payable to bearer and offered it to Paz. Thaddeus paled and returned the paper, saying he had thought they were “one for life or death” and asked if Adam wanted to be rid of him. When Adam insisted he would share ruin as easily as fortune, Paz declared that Adam lacked sufficient fortune to live as a Laginski should and needed a friend to manage affairs. He accepted, not as a subordinate, but as a father, brother, and confidant—displaying gratitude, fidelity, and a kind of fierce devotion. Adam called him a poet of soul, superior in restraint and grandeur of feeling.

Adam explained that Paz found the house and bought it nearly at nothing; he managed funds, sold Adam’s securities high and bought low, and thus paid for most of the property by profitable operations. Paz knew horses, buying and selling advantageously, and chose bold, loyal Polish servants who would follow them through fire. He disliked social life, preferred Poland, and spent much of his resources—often in Adam’s name—on poor Polish exiles. He would sometimes pay Adam’s debts without complaint; once, after Adam lost twenty thousand francs at whist, Paz paid it, only sighing that twenty poor Poles could have lived a year on that sum. That sigh restrained Adam more than reproaches could. Paz lived to make Adam elegant and secure, and Adam returned total devotion.

Clémentine, touched, promised to love him as an old friend. Adam insisted their marriage combined friendship and love, praising the durability of friendship, which increases rather than exhausts. He explained that Paz’s extreme discretion and concealment of feeling gave him a moral superiority. Paz had in his heart a vast domain where he lived and thought; though he had a right to the title of count in Poland, in Paris he accepted being called simply “captain.”

Clémentine accepted the arrangement warmly and they prepared to ride together. The scene leaves the house full of carefully arranged luxury—an external show of ease and taste—while inside the singular, self-effacing, heroic bond between Adam and Paz governs the household. Paz’s devotion, simplicity, and fidelity, contrasted with the fashionable, pleasure-loving count and his elegant wife, sketch a moral portrait of friendship and sacrifice amid Parisian display.

Paz lived so privately that Parisian society wondered who he was when the Comtesse Laginska, seen riding in the Bois de Boulogne between her husband and an unknown man, brought him into their circle. During the ride Clémentine insisted that Thaddeus should dine with them; this whim forced Paz to dress for the evening. Clémentine prepared with coquettish care that even Adam admired as she entered the salon where the two friends waited.

“Comte Paz,” she said, “you must go with us to the Opera.” Her tone threatened a quarrel if he refused.

“Willingly, madame,” Paz replied, but asked to be called captain, since he lacked a count’s fortune.

“Very good, captain; give me your arm,” she said with the flattering familiarity lovers enjoy.

At table the captain sat beside her, behaving like a modest junior officer at his general’s table: attentive, deferential, speaking little unless questioned. Clémentine’s coquetries found no purchase on his reserved gravity. Adam urged him to be more lively, but Thaddeus stayed solemn and half-asleep. After dessert Paz explained that his habits were opposite to society’s—he went to bed early and rose early—and excused his dulness as sleepiness.

“I meant to amuse you at the Opera,” Clémentine complained; Paz agreed to go. Adam suggested seeing Duprez in “Guillaume Tell” or perhaps the lighter “Variétés.” The captain, smiling and ringing for the carriage, had Constantin put horses to the carriage rather than the coupé, recalling a foreign politeness Clémentine attributed to their Florentine ancestry. She scrutinized him covertly; a brief contrast emerged between his refined accent and his earlier stiffness.

Silence reigned in the salon as the three finished coffee. Thaddeus kept his military stiffness through the trip and in the box, where he seemed to sleep. During the last act of “Guillaume Tell” he murmured he was a stupid man, fit only for his specialty, and jested he should return home to bed and send the carriage back. Clémentine dismissed him coolly.

“What a bear!” she said to Adam as he pressed her hand unseen. “You are a great deal nicer.” Adam thought Thaddeus was trying to make himself disagreeable in a way most men would not; Clémentine suspected calculation in his behavior—that he might deceive an ordinary woman by posing as cold.

Half an hour later the carriage was at the gate. Clémentine asked where the captain lived; Adam pointed to an upper room over the coachhouse. As they passed, lights burned in Paz’s rooms. In his dressing-gown and with a pipe, Paz watched Clémentine enter the vestibule. The day had been hard. When Adam had made him go to the Opera to see and judge Mademoiselle du Rouvre, he felt a great emotion; upon familiarity with Clémentine he recognized in her the woman a man is compelled to love single-mindedly. His counsel that the couple take a long journey after their marriage had been meant to put some distance between him and Clémentine; during their absence he had found peace. Now the household’s return renewed his trial.

Smoking a long cherry-wood pipe gifted by Adam, Paz reflected: only he and God knew how he suffered loving Clémentine in silence; he wondered how to avoid having either her love or her dislike. Yet he did not live without pleasure amid suffering. Since Adam and Clémentine’s return he derived inward joy from being indispensable to a household that would have fallen to ruin without him. Clémentine, raised by a profligate father and ignorant of domestic economy, and Adam, a generous son of a Polish great house, would have been wholly ruined but for Paz’s vigilance—he stopped Adam gambling at the Bourse and managed the family affairs. Gratitude held him bound to the household; love could not drive him away.

Clémentine’s Parisian life, however, distracted her. Winters in Paris demand that women be ever present, competing for attention with gowns and fêtes, leaving little time for domestic concerns or sustained love. The strain ages many young women, even the healthy. She became absorbed in reigning at society, and Paz was often forgotten. Yet in May, driving from the Bois before leaving for Ronquerolles, Adam’s estate, Clémentine noticed Thaddeus sauntering on a side-path and bade him join them. Thaddeus, flushed with delight at seeing the countess, took the seat beside her and blushed like a schoolboy. Her smile melted whatever stoicism he had resolved; she folded her arms and watched people pass, and in that look she noticed the contrast between Thaddeus’s fine presence and Adam’s less refined frame. The sight roused dangerous thoughts in Clémentine: Thaddeus seemed to possess some superiority over Adam—something that appealed to her sensibility.

“You will dine with us,” she told him as the carriage returned. “You are Thaddeus to me, as you are to Adam… Dress yourself.” She pressed his hand on stepping from the carriage.

Thaddeus dressed with joy and dread and behaved through dinner as before—soldierly, suited to stewardly duties. Clémentine’s glance had altered the evening: she made the marquis and his sister aware of him. The Marquis de Ronquerolles, an astute diplomatist, had been told by his niece of Paz’s worth and knew how modestly he served Laginski’s affairs. Curious glances and rapid exchanges passed among those present—an exchange of eyes that in a moment told others what words might have. Paz understood the implication: perhaps he might be loved; audacity could yield happiness—but Adam stood in the way.

For a moment love overrode gratitude: Paz became bright and fluent, telling of the Polish insurrection with noble words to the diplomat’s questions. By the end of dinner Clémentine listened to him as to a hero, temporarily forgetting Adam’s sacrifices. When the guests left, Paz and Clémentine remained alone.

“I will leave you now, madame,” Paz said. “You will rejoin them at the Opera?”

“No,” she answered. “They give an odious ballet tonight.” A pale pause followed.

“Two years ago Adam would not have gone to the Opera without me,” she remarked, not looking at Paz.

“He loves you madly,” Paz said.

“Yes, and because he loves me madly he is all the more likely not to love me tomorrow,” she observed. Paz lamented the inconstancy of Parisian women: loved madly they want a reasonable love; loved reasonably they reproach their lovers. She insisted Adam was volatile but proud, content to have her as his wife and not to oppose her tastes.

Thaddeus tried to steer the conversation; he felt that if he did not declare his love now he would be a fool. Yet words failed him. In a silence heavy with thought the countess studied him and he her through a mirror. Seated like a man digesting dinner, Paz crossed his hands upon his belly and twirled his thumbs mechanically.

“Why don’t you tell me something good of Adam?” Clémentine asked suddenly, challenging his loyalty.

“I love him,” Paz answered. “You would not believe me; I am incapable of saying harm of him. My position between you is very difficult.”

Clémentine lowered her head. “You Northern men have nothing but physical courage,” she complained; “you have no constancy in your opinions.”

“Will you keep me company?” Paz asked casually, trying to appear careless.

“Are you not?” she asked in return.

He conceived a falsehood: “I’m going to the Circus in the Champs-Élysées; it opens tonight.” She questioned him, half-angry. He colored, speaking of a disgraceful secret he hid even from Adam—a passion for a circus horsewoman called Malaga who seemed to him the quintessence of grace and daring. He described her—black hair, blue ribbon, white tunic, daring feats on horseback—and declared that her agility and bravery moved him more than any ballet star. Clémentine, amazed, smiled indulgently and told him to go. He kissed her hand and left, leaving a tear upon it.

This tale was a fiction born of cowardice and despair. The only truth was a passing attention he had given a rider at Saint-Cloud. Yet his invented passion gave him a pretext to leave that night and, instead of disclosing his love for Clémentine, to make the fiction real. He traced Malaga’s real name—and address—from circus gossip: Marguerite Turquet, who boarded with the Chapuzots and performed at the Cirque-Olympique in summer and in a boulevard theatre in winter.

At the faubourg du Temple, after being announced, Paz stumbled into the attic where Malaga was ironing. He began with a fable—she reminded him of a lost daughter—proposing to be of service. The Chapuzots encouraged the notion. When he proposed she be his daughter in public, so he might assist her properly—lodge her comfortably and provide servants—she accepted, half-jesting and half-skeptical, noting his Polish nationality. Paz left promising to return.

A month later Malaga lived in a furnished apartment provided by Adam’s upholsterer at Paz’s direction, and the Chapuzots served her as friends and servants. Paz visited rarely, spending an hour a week in the salon and never entering her bedroom. He left money on the mantel—two gold pieces of forty francs each—every time he visited. The Chapuzots and the circus people could not fathom the Pole’s conduct: he seemed indifferent yet generous. The weekly sum, combined with Malaga’s salary, allowed her a comfortable life; gossip turned six thousand francs of Paz’s spending into sixty thousand. In the demi-monde she was talked about, envied and accused. Some called her magnetized or a somnambulist, hinting at strange explanations for her fortune. These scandals distressed her; she complained to Paz that people had maligned her and made her weep.

Paz kept a relentless silence about his motives. Madame Chapuzot discovered his name and title and heard at the Laginski house that Paz had no known daughter; that raised suspicion. Malaga, fearing disgrace or trouble with the police, grew anxious. One day, when Paz again left money on the mantel, she snatched the gold and flung it back at him, crying, “I don’t want stolen money!” Paz calmly handed the coins to Chapuzot, left without a word, and did not return.

While Clémentine was at Ronquerolles, news of Malaga’s sudden loss of her Polish benefactor spread through the circus. Some praised her display of dignity; others called it cunning. Thaddeus received a flood of letters from women curious about and scandalized by his reserve, but most of the fashionable world did not pursue the matter; it was chiefly the demi-monde that speculated.

Two weeks later Malaga, crushed by debt, wrote a plaintive letter to Comte Paz pleading for support. The letter, found in Adam’s hands and read by dandies who admired its melodrama, ran with desperate detail: she addressed him as “my friend,” imploring his pity after he had misread her intentions, protesting her gratitude and attachment, and describing her destitution—eating a herring for two sous and a sou’s worth of bread, the Chapuzots gone, landlords and jewelers pressing claims. She begged forgiveness and counsel, beseeching him to tell her if she had lost him forever. She feared returning to the country fairs where she would at least earn a living but where her thoughts of him would distract her and potentially cause harm. She signed, “Marguerite Turquet,” and vowed to be his for life.

Paz, reading or hearing of this letter, was amused and perhaps gratified; to him the melodramatic petition seemed to justify the ten thousand francs he had spent in his private folly. The affair cast light on his character: a man of deep feeling and devotion, trapped by gratitude, pride, and a reticence that turned inward love into eccentric acts rather than declarations. He had preferred to act indirectly—furnishing a home, providing money, setting up an appearance of paternal protection—rather than reveal his passion for the countess. The result was a series of misunderstandings: Clémentine had been tempted, momentarily impressed by his nobility; Malaga had mistaken his beneficence for some other sentiment and then condemned it as suspect when proprieties were threatened.

Throughout, the household survived on Paz’s unacknowledged labors. He had checked Adam’s extravagance, guarded their fortunes, and stabilized a family that would have been ill-served by their own indulgence. His love had become at once a source of personal torment and the motive for public charity and private eccentricity. By turning his passion outward toward Malaga he sought an outlet that would preserve his loyalty to Adam and spare Clémentine scandal, but the scheme collapsed when Malaga’s fears and pride repelled him and the public misread both benefactor and beneficiary.

Thus the little comedy of the circus—Paz’s invented Malaga, the moved countess, the diplomat’s curiosity, the Chapuzots’ gossip, Malaga’s letters and threats—revealed the tangled motives behind acts of devotion, the way silent love can become a social farce, and how generous restraint may be as misinterpreted as it is noble. Thaddeus’s final private thought—that Malaga’s letter was worth the money he had lavished—sums up his mingled satire and seriousness: the expenditure, the deception, the pain, all seemed at once ridiculous and justifiable in the ledger of a man who loved without hope and cared for a household at the cost of his own heart.

Clementine returned the next day, and Paz saw her again, more beautiful than ever. After dinner, when Thaddeus had gone, the count, on pretence of asking Adam’s advice, left Malaga’s tobacco-scented letter with Clementine. She read it with disgust. Adam denounced Malaga’s trickery and bewailed Thaddeus’s degradation; Clementine blamed him for indulgence toward such women. Adam praised Paz’s restraint—only ten thousand spent at first—and suggested Paz could be made to give her up if necessary. The countess, however, was scornful.

Paz, who knew Adam well, had begged secrecy and pleaded his friend’s case; he had also asked Adam for money for Malaga. The count and countess argued that his follies would ruin him. Clementine’s feline distrust persisted; she feared Paz would cause ruin despite his usefulness in managing their affairs and saving money. Yet her contempt for him deepened after Adam confided more about his concerns.

Thaddeus felt the weight of that contempt. Though he continued to adore Clementine silently—living in her life, tending her horse, overseeing her household—her disdain wounded him more than any former hardship. His love was selfless and constant; he found daily, secret joy in serving her, pleasures without recognition. Still, lacking her respect, he grew sick and feverish. Adam was uneasy; the countess dismissed the illness as a passing trouble. Thaddeus recovered after trying amusements.

In late February Adam lost a large sum at the Jockey-Club and begged Thaddeus to let the loss appear in the accounts as if spent on Malaga, to avoid the countess’s suspicion. Thaddeus sighed. When Clementine later discovered the money attributed to Malaga—thirty thousand francs in all—she exploded, accused the Poles of incomprehensible waste, and resolved to take over management of the household herself, though she admitted Paz’s utility. Adam defended Paz, noting his savings and the hundred thousand placed with Nucingen under Paz’s care.

Clementine summoned Paz alone and demanded he give up Malaga if he wished to remain in their house. She spoke with condescending familiarity, scolding him for being caught like a boy and for making the woman celebrated. Paz, stung, refused: giving up Malaga was not the sacrifice she imagined. He insisted that he had become indispensable in running their affairs and would not leave until he was certain Clementine could manage the household without him. She told him to go and return "cured." Paz answered that he would never be cured, and praised Malaga’s unexpected charm, provoking further contempt from the countess.

Paz then instructed Clementine in the management of her property, teaching her prices, servant oversight, and trusting Constantin as major-domo. By May he judged her competent; she proved instinctive and capable. But his inner trials continued. Adam fell gravely ill, and Paz stayed to nurse him, unwearied and devoted. Clementine, seeing only an Olympian sadness in Paz, had been cruel—she once dismissed him with the cutting words, “Poor Malaga!”—and that contempt haunted him. In the pavilion one day, speaking frankly, Thaddeus told her he had loved Adam like a son and that if Adam died he would be bereft of all interest in life; if Adam lived, he would continue to care for him. When he suggested that she might yet know what true love is, she countered with disdain, calling him “Poor Malaga!” and left him half crushed.

After that he redoubled his silent devotion to Adam. For forty-five days he watched without appearing to think of Malaga; his tireless care impressed the doctors. When the chief physician warned that Adam’s survival rested with his nurses, Paz devoted himself utterly. Clementine, exhausted, was forced to give up direct nursing; Paz continued until, at last, Bianchon declared Adam out of danger and publicly credited Comte Paz for his recovery.

Shortly thereafter, Paz surprised them with a sealed government despatch summoning him: he was appointed a captain for an expedition (first he seemed to say Khiva, later the Caucasus). He told them he must go, that he would leave Malaga a little independence, and spoke with a bitterness that made Clementine uneasy. He compared his leaving to seeking death, saying that to carry her contempt with him was more than he could bear. He declared that he had sacrificed his honor—“that which we desire to keep at any cost in the eyes of our idol”—and, driving past a wooden building on the Champs-Élysées, pointed out the Circus. By the next morning he had left Paris.

Adam mourned a friend’s loss, unable to understand why Paz would abandon him. Clementine, too, was stunned and uneasy; she found a letter Paz had written during the night to her in which he poured out the story of his love and his reasons for leaving. In that letter Paz confessed that, having loved Clementine from afar and found joy in being indispensable to her household, he had come to believe she might love him. Fearing to reveal his passion and knowing gratitude and duty barred him from declaring it—especially since she was Adam’s chosen—he had feigned a liaison with Malaga to ward off any hope that would violate his fidelity to Adam. He admitted that his fabricated scandal had been both a folly and a punishment: her contempt, once shown, made any return impossible. He explained that when the doctors had said Adam’s life depended on nursing, the thought of letting Adam die rather than betray him had made him resolve to depart; he would go to the Caucasus, he wrote, either to become someone greater or to die. The letter ends with his humble farewell and signature, “Your devoted Thaddeus Paz.”

Clementine sat stunned, reflecting on what she had lost and on Paz’s devotion. A private note Paz directed to Adam—delivered through Constantin—explained briefly that Malaga had told him everything and begged Adam never to mention his visits to the girl to Clementine, advising that it was better she think Malaga had cost Paz a fortune. He signed off again, telling Adam he was not going to Khiva but the Caucasus, and that he had taken sixty thousand francs from Nucingen so their accounts were even.

Years passed. Comte Paz’s fate remained ambiguous; newspapers had not announced any elevation to prince as he had hinted. The Comtesse Laginska, ever Russian-hearted, read eagerly of the Emperor Nicholas’s campaigns and sometimes asked the Russian ambassador indifferently what had become of poor Comte Paz. The narrative remarks on the sad fact that many women pass by a Paz—noble, devoted men—and fail to recognize them, often because their love lacks worldly polish.

In January 1842, the Comtesse inspired passionate attention from the Comte de La Palferine, who contrived a seduction with the help of a jealous friend. Clementine, imprudently trusting this friend, attended a masked ball at the Opera. Near dawn, amid the excitement, she accepted a supper and, as she was about to enter her companion’s carriage, was seized by a powerful hand and pulled into her own carriage—where she recognized Thaddeus, who vanished as the carriage drove off. Clementine thereafter often hoped she might see Paz again.

ADDENDUM

Several characters from this story recur elsewhere in Balzac’s Human Comedy: notably Bianchon (appearing in numerous works including Father Goriot and César Birotteau), Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski (Another Study of Woman; Cousin Betty), the Comte de La Palferine (A Prince of Bohemia; A Man of Business; Cousin Betty; Béatrix), and Thadée Paz (Cousin Betty). Others—Lelewel, Madame Nathan, Ronquerolles, the Rouvres, Schinner, the Serizys, Souchet, Steinbock, and Marguerite Turquet—also appear across Balzac’s linked tales.
