In September 1835 Mademoiselle du Rouvre, only daughter of the Marquis du Rouvre, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Though the Marquis had squandered much of his fortune, Clémentine was a substantial heiress through her mother’s family; settlements by relatives ensured her a steady annual income on marriage.

Comte Adam, an exile and not a charge on the French government, came from an illustrious Polish family but faced prejudice in Paris, where Polish nobility was poorly regarded. At first he was seen as a student enjoying Paris after losing political prospects. The Polish émigré community in France was divided between republican and noble exiles, and Adam became the butt of Parisian humor, with some questioning his wealth and status.

In 1834 Adam bought a house in the rue de la Pépinière, and by 1836 he was established in Parisian society, having fallen in love with Clémentine. Their marriage elevated his standing; he was soon recognized as fashionable. The couple’s home was lavish, blending various architectural styles and filled with exquisite decor that reflected the tastes and wealth of the new bourgeoisie.

Clémentine, now a countess, began to notice Captain Paz, a factotum who managed their affairs yet remained largely invisible. She questioned Adam about Paz, who was a constant presence, handling everything from finances to horse care. Adam explained that Paz came from a poor branch of the Paz family, had fought bravely for Poland, and had become a close friend through shared hardships.

Paz’s loyalty and discretion were evident; he ran the household with remarkable efficiency. Adam admired Paz’s character, insisting their friendship rested on mutual respect and understanding. Despite social hierarchies, Adam regarded Paz as an equal; once he offered Paz money, which Paz declined—an example of their proud bond.

As the couple prepared for a day out, Clémentine’s affection for both men grew, illustrating themes of loyalty, friendship, and the complexities of social status in 19th-century Paris, where personal ties could defy social norms.

Paz lived a reclusive life, largely unnoticed in Paris until the Comtesse Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne. Clémentine insisted that Thaddeus join them for dinner; he dressed for the occasion but behaved like a subordinate, listening and answering minimally. Adam urged him to be more lively, frustrated by Thaddeus’s dull demeanor, unaware that Thaddeus was struggling to conceal deep feelings for Clémentine.

After dinner they went to the Opera, where Thaddeus remained detached. Clémentine remarked on his lack of engagement; Adam called him disagreeable. Thaddeus’s coldness stemmed from a painful, unrequited love intensified after Clémentine returned from a trip. He took a bittersweet pride in being indispensable to their household, knowing the home would falter without his management, and found both joy and suffering in that role.

Clémentine, now more beautiful and secure in her marriage, concentrated on her social standing in Paris and had little space for thoughts of Thaddeus. Still, she once noticed him while driving home and invited him to join them, an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken emotion: Thaddeus felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance, preferring silent devotion to overt expression.

As days passed, Thaddeus settled back into routine, often overlooked while Clémentine was absorbed by social obligations and rivalry. He found solace in managing their affairs, yet his feelings remained a source of inner turmoil and reflection on the nature of unreciprocated love. When Clémentine held a reception, his absence went unnoticed; he returned to solitary habits, content in serving from afar while privately aching over the dual joy and pain his attachment brought.

Clémentine’s Parisian life was a constant contest for attention. Distracted by rivals and the strain of maintaining her status, she often neglected Adam. Thaddeus remained a steadfast presence, quietly overseeing their household while wrestling with his emotions.

In May, as Clémentine prepared to leave for her uncle’s estate, she again encountered Thaddeus; their brief exchange revived a curiosity in her. She insisted he join them for dinner. Though hesitant, he complied and attempted to engage more, but shyness and duty constrained him. As the evening progressed, Clémentine began to see him differently; their conversation revealed a deeper connection and Thaddeus felt momentarily emboldened. Still, loyalty to Adam restrained him from pursuing his desires openly.

Clémentine voiced dissatisfaction with Adam’s behavior and a yearning for more passion. Thaddeus, torn between love for her and friendship with Adam, recognized the complexity and felt the urge to confess but hesitated. Her insecurity and vulnerability gave him hope, yet he remained cautious; their talk deepened and the silence between them became charged.

To deflect the risk of revealing his true feelings, Thaddeus invented a story about being in love with a circus performer named Malaga — a captivated, agile contrast to Parisian women. The ruse allowed him to express longing for something simpler and more genuine without exposing his love for Clémentine. He visited Malaga as an emotional escape, providing for her comfortable living but maintaining no romantic entanglement. Though Malaga’s presence eased his solitude, his heart remained with Clémentine.

As Malaga’s circumstances shifted, she began to question Thaddeus’s intentions, causing misunderstandings and tension. Despite this, he continued as her benefactor while grappling with his own conflicted heart. Eventually he received a heartfelt letter from Malaga expressing despair over their separation and longing for his companionship. The letter struck him, echoing the depth and pain of his own unreturned love for Clémentine, and underscored the unwavering devotion that defined his life amid the intertwined demands of love, loyalty, and sacrifice.

Clementine returned home, and Paz saw her again, more beautiful than ever. After dinner the count and countess discussed Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam worried over Thaddeus’s misfortune, fearing he would lose everything because of his attachment to a circus performer; Clementine dismissed such women, while Adam defended Thaddeus, pointing out the sacrifices he had already made for Malaga.

Paz had asked Adam for money to help Malaga, and the countess regarded Paz as a possible ruin for Adam. Adam, though uneasy, believed Thaddeus would abandon Malaga if it suited him; the countess remained skeptical.

Soon Paz noticed a change in Clementine’s manner toward him and sensed her contempt, which plunged him into despair. Convinced he had caused her to despise him, he nevertheless found solace in admiring her silently at social gatherings and devoted himself to her comfort and the household. His love was deep, but he felt unworthy of her respect; the emotional strain briefly made him ill, though he later recovered.

After Adam lost a significant sum gambling, he asked Thaddeus to cover it and to blame the loss on Malaga. Thaddeus, feeling the weight of obligations, reluctantly complied. When Clementine discovered Thaddeus’s financial generosity toward Paz she expressed frustration and questioned his loyalty; Thaddeus, burdened by expectations and the need to manage her affairs, resolved to teach her household management so she might be more self-sufficient.

As spring arrived Adam fell seriously ill; Thaddeus devoted himself to nursing him while Clementine sought the best medical care. Through attentive, unceasing care Thaddeus managed to keep the count alive. After weeks Adam improved and Thaddeus received praise, but he remained tormented by his love for Clementine and the knowledge that he could never possess her. Concluding that leaving was the only way to escape his unrequited passion, he decided to go to the Caucasus.

Before departing Thaddeus wrote a heartfelt letter to Clementine, confessing his deep feelings and the sacrifices he had made for her happiness, and acknowledging that remaining in Paris would only prolong his pain. Clementine, on receiving the letter, reflected on her feelings and the loss of a devoted friend. Adam, unaware of the full extent of Thaddeus’s sacrifices, lamented the departure, convinced their bond might have withstood trials.

Years later Clementine often thought of Thaddeus, wondering about his fate. Unaware of his true feelings and depth of devotion, she was eventually drawn into a new romance, yet his memory lingered—a bittersweet reminder of a love that could never be.
