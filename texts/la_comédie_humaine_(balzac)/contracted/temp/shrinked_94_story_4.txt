**Summary of "Vendetta" by Honoré de Balzac**

**Dedication**
The novel is dedicated to Puttinati, a Milanese sculptor.

**Prologue**
In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo, a foreigner, stands outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and daughter. They are in despair, having lost everything in Corsica due to a vendetta against the Portas, who killed Bartolomeo's son. Bartolomeo decides to confront the Bonapartes, believing they have wronged him. He attempts to gain access to Napoleon but is initially rebuffed. However, he eventually meets Lucien Bonaparte, who takes him to see Napoleon. Bartolomeo reveals he has killed the Portas in revenge for his son's death, which shocks Napoleon. Despite his past, Napoleon offers Bartolomeo asylum in Paris, urging him to abandon the vendetta.

**The Studio**
Servin, a respected artist, opens a studio for young women to learn painting. He only accepts students from wealthy families, ensuring a reputation for propriety. Among his students is Ginevra Piombo, Bartolomeo's daughter, who is talented and admired. However, political tensions arise after the Bourbon Restoration, leading to jealousy among the students. Ginevra, a Bonapartist, faces ostracism from her peers, particularly from Amelie Thirion, who leads the aristocratic clique. Despite this, Ginevra remains dignified and focused on her art.

**Labedoyere’s Friend**
Ginevra discovers that a young officer, Luigi Porta, is hiding in Servin's studio after being wounded. He is a friend of Labedoyere, a Bonapartist leader. Ginevra feels a strong connection to Luigi, who shares her political beliefs. They fall in love, but their relationship is complicated by the vendetta between their families. Ginevra's parents disapprove of their union, leading to tension at home. Despite this, Ginevra is determined to marry Luigi, believing their love can overcome the feud.

**Marriage**
Ginevra and Luigi marry in a modest ceremony, reflecting their struggles. They face poverty but find joy in their love and the birth of their son, Bartolomeo. However, as financial difficulties mount, Ginevra's health deteriorates. She works tirelessly to support their family, but the strain takes its toll. Luigi, desperate to provide for them, sells his belongings and ultimately decides to enlist as a soldier to secure their future.

**Retribution**
Tragedy strikes when Ginevra dies from the hardships they endured. Luigi is devastated, and the couple's dreams are shattered. Bartolomeo, Ginevra's father, learns of her death and is filled with regret for his role in the vendetta that drove her away. The story concludes with a sense of loss and the futility of the vendetta, highlighting the destructive nature of familial feuds and the sacrifices made in the name of love.

**Addendum**
The novel connects with other stories in Balzac's "Human Comedy," featuring characters like Napoleon, Lucien Bonaparte, and various notaries, illustrating the intertwining lives and fates within French society during this tumultuous period.
