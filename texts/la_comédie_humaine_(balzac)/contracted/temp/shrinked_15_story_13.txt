THE MARRIAGE CONTRACT

CHAPTER I. PRO AND CON

Monsieur de Manerville was a respectable Norman gentleman whom the Maréchal de Richelieu married to a wealthy Bordeaux heiress when the old duke administered Guienne. The Norman sold his Bessin estate, became a Gascon by moving to his wife’s château de Lanstrac, and purchased the post of major of the Gate Guards toward the end of Louis XV’s reign. Having escaped the Revolution, he lived until 1813.

In late 1790 he went to Martinique, leaving his Gascogne property under the care of Mathias, a notary’s clerk who sympathized with, if not wholly adopted, revolutionary ideas. On his return the Comte de Manerville found his estates intact and well-managed—the Gascon’s energy grafted onto the Norman’s steadiness.

Madame de Manerville died in 1810. The old man, hardened by youthful dissipation, became penurious and niggardly, making almost nothing over to his only son.

Paul de Manerville returned from the college of Vendôme in 1810 and lived under strict paternal discipline for three years. The seventy-year-old despot shaped a young heart and character still unformed; Paul, naturally bold in the Gascon tradition, dared not resist his father and lost that formative faculty of resistance. His suppressed feelings hardened inwardly; later, when they collided with societal maxims, he could reason rightly but act wrongly. He might fight passionately for a word or look yet tremble at dismissing a servant—his timidity showing in struggles requiring steady will. Capable of bold deeds to escape persecution, he would not have prevented it by sustained opposition. Timid in thought, bold in action, he retained a simplicity that made him a dupe and a voluntary victim of things against which other souls revolt. Imprisoned by his father’s mansion and lacking money to consort with peers, he envied their pleasures without sharing them.

Each evening the old man took him in a shabby carriage to royalist houses, where the relics of parliamentary and martial nobility gathered—landed aristocrats crushed by the wealth of maritime cities, priding themselves on disdain for commerce’s display. Too young to grasp such social distinctions, Paul was bored by these ancients, unaware that those early connections would later secure the preeminence Frenchmen seek.

He found some compensation in the manual exercises his father insisted upon. The old gentleman believed that fencing, riding, pistol practice, tennis, and good manners—frivolous accomplishments of old nobility—made a modern young man a gentleman. Thus Paul took fencing lessons, rode, practiced at a pistol gallery, and spent the rest of his time reading novels, his father forbidding the abstruse studies then becoming necessary.

So monotonous a life would have killed him had not his father’s death delivered him when it became intolerable. Paul then found himself possessor of considerable capital accumulated by paternal avarice, with well-kept estates. But he disliked Bordeaux; Lanstrac, the summer hunting place, did not captivate him.

Once the estate was settled, the young heir, eager for enjoyment, invested in consols, entrusted estate administration to Mathias, and spent six years abroad: attached to the French embassy at Naples, secretary at Madrid, then in London—making a European tour.

After seeing the world, losing illusions, and dissipating his father’s capital, Paul had to draw upon the territorial revenues Mathias had been saving. Seized by a virtuously named impulse, he resolved to leave Paris, return to Bordeaux, regulate his affairs, live as a country gentleman at Lanstrac, improve his property, marry, and eventually pursue a deputy’s seat.

Paul was a count; nobility had matrimonial value again; he could and ought to make a good marriage. In exchange for seven hundred thousand francs squandered in six years he had gained what cannot be bought—experience: the knowledge drawn from social apprenticeship, love affairs, duels, lost bets, disillusions and pleasures. He had become what is called elegant. Despite extravagance he had not become a mere man of fashion. In that burlesque army of the worldlings, the man of elegance ranks above the mere fashionable man. Paul enjoyed that reputation and sustained it: his servants were well dressed, his equipages noted, his suppers in vogue; his bachelor establishment rivaled the finest Paris houses.

He had not debauched any woman; he gambled openly, was honest enough not to mislead, left no billets-doux about, and kept no chest of love-letters. Not willing to tap his Guienne property, he lacked the bold extravagance that attracts attention. He did not borrow, though he foolishly lent to friends who deserted him. He seemed methodical in his dissipations. The secret of his character lay in his father’s tyranny, which had made him, as it were, a social mongrel.

One morning he told his friend de Marsay, soon to be celebrated,—

“My dear fellow, life has a meaning.”

“You must be twenty-seven to find it,” de Marsay laughed.

“Well, I am twenty-seven; and because I am twenty-seven I mean to live the country-gentleman life at Lanstrac. I’ll move my belongings to Bordeaux, and spend three months a year in Paris in this house I shall keep.”

“Will you marry?”

“I will marry.”

“I’m your friend,” de Marsay said after a pause, “and I tell you: settle down into a worthy father and husband and you’ll be ridiculous for the rest of your days. If you could be happy and ridiculous, it might be bearable; but you won’t be happy. You don’t have the strong wrist to manage a household. I’ll do you justice: you are a perfect horseman—no one knows how to set a horse—but marriage is another thing. I see you led by Madame la Comtesse de Manerville, going where you would not, oftener at a gallop than a trot, and presently unhorsed—broken. Listen. You still have some forty-odd thousand francs a year from your Gironde property. Take your horses and servants and furnish your house in Bordeaux; you can be king of Bordeaux, proclaim our Paris edicts; be the correspondent of our follies. Play the provincial rake; commit follies that may bring celebrity. But don’t marry. Who marries nowadays? Merchants for capital, peasants for children to work, brokers and notaries for wives’ dots, miserable kings to continue dynasties. We are exempt from the pack, and you want to join it! Why marry? If you wed an heiress as rich as yourself, eighty thousand francs for two is not the same as forty for one; two become three or four with children. Do you love the old Manerville race enough to be hampered by it? Consider what parents must be. Marriage, my old Paul, is the silliest social immolation; our children alone profit and don’t know its price until their horses nibble the flowers on our grave. Do you regret your father—the old tyrant who made your youth wretched? How can you be sure your children will love you? The care you take and the necessary sternness will lessen their affection. Children love a weak or prodigal father, whom they later despise. You’ll live between fear and contempt. No man becomes a good head of a family simply by willing it. Look at our friends and name one you’d like as a son. Marriage breeds many who dishonor their names. Children, my dear Paul, are the most difficult merchandise to keep. You think yours will be angels. Fine. Have you measured the gulf between bachelor and married life? As a bachelor you limit your ridiculousness; as a married man you fall into its infinitude. A bachelor makes his own happiness; married, you must take it as it comes. Marry and you’ll grow a blockhead; you’ll calculate dowries; you’ll talk morality; you’ll become a social academician. It’s pitiable! The old bachelor hoarding his spoon is blessed compared with the married man. I speak not merely of annoyances, bores, coercions and tyrannies of constant companionship; to relate them would repeat Boileau. Still, I’ll forgive your absurd idea if you promise to marry ‘en grand seigneur’: entail your property; have two legitimate children; give your wife a house and household distinct from yours; meet her only in society, never return from a journey without sending her a courier. Two hundred thousand francs a year will suffice, and your antecedents will enable you to marry some rich Englishwoman hungry for a title. That’s an aristocratic life thoroughly French; it retains a woman’s respect and a man’s distinction. Thus the Comte de Manerville may advise his epoch, place himself above the world, be minister or ambassador. Ridicule can never touch him; he gains social advantages of marriage while keeping bachelor privileges.”

“But, my good friend, I am not de Marsay,” said Paul; “I am Paul de Manerville, a worthy father and husband, deputy of the Centre, possibly peer of France—a destiny commonplace; but I am modest and I resign myself.”

“Yes, but your wife,” the pitiless de Marsay went on, “will she resign herself?”

“My wife, my dear fellow, will do as I wish.”

“Ah! my poor friend, is that where you are? Adieu, Paul. Henceforth I refuse to respect you. One word more; I cannot agree coldly to your abdication. Look where the strength of our position lies. A bachelor with only six thousand francs a year left has at least his reputation for elegance and memory of success. Life still offers many chances to the unmarried man. You can aim at anything. But marriage is the social ‘Thus far shalt thou go and no farther.’ Once married you can never be anything but what you then are—unless your wife deigns to care for you.”

“But,” said Paul, “you crush me with exceptional theories. I am tired of living for others; of having horses merely to exhibit them; of doing everything for what may be said; of wasting my substance to keep fools from crying, ‘Dear! Paul is still driving the same carriage. What has he done with his fortune?’ Believe me, Henri, I admire your power, but I don't envy it. You judge life like a statesman, place yourself above prejudices, and grasp the profits of situations in which I would find only ill luck. Your cool deductions are, to the masses, shockingly immoral. I belong to the masses. I must play by society’s rules. While placing yourself above things on peaks of ice, you still have feelings; as for me I should freeze to death. The life of the great majority, to which I belong, is made of emotions I now need. Often a man coquets with a dozen women and obtains none. Whatever his strength, he is crushed in convulsions. For my part, I like the peaceful chances and changes of life; I want a woman always at my side.”

“A trifle indecorous, your marriage!” exclaimed de Marsay.

Paul was not disconcerted: “Laugh if you like; I shall feel happy when my valet says in the morning, ‘Madame is awaiting monsieur for breakfast’; happier still at night, to return to find a heart—”

“Altogether indecorous,” said de Marsay. “You are not yet moral enough to marry.”

“But,” Paul continued, “a heart to confide my interests and secrets. I wish a close union with a woman so our affection shall not depend on a yes or no, nor be open to love’s disillusions. I have the courage to become, as you say, a worthy husband and father. I feel fitted for family joys; I want to submit myself to society’s conditions; I desire a wife and children.”

“You remind me of a hive of honey-bees! But go your way; you’ll be a dupe all your life. Ha! you wish to marry to have a wife! In other words, you wish to solve to your own profit the most difficult problem created by bourgeois morals of the Revolution; and, more, you mean to begin by a life of retirement. Do you think your wife won’t crave the life you say you despise? If you won’t accept the noble conjugality I’ve outlined, at least hear my final advice. Remain a bachelor thirteen years; amuse yourself; at forty, on your first gout attack, marry a widow of thirty-six; then you may be happy. If you take a young girl now, you’ll die a madman.”

“Ah ça! tell me why!” cried Paul, piqued.

“My dear fellow,” de Marsay replied, “Boileau’s satire on women is common enough. But the problem of marriage is not where Boileau puts it. Do you suppose marriage equals love and that being a man suffices to make a wife love you? Have you learned nothing in your boudoir experience but agreeable memories? Everything in our bachelor life leads to fatal errors in married life unless one profoundly observes the human heart. In youth a man triumphs over women ready to be triumphed over. Obstacles created by law, sentiment and natural defenses engender sensations that deceive the superficial about future married relations, where obstacles no longer exist. The wife submits to love instead of permitting it and often repulses pleasure. The whole aspect of life changes. The bachelor, free and without care, need never fear repulsion; in marriage, repulsion is nearly certain and irreparable. A lover may sometimes change a woman’s unfavorable decision, but such a change is the husband’s Waterloo. Like Napoleon, the husband achieves victories which cannot undo the first defeat. The woman, flattered by perseverance as a lover, calls the same ardor brutality in a husband. You, who talk of marrying, have you reflected on the Civil Code? I never loved that Law-school’s gossip, but I see the Code’s application. The Code makes woman a ward; it treats her like a minor. How must we govern children? By fear. In that word, Paul, is the curb of the beast. Now feel your pulse! Have you the strength to play the tyrant—you, so gentle, so confiding? This is science—Anthropology. Ah! if I had not solved life’s mystery by pleasure, if I did not despise those who think instead of act, I’d write a book on modern marriages and stick a lantern on that heap of sharp stones where votaries of the social ‘multiplicamini’ lie. But does humanity require my time? Besides, isn’t ink better used to snare hearts with love-letters? Well, shall you bring the Comtesse de Manerville here and let us see her?”

“Perhaps,” said Paul.

“We shall still be friends,” said de Marsay.

“If—” replied Paul.

“Don’t be uneasy; we will treat you politely, as Maison-Rouge treated the English at Fontenoy.”

Though the foregoing conversation affected the Comte de Manerville, he carried out his intentions and returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821.

Restoring and furnishing his family mansion sustained the elegant reputation that preceded him. Reintroduced by old connections to Bordeaux’s royalist society—which his opinions, name, and fortune bound him to—he quickly took a fashionable pre‑eminence. The Faubourg Saint‑Germain of Bordeaux admired his knowledge of life and Parisian accomplishments. An old marquise revived a courtly phrase, calling him “the pink of fashion”; the liberal clique used it satirically, the royalists sincerely.

Paul de Manerville fulfilled his flowery title. In public he seemed superior, and at ease displayed the fine qualities that accompanied his defects. His wit was mild; his manners not supercilious; his intercourse with women expressed a temperate respect they liked. His foppery was merely a carefulness of appearance that pleased. He respected rank, granted youth a certain freedom, and though adept with sword and pistol, was noted for a feminine gentleness. Medium height and plumpness did not prevent his playing the part of a Bordelais Brummell: fair complexion, handsome hands and feet, blue eyes fringed by long lashes, black hair, graceful motions, and a chest voice that vibrated in listeners’ hearts harmonized with his sobriquet. Paul was a delicate flower needing careful culture—one more to receive than to give happiness, with something of the woman in his nature—one to whom conjugal love should come as a providence.

Such a character creates private difficulties but commands public attraction. Consequently Paul succeeded greatly in the narrow provincial circle, where his half‑tinted mind was better appreciated than it would have been in Paris.

Restoring his town house and the château de Lanstrac—into which he introduced English country‑house comfort—consumed the capital the notary had saved over six years. Reduced now to his strict income of about forty thousand a year, he believed himself prudent in regulating his household accordingly.

After flaunting his equipages, entertaining the town’s most distinguished young men, and giving hunting parties at Lanstrac, Paul saw that provincial life would not do without marriage. He was too young for stinginess or for speculative improvements provincials undertake to secure children; he needed the variety of Parisian life. A name to preserve, property to transmit, a household to create social relations, and a weariness of irregular connections were motives, but the chief was his secret love for Bordeaux’s queen, the great beauty Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the century’s beginning a rich Spaniard named Evangelista had settled in Bordeaux; letters of recommendation and large fortune opened noble salons to him. His wife, a Creole from the Casa‑Reale, lived as a great lady, ignorant of money’s value and indulged by an adoring husband. From 1800 to 1812 the Evangelistas were objects of interest. The Spaniard died in 1813, leaving Madame Evangelista a thirty‑two‑year‑old widow with an immense fortune and a prettiest little girl of eleven who became an accomplished young woman. The Restoration shifted social positions; royalist ranks cleared and old families left Bordeaux. Lacking her husband’s direction, Madame Evangelista resolved not to change her mode of living: by squandering large sums on brilliant fêtes and a quasi‑regal life she maintained the public belief in her wealth while her capital steadily diminished.

Natalie, now nineteen, seemed the richest match in Bordeaux, but proposals had not come. Accustomed to indulgence, she wore cashmeres and jewels and lived in a luxury that alarmed the calculating provincials. A fatal remark circulated: “None but a prince can afford to marry Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Mothers jealous of Natalie’s beauty and tyrannical elegance spread treacherous warnings—her costly toilette, horses, maid, Mechlin laces—deterring suitors. Pampered as she was, Natalie knew nothing of the value of money or household maintenance; she bounded through life unbridled and unshod.

Six months after Paul’s arrival the Pink of Fashion and the Queen of Balls met in Bordeaux’s highest society. They exchanged apparently cold glances, each thinking the other charming. Madame Evangelista muttered, “He will be my son‑in‑law.” Paul thought, “She will be my wife.”

The Evangelistas’ reputed wealth lingered in Paul’s memory, so their pecuniary condition was known to him. When society’s flattering remarks about Natalie ended with warnings about extravagance, Paul replied with disdain that shut down provincial pettiness. He brought to Bordeaux a certain Britannic reserve and Byronic pessimism, together with English plate, boot‑polish, ponies, yellow gloves, cigars, and a habit of galloping—thus avoiding discouragements that deterred other suitors. Madame Evangelista asked him to formal dinners; he accepted, pleased to breathe the grandeur and luxury he loved.

When he rode by in his tilbury or on his English horse, young men said, “There’s a lucky man. He is rich and handsome, and is to marry, so they say, Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Society, which never does good without doing evil, credited Natalie with a dowry of a million and bestowed her, almost before consent, upon Paul. Their fortunes were thought to match: Paul’s habits of luxury and his newly arranged house made him seem uniquely fitted to meet the pecuniary difficulties of marrying such a daughter of a Creole great lady. Royalist friends flattered him: “Everyone gives you Mademoiselle Evangelista. You could not find, even in Paris, a more delightful girl. Her mother is intelligent and adroit; she will be a great help in public life. You must soon bring the matter to a close.”

“That is all very well,” replied Paul, anxious to keep freedom, “but I must be sure that the conclusion shall be a happy one.”

He therefore went often to the Hôtel Evangelista, partly to occupy idle hours harder for him than for most men. Madame Evangelista, at forty, had the beauty of a glorious summer sunset. Her spotless reputation was much discussed; her temperament combined the force and seductiveness of Spanish and Creole women. She had black eyes and hair, a swaying form and a complexion like muslin over crimson—white suffused with color. Full and rounded, she possessed the nonchalance of strength united with vivacity, sometimes the carriage of a queen. Men were taken by her conversation; she had the genius for schemes necessity bestows, advancing from concession to concession and retreating when asked for too much. Though ignorant of facts, her familiarity with courts and illustrious families gave a superficial but impressive education. She received society with natural grace and dignity, and her reputed virtue lent authority to her actions.

Mother and daughter had a true friendship that went beyond maternal and filial bonds; perpetual contact produced no jar. Madame Evangelista’s perpetual widowhood was not explained solely by maternal devotion: she had once loved a man who recovered titles and property under the Restoration, courted her to marry in 1814, and severed the connection by 1816. She nursed a resentment—“Odiate e aspettate”—and relished a revengeful triumph when he later ruined himself. This misadventure and pride kept her from remarrying and turned her motherly devotion into an ambition to place her daughter at the heart of Parisian society.

Madame Evangelista divined Paul’s nature and concealed her own. He was precisely the man she wanted for a son‑in‑law, an instrument for future influence. Through his mother he belonged to the Maulincour family; his connection to the Baronne de Maulincour and her grandson Auguste’s army position made Paul an excellent introducer for the Evangelistas into Parisian society. The widow, who disliked residing in Bordeaux, longed to shine in Restoration Paris, where political fortune lay. She looked to Paul as a means to that end; many men serve as screens for secret feminine ambitions, and Madame Evangelista had more than one interest in holding her daughter’s husband.

Paul was captivated. The mother charmed him by seeming to seek no influence while subtly magnifying herself and her daughter in his eyes. He valued himself more for their appreciation; he fancied himself clever when his remarks pleased Natalie and flattered when the mother’s praise appeared involuntary. So kind and welcoming were they that he spent most of his time at the Hôtel Evangelista.

A year after his return, though Paul had not declared himself, his attentions made the world consider him a suitor. Neither mother nor daughter pressed marriage. Natalie preserved the reserve of a great lady who can charm without allowing intimacy—a reserve that pleased Paul, timid and disliking sudden proposals. He therefore committed himself inwardly all the more. The mother’s remark that superior women sometimes place ambition above earlier emotions seduced him: “That woman is fitted,” he thought, “to advance me in diplomacy before I am even made a deputy.”

Paul was an optimist; he saw everything to advantage and did not suspect that an ambitious mother‑in‑law might become a tyrant. He pictured himself married each evening and embraced wedlock cheerfully—bachelor life seemed dull. “Marriage,” he mused, “is disagreeable for people without means, but half its troubles disappear before wealth.” Every day some favorable consideration swelled his sense of advantage.

“No matter what position I attain, Natalie will always be on the level of her part,” he told himself. “How many Empire men I’ve seen who suffered horribly through their wives! It is a great condition of happiness not to feel one’s pride or vanity wounded by the companion we have chosen. A man can never be really unhappy with a well‑bred wife; she will never make him ridiculous.” He taxed his memory among Faubourg Saint‑Germain women to see Natalie as their equal; all comparisons favored her, resting on his imagination, which followed his desires. Paris would have shown other types to balance his mind; in Bordeaux Natalie had no rivals and appeared when Paul was under an age‑old tyrant: the domination of an idea.

Thus propinquity, self‑love, and a real passion whose outlet was marriage led Paul into an irrational love he prudently kept to himself. He tried to study Natalie, mindful of de Marsay’s warnings. But luxury breeds indifference; Paul never imagined the gulf of ruin beneath the ladies’ habits. Moreover, there are no rules to predict conjugal harmony; young people display favorable weather only, oblivious to life’s common gray days. Each shows a favorable mask; later they blame marriage for evils inherent in life.

To detect in Natalie any sign of future imperfections would have required not only physiognomy but a kind of personal science few possess. Her face, like most young girls’, was impenetrable. The sculptural calm of virgin faces—Justice, Innocence—marked purity; such placid expression had not been clouded by passion or betrayal. If that expression were assumed, then no young woman remained behind it.

Yet any clever analyst might find in Natalie indications of future difficulties. Her beauty arose from extreme regularity of feature and proportion—perfection that often foreshadows coldness of organization. Superior nature usually shows slight imperfections that become luminous attractions; perfect harmony often indicates a mixed, colder nature. Her round waist signaled strength and a will liable to obstinacy; statue‑like hands suggested an inclination for illogical domination; meeting eyebrows hinted at jealousy that in inferior minds becomes hatred. The “hate and wait” of her mother lived in her. Her eyes were blackish brown with orange streaks, her hair a ruddy auburn—an external contrast giving charm but not necessarily soul. Where outlines lack soft roundness, beauty is not of the soul. Such roses of youth will drop their leaves, revealing hardness and dryness beneath admired nobility.

Though her face had some august outlines, her slightly full chin suggested passions that might grow violent; her astringent mouth expressed haughty pride consistent with hand, chin, brows, and figure. Her pure voice, though seductive, had metallic tones hinting at Casa‑Reale temperament. These marks suggested violent passions without tenderness,—sudden devotions, irreconcilable dislikes, a mind not deeply intelligent, and a desire to rule natural in those who feel their station inferior to their pretensions.

These defects lay in Natalie like ore in a mine and would only surface under life’s shocks. Meantime grace, freshness, distinction, sacred ignorance, and youthful sweetness gave a glamour that misled unthinking minds. Her mother taught her the art of agreeable talk, replying to arguments with clever jests and concealing the subsoil of her mind with volubility. Natalie had the charm of those who never knew suffering: frank, gay, natural, expecting pleasure from marriage and answering objections with jest.

How could Paul, dazzled by such beauty, foresee the woman she might be at thirty? Besides, happiness in such a marriage was not impossible. Embryo defects could be stifled by proper training, especially in a young girl who loved him. But to render ductile an intractable woman the iron wrist de Marsay recommended was necessary. Fear inspired by love is an instrument to manage women; he who loves fears, and thus is nearer affection than hatred.

Had Paul the coolness, firmness, and judgment required for this struggle? Did Natalie love him? Like most young girls, she mistook instinct and the pleasure of his external appearance for love; she knew nothing of marriage or its demands. Would she have the tact to distinguish his noble qualities among lesser defects, or would she magnify the latter and forget the former, as inexperienced wives often do? There comes a time when wives pardon defects in a husband who spares them annoyances; what conciliating power or wise experience would uphold this home? Paul, weak where even a strong man runs risks, might yield to tyranny instead of establishing his rule. Could he say “No”?

The subject here is not merely the change from bachelor to married man—a picture that would itself offer rich drama—but the great comedy that precedes all conjugal pairing in France: the discussion before a notary about the marriage contract, played in families noble and bourgeois, where human passions are excited by small interests as by large ones. This scene, neglected by dramatists though full of wit, controlled Paul’s future life and was awaited by Madame Evangelista with terror. It is to this comedy—the notarial negotiation—that we now turn.

At the beginning of the winter of 1822, Paul de Manerville, through his great-aunt the Baronne de Maulincour, formally asked for Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand. The baroness lingered in Medoc until late October to assist him and then returned with good news.

“My child,” she reported, “the affair is won. Madame Evangelista gives nothing of her own to her daughter; Mademoiselle Natalie’s dowry is her patrimony. Marry her. Men with a name and estate must marry to continue the family. Consult Maitre Mathias; the marriage contract is a sacred duty.”

Troubled by his aunt’s hint that Madame Evangelista was a “sly cat,” Paul followed her counsel and engaged Maitre Mathias to draw the contract. To avoid offending his future mother-in-law he approached her with hesitant courtesy.

“Of course,” she said when he mentioned his notary. “Notaries of both families usually agree.” Inwardly anxious, she concealed the truth: legally she owed her daughter one-third of Monsieur Evangelista’s fortune—about 1,156,000 francs—but lacked liquid means to pay it. If Paul learned this through his notary he might withdraw and spoil her plan. She resolved to fight for her interests, confident she could secure Natalie’s happiness and find an escape if necessary.

Madame Evangelista summoned her own notary, Maitre Solonet, a young, ambitious royalist and dandy noted for gallantry and political zeal. He arrived promptly and was flattered to be received. She explained the situation bluntly.

“Mademoiselle Natalie’s dowry is her patrimony,” Solonet said. “How have you comported yourself toward Monsieur de Manerville? In matters like these deportment matters more than law. Is the Comte de Manerville in love with Mademoiselle Natalie?”

“He adores her,” the mother answered. Solonet counseled display and charm. “Make her look her best tonight. The marriage-contract dress is half the battle,” he declared.

Madame Evangelista dressed Natalie herself. In white tulle with pink ribbons and hair à la Sévigné, Natalie looked charming. Her mother probed her feelings for Paul.

“If you and I had to part forever would you still persist in the marriage?”

“I should give it up,” Natalie replied.

“You do not love him,” her mother said, kissing her brow.

“But I love him,” Natalie insisted.

Madame Evangelista warned that if Paul hesitated at sight of them in all their glory, she would break off the match and seek another husband. Natalie, loyal and curious, promised that if property difficulties separated them she alone would bear the blame.

Paul arrived with his habitual bouquet. He and Natalie spoke fondly of the future—Lanstrac, Paris, children—while awaiting the notaries. But the business of marriage soon intruded. For Paul the alliance seemed simple; yet the legal and financial details were a minefield. The two notaries embodied different eras and temperaments.

Maitre Mathias, Paul’s notary, was an elderly, venerable professional with forty years’ experience: slight, odd in appearance, but respected for uprightness, painstaking care, and long faithful service. Solonet, by contrast, was young, fashionable, mixing politics with practice, eager for profit and advancement. When they met at Madame Evangelista’s salon they represented “former times and the present day.”

Solonet, eager to secure his client’s ends, had pre-drawn a contract favoring the widow. Mathias insisted on details and clarity. He listed Paul’s property—Lanstrac (23,000 francs rental), farms and vineyards, houses in Bordeaux and Paris, furniture—totaling significant landed wealth. Solonet countered that the Evangelista inheritance had been invested in Five-per-cent funds and was effectively about 800,000 francs, that the Paris house added value, and that the mother could convey titles while reserving a life interest.

Mathias challenged vague claims and demanded inventories. Solonet replied the family, being Spanish and Creole, had not followed French formalities at the father’s death and had reinvested English securities in Paris, making strict accounting difficult. When Mathias pressed the difference between land and marketable funds, the conversation turned heated. Solonet’s aim was a settlement that preserved the widow’s respectability while releasing Natalie’s patrimony; Mathias sought to protect Paul’s estate and his future children.

Natalie, playing with a fan, watched and then, moved by loyalty, told Paul in private that if property difficulties separated them she alone would bear the blame. Paul, not yet told of Mathias’s alarm, pressed her hand and returned to the group. Mathias, alarmed, warned Paul in a private aside that there was little ready dowry; he advised postponement to reflect.

Solonet quickly offered a way out: sell the house, reckon the Five-per-cents as nearly at par, and convey titles into Natalie’s name while reserving a life interest for Madame Evangelista. This would give immediate recognition of a large patrimony and close the guardianship account. Mathias saw a trap: the scheme would acknowledge receipt of about 1,156,000 francs, binding Paul to account to his children for that sum later while the family actually had less secure capital. He protested that the arrangement would impoverish Paul and his heirs and leave him liable under misfortune.

Madame Evangelista, provoked, threatened to break off the marriage and leave Bordeaux; she appealed to Spanish ways and to the sentimental value of her name as a kind of dowry. The argument grew tense: Paul, torn between love and prudence, nearly yielded to emotion. Mathias, seeing Paul’s distress and Madame Evangelista’s ambition—she wanted Paul to become a peer of France—suddenly conceived a legal remedy that could reconcile interests.

Mathias proposed that to secure Paul’s future public prospects and protect his estate, the parties entail Lanstrac and the Paris house and include eight hundred thousand francs of Natalie’s patrimony in that entail by purchasing adjoining domains and having them included in the royal ordinance required for an entail. In other words, apply the convertible portion of Natalie’s fortune to create inalienable capital that would strengthen the family’s landed estate, thus satisfying Madame Evangelista’s desire to release Natalie’s patrimony and calming Paul’s and Mathias’s fear of immediate impoverishment. This would allow Mathias to accept the guardianship account’s deficiency.

Solonet was surprised but recognized that the entail would placate distrust: an entail makes property inalienable and would reassure Paul and his advisers that the capital could not be wasted. Madame Evangelista, ignorant of legal technicalities, accepted Mathias’s plan because it seemed to secure Natalie’s patrimony and her own safety. Both she and Paul agreed that if the entail were created, the marriage should proceed.

Mathias emphasized that the formalities required a royal ordinance and immediate action, but that consent to proceed would suffice for the parties to go on. Natalie, a minor, was not party to the formal bargaining beyond assent.

It was arranged that Mathias would draw up the contract, Solonet the guardianship account and release, and both documents should be signed days before the marriage. The notaries left together, each satisfied professionally: Solonet for getting his client married under an appearance of security, Mathias for having devised a legal mechanism to protect Manerville.

Left alone, Paul and Madame Evangelista reconciled their earlier sharp words. Paul was ecstatic at the prospect that, with the entail and a combined household, he and Natalie could have an income near one hundred thousand francs and he might obtain a peerage. Madame Evangelista, relieved, expressed gratitude and tenderness; she proposed to give Natalie her diamonds and assured Paul she would sell her house and find means to secure herself without injuring her daughter’s patrimony.

Natalie reacted with emotional generosity, insisting her father’s diamonds remain but accepting that her mother’s sacrifice might be required. Madame Evangelista planned to have the jewels appraised and to sell plate and other items. She spoke of moving winters to Paris, of Lanstrac as their home, and of Solonet’s aid in settling debts. She fell asleep convinced she had achieved her ends: Natalie married to a man she could influence, a household assured, and an ambitious plan for Paul’s political advancement.

Thus the first day of negotiation ended with apparent satisfaction on all sides: Paul convinced, Madame Evangelista appeased, Natalie content, Mathias relieved that a protective entail might save Manerville, and Solonet pleased at having guided the match. Yet beneath this harmony lay seeds of distrust and competing designs—Madame Evangelista believing herself triumphant, Mathias guarding his client’s legacy, Solonet enjoying the victory, and Paul dreaming of a bright future of love and ambition.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, obeyed Madame Evangelista’s summons. Rumors that Comte Paul was marrying Mademoiselle Natalie made it seem she wanted jewels for the bride; instead she had him value the mother-in-law’s property. Suspecting the diamonds’ worth would be included in the marriage contract, she instructed him to value them as if for a private buyer even though they would not be sold. He explained that expert jewellers distinguish Asian from Brazilian diamonds: Golconda and Visapur stones have superior whiteness and brilliance that Brazilian stones, with a yellow tinge, lack. Madame Evangelista’s necklace and earrings, composed entirely of Asiatic diamonds, he valued at 250,000 francs; the “Discreto” he called one of the finest private diamonds, worth 100,000 francs. She asked if she could obtain that money immediately.

Magus replied that if she wished to sell he could only give seventy-five thousand for the brilliant and one hundred and sixty thousand for the necklace and earrings. He explained the dealers’ margin: rarer gems can remain unsold longer, so buying prices are lower than selling prices; owning them ties up capital and yields no interest. He illustrated his point with calculations about the practical cost of wearing diamonds. Madame Evangelista thanked him and, when he asked if she would sell the rest, he valued other jewels—Brazilian diamonds, pearls, rubies—at about one hundred thousand to a dealer, though a dealer might resell them for a hundred and fifty thousand.

Madame Evangelista declared she would keep them; Magus warned that investing the income from that capital could replace similar diamonds in five years while preserving the principal. The conference leaked. Servants overheard raised voices, gossip exaggerated practical difficulties, and the rumor mill magnified the story: had Madame Evangelista sold her house? Was the marriage off? Notaries denied collapse, blaming only delay in constituting the entail, but society persisted. Young women and thwarted provincial mothers, envious after two decades of Evangelista magnificence, spread and embellished the tale. Officials at the prefecture hinted the notaries’ language implied a rupture. People speculated about quibbles over the entail, outraged that a wealthy widow might lack funds to pay her daughter’s patrimony. Bets were laid; yet no one told Paul or Madame Evangelista the gossip. Paul had business at Lanstrac and gave a hunting-party as a kind of farewell to bachelor life; society interpreted this as evidence of trouble.

Madame de Gyas, hoping to marry her daughter, felt obliged to sound out the Evangelistas and addressed them with a long face as if to disclose misfortune. Natalie and her mother laughed at the recital of gossip but recognized the motives: provincial mothers, thwarted by lack of dowry or fortune, might imagine such plots. The Marquise launched into a spirited harangue about mothers desperate to secure husbands for daughters, likening them to predatory creatures; she mocked public judgment of private marriage clauses. Madame Evangelista, preparing to leave the city, could safely mock both friends and enemies and enjoyed making remarks that stung.

When a footman announced Paul—who had ridden twelve miles to spend an hour with Natalie—she greeted him, and he dismissed the gossip with laughter. “These worthy people have found out, perhaps, that there will be no wedding festivities,” he said. He suggested pacifying them with a ball on the day they signed the contract: a public show to refute the rumors. Madame Evangelista took him at his word and invited all Bordeaux to witness the contract signature; she intended the last fete to silence the gossip.

Preparations for the camellia fête consumed a month. Camellias banked staircases and supper-rooms; partitions were removed to enlarge rooms; estates near Lanstrac were purchased, banns published, and doubts dissipated. For a while the earlier stormy conference faded—after all, as Madame Evangelista had said, it had seemed only the notaries’ affair.

Yet on the morning of the signing a flash of memory returned to Madame Evangelista: Mathias’s earlier words when he had yielded to Solonet’s conditions—“Questa coda non e di questo gatto”—suggested someone would pay the cost. If Mathias had seen compensation, it could not be Paul. Could her daughter’s fortune be made to meet the price? She resolved to seek explanations for the contract’s tenor. This day would profoundly affect Paul de Manerville’s conjugal life.

Despite the winter, the courtyard was tented, shrubs and banked camellias decorated the house, and the ballroom and supper-room were fitted for the fairyland Bordeaux expected. About eight, as the final contract was discussed, a dense crowd gathered to see the ladies in full dress. The two notaries had dined with the bridal pair and their mother; Mathias’s head-clerk was present to receive signatures and guard against surreptitious readings.

No bridal toilette equalled Natalie’s; she was superb. Madame Evangelista, in cherry velvet, wore her pearl necklace clasped with the “Discreto,” a visible contradiction to calumnies of ruin. Paul and Natalie sat together by the fire, inattentive to the reading, whispering and touching; Natalie toyed with a peacock-feather fan Paul had given her. Seated by the notaries, Madame Evangelista listened as Solonet read the guardianship account: Natalie’s share of Monsieur Evangelista’s three million-plus estate amounted to eleven hundred and fifty-six thousand francs. “Come, listen, listen, my children; this is your marriage contract,” she said.

The clerical routine proceeded: statement of each party’s property, general deeds of gift in case of death without issue, deed of gift of a life interest and a quarter of capital allowed by the Code, constitution of a common fund, settlement of the diamonds on the wife, the library and horses on the husband, and then the constitution of the entail. When all but signatures remained, Madame Evangelista asked what an entail would ultimately mean. Solonet explained generally, but Mathias bluntly defined the entail’s practical effect: if the wife died first leaving several children including a son, Monsieur de Manerville would owe those children only three hundred and sixty thousand francs, from which he would deduct his fourth in life-interest and his fourth in capital, reducing his debt to about one hundred and sixty thousand francs, excluding savings and profits from the common fund. If the husband died first leaving a male heir, Madame de Manerville’s right would be three hundred and sixty thousand francs plus her deeds of gift of property not in the entail, the diamonds settled on her, and profits and savings from the common fund.

The consequence struck Madame Evangelista: “My daughter is ruined,” she whispered. Both notaries overheard. Mathias, with gentle irony, replied that constituting an indestructible fortune for her family could hardly be ruin. The younger notary, seeing her face, whispered bluntly that they had tried to trick them out of three hundred thousand francs but actually the children had secured eight hundred thousand—arguing it was a loss of four hundred thousand from their interests for the children’s benefit. He advised her to break the marriage immediately or carry it through.

Silence followed. Natalie, unaware she had lost half her fortune, and Paul, ignorant the Manerville house had gained, conversed lightly. Solonet and Madame Evangelista exchanged glances—one masking indifference, the other curbing bitter feeling. Madame Evangelista’s remorse and indignation at having compromised her honor in Natalie’s interest turned to fury when she realized she would be the loser and that Mathias must have foreseen this and enlightened Paul. The thought that the old notary might soon be lauded for his sagacity and turn Paul against her was intolerable. She faced a dilemma: three months earlier she might have broken the match; now banns were published, friends had arrived for the fête, and postponing would bring public scandal. She felt trapped and knew she must yield.

Her anger surfaced: her chin trembled; Solonet noticed the muscles play and read the birth of a mortal hatred. She turned to Mathias: accusing him of incapacity or worse, of seeing the entail’s effect and not warning her sooner. The younger notary drew her aside, thinking of his fees but speaking coolly: she had overstepped legal craft for her own sake; if she did not want Comte Paul, she need not accept him—the contract was not yet signed; she could postpone it and blame some error in Paris. He suggested she might find another count if title were the chief aim. She protested that she could not chance her honor. He insisted that Mademoiselle Natalie’s happiness mattered most and argued that she, as wife, could dominate the home and lead Paul by the nose; if she played her cards right, the count’s fate was within her power. This mollified Madame Evangelista; she declared she would be grateful.

Mathias and Solonet then amended the contract to protect the wife’s interests further. The young notary proposed—and Mathias concurred—clauses to ensure that if Manerville died without children or left only daughters, the entailed property should pass under the general deed of gift between husband and wife or that the entail be declared void. Solonet added this clause in the margin. Madame Evangelista listened, dropped her eyes while Mathias read the added sentence aloud.

“We will now sign,” she said, her voice betraying violent emotion. She thought: she would not allow her daughter to be ruined; she intended that Natalie should have the name, title, and fortune. If Natalie later loved another, Paul would be driven out of France. Her mind resolved on measures to keep her daughter rich and free. Mathias took Paul aside and explained the saving stipulation—that he held the documents and that the contract contained a rescript for the diamonds; Paul must ask for them and could use them toward payments. Mathias spoke in practical terms: the first payment of two hundred thousand francs was due after formalities; use the diamonds; the house could be sold to pay the rest; plant vineyards to raise income. Paul pressed Mathias’s hand affectionately; Madame Evangelista noticed and suspected collusion. Rage and hatred overwhelmed her.

After verifying signatures and initials, Mathias looked at the mother-in-law. Seeing Paul say nothing of the diamonds, Mathias remarked there could be no doubt about their transfer now they were family. Solonet urged it was more regular for Madame Evangelista to make them over immediately since Monsieur de Manerville had become responsible for guardianship funds and one never knew who might live or die. Paul protested that it would be insulting; Madame Evangelista, angered at what she took as an insult masked in Mathias’s words, cried she would tear the contract up if he did not take the diamonds.

She left in fury, nearly mad with impotence. Natalie whispered to Paul to take them: her mother was angry and she would know why that night; they must pacify her. Calmed, Madame Evangelista kept the necklace and earrings she wore and brought the other jewels, which Magus had valued at 150,000 francs. Mathias and Solonet examined them and found they would meet the first payments. Solonet assured Paul he would lose nothing on the spot; Mathias said the jewels would meet the first payment on the estate purchase and the contract costs.

Hatred fed on small things; Madame Evangelista took Paul’s reluctance to accept the jewels as hypocrisy. She interpreted his embarrassment—he did not want them—as hypocrisy and flung a look that seemed to say “Take your property.” Paul, eager to end the dispute, told Natalie to lock the jewels away as her own gift. Natalie stowed them in a console drawer. At that moment carriages and voices swelled; salons filled and the fête began.

During the ball, whispers and judgments circulated among guests: speculation about the marriage’s financial and social results, about Madame Evangelista’s supposed penury, and about Mathias’s hand in safe-guarding the family. Some predicted future domestic friction; others speculated that Mademoiselle Evangelista, as a merchant’s daughter, could not hold her place socially. Card-players took tables, the young danced, supper was served, and the ball lasted till morning.

After Paul left, Madame Evangelista and Natalie retired. Though overtired, they exchanged a few words. Madame Evangelista confessed she had acted out of tender devotion and unscrupulous business ignorance in order to secure Natalie’s happiness; she had trampled her pride. Natalie, half asleep, spoke warmly of Paul’s innocence; she had no idea her mother’s fears or the implications of the contract. Madame Evangelista, in the dark, felt the first stirrings of avarice induced by passion and hate: a sudden resolve to secure her daughter’s entire fortune by reclaiming her husband’s property for Natalie. She told herself honor required it; devotion to Natalie made her shrewd and calculating. She planned to invest capital in the Funds and to manage matters carefully. Passion and hatred had transformed the once-prodigal woman into a miser and a plotter. She fell asleep muttering, “To-morrow!” Her mind, in sleep, marshalled ideas and arranged a plan to rule Paul’s life—a plan she began to put into action the next day.

Though the feast had driven away Paul’s anxious thoughts, they returned to torment him in bed.

“It seems to me,” he told himself, “that without that good Mathias my mother-in-law would have tricked me. Yet why should she deceive me? Are we not joining fortunes? Well, in two days Natalie will be my wife; our money relations are defined. Nevertheless, I’ll be upon my guard. Suppose Mathias was right? If so, I needn’t marry my mother-in-law.”

In this second battle over the contract Paul’s future had already shifted, though he did not know it. Of the two persons he was marrying, one—the cleverest—had turned into his mortal enemy, already planning to withdraw her interests from the common fund. Paul, incapable of discerning a Creole temperament, did not suspect her craftiness. The Creole mixes European intellect, tropical violence of passion, and an apathetic Eastern indifference; graceful but dangerous, like an unwatched child. She must have her way, broods over desires until she hatches them; impassioned, she plans. Naïve yet perfidious from her upbringing among Negroes, bolstered by Spanish obstinacy and glazed by French politeness—this described Madame Evangelista.

Dormant for sixteen years in married ease and social trifles, that nature now flamed up: she was about to lose her dearest object and needed a new outlet. Natalie would be apart from her in three days. Vanquished on other counts, Madame Evangelista had this one clear day—the last day a daughter spends with her mother. A few words could sway two lives; Natalie believed her blindly. No institution can prevent a crime that kills by words—social law’s weakness; the great world uses venomous ideas instead of knives.

The next morning, mid-day, Madame Evangelista half-sat on Natalie’s bed. In that waking hour they caressed and recalled a life without discord.

“Poor darling!” the mother wept. “How can I be calm when I think that after I’ve fulfilled your every wish you will belong to a man you must obey?”

“Oh, mamma, obeying!” Natalie made a graceful gesture of mock rebellion. “You are joking. Father gratified your caprices. I am loved too.”

“Yes, Paul loves you; but conjugal love evaporates if a married woman is not careful. The influence a wife should have depends on how she begins. You need the best advice.”

“But you will be with us.”

“Possibly. Last night I reflected on the dangers of our being together. If my presence injures you—if the little acts that should slowly establish your authority are attributed to me—your home will be a hell. At the first frown of your husband I, proud as I am, should instantly leave. If I were driven to leave, better not to enter at all. I should never forgive your husband for causing trouble between us. Whereas, when you are once mistress, when your husband is to you what your father was to me, that danger disappears. Though painful, you must become absolute sovereign of your home.”

“Then why did you say just now I must obey him?”

“My dear, to rule you must seem to do what he wishes. If you were not told this, impulsive opposition might destroy your future. Paul is a weak man; he might allow a friend or another woman to rule him. Prevent such disasters by making yourself his ruler from the start. Is it not better he be governed by you than by others?”

“Yes, certainly,” Natalie said. “I shall think only of his happiness.”

“And it is my privilege to think only of yours,” the mother answered. “I will not leave you without a compass among the reefs you must steer.”

“But, mamma, are we not strong enough together, you and I, to stay beside him? Paul loves you.”

“Oh! He fears me more than he loves me. Watch him when I tell him I shall not go to Paris; you will see, though he hides it, his inward joy.”

“Why should he feel so?”

“I will tell him myself and before you.”

“But suppose I marry on condition that you do not leave me?” urged Natalie.

“Our separation is necessary,” Madame Evangelista declared. “Several considerations have changed my future. I am poor now. You will lead a brilliant life in Paris, and I could not live with you suitably without spending my little remaining capital. If I go to Lanstrac, I can manage your property there and restore my fortune by economy.”

“You, mamma! You practise economy!” Natalie laughed. “Do you mean to leave me for such reasons? Paul is not selfish.”

“Ah!” Madame Evangelista’s tone suggested more; the girl’s heart throbbed. “Those discussions about the contract have made me distrustful. I have my doubts about him—But don’t be troubled, dear child,” she added, kissing her, “I will not leave you long. Whenever my return can take place without making difficulty, whenever Paul can rightly judge me, we will resume our happy life—”

“Oh, mother, how can you think of living without your Natalie?”

“Because, dear angel, I shall live for her. A mother’s heart will be satisfied thinking I contribute to your happiness.”

“But must I be alone with Paul, already? What will become of me? What must I do or not do?”

“Poor child! Do you think I would utterly abandon you for your first battle? We will write three times a week like lovers. Nothing will happen to you that I shall not know, and I can save you from misfortune. Besides, it would be ridiculous if I never came to see you; it would suggest dislike or disrespect to your husband. I shall always spend a month or two every year with you in Paris.”

“Alone, already alone, with him!” cried Natalie.

“But you wish to be his wife?”

“Yes, I wish it. Tell me how to behave—you who did what you pleased with my father. I’ll obey you blindly.”

Madame Evangelista kissed her forehead; she had willed and awaited this request.

“Child, my counsel must adapt to circumstances. All men are not alike; I can only give general advice.”

“Tell me, quickly, all you know.”

“First, the cause of failure for married women who wish to keep their husbands’ hearts—and to keep hearts and rule them is one and the same—is perpetual intercourse. This never existed in old times but has been introduced by the mania for family. Since the Revolution bourgeois manners invaded aristocratic homes. Rousseau’s heresy pretended all women had the same rights and faculties; as if a grandee’s wife had anything in common with women of the people! Well-bred women have stuck at home; life became involved; perfect harmony is almost impossible. Perpetual contact is as dangerous for parents and children as for husband and wife. Few souls survive omnipresence. Erect between yourself and Paul the barriers of society; go to balls and operas; go out mornings, dine out evenings, pay visits constantly, and give little time to your husband. By this you keep your value. When two beings live on sentiment alone, its resources are soon exhausted; indifference, satiety, contempt succeed. Be ever young and new to him. Even if he wearies you, you must never weary him. The faculty of being bored without showing it is a condition of all power. You cannot diversify happiness by property or family cares; if you don’t make him share your social interests and amuse him, you will fall into apathy—the SPLEEN of love. A man will always love the woman who amuses him and keeps him happy. To give happiness and to receive it are two feminine lines separated by a gulf.”

“Dear mother, I am listening, but I don’t understand one word,” Natalie said.

“If you love Paul so much that you do all he asks, if you make your happiness depend on him, all is over; you will never be mistress of your home, and rules will not help.”

“That is plainer; but how to apply it?” laughed Natalie. “I have the theory; practice will come.”

“My poor Ninie,” the mother said, sadly, “things will teach you—and remember, Natalie: we all have a destiny as women. A woman is born to be a woman of the world and a charming hostess, as a man is born to be a general or a poet. Your vocation is to please. Your education formed you for society. Nowadays women should be educated for the salon as once for the gynoecium. You were not born to be mother and steward. If you have children, I hope they will not spoil your figure at once; nothing is so bourgeois as having a child immediately. If they come two or three years later, governesses and tutors will bring them up. YOU are to be the lady who represents luxury and pleasure. But make your superiority visible only in things that flatter a man’s self-love; hide superiority acquired in greater matters.”

“But you frighten me, mamma,” cried Natalie. “How can I remember all these precepts? How shall I, such a child and heedless, reflect and calculate before acting?”

“But, my dear little girl, I tell you today what you must learn later by experience bought with faults and regrets.”

“How must I begin?” asked Natalie artlessly.

“Instinct will guide you,” her mother answered. “At this moment Paul desires you more than he loves you; love born of desire is hope; love after satisfaction is reality. There lies your power. What woman is not loved before marriage? Be so afterward and you will remain so always. Paul is weak, trained to habit. If he yields once he will yield always. A woman ardently desired can ask all things; do not waste the first hours of your sway on trifles. Use the empire his first emotions give you to accustom him to obedience. When you make him yield, choose some unreasonable point to test his concession. What victory is there in making him agree to a reasonable thing? Like the Castilian proverb says, take the bull by the horns; when a bull sees his defense useless he is beaten. When your husband does a foolish thing for you, you can govern him.”

“Why so?”

“Because marriage lasts a lifetime, and a husband is not a man like other men. Therefore never give yourself into his power in everything. Keep reserve in speech and action. You may be cold to him without danger; you can modify coldness at will. Nothing is easier to maintain than dignity. The words ‘It is not becoming in your wife to do thus’ are a talisman. A woman’s life lies in the words ‘I will not.’ They are the final argument; feminine power is in them, used on real occasions. They govern far beyond argument. I reigned over your father by his faith in me. If your husband believes in you, you can do all things with him. To inspire that belief you must make him think you understand him. A woman can always make a man think he is loved; to make him admit he is understood is harder. I tell you this now, for tomorrow life with its complications begins. Bear in mind that difficulty. The only means of harmonizing two wills is to arrange from the first that there shall be but one; and that will must be yours. Many say a wife creates unhappiness by wavering; but she becomes mistress only by controlling events instead of bearing them.”

Natalie kissed her mother’s hands with tears of gratitude. Quick to feel but not practically wise, she still returned to a personal plea:

“Dear mamma,” she said, “a few days ago you spoke of Paul’s advancement and said you alone could promote it; why then do you abandon us?”

“I did not then know the extent of my obligations nor the amount of my debts,” the mother replied, concealing her real motive. “Besides, a year hence I can take up that matter again. Come, let us dress; Paul will be here soon. Be as sweet and caressing as when we first discussed this fatal contract; to-day we must save the last fragments of our fortune, and I must win for you something to which I am superstitiously attached.”

“What is it?”

“The ‘Discreto.’”

Paul arrived about four. Though trying to look gracious, traces of the earlier counsels showed on his face.

“Mathias has told him!” Madame Evangelista thought, resolving to defeat the notary. “My dear son,” she said, “you left your diamonds in the console drawer; I frankly confess I would rather not see again the things that threatened to bring a cloud between us. Besides, as Monsieur Mathias said, they ought to be sold at once to meet the first payment on the estates you have purchased.”

“They are not mine,” Paul said. “I gave them to Natalie, and when you see them upon her you will forget the pain they caused you.”

Madame Evangelista took his hand and pressed it with a tear.

“Listen, my dear children,” she said, looking from Paul to Natalie; “I find myself obliged to sell my pearl necklace and ear-rings. Yes, Paul, it is necessary; I do not choose to put a penny of my fortune into an annuity; I know my duty to you. But I have a weakness; to sell the ‘Discreto’ seems a disaster. To sell a diamond that bears the name of Philip the Second and once adorned his royal hand—no, I cannot! Elie Magus values my necklace and ear-rings at a hundred and some odd thousand francs without the clasps. Will you exchange the jewels I made over to you for these? You will gain by the transaction. I am not selfish. Instead of those mere fancies, your wife will have fine diamonds she can really enjoy. Isn’t it better I sell ornaments that will go out of fashion, and that you keep these priceless stones in the family?”

“But, mother, consider yourself,” Paul said.

“I,” she answered, “I want such things no longer. Yes, Paul, I am going to be your bailiff at Lanstrac. It would be folly to go to Paris when I ought to be here to liquidate my property and settle my affairs. I shall grow miserly for my grandchildren.”

“Dear mother,” Paul, moved, said, “ought I to accept without paying you the difference?”

“Good heavens! are you not my dearest interests? Do you suppose I shall not find happiness thinking, as I sit by the chimney, ‘Natalie is dazzling tonight at the Duchesse de Berry’s ball’? When she wears my diamond she will have a little vanity that adds to a woman’s happiness. Nothing saddens a woman more than repressed vanity; I’ve never seen an ill-dressed woman amiable.”

“What was Mathias thinking?” Paul wondered. “Well then, mamma,” he said low, “I accept.”

“But I am confounded!” Natalie cried.

At that moment Solonet arrived: among Bordeaux speculators he’d found two contractors for the house grounds.

“They offer two hundred and fifty thousand francs,” he said; “but if you consent I can make them give three hundred thousand. There are three acres.”

“My husband paid two hundred thousand for the place, therefore I consent,” Natalie replied. “But reserve the furniture and mirrors.”

“Ah!” Solonet said. “You are beginning to understand business.”

“Alas! I must,” she sighed.

“I am told many are coming to your midnight service,” Solonet added, sensing his presence was inopportune.

Madame Evangelista accompanied him to the salon door and said low: “I now have personal property to the amount of two hundred and fifty thousand francs; if I get two hundred thousand for my share of the house it will make capital I need to invest. I count on you. I shall probably live at Lanstrac.”

The notary kissed her hand; her tone made him fancy the alliance might extend beyond business.

“You can count on me,” he replied. “I can find investments in merchandise with no risk and good profits.”

“Adieu until tomorrow,” she said. “You are to be our witness, with Monsieur le Marquis de Gyas.”

“My dear mother,” Paul asked when she returned, “why do you refuse to come to Paris? Natalie is provoked, as if I caused your decision.”

“I have thought it over, my children, and I am sure I should hamper you. You would feel obliged to make me a third in all you did, and young people have ideas I might unintentionally thwart. Go to Paris. I do not wish to exercise over the Comtesse de Manerville the gentle authority I held over Natalie. I desire to leave her wholly to you. Don’t you see, Paul, there are habits between us which must be broken? My influence ought to yield to yours. I want you to love me and to believe I have your interests at heart. Young husbands grow jealous of a wife’s love for her mother. Perhaps rightly. When you are thoroughly united, my living with you would not be feared. I have seen many homes destroyed by blind mothers who became intolerable. Let me make one more sacrifice for your happiness. I have given you my fortune, and now I desire to resign my last vanities as a woman. Your notary Mathias is getting old. He cannot look after your estates as I will. I will be your bailiff; I will create for myself those occupations which are the pleasures of old age. Later, if necessary, I will come to Paris and second you in your ambitions. Come, Paul, be frank; my proposal suits you, does it not?”

Paul would not admit it, but he was delighted to get his liberty. Mathias’s suspicions about Madame Evangelista were dissipated by this talk, which she prolonged.

“My mother was right,” Natalie thought, watching Paul. “He is glad to know I am separated from her—why?”

That “why” was the first note of rising distrust; did it prove the power of her maternal instructions?

Certain people stop at single proofs; Paul’s confiding nature relied on effects, not causes. His weakness sprang more from kindness and belief in good than from moral defect.

Natalie was sad and thoughtful, not knowing what to do without her mother. Paul, confident in love, smiled at her sadness, thinking Paris and marriage would soon dispel it. Madame Evangelista saw this with satisfaction: she had already taken two great steps—her daughter had the diamonds that cost Paul two hundred thousand francs; and she had gained her point of leaving the two to themselves with no other guide than their illogical love. Her revenge was preparing, unknown to Natalie, who would become its accomplice. Did Natalie love Paul? That question, still undecided, might modify her projects, for she loved her daughter too much to disregard her happiness. Paul’s future thus still depended on himself: if he could make his wife love him, he was saved.

The next day, at midnight, after a formal dinner for the four witnesses—dinner Madame Evangelista had given—the bridal pair, accompanied by friends, heard mass by torchlight before an inquisitive crowd. A marriage at night suggests an omen; light symbolizes life and pleasure, lacking at a midnight wedding. Darkness chills, its black arches sadden. Natalie, parted from her mother, wept—seized by the doubts that grip any heart entering a new career filled with pitfalls. She was cold and wanted a mantle. The air and manner of Madame Evangelista and the bridal pair excited comment among the elegant crowd.

“Solonet tells me the bride and bridegroom leave for Paris tomorrow, all alone.”

“Madame Evangelista was to live with them, I thought.”

“Count Paul has got rid of her already.”

“What a mistake!” said the Marquise de Gyas. “To shut the door on the mother is to open it to a lover. Doesn’t he know what a mother is?”

“He has been very hard on Madame Evangelista; the poor woman sold her house and diamonds, and is going to live at Lanstrac.”

“Natalie looks very sad.”

“Would you like to be made to take a journey the day after your marriage?”

“It is awkward.”

“I am glad I came,” said a lady. “I am now convinced of the necessity of a wedding’s pomps; a scene like this is bare and sad. If I may say, this marriage seems indecent.”

Madame Evangelista took Natalie in her carriage and accompanied her alone to Paul’s house.

“Well, mother, it is done!” said Natalie.

“Remember, my dear, my last advice, and you will be happy. Be his wife, not his mistress.”

When Natalie retired, the mother flung herself with tears into the arms of her son-in-law—an apparently provincial display she allowed herself for effect. Amid tears and speeches, she obtained from Paul concessions all husbands make.

The next day she put the married pair into their carriage, accompanied them to the ferry crossing the Gironde toward Paris, and with a look and a word enabled her daughter to see that if Paul had won the contract-game, her revenge was beginning. Natalie was already reducing her husband to perfect obedience.

Five years later, on an autumn afternoon, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and with a bowed, mysterious air, arrived at the house of his old friend Maître Mathias in Bordeaux.

Mathias, now retired and too old to practice, was out on urgent business; his housekeeper Josette showed the count to Madame Mathias’s room. Fatigued by his journey, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned he climbed to the room and watched him sleep, as a mother watches a child; Josette followed, hands on hips.

“It is a year today, Josette, since I heard my dear wife’s last sigh; I little thought then I should see the count half dead,” murmured Mathias.

“Poor man! he moans in his sleep,” said Josette.

“Sac à papier!” the notary muttered. “At any rate I have saved the title to Lanstrac for him, and Ausac, Saint-Froult, and his house, though the usufruct is gone.” He counted on his fingers. “Five years! since his aunt, Madame de Maulincour, asked for the hand of that little crocodile of a woman who has ruined him—as I expected.”

Leaning on his cane, the gouty old man paced the garden until Paul awoke. At supper Mathias, astonished at Paul’s calm but noting shocks of mind that had aged him, forced him to sit.

“Dear Mathias, you too have troubles,” Paul said.

“Mine are natural, Monsieur le comte; but yours—”

“We will speak of that over supper.”

“If I had not a son in the magistracy and a married daughter,” Mathias began, “I would have done more than offer hospitality. Why come to Bordeaux when placards announce seizure of your farms at Grassol and Guadet, the vineyard of Belle-Rose and the family mansion? To see my name on those yellow posters—ah! a notary of forty-one years’ standing must feel ashamed.” He opened the paper: “‘At the request of Dame Natalie Evangelista, wife of Paul-François-Joseph, Comte de Manerville, separated from him as to worldly goods and chattels by the Lower court of the department of the Seine—’”

“Yes, and now separated in body,” Paul answered.

“Ah!” cried Mathias.

“Oh! against my wife’s will,” Paul hastened. “I had to deceive her; she didn’t know I was leaving.”

“You have left her?”

“My passage is taken; I sail for Calcutta on the Belle-Amélie.”

“Two days hence! Then we shall never meet again,” Mathias lamented.

“You are only seventy-three and have the gout, Mathias. When I return I shall be forty and rebuild my fortune. I intend to make a noble fortune in seven years. All is possible.”

“You? to undertake commerce!” Mathias protested.

“I am no longer ‘Monsieur le comte,’ dear Mathias. I sail under the name Camille. Commerce is my final chance. I start with a sum to redeem my future on a large scale.”

“Where is that money?”

“A friend will send it.”

The notary dropped his fork; his look expressed grief at Paul’s illusion. “I have been fifty years in the notariat,” he said, “and I never knew a ruined man whose friend would lend him money.”

“You don’t know de Marsay. He has sold some investments already; to-morrow you will receive from him a bill of exchange for one hundred and fifty thousand francs.”

Mathias brightened. “If that be so, cannot your friend settle your difficulties here? You could live at Lanstrac five or six years on your wife’s income and recover.”

“No assignment or economy could pay off fifteen hundred thousand francs of debt, in which my wife is involved to the amount of five hundred and fifty thousand.”

“You cannot mean you have incurred a million and a half of debt in four years?”

“Nothing is more certain, Mathias. I gave those diamonds to my wife; I spent the 150,000 from selling Madame Evangelista’s house on the Paris house; I used other money for payments required by the marriage contract. I sold Natalie’s forty thousand a year in the Funds at eighty-seven, so I became two hundred thousand francs in debt within a month after marriage. That left us sixty-seven thousand francs a year; but we spent three times that annually. Add usurers’ interest and other payments and you have a million.”

“Br-r-r!” said Mathias. “Go on. What next?”

“I wanted to complete my wife’s set of jewels. I paid a hundred thousand for a coronet of diamond wheat-ears. There’s eleven hundred thousand. And now I find I owe my wife’s fortune, which amounts to three hundred and sixty-six thousand francs of her dot.”

“If Madame la comtesse had surrendered her diamonds and you had pledged your income you could have pacified creditors and paid them,” Mathias insisted.

“When a man is down, Mathias, when his property is mortgaged, his wife’s claims take precedence, and he has notes of a hundred thousand francs to pay, what you propose is not possible.”

“This is dreadful! Would you sell Belle-Rose with the 1825 vintage still in the cellars?”

“I cannot help myself.”

“Belle-Rose is worth six hundred thousand francs.”

“Natalie will buy it in; I advised her to do so.”

“I might push the price to seven hundred thousand, and the farms are worth a hundred thousand each.”

“If the house in Bordeaux could be sold for two hundred thousand—”

“Solonet will give more; he wants it. He has made a handsome property gambling on the Funds; he has sold his practice for three hundred thousand and marries a mulatto woman. People say he speculates for your mother-in-law with her funds.”

“She has greatly improved Lanstrac and repaid me for the use of it.”

“I shouldn’t have thought her capable of that.”

“She is so kind and devoted,” Paul said; “she has always paid Natalie’s debts during the three months she spent with us in Paris.”

“She could afford to do so; she lives on Lanstrac. They say she bought Grainrouge between Lanstrac and Grassol, paying one hundred thousand francs down.”

“She is as handsome as ever,” Paul added; “country life preserves her. I don’t mean to go to Lanstrac to bid her good-bye; her heart would bleed for me too much.”

“You would go in vain; she is now in Paris.”

“No doubt she left as I did.”

“No complaint,” Paul resumed; “I am truly loved—by two women who outdo each other in devotion; they are jealous of each other. This great affection has been my ruin. How could I fail to satisfy my wife’s every caprice? Impossible to restrain myself. Neither could I accept sacrifice on her part. We might live at Lanstrac and part with her diamonds, but I would sooner go to India and work for a fortune than tear Natalie from the life she enjoys. So I proposed a separation as to property. Women are angels who should not be mixed in sordid interests.”

Mathias listened in doubt and amazement.

“You have no children, I think.”

“Fortunately, none,” Paul replied.

“That is not my idea of marriage,” the notary said. “A wife should share good and evil. Your marriage contract made it incumbent upon you to have a son to consolidate the entail. Why not? Madame Evangelista was strong and healthy. In those noble families the wife thought it her duty to bear children. A wife is not an instrument of pleasure but the honor and virtue of her household.”

“You don’t know women, my good Mathias,” Paul returned. “To be happy we must love them as they want to be loved. Is there not something brutal in depriving a wife of her charms at once, spoiling her beauty before she has enjoyed it?”

“If you had children your wife would not have dissipated your fortune; she would have stayed at home and cared for them.”

“If you were right,” Paul said, “I should be more unhappy than I am. Do not aggravate me by preaching after my fall. Let me go without the pang of looking back on my mistakes.”

The next day Mathias received a bill of exchange for one hundred and fifty thousand francs from de Marsay.

“You see,” Paul said, “he does not write to me. He begins by obliging me. Henri’s nature is the most imperfectly perfect, the most illegally beautiful that I know. If you knew with what superiority that man can rise above sentiments and self-interest, you would be astonished.”

Mathias tried to deter Paul from leaving but found his decision fixed, and, after hearing many cogent reasons, ceased his efforts.

By a fortunate circumstance the wind was fair and the Belle-Amélie sailed the next day. The quay was lined with relations, friends, and idle persons. Many who had known Manerville came to see the spectacle his ruin made of him. Curiosity replaced the old fashion; his disaster made him as celebrated as his former wealth. Mathias accompanied Paul to the quay and suffered as he heard the remarks:—

“Who could recognize that dandy, the Pink of Fashion, now ruined, going to India to look for luck?”

“How did he ruin himself? Paris, women, play, the Bourse—”

“Manerville was always a poor creature; soft as papier-mâché.”

Paul wrung Mathias’s hand and went aboard. Mathias stood on the pier, watching his client lean against the shrouds, defying the crowd with a contemptuous glance. As the sailors weighed anchor, Mathias made signals with his handkerchief; Josette hurried to him, excited. Paul asked the captain to wait and sent a boat. Too feeble to go himself, Mathias put two letters into the sailor’s hands.

“My friend,” he said, “this packet” (showing one letter) “is important; it has just arrived by courier from Paris in thirty-five hours. Tell Monsieur le comte; it may change his plans.”

“Would he come ashore?”

“Possibly, my friend,” the notary answered imprudently.

The Breton sailor, valuing his time, preferred not to return and gave Paul the letters without stating the message. Recognizing the handwriting of his wife and de Marsay, Paul supposed their content; anxious not to be influenced, he put the letters unread into his pocket.

Absorbed in sad thoughts, he waved farewell; the spires of Bordeaux shrinking in the distance. Night brought doubts. He searched the future and found uncertainty and danger, and asked himself whether courage would hold. A vague dread seized him when he thought of Natalie alone; he repented. Sea-sickness then dissolved his will: he was carried to his cabin and stayed three days, vomiting and sleeping under the sailors’ grog. On the first morning he felt better, went on deck, and found the letters in his pockets. He opened his wife’s letter first.

To understand the Comtesse de Manerville’s letter it is necessary to recall the one Paul had written the day he left Paris.

From Paul de Manerville to his wife:

My beloved,—When you read this I shall be far away, perhaps already on the vessel for India, where I go to repair my shattered fortune.

I lacked courage to tell you of my departure; I deceived you because it was best. You would have wished to sacrifice your fortune; I could not suffer that. When I return with millions I shall lay them at your feet, as your father did for your mother. I love you madly, Natalie; my love is the only accomplice in my disaster. As money diminished, my delight grew; each fragment of my fortune turned into some pleasure for you and gave me untold happiness. I have hidden my sacrifices; now I reveal them, for when you hold this paper I shall be far away. I leave you free in all things; amuse yourself, go to theatres, balls, enjoy society. I beg you to trust de Marsay; I place liquidation of my affairs in his hands. If he advances sums ask you to repay them. I do not leave you to him, but to yourself.

I cannot write more; I count your breaths. Ah! I should infuse my blood into your veins! Keep me as a religion in your heart. I go like a man vowed to a great enterprise; the fire of your eyes will light my way. Adieu, my precious love. Your poor Pink of Fashion is blown away, but the wings of good luck shall waft him back. I shall never leave you.

From the Comtesse de Manerville to her husband:

Dear, beloved one,—Your letter plunged me into affliction. Had you the right to take this course without consulting me? Do you not belong to me? If you must go, why should I not follow? You make me feel deprived of rights. I was right when I refused that separation as to property which my mother and you pressed. What did I say then? I warned it would cast a reflection on you and ruin your credit. I was forced to yield.

My dear Paul, never have you been so noble in my eyes as now. To despair of nothing and start courageously to seek fortune—only your strength could do it. I sit at your feet. A man who avows his weakness with such good faith, who rebuilds his fortune for the same motive that wrecked it, is sublime. Fear nothing; do not doubt your Natalie. I shall go to live at Lanstrac with my mother; I will economize and pay your debts. From this day I am another woman; I bid farewell to society and pleasures you cannot share. Besides, Paul, you will have a noble reason to make your fortune—perhaps I am with child; I fear not to give you false hopes, but I am certain to-day that we shall have a child—yes, a son must it not be?

This morning my mother brought thirty thousand francs by post to help you. I felt joy. Then Augustine brought me your letter—when we had slept together. I read it weeping. The richest fortunes of the heart and the momentary ruin of all interests! To lose you at a moment when I admired your greatness. I wished to follow you, but my mother said it was madness in my state; I was taken ill and write now.

My mother is doing all she can to stop calumnies. The Vandenesses defend you; but de Marsay treats attacks satirically. I will obey you, make him my friend. My diamonds shall be pledged; mamma and I will use our utmost resources. She blames you for not telling her of your embarrassments; she would not have bought Grainrouge had she known. She fears India for your health. She entreats you to be sober and not be trapped by women—that made me laugh. I am as sure of you as of myself. You will return rich and faithful. Who will take care of my delicate flower? I am here and may suffer while you suffer far away alone. I shall live at Lanstrac with my mother and my child and await you, sending daily prayers. Remember our love is a talisman. Notwithstanding my grief, we have our son to brighten the future.

Adieu, my beloved; our love and prayers follow you. The tears on this paper tell what I cannot write. I kiss you.

Your Natalie.

Paul read and re-read his wife’s ardent letter. It threw him into reverie, alternately assailed by memories and fresh assurances of love. Exclusive tenderness carries weakness—jealousy and uneasiness rising with happiness; yet his heart kept returning to faith: the lover’s conviction that silenced doubt.

To understand what followed we must give the letter Paul had written to de Marsay as he left, and de Marsay’s reply received through Mathias.

From Paul to Henri de Marsay:

Henri,—I am ruined. When you read this I shall be on the point of sailing from Bordeaux to Calcutta on the Belle-Amélie.

You will find with your notary a deed which needs only your signature to lease my house to you for six years at a nominal rent. Send a duplicate to my wife. I lease the income of my share of the entailed property for four years, amounting to 150,000 francs, which I beg you to lend me and send by bill of exchange to Maître Mathias. My wife will endorse the paper. If the revenues do not pay the loan quickly enough, we will settle on my return. The sum is necessary for me to seek fortune in India; I expect to receive it in Bordeaux the night before I sail.

I have hidden my ruin. Before seizure news reached Paris I tried to save myself with 100,000 francs from notes and a last gamble at play. I lost.

How have I ruined myself? By my own will. From the first month of marriage I saw I could not keep up the style but shut my eyes; I could not say, “We must leave Paris and live at Lanstrac.” I ruined myself for her like a man for a mistress. I am neither fool nor weak—going to India to reconstruct my fortune, instead of committing suicide, is not weakness.

I shall return rich or never return at all. I confide to you my wife; being childless, a lover might be dangerous. I love her madly; I would forgive an infidelity rather than chain her to misery. But you know Natalie; you will be her guardian, chaperon, adviser, banker if need be. I count on you.

Send letters by cover to the Governor at Calcutta. I hope to find you the same de Marsay on my return.

From Henri de Marsay to Paul de Manerville:

So, Monsieur le comte, you have made a wreck! Monsieur l’ambassadeur has gone to the bottom! Why have you kept away from me? If you had told me I would have made your position plain. Your wife refused to endorse. May that unseal your eyes! Your notes were protested at Lecuyer’s instigation, former head-clerk to Solonet. That usurer is proxy for your mother-in-law, who actually holds your notes for 100,000 francs, of which she gave you only 70,000. Compared with Madame Evangelista, papa Gobseck is mild. Your vineyard Belle-Rose will fall into your wife’s hands, paid for by her mother; the farms Guadet and Grassol and mortgages on your Bordeaux house already belong to her via straw men arranged by Solonet.

These two women have calculated, evidently, that you are now at sea; I send this by courier so you may have time to follow advice.

I made Lecuyer talk and uncovered a domestic conspiracy hatched against you. This evening I shall pay my compliments to your mother-in-law and wife at the Spanish embassy; I shall pay court to Madame Evangelista and say sly things about you—nothing overt. How did you make her such an enemy? If you had loved her before marrying her daughter you might now be peer of France, Duc de Manerville.

You should have come to me at marriage; I’d have protected you. Your wife maintained the social life with ease; she is made for it; she is a fashionable woman—dress, balls, theatre—and in love with a public life. To starve that out of such a nature a man of iron was needed. Vandenesse stirred a chord in your wife you never touched. Where you lavished gifts, Felix awakened love. Her mother is naturally on the side of the lover. Your dissipations and easy nature have opened the way for your wife and mother-in-law to get rid of you by this ruin. The mission you gave me is null and void—“consummatum est.”

Forgive my tone; I treat you like a man of the world. This blow should make you turn and possess marriage; you are freed of one evil. You now have the brand on your shoulder of a galley-slave; turn it to systematic opposition to society. I love you, Paul—read what I have yet to say.

I am turning to politics; within five years I aim for a ministry or ambassadorship. Several of us—Ronquerolles, Montriveau, the Grandlieus, La Roche-Hugon, Serisy, Feraud, Granville—have allied against the “parti-prêtre.” We intend to form an oligarchy to direct the nation.

A fortunate accident set the question of my embarking on this career; my mother, after thirty-five years, decided to find me a wife—an English heiress, Miss Dinah Stevens, with 240,000 francs a year. She is puritanic and will manage house, estates, servants, and will adore her husband. We will make settlements; she devotes part of her fortune to an entail bearing an income of 240,000 and purchases a house entailed. The settlement credited to me is a million francs. I shall sell out of the funds and invest in land. Thus in two years I may have six hundred thousand francs a year in real estate. If I reach the top I will not refuse you a helping hand.

Here is my proposal. Let the Belle-Amélie sail without you; return at once to Paris; I’ll arrange a duel with Vandenesse in which you will have the first shot, and you can wing him like a pigeon. In France the husband who shoots his rival becomes respectable. Kill Vandenesse, your wife and mother-in-law tremble, society trembles, and you recover your position. I hold 100,000 francs for you to pay principal debts; you can sell what property you have left with power of redemption, for you will soon obtain an office enabling you to pay creditors. Once enlightened as to your wife’s character you can rule her. I will make your mother-in-law supple. Renounce this expatriation—it is vain. Stay, return to Paris, and we will play the game again: you and I gamblers on the green baize of politics, with loans in order. Come; you will find in me a friend.

Paul ended de Marsay’s letter as if struck by a pickaxe. It demolished his hopes, illusions, and love. The ship had passed beyond the Azores. In the midst of devastation a cold impotent anger seized him.

“What had I done to them?” he said.

That is the question of fools and feeble beings who see nothing and foresee nothing. Then Paul cried aloud, “Henri! Henri!” Many men would have been driven mad; Paul went to bed and slept that heavy sleep that follows immense disasters—the sleep that seized Napoleon after Waterloo.
