Monsieur de Manerville was a worthy Norman gentleman, known to the Maréchal de Richelieu, who married him to a rich Bordeaux heiress when the old duke governed Guienne. The Norman sold his Bessin estate, became a Gascon, and settled at the château de Lanstrac, his wife’s property. Toward the close of Louis XV’s reign he bought the post of major of the Gate Guards and, having escaped the Revolution, lived on until 1813.

In late 1790 he went to Martinique on business connected with his wife’s interests, leaving the management of his Gascogne property to Mathias, a notary’s clerk who, if not a convert, at least adopted the new ways. On his return the Comte de Manerville found his estates intact and well tended—the prudent Mathias having grafted progress onto tradition.

Madame de Manerville died in 1810. The old man, hardened by youthful dissipations, had grown miserly and sordid and allowed almost nothing to his only son. Paul de Manerville returned from the college of Vendôme in 1810 to live under this strict paternal discipline for three years. The tyranny of the old man of seventy shaped a young heart yet unformed. Paul, naturally brave in the Gascon fashion, dared not oppose his father and so lost the faculty of moral resistance. His thwarted feelings were driven inward; later, when they conflicted with the world’s maxims, he could think rightly but act wrongly. He might fight for honor yet trembled at dismissing a servant—timidity ill suited to sustained will. Capable of great deeds to escape persecution, he would not have prevented it by steady opposition. Timid in thought, bold in action, he preserved a simplicity that made him a dupe and a voluntary victim of things other souls would revolt against. Imprisoned by his father’s mansion and without money to mix with peers, he envied their pleasures without sharing them.

Every evening his father took him in a shabby carriage to royalist houses, meeting a society of the relics of parliamentary and military nobility. These men, crushed by the vast fortunes of maritime cities, coalesced into a landed aristocracy that disdained the displays of commerce. Too young to understand such social distinctions, Paul was bored by these ancients, unaware that such early connections could later secure the preeminence Frenchmen desire.

Some compensation came from the manual exercises his father insisted upon. The old gentleman believed fencing, arms, riding, tennis, and good manners—old noble accomplishments—made a modern young man a gentleman. Thus Paul learned fencing, rode, practiced at a pistol-gallery, and spent his remaining leisure reading novels; his father forbade the more abstruse studies now seen as necessary.

So monotonous a life might have killed him had not his father’s death liberated him when it became intolerable. Paul then found himself possessor of considerable capital accumulated by paternal avarice and with estates well kept. But he hated Bordeaux; Lanstrac, his father’s favorite hunting seat, did not attract him.

Once the estate was settled, the young heir, eager for enjoyment, bought consols with his capital, left estate management to Mathias, and spent six years away: attached to the French embassy at Naples, secretary at Madrid, then in London—making a tour of Europe. After seeing the world, losing illusions, and dissipating the capital his father had amassed, Paul had to draw upon the territorial revenues Mathias had been saving. Seized by a so-called virtuous impulse, he resolved to leave Paris, return to Bordeaux, regulate his affairs, live as a country gentleman at Lanstrac, improve his property, marry, and ultimately become a deputy.

Paul was a count; nobility again had matrimonial value. In exchange for seven hundred thousand francs squandered in six years he had won experience—the social apprenticeship of love affairs, duels, disillusions, toils, and pleasures—and had become what is called elegant. Despite extravagance he was not merely a man of fashion: his servants were well dressed, his equipages noted, his suppers in vogue; his bachelor establishment compared with the finest Paris houses. He neither seduced nor defamed any woman; he gambled and lent recklessly to friends who deserted him, but he did not borrow nor keep love-letters. His character’s secret lay in his father’s tyranny, which had left him a kind of social mongrel.

One morning he told his friend Henri de Marsay, later celebrated, that he had found life’s meaning: at twenty-seven he would live the life of a country gentleman at Lanstrac, move his belongings to Bordeaux, and spend three months a year in Paris in a house he would keep. He would marry.

De Marsay laughed and warned him sharply. He urged Paul to remain free: marriage, he argued, would make him ridiculous and curtail all future possibilities. If Paul wanted a reputation, he already had it; a bachelor with even little income enjoys chances denied to the married man. Marriage, for de Marsay, was a social immolation. It brought children, cares, pretensions to morality, and a tedious domestic routine that blunted a man’s wit and freedom. He painted a satirical prospect: Paul, led by his wife, constrained, unhorsed, transformed into a respectable but ridiculous paterfamilias, calculating dowries, moralizing, and losing the social mobility afforded to unmarried men.

Yet de Marsay, in a concession, described a way to marry without disgrace: “en grand seigneur.” Entail property, have two legitimate children, give the wife a distinct house and household, meet her only in society, never return from a journey without sending a courier to announce it. With two hundred thousand francs a year and a well-ordered ceremonial life, a man could gain social advantages while retaining many bachelor privileges—an aristocratic compromise that might lead to ministry or ambassadorship.

Paul, however, was not de Marsay. He replied that he was tired of living for others, of maintaining appearances to avoid gossip; he did not admire de Marsay’s cold calculation. He belonged to the masses and needed the emotions of ordinary life. He liked the peaceful chances and changes of having a woman always at his side. He wished to feel happier when his valet said, “Madame is awaiting monsieur for breakfast,” and to return at night to find a heart in which to confide his secrets. He declared himself ready to become a worthy husband and father, to submit to society’s conditions and derive the family’s joys.

De Marsay mocked the notion as indecorous and warned that Paul lacked the moral strength to govern a family. He argued that bachelor habits deceive: in youth a man triumphs over women ready to be triumphed over; obstacles in courtship create mutual sensations that vanish in marriage, where the wife submits to love differently and may even repulse pleasure. A lover can sometimes undo a woman’s initial coldness, but that is the husband’s Waterloo—victories in love as a husband often cannot erase the first defeat. The Code, de Marsay said, makes woman a ward; legally treated like a minor, her governance requires fear. He asked whether Paul had the toughness to play the tyrant, to use coercion, and suggested that such mastery, not tenderness, is often what holds a household together.

He summarized his anthropology of marriage with cynical experience: marriage destroys the liberties that make life full of chances; husbands become narrow and ridiculous, their choices limited by conjugal duties. He advised, if Paul insisted on marrying, to do so grandly or to postpone until middle age: “Remain a bachelor thirteen years; amuse yourself; at forty, on your first gout attack, marry a widow of thirty-six; then you may be happy. If you take a young girl now, you’ll die a madman.” Waiting to marry a mature woman with life experience, he thought, would reduce the risks of conflict and disappointment.

Paul defended his wish as sincere and simple: he wanted the certainty of a close union, a companion to trust, not the ostentation or the abstinence of de Marsay’s ideal. He wanted a home and to be a husband who could feel the daily intimacies and confidences of married life. De Marsay retorted that Paul was not yet moral enough to marry and that his plan risked making him a dupe for life. Yet even as he ridiculed the project, he promised to treat Paul politely and to receive the future Comtesse de Manerville as one would a guest of honor, with the same formal civility as Maison-Rouge treated the English at Fontenoy.

Thus the chapter ends on the wry contrast between Paul’s yearning for domestic happiness and his friend’s worldly cynicism: Paul’s desire for a faithful, intimate conjugal life collides with de Marsay’s hard-won lessons about marriage as social risk, a battlefield of compromises, illusions, and reduced freedoms. The reader is left to see Paul poised between sentimental aspiration and the cynical prudence of the world he inhabits.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Though the foregoing conversation affected the Comte de Manerville, he carried out his intentions and returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821.

Restoring and furnishing his family mansion sustained the elegant reputation that preceded him. Reintroduced to Bordeaux’s royalist society, where his opinion, name, and fortune held weight, he soon attained fashionable pre‑eminence. His knowledge of life, manners, and Parisian accomplishments charmed the Faubourg Saint‑Germain of Bordeaux. An old marquise revived a courtly phrase, calling him “the pink of fashion.” The liberals used it satirically; the royalists sincerely.

Paul de Manerville fulfilled his title. When observed he, like many mediocre actors, seemed to improve: at ease he revealed the amiable qualities that accompanied his defects. His wit was mild, his manners not supercilious; with women he showed a temperate respect that pleased them. His foppery masked a carefulness of appearance that made him agreeable. He respected rank, granted youth liberties, and though skilled with sword and pistol, had a feminine gentleness appreciated by others. Medium height and well‑rounded, he aimed to be a Bordelais Brummell: fair complexion, handsome hands and feet, blue eyes with long lashes, black hair, graceful motions, and a resonant chest voice harmonized with his sobriquet. Paul was a delicate flower needing careful culture—more made to receive than to give happiness, with something of the woman in his nature—one to whom conjugal love should come as a providence.

Such a character fosters private difficulty but public attraction. In the narrow provincial circle his half‑tinted mind was better appreciated than it would be in Paris.

Restoring his town house and the château de Lanstrac—into which he introduced English country‑house comfort—consumed the capital the notary had saved over six years. Reduced now to his strict income of about forty thousand francs a year, he thought himself prudent in regulating his household accordingly.

After flaunting his equipages, entertaining the town’s distinguished young men, and giving hunting parties at Lanstrac, Paul perceived that provincial life would not do without marriage. Not merely for establishing heirs or solidifying a name, but because his Parisian tastes craved variety. From his first return he had been secretly in love with Bordeaux’s queen, the great beauty Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the century’s beginning a rich Spaniard named Evangelista settled in Bordeaux. His letters of recommendation and large fortune granted him access to noble salons. His wife, a Creole from the Casa‑Reale, lived as a great lady, ignorant of money’s value and indulging whims supplied by an adoring husband. From 1800 to 1812 they were objects of interest in Bordeaux.

The Spaniard died in 1813, leaving Madame Evangelista a thirty‑two‑year‑old widow with an immense fortune and a prettiest little girl of eleven who became a very accomplished young woman. Clever as the mother was, the Restoration altered her position: royalist ranks cleared and old families drifted from Bordeaux. Lacking her husband’s direction, Madame Evangelista resolved to maintain her mode of living. By squandering large sums on brilliant fêtes and a quasi‑regal life she preserved public belief in her wealth; the steady diminution of her capital remained unknown.

Natalie, now nineteen, seemed the richest match in Bordeaux, yet no proposals had reached her mother. Accustomed to being gratified, she wore cashmeres and jewels and lived in a luxury that alarmed calculating suitors. The fatal remark “None but a prince can afford to marry Mademoiselle Evangelista” circulated in salons. Jealous mothers spread treacherous warnings—her mother gave her a thousand francs a month for her toilet, she had horses and a maid, wore Mechlin laces—so praise disguised as scandal deterred suitors.

Pampered, Natalie knew nothing of the value of money or household maintenance. She bounded through life unbridled and unshod, like a courser on his steppe.

Six months after Paul’s arrival the Pink of Fashion and the Queen of Balls met in Bordeaux’s highest society. They looked at one another with apparent coldness, each thinking the other charming. Madame Evangelista read Paul’s looks and muttered, “He will be my son‑in‑law.” Paul thought, “She will be my wife.”

The Evangelistas’ reputed wealth lingered in Paul’s mind from childhood, so their pecuniary condition was known to him. When flattering remarks ended with cruelly practical warnings about the family’s extravagance, Paul replied with disdain that shut down provincial pettiness. He brought to Bordeaux a certain Britannic reserve and Byronic pessimism, together with English plate, boot‑polish, ponies, yellow gloves, cigars, and a habit of galloping. Thus he escaped the discouragements that deterred other suitors. Madame Evangelista asked him to formal dinners; he accepted, pleased to breathe the grandeur and luxury he loved.

When he rode by in his tilbury or on his English horse, young men said, “There’s a lucky man. He is rich and handsome, and is to marry, so they say, Mademoiselle Evangelista.” Society, which never does good without doing evil, credited Natalie with a dowry of a million and bestowed her, almost before consent, upon Paul. Their fortunes seemed to match: Paul’s habits of luxury and his newly arranged house made him seem uniquely fitted to cope with a daughter of a Creole great lady. The marriage appeared made. Royalist friends flattered him: “Everyone gives you Mademoiselle Evangelista. You could not find, even in Paris, a more delightful girl. Her mother will be a great help in public life. You must soon bring the matter to a close.”

“That is all very well,” replied Paul, anxious to keep freedom, “but I must be sure the conclusion will be a happy one.”

He now visited the Evangelistas often, partly to fill idle hours harder for him than for most men. Madame Evangelista, at forty, had the beauty of a glorious summer sunset and a spotless reputation much discussed. Her temperament combined Spanish force and Creole seductiveness. She had black eyes and hair, a swaying form, and a muslin‑over‑crimson complexion. Full and rounded, she possessed nonchalance of strength with vivacity and sometimes the carriage of a queen. Men were won by her conversation; she had the genius for schemes necessity bestows, advancing from concession to concession and retreating quickly when concessions were demanded in return. Though ignorant of many facts, she had known many courts and illustrious families, granting her a superficial but impressive polish. She received society with natural grace and dignity, and her reputed virtue lent authority to her actions.

Mother and daughter had a true friendship beyond maternal ties; perpetual contact produced no jar. Madame Evangelista’s perpetual widowhood was not explained solely by maternal devotion: she had once loved a man who regained titles under the Restoration and courted her in 1814, only to sever the connection by 1816. Believing in the power of resentment—“Odiate e aspettate”—she nursed this hate. Events seemed to prove her jettatura when this man began to ruin himself; she passed him on foot in the Champs‑Élysées in her brilliant equipage and crushed him with a triumphant look. This misadventure and later pride kept her from remarrying. She had reached the age when women often sacrifice themselves to their children and place their interests in another household—the last refuge of human affections.

Madame Evangelista divined Paul’s nature and concealed her own. He was precisely the man she wanted for a son‑in‑law, an instrument for future power. Through his mother he belonged to the Maulincour family; his connection to the Baronne de Maulincour and her grandson Auguste’s army post made Paul an excellent introducer for the Evangelistas into Parisian society. The widow, who disliked residing in Bordeaux, longed to shine in Restoration Paris, where political fortune lay. She looked to Paul as a means: through him she could enjoy power indirectly. Many men serve as screens for secret feminine ambitions; Madame Evangelista had more than one interest in holding her daughter’s husband.

Paul was captivated by Madame Evangelista, who charmed him by seeming to seek no influence while subtly magnifying herself and her daughter in his eyes. He valued himself more for their appreciation; he fancied himself clever when his remarks pleased Natalie and flattered when the mother’s praise seemed involuntary. So kind and welcoming were they that he spent most of his time at the Hôtel Evangelista.

A year after his return, though Paul had not declared himself, his attentions made the world consider him a suitor. Neither mother nor daughter seemed intent on marriage. Natalie preserved toward Paul the reserve of a great lady who can charm without allowing intimacy—a reserve that pleased Paul, a timid man who recoiled from sudden proposals and surrendered to mild advances. He therefore committed himself inwardly all the more. The mother’s remark that superior women sometimes put ambition above earlier emotions seduced him: “That woman is fitted,” he thought, “to advance me in diplomacy before I am even made a deputy.”

A man who does not examine things and ideas in different lights is weak and in danger of failure. At this moment Paul was an optimist; he saw everything to advantage and did not suspect that an ambitious mother‑in‑law might become a tyrant. He pictured himself married each evening and embraced the idea cheerfully—his bachelor life, offering nothing new, now appeared tedious. “Marriage is disagreeable for people without means, but half its troubles disappear before wealth,” he said. Every day some favorable consideration swelled his sense of advantage.

“No matter what position I attain, Natalie will always be on the level of her part,” he told himself. “How many Empire men I’ve seen ruined by their wives! It is a great condition of happiness not to feel one’s pride or vanity wounded by the companion we have chosen. A man can never be really unhappy with a well‑bred wife; she will never make him ridiculous.” He compared Natalie in his mind to Faubourg Saint‑Germain women and, freed from Parisian contrasts, allowed fancy to follow desire. Paris would have shown other types to balance his view; in Bordeaux Natalie had no rivals and appeared when Paul was under an age‑old tyrant: the domination of an idea.

Propinquity, self‑love, and a real passion whose only outlet was marriage led Paul into an irrational love that he prudently kept to himself. He tried to study Natalie, mindful of de Marsay’s warnings. But luxury breeds indifference; Paul never imagined the gulf of ruin beneath the ladies’ habits. Moreover, there are no rules to predict conjugal harmony; young people display only favorable weather and forget life’s common gray days. Each shows a favorable mask; later they blame marriage for evils inherent in life.

To detect in Natalie any sign of future imperfections would have required not only physiognomy but personal science few possess. Her face, like most young girls’, was impenetrable. The sculptural calm of virgin faces—Justice, Innocence—marks purity; such placid expression had not been clouded by passion or betrayal. If that expression were assumed, then no young woman remained behind it.

Yet a clever analyst might have found indications of difficulty. Her beauty came from extreme regularity of feature and proportion—perfection that often foreshadows coldness of organization. Superior nature usually shows slight imperfections that become luminous attractions; perfect harmony often indicates a mixed, colder nature.

Natalie’s round waist signaled a will that could become obstinacy where the mind is not keen. Her statue‑like hands suggested an inclination for domination, willing for will’s sake. Meeting eyebrows were said to indicate jealousy; in superior minds jealousy becomes emulation, but in small minds it turns to hatred. The “hate and wait” of her mother lived in her. Her eyes were blackish brown with orange streaks, her hair a ruddy auburn—contrasts giving inexpressible charm but mainly external; where outlines lack soft roundness, beauty is not of the soul. Such roses of youth will drop their leaves, revealing hardness and dryness where nobility was admired.

Though her face had august outlines, a slightly full chin hinted at passions that might grow violent; her astringent mouth expressed haughty pride consistent with hand, chin, brows, and figure. Her pure voice, though seductive, had metallic tones suggesting Casa‑Reale temperament. These marks hinted at violent passions without tenderness, sudden devotions, irreconcilable dislikes, a mind not deeply intelligent, and a desire to rule natural to those who feel inferior to their pretensions.

These defects, buried in Natalie like ore in a mine, would only surface under life’s shocks. Meantime grace, freshness, distinction, sacred ignorance, and youthful sweetness gave a glamour that misled unthinking minds. Her mother taught her agreeable talk, replying to arguments with witty jests and concealing the subsoil of her mind with volubility. Natalie had the charm of those who never knew suffering: frank, gay, natural, expecting pleasure from marriage and replying to objections with jest.

How could Paul, dazzled by such beauty, foresee the woman she might be at thirty? Besides, happiness in such a marriage was not impossible. Embryo defects could be stifled by wise training, especially in a young girl who loved him. But to render ductile an intractable woman the iron wrist de Marsay recommended was necessary. Fear inspired by love is an instrument to manage women; he who loves fears, and thus is nearer affection than hatred.

Had Paul the coolness, firmness, and judgment required for this struggle? Did Natalie love Paul? Like most young girls, she mistook instinct and the pleasure of his exterior for love; she knew nothing of marriage’s demands. To her, Comte de Manerville—a rising diplomatist acquainted with European courts and one of Paris’s most elegant young men—could hardly seem an ordinary man with moral force: timid though brave in some ways, energetic in adversity perhaps, but helpless against the vexations that hinder happiness. Would she have sufficient tact to distinguish his noble qualities among lesser defects? Would she not magnify the latter and forget the former, as inexperienced young wives often do? There comes a time when wives pardon defects in the husband who spares them annoyances; what conciliating power or wise experience would uphold this home? Paul, weak where even a strong man runs risks, might yield to tyranny instead of establishing his rule. Could he say “No”?

The subject here is not merely the transition from bachelor to married man—a picture that would itself offer rich drama—but the great comedy that precedes all conjugal pairing in France: the discussion before a notary about the marriage contract, played in families noble and bourgeois, where human passions are excited by small interests as by large ones. This scene, neglected by dramatists though full of wit, controlled Paul’s future life and was awaited by Madame Evangelista with terror. It is to this comedy—the notarial negotiation—that we now turn.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At the beginning of the winter of 1822, Paul de Manerville asked, through his great-aunt the Baronne de Maulincour, for the hand of Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista. The baroness lingered in Médoc until late October to help him, then reported back: Madame Evangelista gave nothing of her own to her daughter; Natalie’s dowry was her patrimony. “Marry her,” the baroness urged. Men with name and estate must marry to continue the family; consult Maitre Mathias and draw up the marriage contract.

Troubled by the baroness’s hint that Madame Evangelista was a “sly cat,” Paul followed her advice and engaged Maitre Mathias. He approached Madame Evangelista with hesitant courtesy about the notary. She agreed to the usual arrangement, but inwardly worried: she owed Natalie one-third of Monsieur Evangelista’s fortune—about 1,156,000 francs—but lacked ready means to pay it. If Mathias uncovered the truth, Paul might withdraw and ruin her plan. She resolved to press on, convinced she could still secure Natalie’s happiness and preserve herself if necessary.

Madame Evangelista summoned her own notary, Maitre Solonet, a young, ambitious royalist and dandy noted for gallantry and political zeal. Flattered to be chosen, he arrived ready to serve. He advised display and charm as essential: “Make her look her best tonight. The marriage-contract dress is half the battle.” Madame Evangelista dressed Natalie herself; in white tulle with pink ribbons and coiffure à la Sévigné, Natalie was a picture of charm. When her mother questioned whether Natalie loved Paul, the daughter’s replies were ambiguous—she insisted both that she would give up the marriage if they parted forever and that she loved Paul—enough to reveal loyalty rather than passion. Madame Evangelista warned that if Paul hesitated at sight of their show, she would break off the match and seek another husband. Natalie promised that if property difficulties separated them she would bear the blame.

Paul arrived with his habitual bouquet. He and Natalie talked lovingly of Lanstrac, Paris, and children while they awaited the notaries. But the business of marriage soon disturbed the romance. For Paul, the alliance seemed simple; legally and financially it was a minefield. The two notaries embodied different eras and temperaments: Maitre Mathias, Paul’s man, an elderly, respectable professional with forty years’ experience, painstaking care, and uprightness; and Solonet, young, fashionable, politically engaged, and eager to secure his client’s ends.

At Madame Evangelista’s salon, their differences quickly surfaced. Solonet, who had pre-drawn a contract favoring the widow, presented a scheme to make Natalie’s patrimony appear large and immediate. Mathias insisted on particulars and clarity. He listed Paul’s estate—Lanstrac (23,000 francs rental), farms and vineyards, houses in Bordeaux and Paris, and furniture—showing substantial landed wealth. Solonet countered that the Evangelista inheritance had been invested largely in Five-per-cent funds and marketable securities, effectively about 800,000 francs, that the Paris house added value, and that the mother could convey titles while reserving a life interest.

Mathias pressed for inventories and distinctions between land and marketable funds; Solonet explained that, being Spanish and Creole, the family had not followed French formalities at the father’s death and had reinvested English securities in Paris, making strict accounting difficult. When Mathias pressed the difference between landed estate and capital, the exchange grew heated. Solonet’s tactic was to secure a settlement by which Madame Evangelista would preserve apparent respectability while releasing Natalie’s patrimony; Mathias sought to protect Paul’s estate and his prospective children.

Natalie, playing with a fan, listened and privately told Paul she would accept blame if property problems ended the marriage. Paul, unacquainted with Mathias’s alarm, pressed her hand and rejoined the group. Mathias, alarmed, warned Paul in private that there was little ready dowry; he advised postponement to reflect.

Solonet then offered an expedient: sell the house, reckon the Five-per-cents as nearly at par, and convey titles into Natalie’s name while reserving a life interest for Madame Evangelista. This would recognize at once a large patrimony and close the guardianship account. Mathias saw a trap: such an arrangement would acknowledge receipt of about 1,156,000 francs, binding Paul to account to his heirs for that sum later while in fact the family lacked secure capital. He protested that the scheme could impoverish Paul and leave him liable if misfortunes occurred.

Provoked, Madame Evangelista threatened to break off the marriage and leave Bordeaux; she appealed to Spanish customs and the sentimental value of her name as a kind of dowry. The argument grew tense: Paul, torn between love and prudence, nearly yielded to emotion. Mathias, seeing Paul’s distress and Madame Evangelista’s ambition—she hoped Paul might become a peer of France—conceived a legal remedy to reconcile interests.

Mathias proposed that to secure Paul’s future public prospects and protect his estate, the parties should entail Lanstrac and the Paris house and include eight hundred thousand francs of Natalie’s patrimony in that entail by purchasing adjoining domains and having them included in the royal ordinance required for an entail. In effect, the convertible portion of Natalie’s fortune would be applied to create inalienable capital that would strengthen the landed estate, satisfying Madame Evangelista’s desire to release Natalie’s patrimony while preventing immediate dissipation and calming Paul and Mathias’s fear of impoverishment. This would allow Mathias to accept the apparent deficiency in the guardianship account.

Solonet, surprised, recognized that an entail would placate distrust: entailed property is inalienable and would reassure Paul and his advisers that the capital could not be wasted. Madame Evangelista, ignorant of legal technicalities but eager for security and respectability, accepted Mathias’s plan because it seemed to secure Natalie’s patrimony and protect her own safety. Both she and Paul agreed that if the entail were created, the marriage should proceed.

Mathias emphasized that the formalities required a royal ordinance and immediate action, but that consent to proceed would suffice for the parties to continue. Natalie, a minor, was not party to the formal bargaining beyond assent. It was arranged that Mathias would draw up the marriage contract, Solonet the guardianship account and release, and both documents would be signed days before the wedding. The notaries left together, each professionally satisfied: Solonet had guided the match under an appearance of security; Mathias had devised a legal mechanism to protect the Manerville estate.

Left alone, Paul and Madame Evangelista reconciled their earlier sharp words. Paul was ecstatic at the prospect that, with the entail and a combined household, he and Natalie could enjoy an income near one hundred thousand francs and he might obtain a peerage. Madame Evangelista, relieved, expressed gratitude and tenderness; she proposed to give Natalie her diamonds and assured Paul she would sell her house and find means to secure herself without injuring her daughter’s patrimony.

Natalie reacted with emotional generosity, insisting her father’s diamonds remain but accepting that her mother’s sacrifice might be required. Madame Evangelista, elated, planned to have jewels appraised and to sell plate and other items; she spoke of winters in Paris, of Lanstrac as their home, and of Solonet’s aid in settling debts. She fell asleep convinced she had achieved her ends: Natalie married to a man she could influence, a household assured, and an ambitious plan for Paul’s political advancement.

Thus the first day of negotiation ended with apparent satisfaction: Paul convinced, Madame Evangelista appeased, Natalie content, Mathias relieved that an entail might save Manerville, and Solonet pleased at having guided the match. Beneath this harmony, however, lay seeds of distrust and design—Madame Evangelista believing herself triumphant, Mathias guarding his client’s legacy, Solonet enjoying his victory, and Paul seeing a bright future of love and ambition.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, obeyed Madame Evangelista’s summons. Rumors that Comte Paul was marrying Mademoiselle Natalie made it seem she wanted jewels for the bride; instead she had him value the mother-in-law’s property, suspecting the diamonds’ worth might be included in the marriage contract. She told him to value them as if for a private buyer even though they would not be sold. Magus explained that expert jewellers distinguish Asian from Brazilian diamonds: Golconda and Visapur stones have superior whiteness and brilliance that Brazilian stones, with a yellow tinge, lack. He valued Madame Evangelista’s necklace and earrings, composed entirely of Asiatic diamonds, at 250,000 francs; the “Discreto” he called one of the finest private diamonds, worth 100,000 francs. She asked if she could obtain that money immediately.

Magus replied that if she wished to sell he could only give seventy-five thousand for the brilliant and one hundred and sixty thousand for the necklace and earrings. He explained dealers’ margins: rarer gems can remain unsold longer, so buying prices are lower than selling prices; owning them ties up capital and yields no interest. He illustrated with witty calculations about the cost of wearing diamonds. Madame Evangelista thanked him and, when he asked if she would sell the rest, he valued other jewels—Brazilian diamonds, pearls, rubies—at about one hundred thousand to a dealer, though a dealer might resell them for a hundred and fifty thousand.

Madame Evangelista declared she would keep them; Magus warned that investing the income from that capital could replace similar diamonds in five years while preserving the principal. The conference leaked. Servants overheard raised voices, gossip exaggerated practical difficulties, and the rumor mill magnified the story: had Madame Evangelista sold her house? Was the marriage off? Notaries denied collapse, blaming only delay in constituting the entail, but society persisted. Young women and thwarted provincial mothers, envious after two decades of Evangelista magnificence, spread and embellished the tale. Officials at the prefecture hinted the notaries’ language implied a rupture. People speculated about quibbles over the entail, outraged that a wealthy widow might lack funds to pay her daughter’s patrimony. Bets were laid; yet no one told Paul or Madame Evangelista the gossip. Paul had business at Lanstrac and gave a hunting-party as a kind of farewell to bachelor life; society interpreted this as evidence of trouble.

Madame de Gyas, hoping to marry her daughter, felt obliged to sound out the Evangelistas and addressed them with a long face as if to expose misfortune. Natalie and her mother laughed at the recital of gossip but recognized the motives: provincial mothers, thwarted by lack of dowry or fortune, might imagine such plots. The Marquise launched into a spirited harangue about mothers desperate to secure husbands for daughters, likening them to predators; she mocked the idea that the public should judge private marriage clauses. Madame Evangelista, preparing to leave the city, could safely mock both friends and enemies and enjoyed making remarks that stung.

When a footman announced Paul—who had ridden twelve miles to spend an hour with Natalie—she greeted him, and he dismissed the gossip with laughter. “These worthy people have found out, perhaps, that there will be no wedding festivities,” he said. He suggested pacifying them with a ball on the day they signed the contract: a public show to refute the rumors. Madame Evangelista took him at his word and invited all Bordeaux to witness the contract signature; she intended the last fete to silence the gossip.

Preparations for the camellia fete consumed a month. Camellias banked staircases and supper-rooms; partitions were removed to enlarge rooms; estates near Lanstrac were purchased, banns published, and doubts dissipated. For a while the earlier stormy conference faded—after all, as Madame Evangelista had said, it had seemed only the notaries’ affair.

Yet on the morning of the signing a flash of memory returned to Madame Evangelista: Mathias’s earlier words when he had yielded to Solonet’s conditions—“Questa coda non e di questo gatto”—suggested someone would pay the cost. If Mathias had seen compensation, it could not be Paul. Could her daughter’s fortune be made to meet the price? She resolved to seek explanations for the contract’s tenor. This day would profoundly affect Paul de Manerville’s conjugal life.

Despite the winter, the courtyard was tented, shrubs and banked camellias decorated the house, and the ballroom and supper-room were fitted for the fairyland that Bordeaux expected. About eight, as the final contract was discussed, a dense crowd gathered to see the ladies in full dress. The two notaries had dined with the bridal pair and their mother; Mathias’s head-clerk was present to receive signatures and guard against surreptitious readings.

No bridal toilette equalled Natalie’s; she was superb. Madame Evangelista, in cherry velvet, wore her pearl necklace clasped with the “Discreto,” a visible contradiction to calumnies of ruin. Paul and Natalie sat together by the fire, inattentive to the reading, whispering and touching; Natalie toyed with a peacock-feather fan Paul had given her. Seated by the notaries, Madame Evangelista listened as Solonet read the guardianship account: Natalie’s share of Monsieur Evangelista’s three million-plus estate amounted to eleven hundred and fifty-six thousand francs. “Come, listen, listen, my children; this is your marriage contract,” she said.

The clerical routine proceeded: statement of each party’s property, general deeds of gift in case of death without issue, deed of gift of a life interest and a quarter of capital allowed by the Code, constitution of a common fund, settlement of the diamonds on the wife, the library and horses on the husband, and then the constitution of the entail. When all but signatures remained, Madame Evangelista asked what an entail would ultimately mean. Solonet explained generally, but Mathias bluntly defined the entail’s practical effect: if the wife died first leaving several children including a son, Monsieur de Manerville would owe those children only three hundred and sixty thousand francs, from which he would deduct his fourth in life-interest and his fourth in capital, reducing his debt to about one hundred and sixty thousand francs, excluding savings and profits from the common fund. If the husband died first leaving a male heir, Madame de Manerville’s right would be three hundred and sixty thousand francs plus her deeds of gift of property not in the entail, the diamonds settled on her, and profits and savings from the common fund.

The consequence struck Madame Evangelista: “My daughter is ruined,” she whispered. Both notaries overheard. Mathias, with a gentle irony, replied that constituting an indestructible fortune for her family could hardly be ruin. The younger notary, seeing her face, whispered bluntly that they had tried to trick them out of three hundred thousand francs but actually the children had secured eight hundred thousand—arguing it was a loss of four hundred thousand from their interests for the children’s benefit. He advised her to break the marriage immediately or carry it through.

Silence followed. Natalie, unaware she had lost half her fortune, and Paul, ignorant the Manerville house had gained, conversed lightly. Solonet and Madame Evangelista exchanged glances—one masking indifference, the other curbing bitter feeling. Madame Evangelista’s remorse and indignation at having compromised her honor in Natalie’s interest turned to fury when she realized she would be the loser and that Mathias must have foreseen this and enlightened Paul. The thought that the old notary might soon be lauded for his sagacity and turn Paul against her was intolerable. She faced a dilemma: three months earlier she might have broken the match; now banns were published, friends had arrived for the fete, and postponing would bring public scandal. She felt trapped and knew she must yield.

Her anger surfaced: her chin trembled; Solonet noticed the muscles play and read the birth of a mortal hatred. She turned to Mathias: accusing him of incapacity or worse, of seeing the entail’s effect and not warning her sooner. The younger notary drew her aside, thinking of his fees but speaking coolly: she had overstepped legal craft for her own sake; if she did not want Comte Paul, she need not accept him—the contract was not yet signed; she could postpone it and blame some error in Paris. He suggested she might find another count if title were the chief aim. She protested that she could not chance her honor. He insisted that Mademoiselle Natalie’s happiness mattered most and argued that she, as wife, could dominate the home and lead Paul by the nose; if she played her cards right, the count’s fate was within her power. This mollified Madame Evangelista; she declared she would be grateful.

Mathias and Solonet then amended the contract to protect the wife’s interests further. The young notary proposed—and Mathias concurred—clauses to ensure that if Manerville died without children or left only daughters, the entailed property should pass under the general deed of gift between husband and wife or that the entail be declared void. Solonet added this clause in the margin. Madame Evangelista listened, dropped her eyes while Mathias read the added sentence aloud.

“We will now sign,” she said, her voice betraying violent emotion. She thought: she would not allow her daughter to be ruined; she intended that Natalie should have the name, title, and fortune. If Natalie later loved another, Paul would be driven out of France. Her mind resolved on measures to keep her daughter rich and free. Mathias took Paul aside and explained the saving stipulation—that he held the documents and that the contract contained a rescript for the diamonds; Paul must ask for them and could use them toward payments. Mathias spoke in practical terms: the first payment of two hundred thousand francs was due after formalities; use the diamonds; the house could be sold to pay the rest; plant vineyards to raise income. Paul pressed Mathias’s hand affectionately; Madame Evangelista noticed and suspected collusion. Rage and hatred overwhelmed her.

After verifying signatures and initials, Mathias looked at the mother-in-law. Seeing Paul say nothing of the diamonds, Mathias remarked there could be no doubt about their transfer now they were family. Solonet urged it was more regular for Madame Evangelista to make them over immediately since Monsieur de Manerville had become responsible for guardianship funds and one never knew who might live or die. Paul protested that it would be insulting; Madame Evangelista, angered at what she took as an insult masked in Mathias’s words, cried she would tear the contract up if he did not take the diamonds.

She left in fury, nearly mad with impotence. Natalie whispered to Paul to take them: her mother was angry and she would know why that night; they must pacify her. Calmed, Madame Evangelista kept the necklace and earrings she wore and brought the other jewels, which Magus had valued at 150,000 francs. Mathias and Solonet examined them and found they would meet the first payments. Solonet assured Paul he would lose nothing on the spot; Mathias said the jewels would meet the first payment on the estate purchase and the contract costs.

Hatred fed on small things; Madame Evangelista took Paul’s reluctance to accept the jewels as hypocrisy. She interpreted his embarrassment—he did not want them—as hypocrisy and flung a look that seemed to say “Take your property.” Paul, eager to end the dispute, told Natalie to lock the jewels away as her own gift. Natalie stowed them in a console drawer. At that moment carriages and voices swelled; salons filled and the fete began.

During the ball, whispers and judgments circulated among guests: speculation about the marriage’s financial and social results, about Madame Evangelista’s supposed penury, and about Mathias’s hand in safe-guarding the family. Some predicted future domestic friction; others speculated that Mademoiselle Evangelista, as a merchant’s daughter, could not hold her place socially. Card-players took tables, the young danced, supper was served, and the ball lasted till morning.

After Paul left, Madame Evangelista and Natalie retired. Though overtired, they exchanged a few words. Madame Evangelista confessed she had acted out of tender devotion and unscrupulous business ignorance in order to secure Natalie’s happiness; she had trampled her pride. Natalie, half asleep, spoke warmly of Paul’s innocence; she had no idea her mother’s fears or the implications of the contract. Madame Evangelista, in the dark, felt the first stirrings of avarice induced by passion and hate: a sudden resolve to secure her daughter’s entire fortune by reclaiming her husband’s property for Natalie. She told herself honor required it; devotion to Natalie made her shrewd and calculating. She planned to invest capital in the Funds and to manage matters carefully. Passion and hatred had transformed the once-prodigal woman into a miser and a plotter. She fell asleep muttering, “To-morrow!” Her mind, in sleep, marshalled ideas and arranged a plan to rule Paul’s life—a plan she began to put into action the next day.

Though the excitement of the fete had driven away Paul’s occasional anxieties, they returned at night when he lay alone.

“It seems to me,” he thought, “that without that good Mathias my mother-in-law would have tricked me. And yet, is that believable? What interest could lead her to deceive me? Are we not to join fortunes and live together? Well, well, why should I worry? In two days Natalie will be my wife; our money relations are plainly defined, nothing can come between us. Vogue la galere—Nevertheless, I’ll be upon my guard. Suppose Mathias was right? Well, if he was, I’m not obliged to marry my mother-in-law.”

Unseen by Paul, this second battle over the contract had already changed his future. Of the two people he was marrying the cleverer had become his mortal enemy, resolved to withdraw her interests from the common fund. Incapable of noting how a Creole nature differs from others, Paul did not suspect her craftiness. The Creole spirit, Madame Evangelista’s, blended European intellect, tropical passion, and Eastern indolence; graceful but dangerous, brooding on desires until she hatched them. She could be perfidious yet naïve, stubbornly Spanish and smoothly French. Dormant for sixteen years in complacent married life, that nature now flamed up when she saw herself losing the dearest object of her affection and needing a new outlet. With Natalie leaving in three days, Madame Evangelista had one final chance—the last day a daughter spends beside her mother—to sway her daughter’s future. A few words, whispered to a trusting girl, might determine their whole lives: society’s codes cannot stop crimes committed by language and ideas.

The next midday Madame Evangelista half-sat on her daughter’s bed. During that waking hour they caressed and recalled a life without discord.

“Poor little darling!” the mother cried, truly moved. “How can I help being sorrowful when I think that after I have fulfilled your every wish you will, to-morrow night, belong to a man you must obey?”

“Oh, my dear mother, as for obeying!” Natalie made a graceful motion of rebellion. “You are joking. My father always gratified your caprices. And I am loved, too.”

“Yes, Paul has a certain love for you. But conjugal love evaporates quickly if a wife is not careful. The influence a wife ought to have over her husband depends on how she begins with him. You need the best advice.”

“But you will be with us.”

“Possibly. Last night, while the ball went on, I thought of the dangers of our being together. If my presence should interfere—if the little acts by which you should establish authority were attributed to me—your home would become a hell. At the first frown on your husband’s brow I, proud as I am, should instantly leave his house. If I were driven to leave it, better not to enter it at all. You must become the absolute sovereign of your home.”

“Then why say I must obey him?”

“My dear, to rule you must always seem to do what he wishes. If you oppose him impulsively you might destroy your future. Paul is a weak young man; he might allow a friend or another woman to govern him. Have him governed by you rather than by others.”

“Yes, certainly,” said Natalie. “I shall think only of his happiness.”

“And it is my privilege to think only of yours, and not to leave you at this crucial moment without a compass among the reefs through which you must steer.”

“But, dearest mother, are we not strong enough, you and I, to stay together beside him, without fearing those frowns you dread? Paul loves you, mamma.”

“Oh! He fears me more than he loves me. Watch him today when I tell him I will let you go to Paris without me; you will see, concealed though it is, his inward joy.”

“Why should he feel so?”

“Why? Dear child! I will tell him myself—before you.”

“But suppose I marry on condition that you do not leave me?”

“Our separation is necessary,” the mother replied. “Several considerations have changed my future. I am now poor. You will lead a brilliant life in Paris; I could not live with you suitably without spending the little that remains to me. If I go to Lanstrac, I can take care of your property there and restore my fortune by economy.”

“You practise economy!” Natalie laughed. “Do you mean to leave me for such reasons? Paul may seem to you a trifle stupid, but he is not selfish or grasping.”

“Ah!” Madame Evangelista said, with hints that made the girl’s heart throb. “Those discussions about the contract have made me distrustful. I have my doubts about him—But don’t be troubled, dear child,” she added kissing her, “I will not leave you long. Whenever my return can take place without making difficulty between you, whenever Paul can rightly judge me, we will begin again our happy life, our evening confidences—”

“Oh! mother, how can you think of living without your Natalie?”

“Because, dear angel, I shall live for her. My mother’s heart will be satisfied that I contribute to your future happiness.”

“But, my dear, adorable mother, must I be alone with Paul, here, now, all at once? What will become of me? what must I do? what must I not do?”

“Poor child! do you think I would utterly abandon you to your first battle? We will write to each other three times a week like lovers. Nothing can happen to you that I shall not know, and I can save you from misfortune. Besides, I will always spend a month or two every year with you in Paris.”

“Alone, already alone, and with him!” cried Natalie.

“But you wish to be his wife?”

“Yes, I wish it. But tell me how I should behave—you, who did what you pleased with my father. You know the way; I’ll obey you blindly.”

Madame Evangelista, having wanted this moment, kissed her daughter’s forehead.

“Child, my counsel must adapt to circumstances. All men are not alike. I do not know what will befall you tomorrow, so I can only give general advice.”

“Dear mother, tell me, quick, all that you know.”

“You must understand the chief cause of failure for wives who wish to keep their husbands’ hearts—and to keep their hearts and rule them is one and the same thing. The principal cause is perpetual intercourse, which never existed until recently with the mania for family. Since the Revolution bourgeois habits invaded aristocratic homes. Women now stay at home, nurse children, and mix too constantly with husbands. Perpetual contact exhausts sentiment. You must erect between yourself and Paul the barriers of society: go to balls and operas, dine out, pay visits; give little of your time to your husband. By this you keep your value. When two beings live on sentiment alone, it soon dries up; indifference and disgust follow. Be always young and always new to him. The faculty of being bored without showing it is essential. You cannot diversify happiness by household cares alone; if you do not widen your social life you will fall into apathy and the SPLEEN of love. A man will always love the woman who amuses him.”

“Dear mother, I am listening, but I don’t understand one word you say.”

“If you make your happiness depend on him, if you do everything he asks, you will never be mistress of your home. The practice will come with experience. A woman’s vocation is to be a woman of the world and a charming hostess, not merely mother and steward. If you have children let them come later; governesses and tutors will bring them up. YOU are to represent the luxury and pleasure of the house. Let your superiority be visible only in things which flatter a man’s self-love; hide the superiority you must acquire in great things.”

“But you frighten me, mamma,” Natalie cried. “How can I remember all these precepts? How shall I manage, such a child and so heedless, to reflect and calculate before I act?”

“You will learn by faults and experience. For now, instinct will guide you. At this moment Paul desires you more than he loves you; love born of desire is hope; after satisfaction comes real love. That first ardor is your power. A woman ardently desired can ask all things; do not waste those first hours on trifles. Use that empire to accustom him to obedience. When he yields once he will yield habitually. Choose an unreasonable point for a test; making him give in to something reasonable is no victory. Marriage lasts a lifetime; therefore never give yourself into his power in everything. Keep reserve in speech and action. You may be cold without danger; dignity is easy to maintain. The words ‘It is not becoming in your wife to do thus’ are a talisman. ‘I will not’ is the woman’s final argument. Feminine power lies in such refusals, used on real occasions. I reigned over your father by his faith in me. If your husband believes in you, you can do all things. To inspire that belief you must make him think you understand him. Many say a wife creates unhappiness by wavering; but a wife becomes mistress by controlling events, not bearing them.”

Natalie kissed her mother’s hands, grateful and moved. Quick to feel but inexperienced, she still asked one childish question.

“Dear mamma,” she said, “only a few days since you spoke of Paul’s advancement and said you alone could promote it; why then do you now abandon us?”

“I did not then know the extent of my debts,” the mother said, concealing her true motive. “Besides, a year hence I can take up that matter again. Come, let us dress; Paul will be here soon. Be as sweet and caressing as you were that night when we first discussed this fatal contract; to-day we must save the last fragments of our fortune, and I must win for you something I am superstitiously attached to.”

“What is it?”

“The ‘Discreto.’”

Paul arrived about four. Though he tried to look gracious, signs of the prior counsels showed on his face. “Mathias has told him!” Madame Evangelista thought, resolving to defeat the old notary. She contrived another plan.

“My dear son,” she said, “you left your diamonds in the drawer; I would rather not see again the things that threatened to bring a cloud between us. Besides, as Monsieur Mathias said, they ought to be sold at once to meet the first payment on the estates you have purchased.”

“They are not mine,” Paul answered. “I have given them to Natalie; when you see them upon her you will forget the pain they caused you.”

Madame Evangelista took his hand and pressed it with a tear. Then, looking from Paul to Natalie, she offered a proposition. She admitted she must sell her pearl necklace and ear-rings but refused to sell the ‘Discreto,’ a diamond with historic associations—Philip II, the Duke of Alba. Elie Magus valued her necklace and ear-rings highly; would they exchange the jewels she had conveyed to Paul for these instead? He would gain but she would keep the family’s historic stones.

“They are mine,” Paul demurred.

“I am going to be your bailiff at Lanstrac; it would be folly to go to Paris when I ought to liquidate my property and settle my affairs. I shall be miserly for my grandchildren.”

“Dear mother,” Paul said, moved, “ought I to accept this exchange without paying you the difference?”

“Good heavens! are you not both my dearest interests? Do you suppose I shall not find happiness thinking, as I sit by my chimney, ‘Natalie is dazzling to-night at the Duchesse de Berry’s ball’? When she wears my diamond at her throat and my ear-rings in her ears she will have the little vanity that makes a woman gay. Nothing saddens a woman more than repressed vanity.”

Paul thought, “What was Mathias thinking?” Then, yielding, he accepted. Natalie was confounded.

At that moment Solonet arrived with news: speculators from Bordeaux wanted the house’s grounds for dwellings.

“They offer two hundred and fifty thousand francs,” he said; “but if you consent, I can make them give three hundred thousand. There are three acres.”

“My husband paid two hundred thousand; therefore I consent,” Natalie replied. “But reserve the furniture and mirrors.”

“Ah! you are beginning to understand business.”

“I must,” she said, sighing.

Solonet added that many were coming to the midnight service. Madame Evangelista accompanied him to the salon door and, in a low voice, revealed she now had personal property to the amount of two hundred and fifty thousand francs; if she could get two hundred thousand for her share of the house she would have a handsome capital to invest. She planned to live at Lanstrac. The young notary, flattered and hopeful, promised to find safe investments.

When she returned, Paul complained that Natalie was provoked as if he had caused the decision to leave.

“I have thought it over,” she said. “I should hamper you. You would feel obliged to make me a third in all you did; young people have ideas of their own which I might unintentionally thwart. My influence ought to yield to yours. Young husbands are sooner or later jealous of a wife’s love for her mother. When you are thoroughly united then my living with you would not be feared. I have seen many homes destroyed by blind mothers who made themselves intolerable. Let me make one more sacrifice for your happiness. I have given you my fortune; now I resign my last vanities as a woman. Your notary Mathias is getting old; he cannot look after your estates as I will. I shall be your bailiff. Later, if necessary, I will come to Paris and second you in your projects of ambition. Come, Paul, be frank; my proposal suits you, does it not?”

Paul would not admit it, but he was delighted to get his liberty. Mathias’s doubts faded in the face of this talk, which Madame Evangelista prolonged.

“My mother was right,” thought Natalie, watching Paul. “He is glad to know that I am separated from her—why?”

That “why” was the first note of distrust; did it prove the power of those maternal instructions? Paul’s trusting nature assumed effects without tracing causes; his weakness sprang from kindness and belief in goodness rather than vice.

Natalie, sad and thoughtful, did not know what to do without her mother. Paul, confident in love, smiled at her sadness, thinking Paris and marriage would soon dispel it. Madame Evangelista was satisfied: her daughter wore the diamonds that had cost Paul two hundred thousand francs, and she had won the children’s isolation—left to each other and their illogical love. Her revenge was preparing; Natalie would unwittingly become its accomplice. Did Natalie love Paul? That question, undecided, might change Madame’s plans, for she loved her daughter too much to disregard her happiness. Paul’s future still depended on himself: if he could make his wife love him, he might be saved.

The next day, at midnight after a formal dinner with the four witnesses Madame Evangelista had provided, the bridal pair and friends heard mass by torchlight before an inquisitive crowd. A midnight wedding suggested an omen: light, symbol of life and pleasure, was lacking. Darkness chilled and saddened the assembly; Natalie, parted from her mother, wept. She was gripped by the doubts that haunt anyone entering a new stage where despite assurances pitfalls lie. She shivered and wanted a mantle; the air and manner of the mother and bridal pair provoked comment.

“Solonet tells me the bride and bridegroom leave for Paris tomorrow morning, all alone.”

“Madame Evangelista was to live with them, I thought.”

“Count Paul has got rid of her already.”

“What a mistake!” said the Marquise de Gyas. “To shut the door on the mother of his wife is to open it to a lover. Doesn’t he know what a mother is?”

“He has been very hard on Madame Evangelista; the poor woman has had to sell her house and diamonds, and is going to live at Lanstrac.”

“Natalie looks very sad.”

“Would you like to be made to take a journey the day after your marriage?”

“It is very awkward.”

“I am glad I came,” said a lady. “I am convinced of the necessity of wedding pomps; a scene like this is bare and sad. If I may say so, this marriage seems indecent.”

Madame Evangelista accompanied Natalie in a carriage to Paul’s house. “Well, mother, it is done!” Natalie said.

“Remember my last advice, and you will be happy. Be his wife, not his mistress.”

When Natalie had retired the mother played the little comedy of flinging herself with tears into her son-in-law’s arms—a provincial gesture which suited her purpose. Amid tears and speeches she obtained from Paul the concessions all husbands make. The next morning she put the married pair into their carriage, accompanied them to the ferry across the Gironde, and with a look and a word enabled her daughter to see that if Paul had won in the contract-game, her revenge was beginning. By her counsels Natalie was already learning to make Paul obedient.

CHAPTER VI. CONCLUSION

Five years later, on an November afternoon, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and haggard, arrived at the house of his old friend Maître Mathias in Bordeaux.

Mathias, retired and too old to practice, was out on urgent business; his housekeeper Josette showed Paul to the late Madame Mathias’s room. Fatigued by the rapid journey, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned he climbed to his client’s room and watched him sleep. “It is a year to-day, Josette, since I received my dear wife’s last sigh; I little thought then I should see the count half dead,” he said. Josette noted Paul’s moans; Mathias, distressed, muttered an oath and recalled that he had saved the title to Lanstrac for Paul’s family.

Leaning on his cane, the gouty notary paced the garden until Paul awoke. At supper Mathias, surprised at Paul’s calm yet aged face, pressed him about his affairs.

“Dear Mathias, you too have troubles.”

“Mine were natural, Monsieur le comte; but yours—”

“We will speak of that over supper.”

Mathias produced a paper: seizure placards announced the seizure of Paul’s farms at Grassol and Guadet, the vineyard of Belle-Rose and the family mansion. Paul confirmed the separation of property: “Yes, and now separated in body,” he added.

“You have left her?”

“My passage is taken; I sail for Calcutta on the Belle-Amélie in two days.”

“You are only seventy-three and have the gout, Mathias. When I return I shall be forty and rebuild my fortune. I intend to make a noble fortune in seven years. All is possible.”

Mathias, incredulous, asked where the money to begin such a venture would come from. Paul named de Marsay. “He has sold some investments already; to-morrow you will receive from him a bill of exchange for one hundred and fifty thousand francs.”

Mathias brightened. “If that be so, cannot your friend settle your difficulties here? You could live at Lanstrac and recover.”

“No assignment or economy could pay off fifteen hundred thousand francs of debt, in which my wife is involved to the amount of five hundred and fifty thousand,” Paul said. He explained how he had squandered fortunes: giving diamonds to his wife, spending 150,000 obtained from selling Madame Evangelista’s house on his Paris residence, using funds to satisfy marriage-contract demands, selling Natalie's forty-thousand-a-year in the Funds at eighty-seven and thereby falling two hundred thousand francs into debt within a month. They lived on far more than their income; add usurers’ interest and other payments and Paul declared he was ruined to the tune of about a million and a half francs. He had paid 100,000 for a coronet of diamond wheat-ears. Now he found he owed his wife’s dowry interest and other claims.

Mathias suggested selling Belle-Rose and other properties or pledging Natalie's diamonds, but Paul insisted these measures were impracticable when one is already mortgaged and drowning in large notes. Mathias lamented Belle-Rose’s possible sale (worth about six hundred thousand francs), while Paul said Natalie might buy it in; he praised the devotion of his mother-in-law, Madame Evangelista, who had helped pay Natalie’s debts and improved Lanstrac, even buying the domain of Grainrouge.

Paul admitted he had proposed the separation of property to spare his wife sacrifice: “Women are angels who should not be mixed in sordid interests.” Mathias protested that a wife should share good and evil and that children would have held Natalie at home. Paul, who had none, pleaded not to be reproached and reiterated his purpose to go to India under the alias Camille, to work and make a fortune. “I sail under the name Camille. Commerce is only a final chance. A friend will send the money.”

Next morning Mathias received de Marsay’s bill of exchange for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. Paul rejoiced that de Marsay had obliged him without a word. Mathias tried to deter him from leaving, but Paul’s decision was fixed.

By a fortunate wind the Belle-Amélie sailed the next day. The quay was crowded; many remarked on the spectacle of the once-fashionable Pink of Fashion ruined and bound for India. Mathias accompanied Paul to the quay. As the ship prepared to weigh anchor, Mathias, too feeble to go aboard, entrusted two letters to a sailor and begged him to give them to Paul if he would come ashore. The sailor, unwilling to return, handed the letters directly to Paul without delivering Mathias’s message. One was in his wife’s handwriting; the other from de Marsay. Paul put them unread into his pocket.

At sea, plagued by doubt and sea-sickness, Paul vomited and slept for three days. On the first morning he felt better and opened his wife’s letter first.

To understand her reply, the letters Paul had written as he left must be given.

From Paul de Manerville to his wife:
He declared himself going to India to repair his shattered fortune, had lacked courage to tell her and thus deceived her for her good. When he returned, rich, he would restore all; he begged her to trust de Marsay to liquidate his affairs and to be free in all things. He could not write more, said he counted her breaths, and signed himself her “poor Pink of Fashion” who would never leave her.

From the Comtesse de Manerville to her husband:
Natalie’s reply was ardent and reproachful. How could he go without consulting her? Did he not belong to her? If he insisted, why should she not follow? She regretted the separation of property and resented being deprived of his confidence. Yet she called his departure noble: to seek fortune for the same passion that wrecked him was sublime. She promised to live with her mother at Lanstrac, economize, repay debts, and make de Marsay her friend. She hinted she might be with child—a son perhaps—and her mother had already sent thirty thousand francs. She would pledge diamonds if necessary and awaited him with prayers. Her tears and vows closed the letter.

Paul read and re-read Natalie’s ardent lines, alternately uplifted by love and shadowed by jealousy. Faith and doubt warred in him; he returned again to belief in her devotion.

From Paul to Henri de Marsay:
Paul’s letter to de Marsay asked him to sign a deed leasing Paul’s house to him at a nominal rent, send by bill of exchange to Maître Mathias 150,000 francs—on which his wife would endorse—and to take charge of liquidating his affairs. He admitted he had hidden his ruin, lost his last chances at play, and preferred to seek fortune in India rather than suicide. He confided his wife to de Marsay, asking him to guard her in their childless state, to be her adviser, banker, and protector. He expected to return rich or not at all.

From Henri de Marsay to Paul de Manerville:
De Marsay’s reply, received through Mathias, was brutal and shattering. He berated Paul for his secrecy and folly: his notes had been protested through Lecuyer, proxy for Solonet; Solonet and Madame Evangelista had used straw men to seize mortgages and properties—Belle-Rose, the farms, and the Bordeaux house—planning, it seemed, to profit now that Paul was at sea. De Marsay had uncovered a domestic conspiracy and proposed that Paul return immediately to France. He suggested arranging a duel with Vandenesse in which Paul would fire first—“in France the husband who shoots his rival becomes respectable.” Kill—or at least wound—Vandenesse, and society’s and his wife’s position would tremble; Paul could recover his place. De Marsay claimed 100,000 francs ready to pay debts and promised political and financial advancement: he planned within five years to secure a ministry or ambassadorship, was allying with an oligarchy of men like Ronquerolles and the Grandlieus and intended to marry an English heiress, Miss Dinah Stevens, with 240,000 francs a year and settlements that would give him a substantial fortune. He urged Paul to renounce expatriation, return to Paris, and let him manage the rest.

The bluntness of de Marsay’s letter demolished Paul’s hopes and illusions. The ship had passed the Azores. In the midst of devastation a cold impotent anger seized him.

“What had I done to them?” he asked—then cried aloud, “Henri! Henri!” Where many would rage or go mad, Paul instead went to bed and slept that heavy sleep which follows immense disasters—the stupefying sleep that follows the fall of great hopes.
