In late November 1809, Paris celebrated Napoleon's marriage to an Austrian archduchess, highlighting the contrast between the opulence of the French aristocracy and the Republic's previous austerity. Women, influenced by the frivolity of Louis XV's reign, sought fleeting romances with soldiers amid frequent calls to arms.

Luxury, especially diamonds, defined this era. The Comte de Gondreville hosted a lavish gathering to impress Napoleon, who did not attend due to a dispute with Josephine, foreshadowing their divorce. The soirée attracted beautiful women eager to catch the attention of wealthy generals and officials, with families using these events to introduce their daughters to powerful men.

The narrative focuses on a Parisian social gathering featuring a mysterious young woman and interactions among General Montcornet, Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon, and the Comtesse de Vaudremont. Montcornet admires the young lady's delicate beauty, overshadowed by more flamboyant women. The men speculate about her status, with Montcornet suggesting she is likely married or widowed, while Martial argues she seems too youthful for marriage, revealing the competitive nature of their social interactions.

Montcornet humorously critiques Martial's engagement to Madame de Vaudremont, advising caution in his flirtations. Jealousy simmers as both men are drawn to the enigmatic woman, who remains isolated despite her beauty. The arrival of the Comtesse de Vaudremont shifts the focus; she is strategic in her social appearances, ensuring she is always seen at her best. Martial's growing infatuation with the young lady leads him to neglect the Countess, straining their relationship. Montcornet attempts to learn more about the mysterious woman but faces rejection, revealing her sadness. The narrative culminates in a gambling scene where Soulanges, despite earlier turmoil, becomes engrossed in risking his fortune. Montcornet informs him of the Emperor's praise and a likely promotion, but Soulanges, consumed by dark moods, threatens violence if Martial approaches the woman.

In the ballroom, Montcornet notices Madame de Vaudremont's agitation over Martial's interest in the mysterious woman. The Countess reveals her jealousy, while Madame de Lansac, an observant dowager, offers cryptic advice against pursuing Martial. The evening buzzes with excitement, revealing the complexities of love and desire among the characters, while Soulanges broods in a corner.
