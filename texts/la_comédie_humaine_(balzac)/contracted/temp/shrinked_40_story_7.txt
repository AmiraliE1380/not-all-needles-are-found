In late November 1809, at the height of Napoleon’s empire and amid the aftershocks of Wagram, Austria still felt the war’s reverberations while peace talks continued. Napoleon basked in power and Parisian spectacle around his marriage to an Austrian archduchess. The newly ostentatious French aristocracy—diamonds and gold on women and uniforms—contrasted sharply with the Republic’s former austerity. The atmosphere was intoxicating: military leaders indulged in wealth earned by countless soldiers’ sacrifices, and society’s hedonism, whether revived from the fallen monarchy or urged by the Imperial family, seemed to foreshadow disaster.

Women adopted a frivolous, Louis XV–like ease and a frenzy for military men, since frequent calls to arms made peace feel provisional and could turn mistresses into widows within weeks. Soldiers’ appeal rested on the grim prospect of early widowhood, the lure of historical renown, and the romantic ideal of courage; bravery became a prized trait, often assumed to remain secret and sealed on the battlefield. Alongside valor ran a mania for glittering possessions—diamonds, fireworks, flamboyant ornamentation—with figures like Murat setting extravagant examples.

Comte de Gondreville, once Citizen Malin, had risen socially and delayed a grand entertainment to outshine rivals and impress Napoleon. His salons filled with ambassadors, influential men, and princes expecting the Emperor, who was absent following a private quarrel with Josephine that hinted at divorce. The most beautiful women of Paris flocked there: the financial elite used balls to present daughters to Napoleon’s circle, masking ambition beneath smiles while distrusting acquaintances as competitors for wealth and favor.

These remarks are necessary to explain the incidents surrounding the little imbroglio that is the subject of this study, reflecting the tone prevalent in Paris drawing-rooms at the time.

The scene opens with a group of men at a Gondreville ball, staring toward a candelabrum where a small, pale young woman sits in a corner. Her hair is à la Chinoise, adorned with bluebells; her almond-shaped blue eyes seem made for tears. She leans forward to watch Madame de Vaudremont below the crowd. Conversation immediately turns to guessing who she is. One man remarks on her exquisite complexion and delicate shoulders; another calls her either a prude or a coquette. They have all noticed her isolation—buried in a gloom of candles and surrounded by dazzling coquettes—and they wonder how anyone could spot her.

Speculation runs through the group. Is she an aristocrat? A widow? A companion? Montcornet, the Colonel of Cuirassiers, teases, proposes various romantic backstories—widow of a gambler, German princess—while Martial, a young lawyer (Maitre des Requetes), analyzes her face with the dry connoisseurship of a man used to judging social signs: too innocent to be married, yet saddened as if already in love. The conversation is more about the men’s curiosity and rivalry than about the woman herself; each interpretation of her looks reveals the observer’s prejudices.

The lawyer and the Colonel exchange barbs about rivalries and conquests. Montcornet jibes at Martial for behaving like a lover, reminding him of his impending marriage to the young widow Madame de Vaudremont, who brings four thousand napoleons a year—and who, Montcornet hints, slipped a diamond ring on Martial’s finger. Martial bristles at the advice but hides his ambitions beneath a cavalier demeanor; he is a diplomat by temperament, adept at masking desire with the show of indifference. Montcornet warns Martial not to flutter about the unknown lady; Martial answers with the boldness of a man trained to seize opportunity. The tone is light, mocking, but edged with the anxieties of men who measure their standing by conquests and by how they are seen in society.

Their whispered conversation, taking place in the interval between dances, is visible to Madame de Vaudremont herself and to the unknown stranger—those silent observers whose light scrutiny is one of the petty enjoyments of idle social life. To understand why the chatter matters, we must follow an incident that binds these characters: the entrance, at around eleven, of Madame de Vaudremont and the Comte de Soulanges, which stirs the assembly.

Madame de Vaudremont, the queen of fashion, makes it a rule to arrive at the precise hour when a ball is at its most brilliant, avoiding the later slump when dresses and faces lose their freshness. She enters with calculation, instantly scanning the room to ensure she outshines the other women. On this occasion she arrives with the Comte de Soulanges, a brave colonel of the Guards’ Artillery favored by the Emperor. Their joint appearance causes an immediate stir—some rise, men press forward—and the pair are observed like a spectacle. Soulanges, pale and nervous, is not outwardly handsome but has vivacious, black eyes; he gives off a reserved intensity that presages capabilities beyond his present posture. Madame de Vaudremont, tall and somewhat plump but exquisitely carried, embodies the cultivated charm that enlists affection rather than astonishes.

Martial, who aspires to ambition disguised as indolence, feels a prick of jealousy at their entrance. He approaches Madame de Vaudremont with a bow; Soulanges casts Martial a covert glance and then, suddenly flushing scarlet, moves away toward a cardroom. The company assumes Soulanges has been humiliated, perhaps forced to withdraw from a contest for the Countess’ favor. Martial, seated by Madame de Vaudremont, fails to hear her whispered request not to wear that ring she had given him—she hints at private reasons and asks him for his arm to go to the Princess de Wagram’s. Martial doesn’t respond as he is distracted by the unknown lady. The Countess observes Martial’s furtive looks with unease.

While Martial yields more and more to the charm of the fair stranger, the Colonel of Cuirassiers, unable to bear being outdone, threads through the crowd to the chair beside her. Too gentlemenlike to address her at once, he engages the woman on her left to coax the stranger into conversation. The woman to Montcornet’s left is only a screen; the lady in blue remains absorbed in watching Madame de Vaudremont and has tears in her eyes. When finally addressed, she answers in a mournful, measured way: yes, she is married; yes, her husband is present; she does not intend to dance. The Colonel’s bland compliments and offers to take her as his partner are met by the quiet, sad refusal.

Martial, watching this from the dance, smiles, stroking his chin and showing the diamond on his finger—an emblem of the tie between him and Madame de Vaudremont that he still thinks secure. The stranger, however, notices the ring and asks the Colonel who the owner is. When told it is Martial de la Roche-Hugon, one of Montcornet’s intimate friends, she thanks him and comments on Martial’s agreeable appearance. The Colonel confesses Martial’s fickleness and remarks on a rumor that Madame de Vaudremont had long been devoted to Soulanges—though lately, the Countess has been inattentive to the Count. The unknown instantly pales and recovers, but believes Soulanges’ public reaction at their entrance may explain something of her own interest in him.

The quadrille ends; the men draw aside to breathe fresh air in a window-bay and trade wagers. Montcornet, moral and conventional, declares that because the stranger is married he will not pursue her—he is a decent man. Martial, more daring, bets a hundred napoleons to the Colonel’s grey horse that the stranger will dance with him that evening, and the Colonel, confident he has been properly rebuffed, accepts. There is also the confession that the stranger seems fond of diamonds—a hint at motives that, to men like Montcornet and the Colonel, is both an insult and a challenge. The two men agree to part without malice but each with the intent of testing his luck.

Montcornet moves to the cardroom and finds Soulanges at a bouillotte table, playing with a fury that alarms the Colonel. Soulanges is engaged in reckless, high-stakes play; piles of gold and banknotes accumulate at the table, and a circle of silent men watches with tense interest. The Count is winning—sweeping up piles of stakes with a harsh curl of his lips, not rejoicing but defying fortune. His behavior suggests that his passion and despair are driving him to gamble as if to annihilate something within himself. The scene of the bouillotte table, with its strained silence and the dramatic piles of money, contrasts sharply with the glittering lightness of the ballrooms: here are men who gamble their fortunes to silence their inner storms.

Back in the drawing-room the unknown’s passive sadness sets her apart from the usual coquettes. Surrounding ladies, jealous or cautious, form a silent rampart around her, unwilling to let any man claim her; yet no one speaks to her. The Countess’s watchful glances and the anxious whispers of the men show how social performance and private motives intersect. Martial is torn between the charm of the stranger and the advantages of his alliance with Madame de Vaudremont. Montcornet teases him about the ring—this diamond symbolizes not only a sentimental tie but a social contract, and Martial’s flirtations might compromise a prosperous future. Martial, who has the diplomat’s apathy, is nonetheless visibly moved when he meets the unknown’s eyes.

The unknown’s answers—short, melancholic—make it clear that she is not seeking to charm; her beauty and stillness provoke the imaginations of men accustomed to being desired. They interpret her tears as evidence of fresh sorrow or secret loves; the lawyer assumes she dressed for this evening with intent and might already be in love. The Colonel, by contrast, takes the socially proper stance: he won’t pursue a married woman. Yet he allows himself to be baited into a wager, not by desire but by esprit de corps and the gambler’s love of a stake. Martial, ever the strategist, both suspects and relishes the risks.

Madame de Vaudremont herself continues to manage her display. She has the habitual tactic of arriving late to appear always at her best and to measure others; she has an artful face and dress that she plans carefully to outshine rivals. Her reaction to Martial’s distraction—sorrowful then teasing—is both a personal complaint and a public performance, for she needs to maintain her precedence and ensure nothing undermines the alliance that benefits her as well as her suitor. When she begs Martial to remove the ring, it is with reasons she reserves for private revelation; for now, she must endure his absent-mindedness in public.

As the evening progresses, multiple tensions converge: Martial’s daggers of ambition and flirtation; Montcornet’s protectiveness and conventionalism; the Colonel’s pride and curbed passion; Soulanges’ self-destructive gambling; the stranger’s inscrutable sorrow; Madame de Vaudremont’s calculated management of her advantage. The ball’s brightness hides these private struggles, but attentive observers—those who know how to read the smallest glances—detect the threads tying these lives together.

Thus the imbroglio begins: the men’s idle curiosity about a sad, pale woman becomes a test of character and will, revealing the social codes and private urgencies that govern behavior in salons where appearances are everything. The candelabrum’s little blue stranger, the diamond ring, the wagers, the entrance with Soulanges—all set the scene for events in which personal honor, ambition, and the fragile inventions of society will collide. The bouillotte table’s piles of gold—Soulanges’ reckless winnings—offer the darker underside of the evening’s light displays, a reminder that beneath frivolity often lurk despair and ruin.

“Courage,” the Colonel cried, tugging Soulanges away from the gaming table as if to help him. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“That you will answer a question,” Montcornet added.

The Comte de Soulanges, gathering his winnings with a grim expression, rose abruptly. Montcornet drew him aside. “The Emperor praised you this morning; your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

Soulanges only scowled. “The Master does not love the Artillery.”

“No, but he adores nobility, and you are an aristocrat. He said men who married in Paris during the campaign are not disgraced. Well?”

Montcornet then asked whether Soulanges knew the charming woman beneath a huge candelabrum. The Count’s face brightened; he gripped Montcornet’s hand. “If anyone else had asked me that, I would have cracked his skull,” he said, then, with sudden despair: “Leave me. I feel more like blowing out my brains tonight than— I hate everything I see. This gaiety, this music, these stupid faces, all laughing, are killing me!”

Montcornet tried to soothe him. “You are too vehement. What would you say if I told you Martial is so taken with that little lady that he is smitten?”

“If he speaks to her,” Soulanges shouted, trembling with rage, “I will thrash him flat, even if he’s in the Emperor’s lap!” He sank into a chair; Montcornet, seeing him beyond consolation, left him to his fury.

Back in the ballroom Montcornet found Madame de Vaudremont visibly agitated and sat beside her. She feigned irritation but was deeply distressed: she intended to go on to other balls but had to stop at the Princesse de Wagram’s; Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon was amusing himself by flirting with dowagers. Montcornet teased her and guessed the truth—she was watching Martial and feared he would fall at the feet of the pale stranger under the candelabrum. The Countess admitted it. Old Madame de Lansac had told her that de la Roche-Hugon was flirting with that pale, cruelly beautiful woman, likening her to a skeleton. She called the stranger her evil genius and threatened Martial with vengeance if he made her unhappy.

Montcornet confessed he had just left a man who promised to blow his brains out if he spoke to that lady—Soulanges, a man to keep his word. But he also knew Martial: threats only encouraged him. He lowered his voice—there was a wager in play. The Countess, eager to know the stranger’s identity, agreed to dance with Montcornet later; for now she would watch what happened.

At such entertainments there are always women like Madame de Lansac, older observers who take a perverse interest in the young. The Duchess—sharp-eyed and mischievous—saw through the Countess’s bravado. Madame de Vaudremont put on gaiety while feeling as much sorrow as she feigned: she had hoped Martial would bring power and love but realized painfully she had been mistaken. The Duchess, detecting every subtle movement, read the Countess’s heart and, amused, resolved to intervene.

Madame de Lansac sat rouged and diamonded in a large armchair, her sharp nose and ironical smile promising epigrams. Her politeness concealed a mischievous mind; she watched the drama with a triumphal glance that said “I told you so,” and signaled toward the stranger. That look crossed the room and lit some hope in the pale young woman beneath the chandelier. The Comtesse de Vaudremont noticed the alliance forming between the Duchess and the unknown and, wary, wanted to know more.

The Baron de la Roche-Hugon, having questioned the dowagers without success, turned to the Comtesse de Gondreville and learned only that the Duchess had introduced the blue lady. Seizing the chance when the Duchess sent a meaningful look, the Baron approached despite past quarrels with the old woman. Sensing his motive, the Duchess smiled with sardonic irony and glanced at Madame de Vaudremont, making Montcornet laugh.

“Madame,” the Baron said, “you have, I am told, undertaken the charge of a very precious treasure.”

“Do you take me for a dragon?” she replied, then sweetly asked whose treasure he meant. “Of that little lady, unknown to all, whom the jealousy of these coquettes has imprisoned in that corner. You, no doubt, know her family?”

“Yes,” the Duchess said easily. “But what concern have you with a provincial heiress, married some time ago, a woman of good birth, who goes nowhere?”

The Baron begged: Why does she not dance? She is so pretty. Will the Duchess tell him who she is in exchange for his helping petition the Commissioners of Crown Lands for restitution of the woods of Navarreins? It was a crafty bait: the Baron invoked the Duchess’s past influence at Court—her liaison with Louis XV—and dangled political advantage to open her lips.

“Monsieur,” she answered, with feigned gravity, “bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to me. I promise to reveal the mystery. Every man here is curious; all eyes turn to my protégée. Happy the man she chooses for her partner!” She fixed the Countess with a look that plainly said, “We are talking of you.” “I imagine you would rather hear the stranger’s name from your own lips than mine.”

There was defiance in the Duchess’s tone. Madame de Vaudremont rose, took the chair Martial offered, and, without acknowledging him, said to the Duchess that she suspected as much but did not know if it was good or bad. The Duchess, pressing the young woman’s hand, replied softly, “Poor child!”

They spoke privately. Madame de Vaudremont dismissed Martial with a commanding “Leave us.” The Baron, annoyed to see the Countess under the duchess’s influence, gave one of those potent looks men use to sway hearts—effective to the blinded, laughable to the newly critical woman.

Martial was a man of the world—witty, urbane—and would not needlessly quarrel with a woman favored at Court. He hoped the Emperor wanted her married; he counted on stirring jealousy to uncover her reasons for coolness and withdrew willingly as a new quadrille began. The Baron, folding his arms against a marble console, watched the two ladies. Torn between a near fortune and the thrill of caprice, his face took on a sullen, anxious look; observers might have called him an evil genius relishing the spectacle.

Montcornet, leaning in the doorway between the ballroom and the cardroom, smiled beneath his mustache. He enjoyed the agitation—the Countess’s distress, Martial’s intrigue, Soulanges’ gloom tied to the fair unknown whose expression shifted between hope and dread. As a Colonel of the Imperial Guard he stood like the king of the feast, receiving inviting smiles and counting as one of the best matches in the army.

It was nearly midnight. The ballroom swelled with conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries, and schemes. Madame de Lansac, playing the indulgent old woman who knows youth errs, chose this moment to give the Countess harsh counsel. “You are at the age when I made many mistakes,” she began. “To go wrong at twenty-two is to spoil your future. Make clever enemies and friends without a sense of conduct, and you will see what a pleasant life you lead!” The Countess, eager for guidance, asked earnestly whether it is very hard for a woman to be happy.

The Duchess answered with cold, practical frankness. “At your age you must choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is neither wise enough to be a good husband nor passionate enough to remain a lover. He’s in debt; he’ll squander your fortune. He may be ill; he is making the most of what remains. In three years he will be a wreck. He may be ambitious later—perhaps—but I doubt it. What is he? A man of intrigue who gossips agreeably, but he is too presumptuous for real merit. Besides, look at him; is it not written on his brow that what he sees in you is not your youth or beauty but your two million francs? He doesn’t love you; he values you as an investment. If you are bent on marrying, find an older man with an assured position, one halfway in his career. A widow’s marriage should be a sound alliance; it should be a good speculation. Marry well, and at least hope to be addressed as Madame la Marechale.”

Her counsel was blunt and strategic: Martial is short-term comfort and long-term danger to the Countess’s fortune and happiness. Marry an older, secure man whose position promises steadiness and respect rather than the risky charm and debts of Martial.

Thus, at the ball’s climax, intrigue centered on the pale stranger beneath the candelabrum and the private maneuverings around her: Soulanges’ desperate jealousy and threat, Montcornet’s teasing and protective manipulations, Madame de Vaudremont’s hurt and anger, the Baron’s opportunism, and Madame de Lansac’s deliberate orchestration. The Duchess used the social stage to control disclosures and steer a young woman’s destiny, offering ruthless but practical wisdom about love, marriage, and social advantage in a world where appearances mask calculation and fortunes hinge on prudent choices.

As she spoke, both women turned to Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

“If you prefer to flirt rather than marry again,” the Duchess said bluntly, “you, better than any woman, know how to stir up trouble and then calm it. But don’t destroy families or ruin wives’ happiness. I’ve played that dangerous game. For a moment of vanity, a virtuous woman can be destroyed—there are truly virtuous women—and we can become mortally hated. I learned too late that, as the Duc d’Albe said, one salmon is worth a thousand frogs! Genuine affection brings far more happiness than fleeting passion. I came to preach to you; you are the reason for my visit to this lower-class house. Have I not just seen actors here? We used to receive them in our boudoir, but never in the drawing-room! If you want to play with men, toy only with those whose lives are unsettled and who have no duties. The others won’t forgive us for the errors that make them happy. Take this advice from my long experience. Look at Soulanges, whom you’ve made lovesick for the past fifteen months. Do you realize what you’ve done? You’ve affected his entire life. He’s been married two years and is adored by a charming wife whom he loves but neglects; she lives in tears. Soulanges has felt remorse far worse than any pleasure you’ve given him. And you, you little minx, have abandoned him. Come see the result of your actions.”

The old lady took Madame de Vaudremont’s hand and they rose.

“There,” Madame de Lansac said, pointing to a pale, trembling figure under the candlelight, “that is my grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges. Today she finally yielded to my persuasion and left the sorrowful room where her child brings her little consolation. You think her charming? Imagine how she must have looked when happiness and love illuminated that now-blighted face.”

The Countess looked away, lost in sad thought.

The Duchess led her to the card-room door, then, scanning the room, said, “And there is Soulanges!”

The Countess shuddered at the sight of Soulanges, pale and rigid in an easy chair. His indifference and the tension in his brow revealed his suffering. The players passed him by, ignoring him as if he were dead. The image of the tearful wife and the morose husband, separated amidst festivity, struck the Countess with a sense of foreboding about her own future. Though her heart was not entirely hardened, she pressed the Duchess’s hand, thanking her with a childlike smile.

“My dear child,” the old lady whispered, “remember that we can repel a man’s attentions just as easily as we can attract them.”

“She is yours if you’re not a fool,” Madame de Lansac murmured to Colonel Montcornet while the Countess remained absorbed in her compassion for Soulanges, still loving him enough to wish to restore his happiness.

“Oh! I will talk to him!” the Countess declared.

“Do nothing of the sort!” the Duchess exclaimed as she returned to her chair. “Choose a good husband and shut your door to my nephew. A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back. By bringing my niece here, I believe I’ve given her a chance to regain her husband’s affection. All I need from you is to play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet, and the Countess smiled.

Later, when alone with Martial, she amusedly confirmed she finally knew the name of the unknown. Her smile and bright eyes played on him; believing she still loved him, Martial adopted a charming demeanor.

“Will you be annoyed if I seem to care about that name?” he asked.

“Will you be annoyed if a remnant of affection prevents me from telling you? And if I forbid you to approach that young lady? It could be dangerous.”

“To lose your favor would be worse than losing my life.”

“Martial,” the Countess said sternly, “she is Madame de Soulanges. Her husband would kill you if he knew.”

“Ha! So the Colonel can leave the man in peace who has taken his love, yet would fight for his wife! What a reversal of principles! I beg you to let me dance with her. You’ll see how little love that icy heart could feel for you; if the Colonel allows me to dance with his wife after letting me—”

“But she loves her husband.”

“An even greater obstacle I shall enjoy overcoming.”

“But she is married.”

“A whimsical objection!”

“Ah!” the Countess said with a bitter smile, “you punish us for both our faults and our repentance!”

“Do not be angry!” Martial exclaimed eagerly. “Forgive me, I beseech you. I will think no more of Madame de Soulanges.”

“You deserve that I should send you to her.”

“I’m off then,” he laughed, “and I’ll return more devoted to you than ever. You’ll see that the prettiest woman cannot capture the heart that is yours.”

“Meaning you want to win Colonel Montcornet’s horse?”

“Ah! Traitor!” he said, playfully threatening his friend. The Colonel smiled and joined them; the Baron gave him a seat near the Countess, saying with a sardonic tone, “Here, madame, is a man who boasted he could win your favor in one evening.”

He left, thinking he had cleverly piqued the Countess’ pride and done Montcornet a disservice, unaware of the irony in Madame de Vaudremont’s words, as both were unconsciously moving toward each other.

At that moment, the lawyer approached Madame de Soulanges, who sat pale and timid, seemingly alive only in her eyes. Her husband entered the ballroom, his eyes flashing with anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, rushed to her nephew, asking for his arm to find her carriage, feigning boredom to prevent a scene. Before leaving, she shot a knowing glance at her niece, signaling her to take revenge.

Madame de Vaudremont noticed the exchange and felt a sudden realization; she feared being the target of the Duchess’s cunning.

“That duplicitous Duchess,” she thought, “may be preaching morality while plotting against me.”

This thought stirred her pride more than curiosity. In her distraction she lost composure; the Colonel misread her embarrassment and became more ardent. The diplomats, entertained by the drama, had never seen so many intrigues at once. The passions of the two couples became evident in the crowded rooms, reflected on the faces around them, amplifying their own feelings of helplessness.

Finally, the Baron found a seat beside Madame de Soulanges. He admired her beauty close at hand, captivated by her pale neck and graceful figure. Women's sashes were tied under the bosom then, a style flattering to perfect figures. As he stole glances at the Countess, Martial was enchanted by her perfection.

“You haven’t danced once this evening, madame,” he said in soft, flattering tones. “Not for lack of a partner, I presume?”

“I never go to parties; I’m quite unknown,” Madame de Soulanges replied coldly, unaware of her aunt’s signal to attract the Baron.

Martial, adjusting the diamond on his hand, felt a spark of hope as he caught the Countess’s eye. She blushed and looked at him with an indefinable expression.

“Do you enjoy dancing?” he asked to continue the conversation.

“Yes, very much, monsieur.”

Their eyes met, and the earnestness in her voice stirred him. “Then, am I too bold in offering to be your partner for the next quadrille?”

Artless confusion colored her cheeks.

“But, monsieur, I have already refused one partner—a military man—”

“Was it that tall cavalry colonel over there?”

“Precisely.”

“Oh! He’s a friend of mine; don’t worry. Will you grant me the favor I hope for?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

Her tone revealed a new and deep emotion that touched Martial’s world-weary heart. Overcome with shyness, he lost his confidence, and his southern charm ignited. He tried to speak, but his words felt clumsy compared to Madame de Soulanges’ bright replies. Fortunately, the quadrille was forming, and standing beside her eased his nerves. For many men, dancing is a way to express themselves, believing they can win a woman’s heart through physical grace. Martial likely intended to showcase all his charms, judging by his pretentious movements and gestures.

He led his conquest to the quadrille, where the brightest women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra began, the Baron felt proud surveying the ladies. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled Madame de Vaudremont’s opposite her. Eyes turned to Madame de Soulanges; a flattering murmur followed and her blush only increased her charm. When she raised her eyes she sought her partner, seeming to transfer the glory of admiration to him; she combined innocence, modesty, and the artful glance of youth, knowing when to look at Martial.

When a new figure, invented by a dancer named Trenis, brought Martial face to face with Colonel Montcornet, he joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial waved it off: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

After the quadrille whispers circulated. Less attractive women moralized about Martial and the Comtesse; the prettier admired the Countess’s apparent ease in surrendering attention to him. The men, unable to explain the Baron’s luck, found him unremarkable. A few women defended the Countess, arguing that young wives would suffer if a single graceful dance could stain a woman’s reputation.

Only Martial understood the depth of his happiness. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand and felt her response through the thin glove. “Madame,” he said as the quadrille ended, “do not return to that corner where you’ve been hiding. Is admiration the only reward you gain from your jewels? Come and enjoy the scene and yourself.”

She yielded to his assurance, and they crossed the crowded rooms. She hesitated at each doorway, peering into the next room and reassured only by his repeated “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery, where a lavish collation awaited three hundred guests. As supper neared, Martial led her to an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, fragrant with flowers and hung in blue. The hubbub faded; startled, the Countess at first resisted but finally sat on an ottoman after glancing in a mirror, perhaps reassured they might be seen.

“This room is charming,” she said. “All here is love and delight!” Martial watched her—unease, modesty, and eagerness that captivated him. She smiled, calming her inner struggle. Playfully she took his left hand and removed his ring, exclaiming, “What a fine diamond!”

Intoxicated, Martial looked at her with eyes bright as the gem. “Wear it,” he said, “in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He stopped, caught by her rapture. She asked, “You give it to me?” “I wish I had the whole world to offer you!” he replied. “Will you accept only my diamond?” She pressed, “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured.

She placed the ring on her finger, and as Martial prepared to embrace her she suddenly stood and said, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.” The Baron was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it.”

“You are mistaken, madame,” Martial said, irritated. “It was given to me by Madame de Vaudremont.” “Precisely,” she replied, smiling. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gifted it to you. This ring has made a little journey, that is all. It may reveal secrets to me.” She touched a spring in the ring; a tiny compartment opened, showing a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair. Startled and affronted, she fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded. Nearby the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughed; the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial accepted their banter gratefully, relieved they kept silent about the evening’s other matters, as he exchanged a horse for a young wife.

Crossing Paris to the Faubourg Saint-Germain, the Comtesse’s anxiety deepened. Before leaving the Hôtel Gondreville she had searched for her aunt and husband, who had departed without her. Disturbing suspicions plagued her. She had silently witnessed her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont ensnared him and had hoped for his repentance. Now she feared she had agreed to her aunt’s scheme and ruined herself.

The evening had darkened her innocence. Alarmed first by her husband’s suffering, she grew more distressed seeing her rival’s beauty and feeling the weight of social corruption. Crossing the Pont Royal, she cast away the lock of hair found in the ring—what she had kept as a remnant of pure affection. Tears came as she remembered long-standing grief and the thought that a woman in search of peace must bury her pain without complaint.

“Alas!” she thought, “What can women do when they do not love? What sustains their devotion? I cannot believe, as my aunt claims, that reason alone is enough.”

Still sighing, she arrived home and rushed upstairs, startled to find her husband by the fire. “How long has it been since you attended balls without telling me?” he asked, his voice strained. “You know a woman is out of place without her husband. You compromised yourself by hiding in the dark corner.”

“Oh, my dear Léon,” she replied coaxingly, “I couldn’t resist the joy of seeing you without your knowing. My aunt took me to the ball, and I was very happy there!”

Her words softened the Count’s sternness; he had feared she would learn of his infidelity and had tried to be the first to blame her. Delighted to see him smile, the Countess looked at him tenderly; her simplicity and grace restored his mood. He took her hand and kissed it gratefully—gratitude often accompanies love. She explained the diamond’s story; he laughed at the bruise it had made on his lip and the scene closed on reconciliation rather than scandal.

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont; she later perished in the tragic fire that marred the ball given by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to the daughter of Emperor Joseph II.
