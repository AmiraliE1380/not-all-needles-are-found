**EUGENIE GRANDET**

**I**

In certain provincial towns, some houses evoke profound melancholy, reminiscent of cloisters or ruins. One such house in Saumur, at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau, embodies this sadness. The narrow, seldom-traveled street is lined with ancient homes over three centuries old, their carved oaken beams and worn window-sills narrating the history of France.

Next to a dilapidated artisan's home stands the mansion of Monsieur Grandet, a country gentleman whose door bears remnants of armorial bearings. The ground-floors of merchants here are dark, deep rooms without glass, where goods are displayed simply. The inhabitants of Saumur, deeply connected to the land, are acutely aware of the weather's impact on their livelihoods, with conversations revolving around harvests and market conditions.

On Saturdays, merchants leave for their vineyards, leaving the streets empty. Life unfolds outdoors, with neighbors observing each other closely. The old town of Saumur, once home to local nobility, houses the Grandet family, whose story is central to this narrative.

Monsieur Grandet, known as le Pere Grandet, was a master cooper who became wealthy during the Revolution by purchasing church properties. With his wife’s dowry, he acquired prime vineyards and farms, becoming a respected figure in Saumur. His wealth grew through shrewd investments and a keen understanding of the wine trade, making him a significant player in the local economy.

By 1816, Grandet's estate was estimated at nearly four million francs, with many believing he had hidden treasures. His reputation was built on financial acumen and market manipulation, earning him both admiration and fear. He lived frugally, relying on his land for sustenance and employing only a few trusted individuals.

Grandet's demeanor was simple and unassuming, yet he commanded respect. He rarely spoke, preferring short, decisive phrases. His stutter, often mistaken for a lack of education, was a calculated facade. He was a man of routine, dressed consistently in practical clothing, and his life revolved around his daughter, Eugenie, whom he intended to marry off advantageously.

The social dynamics of Saumur were influenced by the competition for Eugenie's hand in marriage. Two families, the Cruchots and the des Grassins, vied for her favor, each believing they could secure a more advantageous match. The Cruchots, led by the ambitious C. de Bonfons, sought to align with the Grandets through marriage, while the des Grassins, represented by the charming Adolphe, aimed to win Eugenie’s heart.

As the story unfolds, the tension between these families escalates, with stakes raised by the potential inheritance of Eugenie’s fortune. The arrival of the estate of Froidfond for sale creates further intrigue, as both factions see it as a means to elevate their status and secure Eugenie’s future.

Monsieur Grandet, seizing the opportunity, purchases the estate, further solidifying his wealth and influence. This acquisition sends ripples through Saumur, as townspeople speculate on the implications for the Grandet family and the ongoing competition for Eugenie’s hand.

In this world of ambition, avarice, and social maneuvering, the fate of Eugenie Grandet hangs in the balance, reflecting the complexities of love, wealth, and familial duty in a changing society.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur Grandet's house is a cold, silent tufa stone structure resembling a jail, with a weathered archway and crumbling bas-relief. The entrance features a solid oak door with a rusty grating, leading to a dark vault and a damp garden enclosed by thick walls. The main room serves multiple purposes—antechamber, salon, office, and dining room—where local townsfolk conduct business with Grandet. Adorned with gray wooden panels, an old brass clock, and faded tapestries, the hall reflects the family's modest lifestyle.

Madame Grandet and their daughter, Eugenie, live under strict conditions, with Grandet tightly controlling their resources. They manage household tasks diligently, often working late into the night. Their servant, La Grande Nanon, has worked for thirty-five years, earning a meager wage but saving enough for her future. Despite her rough appearance, she is devoted to Grandet, who treats her with disdain yet grudging affection.

In November 1819, the family prepares to celebrate Eugenie's birthday. Grandet gifts her a gold coin, while Madame Grandet presents a new dress. Their dinner is interrupted by local notables, including the Cruchots and the des Grassins, who bring gifts for Eugenie. The des Grassins' son, Adolphe, delights her with a workbox, while Grandet remains focused on wealth and marriage prospects for Eugenie.

As the evening progresses with card games, tensions among the guests emerge, all vying for Grandet's favor. The atmosphere blends familial warmth with cold calculations of social status and financial gain. The night takes a turn when a loud knock at the door interrupts the festivities, signaling an unexpected visitor and heightening the tension.

Nanon lit a candle and opened the door for her master, Monsieur Grandet, who was called by his alarmed wife while the players exchanged worried glances. Sensing something ominous, Monsieur des Grassins suggested they leave. When a young man arrived with trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game, leaving the players uneasy.

The newcomer, Charles Grandet from Paris, was handsome and stylish, contrasting sharply with the provincial attire of the others. He greeted the company gracefully, but Grandet's stern demeanor overshadowed the welcome. Madame Grandet offered hospitality, but Grandet insisted he needed no refreshment.

Visiting Saumur for the first time, Charles aimed to impress with his Parisian sophistication, bringing an extravagant collection of clothing and accessories, unaware of the modest lifestyle that awaited him. The provincial guests, including the Cruchots and des Grassins, were taken aback by his elegance, feeling both admiration and disdain. Eugenie Grandet, Charles's cousin, was particularly captivated, longing for the refinement he represented, contrasting sharply with her mundane life.

As the game of loto concluded, Nanon announced preparations for Charles's room. Eager to impress, Eugenie rushed to help, suggesting improvements for his comfort, even proposing to buy a wax candle, a luxury her father would typically deny.

Meanwhile, Madame des Grassins attempted to charm Charles, hinting at social gatherings to alleviate his boredom in Saumur. She subtly criticized Grandet's miserly nature, positioning herself as a potential matchmaker.

As the evening progressed, Grandet received a letter revealing a family tragedy: his brother was bankrupt and dying, leaving Charles in a precarious situation. The letter implored Grandet to care for Charles, emphasizing urgency. Grandet's reaction was mixed, concealing his thoughts while contemplating the implications of his brother's demise.

After the guests departed, Grandet's demeanor shifted, revealing a man grappling with familial duty and financial responsibility. Charles's arrival disrupts the provincial status quo, igniting curiosity and ambition among the local elite while unveiling the burdens of family legacy and expectations of wealth.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur Grandet, a miser, instructs his nephew Charles on household routines upon his arrival, emphasizing the family's hard work and lack of wealth despite rumors of riches. Charles feels out of place in his uncle's shabby home and is warned about the ferocious dog, questioning why his father sent him there.

As Charles settles in, he reflects on his visit's purpose. Meanwhile, Eugenie, Grandet's daughter, becomes aware of her feelings for Charles while preparing for the day. She admires her appearance but doubts her beauty, feeling unworthy of him.

Eugenie's morning is consumed with thoughts of Charles, and she asks Nanon, the servant, to prepare a special breakfast to impress him. Grandet, however, is stingy and engages in a comical argument with Nanon over provisions. Despite his frugality, he reluctantly allows Nanon to make a galette for the young ones.

Later, Grandet takes Eugenie for a walk, where they meet Cruchot, the notary. Their conversation reveals Grandet's disdain for the idea of marrying Eugenie to Charles, stating he would rather throw her into the Loire than allow such a union. This devastates Eugenie, who has begun to develop feelings for Charles.

Grandet soon receives news of his brother's suicide, shocking the family. Eugenie is deeply affected, feeling compassion for Charles, who remains unaware of his father's fate. Grandet insists Eugenie should not inform Charles until he returns, revealing his cold-heartedness.

Overwhelmed, Eugenie confides in her mother, who understands but urges her to respect her father's wishes. Despite the tension, Eugenie expresses her affection for Charles and desires to prepare a special breakfast for him, highlighting her growing attachment amid financial hardship and emotional turmoil.

After two hours of anxious preparation, Eugenie creates an unusual breakfast for Charles, deviating from their typical meal of bread, fruit, and wine. She sets the table carefully, worried about her father's reaction if he returned to find such a spread. Madame Grandet reassures her, promising to take the blame if necessary. When Charles arrives, flamboyantly dressed, he greets them cheerfully despite the recent destruction of his family estate.

As they eat, Charles expresses a desire for more extravagant food, making Eugenie wish she could provide him with a partridge. Their conversation highlights the contrast between Charles's Parisian lifestyle and Eugenie's simple country life. Charles compliments Eugenie, causing her to blush, and they share a moment of connection. However, the mood shifts when Nanon brings in coffee, prompting Charles to comment on their outdated brewing method.

The atmosphere darkens with the familiar knock of Monsieur Grandet. The women panic, quickly hiding evidence of the breakfast. Grandet enters, scrutinizing the scene and chastising them for the lavish meal, unaware of its significance to Charles. As he prepares his own meager breakfast, he notices the sugar and questions its source, leading to a tense silence.

Charles, oblivious to the tension, inquires about his father's health, only to learn of his father's suicide. The revelation shatters him, prompting sympathy from Eugenie and her mother, while Grandet remains detached, focusing on financial implications. Moved by Charles's grief, Eugenie feels a growing affection for him, encouraging him to think of his honor as they sew in silence, contemplating wearing mourning for her uncle, indicating her deepening connection to Charles and their shared grief.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At around four o'clock, a sharp knock startled Madame Grandet. She felt relieved when her husband, Grandet, entered joyfully, announcing he had sold his wine to a Belgian for a good profit. However, when Eugenie suggested helping her grieving cousin Charles, Grandet's mood soured. He scolded her for even considering spending money on him, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again.

Consumed by thoughts of investments, Grandet retreated to his secret den to calculate his wealth. Meanwhile, Charles mourned in his room, refusing to eat. Grandet dismissed the idea of mourning attire, insisting true mourning was internal. The family dinner was tense, with Madame Grandet suggesting they wear mourning, but Grandet scoffed at the expense.

Feeling compassion for Charles, Eugenie secretly visited him, offering comfort. She admired his handsome face, now marked by grief, awakening a desire for love she had never known.

The next morning, Grandet's demeanor shifted; he appeared more thoughtful and less harsh. He planned to manipulate the situation to his advantage, intending to help Charles while safeguarding his financial interests. He devised a scheme to impress the townspeople without spending his own money.

Eugenie busied herself caring for Charles, bringing him breakfast and attending to his needs. Their bond deepened as they shared tender moments amidst their sorrows. Grateful for her kindness, Charles began to see Eugenie in a new light, appreciating her innocence and compassion.

As the day progressed, Grandet prepared to host a dinner for the Cruchots, signaling a rare moment of generosity. The household buzzed with activity, and Charles, though still grieving, joined them with quiet dignity. The atmosphere was charged with unspoken emotions, as Eugenie and Charles exchanged glances filled with understanding and hope.

At this moment, the town of Saumur was more focused on Grandet's dinner for the Cruchots than on the previous night's controversial wine sale. Grandet, a shrewd miser, disregarded public opinion, viewing himself as superior to the community he often deceived.

The des Grassins family learned of Guillaume Grandet's death and decided to visit that evening to express condolences and understand his motives for inviting the Cruchots. The dinner atmosphere was somber, with Grandet grave, Charles silent, and Eugenie and Madame Grandet saying little. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to speak privately with the Cruchots.

Grandet, known for his cunning, used his stutter and feigned deafness to manipulate the conversation, avoiding responsibility. He discussed bankruptcy with Monsieur C. de Bonfons, who tried to guide him through insolvency complexities. While pretending to be confused, Grandet assessed how to navigate his brother's financial troubles without damaging his own reputation.

The Cruchots suggested that Grandet could liquidate his brother's affairs to preserve family honor. However, Grandet expressed reluctance to engage in matters outside his expertise, insisting he was merely a wine-grower focused on his vines. The conversation shifted to the possibility of buying his brother's debts at a reduced rate, which intrigued Grandet but seemed complicated.

As the discussion progressed, the notary cautioned against compromising integrity. Just then, the des Grassins arrived, offering condolences for the Grandet family's misfortunes. The notary seized the moment to assert that Grandet intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably, surprising the des Grassins, who had expected selfishness.

Impressed by Grandet's supposed nobility, the des Grassins offered to help manage the financial situation, but Grandet preferred to work with them rather than the Cruchots. He confided in the banker about his interest in investing in government bonds, indicating a desire to profit while maintaining a façade of familial duty.

After the des Grassins left, Grandet instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey to sell his gold in Angers, taking advantage of rising prices due to military preparations. His meticulousness ensured that no one suspected his true intentions as he departed under the cover of night. Grandet's dinner highlighted his manipulation of those around him, reflecting a complex interplay of greed, deception, and the desire to uphold a facade of honor amidst personal gain.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Eugenie stood at the top of the stairs, reflecting on her father’s departure. The house was silent until she heard a cry from her cousin Charles's room. Concerned, she rushed upstairs to find him asleep in an armchair, exhausted from writing letters. She noticed addresses of various businesses, realizing he was preparing to leave France. Her heart sank upon spotting a letter addressed to "My dear Annette," igniting feelings of jealousy and despair.

Despite her resolve not to read it, curiosity overwhelmed her. The letter revealed Charles's tragic situation: his father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute. He expressed his determination to seek fortune in the Indies, lamenting that he could not return to Paris, where he would face scorn as the son of a bankrupt. Eugenie felt compassion for him and vowed to help.

Continuing to read, she discovered that Charles had no money for his journey or even a proper outfit. He contemplated sailing as a common sailor if necessary. The letter also revealed his deep love for Annette but his decision to part ways for her sake, believing it would be better for both. This filled Eugenie with joy, interpreting it as a sign of his love for her.

Eugenie then found another letter addressed to Alphonse, where Charles detailed his financial ruin and requested assistance in settling his affairs. Moved by his situation, Eugenie decided to offer him her savings, nearly six thousand francs. She approached Charles, who was initially hesitant to accept her gift.

Eugenie insisted, kneeling before him and imploring him to take the money. Overcome with emotion, Charles eventually agreed, recognizing their deep bond. He then revealed a family heirloom, a dressing case containing portraits of his parents, asking her to keep it safe. Their exchange solidified their connection, transcending mere familial ties.

As they shared this intimate moment, both felt a shift in their relationship. Charles began to see hope in their bond, while Eugenie vowed to support him. The next day, they spent time together, their mutual affection growing stronger amidst family struggles.

Eugenie's father, Grandet, returned home with news of his financial dealings, having profited from the sale of gold. His jovial demeanor contrasted sharply with Charles's somber reality, highlighting disparities in their lives. As the day unfolded, tension between the characters became palpable. Charles grappled with his newfound responsibilities, while Eugenie remained steadfast in her support. Their relationship blossomed against societal expectations, setting the stage for future challenges. Ultimately, both found solace in each other, their bond deepening as they navigated love, loss, and harsh realities.

The next day, the Grandet family gathered for breakfast, creating a scene of domestic intimacy. Grief united Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles, while the old man, focused on his avarice, remained indifferent. He busied himself with his land and vineyards, allowing the young couple to express their feelings under Madame Grandet's watchful eye.

Eugenie experienced the awakening of love, having shared a secret with Charles that deepened their bond. Their relationship blossomed amidst mourning, as they found joy in each other's company, exchanging tender glances and words. Charles, who had known only tumultuous love, now embraced a pure affection for Eugenie and even the old house, which he had once found ridiculous.

After breakfast, while Grandet worked, Charles enjoyed the simple pleasure of being with Eugenie and her mother, appreciating their beauty. Eugenie became his ideal, reminiscent of Goethe's Margaret before her fall. Their innocent love was tinged with the awareness of impending separation.

Three days after des Grassins' departure, Grandet took Charles to the Civil courts to renounce his rights to his father's estate, a painful act for Charles. He also arranged for a passport and sold his belongings, which pleased Grandet. Dressed in mourning clothes, Charles appeared more mature, and the family attended a Requiem for Guillaume Grandet.

During breakfast, Charles received letters from Paris, prompting Eugenie to inquire about his affairs. Grandet, irritated by the question, insisted on privacy. Later, in the garden, Charles revealed that he was preparing to leave for the Indies and expressed concern for Eugenie's future, suggesting she might find happiness elsewhere.

Eugenie declared her love for him, and they shared a passionate kiss, interrupted by Nanon. As Charles prepared to leave, Grandet showed unexpected generosity, taking an interest in his nephew's departure and arranging for his belongings to be sent to Nantes.

On the day of Charles's departure, sadness filled the air. Nanon, usually cheerful, shed tears for him. The family escorted Charles to the diligence, where Grandet offered parting words of encouragement. As the coach departed, Eugenie wished for divine power to keep Charles close.

Meanwhile, Grandet engaged in financial dealings in Paris, acquiring a significant fortune through investments. His cunning allowed him to manage his brother's debts while maintaining his reputation. Over time, creditors grew impatient, but Grandet skillfully delayed payments, leveraging their changing moods to his advantage.

As months passed, many creditors forgot their claims, and Grandet continued to manipulate the situation, ultimately selling his investments for a substantial profit. Des Grassins, now living in Paris, faced personal troubles, while Grandet distanced himself from any obligation to him.

In the aftermath of Charles's departure, Eugenie faced profound loneliness. She kept Charles's room as it was, hoping for his return. Her beauty transformed, reflecting the depth of her love. She followed his journey on a world map and mourned his absence while maintaining a facade of cheerfulness around others.

Eugenie's love became a constant presence in her life, overshadowing her daily activities. Nanon, sympathetic to her plight, expressed frustrations about love, highlighting the sacrifices women make. Despite the pain, Eugenie's devotion to Charles remained unwavering as she navigated her emotions in a world that felt increasingly empty without him.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Two months passed, and the once monotonous life of Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, was now filled with anxiety over a shared secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often gazed at her aunt's portrait, searching for traces of Charles in her features. One Sunday, her mother discovered Eugenie's secret exchange of gold for Charles's affection, leading to panic about how they would face Grandet on New Year’s Day when he would expect to see Eugenie's gold.

As the new year approached, dread consumed the women. Madame Grandet, feeling the weight of her husband's anger and their precarious situation, suggested they avoid confronting him directly. On January 1, 1820, the cold winter morning prompted Madame Grandet to request a fire in her room, hoping to delay their visit to Grandet. Surprisingly, he agreed, displaying an unusual good humor that bewildered both women.

Grandet's joy stemmed from a successful financial speculation, having received a substantial sum from Monsieur des Grassins. He reveled in his newfound wealth, oblivious to the turmoil within his family. As he prepared for breakfast, he expressed a desire to see Eugenie's gold, heightening the tension. Eugenie, feeling the weight of her secret, suggested they eat first to postpone the inevitable confrontation.

After breakfast, Grandet pressed Eugenie for her gold, insisting she return it. When she claimed she no longer had it, he erupted in anger, accusing her of deceit and theft. Desperate to protect her mother from Grandet's wrath, Eugenie fell at her mother's feet, pleading for mercy. Madame Grandet, weakened by the confrontation, was taken to bed, leaving Eugenie to face her father alone.

Grandet's fury escalated as he demanded to know what Eugenie had done with her gold. She maintained her silence, asserting her independence. Grandet, unable to comprehend her defiance, threatened to confine her to her room until she submitted to his authority. Heartbroken, Eugenie retreated to her mother, who tried to comfort her despite her own suffering.

The confrontation reached a boiling point, with Grandet cursing Eugenie and accusing her of giving her fortune to Charles. He refused to forgive her, insisting she confess where her gold had gone. Caught between her husband and daughter, Madame Grandet implored him to show compassion, but Grandet remained resolute in his anger.

As the evening wore on, Grandet dined alone for the first time in years, while Nanon, the servant, tried to comfort Eugenie. The tension in the household was palpable, with Madame Grandet's health deteriorating under the strain of the conflict. The arrival of guests for New Year’s festivities only highlighted the absence of the women, raising suspicions about the family's troubles.

In the quiet of the night, Nanon brought Eugenie a special treat, a hare pâté, determined that she would not suffer on dry bread alone. This small act of kindness amidst the turmoil provided a glimmer of hope for Eugenie, who felt the weight of her choices and the love she held for Charles. The family dynamics, marked by love, fear, and the struggle for power, set the stage for a tragic unfolding of events in their lives.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For months, the old wine-grower, Grandet, visited his wife’s room without mentioning their daughter, Eugenie, who was kept under harsh conditions. As Madame Grandet's health declined, townspeople speculated about the family's situation, eventually learning that Eugenie was being fed only bread and water by her father's orders. Despite the town's condemnation, Eugenie remained unaware of the gossip, focused on her love for Charles and her ailing mother.

As her death approached, Madame Grandet urged reconciliation between father and daughter, but Grandet remained indifferent. She confided in the Cruchots about Eugenie's treatment, prompting them to consider legal action against Grandet. However, Eugenie insisted on loyalty to her father, asking them to remain silent to protect family honor.

With spring nearing, Madame Grandet's condition worsened, and she sought help from the notary, Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her mother’s estate, potentially forcing him to sell his property. This revelation shocked Grandet, who had never considered the legal implications of his daughter's inheritance. Cruchot advised him to treat Eugenie kindly to avoid conflict over the estate.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Grandet decided to reconcile with Eugenie. However, upon entering their home, he found Eugenie and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold given to Eugenie by Charles. Driven by greed, Grandet attempted to seize the gold, leading to a confrontation where Eugenie threatened self-harm if he did not relent. 

In a moment of hesitation, Grandet backed down, allowing Eugenie to keep the dressing-case. He then attempted to show affection towards both his wife and daughter, scattering coins on the bed and promising to care for them. Despite his efforts, Madame Grandet passed away peacefully, leaving Eugenie to navigate a world filled with selfishness and sorrow. In her final moments, Madame Grandet imparted a poignant message about happiness, emphasizing that it lies in heaven.

The day after her mother’s death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home, despite its painful memories. Her father, M. Grandet, showed unexpected tenderness, which soon revealed itself as a façade for his self-serving agenda.

During a family dinner with notary Maitre Cruchot, M. Grandet informed Eugenie that she was now her mother’s heiress. He pressured her to renounce her rights to the estate, claiming it would spare him taxes and the burden of an inventory. Confused, Eugenie agreed, believing it would ease her father’s worries. M. Grandet promised her a monthly allowance of a hundred francs, which he never fulfilled, instead giving her a few jewels.

In the following years, M. Grandet trained Eugenie in managing the household and his finances, instilling his miserly habits in her. Their lives became monotonous, marked by daily routines devoid of joy. Eugenie’s only social interactions were with the Cruchot family and their friends, who played whist with her father. As M. Grandet aged and became frail, Eugenie devoted herself to caring for him after he suffered a stroke.

M. Grandet’s death was uneventful, overshadowed by his obsession with hidden wealth. In his final moments, he expressed concern for his treasures rather than for Eugenie. After his passing, she learned from Maitre Cruchot that she inherited a vast fortune, including three hundred thousand francs in income and millions in investments and gold. Yet, her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles.

Eugenie mourned her father and lost love in solitude, confiding only in her loyal servant, Nanon. Nanon’s marriage brought some joy, but Eugenie remained isolated in her grief. Despite her wealth, she felt no happiness, overshadowed by her mother’s suffering and her unfulfilled love for Charles.

As time passed, the Cruchots and other suitors surrounded Eugenie, eager to win her favor and wealth. They flattered her, but she remained indifferent, still longing for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years, deepening her emotional desolation despite her wealth.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles Grandet was amassing wealth in the Indies. His initial success of six thousand dollars revealed the profitability of the slave trade. He ventured to Africa, purchasing enslaved individuals and engaging in exploitative practices, including usury and smuggling. Initially inspired by his cousin Eugenie, he became hardened and lost sight of right and wrong, ultimately severing ties with his family.

In the Indies, he adopted the alias "Shepherd" to protect his identity while pursuing wealth through unscrupulous means. By 1827, he returned to Bordeaux aboard the "Marie Caroline," bringing gold-dust worth nearly two million francs. On the ship, he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, whose financial struggles stemmed from marrying into a fashionable family. The d’Aubrions were desperate for a wealthy son-in-law, and Madame d’Aubrion saw potential in Charles.

Upon arriving in Paris, Charles became close to the d’Aubrion family, who sought to marry him to their unattractive daughter. Madame d’Aubrion promised to help him gain a noble title and a lucrative court position, exciting Charles and overshadowing thoughts of Eugenie.

Meanwhile, Eugenie remained in Saumur, reflecting on her past with Charles. One day, she received a letter from him, which she hesitated to open. The letter revealed that Charles had moved on from their youthful love, expressing that he was free and engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, prioritizing ambition over their childhood promises. Devastated, Eugenie grappled with feelings of loss, recalling her mother’s prophetic words about suffering and resigning herself to a life of quiet anguish.

Eugenie returned from the garden, deliberately avoiding the corridor filled with memories of her cousin. The day was significant due to the parish priest's visit, who urged her to consider marriage as a means to fulfill her societal obligations, especially to the poor. Overwhelmed by her unhappiness, Eugenie expressed a desire to retreat from the world and devote herself to God.

Madame des Grassins interrupted with shocking news: Eugenie's cousin, Charles Grandet, had returned from the Indies and was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Despite her calm exterior, Eugenie felt turmoil inside. She read a letter from Madame des Grassins detailing Charles's financial troubles and the potential bankruptcy of his father, feeling betrayed as she returned the letter coldly.

Later, Eugenie asked the priest if it would be a sin to remain a virgin after marriage, revealing her conflicted feelings about the institution. After he left, she spent the day alone, preparing for the evening gathering at her home. The news of Charles's return had spread, and guests were curious about her reaction. Despite their scrutiny, Eugenie maintained her composure, hiding her pain behind a polite facade.

As the evening progressed, Eugenie made a surprising declaration to Monsieur de Bonfons, the president, asking him to stay. Guests interpreted this as a promise of marriage, solidifying her social position. Alone with the president, she stipulated that he must leave her free and not claim marital rights. In exchange for her hand, she asked him to pay off her uncle's creditors, offering a substantial sum for this.

The president agreed, recognizing the bitterness behind her request, and hurried to Paris to settle her debts. Upon his return, he presented Eugenie with the receipts, and she expressed gratitude, acknowledging the complexities of their relationship.

Meanwhile, Charles learned of Eugenie's actions and received a letter from her severing ties due to his engagement. She wished him happiness but emphasized that their love could not thrive within societal conventions. The president, now aware of the dynamics at play, felt a mix of triumph and concern.

Eugenie and the president's marriage was announced, and he quickly rose in social standing, becoming a judge and later a deputy. However, his ambitions were cut short when he died shortly after his election, leaving Eugenie a widow. Despite her wealth, she lived modestly, dedicating her life to charitable works and maintaining a simple lifestyle reminiscent of her past.

Eugenie's life became a testament to her resilience and compassion as she continued to support the less fortunate while grappling with emotional scars. Though pursued by suitors, including the Marquis de Froidfond, she remained steadfast in her independence, embodying a spirit that transcended societal expectations. Her story reflects the complexities of love, duty, and the pursuit of personal fulfillment amidst the constraints of her world.
