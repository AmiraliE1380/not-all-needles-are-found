EUGENIE GRANDET

I

In certain provincial towns, some houses evoke a melancholy reminiscent of cloisters, moorlands, or ruins. Within these homes, silence prevails, and life seems stagnant, interrupted only by the cold gaze of a solitary figure peering from a window.

Such a somber atmosphere characterized a house in Saumur, located at the end of a steep street leading to the chateau. This street, now seldom traveled, is notable for its clean, dry pebbly pavement and the peaceful stillness of its ancient houses, which date back three centuries. The houses, built of wood, display unique architectural features that attract artists and antiquarians.

As one walks past these homes, the enormous oaken beams, intricately carved, catch the eye. Some houses are adorned with slate, while others have worn window-sills barely able to support the pots of flowers. Doors are studded with large nails, bearing the marks of history, from Protestant symbols to the insignia of long-forgotten bourgeois families. The entire history of France is encapsulated in these structures.

Next to a dilapidated house, a country gentleman's mansion stands, its stone archway still showing traces of armorial bearings, worn by the revolutions since 1789. The ground floors of merchants' homes are not shops but simple, dark rooms with no glass fronts, where goods are displayed in a straightforward manner. A young girl, knitting, calls for her parents to assist customers, who are served with varying degrees of politeness.

In this region, the weather significantly impacts commerce. Merchants, wine-growers, and coopers closely monitor the skies, fearing frost or rain that could ruin their fortunes. On Saturdays, merchants leave for their vineyards, leaving the streets quiet, where life unfolds openly, with neighbors observing each other's activities.

The old town of Saumur, where the story unfolds, is home to a particularly melancholic mansion belonging to Monsieur Grandet. To understand the significance of this house, one must first know the man himself.

Monsieur Grandet was a master cooper in 1789, married to the daughter of a wealthy wood merchant. With his own savings and his wife's dowry, he purchased prime vineyard land during the sale of church properties. His reputation grew as he became a protector of the local nobility and a supplier to the Republican armies, gaining wealth and influence.

By 1806, Grandet had become mayor and continued to amass wealth through wise investments in vineyards and properties. He inherited three fortunes that year, further solidifying his status as a prominent figure in Saumur. His estate included vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him one of the wealthiest men in the region.

Grandet's wealth was a source of pride for the townspeople, who speculated about the extent of his riches. He was known for his shrewdness in business, always knowing when to sell his wine for maximum profit. His financial acumen made him a figure of both admiration and fear.

In 1816, estimates of Grandet's wealth reached nearly four million francs, with many believing he had an equal amount in cash. His reputation overshadowed any past ridicule, as his actions were now seen as law in the community. His frugality extended to his personal life; he rarely spent money on anything beyond necessities.

Grandet's demeanor was simple and unassuming. He spoke little, often stuttering when required to engage in lengthy conversations. His apparent lack of eloquence was a calculated facade, allowing him to maintain control in business dealings. He never committed to anything without consulting his wife, whom he kept in a state of subservience.

Physically, Grandet was a stout man, with a round face and a cunning expression. His attire was consistent and practical, reflecting his frugal nature. Only a few individuals had access to his home, including a young magistrate, C. de Bonfons, who was keen to marry Grandet's daughter, Eugenie.

The social dynamics in Saumur revolved around the competition for Eugenie's hand in marriage, primarily between the Cruchots and the des Grassins. The Cruchots, a powerful family, sought to marry Eugenie to their nephew, while the des Grassins aimed to secure a match for their son, Adolphe. This rivalry created a buzz in the community, with speculation about Grandet's true intentions for his daughter.

In early 1811, the Cruchots gained an advantage when the estate of Froidfond was put up for sale. They successfully negotiated the purchase of the estate for Grandet, who paid in gold, further enhancing his wealth and influence. This acquisition was a significant event, echoing throughout the region and solidifying Grandet's status as a formidable figure in Saumur.

It is now clear what is meant by “the house of Monsieur Grandet,” a cold, silent dwelling perched above the town, sheltered by the ruins of the ramparts. The entrance features two pillars and an arch made of tufa, a soft white stone that lasts only about two centuries. Numerous irregular holes, weathered by time, give the arch and walls a jail-like appearance. Above the arch is a crumbling bas-relief depicting the four seasons, topped by a plinth where various plants, including a small cherry tree, have taken root.

The door is made of solid oak, brown and split, yet secured by iron bolts. A small, rusted grating in the middle panel allows inquisitive visitors to glimpse a dark, slimy vault leading to a garden enclosed by thick, damp walls, remnants of the ramparts that also border neighboring gardens.

The most significant room on the ground floor is a large hall, serving multiple purposes: antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining room. It is where the barber comes to cut Monsieur Grandet’s hair and where local farmers and officials conduct business. The hall, with two street-facing windows, is entirely wooden, featuring gray panels and a ceiling of exposed beams. An old brass clock and a greenish mirror adorn the mantel above a white stone chimney. The room is decorated with antique chairs covered in faded tapestry, and at each corner are closets or buffets with dirty shelves.

In the hall, an old card table stands between the windows, topped with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. The windows are draped with red gros de Tours curtains, a luxury that contrasts with Monsieur Grandet’s frugality. A straw chair by the window allows Madame Grandet to observe passersby, while a work table and Eugenie’s armchair occupy the space. For fifteen years, the lives of Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet have revolved around constant work, with fires only lit on specific dates, relying on a foot-warmer for warmth during chilly months.

La Grande Nanon, the family servant, has been with Monsieur Grandet for thirty-five years, earning only sixty francs a year but managing to save enough to invest in an annuity. Despite her unattractive appearance, she was welcomed into the Grandet household, where she worked tirelessly, cooking, washing, and managing the household with unwavering loyalty. Grandet’s rare acts of kindness, such as giving her an old watch, were seen as monumental gestures.

In 1811, during a difficult grape harvest, Grandet’s treatment of Nanon was marked by a mix of affection and control, as he relied on her for labor while she remained devoted to him. Nanon’s gratitude for her employment and the meager kindnesses she received from Grandet formed a bond that was both simple and complex, reflecting the dynamics of power and dependency in their relationship.

The household was marked by a stark contrast between the treatment of Nanon and the expectations of other servants in Saumur. Nanon’s kitchen was a model of cleanliness and efficiency, and she often worked late into the night, sleeping in a small closet-like space. The upper floors of the house, in contrast to the hall’s modest luxury, were bare and unadorned.

On a November evening in 1819, Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The family prepared for a gathering, with local notables arriving to pay their respects. Grandet presented Eugenie with a gold coin, a tradition he had maintained for years, while Madame Grandet gifted her a new dress. The family’s financial practices were steeped in tradition, with Grandet encouraging Eugenie to save for her future marriage.

As the evening progressed, the arrival of guests, including the des Grassins family, introduced a competitive atmosphere. Madame des Grassins, a lively woman, and her son Adolphe presented gifts to Eugenie, which sparked jealousy among the other guests. The interactions were laced with underlying tensions, as Grandet observed the dynamics with a mix of pride and possessiveness over his daughter.

The gathering, filled with laughter and light-hearted games, masked the deeper motivations of the attendees, all of whom were vying for a share of Grandet’s wealth. The scene was a blend of familial warmth and the cold calculations of social status, with Grandet at the center, manipulating the relationships for his benefit. The simplicity of Eugenie and her mother’s lives contrasted sharply with the materialistic pursuits of their guests, highlighting the complexities of human relationships and the often hidden motivations behind them.

As the evening unfolded, the atmosphere shifted with the arrival of a loud knock at the door, signaling an unexpected interruption that would change the course of the night.

Nanon took a candle and opened the door, followed by her master, Monsieur Grandet. His wife, alarmed, called out to him, and the players exchanged worried glances. Monsieur des Grassins suggested they all go, sensing something ominous. When a young man appeared with a porter carrying trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game, leaving him to speak with the newcomer.

The players resumed their seats but did not continue the game. Madame des Grassins inquired if the traveler was from Saumur, to which her husband replied he was a traveler from Paris. The notary noted the time, confirming the diligence was on schedule. The Abbe Cruchot asked if the gentleman was young, and des Grassins confirmed he was, with heavy luggage.

Eugenie remarked on Nanon's absence, and the president suggested the newcomer might be a relative. Madame Grandet gently urged them to continue playing, sensing her husband’s annoyance. Adolphe, trying to be discreet, hinted that the young man might be a cousin, but his mother silenced him.

Grandet returned without Nanon, and the young traveler, who had piqued everyone's curiosity, entered. He was greeted with polite bows from the men and a ceremonious curtsy from the women. Madame Grandet offered him warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed her concern. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugenie for her hospitality but insisted he was not tired.

Madame des Grassins asked if he had come from the capital, and Charles, adjusting his eye-glass, examined the room and its occupants. He acknowledged the game of loto, which had captivated the players, while Eugenie observed him with a mix of admiration and curiosity. Charles, a handsome young man of twenty-two, was determined to impress his provincial relatives with his Parisian sophistication.

He had been sent by his father to stay with his uncle in Saumur, intending to showcase his fashionable attire and lifestyle. He brought an array of luxurious clothing and accessories, including a hunting costume, various waistcoats, and a gold toilet set. His father had instructed him to travel modestly, but Charles took a private coupe, relishing the opportunity to flaunt his wealth.

As he entered the room, the contrast between his elegance and the provincial guests was stark. The Cruchots, with their shabby attire and unkempt appearance, were taken aback by his presence. Charles’s nonchalant demeanor and fashionable attire drew both admiration and disdain from the locals, who were unaccustomed to such refinement.

Eugenie, captivated by her cousin's charm, felt a longing for the world he represented, contrasting sharply with her mundane life. She was enchanted by his appearance and the delicate items he carried, feeling a deep desire to connect with him.

The game of loto ended abruptly when Nanon returned, asking for sheets for Charles’s bed. Madame Grandet followed her, and the players, sensing the evening's end, began to leave. Eugenie, driven by a newfound impulse, excused herself to assist her mother and Nanon in preparing Charles’s room, eager to make it comfortable and elegant.

She suggested various improvements, including a warming pan for the bed and a wax candle instead of tallow, using her birthday as an excuse to persuade Nanon to comply. Meanwhile, Madame des Grassins engaged Charles in conversation, subtly vying for his attention and hinting at the social opportunities in Saumur.

Charles, feeling out of place, responded graciously to her advances, while the other guests speculated about his intentions and potential interest in Eugenie. The Abbe Cruchot, observing the dynamics, made sly comments that hinted at the underlying tensions and ambitions among the guests.

As Grandet read a letter from his brother, revealing a dire financial situation and a plea for his son Charles to be cared for, he masked his emotions. The letter detailed the brother's impending bankruptcy and his hopes that Grandet would protect his son from the fallout. Grandet's demeanor shifted as he processed the implications of the letter, revealing a complex interplay of familial duty and personal greed.

When the guests departed, Grandet's focus returned to his nephew, and he offered him a modest room, downplaying his wealth. The evening concluded with the guests speculating about the future, aware that Charles's presence could disrupt the delicate balance of relationships in Saumur. The Cruchots and des Grassins recognized the need to unite against a common rival, determined to prevent Eugenie from falling for her cousin and to manipulate the situation to their advantage.

When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew, Charles, that it was too late to discuss the matters that had brought him there. They would have breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, and dinner at five. He mentioned that while people might think he was wealthy, he was actually quite poor and worked hard for his money. He instructed Nanon to bring candles for Charles, who assured his aunt that he needed nothing and bid them goodnight.

Charles took a candle from Nanon, which was so worn it resembled tallow. Grandet led him through the house, avoiding the cold air from the staircase. As they ascended, Charles's expectations began to diminish; the staircase felt shabby, and he wondered why his father had sent him to such a place. He noticed the doors on the landing, particularly one that was walled up, which led to Grandet's office, a place where he conducted his business in secrecy. Grandet was known for his meticulousness, and it was rumored that he hoarded his wealth there.

Eugenie, Grandet's daughter, was in her room across from the office. After a brief goodnight, she and her mother retired to their chambers. Grandet showed Charles to his room in the attic, warning him to call Nanon if he needed anything, as the dog would be dangerous. Nanon appeared with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed, grumbling as he left.

Charles surveyed his room, noting its shabby decor and wondering about his father's intentions for sending him there. He felt out of place and questioned his purpose. Meanwhile, Eugenie, who was up early, began her morning routine, feeling a new sense of beauty and desire as she prepared for the day. She admired her reflection, feeling inadequate compared to her cousin, Charles.

As she dressed, she became aware of her surroundings, finding beauty in the garden and the courtyard, which had previously seemed dull. Her heart swelled with new emotions, and she felt a connection to nature that mirrored her budding feelings for Charles. She longed to impress him and felt a sense of urgency to make things better for him.

Eugenie rushed to Nanon, asking her to prepare cream for Charles's breakfast. Nanon, however, reminded her that they needed to ask Grandet for provisions. As Grandet entered, he and Nanon engaged in a rare quarrel over supplies, revealing his miserly nature. Eventually, he begrudgingly agreed to give Nanon some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast, Grandet took Eugenie for a walk to the fields, where they encountered Cruchot, the notary. Grandet discussed his land and the recent sale of poplar trees, revealing his calculating nature. Cruchot mentioned the gossip about Charles being a potential suitor for Eugenie, to which Grandet reacted vehemently, stating he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin.

This declaration shocked Eugenie, who felt her hopes dashed. She returned home, troubled by her father's harshness and the implications for her feelings for Charles. Meanwhile, Grandet received news of his brother's suicide, which would have dire consequences for Charles. As the family sat down for breakfast, the atmosphere was heavy with unspoken grief and tension.

Eugenie, feeling a deep sense of empathy for Charles, began to cry, realizing the weight of the situation. Grandet, however, remained indifferent, focusing on his own concerns. Eugenie and her mother shared a moment of understanding, and Eugenie expressed her feelings for Charles, wanting to do something special for him. She and Nanon conspired to prepare a nice breakfast for him, despite the risk of Grandet's wrath.

As they worked together, Eugenie's affection for Charles grew, and she felt a sense of urgency to show him kindness and support in the face of their family's troubles.

After two hours of preparation, Eugenie managed to create a simple breakfast that deviated from the family's usual customs. The midday meal was typically taken standing, consisting of bread, fruit, and wine. As she set the table with an armchair for her cousin Charles, she felt anxious about her father's potential reaction. Madame Grandet reassured her, saying she would take responsibility if her husband returned.

Charles, dressed with Parisian flair, joined them, cheerfully inquiring about their well-being. He mentioned he rarely ate before midday but was glad to have something after his difficult journey. He jokingly asked for a partridge, which Eugenie wished she could provide. As they sat down, Charles's charming demeanor captivated Eugenie, who admired his elegance and felt a flutter of affection.

Their conversation revealed the stark contrast between their lives; Charles was accustomed to luxury, while Eugenie lived a modest existence. When Nanon brought in eggs, Charles expressed delight, momentarily forgetting his earlier request. As they enjoyed breakfast, Charles complimented Eugenie, making her blush. He dismissed the idea of ridicule, emphasizing his dislike for it.

Eugenie admired Charles's ring, and he showed it to her, their fingers briefly touching, igniting a spark of connection. However, the atmosphere shifted when Nanon brought in coffee, which Charles criticized for being outdated. The women exchanged worried glances, anticipating the arrival of Monsieur Grandet.

When Grandet entered, he quickly assessed the situation, commenting on the breakfast with suspicion. He noticed the sugar and questioned its source, causing tension in the room. Charles, unaware of the underlying dynamics, asked for sugar for his coffee, but Grandet dismissed him, suggesting he add more milk instead. Eugenie bravely placed the sugar back on the table, defying her father's oppressive presence.

Grandet's demeanor shifted as he addressed Charles, hinting at bad news. The young man, still oblivious, inquired about his father's health. Grandet finally revealed the devastating truth: Charles's father had died by suicide, leaving him without any means. The news struck Charles hard, and he broke down in tears, overwhelmed by grief.

Eugenie and her mother, witnessing his sorrow, felt a deep sense of compassion. Grandet, however, remained detached, focusing on the financial implications of the tragedy. He explained the concept of bankruptcy to Eugenie, emphasizing the dishonor it brought. She struggled to comprehend the harsh realities of the world, feeling sympathy for her cousin's plight.

Grandet mentioned that Charles would be sent to the West Indies to seek fortune, and Eugenie expressed her desire to help him. Grandet reluctantly agreed to fund Charles's journey, which brought a moment of warmth between him and Eugenie. However, the atmosphere remained heavy with grief, and Charles's cries echoed through the house.

Eugenie and her mother decided to comfort Charles, who was still consumed by sorrow. They found him in his room, mourning his father. Eugenie, moved by his pain, offered words of encouragement, urging him to focus on his honor. Charles, however, was lost in his grief, unable to think of anything but his father's suffering.

As they left him to grieve, Eugenie felt a stirring in her heart, recognizing the depth of her feelings for her cousin. The day had transformed her perspective, and she resolved to share in his mourning, marking a significant shift in her life. The quietude of their home was now filled with the weight of loss, and Eugenie found herself drawn to the complexities of love and sorrow intertwined.

About four o’clock, a sharp knock at the door startled Madame Grandet. 

“What can have happened to your father?” she asked her daughter. 

Grandet entered joyfully, rubbing his hands together. 

“Wife,” he said, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I pretended to do nothing in the market-place, and the Belgian came to me in despair. The owners of the good vineyards are holding back their vintages, but I didn’t stop them. I made a deal with him for my vintage at two hundred francs a puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; here are six louis for you. In three months, wines will have fallen.”

His words, though quiet, were laced with sarcasm that would have shocked the townspeople of Saumur, who were already panicking over the news of his sale. 

“Did you have a thousand puncheons this year, father?” Eugenie asked.

“Yes, little one.”

“Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?”

“Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s cold rage was palpable; he had forgotten his nephew until now. 

“What’s this? Ever since that dandy came into my house, everything goes wrong! You act as if you have the right to buy sugar-plums and throw feasts. I won’t have it! I know my duty! I won’t take lessons from you or anyone else. I’ll do what’s proper for my nephew, and you needn’t meddle. Don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon!”

“Where is that fellow? Has he come down yet?” he asked.

“No, my friend,” Madame Grandet replied.

“What is he doing then?”

“He is weeping for his father,” Eugenie said.

Grandet, momentarily at a loss for words, paced the room before retreating to his secret den to contemplate an investment in public funds. His thinning forest land had yielded him six hundred thousand francs, and with the recent sale, he had amassed a total of nine hundred thousand francs. The prospect of earning twenty percent from the funds tempted him. 

Nanon knocked to summon him to dinner. As he descended the stairs, he thought, “I’ll do it; I shall get eight percent interest. In two years, I’ll have fifteen hundred thousand francs, which I will draw out in gold.” 

At dinner, he learned that his nephew refused to eat. 

“So much saved,” he remarked. 

“That’s so,” Nanon agreed. 

“Bah! He won’t cry long. Hunger drives the wolves out of the woods.”

Madame Grandet suggested they put on mourning. 

“What will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not in clothes,” he retorted. 

“But mourning for a brother is necessary; the Church commands it,” she insisted.

“Buy your mourning out of your six louis. A hat-band is enough for me.”

Eugenie, feeling the weight of the evening, sewed in silence, while Grandet calculated his profits. The town buzzed with news of Grandet’s sale and his brother’s failure. 

Nanon’s spinning was the only sound in the house. 

“Nothing should be wasted,” Grandet said, lost in thoughts of future wealth. 

“I will bid my nephew good-night and see if he will eat anything,” he declared. 

Madame Grandet and Eugenie listened from the landing as Grandet entered Charles’s room. 

“Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” he offered, noticing the lack of light in the room. “What’s this? A wax candle! How did they filch a wax candle?”

Hearing this, Madame Grandet and Eugenie retreated to their rooms, frightened. 

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” Grandet asked his wife.

“My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” she replied, trembling.

“The devil take your good God!” he growled. 

Misers like Grandet focus solely on the present, disregarding any belief in a future life. This mindset reflects a broader societal trend where money influences laws, politics, and morals. 

“Madame Grandet, have you done?” he asked again.

“I am praying for you,” she said.

“Very good! Good-night; tomorrow morning we will talk.”

She went to bed, anxious about the conversation to come. Eugenie, worried for her cousin, ran to her mother’s side and kissed her brow. 

“Oh! my good mother,” she said, “tomorrow I will tell him it was I.”

“No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me,” her mother replied.

“Do you hear, mamma?” Eugenie pressed.

“What?”

“He is weeping still.”

“Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold.”

Thus passed a day that would weigh heavily on Eugenie’s life. Her emotions, previously dormant, surged forth, influenced by the day’s events. 

Restless, she woke at intervals, listening for her cousin, imagining him suffering. In the early morning light, she rushed to his room, finding him asleep in an armchair, exhausted from grief. She admired his handsome face, now marred by sorrow. 

“Pardon me, my cousin,” he said, waking. 

“There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus,” she urged.

“That is true,” he replied.

“Well, then, adieu!” she said, retreating, feeling both ashamed and happy. 

Eugenie’s innocent boldness marked a turning point in her life. She worried about what Charles would think of her visit, hoping he would believe she loved him. 

The next morning, she and her mother waited anxiously for Grandet. When he arrived, he seemed absent-minded, kissing Eugenie and sitting down to breakfast without recalling his threats from the night before. 

“What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he remarked.

“Monsieur, he is asleep,” Nanon answered.

“So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” Grandet said, mocking.

This unexpected kindness surprised Madame Grandet. 

Grandet, a poor sleeper, spent his nights calculating his plans, driven by a desire to win at the commercial game of life. He sought to impose upon others, viewing it as a sign of power. 

That day, he devised a scheme to help Charles while maintaining his own financial interests. He aimed to save his brother’s honor without spending a penny, planning to manipulate the Parisians for his own amusement. 

In Grandet’s absence, Eugenie found joy in caring for her cousin, preparing his breakfast with love. She listened for him, eager to know how he felt. 

“Will you breakfast downstairs, or in your room?” she asked through the closed door.

“Where you like,” he replied.

“How do you feel?” she inquired.

“Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry,” he admitted.

“Well, then, we will bring your breakfast to your room, so as not to annoy my father,” she decided, rushing to the kitchen. 

Eugenie’s heart soared as she prepared for Charles’s arrival. After setting the table, she and her mother joined him, offering him comfort and care. 

As they shared breakfast, Charles felt the warmth of their kindness, which contrasted sharply with the indifference he had known in Paris. 

Eugenie’s innocence and genuine affection drew Charles closer to her, and they shared a silent understanding of their mutual feelings. 

When Grandet returned, he found the women seated at their work, unaware of the emotional connection that had blossomed between his nephew and daughter. 

After breakfast, Grandet invited the Cruchots for dinner, surprising everyone. 

As the day unfolded, the atmosphere shifted, hinting at the complexities of love, family, and the burdens of wealth that would shape their lives.

At this moment, the town of Saumur was more excited about the dinner Grandet hosted for the Cruchots than it had been the previous night during the sale of his vintage, which was considered a betrayal against the wine-growing community. Grandet, viewing himself as superior to the community he often deceived, paid little attention to their opinions.

The des Grassins soon learned of Guillaume Grandet's failure and death, deciding to visit their client that evening to express sympathy and to understand his motives for inviting the Cruchots. At five o'clock, Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle, the notary, arrived in their Sunday best. The dinner was somber, with Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, and Madame Grandet speaking little, making it a proper meal of condolence. After dinner, Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet skillfully employed his shrewdness, which had earned him the nickname "the old dog." If the mayor of Saumur had aimed higher, he might have been a great asset to his country, but outside Saumur, he would have struggled. 

“M-m-mon-sieur le p-p-president, you said t-t-that b-b-bankruptcy--” Grandet began, stuttering as he often did to manipulate conversations. The Cruchots, growing impatient, began to mimic his stutter. Grandet's speech impediment was a learned tactic, stemming from a past encounter with a cunning Jew that taught him how to frustrate his opponents. Now, he needed to avoid taking responsibility for his plans while controlling the dialogue.

“M-m-monsieur de B-B-Bonfons,” he stuttered again, “you-ou said th-th-that b-b-bankruptcy c-c-could, in some c-c-cases, b-b-be p-p-prevented b-b-by--”

“By the courts of commerce themselves. It is done constantly,” replied Monsieur C. de Bonfons, trying to assist him. 

Grandet feigned humility, listening intently. The president explained how a respected man facing insolvency could have a receiver appointed by the court, distinguishing between liquidation and failure. Grandet, eager to understand, asked if liquidation could be managed without the courts. 

“Yes, if the merchant does not file his schedule and no creditor appears, the family or heirs can liquidate the business,” the president explained. 

“Ah! Grandet,” the notary interjected, “that would be the right thing to do. You will save your name.”

Grandet acknowledged that his brother's name was indeed Grandet, and he considered the advantages of liquidation for his nephew. However, he expressed his ignorance of Parisian tricks, emphasizing his focus on his vineyards and business in Saumur.

“B-b-because, don’t you see, Monsieur de B-Bonfons, a man must l-l-look b-b-before he l-leaps,” he said, wanting to ensure he understood the implications of the conversation.

The president assured him that debts could be bought at a discount, and if handled correctly, Grandet could emerge unscathed. Grandet, still confused, asked for clarification on the process of negotiating debts.

“Legally, if you acquire a title to all the debts, your brother or his heirs will owe nothing,” the president explained. 

“Very g-good,” Grandet repeated, still processing the information. 

The president offered to go to Paris to negotiate with creditors, suggesting that Grandet could provide some assets to facilitate the process. Grandet hesitated, wanting to think it over, and the notary urged him to clarify his intentions.

Just then, the des Grassins arrived, interrupting the conversation. They expressed their condolences for the Grandet family's misfortunes, and the notary, eager to portray Grandet positively, claimed he intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably. This surprised the des Grassins, who had expected Grandet to act selfishly.

“Ah! I was sure of it,” exclaimed the banker, praising Grandet's honor. 

Grandet, however, was cautious, stating he would not bind himself to anything he could not manage. Madame des Grassins suggested she could go to Paris, trying to undermine the president's authority. Grandet, preferring the banker, pulled him aside to discuss a separate investment opportunity in government bonds.

“Say nothing to the Cruchots; that’ll annoy them,” Grandet instructed, indicating his desire to keep his plans discreet. 

Des Grassins agreed to leave the next day, and they arranged to meet again for final instructions. After the des Grassins departed, Grandet expressed his need to deliberate on the matter.

As news of Grandet's supposed generosity spread, the town began to admire him, forgiving his earlier actions against the wine community. The townspeople's enthusiasm for Grandet's honor was fleeting, reflecting a tendency to quickly forget past grievances.

After closing the door, Grandet summoned Nanon, instructing her to keep quiet about his plans. He prepared for a secret journey, intending to sell gold in Angers while the market was favorable. He took precautions to avoid drawing attention, ensuring no one would suspect his departure or the true purpose of his trip. 

In the early hours, as he and Nanon prepared to leave, Eugenie, worried about Charles, thought she heard a cry. She considered checking on him but was distracted by the sight of her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg. Grandet, focused on his plans, instructed Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried off in the carriage, determined to reach Angers before morning.

“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie, listening from the top of the stairs. Silence enveloped the house, and the distant rumble of the carriage faded away. Suddenly, she heard a cry from her cousin’s room, a sound that pierced through the walls. A thin line of light shone through the door, illuminating the old staircase.

“He suffers!” she exclaimed, rushing up the stairs. A second moan brought her to the landing. The door was ajar, and she pushed it open. Charles was asleep, his head drooping over the armchair, pen fallen from his hand. His labored breathing alarmed Eugenie, prompting her to enter the room.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, noticing the sealed letters on the table addressed to various tradesmen. “He’s settling his affairs to leave France at once.” Her gaze fell on two open letters, and the words “My dear Annette” struck her like a blow. Her heart raced, and she felt rooted to the spot.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” Thoughts swirled in her mind. “Should I read it? No, I mustn’t.” Yet, curiosity overwhelmed her. She looked at Charles, gently adjusted his head, and kissed his hair. “Dear Annette!” a voice screamed in her mind.

“I must read it,” she decided, turning away in shame. For the first time, she felt the struggle between good and evil within her. Passion and curiosity won out. As she read, her heart swelled, and the intensity of her emotions made the joys of first love even more precious.

  My dear Annette,--Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has now overwhelmed me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are lost. I am orphaned at an age when I am still a child, yet I must rise as a man from this abyss. I have spent half the night facing my position. If I wish to leave France an honest man, I have not a hundred francs to try my fate in the Indies or America. I cannot remain in Paris, where the disdain shown to a ruined man would kill me. Your love cannot draw me back. Alas! I have no money to go to you for a last kiss to give me strength for my forlorn enterprise.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him,” thought Eugenie, wiping her eyes and continuing.

  I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. I have not even a sou for an outfit. If I have nothing, I shall go to Nantes and ship as a common sailor, starting anew like others who have begun without a sou and returned wealthy. I have faced my future coolly. It seems more horrible for me than for others because I have been so petted by my parents and blessed by your love. Such happiness could not last. Nevertheless, I feel more courage than a careless young man is supposed to feel. Dear Anna, if to keep me in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, we would still not have enough for my extravagant living. I would never accept such sacrifices. We must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy. Charles stirred, and a chill of terror ran through her, but he did not wake, and she resumed reading.

  When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European, especially one who works hard. In ten years, your daughter will be eighteen; she will be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, and your daughter perhaps more so. Keep in your soul the memory of our happiness, and be faithful, if you can, to the memory of your poor friend. I cannot exact such faithfulness, for I must conform to my new life; I must think of marriage, which becomes a necessity. I have found a cousin here in Saumur whose face, manners, and heart would please you—

Eugenie noted the letter’s abrupt end. She defended him, thinking how an innocent girl might not perceive the coldness in his words. To young girls raised in purity, all is love when they enter the realm of passion. They see their lover through the lens of their own emotions. Eugenie’s heart echoed with the words, “My dear Annette, my loved one,” as if they were the sweetest language of love.

Charles, though mourning his father, was more affected by the loss of his fortune than by genuine feelings. His parents had indulged him, preventing him from making the harsh calculations many sons face. The liberality of his father had instilled in him a real love, devoid of self-interest.

Yet, Charles was a product of Paris, taught by society to calculate everything. He had been molded by the world where more crimes are committed in thought than punished. He had learned to admire nothing and to see self-interest as the driving force behind all actions. Annette had compelled him to think seriously about his future, teaching him lessons of worldly prudence.

“You are very foolish, Charles,” she would say. “I shall have a great deal of trouble teaching you to understand the world. You behaved extremely ill to Monsieur des Lupeaulx. I know he is not honorable, but wait until he is out of power, then you may despise him.”

Charles had not yet applied the maxims of Parisian morality; he still possessed the beauty of inexperience. However, he had been unknowingly inoculated with selfishness. The seeds of Parisian political economy lay dormant in his heart, waiting to emerge when he faced real life.

Most young girls succumb to the tender promises such appearances offer. Eugenie, despite her innocence, was enchanted by Charles’s demeanor, unaware of the coldness in his heart. A chance encounter with his last heartfelt words stirred her emotions. She laid down the letter, filled with love, and watched her sleeping cousin, vowing to love him always. She then glanced at the other letter, seeking further proof of his noble qualities.

  My dear Alphonse,--When you receive this letter, I shall be without friends. While I doubt the world’s friendship, I have never doubted yours. Please settle my affairs and get as much as you can from my possessions. I intend to go to the Indies. I have written to those I owe money, and you will find a list of their names. I do not wish to keep anything except perhaps a few baubles for my journey. I will send you a power of attorney for the sales. Keep Briton for yourself; I would rather give him to you than see him sold. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a comfortable carriage, which they have not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it without payment. If they refuse, do what you can to avoid dishonor in my present circumstances. I owe the British Islander six louis, which I lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, running back to her room with a candle. A thrill of pleasure coursed through her as she opened an old oak cabinet, revealing a red velvet purse with gold tassels, a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it in her hand and began counting her hidden treasures: twenty _portugaises_, five _genovines_, three gold _quadruples_, a hundred Dutch ducats, and a few rare rupees. She realized she possessed about five thousand eight hundred francs, which could be sold for nearly six thousand.

Eugenie, filled with joy, thought only of her cousin and ran upstairs without hesitation. The secret misery of Charles made her forget propriety. Standing at the threshold, she held the candle and purse, and when Charles awoke, he was speechless with surprise. She placed the candle on the table and said, “My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me if you will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” he asked, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles blushed. “How did it happen?” he asked.

“I do not know. I am tempted not to regret reading them; they have shown me your heart, your soul, and your plans.”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush! Not so loud; we must not wake others. Here,” she said, opening her purse, “are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I was ignorant of the value of money; you have taught me. It is just a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow from your sister.”

Eugenie, confident in her offer, was taken aback by his silence. “Oh! you will not refuse?” she implored, kneeling down. “I will never rise until you have taken that gold! My cousin, I implore you, answer me! Let me know if you respect me, if you are generous—”

As he heard her noble distress, tears fell from Charles onto her hands. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents onto the table. “Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she cried, weeping with joy. “Fear nothing, my cousin; you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

Charles finally expressed his feelings. “Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she asked, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square box covered in leather. “This is something as precious to me as life itself. My mother would sell the gold she lavished on this dressing-case, but I cannot. Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the box, revealing a beautifully crafted dressing-case. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, revealing a hidden drawer. “Here is something worth the whole world.” He drew out two portraits, masterpieces of Madame Mirbel, set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he replied, smiling. “This is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. You alone are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent. “Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he urged.

Hearing the same words she had used to him, she looked at him with love, her first look of womanhood. He took her hand and kissed it. “Angel of purity! Between us, money is nothing. Feeling, sentiment, must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, lowering her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She gently disengaged her hand, and he followed her to her room, lighting the way. At the threshold, he said, “Ah! why am I ruined?”

“What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she replied.

“Poor child!” said Charles, leaning against the wall. “If that were true, he would not have let my father die; he would not let you live this way.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” Charles said disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year, do you think you would live in this cold, barren room?” He glanced at the old cabinet. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said.

“Go and sleep,” she urged, preventing him from entering her disordered room.

Charles stepped back, and they exchanged good-nights with smiles. Both fell asleep in the same dream, and from that moment, Charles began to wear roses with his mourning.

The next day, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie in the garden with Charles. He was still sad, burdened by the weight of his misfortune. “My father will not be home until dinner,” Eugenie said, noticing her mother’s anxious look.

The connection between Eugenie and Charles was evident in their shared thoughts and the sweetness of her voice. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected by the three women, each occupied with their tasks. Grandet had neglected his affairs, and various tradesmen came to discuss business. Madame Grandet and Eugenie listened to the endless chatter of workmen and farmers, while Nanon prepared the produce they brought.

Around five, Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs from the exchange on his gold. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, which were nearly foundered.

“I am hungry,” he said upon entering.

Nanon called from the kitchen, “Haven’t you eaten since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he replied.

Nanon brought in soup just as Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet had not even noticed his nephew. “Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker. “Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” Grandet replied. “They have enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say _was_ worth—”

“Where have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” Grandet said quietly.

The banker was astonished, and a whispered conversation began between them, often glancing at Charles. Des Grassins looked surprised, likely learning of Grandet’s plans to invest a sum that would yield him a hundred thousand francs a year.

“Monsieur Grandet,” Des Grassins said to Charles, “I am going to Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” Charles replied.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” Charles asked eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle with feigned pride. “Are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, kissed his uncle, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-bye, Des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead them by the nose.”

The two men shook hands, and Grandet returned to his armchair, asking Nanon for some black-currant ratafia. Too excited to sit still, he got up, looked at a portrait, and began to sing, doing what Nanon called his dancing steps.

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie exchanged silent glances. Grandet’s joviality frightened them when it reached its peak. The evening passed quickly, and Grandet chose to go to bed early, expecting everyone else to follow suit. As for Madame Grandet, she lived according to her husband’s will. During the two hours of digestion, Grandet, more jovial than ever, shared his peculiar sayings, one of which captured his mindset: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.” He was cheerful and generous, offering Nanon some ratafia, which she gladly accepted.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles was thriving in the Indies. His commercial ventures yielded six thousand dollars, and he quickly realized that buying and selling people was the most lucrative business. He traveled to Africa, purchasing enslaved individuals and combining this trade with other profitable goods. His relentless ambition was fueled by the desire to return to Paris with a significant fortune and reclaim a more prestigious position than before.

Through his experiences, Charles's views shifted; he became skeptical and lost his moral compass, recognizing that what was deemed a crime in one culture could be celebrated in another. His heart hardened, and he became ruthless, engaging in various exploitative trades, including selling people and defrauding customs. He bought pirate-captured goods at low prices and sold them for profit. Initially, he carried the memory of Eugenie with him, but as he indulged in various escapades, he distanced himself from his past, viewing his family with disdain and regarding Eugenie merely as a creditor.

This transformation explains Charles Grandet's silence. In the Indies and elsewhere, he adopted the alias "Shepherd" to protect his identity while pursuing wealth through unscrupulous means. His efforts paid off, and by 1827, he returned to Bordeaux aboard the "Marie Caroline," bringing with him gold-dust worth nearly two million francs, expecting to profit further at the Paris mint.

On the ship, he encountered Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman connected to the royal court, who was returning from the Indies with his family. The d’Aubrions, once a prominent family, were now struggling financially, and Madame d’Aubrion was eager to marry off her unattractive daughter to a wealthy suitor. Des Grassins, aware of Charles's return and impending marriage, approached him about settling his father's debts. Charles, now a man of means, dismissed Des Grassins's concerns, asserting that his father's financial troubles were no longer his responsibility. He was focused on his new identity as the Comte d’Aubrion, believing that wealth would shield him from his father's failures.

In August, Eugenie sat on the bench where she and Charles had once pledged their love, reflecting on their past. She received a letter from him, which she struggled to open, filled with anticipation and dread. The letter revealed Charles's success and his new life, but it also conveyed a painful message: he was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, a marriage of convenience that would secure his social ambitions. He expressed that love in marriage was a delusion and that he could not pursue their youthful dreams due to the differences in their lives.

Eugenie was devastated by the letter, feeling abandoned and heartbroken. She realized that her love for Charles was profound and enduring, contrasting sharply with his pragmatic approach to marriage. As she processed the news, she recalled her mother's prophetic words about suffering and enduring pain.
