AN OLD MAID

CHAPTER I. ONE OF MANY CHEVALIERS DE VALOIS

Many provinces in France boasted a Chevalier de Valois. One lived in Normandy, another at Bourges, and the subject of this story flourished in Alençon. Their precise number mattered little; they knew little of one another. If any Valois remain they descend from Charles de Valois, Duc d’Angoulême; the male line had, until proved otherwise, ended in the Abbe de Rothelin; another branch ended in scandal. The name carried more tradition than truth.

The Chevalier of Alençon was, like his fellows, an old gentleman—tall, thin, withered and nearly penniless. He had spent his youth in Paris and reached thirty amid conquests and gallantries when the Revolution came. Accepted by the provincial aristocracy as a true Valois, he distinguished himself by manners. He dined out daily, played cards every evening, and told anecdotes of Louis XV’s reign and the Revolution’s beginnings that, when first heard, seemed witty. He never repeated his bons mots, never spoke of his love affairs, and used his privilege as a Voltairean noble to miss mass; his royalist devotion secured indulgence for his irreligion.

His manner—perhaps borrowed from Mole—involved taking snuff from a gold box adorned with the portrait of the Princess Goritza, a Hungarian for whom he had once fought a duel. At fifty-eight he owned only fifty; his thin, fair figure helped preserve a youthful air. A single large nose divided a pale face: the left side reddened by digestion, the right remained white. He ate with appetite and declared he had a “hot liver,” perhaps to excuse it; in a region given to long meals his stomach seemed providential. Doctors said heat on the left denotes a prodigal heart; his gallantries confirmed the diagnosis.

His constitution remained vigorous; his face bore the furrows of pleasure—crow’s-feet and marches-du-palais—signs of passion. He was fastidious in dress and ablutions: fine white linen, a well-worn but tidy coat, a maroon coat with gilt buttons, half-tight breeches, a plain waistcoat, a tight cravat, square-buckled shoes, and two watch-chains. His hair and brows were combed to preserve youth; part of his skull shone like ivory. His hands were delicate. Were it not for his magisterial nose he might seem too dainty.

He kept some oddities—cotton in his ears and two little diamond ear-rings representing negroes' heads, which he claimed cured headaches—but these were forgiven because of his graces. He labored to conceal his age and give pleasure, comporting himself with an old-marquis ease rare since Mole’s disappearance.

His private life, though seemingly open, remained a mystery. He lodged modestly on the second floor of Madame Lardot’s house in the rue du Cours, paying a hundred francs a year for two rooms. He dined out daily and returned only to sleep; at home he breakfasted on chocolate, bread and butter, and seasonal fruit. He made no fire except in severe weather. Between eleven and four he walked, read papers, and made his quarterly call on his man of business. He pretended poverty—his whole fortune, he said, an annuity of six hundred francs, the remnant of old opulence—and demanded secrecy about any other income.

Monsieur de Valois was welcome in Alençon’s best houses. His skill at cards, stories, amiability, and approving grimace made gatherings feel incomplete without him. Young women treasured his compliments; his old-fashioned diminutives—“my heart,” “my jewel,” “my little pet”—had a grace few resisted. At cards he never complained, praised opponents when they lost, and with a snuff-box gesture silenced tedious dissertations on play. He tolerated poor players and made the best of them. His equability won admiration: “I do admire the Chevalier de Valois!”

To grisettes he was paternal and flattering; he supplied trifles—chocolate, bonbons, ribbons—and they returned secret attachments and discretion. Mornings he amused himself with their gossip—his “petticoat gazettes.” Despite small winnings—he left salons with about six francs—he was the town’s darling.

Yet his means were not so slender as he pretended. After the “glorious” sad days of July some discovered his nightly winnings were about one hundred and fifty francs every three months and that he had the pluck to send himself his annuity to avoid appearing wholly destitute. Friends defended him as respectable and calumniated. Over about fifteen years he had amassed ten thousand and some odd hundred francs. With the Bourbon return an old friend, the Marquis de Pombreton, allegedly repaid twelve hundred pistoles lent long ago; this enabled him to consult moneyed friends and to invest on the Grand-Livre at the right moment. Through unnamed influential persons he obtained a pension of three hundred francs from the king’s privy purse and the cross of Saint-Louis, permitting him to take rank as a retired colonel for service in the Catholic armies of the West.

Thus, besides the fiction of his small annuity, he had an honest income of about a thousand francs. He changed little in lifestyle; the red ribbon of the cross completed his physiognomy. After 1802 he sealed letters with an old family seal and bore arms: Party of France, with mascles and a cross; motto: “Valeo.” Many envied his tranquil life—whist, boston, backgammon, reversi, piquet; snuff gracefully inhaled; tranquil walks. But he harbored an ambition: to marry a certain rich old maid, using her as a stepping-stone to court. That explained his royal bearing and residence in Alençon.

CHAPTER II. SUSANNAH AND THE ELDERS

One Wednesday morning in mid-spring the chevalier, dressing in his old green-flowered damask gown, heard the light step of a young girl climb the stair. Three taps, and without waiting she slipped into his room.

“Ah! is it you, Suzanne?” said the chevalier, still stropping his razor. “What have you come for, my dear little jewel of mischief?”

“I have come to tell you something which may perhaps give you as much pleasure as pain.”

“Is it anything about Cesarine?”

“Cesarine! much I care about your Cesarine!” she answered saucily.

Suzanne was a work-girl at Madame Lardot’s. The wash-rooms occupied the ground floor; the tiny courtyard dried the fine linen of the town’s best families. The chevalier, claiming to know love-affairs from the linen’s condition, was discreet; he was never betrayed into epigrams that might close a salon to him. He frequented the work-room, bestowing trifles on his “little ducks,” who adored him. Women have an instinct for men who love them and ask nothing in return; the chevalier’s gallant protection—an echo of seigneurs’ ways—made him beloved. He could no longer ruin himself for a mistress, so he gave toffees instead of bank-bills; they accepted gladly. They made him seem older and more respectable in public, but among themselves they perched on his shoulders like parrots. He enjoyed their household gossip and was amused by their “talking feuilletons” at breakfast.

Suzanne was one of his favorites—clever, ambitious, handsome—reminiscent of Sophie Arnould. She had a Norman, rubenesque beauty.

“Well, my child, tell me your great or little adventure,” said the chevalier.

He listened with a paternal manner; the shabby room—threadbare tapestry, tavern-paper profiles of Louis XVI and his family—exhaled an eighteenth-century air; libertine graces of his youth reappeared. He draped Suzanne before him, holding her legs between his knees; she indulged him but could be offish in the street.

“We slander ourselves,” he said craftily; “we are as virtuous as that biblical girl whose name we bear; we can always marry as we please, but we are thirsty for Paris. We want to go see if the great capital hasn’t some young Chevalier de Valois with a carriage, diamonds, an opera-box.” He counseled her to go to Paris at an old bachelor’s expense—“an old bachelor is the natural money-box of a young girl”—but warned: “don’t drag me into it. I haven’t a penny to get you out of trouble. If I marry Mademoiselle Cormon and become rich, of course I would prefer you.”

“Monsieur le chevalier, I assure you, you are mistaken,” she began, blushing; but the chevalier had already guessed her plan.

“Yes, yes! I understand: you want me to believe it. Well! I do believe it. But take my advice: go to Monsieur du Bousquier. Don’t you take linen there? He’s an old bachelor and very rich; if you’re clever you can go to Paris at his expense. Twist him round your finger; he fears scandal and is ambitious.” At these words Suzanne hurried off—to du Bousquier.

Du Bousquier belonged to an old Alençon family, had gone to Paris, engaged in public business, and made a fortune as commissary of provisions to the armies from 1793 to 1799. He lived magnificently, was intimate with Barras and Fouché, and had aimed for ministerial rank by supporting a secret party until Marengo. His speculations failed when Kellermann and Desaix altered events; Napoleon disfavored contractors who had speculated on his defeat, and du Bousquier was ruined. From his past he retained only twelve hundred francs a year placed on the Grand Livre. He returned to Alençon furious with Bonaparte and was received among royalists though shunned by the leading nobility. He had once proposed to Mademoiselle Armande and been refused. Now middle-aged, he was plump, with strong calves and a broad chest, but his voice was stifled, as if cutting moist wood. He favored the liberal party in Alençon, manipulating elections and harming the Restoration; his hatred of royalists was patient and inexhaustible.

The chevalier had turned Suzanne upon du Bousquier because the two old bachelors were secret rivals for the hand of Mademoiselle Cormon. Though their designs were discreet, both hoped for this wealthy old maid. The chevalier had never asked her hand; du Bousquier had and been refused. Their war was courteous in the chevalier’s manner, less so in du Bousquier’s, yet they met nearly every evening on the same ground.

Suzanne tripped from the rue du Cours to du Bousquier’s house in the rue du Cygne, where he had bought a grey Jura-stone house about five years before. It preserved some furniture and decorations of his former splendor; provincial neglect made the whole a jumble—magnificence marred by dirt. He lived as a man of leisure with a single Breton servant, René, a ninny trained to rub floors and black boots; René was a glutton and a trusty confidant.

“’Tisn’t your day. We haven’t any linen for the wash,” René grunted when Suzanne entered. She left him and went upstairs.

Du Bousquier, in bed, listened as Suzanne sat swinging curtain-rings.

“What is it, my charmer?” he said, sitting up.

“Monsieur,” she said gravely, “I know how ridiculous it is in a poor girl to nag a man. But I can be of use to you if you’ll attach yourself to me as I am to you. If we went to Paris you’d see what I could do with a man of your mind and capacity; they are remaking the government. Perhaps this misfortune is a piece of luck. Who and what are you working for now?”

“For myself, of course!” cried du Bousquier.

“Monster! you’ll never be a father!” she retorted.

Du Bousquier, torn between joy, distrust, and calculation, had long planned to marry Mademoiselle Cormon for her property and social standing. That marriage would have advanced his career; but Suzanne’s sudden offer bewildered him. Without it, he might have married her and led the liberal party openly; with it, he hesitated. He rubbed his night-cap and thought. Finally: “I’m not to be taken in with such bosh, not I!”—the proud remark of a man who prided himself on never being duped by women.

“Remember, Monsieur du Bousquier,” Suzanne replied, “I have offered you my hand with dignity. I shan’t beg. I can find other husbands. But I cannot stay in Alençon: my mother would beat me, Madame Lardot would turn me off. If I go to Paris I must have money for the journey and for—you know what.”

This struck du Bousquier more than the chevalier; had she truly been desperate she would have died a thousand deaths before asking. “Will you really go to Paris, then?” he asked.

“Yes, monsieur.”

Du Bousquier lamented his debts—payments on the house, bills for painter and upholsterer. He offered three hundred francs. Suzanne pretended to depart.

“Stop, where are you going?” said du Bousquier uneasily. “The devil take me if I ever did anything more than rumple her collar, and, lo and behold! she makes THAT a ground to put her hand in one’s pocket!”

“I’m going to Madame Granson, treasurer of the Maternity Society,” Suzanne said. “They save many poor girls in my condition from suicide.”

“Madame Granson!” Alarmed, du Bousquier gave her a key and told her to take from his secretary a bag containing about six hundred francs—“it is all I possess.”

“If you deceive me, Suzanne,” he cried as she handled the sack.

“Monsieur,” she answered impertinently, “wouldn’t you have given me money if I had asked for it?” Recalled to gallantry, du Bousquier kissed her; she left, cursing his stinginess. She hid the sack in an osier gamebag, already thinking that the Maternity Society, presided over by Mademoiselle Cormon, might be induced to complete the sum she had calculated for Paris—one thousand francs—and, in revenge for du Bousquier’s parsimony, perhaps to entangle him in scandal. With a mind quick to mischief, she went to Madame Granson, composing an expression of deepest dejection.

Madame Granson, widow of a lieutenant-colonel of artillery killed at Jena, lived on a meagre pension of nine hundred francs plus three hundred from a small property; her savings had been spent on educating and supporting her son. Their lodging in the rue du Bercail was a modest ground-floor apartment in a provincial town: a street door up three steps, a passage to an interior courtyard, a gallery staircase, a dining-room and kitchen on one side, and a salon that doubled as the widow’s bedroom on the other.

Athanase Granson, twenty-three, slept in an attic and added six hundred francs to the household by a post in the mayor’s office—obtained through his relation Mademoiselle Cormon—where he was put in charge of the archives. Madame Granson, plainly dressed beneath a portrait of the lieutenant-colonel, kept strict provincial morals and spent hours watching the street. At breakfast mother and son shared a simple meal of coffee, bread and butter, and radishes. To grasp how significant Suzanne’s visit would be, certain secret interests of both mother and son must be explained.

Athanase was thin, pale, of medium height, with a hollow face and coal-black eyes—marks of repressed talent. Irregular lines, a strong chin, a well-formed forehead and a melancholy born of poverty suggested an imprisoned spirit. In any larger place his appearance might have attracted patrons; in Alençon timidity and provincial chill stifled action. Poverty had intensified in him a savage pride—noble yet obstructive to a beginning in life. He belonged to a kind of talent that doubts, is easily discouraged, more contemplative than active, liable at times to sudden resolves others might call madness.

Contempt for his poverty crushed him; solitude enervated him; without encouragement his powers languished. He worked quietly in the archives, kept his ambitions hidden, and beneath all lay a secret passion that hollowed his cheeks: he loved his distant cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon. She was also pursued by the Chevalier de Valois and by du Bousquier. Athanase’s love began with calculation—Cormon was thought rich—and he imagined marriage would provide steady means to pursue fame and secure his mother’s comfort. That practical motive degraded the feeling in his own eyes; he also feared ridicule for a young man of twenty-three loving an “old maid” of forty.

Yet in provincial life such a marriage could be sincere. Where manners are fixed and opportunities few, marriage often serves necessity; improvident liaisons alarm cautious parents and ruin a young man’s prospects. A poor artist or scholar in the provinces cannot rely on marriage for anything but material relief; efforts to combine love and subsistence are generally denied. Athanase’s practical motive gradually became genuine passion as he studied Cormon and, by the charm of habit, came to see only her perfections. In his isolation she concentrated his affections; his secret feeling swelled silently, and with it his timidity.

Madame Granson suspected the truth and calculated candidly. She saw advantages in the match: a talented husband would honor the family and neighborhood. She judged the obstacles—his poverty and Cormon’s age—formidable but surmountable by patience. Like other provincials she watched for propitious circumstances and quietly plotted. She distrusted du Bousquier while concealing her tactics from her son.

Suzanne’s lie, which she confided to Madame Granson, became a powerful weapon in the widow’s hands. As treasurer of the Maternity Society, Madame Granson could spread news under the guise of charity while mobilizing support for “the chaste Suzanne,” using the scandal to wound du Bousquier. The Chevalier de Valois suspected only part of what would follow; the impending scandal would exceed his expectations.

At breakfast Athanase, leaning on his elbow and twirling his spoon, surveyed the poor room—red brick floor, straw chairs, a painted wooden buffet, chequered curtains. His pallid face framed by black hair and lit by the window was the first thing Suzanne saw when she entered. Suzanne, a grisette long familiar with misery and heartache, felt an electric attraction—an inexplicable flash that convinces one, “It is he!” Often reason quells such moments, but here Suzanne was struck by a genuine feeling that dispersed the cynicism of her dissipated life. What had seemed a joke the night before now seemed a grave misdeed; she recoiled at her own readiness to deceive. Yet practical considerations—his poverty, hopes of enriching herself, perhaps destiny—checked this impulse.

She asked for a private interview with Madame Granson, who led her to the bedchamber. When Suzanne emerged and looked at Athanase again, tears came to her eyes. Madame Granson was radiant: she had at last a terrible weapon against du Bousquier and promised the seduced girl the sympathetic support of charitable ladies, foreseeing a storm over the guilty man’s head.

“My dear child,” Madame Granson told her son, “we dine to-night with Mademoiselle Cormon; do take pains with your appearance. Put on that handsome frilled shirt and your green coat of Elbeuf cloth. I have my reasons.” She urged him to study the Chevalier de Valois’s easy manner and soften his stiff air. Athanase, surprised but obedient, rose, took his cap, and went to the mayor’s office, wondering whether his mother suspected his secret. Passing the rue du Val-Noble, where Mademoiselle Cormon lived, he fancied himself beside her: how little she knew that a young man loved her, would be faithful, let her manage her fortune, yet could not speak.

Suzanne returned to her mother’s thinking of Athanase and, like many who long to help an adored man beyond their power, considered making her body a stepping-stone for his advancement. Her falsehood had achieved its purpose: a few days later she left town with money and fine clothes, including a green reps dress and a green bonnet lined with pink given by Monsieur de Valois—a gift she preferred even to money—and set off joyfully for Paris. Alençon lamented her misfortunes as the ladies of the Charity and Maternity Societies showed lively sympathy. Though Suzanne belonged to the class absorbed by Paris, she remained in a more decorous stratum of gallantry and styled herself “Madame du Val-Noble.” One distinguished Restoration writer, a journalist shaping public opinion, took her under his protection and might marry her.

In nearly every smaller French prefecture there is a salon that assembles the thoughtful, settled leaders of the community who are not, however, the very highest rank. The master and mistress of such a house rank among the town’s leaders: they are received wherever they visit, and no fête or formal or diplomatic dinner takes place without them. Yet the château people—heads of great estates, the department’s highest personages—do not attend; their intercourse with these salon-keepers proceeds by cards and occasional reciprocal dinners or soirées.

This salon, where lesser nobility, clergy, and magistracy meet, exerts great influence. Judgment and mind of the region reside in that solid, unostentatious circle, where each man knows his neighbor’s resources and where indifference to luxury and dress prevails—trifles beside long schemes to obtain ten or twelve acres of pasture. Immovable in its prejudices, this group follows a beaten track and accepts nothing from Paris without long trial; it rejects cashmeres as it rejects speculative investments; it scoffs at fashions, reads little, and is indifferent to science, literature, or industrial inventions. It insists on removing a prefect who does not suit it; if administration resists, it isolates him like bees walling up a snail in wax.

Gossip in this society often becomes solemn verdict. Young women seldom appear, and when they do it is to seek approbation of their conduct—a consecration of self-importance. The supremacy of one house wounds other natives, who console themselves by calculating the profit from its entertainments. If no sufficiently wealthy home exists to keep open house, the leading people choose a neutral meeting-place, as at Alençon, where the salon centered in the house of an old maid whose fortune, unknown to her, was coveted by Madame Granson, her second cousin, and by two old bachelors—the Chevalier de Valois and du Bousquier—whose hopes had already been disclosed.

Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon’s family had long been among the province’s most considerable. Though bourgeois, they consorted with nobility and were allied to it: stewards to the Duke d’Alençon, magistrates, bishops among their members. Monsieur de Sponde, her maternal grandfather, had been chosen by the Nobility to the States-General; Monsieur Cormon, her father, by the Tiers-État—though neither accepted the mission. For a century daughters of the family had married provincial nobles; the Cormons appeared in many genealogies; no bourgeois family seemed so like nobility.

Mademoiselle Cormon lived in the family house, built in Henri IV’s time by Pierre Cormon, steward to the last Duke d’Alençon; the mansion had always belonged to the family. It produced little revenue and was costly to maintain, but such a handsome private dwelling in a provincial town naturally attracted envy and made her an obvious host for the upper circle. The house—granite-built, diamond-cut corner blocks, steep roof with projecting oak-framed windows, lead bouquets surmounting gables that once marked bourgeois aspiration to noble airs—stands mid rue du Val-Noble. Stables and coach-house flank the courtyard on one side; kitchen and laundry on the other. A side of the porte-cochère opens onto a small courtyard garden, raised and edged with a privet hedge, planted with roses, pinks, lilies and Spanish broom, with pomegranate and myrtle in pots in summer. The courtyard and dependencies are scrupulously clean—proof of a ferreting eye and the Dutch neatness introduced by an old maid who, for lack of other occupation, keeps every corner in order.

A glass door from the portico leads into an antechamber: a red-tiled gallery wainscoted and lined with family portraits—some damaged—where guests’ cloaks, overshoes, umbrellas, and pelisses hang; benches for servants and a large stove counter the north wind. The house divides into two halves: toward the courtyard a staircase well, a large dining-room overlooking the garden, and a pantry linking to the kitchen; on the other side a salon with four windows and two smaller rooms—a boudoir and an office. Above is the family apartment and a suite where the venerable Abbé de Sponde lives; the garrets are full of rats and mice. The garden, half an acre, borders the Brillante river—its shallow waters stained by dyehouses and loaded with refuse—and ends in a natural terrace forming a quay with steps down to the river, trimmed with linden hedges and tall pottery vases. Nothing is pretentious; all seems permanent rather than fleeting.

The ground floor is wholly for reception, suffused with the provincial spirit. The great square salon has four windows, modest gray woodwork, a single oblong mirror above the fireplace, and draped green damask curtains looped like a vast dais. Furniture covered with tapestry and varnished woodwork bears scenes from La Fontaine; some tapestry is mended. A huge beam divides the ceiling and an old rock-crystal chandelier hangs swathed in green gauze. On the mantel two Sèvres-blue vases, old girandoles, and a clock whose subject, "Le Déserteur," shows Sedaine’s popularity—a bronze-gilt group of soldiers bringing a deserter and a fainting young woman—stand as ornaments. Portraits by Rigaud and pastels by Latour hang on the walls. Four card tables, a backgammon board, and a piquet table occupy the vast room—the only one ceiled. The dining-room, paved in black-and-white stone with painted beams and huge marble-topped sideboard, bears frescoed trellis walls and varnished cane seats; its patriarchal air exemplifies the genius of the provinces that preserves rather than innovates. A cold precision pervades the house.

Tourists in Normandy, Brittany, Maine, and Anjou will have seen many houses like the Cormon’s; it is an archetype of bourgeois homes there and helps explain manners and customs. Life within was calm and monotonously regular. The library, placed below river level, contains well-bound books: transport costs and provincial thrift mean only desirable titles from regional presses appear—careful rather than copious reading.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s society numbered about one hundred and fifty persons; some visited country estates, some were occasionally ill or traveled the department, but many came every night unless elsewhere invited, and some lived permanently in town by duty or habit. All were of mature age; few had traveled; most had lived their lives in the provinces, and some had taken part in the chouannerie. The latter were beginning to speak openly of that war now that rewards were given defenders of the good cause. Monsieur de Valois, a mover in the last uprising, had shared stratagems used against an old republican commander, Hulot, who years earlier had been stationed at Alençon.

Women in this society took little trouble with dress except on Wednesdays, when Mademoiselle Cormon gave a dinner and previous Wednesday’s guests paid their “visit of digestion.” Wednesdays were gala days: the assembly was numerous, visitors appeared in ribbons; some women brought their sewing or knitting, and young girls made patterns for Alençon point lace to pay their expenses. Certain husbands attended out of policy; young men were few in that house, and whispers were impossible without attracting attention—so there was no real danger for young girls or married women of love-making.

Every evening at six the antechamber filled. Each habitué arrived with cane, cloak, and lantern. Habits were so patriarchal that if the Abbé de Sponde lay down or Mademoiselle Cormon retired, servants did not inform the rest: the first comer received the second; when company sufficed for whist, piquet, or boston, they began without awaiting either abbé or hostess. If it were dark, Josette or Jacquelin lit candles at the first bell; seeing the salon lit, the abbé would slowly descend. Backgammon and piquet, three boston tables, and a whist table were filled—twenty-five or thirty persons; sometimes forty. Between eight and nine servants came to accompany their masters home; short of a revolution no one remained after ten. Guests departed in groups along the street, discoursing on the game or continuing conversations on land, wills, quarrels among heirs, or aristocratic haughtiness—like Parisians leaving a theatre.

Critics who declaim about provincial habits might imagine their few verses superior, but to sit in such a salon is to see human life reduced to faithful repetition of steps and pleasures, which for many resembles happiness. Du Bousquier, the society’s born statistician, estimated that those who frequented it commanded 131 votes in the electoral college and mustered eighteen hundred thousand francs a year from nearby landed estates.

Alençon was not entirely represented by this salon; higher aristocracy had its own, and the receiver-general’s house was like a government inn where society danced, plotted, loved, and supped. These salons communicated through mixed individuals with Mademoiselle Cormon’s house, which nonetheless sat in judgment on the others, criticizing their dinners’ luxury, their ices at balls, the women’s behavior, dresses, and novelties.

To preside over such a coterie naturally attracted suitors and ambitious men. To Mademoiselle Cormon it meant influence and rank—election as deputy—for her suitors: the noble’s peerage and the purveyor’s receiver-generalship were at stake. A leading salon is hard to create; here was one already established. To marry Mademoiselle Cormon was to reign in Alençon. Among three suitors, Athanase Granson alone no longer calculated profit: he truly loved her person as well as her fortune. The other two—the chevalier and du Bousquier—had political and social calculations.

There is a singular drama in these unobserved rivalries circling a woman who never guessed they existed, despite her eager desire to be married. It is not hard to see why, despite fortune and suitors, she remained unmarried. First, she always wished to marry a nobleman; between 1788 and 1798 political circumstances were unfavorable, and though she wanted to be a woman of condition she feared the Revolutionary tribunal. These conflicting sentiments kept her motionless; uncertainty pleased unmarried women as long as they felt capable of choosing. Under the Consulate and Empire suitable bachelors were rare: provinces married sons early to escape conscription; she obstinately refused soldiers—she would not give a man up to the Emperor; she wanted him for herself. From 1804 to 1815 she found no match among young men. Beyond predilection for nobility, she had another, excusable mania: to be loved for herself.

She set traps for suitors to test sincerity; her nets were so subtle that unlucky aspirants ensnared themselves and failed without realizing it. A single heedless word or a joke sufficed to condemn a man: one had no heart or delicacy, another told lies, a third was irreligious, a fourth sought money under the cloak of marriage; others showed hereditary gout or immoral antecedents. Like the Church, she demanded a noble priest at her altar: she wanted to be married for imaginary defects she would afterwards redeem by revealing concealed virtues. Nobles married common souls who prioritized interest and lacked understanding of her nobler calculations.

As she approached what is called a “second youth,” her distrust increased. She presented herself unfavorably and played the part so well that last wooers hesitated to link their fate to a woman whose virtuous blind-man’s-buff required penetration men who only wanted ready-made virtue did not have. Fear of marrying for her money made her suspicious beyond reason. She turned to the rich, who sought great marriages; she feared the poor, whom she saw as lacking disinterestedness. After each disappointment she grew to despise mankind, adopting a secret misanthropy which tinged her conversation with bitterness and her eyes with severity. Celibacy hardened her manners; she strove to sanctify herself in despair: noble vengeance, she cut for God the rough diamond rejected by man. Public opinion judged her accordingly; society imagined secret causes of her rejections—deformity or hidden faults—but she was as pure as a saint: healthy and full of kindness; Nature had intended her for motherhood.

Mademoiselle Cormon did not have flattering features that help a woman’s cause. Her beauty was the rustic buxom freshness called la beauté du diable. Broad feet, sinewy legs with thick calves, a stout waist, plump arms—the Norman outline—marked her. Projecting undecided eyes gave her face a sheepish simplicity; an aquiline nose contrasted with a narrow forehead; thick red lips showed kindliness but the forehead revealed a lack of ideas; she was benevolent without grace. Chestnut hair of extraordinary length added vigor. In a ball-dress she might have seemed handsome; officers admired her robust figure. But with years plumpness saturated her body; no corset could restore the young proportions. Her bosom’s amplitude threatened to overwhelm her in bending; her chin doubled, shortening her neck and hindering carriage of the head. No wrinkles, but folds of flesh; jests said she powdered her skin like an infant’s.

This ample person attracted Athanase. Young imaginations often wander over such abundance; Rose was like a plump partridge to a gourmet. Yet at forty she fortified virtue by stern religious practices. A confessor, for three years, directed maceration and scourging—advice modern medicine would find counterproductive. These practices cast a monastic tint; her white skin assumed yellow tones, a down darkened her upper lip, her temples shone; decadence began. Rumor said she suffered rushes of blood to the head. She confided her ills to the Chevalier de Valois, who would pull out his snuff-box and say: “The right composing draught, my dear lady, is a good and kind husband.”

The violence of hope made Rose afraid to look a man in the face lest he perceive her feelings. By wilfulness—perhaps a habit of testing—she treated those she felt attracted to ungraciously, fearful of being deemed foolish. Society took her behavior for revenge for refusals and made fun of her abortive marriages. When 1815 began she was forty-two; desire for marriage bordered on monomania because she saw the chance of children slipping away. She loved in bulk without imagination—Catholic, chaste, incapable of the wiles even of Molière’s Agnès.

For months she counted on chance: the disbandment of Imperial troops and reorganization of the Royal army sent many officers home—some on half-pay—who might have suited her by character and rank. But those returning were either too old or too young, too Bonapartist or too dissipated; their situations mismatched Rose’s rank, fortune, and morals. She prayed for a husband with whom she could be piously happy; none came. Conversations in her salon kept her informed of strangers’ arrivals, fortunes, rank, and habits; but Alençon is not a town of travelers. She despaired; her eye assumed a savage expression. Monsieur de Valois, aware that fidelity to a first attachment is considered a pledge for the future in feminine ethics, responded to her dejection with the officious snuff-box gesture that made her uneasy.

The Abbé de Sponde could not aid her matrimonial manoeuvres. The worthy man, seventy, attributed the Revolution’s disasters to Providence punishing a dissolute Church. He adopted an ascetic life, hiding his charity, prayers, and penances. Detaching from earthly affairs, his mind turned to sufferers and the Church’s needs; he left property management to his niece, who gave him its income while he spent surpluses in secret alms. The abbé loved his niece as a daughter but was abstracted and unable to conceive the flesh’s agitations; used to receiving reverence, Rose dared not tell him of her desires. He would scarcely have liked a husband brought into the house. His silence was affable and benevolent; tall, spare, grave and solemn, his presence carried sacred authority.

Some may think Mademoiselle Cormon employed every means to attain marriage—dress, coquetries—but she did not. She was heroic and immovable in her high-necked chemisette. Her gowns were made by Alençon’s two hump-backed sisters, who had taste. She refused the airy deceits of elegance, choosing substance in all things. Those who laugh at her would have called her sublime for sincere feeling regardless of form.

Critics may say no girl is so ignorant as not to angle for men, and that Mademoiselle Cormon is an impossible type; yet faith and piety are still strong in Brittany and old Alençon. Devotion allows no subtleties: Mademoiselle Cormon trod the path of salvation, preferring prolonged virginity’s sorrows to trickery and sin. In a woman armed with a scourge, virtue will not compromise; consequently love and self-interest must seek her resolutely. Devotion can cause a moral myopia: pious persons turn so to celestial things that they become blind to many earthly matters. The Chevalier de Valois found it hard to decide whether stupidity makes one pious or piety makes one stupid. But whether stupidity or sanctity, their sorrows deserve pity like those of genius—stupidity being more abundant socially.

Thus Rose was guilty in the world’s eyes of virginal ignorance. She was not observant, so she failed to read Athanase Granson’s eyes as others would. Shy herself, she never suspected shyness in others and did not recognize quavering tones as unuttered sentiment. Capable of inventing refinements of feeling that had hindered her early marriage, she could not recognize such refinements in Athanase. That a devoted soul may be foolish and fail to recognize a noble soul is not surprising: heart qualities differ from mind qualities as genius differs from nobility of soul.

This good creature, who grieved at making yearly preserves for only her uncle and herself, was becoming almost ridiculous. Sympathy and mockery mingled: some pitied her goodness, others derided her defects. People speculated on who would inherit her fine property and her uncle’s savings. For a while she was suspected of being, despite appearances, an “original”—a dangerous trait in the provinces, where originality means ideas not understood by others and equality of mind and manner is demanded.

After 1804 the phrase “married like Mademoiselle Cormon” became proverbial in Alençon for ridiculous failures. It was Parisian sarcasm that so excellent a woman should excite laughter. She received the whole society at her house, was charitable, pious, incapable of malice, and embodied the spirit and habits of the place; she had never left them and loved their prejudices and interests.

In spite of an income of eighteen thousand francs from land—a considerable provincial fortune—she lived on a footing with less rich families. At her country place at Prébaudet she rode in an old wicker carriolle hung on white leather straps, drawn by a wheezy mare and hardly protected by rusty leather curtains. Jacquelin cared for it as if it were the finest coupe in Paris. Mademoiselle valued it, having used it for twelve years—a fact she noted with thrifty pride. Most inhabitants were grateful she did not humiliate them by luxury; had she imported a calèche from Paris they would have gossiped more about it than her matrimonial failures. The provinces look solely to results and care little about the elegance of means provided they are efficient.

To complete the picture of the household’s internal habits, one must place around Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde the figures of Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, the cook, who provided for the uncle and niece’s comfort.

Jacquelin, about forty, short, fat, ruddy, with a Breton-sailor face, had been in their service twenty-two years. He waited at table, groomed the mare, gardened, blacked the abbé’s boots, ran errands, chopped wood, drove the carriole, fetched oats, straw, and hay from Prébaudet, and sat in the antechamber evenings, where he slept like a dormouse. He was in love with Josette, a thirty-year-old short, plump woman whose face, “like a dirty apricot,” showed sense and character; it was said she ruled her mistress. Mademoiselle would have dismissed Josette had she married Jacquelin, so the pair, resigned, saved their wages and loved silently, awaiting—like Jews awaiting the Messiah—some distant change. Mariette, the cook of fifteen years’ standing, knew how to prepare the most esteemed dishes of Alençon.

The fat old Norman mare, Penelope, drawn to Prébaudet by Mademoiselle Cormon, deserves mention: she had served the family eighteen years and was treated with the same affectionate mania as any human servant. Kept carefully and fed regularly, Mademoiselle and Jacquelin hoped to use her ten years more. The household’s four dependents—raised and lowered in their mutual subservience to the beast—moved each day with mechanical accuracy. As they said, “they had eaten their white bread first.” Mademoiselle Cormon, driven by a fixed idea, grew hard to please, petty, and nagging—less by nature than to occupy her active mind. Without husband or children, she fell back on minute details: napkins marked “Z” before the “O’s,” whether Penelope had her oats at two o’clock, a neglected dust-layer, a poorly made toast, Josette’s delay in closing blinds that faded furniture. Small things became grounds for serious quarrels. Once Josette gave her the “Journée du Chrétien” instead of the “Quinzaine de Pâques”; Mademoiselle left church and returned home in embarrassment; the town heard of the calamity. She would admonish Josette gently: “Such a thing must never happen again.”

These little quarrels served as catharses for Mademoiselle Cormon, and in secret she was steadied by them. Josette and Jacquelin accepted the mood swings as weather-changes: “It is fine to-day,” or “It rains,” without arraigning the heavens. Each morning they wondered in what humor Mademoiselle would rise, as farmers wonder at dawn mists.

She had ended, naturally, contemplating herself in the infinite pettinesses of life: herself and God, her confessor and the weekly wash, her preserves and church services, and her uncle to care for, occupied her feeble intellect. Her perfect health magnified the slightest digestive trouble. She lived under the iron rod of medical practice, taking quarterly precautionary doses strong enough, it was said, to have killed Penelope but seeming to rejuvenate her mistress. A pimple on her satiny shoulder occasioned endless inquiries into the week’s alimentary articles until Josette triumphantly attributed it to a high hare or to Mariette’s over-seasoning: “No doubt, no doubt, it was the hare!”

Four times a year she spent a number of days at her estate, Prébaudet. In mid-May she liked to see how her apple-trees had “snowed” (the fallen blossoms resembling snow promised an abundant cider harvest), attend winter repairs, order the digging of flower-beds and the vegetable garden that supplied her table. Every season had its business. Before departing she always gave a farewell dinner to intimate friends, though she expected to see them again within weeks. Her departure was the town’s news: shopkeepers repeated it from door to door—“So Mademoiselle Cormon is going to Prébaudet!”—with all manner of provincial commentary.

The Wednesday Suzanne chose to reveal her scandal happened to be such a farewell Wednesday, when Mademoiselle was driving Josette distracted packing. Madame Granson had spent the morning visiting houses, and the malicious Chevalier de Valois was playing piquet with Mademoiselle Armande, who governed aristocratic salons. The scene was set: the chevalier and Madame Granson wanted to see how Mademoiselle Cormon, in her double capacity as marriageable woman and president of the Maternity Society, would receive the news. Du Bousquier, the innocent suitor, walked on the promenade, beginning to suspect Suzanne had tricked him.

On gala days Mademoiselle Cormon’s table was laid about half-past three; the fashionable dined at four. One of her chief pleasures was seeing herself dressed as mistress of the house to receive guests. When fully equipped, a ray of hope would glide into her heart; she felt that nature had not provided in vain and that some brave man would present himself. She revelled in inspecting the dining-room, the oblong table set with a snow-white cloth and twenty covers, the squadron of bottles with honorable labels, the names on little paper bits in the trembling hand of the abbé—the only duty he assumed. After these preliminaries she would go in her finery to her uncle, who took his walk on the terrace overlooking the Brillante and the birds in the coppice. In waiting she never joined the Abbé de Sponde without asking some ridiculous question to rouse him from his meditative silence; she regarded it as her duty to talk and make herself agreeable, a practice her spiritual director, the Abbé Couturier, had advised. She perspired in her corset thinking up ideas; her efforts produced such absurdities as: “No one can be in two places at once—unless it is a little bird,” which somehow provoked a debate on the apostles’ ubiquity. Such talk made the beaux esprits call her “that good Mademoiselle Cormon”—a polite way of saying she was rather a fool; but many of her peers took it literally, answering, “Yes; oh yes! Mademoiselle Cormon is an excellent woman.”

When conversation flagged the Chevalier de Valois, who loved to assist women, would transform her sayings into wit by paradoxically sustaining them; once, when she could not distinguish an ox from a bull he was rescued by the chevalier claiming the only difference was as between sheep and lamb. He often covered her retreats so well that she seemed never to have said anything silly. She took his help as proof of her own adequacy and grew complacent in her ignorance, acquiring a self-possession that gave her speeches a solemnity like patriotic absurdities.

Approaching her uncle one day, she tried to draw him into a matrimonial discussion.

“Uncle,” she said, leaning on his arm, “if everything here below happens according to the will of God, there must be a reason for everything.”

“Certainly,” replied the abbé.

“Then if I remain unmarried—supposing that I do—God wills it?”

“Yes, my child.”

“And yet, as nothing prevents me from marrying to-morrow if I choose, His will can be destroyed by mine?”

“That would be true if we knew what was really the will of God,” replied the former prior of the Sorbonne. “Observe, my daughter, that you put in an if.”

Such childish logic perplexed her. “But, uncle, God did not make women intending them not to marry; otherwise they ought all to stay unmarried; if not, they ought all to marry. There’s great injustice in the distribution of parts.”

“You are blaming the Church, which declares celibacy the better way to God,” replied the abbé.

“But if the Church is right, and all the world were good Catholics, wouldn’t the human race come to an end, uncle?”

“You have too much mind, Rose; you don’t need so much to be happy.”

That remark pleased her and confirmed her good opinion of herself.

Guests arrived and the familiarities between servants and company began: Mariette telling Monsieur du Ronceret she had cooked cauliflowers au gratin “expressly for you, for Mademoiselle knows how you like them”; Josette informing Madame Granson that “Mademoiselle has thought of you, and there’s fish for dinner”; the Chevalier de Valois, in his easy, condescending tone, asking Mariette if there was some dainty he should reserve himself for. Du Bousquier was not invited. Mademoiselle Cormon, faithful to her ambivalence, treated the fifty-year-old suitor ill though she felt inexplicable sentiments toward him. She had refused him yet sometimes repented; a presentiment haunted her that she might yet marry him, and terror prevented her desiring it. Madame Granson and the chevalier, observing her naive glances toward du Bousquier, resolved to ruin his hopes.

Two tardy guests—Monsieur du Coudrai, recorder of mortgages, and Monsieur Choisnel, the notary—kept dinner waiting. As they arrived, Jacquelin announced “They are all in the garden.” The company strolled the terrace above the river, looking at water-plants, mosaics of currents, houses across the Sarthe with old wooden galleries and little gardens where clothes dried; a charming provincial landscape.

The chevalier scanned faces; he had introduced his firebrand into the chief houses. The scandal about Suzanne and du Bousquier had not yet been openly discussed: provincials distill gossip, waiting the right moment. Whispers circulated: “You have heard?” “Yes.” “Du Bousquier?” “And that handsome Suzanne.” “Does Mademoiselle Cormon know of it?” “No.” “Ha!”

The chevalier, noting Madame Granson’s triumphant look, suspected not only her joy but self-interest. He watched Athanase and saw, in a furtive look at Mademoiselle Cormon, a flash that set his plans awry. He offered his arm to Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he revered. She, secretly wishing for the alliance, admired the name de Valois as a social object, though his seeming purity and indifference worried her; she wanted a husband a little dissipated—someone with experience to purify. Observers have remarked pious women’s leaning toward scamps; Mademoiselle’s wish shows this: she would have liked a man to combine the chevalier’s nobility with du Bousquier’s vigor.

To explain what followed, two matters agitated the town, matters in which du Bousquier was implicated. One concerned the rector of Alençon, who had once taken the constitutional oath but was winning Catholics by high virtues. He had supporters among the middle classes rallied by du Bousquier, while Mademoiselle Cormon and the Abbé de Sponde, belonging to the uncompromising “little Church,” refused to receive him. The second was du Bousquier’s idea of building a theatre in Alençon; Athanase Granson and other young clerks championed the project. The chevalier urged Mademoiselle Cormon to save Athanase from this influence.

“Mademoiselle,” he said, “you have so much sense and judgment in social proprieties and influence with his mother; ought you not to use it to save him from perdition? He is irreligious, liberal, agitating for a theatre, the dupe of that disguised republican du Bousquier.”

Mademoiselle responded that his mother said he had so much mind, “and yet he can’t say two words; he stands planted before me as mum as a post—”

“Which doesn’t think at all!” cried the recorder, and the chevalier led Mademoiselle to a flower-pot to speak privately. He warned that lads raised in lycées had bad morals, were marched about in uniform, and, he implied, Athanase’s shyness and pale looks foretold a sad end. He urged her to warn the mother that her son might lose his place in the mayor’s office for such associations.

Madame Granson, who had heard part of the talk, followed Mademoiselle, hoping to learn the outcome. But Jacquelin announced dinner in, and the recorder of mortgages, sensing his opportunity, offered his arm and cut off Monsieur de Valois. The chevalier then addressed Madame Granson directly, warning that Athanase’s theatre intrigues and rector sympathies could cost his job; she gratefully promised to enlighten him.

The chevalier had long seen Athanase’s unyielding republican convictions—rooted in artistic sorrows and a bitter view of society—and knew he would persist. To remain faithful to the Left in Alençon was to earn Mademoiselle Cormon’s aversion.

Thus this outwardly peaceful society was internally agitated by craft and passion like any diplomatic circle. The guests sat and ate as provincials, with appetites undisturbed by Parisian sumptuary fads. Toward the end of the first course Mademoiselle Cormon made the celebrated remark that would be repeated for two years: in the debate against du Bousquier—whom the chevalier and others depicted as a pernicious intriguer—she had innocently said, “As for me, I thought that Monsieur du Bousquier cared chiefly for childish things.” The phrase had enormous success: it undermined the Princess Goritza’s dignity and astonished the chevalier into silent admiration; he applauded with fingers as they do at the Opera.

Prompted by the chevalier, du Bousquier was caricatured as a monster, a Pere Gigogne who had supplied the Foundling Hospital for fifteen years, his immoral habits explained as the cause of Parisian revels. The chevalier, leading the cancan, speculated that a du Bousquier might marry a “Suzette,” invoking Madame du Barry and the Emperor Joseph; Mademoiselle Cormon corrected him with naïve historicism. The Abbé de Sponde, touched with charitable solemnity, urged succor for the poor girl now unlikely to marry. The judge, Madame Granson, and others volleyed puns and jests until the Abbé de Sponde’s grave “Nor can he be a foster-father” quieted them.

Usually a man is ignorant of town rumors; du Bousquier remained superbly unaware of Suzanne’s revelations and was jaunty at coffee. Mademoiselle Cormon, embarrassed, chose to lecture Athanase with platitudes about royalist politics and morality. Athanase listened with a stupid air, gazing at her enormous bust and swelling in his love, which transformed her shrill voice into a soft murmur and her flat remarks into witticisms. Love, the narrator notes, is a maker of false coin, turning copper into gold.

“Will you promise me?” she asked Athanase, and the words pierced his absorbed mind. Had he spoken then, he might have revealed his passion; but his shy absorption ruined him.

“What have you been saying to Mademoiselle Cormon?” demanded his mother.

“Nothing.”

Madame Granson, who believed du Bousquier forever lost in Mademoiselle’s esteem after that evening, thought little more of the remark. The salon later filled: four tables set for piquet, others for backgammon; the group gathered by the fire in evening finery, each repeating their farewell: “So you are going tomorrow to Prébaudet?” “Yes, I really must.”

Mademoiselle Cormon appeared preoccupied; Madame Granson noticed she was thinking.

“What are you thinking of, cousin?” she asked.

“I am thinking,” Mademoiselle replied, “of that poor girl. As president of the Maternity Society, I will give you fifty francs for her.”

“Fifty francs!” cried Madame Granson in astonishment. Mademoiselle never gave so much, but her sudden generosity showed du Bousquier had advanced in her estimation.

“Du Bousquier is not only a monster, he is a villain,” said Madame Granson. “When a man has done a wrong like that, he ought to pay the indemnity. Isn’t it his place rather than ours to look after the girl?—who, to tell you the truth, seems rather questionable.”

Mademoiselle, who could not quite grasp how hearts work, said innocently that perhaps one could be calumniated for loving Athanase, yet be loved truly. Madame Granson, exasperated by her cousin’s naiveté, tried to explain the physical side of things: “It is impossible for Suzanne to love du Bousquier. And if the heart counts for nothing in this affair—” “But, cousin, what do people love with if not their hearts?” inquired the old maid, widening her eyes in honest puzzlement.

Madame Granson advised a show of displeasure: not receiving du Bousquier in her house until he married would manifest exemplary morals. Mademoiselle promised to consult her uncle and the Abbé Couturier upon her return from Prébaudet.

The lights, the women in best clothes, the solemn assembly made Mademoiselle Cormon proud: to many it was the best of Parisian society. Du Bousquier, whistling the pastoral air, was the centre of curiosity. Some declared him strong and misjudged; others saw danger in him. The chevalier, going home, planned to spread a rumor of his marriage to Mademoiselle Cormon to secure attention and advantage, to use his connections to become mayor and then deputy, dreaming of peerage and power, imagining how the marriage would serve his ambitions.

When Madame Granson reached home she fiercely argued with her son Athanase, who could not see the link between his political opinions and his love—a quarrel the household had never known before.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day Mademoiselle Cormon hurried back to Alençon from Prebaudet with Josette and a heap of parcels after an unexpected letter. At breakfast Jacquelin burst in with news: the Abbe de Sponde sent an express—Monsieur de Troisville, the grandson of one of the abbé’s old friends and a former émigré in the Russian army, intended to retire to Alençon and had asked for the abbé’s hospitality. The abbé, absent-minded and alarmed at the responsibility, begged his niece to return at once because the viscount might arrive that very night.

Mademoiselle Cormon, whose monomania for marriage governed her life, was electrified. She ordered instant preparations despite Penelope the mare’s oats and the rain, insisting on leaving immediately and berating Jacquelin and Josette when they hesitated. She dressed, packed, and steered her thoughts toward trousseau and wedding plans as if the viscount’s arrival guaranteed a match. Her sudden, rain-soaked return—Penelope at a gallop, parcels half-packed, mistress flushed with excitement—set Alençon abuzz. Cooks, shopkeepers, and idle townsfolk whispered and soon the news reached high society.

At the Cormon house, Mademoiselle fretted about provisions and lodging for her guest. Mariette scoured the market for delicacies; Jacquelin and Moreau were sent to secure furniture and beds. The house was hurriedly refreshed: china and damask were brought out, the wines and three precious bottles of Madame Amphoux’s liqueur were reserved, and everyone was ordered to appear in their best. Mademoiselle planned to install Monsieur de Troisville in a comfortable chamber near her, picturing herself Vicomtesse de Troisville and arguing with herself about which room to use.

Rumors spread: some said the vicomte was a poor younger son; others called him a wealthy Russian general. The Abbe de Sponde, when pressed, only said he expected Monsieur le Vicomte de Troisville, a noble who had served in Russia and wished to settle. Preparations intensified; the town was in an uproar comparable to the uneasy days of 1814–1815.

At about four the postilion’s click announced the arrival. A lone post-chaise delivered a single gentleman. The abbé introduced him: “Monsieur le Vicomte de Troisville.” Mademoiselle, radiant and trembling, met him with all the hope of a long-besieged spinster. The viscount was not the youthful soldier she imagined but a dignified, elegant man nearer fifty—handsome, with black hair, blue eyes, olive skin, and the air of a seasoned diplomat, as if preserved in Russian ice. He seemed fatigued and composed; the abbé and servants installed him and retired while Mademoiselle walked the river with the abbé until he had made his toilette.

Back at the house, Mademoiselle read the viscount’s manners as proof of his suitability. The abbé casually told her that Monsieur de Troisville was a younger son of a younger branch and probably not wealthy; but that did not deter her. At dinner the vicomte admired the house and its harmony. Mademoiselle, eager and proud, extolled her trees, the nightingales, and her family’s antiquity; she boasted of her assemblies and named the local notables who would visit that evening, among them the Chevalier de Valois, the Marquis d’Esgrignon, and Mademoiselle Armande. The viscount listened with courteous detachment and remarked he had come to look for a house in Alençon.

At dessert the assembled guests arrived. The sight of Madame Amphoux’s precious liqueur-stand, brought out for a rare occasion, confirmed town gossip: clearly something official—perhaps a marriage—was afoot. The Chevalier de Valois, du Bousquier, Madame Granson, and others observed and began to conjecture whether the affair had been arranged for months. Under pressure to be agreeable, Mademoiselle poured out every charm she could muster. The viscount and the Chevalier, both aristocrats by nature, found one another easily, leaving the hostess to watch their exchange like a woman surveilling a promising suitor.

During the conversation, Mademoiselle, cup in hand, overheard a decisive exchange: the Chevalier asked if Monsieur de Troisville had come to settle and whether he was married. Troisville replied simply that he had been married for sixteen years to a daughter of Princess Scherbellof. The words struck Mademoiselle like a blow. She fainted, collapsing into du Bousquier’s arms. He carried her to her bed; Josette cut her corset, and du Bousquier threw water on her to revive her. Six women tended her while gossip and suppressed laughter filled the salon.

The scene was comic to the assembly but devastating to Mademoiselle. The viscount had not intended to deceive; he had come to look for a house, expecting to find a residence suitable for his family of two sons and two daughters. The abbé had been oblivious, and Monsieur de Troisville had no reason to suppose his hostess was considering marriage. The misconception had been entirely unilateral, born of Mademoiselle’s fantasies and her eagerness to welcome a nobleman.

Bleeding and attended by the doctor, Mademoiselle recovered with Christian resignation and even, in the privacy of breakfast, proposed to the viscount the use of her house while he sought a permanent residence. He explained he had four children and would inconvenience her; when she learned that the abbé had already written an answer to her letter offering his help, she realized her mistake and that she had been the town’s laughingstock. Josette urged her to marry quickly to silence gossip; Mademoiselle considered her choices.

Two suitors, aware of the town’s talk and of Mademoiselle’s plight, resolved that morning to press their suit: the Chevalier de Valois, attentive and fastidious, prepared with elaborate toilette, and Monsieur du Bousquier, coarse but ardent and steady, hurried in without affectation. Small things made the difference: the Chevalier’s final touch of rouge delayed him, while du Bousquier arrived promptly.

Du Bousquier, rough in manner but sincere, had been the first to lift Mademoiselle when she fainted and had borne her to safety. He spoke simply and ardently: he loved her still, despite her rebuffs; he had never changed. Mademoiselle, moved by his devotion and the memory of his arms around her, listened and softened. She set conditions: that he would live a Christian life, respect her religious customs and confessors—and then, more tactically, she asked a curious favor. To avoid town scandal, she wanted him to consent to one lie: that their marriage had been arranged for six months. Du Bousquier, captivated, readily agreed. She placed her hand in his; he kissed it solemnly.

At that moment the Chevalier de Valois entered, jaunty and certain of success. Seeing du Bousquier’s confidence, he bantered and then was told of du Bousquier’s supposed long-standing engagement. Du Bousquier, triumphant, declared he had been arranging to sell his house in the rue du Cygne and had been secretly negotiating—so the viscount’s house-hunting might naturally lead to seeing it. The chevalier believed the story and, though biting with satire, offered a mock blessing. He then noticed with a cruel eye that du Bousquier wore a false front; the revelation detracted from the aristocrat’s scorn and produced a final mortifying picture, but did not alter Mademoiselle’s decision.

To cement appearances, Mademoiselle suggested a game of piquet, and the pair were left to be seen as a couple. The abbé and Monsieur de Troisville returned from house-hunting; in private Mademoiselle asked her uncle to tell the viscount that the rue du Cygne house was for sale—an arrangement that would match du Bousquier’s invented story. She could not confess the lie to her absent-minded uncle. The falsehood prospered: the next day all Alençon knew the Cormons were to be married, and like the town’s earlier commotion the rumor spread and sustained itself. The Chevalier de Valois, wounded in pride, deemed the outcome fitting: the Cormons had ended as they had begun, with a negligible distinction between steward and purveyor.

Thus, through a comic chain of misunderstanding, eagerness, and ambition, Mademoiselle Cormon’s hopes of a distinguished foreign viscount dissolved into a pragmatic match with the faithful, blunt du Bousquier; the town, already fervent, took its amusements and judgments, and the story of the aborted romance and the subsequent convenient lie occupied Alençon’s gossip for days.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage pierced Athanase Granson but he showed no outward sign. He learned of it at du Ronceret’s, where his mother played boston; she thought him pale, unaware he had been shaken all day by rumor. Mademoiselle Cormon was the stake of Athanase’s life; when that hope failed, his imagination magnified the disaster. A product of Imperial fatalism, he sat as if indifferent, eyes fixed on Madame du Ronceret’s cards. His apparent coldness masked despair; it explained his recent withdrawal from politics and the satirical gaiety that repelled others.

After that fatal evening he wandered the Sarthe’s picturesque countryside—beneath a poplar he sat vacant-eyed. Neighbors told Madame Granson something was wrong; she fancied he meditated some great work. He ceased engaging in public life; no friend or woman came to console him. Had the gorgeous Suzanne crossed him then, different miseries might have been born.

Suzanne did return. Fired by tales of the Marquis de Montauran, she had hoped to play upon men and to help her mother and Athanase. Months after leaving, she passed through her native town with an artist to revisit Fougeres and the Marquis’s scene. She imagined arriving incognito and planned—delicately—to send Athanase money.

For a month the town vacillated about Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage. The sale of du Bousquier’s house to the Marquis de Troisville raised doubts, Choisnel heard nothing of a contract, yet on the twentieth day Lepressoir, the liberals’ notary, went to her house and the contract was signed. This was the first of many sacrifices the bride would make to her husband. Du Bousquier bore a hatred of Choisnel; anonymous letters revealed to Mademoiselle Cormon that Suzanne—so scandalous in rumor—was, for du Bousquier, as truly a virgin as she. Suzanne admitted trickery, sent a thousand francs to the Maternity Society, and added what mischief she could against the old purveyor; the society resolved to help only misfortunes that had occurred.

Despite gossip, the banns were published and the bride retired to Prebaudet, where du Bousquier visited each morning with bouquets and returned for dinner. On a dull rainy June morning Mademoiselle Cormon and the Sieur du Bousquier were married at noon in Alencon’s parish church. They drove from the mayor’s office to the church in an open calèche secretly sent from Paris—the loss of the old carriole lamented as a local calamity. The arrival of Parisian luxury alarmed the town: fears of rising rents, foreign furniture, and the triumph of Parisian customs ensued. Jacquelin charged ten sous to show the new vehicle. Penelope had succumbed to pleurisy six weeks before the marriage.

Madame du Bousquier entered the church with her left foot—an omen as the “Left” had political meaning—and the priest opened his book at De Profundis. No wedding party followed; they departed immediately for Prebaudet. Parisian manners seemed poised to supplant provincial ways; only Jacquelin and Josette’s gay wedding suggested the old spirit lived on.

Du Bousquier used proceeds of his former residence to restore and modernize the hotel Cormon, deciding to remain two seasons at Prebaudet and bringing the Abbe de Sponde with them. The town feared comfort. One morning the bridegroom drove in from Prebaudet in a fine tilbury with René in livery; he had deposited his wife’s savings on the Grand-Livre and, by manipulative speculation, made himself a personal fortune nearly equal to hers.

These forebodings were eclipsed by a still grimmer event. On the evening of the ceremony Athanase and his mother sat by a little fagot fire. Madame Granson proposed they go to the du Roncerets’; Athanase, in a childlike voice, asked her to stay a little longer, calling her “my dear mamma.” He clung to her with desperate tenderness, kissed her eyes and gray hair, and revealed he wished to leave Alencon, to suffer away from her. She refused to be parted; he insisted and took money she had hidden for his outfit.

After a brief outing to du Ronceret’s, Athanase went to the Sarthe, sat beneath his poplar, reviewed the glorious future that had vanished, and quietly slipped into the deep pool he had chosen. Madame Granson returned at half-past nine to find a short letter—“My good mother, I have departed; don’t be angry with me.” She thought he had left cleverly and slept in peace.

The Sarthe, swollen since morning, brought eels and a fisherman’s net where Athanase had flung himself. At six the fisherman drew in his net—and the young body. Friends prepared the mother for the remains. The suicide excited Alencon: the poor young man of promise had no protector the night before and many cried, “I would have helped him.” The Chevalier de Valois explained the act in terms of revenge, revealing Athanase’s sincere love for Mademoiselle Cormon; recollections confirmed him. Women wept; Madame Granson’s grief was silent and concentrated—private mourning that society scarcely understood.

Before Madame du Bousquier returned to town, Madame du Ronceret had driven to Prebaudet to fling the corpse upon the bride’s roses and sober her bridal month. On the road back, Madame du Bousquier met Madame Granson; the mother’s dying look, full of malediction and reproach, struck the young woman to the heart.

The evening after the catastrophe Madame Granson sought the town’s rector, though she disliked him. After placing her son in his shroud she found him engaged in charity—putting away yarns for the poor. He took her into his dining-room and promised to avoid scandal: he would say the masses and ensure Athanase lay in consecrated ground, but quietly. Toward midnight a coffin was borne clandestinely to the parish church by four young men, comrades of Athanase. A few friends, veiled women, and half a dozen intimates attended. The rector, assisted by a discreet choirboy, said the mortuary mass. The body was carried to a corner of the cemetery; a black wooden cross without inscription marked its place. Athanase lived and died in shadow; the bishop was silent. The mother’s piety redeemed the impiety of her son’s last act.

Months later, half beside herself with grief, Madame Granson went to the spot where her son had drowned—perhaps to recover his last thoughts. There she saw a woman approach weeping: it was Suzanne. Told of the catastrophe at the hotel du More, Suzanne had planned to send Athanase several thousand francs under false pretense had he lived; seeing Madame Granson, she whispered as she passed, “I loved him!” Suzanne did not leave Alencon without changing the bride’s orange blossoms to rue; she declared Madame du Bousquier would always be Mademoiselle Cormon, revenging Athanase and the Chevalier with one cutting tongue.

Alencon then witnessed a slower suicide: the Chevalier de Valois died in life for fourteen years. Three months after the du Bousquier marriage his linen frayed, hair unkempt; elegance collapsed. Where once he had maintained appearances with meticulous care, he now showed the power of soul over body: hope gone, he grew old overnight. His vanity vanished; clever sayings grew rare, though appetites lingered. His intercourse with the Princess Goritza lessened. One day he appeared in Mademoiselle Armande’s salon with his calf on his shin—the bankruptcy of grace that struck Alencon with horror. The chevalier who had waited ten years for Mademoiselle Cormon now frightened society by his decay. Gossip suggested libertinism; liberals blamed him for scandals once attributed to du Bousquier. The faubourg Saint-Germain pitied him and brought back a few smiles, but enmity piled on du Bousquier. Eleven persons deserted the Cormon salon for the d’Esgrignons.

The old maid’s marriage divided Alencon into two parties. The salon d’Esgrignon represented upper aristocracy (the returning Troisvilles joined it); the Cormon salon under du Bousquier represented a class of opinions neither truly liberal nor resolutely royalist—those who had given birth to votes like the famous 221. The salon du Ronceret, secretly allied to Cormon, was boldly liberal.

The Abbe de Sponde, returning from Prebaudet, suffered inwardly. He told no word to his niece; to Mademoiselle Armande he confessed he would have preferred the Chevalier de Valois to Monsieur du Bousquier. The abbe mourned the loss of his little grove and lindens—symbols of home and prayer—cut down for an English garden. Du Bousquier, acting with Abbe Couterier’s authorization, had committed wrongs to please his wife; restorations destroyed the venerable dignity and patriarchal air of the old house. The salon’s bourgeois dignity evaporated when turned to white and gold with mahogany and blue satin; stucco columns, glass doors, and Greek moldings made magnificence out of season.

At first the town gabbled, but months later took pride in the luxury; manufacturers restored houses, modern furniture arrived, and astral lamps were seen. The Abbe de Sponde perceived the secret unhappiness the marriage imposed on his niece. The character of noble simplicity that had governed their lives was lost the first winter when du Bousquier gave two balls every month; the abbe prayed on his knees while the revels lasted. Political tone in the salon was perverted. The abbe fathomed du Bousquier and shuddered at his imperiousness; he saw his niece lose control of her property—left only the linen and table to manage—while Jacquelin became coachman, René groom, and a Parisian chef took over. The triumph of will intoxicated the narrow-minded du Bousquier.

At first du Bousquier constrained himself out of respect for the abbe and to avoid vexing him, but it was his hand that dealt the blow sending the old priest to his grave. If one interprets “intolerance” as firmness of principle and does not condemn the Abbe de Sponde’s Catholic soul, one can understand his sorrow when he saw in his niece’s salon an apostate priest reconciled with the rector—the renunciation of orthodoxy that to him was treachery. Du Bousquier had reconciled the minister of Saint-Leonard with the parish rector; his wife thought peace had been accomplished, but the abbe saw betrayal. The bishop visited du Bousquier; orthodoxy seemed to expire in the diocese. The abbe died when orthodoxy thus expired.

In 1819 the abbe’s property increased Madame du Bousquier’s income to twenty-five thousand francs, not counting Prebaudet or the Val-Noble house. Du Bousquier returned to his wife the capital of her savings and made her use it to buy lands contiguous to Prebaudet, enlarging the domain. He passed for one of the department’s richest men—constantly a candidate of the liberals, narrowly missing victories, then ostentatiously repudiating liberals by seeking ministerial royalist election. This rancorous republican, mad with ambition, resolved to rival and ruin aristocratic Alencon when it regained power. He strengthened himself with the Church by a deceitful feint of piety—accompanying his wife to mass, giving to convents, aiding congregations, siding with clergy. Secretly supported by liberals, protected by the Church, calling himself a constitutional royalist, he kept beside the aristocracy to ruin it—and he succeeded.

He gave money to revive point d’Alencon manufacture, renewed the linen trade; the town gained a factory. Insulating himself on popular interests, he risked little—backed by fortune he could wait. He posed as a banker, promoted insurance companies, public conveyances, petitions for roads and bridges. Warned, the government struggled injudiciously; authorities yielded as community interest demanded. Du Bousquier embittered provincial nobility against court nobility and the peerage; he rallied constitutional royalists to side with the Journal des Debats and M. de Chateaubriand against the throne—an ungrateful opposition tied to ignoble interests that helped journalism’s triumph in 1830.

After 1830 he became receiver-general, relying on relations with the Duc d’Orleans and Monsieur de Folmon, receiving about eighty thousand francs a year. Publicly he was respectable, steady, upright, and obliging. Alencon owed him its industrial ties and the sudden proliferation of private carriages. Du Ronceret’s remark, “Du Bousquier is a very strong man,” became local consensus.

But that strength had a double meaning. The public politician did not resemble the domestic husband. This great citizen, generous to the world, was a despot at home, devoid of conjugal affection. Astute, hypocritical, sly—this Cromwell of Val-Noble—behaved with velvet glove and iron hand. His wife bore no children; Suzanne’s remark and the chevalier’s insinuations seemed justified. The liberal bourgeoisie and church party blamed Madame du Bousquier, saying she was too old and pregnancy dangerous. Friends tried to console her, praising du Bousquier’s station. Misjudged by society, the pious woman displayed virtues at home. She lived in tears but presented a placid face, telling herself she had loved the Chevalier de Valois but married du Bousquier; Athanase’s death pursued her like remorse. Her uncle’s death deepened her sorrow; it spread slowly through her being.

The Chevalier de Valois contrived to reveal du Bousquier’s baseness to her. The chevalier, expert in love, divined the married man. Du Bousquier’s salon excluded him and those who had slighted the Cormon household; impervious to ridicule, he had vast fortune and power. To please her husband, Madame du Bousquier had broken off relations with the d’Esgrignon household, visiting only briefly when he left for Paris. Three years after her marriage, at the Abbe de Sponde’s death, Mademoiselle Armande led Madame du Bousquier to Mr. d’Esgrignon’s. Finding the Chevalier de Valois there, he took her hand and, in a tender, fatherly manner, questioned her happiness. Surrounded by friends, they drew from her a naïve confession of her house—her inability to have children and the deceptions of her marriage—thus making du Bousquier ridiculous and odious for a time. Ridicule waned but the incident deepened his hatred of the d’Esgrignons.

By way of reprisal, du Bousquier, editor of the Courrier de l’Orne, inserted a spiteful notice offering a reward to anyone proving the existence of “one Monsieur de Pombreton” before, during, or after the Emigration—turning private vengeance into public pen.

Although her marriage was essentially negative, Madame du Bousquier saw some advantages: a remarkable man about her was preferable to solitude. Du Bousquier was preferable to a dog or canaries; he showed for his wife a sentiment more real and less selfish than that of servants or hopeful heirs. Later she regarded him as an instrument of divine wrath for past desires: she had brought sorrow on Madame Granson and hastened her uncle’s death. Obedient to religion that bids one kiss the rod, she praised him publicly but wept in prayer.

Bruised and immolated, compelled to make her husband happy and attached by habit and duty, her life became perpetual contradiction. She had married a man she hated but whom she must care for. Often she walked with angels while he ate her preserves with satisfaction. She watched to satisfy his slightest wish; when he kissed her forehead and called her “a good woman,” tears came to her eyes. Du Bousquier likely made concessions enough to earn respect; Catholic virtue did not require total acting. Often she listened while men she admired argued against the Church and shuddered. A stupid word from her was cut short by his glance.

Worries stupefied her; she found it more dignified to concentrate on inner thoughts and resign herself to a life of meritorious submission, giving herself to severe religious practices and ceasing to think of vanities. She presented to the world a union of Christian virtue; du Bousquier was one of the luckiest men in France. “She will be a simpleton to her last breath,” said the former collector, who nevertheless dined with her twice a week.

This history would be incomplete without noting the coincidence of the Chevalier de Valois’s death with that of Suzanne’s mother. The chevalier died with the monarchy in August 1830. He had joined Charles X.’s cortege at Nonancourt, escorted it to Cherbourg with the Troisvilles, and carried fifty thousand francs—his savings—offering them to a faithful royal friend for transmission, speaking of his approaching death and declaring the money came originally from the king’s goodness. Whether his zeal moved the abandoned Bourbon is unknown; Caesarine, residuary legatee, received only six hundred francs a year. The chevalier returned to Alencon, weakened, and died the day Charles X. landed on foreign shore.

Madame du Val-Noble and her protector, fearing liberal vengeance, lingered where Suzanne’s mother died. At the chevalier’s sale, Suzanne bought the famous snuff-box portrait of Princess Goritza for a thousand francs; two years later a collector obtained it from Madame du Val-Noble. If the dead could see after them, the chevalier’s head might blush.

If this history prompts possessors of relics to make codicils and bequeath souvenirs to loving hands, it serves the romantic. It contains a higher moral: the need for a new species of education—chairs of anthropology—where Germany outstrips France. Modern myths are poorly understood; had Mademoiselle Cormon read or had a professor of anthropology in the Orne, or if she had read Ariosto, the disasters of her conjugal life might have been avoided. She would probably have known why Angelica prefers Medoro to Orlando—Medoro as the mythic courtier, Orlando the myth of impotent revolutions. We publish this opinion of a pupil of Monsieur Ballanche without assuming responsibility.

No information reached us about the fate of the negroes’ heads in diamonds. You may see Madame du Val-Noble every evening at the Opera; thanks to the chevalier’s education she almost has the air of a well-bred woman.

Madame du Bousquier still lives; is not that as much as to say she still suffers? After sixty—the age women allow confessions—she told Madame du Coudrai she had never been able to endure the idea of dying an old maid.
