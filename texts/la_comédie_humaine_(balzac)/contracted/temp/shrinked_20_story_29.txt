EUGENIE GRANDET

I

In some provincial towns certain houses give off a monastic melancholy; silence reigns, life seems frozen, and the routine is punctuated only by the solitary figure at a window. Such an air hung over a house in Saumur at the end of a steep, seldom-used street that climbed toward the château. The pebble pavement and three-century-old buildings, with great carved oaken beams, slate-covered façades, and worn window-sills, recalled antiquarians’ delight. Doors studded with large nails bore traces of history—Protestant marks or the insignia of forgotten bourgeois families—so that the street itself seemed to record France’s past.

Beside a dilapidated house stood a country gentleman’s mansion, its stone arch faintly showing armorial bearings rubbed by revolutions since 1789. The merchants’ ground floors were not modern shops but dark rooms where goods were exposed plainly. A girl knitting might call to her parents to serve customers, who were attended with varying courtesy.

In this region the weather shaped commerce. Merchants, wine-growers, and coopers watched the sky anxiously, fearing frost or rain that could ruin them. On Saturdays vineyards summoned their owners and the streets fell silent, where life unfolded plainly and neighbors observed one another closely.

The old town of Saumur, scene of this tale, contained a particularly melancholy mansion owned by Monsieur Grandet. To grasp the house’s meaning one must know the man.

Monsieur Grandet began life as a master cooper in 1789 and married the daughter of a rich wood merchant. With his savings and his wife’s dowry he bought prime vineyard land during the sale of church property, displaying shrewdness that would mark his rise. He grew in reputation as protector of local nobility and supplier to Republican armies, accumulating wealth and influence through timely purchases and cautious sales. By 1806 Grandet was mayor; that year he also inherited three fortunes, cementing his standing in Saumur. His estate came to include vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him among the region’s wealthiest men.

The townspeople speculated about the extent of his riches. He had a knack for timing sales to secure maximum profit, and his financial acumen made him both admired and feared. By 1816 estimates put his fortune at nearly four million francs, and many believed he kept an equal sum in cash. His reputation suppressed earlier mockery; his decisions came to be treated almost as community law. Frugality governed his household; expenditures were limited to necessities.

Grandet’s manner was plain and unpretending. He spoke little and stuttered when pressed into long conversation—a seeming lack of eloquence that concealed calculation in business. He consulted his wife before committing to affairs, keeping her in a subservient state. Physically he was stout, with a round, crafty visage and simple, practical dress signaling thrift. Only a few had access to his house, among them a young magistrate, C. de Bonfons, who hoped to marry Grandet’s daughter, Eugénie.

Social life in Saumur centred on competition for Eugénie’s hand, chiefly between the Cruchots and the des Grassins. The Cruchots, a powerful family, sought her for their nephew; the des Grassins pushed their son Adolphe. This rivalry buzzed through the town, with many guessing Grandet’s plans for his daughter. In early 1811 the Cruchots gained an edge when the estate of Froidfond was bought through negotiations arranged for Grandet; he paid in gold—an ostentatious act that further displayed his wealth and confirmed his position as a formidable figure in Saumur.

It is now clear what is meant by “the house of Monsieur Grandet,” a cold, silent dwelling above the town, sheltered by ruined ramparts. The entrance has two pillars and a tufa arch, a soft white stone worn to irregular holes that give the arch and walls a jail-like look. Above the arch is a crumbling bas-relief of the four seasons and a plinth where plants, even a small cherry tree, have taken root.

The door is solid oak, brown and split, secured by iron bolts. A small rusted grating in the middle panel lets curious visitors glimpse a dark, slimy vault leading to a garden enclosed by thick, damp walls, remnants of the ramparts that border neighboring gardens.

The principal ground-floor room is a large hall serving as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining room. It is where the barber cuts Monsieur Grandet’s hair and where farmers and officials conduct business. The hall, with two street-facing windows, is entirely wooden, with gray panels and exposed beams. An old brass clock and a greenish mirror adorn the mantel above a white stone chimney. Faded tapestry chairs furnish the room; in each corner are closets or buffets with dirty shelves.

An old card table stands between the windows, topped with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. Red gros de Tours curtains, a rare luxury, contrast with Monsieur Grandet’s frugality. A straw chair by the window lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair occupy the rest. For fifteen years, Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet’s lives have revolved around constant work; fires are lit only on specific dates, and they rely on a foot-warmer for warmth in chilly months.

La Grande Nanon, the family servant, has been with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years, earning only sixty francs a year but saving enough to invest in an annuity. Despite an unattractive appearance, she was welcomed into the household and worked tirelessly—cooking, washing, and managing domestic affairs with unwavering loyalty. Grandet’s rare acts of kindness, such as giving her an old watch, were regarded as monumental gestures.

In 1811, during a difficult grape harvest, Grandet’s treatment of Nanon combined affection and control: he depended on her labor while she remained devoted. Nanon’s gratitude for employment and the meager kindnesses she received formed a bond both simple and complex, reflecting the dynamics of power and dependence in their relationship.

The household displayed a stark contrast between Nanon’s careworn but efficient domain and the expectations of other servants in Saumur. Nanon’s kitchen was a model of cleanliness and order; she often worked late into the night and slept in a small closet-like space. The upper floors, in contrast to the hall’s modest comforts, were bare and unadorned.

On a November evening in 1819, Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The family prepared for a gathering, with local notables arriving to pay their respects. Grandet presented Eugenie with a gold coin, a tradition he had maintained for years, while Madame Grandet gave her a new dress. The family’s finances and habits were steeped in thrift and preparation: Grandet urged Eugenie to save for her future marriage.

As the evening progressed, the arrival of guests, including the des Grassins family, introduced a competitive tone. Madame des Grassins, a lively woman, and her son Adolphe presented gifts to Eugenie, sparking jealousy among other visitors. Interactions were laced with underlying tensions: compliments and attentions masked rivalry and calculation. Grandet observed the dynamics with a mix of pride and possessiveness over his daughter.

The gathering, full of laughter and light games, concealed attendees’ deeper motivations as they subtly vied for a share of Grandet’s wealth. The scene blended familial warmth with cold calculations of social status, Grandet at the center, manipulating relationships for his benefit. The simplicity of Eugenie and her mother’s lives contrasted sharply with the materialism of their visitors, revealing the complexities of human relations and the hidden motives behind polite society.

As the evening unfolded, a loud knock at the door signaled an unexpected interruption that would change the course of the night.

Nanon took a candle and opened the door, followed by Monsieur Grandet. His wife, alarmed, called to him, and the players exchanged worried glances. Monsieur des Grassins suggested they all go, sensing something ominous. A young man arrived with a porter carrying trunks; Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game and left to speak with the newcomer.

The players sat but did not resume. Madame des Grassins asked if the traveler was from Saumur; her husband said he was from Paris. The notary noted the time, confirming the diligence was on schedule. Abbé Cruchot asked if the gentleman was young; des Grassins confirmed he was, with heavy luggage.

Eugénie remarked on Nanon's absence, and the president suggested the newcomer might be a relative. Madame Grandet gently urged them to continue, sensing her husband’s annoyance. Adolphe, trying to be discreet, hinted the young man might be a cousin, but his mother silenced him.

Grandet returned alone; the traveler entered and received polite bows from the men and curtsies from the women. Madame Grandet offered him warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed her concern. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugénie for her hospitality but insisted he was not tired.

Madame des Grassins asked if he had come from the capital. Charles, adjusting his eyeglass, surveyed the room and its occupants. He noted the loto game that had been underway while Eugénie observed him with admiration and curiosity. At twenty-two, handsome and confident, he aimed to impress his provincial relatives with Parisian sophistication.

Sent by his father to stay with his uncle in Saumur, Charles intended to display his fashionable attire and lifestyle. He brought luxurious clothing and accessories—a hunting costume, various waistcoats, a gold toilet set—and, despite his father's instructions to travel modestly, had taken a private coupé to flaunt his means.

His elegance contrasted sharply with the provincial guests. The Cruchots, shabby and unkempt, were taken aback; locals reacted with both admiration and disdain at his nonchalance and fashion. Eugénie, captivated by her cousin's charm, longed for the world he represented; enchanted by his appearance and the delicate items he carried, she felt a keen desire to connect with him.

The loto ended when Nanon returned to ask for sheets for Charles’s bed. Madame Grandet followed her; the guests, sensing the evening's end, began to leave. Eugénie, seized by a new impulse, excused herself to help her mother and Nanon prepare Charles’s room, eager to make it comfortable and elegant. She suggested improvements—a warming pan, a wax candle instead of tallow—using her birthday as an excuse to persuade Nanon.

While Eugénie fussed over the room, Madame des Grassins engaged Charles, subtly vying for his attention and hinting at social opportunities in Saumur. He responded graciously, while the others speculated about his intentions and possible interest in Eugénie. Abbé Cruchot made sly comments that revealed the underlying tensions and ambitions among the guests.

Grandet read a letter from his brother revealing a dire financial situation and pleading that his son be cared for. The letter described impending bankruptcy and hoped Grandet would shield Charles from the fallout. Grandet masked his emotions as he processed the implications; his demeanor shifted, showing the complex interplay of familial duty and personal greed.

When the guests departed, Grandet offered his nephew a modest room, downplaying his wealth. The evening closed with speculation: Charles's presence could unsettle Saumur. The Cruchots and des Grassins recognized a common rival and resolved to unite to prevent Eugénie from falling for her cousin and to manipulate the situation to their advantage.

################################################
When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late to discuss the matters that had brought him and set the household schedule: breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, dinner at five. He professed poverty despite appearances, insisting he had earned his money by hard work. He told Nanon to bring candles for Charles; Charles assured his aunt he needed nothing and bade them goodnight.

Charles accepted a candle from Nanon, so worn it resembled tallow. Grandet led him through the house, avoiding the cold stairwell. As they climbed, Charles’s expectations dwindled; the staircase was shabby, and he wondered why his father had sent him to such a place. On the landing he noticed the doors, especially the one sealed off that led to Grandet’s office — the secretive room where Grandet did his business and was said to hoard his wealth.

Across from the office, Eugénie, Grandet’s daughter, sat in her room. After a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon if he needed anything because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon returned with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed with a grumble as he left.

Charles surveyed the shabby decor and questioned his father’s intentions in sending him there; he felt out of place and uncertain of his purpose. Meanwhile Eugénie rose early and began her morning routine with a new sense of beauty and longing. Dressing, she admired her reflection but felt inadequate beside her cousin Charles.

The garden and courtyard that had once seemed dull now appeared full of charm. Her heart swelled with unfamiliar emotions toward Charles; she longed to impress him and soften his situation.

Eugénie hurried to Nanon, asking her to prepare cream for Charles’s breakfast. Nanon reminded her they needed Grandet’s permission for provisions. When Grandet entered, he and Nanon engaged in a rare quarrel over supplies, revealing his miserly nature. Eventually, reluctantly, he agreed to give some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast Grandet took Eugénie for a walk to the fields, where they met Cruchot, the notary. Grandet spoke of his land and the recent sale of poplar trees, showing his calculating side. Cruchot mentioned gossip that Charles might be a suitor for Eugénie. Grandet reacted vehemently, saying he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin.

The declaration shocked Eugénie, dashing her hopes. She returned home troubled by her father’s harshness and what it meant for her feelings toward Charles. Back at the house, Grandet received news of his brother’s suicide — a development that would have dire consequences for Charles. As the family sat down for breakfast, the atmosphere was heavy with unspoken grief and tension.

Eugénie, feeling deep empathy for Charles, began to cry, grasping the weight of his situation. Grandet remained indifferent, absorbed in his own concerns. Eugénie and her mother shared a moment of mutual understanding; Eugénie expressed her desire to do something kind for Charles and, despite the risk of Grandet’s wrath, she and Nanon conspired to prepare a better breakfast for him.

As they worked, Eugénie’s affection for Charles deepened; she felt an urgent need to show him kindness and support amid their family’s troubles.

After two hours of preparation, Eugenie produced a simple breakfast that broke with the family's usual custom of a standing midday meal of bread, fruit, and wine. She set the table and placed an armchair for her cousin Charles, anxious about her father's reaction; Madame Grandet reassured her she would take responsibility if her husband returned.

Charles arrived in Parisian fashion, cheerful despite a difficult journey, saying he rarely ate before midday but was glad for something now. He joked about wanting a partridge—something Eugenie wished she could provide—and his charm quickly captivated her. Their conversation revealed the contrast between his accustomed luxury and her modest life. When Nanon brought eggs, Charles delightedly forgot his joke; he complimented Eugenie, making her blush, and their brief touch as he showed her his ring sparked a gentle connection.

The mood shifted when Nanon served coffee, which Charles dismissed as old-fashioned. The women exchanged worried glances, uneasy about Monsieur Grandet's imminent return. When he entered, Grandet surveyed the scene with immediate suspicion, noting the sugar and questioning its source, heightening the tension. Unaware of the household dynamics, Charles asked for sugar in his coffee; Grandet curtly suggested more milk instead. Eugenie quietly put the sugar back on the table, a small act of defiance against her father's controlling manner.

Grandet then spoke brusquely to Charles, hinting at bad news. Still oblivious, the young man asked about his father's health. Grandet delivered the devastating truth: Charles's father had committed suicide, leaving him destitute. The news shattered Charles; he broke down in uncontrollable grief. Eugenie and her mother were moved to deep compassion, while Grandet detachedly turned to the financial consequences. He explained bankruptcy to Eugenie, emphasizing the dishonor it brought; she struggled to grasp the harsh reality and felt profound sympathy for her cousin.

Declaring that Charles must seek his fortune in the West Indies, Grandet eventually agreed—reluctantly—to fund the journey. For a moment a warmth passed between father and daughter, yet the house remained heavy with sorrow as Charles's cries echoed. Eugenie and her mother sought him in his room, where he mourned his father. Eugenie, moved, urged him to preserve his honor, though he was engulfed by grief and could think of little else.

Left alone, Eugenie felt a stirring in her heart, recognizing the depth of feeling she had for Charles. The day transformed her perspective: she resolved to share in his mourning, a decisive shift in her life. Their quiet home was now laden with loss, and Eugenie found herself drawn into the entwined complexities of love and sorrow.

About four o’clock a sharp knock at the door startled Madame Grandet.

“What can have happened to your father?” she asked her daughter.

Grandet entered rubbing his hands. “Wife,” he said, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I pretended to do nothing in the market-place, and the Belgian came to me in despair. The owners of the good vineyards are holding back their vintages, but I didn’t stop them. I made a deal for my vintage at two hundred francs a puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; here are six louis for you. In three months, wines will have fallen.”

His quiet words, laced with sarcasm, sent a shock through the town. Eugenie, counting, asked if he had a thousand puncheons that year. “Yes, little one.” “Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?” “Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.” “Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s temper flared; he had not thought of his nephew. “What’s this? Ever since that dandy came into my house, everything goes wrong! You act as if you have the right to buy sugar-plums and throw feasts. I won’t have it! I know my duty! I won’t take lessons from you or anyone else. I’ll do what’s proper for my nephew, and you needn’t meddle. Don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon!”

Madame Grandet said Charles had not come down; Eugenie answered that he was weeping for his father. Grandet, momentarily distracted, retreated to his private room to consider investing the money. His thinning forest land had yielded six hundred thousand francs; with the wine sale his total came to nine hundred thousand. The thought of twenty percent from the funds tempted him; later he resolved, more soberly, “I shall get eight percent interest. In two years, I’ll have fifteen hundred thousand francs, which I will draw out in gold.”

Nanon knocked for dinner. Charles refused to eat. “So much saved,” Grandet observed; Nanon agreed. “Bah! He won’t cry long. Hunger drives wolves out of the woods.” Madame Grandet suggested they put on mourning. “What will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not in clothes,” Grandet retorted. “But mourning for a brother is necessary; the Church commands it.” “Buy your mourning out of your six louis. A hat-band is enough for me.”

Eugenie sewed in silence while Grandet calculated profits and the town buzzed with news of his sale and his brother’s failure. Nanon’s spinning was the only sound in the house. “Nothing should be wasted,” Grandet said, lost in future wealth.

He went to Charles’s room, announcing himself loudly: “Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” Noticing a wax candle, his tone turned scornful: “What’s this? A wax candle! How did they filch a wax candle?” Madame Grandet and Eugenie, frightened, retreated to the landing.

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” he asked his wife. “My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” she answered, trembling. “The devil take your good God!” he growled. His contempt for the future—his focus entirely on the present market and profit—was palpable: money, more than faith or morals, governed his view of law, politics, and human relations.

He bade his wife good-night, promising to speak in the morning. She went to bed anxious. Eugenie kissed her mother’s brow. “Oh! my good mother,” she said, “tomorrow I will tell him it was I.” “No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me,” her mother replied. “Do you hear, mamma?” Eugenie pressed. “What?” “He is weeping still.” “Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold.”

Eugenie’s day was heavy with worry. Her dormant feelings for Charles stirred; she woke often imagining his suffering. In the early morning she hurried to his room and found him asleep in an armchair, exhausted by grief. She admired his handsome face now marked by sorrow.

“Pardon me, my cousin,” she said when he woke. “There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus,” she urged. “That is true,” he replied. “Well, then, adieu!” she said, retreating, ashamed and happy.

Her innocent boldness was a turning point. She worried how Charles would interpret the visit, hoping he would believe she loved him. The next morning she and her mother waited anxiously for Grandet. When he arrived, seeming absent-minded, he kissed Eugenie and sat to breakfast without recalling his threats. “What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he remarked. Nanon said the young man was asleep. “So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” Grandet mocked, an unexpected softness surprising his wife.

A poor sleeper, Grandet spent nights devising schemes to win at the commercial game. He sought to help his nephew but without spending a penny — to save his brother’s honor by manipulating outsiders and preserving his own fortune. It was showy charity: protect family prestige while keeping the purse-strings tight.

In Grandet’s absence, Eugenie found comfort in caring for Charles; she prepared his breakfast with affectionate attention. She asked through his closed door whether he would eat downstairs or in his room; he deferred to her. “How do you feel?” she inquired. “Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry,” he admitted. She decided to bring his breakfast to his room rather than annoy her father, and hurried to the kitchen, her heart uplifted.

The simple, tender care of Eugenie and her mother was a new warmth for Charles, contrasting with the indifference he had known in Paris. Eugenie’s innocence and genuine affection drew him closer, and between them grew a silent understanding and mutual feeling.

When Grandet returned later he found the women quietly at their work, unaware of the emotional bond forming between his nephew and daughter. After breakfast Grandet invited the Cruchots to dinner, surprising everyone.

As the day unfolded, the household’s atmosphere shifted: Grandet’s avaricious mind continued plotting advantages, but a delicate human connection—Eugenie’s care for Charles—had been kindled, hinting at the deeper complexities of love, family duty, and the burdens wealth imposes on them all.

At that moment Saumur buzzed more over the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than it had over the previous night's sale of his vintage, which the wine-growers had taken as a betrayal. Grandet, who considered himself superior to those he duped, paid little heed to public opinion.

When the des Grassins learned of Guillaume Grandet's failure and death they decided to visit that evening to offer sympathy and to learn why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At five o'clock Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle, the notary, arrived in their Sunday best. The meal was properly somber: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet speaking little—a formal dinner of condolence. Afterward Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet showed the craft that had earned him the nickname “the old dog.” His stutter, a practiced tactic copied long ago from a crafty opponent, was put to use to control conversation, avoid clear commitments, and frustrate adversaries. He began, stammering, about bankruptcy and the courts; the Cruchots, impatient, mocked him. But the ploy kept the floor while he steered the discussion.

The president, Monsieur de Bonfons, explained how a respected man facing insolvency could have a receiver appointed, distinguishing liquidation from failure. Grandet wanted to know whether liquidation could be handled without the courts. The president said that if a merchant did not file his schedule and no creditor appeared, the family or heirs could liquidate the business themselves. The notary approved: that would save the family name.

Grandet, attentive, weighed how liquidation might protect his brother and nephew. He disclaimed any knowledge of Parisian tricks, insisting his focus was on vineyards and Saumur business. “A man must look before he leaps,” he stammered, making sure he grasped the implications. The president added that debts could be bought at a discount and, if one acquired title to all the debts, the brother or his heirs would owe nothing. Grandet, still processing, repeated “very good.”

The president offered to go to Paris to negotiate with creditors and suggested that Grandet could provide some assets to facilitate the process. Grandet hesitated; the notary pressed for clarity. At that moment the des Grassins arrived and interrupted.

They too offered condolences. The notary, eager to present Grandet well, declared he would liquidate his brother’s debts honorably; the banker praised this apparent integrity. Grandet, cautious, insisted he would not bind himself beyond his means. Madame des Grassins proposed going to Paris herself, trying to outmaneuver the president. Grandet, however, favored the banker; he pulled him aside to discuss a separate investment in government bonds and asked him to say nothing to the Cruchots to avoid annoyance.

Des Grassins agreed to leave the next day and to meet again to finalize instructions. After they departed, Grandet said he needed time to deliberate. As news of his supposed generosity spread, the town began to admire him, quickly forgiving his earlier affront to the wine community—a fleeting enthusiasm that reflected Saumur’s propensity to forget grievances.

When the door closed, Grandet summoned Nanon and ordered her to keep his plans secret. He prepared a clandestine journey to sell gold in Angers while the market was favorable, taking precautions to avoid attention and ensure no one suspected his departure or the true purpose. In the early hours, as he and Nanon prepared to leave, Eugenie, worried about Charles, thought she heard a cry and considered checking on him but was distracted by seeing her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg. Grandet told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country, then hurried into the carriage, determined to reach Angers before morning.

“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie, listening from the top of the stairs. Silence wrapped the house; the carriage’s rumble faded. A cry from her cousin’s room pierced the walls. A thin line of light shone through the door, illuminating the old staircase.

“He suffers!” she exclaimed and rushed up. A moan brought her to the landing. The door was ajar; she pushed it open. Charles slept in an armchair, head drooping, pen fallen from his hand. His labored breathing alarmed Eugenie, and she entered the room.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, noticing sealed letters on the table addressed to tradesmen. “He’s settling his affairs to leave France at once.” Her gaze fell on two open letters; the words “My dear Annette” struck her like a blow. Her heart raced; she felt rooted to the spot.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” Thoughts swirled. “Should I read it? No, I mustn’t.” Curiosity overwhelmed her. She gently adjusted his head and kissed his hair. “Dear Annette!” a voice screamed in her mind.

She yielded to curiosity, ashamed yet compelled. The struggle between duty and passion was new to her; passion won. As she read, her heart swelled; the intensity of the letter made the joys of first love more precious.

My dear Annette,—Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has now overwhelmed me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are lost. I am orphaned at an age when I am still a child, yet I must rise as a man from this abyss. I have spent half the night facing my position. If I wish to leave France an honest man, I have not a hundred francs to try my fate in the Indies or America. I cannot remain in Paris, where the disdain shown to a ruined man would kill me. Your love cannot draw me back. Alas! I have no money to go to you for a last kiss to give me strength for my forlorn enterprise.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him,” thought Eugenie, wiping her eyes.

I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. I have not even a sou for an outfit. If I have nothing, I shall go to Nantes and ship as a common sailor, starting anew like others who have begun without a sou and returned wealthy. I have faced my future coolly. It seems more horrible for me than for others because I have been so petted by my parents and blessed by your love. Such happiness could not last. Nevertheless, I feel more courage than a careless young man is supposed to feel. Dear Anna, if to keep me in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, we would still not have enough for my extravagant living. I would never accept such sacrifices. We must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered. Charles stirred; terror ran through her, but he did not wake, and she resumed reading.

When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European, especially one who works hard. In ten years, your daughter will be eighteen; she will be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, and your daughter perhaps more so. Keep in your soul the memory of our happiness, and be faithful, if you can, to the memory of your poor friend. I cannot exact such faithfulness, for I must conform to my new life; I must think of marriage, which becomes a necessity. I have found a cousin here in Saumur whose face, manners, and heart would please you—

Eugenie noted the abruptness of the letter’s end. She excused him, thinking an innocent girl might not perceive the coldness in his words. To young girls raised in purity, all is love when they enter passion’s realm; they view their lover through the lens of their own feelings. Eugenie’s heart echoed “My dear Annette, my loved one” as the sweetest language.

Charles, though mourning his father, cared more for lost fortune than for grief alone. Indulgent parents had prevented the hard calculations many sons must face; their liberality had bred in him a love not entirely free from self-interest. Parisian society had shaped him into a calculating youth; Annette had taught him worldly prudence.

“You are very foolish, Charles,” she would say. “I shall have a great deal of trouble teaching you to understand the world. You behaved extremely ill to Monsieur des Lupeaulx. I know he is not honorable, but wait until he is out of power, then you may despise him.”

Charles had not fully applied such maxims; he retained the beauty of inexperience, though seeds of selfishness lay dormant, ready to grow when life demanded calculation.

Most young girls succumb to tender promises such appearances offer. Eugenie, innocent, was enchanted by Charles’s demeanor, unaware of the coldness beneath. A chance encounter with his last heartfelt words stirred her. She laid down the letter, filled with love, and watched her sleeping cousin, vowing to love him always. She glanced at the other letter for further proof of his nobility.

My dear Alphonse,—When you receive this letter, I shall be without friends. While I doubt the world’s friendship, I have never doubted yours. Please settle my affairs and get as much as you can from my possessions. I intend to go to the Indies. I have written to those I owe money, and you will find a list of their names. I do not wish to keep anything except perhaps a few baubles for my journey. I will send you a power of attorney for the sales. Keep Briton for yourself; I would rather give him to you than see him sold. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a comfortable carriage, which they have not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it without payment. If they refuse, do what you can to avoid dishonor in my present circumstances. I owe the British Islander six louis, which I lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, running back to her room with a candle. A thrill of pleasure coursed through her as she opened an old oak cabinet and found a red velvet purse with gold tassels, a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it and began counting her hidden treasures: twenty portugaises, five genovines, three gold quadruples, a hundred Dutch ducats, and a few rare rupees. She realized she possessed about five thousand eight hundred francs, nearly six thousand.

Filled with joy, she thought only of her cousin and ran upstairs without hesitation. The secret misery of Charles made her forget propriety. Standing at the threshold, she held the candle and purse; when Charles awoke, he was speechless with surprise. She placed the candle on the table and said, “My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me if you will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” he asked, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles blushed. “How did it happen?” he asked.

“I do not know. I am tempted not to regret reading them; they have shown me your heart, your soul, and your plans.”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush! Not so loud; we must not wake others. Here,” she said, opening her purse, “are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I was ignorant of the value of money; you have taught me. It is just a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow from your sister.”

Eugenie, confident, was taken aback by his silence. “Oh! you will not refuse?” she implored, kneeling. “I will never rise until you have taken that gold! My cousin, I implore you, answer me! Let me know if you respect me, if you are generous—”

As he heard her noble distress, tears fell from Charles onto her hands. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents onto the table. “Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she cried, weeping with joy. “Fear nothing, my cousin; you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

Charles finally spoke. “Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she asked, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square leather-covered box. “This is something as precious to me as life itself. My mother would sell the gold she lavished on this dressing-case, but I cannot. Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the box, revealing a finely crafted dressing-case. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, revealing a hidden drawer. “Here is something worth the whole world.” He drew out two portraits, masterpieces of Madame Mirbel, set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he replied, smiling. “This is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. You alone are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent. “Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he urged.

Hearing the same words she had used to him, she looked at him with love, her first look of womanhood. He took her hand and kissed it. “Angel of purity! Between us, money is nothing. Feeling, sentiment, must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, lowering her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She gently disengaged her hand, and he followed her to her room, lighting the way. At the threshold, he said, “Ah! why am I ruined?”

“What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she replied.

“Poor child!” said Charles, leaning against the wall. “If that were true, he would not have let my father die; he would not let you live this way.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” Charles said disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year, do you think you would live in this cold, barren room?” He glanced at the old cabinet. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said.

“Go and sleep,” she urged, preventing him from entering her disordered room.

They exchanged good-nights with smiles and fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment Charles began to wear roses with his mourning.

The next day, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie in the garden with Charles. He was still sad, burdened by his misfortune. “My father will not be home until dinner,” Eugenie said, noticing her mother’s anxious look.

The connection between Eugenie and Charles showed in their shared thoughts and Eugenie’s sweet voice. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected by the three women, each occupied with tasks. Grandet had neglected his affairs, and tradesmen came to discuss business. Madame Grandet and Eugenie listened to the endless chatter of workmen and farmers, while Nanon prepared the produce they brought.

Around five, Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs from the exchange on his gold. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, which were nearly foundered.

“I am hungry,” he said upon entering.

Nanon called from the kitchen, “Haven’t you eaten since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he replied.

Nanon brought in soup just as Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet had not even noticed his nephew. “Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker. “Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” Grandet replied. “They have enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” Grandet said quietly.

The banker was astonished, and a whispered conversation began between them, often glancing at Charles. Des Grassins looked surprised, likely learning of Grandet’s plans to invest a sum that would yield him a hundred thousand francs a year.

“Monsieur Grandet,” Des Grassins said to Charles, “I am going to Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” Charles replied.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” Charles asked eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle with feigned pride. “Are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, kissed his uncle, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-bye, Des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead them by the nose.”

The two men shook hands, and Grandet returned to his armchair, asking Nanon for some black-currant ratafia. Too excited to sit still, he got up, looked at a portrait, and began to sing, doing what Nanon called his dancing steps.

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un good papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie exchanged silent glances. Grandet’s joviality frightened them when it reached its peak. The evening passed quickly, and Grandet chose to go to bed early, expecting everyone else to follow suit. As for Madame Grandet, she lived according to her husband’s will. During the two hours of digestion, Grandet, more jovial than ever, shared his peculiar sayings, one of which captured his mindset: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.” He was cheerful and generous, offering Nanon some ratafia, which she gladly accepted.

The next morning the family gathered for breakfast in an intimate scene of shared grief. Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles were united by sorrow; even Nanon felt moved. Old Grandet, absorbed in his avarice and already thinking of sending Charles away cheaply, remained indifferent to the young people’s exchanges, occupied with agricultural chores and leaving Madame Grandet to supervise their closeness.

For Eugenie, love grew from their secret sorrow. Their relationship deepened with tender glances and gentle words, retaining a childhood innocence. Eugenie took pleasure in comforting Charles, soothing him with the sweetness of new affection; their bond, formed in mourning, seemed pure and sacred. Charles, accustomed only to worldly passion, welcomed this genuine feeling and the simplicity of their life together.

After breakfast, while Grandet worked in the fields, Charles enjoyed the company of Eugenie and her mother, finding charm in their modest existence. He had once thought such virtues existed only in Germany, but now found them real and enchanting. Eugenie became his ideal; she surrendered to their love, unaware of the impending separation.

Three days after the des Grassins left, Grandet took Charles to the Civil Court so he could renounce his rights to his father’s estate. The act felt like a painful betrayal. Charles arranged powers of attorney, secured a passport, and prepared to leave, selling his possessions. Grandet praised Charles’s new mourning clothes as the look of a man ready to seek his fortune.

When Charles gathered valuables to sell, Grandet offered to buy them, valuing them at nearly a thousand francs; Charles accepted gratefully. He gave his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet, who was deeply touched. Grandet then insisted on paying Charles’s passage to the Indies, further binding them.

As he left, Eugenie shared her sadness; Charles acknowledged his obligations and his change from boy to man, which she admired. The family wore mourning for Guillaume Grandet and attended a Requiem Mass. At breakfast Charles received letters from Paris about his affairs, which he read to Eugenie.

In the garden Charles reassured her that his affairs were well managed and that he had arranged to send his things to Nantes. He spoke plainly about their coming separation and urged Eugenie not to tie her future to his uncertain fate. Still, their love remained strong, and they sealed it with a passionate kiss.

When Charles announced his departure, Grandet showed an unexpected interest—personally packing Charles’s belongings and ensuring their dispatch to Nantes. The farewell at the diligence was emotional; Grandet offered measured encouragement, and Charles thanked his uncle for supporting him.

After Charles left, the house felt empty to Eugenie, who wished to keep his room unchanged. She and Nanon conspired to preserve the household as it had been, hoping for his return. Eugenie’s beauty deepened as she embraced her love, following his voyage on a map and reflecting on their moments together. Her love became constant, shaping her days; she kept a cheerful front before her father but confided in Nanon, who, despite her own unfulfilled desires, understood and sympathized with her mistress’s pain.

Two months passed, and the once monotonous life of Eugénie and her mother became consumed by the intense secret binding them. Charles lived in their thoughts; Eugénie often studied her aunt’s portrait seeking his features. One Sunday Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugénie had given Charles, and panic set in.

“What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?” Madame Grandet cried. The women spent a morning in dread, missed high Mass, and feared the coming confrontation.

Madame Grandet fretted over their future, unprepared for winter and chilled by her husband’s impending wrath. She suggested consulting Monsieur des Grassins in Paris, but Eugénie insisted it was too late.

On January 1, 1820, a cold morning, Madame asked for a fire in her room to avoid a confrontation. Grandet, in a surprisingly good mood, agreed and revealed he had received a large return from investments. As he prepared breakfast he boasted about his success, oblivious to the tension. Their polite New Year wishes only deepened the unease.

After breakfast Grandet demanded to see Eugénie’s gold and ordered her to return it. Cornered, she denied having it. His anger rose; he accused her of deceit and pressed to know what she had done with the money. Eugénie defended her actions and asserted her independence. Grandet, furious, threatened punishment: confinement to her room on bread and water until she obeyed. Heartbroken, she fled to her mother, who was also strained.

Grandet accused Eugénie of giving the gold to Charles, whom he deemed unworthy. Madame Grandet pleaded for their daughter, but Grandet remained obstinate, insisting on his authority. Feeling his anger, Eugénie resolved to stand firm in her love for Charles. Nanon, the servant, feared for her and vowed support.

That evening Grandet dined alone for the first time in years. Regular guests arrived, surprised by the absence of Madame Grandet and Eugénie; Grandet dismissed their concern, but the household’s tension was palpable. Later Nanon secretly brought Eugénie a hare pâté, determined she would not live on bread alone, and their bond deepened in quiet resistance.

For months the old wine-grower visited his wife constantly but never mentioned their daughter Eugénie, who remained confined. Madame Grandet’s health deteriorated, and Grandet grew harsher and more unyielding, even erring in business. Townspeople learned Eugénie was kept in her room on bread and water by her father’s orders, with only Nanon providing secret meals. The community condemned Grandet, and when Eugénie attended Mass her gentle demeanor drew curiosity and sympathy.

Eugénie’s chief sorrow was her mother’s failing health; she often blamed herself. Madame Grandet tried to comfort her and urged reconciliation, pleading with Grandet to restore their daughter to the family, but he remained indifferent as his wife worsened.

In spring Madame Grandet confided her worries to the Cruchots, who were appalled by Eugénie’s treatment and discussed the legal implications of her confinement. Eugénie overheard them but insisted on obedience to her father and begged them to remain silent, fearing criticism would tarnish the family’s honor.

One day Grandet, walking in the garden, hesitated between stubbornness and a desire to embrace his daughter. At that moment the notary Cruchot visited, warning him about his wife’s health and the consequences of her death for Eugénie’s inheritance. Realizing that her inheritance could force him to sell property, Grandet grew agitated at the thought of losing control over his wealth.

Cruchot advised that Grandet treat Eugénie kindly to avoid legal and financial trouble. Feeling cornered, Grandet began to reconsider his harshness. He returned home determined to reconcile but found Eugénie and her mother admiring a gold dressing-case given by Charles. Seeing the gold, Grandet became possessive and aggressive, prompting a confrontation in which Eugénie pleaded that the dressing-case be returned to its rightful owner.

In desperation Eugénie threatened to harm herself if her father did not relent. Shocked, Grandet ultimately backed down and allowed her to keep the dressing-case. He then tried a show of affection, scattering coins on the bed and promising to be kinder. Despite this temporary tenderness, Madame Grandet’s health continued to decline, and in October 1822 she died peacefully, urging Eugénie to seek happiness in heaven and expressing deep concern for her daughter’s future in a selfish world.

The day after her father's death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home, where she had endured much suffering, including her mother's death. Familiar objects moved her to tears. Her father's sudden tenderness—taking her to breakfast and watching her with an almost kind gaze—made the household suspect his mind was failing. But at the mourning dinner he revealed his plan.

“Dear child,” he said after the meal, “you are now your mother’s heiress, and we have some matters to settle.” Pressed that evening to settle inheritance issues, Eugenie listened as Maitre Cruchot explained that Grandet did not want to divide property or pay taxes on possible funds; to avoid this, he needed her to renounce her rights so he could retain control.

Confused, Eugenie asked for the deed to sign. Grandet, visibly moved, instead proposed she trust him and accept a monthly allowance of a hundred francs. She agreed; he clasped her hand and declared they were quits. The next morning she signed away her inheritance. A year later he had not paid the promised money; when she reminded him he awkwardly offered jewels.

Over the next two years Grandet trained Eugenie in running the household and managing finances, instilling his frugality. By the third year she became mistress of the house, and their monotonous life continued for five years. Eugenie’s sadness was well known though she never spoke of it. Her only company was the Cruchots and their circle, who played whist with her father.

In 1827 Grandet, now eighty-two, suffered a stroke. As he declined, Eugenie devoted herself to his care, feeling increasingly isolated. His death was uneventful; he remained fixated on hidden wealth until the end. When the priest came for last rites, Grandet’s avarice surfaced in his final words, urging Eugenie to take care of his riches.

After his death Eugenie was left alone with only Nanon, her loyal servant and confidante. Maitre Cruchot informed her she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite this fortune, her thoughts centered on finding her cousin Charles. In the empty house she and Nanon lamented their isolation while news of her wealth spread through the province.

Eugenie’s first act was to secure an annuity for Nanon, who soon married Antoine Cornoiller and became Eugenie’s housekeeper. Cornoiller managed the estate; life for the farmers continued unchanged. At thirty Eugenie had never known joy; her childhood had been shadowed by her mother’s suffering and an unfulfilled love for Charles, who had left her with only memories.

Her mourning delayed any thought of marriage, and the Cruchots surrounded her with attention, hoping to win her favor. They praised her beauty; flattery became daily. Monsieur de Bonfons sought to ingratiate himself while the Cruchots discussed their own prospects, Eugenie remaining the center of attention.

As spring approached, Madame des Grassins tried to promote a match between Eugenie and the Marquis de Froidfond, stressing his noble lineage. Eugenie’s indifference was misread as interest, fueling speculation about her future. Despite the attention she was haunted by Charles’s absence; he had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. His early ventures yielded six thousand dollars, and he quickly learned that trading in human beings was the most profitable enterprise. He went to Africa, bought enslaved people, and combined that trade with other lucrative goods. Ambition drove him: he wanted to return to Paris with a fortune and regain a higher social position.

Experience changed Charles. He grew skeptical, lost a moral compass, and came to see that what one society condemned could be applauded in another. His heart hardened; he became ruthless, engaging in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and buying pirate-captured goods cheaply to resell. Although he once carried memories of Eugenie, his various escapades distanced him from his past; he began to view his family with contempt and regarded Eugenie as no more than a creditor.

This explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies and elsewhere he used the alias “Shepherd” to protect his identity while pursuing wealth by unscrupulous means. His efforts succeeded: by 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline with gold-dust worth nearly two million francs, planning further profit at the Paris mint.

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman tied to the royal court, returning with his family. The d’Aubrions, once prominent, were now financially strained. Madame d’Aubrion, eager to secure her family’s future, sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter. She viewed Charles as a suitable son-in-law and promised to help him obtain a noble title and a comfortable income through marriage. The match would raise both families’ status and allow them to live comfortably in Paris.

Intoxicated by ambition, Charles entertained the marriage to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion as a path to social prominence. He consulted Annette, who encouraged him, recognizing the advantage of securing an influential, affluent husband for her friend. Now more confident and assertive after his Indies career, Charles felt ready to embrace this new life.

Des Grassins, aware of Charles’s return and impending marriage, approached him about settling his father’s debts. Charles, newly wealthy, dismissed Des Grassins’s concerns, asserting that his father’s financial failures were no longer his problem. He was preoccupied with his new identity as the Comte d’Aubrion and believed wealth would insulate him from his family’s past.

In August, Eugenie sat on the bench where she and Charles had once pledged their love, reflecting on their past. She received a letter from him that she opened with trembling expectation and dread. The letter detailed his success and changed life but delivered a painful blow: he was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion in a marriage of convenience intended to secure his social ambitions. He declared that love in marriage was a delusion and that their youthful dreams could not be pursued given the divergence of their lives.

Eugenie was devastated, feeling abandoned and heartbroken. Remembering her mother’s warning about suffering and endurance, she resolved in her sorrow to live a life of prayer and acceptance, acknowledging that her dreams had been shattered.

Eugenie returned from the garden cautiously, avoiding the corridor that reminded her of her cousin. That day the parish priest—related to the Cruchots and allied with the president, Monsieur de Bonfons—had been urged to speak to her about marriage. Consumed by unhappiness, Eugenie confessed a desire to withdraw from the world and live only for God; the priest warned against such a drastic renunciation, reminding her of duties to society and the poor.

Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by vengeance and despair, to reveal that Charles Grandet had come back from the Indies and was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Eugenie, taken aback, kept her composure. Madame des Grassins produced a letter from her husband detailing Charles’s situation, his father’s bankruptcy, and the marriage’s implications; contempt and sorrow filled Eugenie, and she returned the letter unread.

Tormented, Eugenie asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be sinful, a question that betrayed her inner conflict. After he left she secluded herself in her father’s secret room, skipped dinner, and avoided guests. Later, when she rejoined the company, a charged curiosity filled the evening. At a surprising moment she asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay—an act taken as a promise of marriage.

Privately, Eugenie told the president (Monsieur de Bonfons) she could offer only friendship and asked him to settle her uncle’s debts in exchange for her hand. He eagerly accepted. After he departed, Eugenie wept, confronting the weight of her choice.

The president hurried to Paris, met creditors, and arranged payment of all debts with interest. He carried a letter from Eugenie to Charles explaining her decision and the conditions of their relationship. Charles, at first excited by the prospect, was shocked when he learned of Eugenie’s fortune and the implications of her actions. Madame d’Aubrion, unaware of the president’s interventions, remained confident of the match.

The president returned to Saumur and announced his marriage to Eugenie. Despite her wealth, she kept a simple life, devoted to charity. Her husband embarked on a political career but died shortly after being elected deputy. Widowed, Eugenie lived in piety and generosity, dedicating her fortune to the community while rejecting worldly desires. Rumors of another marriage—most notably involving the Marquis de Froidfond—circulated, but she remained resolute and independent, sustained by loyal servants Nanon and Cornoiller.

Eugenie’s story shows the tension between wealth and personal fulfillment: she struggled with societal expectations yet stayed true to her values, becoming revered for embodying both the burdens and blessings of inheritance.
