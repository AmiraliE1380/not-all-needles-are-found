**VENDETTA**  
**By Honore De Balzac**  
**Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley**  

**DEDICATION**  
To Puttinati, Milanese Sculptor.  

**CHAPTER I. PROLOGUE**  

In October 1800, a foreigner named Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his wife and young daughter. The family exuded a profound sadness, their bond forged by shared misery rather than love. Bartolomeo, an older man with a serious demeanor, was dressed in tattered foreign clothes. His wife, though visibly distressed, tried to maintain a calm facade for their daughter, who bore an Italian grace despite her weariness.

As they lingered, Bartolomeo's intense gaze drew the attention of passersby, but his fierce demeanor quickly deterred any sympathy. After a moment of contemplation, he made a desperate decision, handing a dagger to his wife and declaring his intention to confront the Bonapartes. He approached the palace but was stopped by a soldier. Despite the soldier's refusal to let him pass without an audience request, Bartolomeo insisted, leading to a tense standoff.

Fortuitously, Lucien Bonaparte, Napoleon's brother, arrived and recognized Bartolomeo. He took him to see Napoleon, who was in a meeting with Murat, Lannes, and Rapp. After dismissing the others, Napoleon asked Bartolomeo why he had come. Bartolomeo revealed that he had killed the Portas, a family that had wronged him, leading to the death of his son and the destruction of his home. He had sought revenge after they set fire to his vineyard.

Napoleon, surprised, questioned Bartolomeo's motives and the implications of his actions. Bartolomeo expressed despair over the lack of support from the Bonaparte brothers, who he felt had forgotten their Corsican roots. Napoleon, now a leader of a nation, explained that he could not protect Bartolomeo due to his responsibilities but offered him a chance to live in Paris, away from the vendetta culture of Corsica.

Bartolomeo accepted this offer, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte family. He expressed gratitude for their recognition of his plight and the possibility of a new life in Paris. Napoleon promised to help him financially, emphasizing the importance of adhering to the law in the city, where personal justice was not tolerated.

Fifteen years passed since Bartolomeo's arrival in Paris, setting the stage for future events that would unfold, deeply intertwined with the themes of vengeance and familial loyalty.

**CHAPTER II. THE STUDIO**

Servin, a distinguished artist, established a studio for young girls interested in painting. At around forty, he was dedicated to his art and had married the daughter of a general. Initially, mothers brought their daughters to the studio, but soon they felt comfortable sending them alone, reassured by Servin's reputation for integrity and the quality of his instruction.

Servin only accepted pupils from wealthy families to avoid complaints about class composition, even refusing those aspiring to be professional artists. His careful approach and the assurance that only well-bred girls attended his classes earned him a stellar reputation. Consequently, he became synonymous with feminine art education, much like other specialists in their fields.

The studio itself was meticulously arranged, with a walled-off entrance leading to a spacious attic filled with light from large glass panes. The walls were adorned with caricatures and models, creating a unique atmosphere that blended artistic chaos with elegance. On a bright July day, the studio buzzed with activity as young girls painted, laughed, and interacted, forming distinct cliques based on social status.

Among them was Ginevra Piombo, the most beautiful and modest girl, who sat apart from her peers. The aristocratic group, led by Amelie Thirion, often looked down on the wealthier but less refined girls from the banking sector. Tensions simmered as Amelie orchestrated a slight against Ginevra, moving her easel away from its usual spot, which sparked whispers of jealousy and rivalry.

Ginevra, a devoted Bonapartist, faced ostracism from the royalist girls, who were influenced by the political climate following the second return of the Bourbons. Despite her beauty and talent, she was the target of jealousy, particularly from Amelie, who sought to undermine her.

When Ginevra arrived, she was met with silence, a stark contrast to the usual camaraderie. Her presence, however, commanded attention, and she remained oblivious to the tension surrounding her. As she settled into her work, she became aware of a mysterious presence behind a partition—a man hidden away, breathing quietly. This revelation filled her with both curiosity and concern for his safety.

Ginevra's indifference to the slight against her only fueled the animosity of her peers, who were perplexed by her calm demeanor. Meanwhile, Amelie and her group attempted to humiliate Ginevra further, but their efforts were thwarted by her apparent indifference.

Madame Servin's arrival interrupted the brewing conflict, and she announced that her husband could not attend that day. Ginevra and Madame Servin exchanged glances, both aware of the hidden man. As the session continued, Ginevra's focus shifted to sketching the officer's head, inspired by the urgency of the moment.

Servin, noticing her talent, praised her work, which drew the attention of the other girls, especially Amelie, who was determined to uncover Ginevra's secret. The atmosphere in the studio shifted as Ginevra's skill became evident, and Servin's admiration for her work only heightened the tension.

As the day progressed, Ginevra and Servin shared a private conversation about the hidden officer, revealing the political stakes involved. Ginevra offered her assistance, demonstrating her commitment to protecting the man and her loyalty to Servin.

The dynamics in the studio shifted as the girls became increasingly aware of Ginevra's focus and the mystery surrounding her. Amelie, driven by jealousy, attempted to spy on Ginevra, but the latter remained resolute in her purpose, determined to keep the officer safe while navigating the complexities of her relationships with her peers.

**CHAPTER III. LABEDOYERE’S FRIEND**

In a dim garret, painter Servin and Ginevra were startled by a knock on the door, revealing a tall young officer in an Imperial uniform, his arm in a sling and face pale from pain. He recoiled upon seeing Ginevra, but Servin reassured him, introducing her as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo. The officer, visibly shaken, revealed he was wounded and soon reacted with horror to the news of Labedoyere's death sentence, collapsing onto a stool.

He expressed despair over his situation, feeling cowardly for hiding while Labedoyere faced execution. Ginevra intervened, urging him to consider his life and offering her savings to help him. She insisted that it was only money, derived from the Emperor’s generosity, and that he should accept it without hesitation. Ginevra’s words inspired hope in the officer, who admired her beauty and kindness, yet remained troubled by Labedoyere’s fate.

As they spoke, Ginevra felt a deep connection to the officer, whose name was Luigi. They shared a moment of silence, realizing their bond had grown in mere minutes. Servin asked if Luigi could save Labedoyere, to which he replied he could only avenge him. Ginevra, moved by his passion, felt a stirring emotion she had never experienced before.

The next day, Ginevra returned to the studio, where Luigi recounted his harrowing experiences during the retreat from Moscow and the disaster at Waterloo. Ginevra listened intently, captivated by his stories. Their friendship blossomed quickly, and they devised a secret communication system through Corsican songs.

However, rumors began to circulate among Ginevra’s classmates about her relationship with Luigi, leading to their gradual departure from the studio. Ginevra remained oblivious to the gossip, focused solely on her growing feelings for Luigi. Despite her encouragement for him to submit to royal authority for safety, he refused, remaining loyal to his beliefs.

One evening, Ginevra learned from her friend Laure that the other girls had left due to rumors about her and Luigi. Laure’s mother insisted she stop attending the studio, believing Ginevra was involved in an inappropriate relationship. Ginevra was shocked by this revelation but remained steadfast in her feelings.

Servin entered the studio, unaware of the turmoil. Ginevra confided in him about the rumors, and he dismissed them, valuing their bond over societal judgment. Luigi, overhearing their conversation, felt guilty for the trouble he had caused Servin.

As the three shared a moment of camaraderie, Ginevra and Luigi’s love became evident, though unspoken. Servin playfully suggested they marry, and the couple exchanged silent affirmations of their feelings. Ginevra proposed to help Luigi secure a pardon, leveraging her father’s influence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra’s parents grew anxious over her late return home. Bartolomeo di Piombo, her father, was a stern yet loving figure, known for his integrity and loyalty to Napoleon. He had a modest income and lived simply, valuing family above all else. Ginevra’s mother, though quiet and reserved, shared a deep bond with her daughter.

As Bartolomeo paced anxiously, he decided to find Ginevra, who was late returning home. When she finally arrived, he carried her in his arms, showcasing the protective and affectionate nature of their family dynamic. 

In this chapter, the themes of loyalty, love, and the impact of societal judgment are explored through the relationships between Ginevra, Luigi, and their families, setting the stage for the unfolding drama surrounding their fates.

**CHAPTER IV. LOVE**

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” cried the old man, Piombo, as he embraced his daughter. Ginevra, though momentarily startled, reassured her mother with a nod. The baroness's initial fright faded, and Piombo's joy was evident as he rubbed his hands, a habit he had developed at court. They were like a revived plant after a long drought.

“Now, to dinner!” Piombo exclaimed, calling Ginevra “Signora Piombellina.” As they sat down, he noted her extended hours at the studio, suggesting that painting had become more important to her than family. Ginevra, however, was preoccupied and eventually confessed, “I love a young man.” 

Her parents were taken aback. When asked about his status, she revealed he was without fortune and a comrade of Labedoyere, who had been proscribed. Piombo reacted with disbelief, insisting that Ginevra should love no one but him. She countered, asserting that love was not a choice. 

“I thought you would remain faithful to me,” Piombo lamented, but Ginevra reminded him of his own absences for political missions. “Life has necessities,” she said, defending her feelings. 

Piombo’s anger flared as he struggled with the idea of losing his daughter to another man. Ginevra pleaded for understanding, promising that her love would not diminish her affection for him. “You will learn to know the man to whom you bequeath me,” she insisted. 

Piombo, however, could not accept this. “If he loved you as you deserve, he would kill me,” he declared, his rage palpable. Ginevra, undeterred, maintained that her love was pure and sincere. 

The tension escalated, with Piombo declaring he would never allow the marriage. Ginevra stood firm, stating, “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta.” 

Piombo’s fury intensified, and he warned her of the consequences of marrying a Porta, the family responsible for her brother’s death. Ginevra argued that Luigi was innocent, having been just a child during the vendetta. 

Despite her father’s threats, Ginevra remained resolute. “I will be his wife,” she asserted, leading to a confrontation where Piombo, in a fit of rage, nearly attacked her but ultimately hesitated. 

The next day, Ginevra found herself barred from leaving the house. She communicated with Luigi through a chambermaid, and their love deepened despite the growing rift with her father. 

On Ginevra’s birthday, her mother attempted to mediate, but the arrival of two notaries to discuss her marriage to Luigi escalated tensions. Piombo’s anger boiled over as he confronted the notaries, who were merely doing their duty. 

In a moment of desperation, Ginevra knelt before her father, pleading for his consent. “Give me Luigi, or I die!” she cried, but Piombo, overwhelmed, rejected her. “You have no father,” he declared, casting her out of the house.

Ginevra entered Luigi’s modest lodging, declaring, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” Luigi promised to love her in place of her parents, and Ginevra, despite her sadness, resolved to find happiness with him. “Then let us be happy,” she said, her spirit unbroken.

**CHAPTER V. MARRIAGE**

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house, she sought refuge with Madame Servin until her marriage to Luigi. However, Madame Servin coldly refused to assist her, annoyed by the trouble Ginevra's situation had caused her husband. Hurt but proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her, painting a bright future that lifted her spirits despite her father's harshness.

One morning, Ginevra received trunks filled with essentials for her new life, sent by her mother, who included a letter urging her to reconsider the marriage. The letter expressed her mother's love and concern, which deeply moved Ginevra. Just as she felt compelled to return home, Luigi arrived, and her resolve to abandon him faded. She chose to support him instead, burying her own grief.

On their wedding day, Ginevra had no friends present. Luigi brought two witnesses: a cavalry sergeant and a master mason, both of whom were dressed simply, reflecting the somber nature of the occasion. Ginevra, though beautiful, wore modest attire, and the atmosphere was grave, lacking the usual joy of a wedding.

As they walked to the mayor’s office, they encountered other wedding parties filled with happiness, contrasting sharply with their own situation. Ginevra felt a mix of emotions, especially as the mayor’s clerk noted the absence of her parents, which heightened her anxiety. The bureaucratic nature of the ceremony felt cold, and the couple's love was overshadowed by the disapproval of their families.

After a brief ceremony, Ginevra and Luigi were married, but they left the mayor’s office feeling the weight of their families' absence. Outside, Luigi expressed his gratitude, and Ginevra felt a sense of relief despite the challenges they faced. They hurried to their modest apartment, where Luigi embraced her, declaring their true wedding had begun.

Inside their new home, Ginevra was delighted by the thoughtful arrangements Luigi had made, including a studio for her art. They explored their small but charming space, filled with love and hope for the future. Their initial days together were consumed by passion, as they reveled in each other's presence, often forgetting the outside world.

However, reality soon set in, and they realized they needed to work to sustain themselves. Ginevra began copying paintings, quickly gaining clients, while Luigi struggled to find work until he decided to use his excellent handwriting to offer copying services. His efforts paid off, and he eventually established a successful business, allowing them to support themselves comfortably.

This period marked the happiest time in their lives, filled with love and shared labor. They enjoyed evenings together, filled with music and laughter, and neither expressed regret about their choices. Yet, Ginevra occasionally felt sorrow for her parents, imagining their loneliness and grief over her absence.

To commemorate their anniversary, Ginevra painted a portrait of herself for Luigi, capturing not just her likeness but the essence of their love. This masterpiece symbolized their happiness and commitment to one another. Another year passed, and their life together continued in blissful harmony, marked simply by their enduring love.

**CHAPTER VI. RETRIBUTION**

In early 1819, Ginevra's picture-dealers urged her to create original works instead of copies due to increased competition. Realizing her earlier mistake of not pursuing genre painting, she attempted portraiture but faced stiff competition from artists in dire financial need. Despite Luigi and Ginevra having some savings, their future remained uncertain.

As winter progressed, Luigi worked tirelessly, struggling with declining writing fees that forced him to work longer hours without assistance. Ginevra produced several commendable paintings, but the market was unkind, and her efforts went largely unsold. Their financial situation grew dire, yet they masked their anxieties with love and affection, fearing the loss of their happiness to poverty.

One night, Ginevra awoke to find Luigi missing. She discovered him working late into the night, and feeling guilty for his sacrifices, she resolved to contribute by coloring lithographs. They both pretended to sleep, only to rise again to work, driven by love and desperation.

One night, Luigi, exhausted, opened his window and saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard, realizing she too was working. When he confronted her, she insisted on sharing the burden of their struggles. They shared a moment of tenderness, gazing at the dawn, hopeful for the future despite their hardships.

Their situation worsened when Ginevra gave birth to a son, Bartolomeo. Initially, the joy of parenthood masked their financial troubles, but soon they were forced to sell their possessions and jewels. Poverty crept in, stripping them of comfort and pride, leading to a grim reality.

Months later, Ginevra, now frail and worn, struggled to care for her sickly child. Luigi, desperate for help, found himself alone in a city where he knew no one. He sold Ginevra’s remaining paintings and furniture for meager sums, but their situation continued to deteriorate. Ginevra displayed remarkable strength, caring for her son and maintaining their home despite her own suffering.

One evening, Ginevra saved a piece of bread for Luigi, who was horrified to realize she was starving. In a moment of despair, he decided to sell himself as a military substitute to secure money for her. He approached an old officer, who provided him with an advance. Luigi rushed home, hopeful, but found Ginevra in a state of despair, holding their lifeless child.

In shock, Ginevra cried out for help, and Luigi, frantic, sought assistance from neighbors. As they gathered supplies, Ginevra, in her delirium, expressed her love for Luigi and her regret over their lost child. She acknowledged her suffering and the happiness they once shared, asking Luigi to remember her fondly.

Despite Luigi's pleas for her to hold on, Ginevra succumbed to her exhaustion and died in his arms, leaving him devastated. Neighbors and a priest arrived, but the room was filled with an unbearable silence.

Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents, Bartolomeo and Elisa, sat in their home, haunted by memories of their daughter. They had long been estranged from her, but a conversation about Ginevra stirred emotions in Bartolomeo. Just as he expressed a desire to see her again, Luigi appeared at their door, broken and holding Ginevra’s hair, revealing the tragic fate of their families.

The revelation shattered the old couple, who realized the depth of their loss and the consequences of their estrangement. Bartolomeo, once resolute, was left in despair, mourning the death of his daughter and the destruction of their family ties.
