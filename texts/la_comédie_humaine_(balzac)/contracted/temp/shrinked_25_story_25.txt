“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!” cried the lawyer’s clerk Simonnin, leaning from a window and tossing a breadcrumb at a stranger crossing Maître Derville’s courtyard.

“Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks, or I will turn you out. However poor a client is, he is still a man!” admonished Boucard, head clerk.

Simonnin, thirteen or fourteen, and others including Godeschal taunted the shabby visitor as “old Box-coat.” Godeschal suggested a prank, claiming M. Derville received clients only at odd hours. The office buzzed with laughter; the cluttered, musty room’s disorder was routine.

When the weary man entered and politely asked for M. Derville, Boucard brusquely dismissed him, telling him to return at one. The clerks jeered—nobleman or porter?—and laughed when he insisted he was Colonel Chabert, reported dead at Eylau. Amid chatter, Boucard finally silenced them, irreverence stark against the old man’s somber presence.

At about one in the morning Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the head clerk—arranging papers—explained Derville preferred to work at night and that the Colonel was only the third late caller.

When Derville arrived he was struck by the man’s thin, pale appearance and scars suggesting past trauma. The stranger claimed to be the Colonel presumed killed at Eylau, prompting Derville and the clerk to suspect madness, but the Colonel insisted on telling his story.

Derville, used to varied clients, urged brevity. Chabert recounted Eylau: gravely wounded and buried among the corpses, he had miraculously survived because his horse’s body shielded him. He described awakening in a grave, struggling free, and being rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery he discovered he had lost everything—especially proof of identity. Without documents, attempts to reclaim his life met disbelief and ridicule; he wandered as a beggar, was often treated as mad, and spent two years confined in a mental institution. Upon release he learned his wife, the Countess Ferraud, had remarried and borne children.

Derville, sensing the gravity though skeptical, was willing to help. Chabert sought to reclaim rights and fortune he believed unjustly taken and lamented that no lawyer had taken him seriously. Moved, Derville offered to obtain necessary papers from Germany, gave a small sum for subsistence, and provided a letter to a notary for further assistance.

Overwhelmed with gratitude, Chabert felt hope for the first time in years, voiced a longing for vengeance, and declared he would rather die than live in obscurity. Derville acknowledged the case’s complexity but promised to treat it seriously; the meeting restored Chabert’s purpose and gave him a glimmer of hope.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat—tasked with advancing half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client—demanded the six hundred francs the old soldier had received be refunded.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed; a young man who had taken the office after his chief fled in bankruptcy.

“Thank you for reminding me of that affair,” Derville replied wryly. “My philanthropy will not extend beyond twenty-five louis; I fear I have already been duped by my patriotism.”

Noticing German-stamped papers on his desk, Derville ordered his head clerk Boucard to translate. A Berlin notary reported the requested documents would arrive and were legally stamped; witnesses to the recorded facts still lived in Eylau, Prussia, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg. “This looks like business,” Derville observed.

Questioning Crottat about Chabert’s estate and the rogue Roguin, Derville learned the facts. Roguin—called by Crottat “the unfortunate Roguin” with a laugh—had indeed absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of clients’ money. Crottat, who had copied the papers as third clerk, recounted that Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe (called Chabert, Count of the Empire), had married without settlement so they held property in common. The personalty was about six hundred thousand francs. Before marriage Colonel Chabert made a will leaving one-quarter to hospitals and assigning another quarter to the State; the will was contested, forcing a sale and leaving the widow with the portion bequeathed to the treasury. Thus Chabert’s personal fortune had been reduced to roughly three hundred thousand francs.

Derville then sought out the Colonel. Chabert lodged in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud; Derville had to walk because his coachman refused the unpaved street. The house was dismal—mismatched windows, trodden-earth floor, a single chair and a straw bed, damp walls—testimony to poverty. Yet when Chabert emerged smoking a pipe he retained the military calm of a man unshaken by hardship.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked, giving practical directions to avoid manure.

“Here, we are not distinguished for luxury,” Chabert added when asked about his lodgings. “But I have done no one wrong, and I sleep in peace.” Derville hesitated about the money he had advanced and instead asked why Chabert had not moved to Paris to live better.

“The good folks here have taken me in and fed me for a year,” Chabert replied. “I cannot leave them now that I have a little money.”

Derville told him the Heilsberg papers were expected and that the woman who saved him was alive; Chabert, frustrated at the thought of money he did not possess, threw down his pipe. Derville then outlined the legal complexities: proving Chabert’s identity would require lengthy, costly proceedings. The case could be prolonged by the bigamy issue—Comtesse Ferraud had remarried—and opponents would demand inquiries, translations, and foreign legal formalities that might take years and drain resources.

“You will face many challenges,” Derville cautioned. “Your opponents will demand inquiries that could take years.” Overwhelmed by the prospect of a drawn-out lawsuit, Chabert expressed despair. Derville reassured him the outcome could be favorable and proposed a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the subsequent marriage. He offered to send a power of attorney for Chabert to sign and to provide financial help. Chabert, feeling hopeless, agreed to trust Derville completely.

As Derville left he met Louis Vergniaud, who asked for a loan to improve the Colonel’s living conditions. Derville agreed but insisted Chabert should be the one to help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud to assess her position and potential leverage.

Monsieur le Comte Ferraud, the son of a former Councillor, had returned to France after the Revolution and married Madame Chabert, who had turned her share of her husband’s fortune into a substantial income. Their marriage was socially accepted; Ferraud was politically ambitious but had begun to regret his union. The Countess had become comfortable and influential, a sharp contrast to Chabert’s squalor.

Derville arrived at the Hotel Ferraud and found her surrounded by luxury. “Good-morning, Monsieur Derville,” she greeted him, but he quickly shifted to serious matters. “I have come to speak with you on a very serious matter,” he stated, revealing that Colonel Chabert was alive. The Countess laughed dismissively until Derville’s serious tone silenced her. He warned her of the danger she faced if Chabert’s identity were proven, hinting at legal and social ramifications. She insisted her second marriage was secure because of her children, but Derville countered that the law might not favor her and that Ferraud might not support her if his name and fortune were at risk.

Derville’s words struck a nerve; the Countess began to weigh the implications. He proposed a compromise, sensing her vulnerability. “Does he still love me?” she asked, contemplating how to use Chabert’s affection to her advantage. Derville left her with the suggestion to consider a settlement, confident he had gained the upper hand.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at their lawyer's office. With Derville's help Chabert arrived restored: dignified in a blue suit and wearing the Legion of Honor ribbon, his soldierly bearing renewed.

When Chabert entered, Madame Ferraud soon followed, made up to appear youthful. Clerks noticed his change; Derville, sensing the couple's tension, separated them to present a memorandum: Chabert would formally acknowledge his identity in exchange for financial support from his wife. The Countess, aghast at public exposure, resisted the obligation.

Back together, Chabert confronted her about their past, laying bare the pain of their separation. She feigned surprise and tried to leave, but Derville urged her to stay. A heated exchange ensued; despite cautions, Chabert pressed for justice. The Countess then took him to her carriage and brought him to her country house in Groslay. During the ride she confessed turmoil: she loved another man, Comte Ferraud, and wanted to preserve her dignity and avoid scandal. Hurt, Chabert offered measured understanding, aware of the social consequences she feared.

At the country house she treated him kindly and they reminisced; Chabert found temporary solace even as resentment lingered. Meanwhile the Countess acted to secure her position, writing to Delbecq to neutralize his claim. When Chabert discovered this betrayal he resolved to confront her but ultimately sacrificed his identity to protect her and their children, renouncing his name and withdrawing from society.

Months later Derville found him reduced to a vagabond in a courtroom. Despite poverty, Chabert retained dignity and scorn for society's indifference, refusing charity. His story traces loss and resilience, love, betrayal, and the struggle for identity in an indifferent world.

In June 1840 Godeschal, an attorney, accompanied Derville to Ris. Near Bicêtre they found an old pauper under an elm, drying his handkerchief. In a shabby wrapper-coat he had a carved, grotesque look; Godeschal compared him to German carvings, while Derville recognized him as Comte Chabert, husband of the pious Comtesse Ferraud, who had likely consigned him to the workhouse because he reminded her of their past.

Curious, Godeschal urged Derville to tell Chabert’s story. Two days later they returned and found the old man drawing in the sand. When spoken to he insisted he was no longer Chabert but “Hyacinthe,” merely a number in the institution; he even envied a condemned man for not being married. Godeschal gave him money for snuff; Chabert accepted eagerly, saluted them as “brave troopers,” waved his stick and cried “Vive Napoleon!” Another pauper noted his odd blend of philosophy and childishness and recalled how Chabert once impressed a Prussian officer with military recollections.

Derville reflected on Chabert’s tragic arc—from foundling to Napoleonic soldier to workhouse inmate—and on the disillusionment facing priest, doctor, and lawyer, all exposed to human baseness: betrayal, greed, moral decay, fathers abandoned by daughters, mothers exploiting children. Godeschal admitted he’d seen similar horrors and longed to flee Parisian corruption for a quiet country life with his wife. An addendum lists characters from Balzac’s Human Comedy and their other appearances, including Napoleon and Comtesse Ferraud.
