**Colonel Chabert**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame la Comtesse Ida de Bocarme née du Chasteler.

---

The story begins in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks an old man in a shabby coat, calling him "old Box-coat." The clerks, absorbed in their work and gossip, dismiss the old man, who is revealed to be Colonel Chabert, thought dead after the battle of Eylau.

Chabert, weary and desperate, seeks to speak with attorney M. Derville, but the clerks refuse to take him seriously, joking about his appearance. Despite their mockery, Chabert insists on waiting for Derville, showing his determination and the seriousness of his situation.

When Chabert reveals his identity, the clerks are shocked, realizing he is a significant figure rather than a beggar. Their laughter turns to disbelief as they process his claim. Boucard, the head clerk, suggests Chabert return later, highlighting their indifference to his plight.

As Chabert leaves, the clerks debate whether he is truly the colonel or a pretender, expressing skepticism about his story, especially since his wife has remarried, leaving him vulnerable. Their attitudes reflect a societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace, illustrating the harsh realities of post-war France.

The scene shifts back to the clerks, who focus on trivial matters rather than the serious implications of Chabert's visit. Their discussions about theater and bets showcase their youthful exuberance and lack of empathy. Ultimately, the story highlights the stark contrast between Chabert's struggles and society's callousness, underscoring a world where dignity is often stripped away, leaving only remnants of past glory.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At around one in the morning, Colonel Chabert arrived at the office of Maitre Derville, a young but renowned attorney in Paris. The porter informed him that Derville was not yet in, but Chabert insisted on his appointment and was shown upstairs. There, he met Derville's head clerk, who explained that Derville often worked late into the night.

When Derville finally arrived, he was startled by Chabert's ghostly appearance. The Colonel, thin and pale with a scarred head, introduced himself as the Colonel declared dead at the Battle of Eylau. Derville and his clerk exchanged skeptical glances, suspecting madness.

Chabert recounted his story: he had commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau, where he was gravely injured and left for dead. Despite being reported dead, he survived, buried among corpses. He escaped and was rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery, he sought to reclaim his identity and fortune, only to find that his wife, Countess Ferraud, had remarried and inherited his estate.

Derville, realizing the gravity of Chabert's situation, listened intently as the Colonel described his struggles to prove his identity and reclaim his rights. Chabert expressed despair over being treated as a madman and his longing for vengeance against his wife.

Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offered to help him obtain the necessary documents to prove his identity and promised financial assistance. Chabert, overwhelmed with gratitude, recognized Derville as a rare ally in his fight for justice.

As they discussed the legal complexities, Chabert's demeanor shifted from despair to determination. He expressed a willingness to fight for his rights, and Derville assured him he would take the case seriously. After their meeting, Chabert left with a sense of hope, clutching the money Derville had given him.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Three months after an interview, Derville, a notary, is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Colonel Chabert, an old soldier. Derville hesitates to continue supporting Chabert financially, having previously been misled by his sense of patriotism. He notices a German letter on his desk and instructs his clerk Boucard to translate it. The letter indicates that documents regarding Chabert's estate will soon arrive, confirming their legitimacy and the existence of witnesses in Prussia.

Derville recalls that Chabert’s estate was managed by Roguin, a disreputable lawyer who recently fled with clients' money. Crottat informs him that Chabert's widow, Rose Chapotel, inherited about 300,000 francs after a contested will. Derville learns that Chabert is living in poor conditions in Faubourg Saint-Marceau with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him in squalor, yet the Colonel retains his dignity. Chabert expresses his desire to reclaim his life and fortune, believing he deserves recognition as a general. Derville explains the complexities of legally proving Chabert's identity, including the potential for a lengthy and costly court battle. He warns Chabert that his wife, now married to Ferraud, may contest his claims, especially since they have children together.

Chabert is disheartened by the prospect of a drawn-out legal struggle and its financial burden. Derville reassures him that they will pursue a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the marriage, aiming to restore Chabert's rights. Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

As Derville leaves, he encounters Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert's well-being and requests financial help to improve the Colonel's living conditions. Derville decides to assist but insists that Chabert should help Vergniaud.

Derville plans to confront Madame Ferraud, Chabert's widow, to gauge her reaction to the news of Chabert's survival. When he meets her, she initially dismisses the idea of Chabert being alive. However, Derville's serious demeanor shifts the conversation. He warns her of the legal implications of Chabert's return and the potential for public opinion to turn against her if the case goes to court.

Realizing the gravity of her situation, Madame Ferraud becomes defensive but intrigued by leveraging Chabert's affection for a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting an amicable settlement that could benefit both parties. He leaves the meeting confident in his ability to navigate the situation to Chabert's advantage.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, arrived at Derville's office from opposite ends of Paris. Chabert, dignified in a new outfit and wig, embodied the soldierly style he had regained, while the elegantly dressed Countess followed shortly after. The office remained unchanged, with clerks joking about the unusual situation.

Derville prepared a memorandum outlining their agreement regarding Chabert's identity and the conditions for their marriage. The Countess was initially resistant to a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, insisting she wanted to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert entered, tension escalated, revealing the complexities of their relationship. The Countess feigned ignorance about Chabert's identity, but her reaction suggested otherwise.

As the discussion progressed, Chabert expressed anger over the Countess's reluctance to acknowledge him and her proposed financial terms. Feeling cornered, the Countess left abruptly, prompting Derville to caution Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert was deeply affected and left the office in despair.

Later, the Countess approached Chabert and took him to her country house, where she expressed emotional turmoil and revealed her love for another man, Comte Ferraud. Chabert, though hurt, offered understanding and support, suggesting they maintain dignity and avoid public scandal.

During their time together, the Countess attempted to charm Chabert, seeking to erase the pain of their past. However, her ulterior motives became clear as she plotted to secure her position while keeping Chabert at bay. After several days, Chabert sensed her manipulations, leading to a confrontation with Delbecq, the Countess's secretary, who suggested exploiting Chabert's situation for financial gain.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronted the Countess, who tried to manipulate him further. He ultimately decided to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions were revealed when she discussed putting him in an asylum if necessary. This shattered Chabert's trust, leading him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life was marked by hardship, falling into poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumed the couple had reached a compromise. He later discovered Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of his dignity. Despite his circumstances, Chabert maintained pride and contempt for societal norms.

In a final act of integrity, Chabert sought to repay Derville for his assistance, demonstrating enduring loyalty despite betrayal. Derville, recognizing Chabert's plight, facilitated the payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their entangled lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story serves as a poignant reflection on identity, love, and the harsh realities of life after war.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visited Bicetre with his predecessor Derville, where they encountered an elderly pauper drying his handkerchief. Godeschal noted the man's resemblance to grotesque carvings, and Derville revealed he was Colonel Chabert, the estranged husband of the Comtesse Ferraud, sent to the workhouse due to reminders of their humble beginnings.

Two days later, they returned to find Chabert drawing in the sand. When addressed, he claimed to be "Hyacinthe," displaying childlike innocence. Godeschal offered him money for snuff, which Chabert gratefully accepted, revealing a mix of military bravado and confusion.

An observing pauper noted that despite Chabert's apparent childishness, he was clever and philosophical, recalling how Chabert had impressed a Prussian officer with his military history. Derville lamented the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse.

Derville reflected on the disillusionment faced by priests, doctors, and lawyers, particularly those who witness the darkest aspects of human nature. He shared harrowing experiences from his legal career, concluding that the horrors he had seen surpassed any fictional accounts. Godeschal, having encountered similar cases, expressed a desire to escape the grim realities of Paris for a quieter life in the countryside. The narrative critiques societal indifference towards those who have fallen from grace, emphasizing the darker truths of human relationships and the legal profession.
