The incident in this sketch occurred in late November 1809, during the height of Napoleon’s empire. The echoes of the Battle of Wagram still resonated in Austria as peace was negotiated between France and the Coalition. Kings and princes orbited Napoleon, who reveled in his power and staged Parisian entertainments around his marriage to an Austrian archduchess. The opulence of the French aristocracy—diamonds and gold on women and military uniforms—contrasted starkly with the Republic’s earlier austerity. Military leaders indulged in wealth amassed through the sacrifices of countless soldiers.

Many women adopted a carefree attitude reminiscent of the Louis XV era. Whether influenced by the fallen monarchy or the Imperial family, both men and women embraced a hedonistic lifestyle that seemed to foreshadow disaster. The allure of military men became a frenzy aligned with the Emperor’s interests, since frequent calls to arms made every peace treaty temporary. Women’s affections shifted rapidly; many moved from mistress to widow in weeks.

The appeal of soldiers sprang from prospects of early widowhood, the lure of historical legacy, or the romantic notion of courage. Women were drawn to the idea that their passions would remain secret, buried on the battlefield. This fascination with military men, seen as embodiments of honor and adventure, produced a culture where bravery was prized. The period was marked by a mania for glittering possessions: diamonds and fireworks were in vogue, and men wore jewels following extravagant examples like Murat.

Comte de Gondreville, formerly Citizen Malin, had risen to prominence and postponed a grand entertainment to outshine other courtiers and impress Napoleon. His drawing-rooms filled with ambassadors, influential figures, and a few princes, all expecting the Emperor. But a private dispute between Napoleon and Josephine, hinting at divorce, kept him from the festivities, though the guests remained unaware of the tension.

The most beautiful women in Paris flocked to Gondreville’s event, embodying luxury and elegance. The financial elite, proud of their wealth, sought to rival the military’s splendor, using grand balls to introduce daughters to Napoleon’s inner circle and hoping to trade fortunes for favor. Women confident in their beauty attended to test their allure, masking true intentions behind smiles and friendly facades. Amidst the gaiety, many distrusted friends more than enemies, as the pursuit of wealth and power underpinned social interactions.

These remarks are necessary to explain the incidents surrounding the little imbroglio that is the subject of this study, reflecting the tone prevalent in Paris drawing-rooms at the time.

“Look toward the pedestal supporting that candelabrum—do you see the young lady with her hair a la Chinoise? She’s in the corner to the left, with bluebells in her chestnut curls. She’s so pale you’d think she’s ill—delicate and very small. Now she’s turning her head; those almond-shaped blue eyes look as if they were made for tears. She’s leaning forward to see Madame de Vaudremont below the crowd.”

“I see her now. You only had to mention her lovely complexion; I would have recognized her. She has the whitest skin here, but she’s either a prude or a coquette; her bodice barely hints at her beauty. What shoulders! What lily-whiteness!”

“Who is she?” asked the first speaker.

“I don’t know.”

“An aristocrat! Do you want to keep them all to yourself, Montcornet?”

“You of all people to tease me!” Montcornet smiled. “Do you think you can insult a poor general like me because you rival Soulanges? Or because I’ve only just arrived in this Promised Land? Come, Monsieur le Maitre des Requetes, let us glean in the field you can only hold precariously. We have a right to live! If you knew the German women, you might help me with the Parisian you love best.”

“Well, General, since you’ve noticed that lady, have you seen her dance?”

“Where have you been? There’s a triple rank of the most undaunted coquettes of Paris between her and the swarm of dancing men. It’s a miracle anyone could spot her in that corner, buried in gloom despite the candles. Between her and us is a sparkle of diamonds and glances, so many floating plumes and fluttering lace that it’s impossible for any dancer to detect her among those stars. How could you think she’s the wife of some sous-préfet from Lippe or Dyle?”

“Oh, he will be!” exclaimed the Master of Appeals.

“I doubt it,” the Colonel of Cuirassiers laughed. “She seems as raw in intrigue as you are in diplomacy. I bet you don’t know how she got there.”

The lawyer looked at the Colonel with a mix of contempt and curiosity.

“She probably arrived punctually at nine, the first of the company, and embarrassed the Comtesse de Gondreville, who can’t think straight. Repulsed by the mistress of the house and driven into the corner by jealous ladies, she became a victim of their coalition against her. Each of those sweet faces would have conspired to keep the men from taking her out. That’s how those tender-looking women would have formed a rampart against the stranger without saying a word beyond, ‘Do you know that little woman in blue?’ If you care to run the gauntlet of flattering glances and inviting questions, try to get through the rampart defending that Queen of Dyle, Lippe, or Charente. You’ll see how even the dullest woman will invent a wile to keep the plaintive stranger in the shadows. Doesn’t she look like an elegy?”

“Do you think so, Montcornet? Then she must be married?”

“Why not a widow?”

“She would be less passive,” the lawyer laughed.

“She might be the widow of a gambler,” the handsome Colonel suggested.

“Of course; since the peace, there are many widows of that sort!” said Martial. “But my dear Montcornet, we are simpletons. That face is too innocent; there’s too much youth and freshness for her to be married. What splendid flesh-tints! Nothing has sunk in the modeling of her nose. Her lips and chin are as fresh as a white rosebud, though clouds of sadness veil her expression. Who could make that young creature weep?”

“Women cry for little,” said the Colonel.

“I don’t know,” replied Martial; “but she’s not crying because she’s left without a partner; her grief is not new. It’s clear she beautified herself for this evening with intention. I would wager she’s in love already.”

“Bah! She’s perhaps the daughter of some German princeling; no one talks to her,” said Montcornet.

“How unhappy a poor child may be! Can anything be more graceful and refined than our little stranger? Yet not one of those furies around her, who think they can feel, will say a word to her. If she would only speak, we’d see if she has fine teeth.”

“You boil over like milk at the least increase of temperature!” cried the Colonel, nettled at finding a rival in his friend.

“What!” exclaimed the lawyer, ignoring the Colonel’s question. “Can nobody here tell us the name of this exotic flower?”

“Some lady companion!” said Montcornet.

“A companion! Wearing sapphires fit for a queen and Malines lace? Tell that to the marines, General. You wouldn’t shine in diplomacy if you jump from a German princess to a lady companion.”

Montcornet stopped a fat little man with iron-gray hair and clever eyes, mingling unceremoniously with various groups.

“Gondreville, my friend,” said Montcornet, “who is that charming little woman sitting under that huge candelabrum?”

“The candelabrum? Ravrio’s work; Isabey made the design.”

“Oh, I recognized your lavishness and taste; but the lady?”

“Ah! I don’t know. Some friend of my wife’s, no doubt.”

“Or your mistress, you old rascal.”

“No, on my honor. The Comtesse de Gondreville is the only one capable of inviting people no one knows.”

Despite this acerbic comment, the fat little man smilingly retained the Colonel’s jest. Montcornet returned to the lawyer, who had rejoined a neighboring group, intent on asking, but in vain, for information about the fair unknown. He grasped Martial’s arm and said in his ear:

“Mind what you’re about. Madame de Vaudremont has been watching you for some time with ominous attentiveness; she can guess by the movement of your lips what you say to me. Our eyes have already told her too much; she’s thinking even more than we are about the little blue lady.”

“That’s too old a trick in warfare, my dear Montcornet! However, what do I care? Like the Emperor, when I make a conquest, I keep it.”

“Martial, your fatuity cries out for a lesson. You, a civilian, engaged to Madame de Vaudremont—a widow of two-and-twenty with four thousand napoleons a year—who slips such a diamond on your finger,” he added, taking the lawyer’s left hand, which the young man complacently allowed; “and you affect the Lovelace, as if you were a colonel obliged to maintain the military reputation! Fie! Just think of all you may lose.”

“At any rate, I shall not lose my liberty,” replied Martial with a forced laugh.

He cast a passionate glance at Madame de Vaudremont, who responded only with an uneasy smile, having seen the Colonel examining the lawyer’s ring.

“Listen to me, Martial. If you flutter around my young stranger, I shall set to work to win Madame de Vaudremont.”

“You have my full permission, my dear Cuirassier, but you will not gain this much,” the young Maitre des Requetes put his polished thumb-nail under an upper tooth with a mocking click.

“Remember that I am unmarried,” said the Colonel; “that my sword is my whole fortune; and that such a challenge is setting Tantalus down to a banquet he cannot devour.”

“Prrr.”

This defiant roll of consonants was the only reply to the Colonel’s declaration, as Martial looked him over before turning away.

Fashion required men to wear white kerseymere breeches and silk stockings at a ball. This costume showcased Montcornet’s fine shape. At thirty-five, he attracted attention with his stalwart height, emphasized by the handsome uniform of the Cuirassiers of the Imperial Guard. A black moustache highlighted his soldierly countenance, with a broad forehead, aquiline nose, and bright red lips. Montcornet’s manner, marked by a certain superiority from his command experience, might please a woman sensible enough not to aim at making a slave of her husband. The Colonel smiled as he looked at the lawyer, one of his favorite college friends, whose small figure made Montcornet look down slightly as he answered the raillery with a friendly glance.

Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon was a young Provencal favored by Napoleon; his fate might lead to a splendid embassy. He had won the Emperor with his Italian suppleness, a talent for intrigue, and a knowledge of manners, good substitutes for higher qualities. Though young and eager, his face had acquired the rigid brilliancy of tinned iron, a trait of diplomatists that conceals emotions. The heart of a diplomat may be an insoluble problem, as the most illustrious ambassadors of the time were distinguished by enduring hatreds and romantic attachments.

Martial, however, was one of those men capable of reckoning on the future amid their enjoyment; he had learned to judge the world and hid his ambition under the guise of a lady-killer, cloaking his talent under the commonplace of mediocrity as he watched the rapid advancement of those who offended their master less.

The two friends parted with a cordial handshake. The introductory tune, signaling the ladies to form squares for a fresh quadrille, cleared the men from the space they had filled while talking in Gondreville’s large drawing-room. Their whispered conversation had taken place during the usual interval between dances. The chandeliers and flambeaux illuminated their faces, failing to conceal the faint expressions of their feelings from the keen-sighted countess or the artless stranger. This espionage of thoughts is perhaps one of society’s idle pleasures; many disappointed people are bored without daring to admit it.

To appreciate this conversation’s interest, it’s necessary to recount an incident that would serve as an invisible bond drawing together the actors now scattered through the rooms.

At about eleven, just as the dancers returned to their seats, the company observed the entrance of the handsomest woman in Paris, the queen of fashion, the only person missing from the brilliant assembly. She made it a rule never to appear until a party reached a pitch of excitement that doesn’t allow women to preserve the freshness of their faces or dresses. This brief hour is the springtime of a ball. An hour later, when pleasure falls flat and fatigue sets in, everything is spoiled. Madame de Vaudremont never committed the blunder of remaining at a party to be seen with drooping flowers, hair out of curl, tumbled frills, and a face courting sleep. She took care not to let her beauty be seen drowsy, as her rivals did; she cleverly maintained her reputation by always leaving a ballroom in brilliant order. Women whispered with envy that she planned and wore as many different dresses as parties she attended in one evening.

On this occasion Madame de Vaudremont was not destined to leave when she wished. Pausing on the threshold, she shot swift, observant glances at the women present, hastily scrutinizing their dresses to assure herself that her own eclipsed them all.

She presented herself at the same moment as one of the bravest colonels of the Guards’ Artillery and the Emperor’s favorite, the Comte de Soulanges. The transient association of these two had an air of mystery. Upon hearing their names announced, a few women rose, and men hurried from side-rooms to the principal doorway. One of the jesters remarked that “women have as much curiosity about seeing a man faithful to his passion as men do in studying a woman difficult to enthrall.”

The Comte de Soulanges, about thirty-two, had a nervous temperament that hinted at fine qualities. His slender build and pale complexion were not initially attractive, but his black eyes revealed great vivacity. He was taciturn in company, and nothing in his appearance hinted at the oratory he would later show in the legislative assembly under the Restoration.

The Comtesse de Vaudremont, tall and somewhat plump, with dazzlingly white skin and a small head carried well, inspired love through her gracious manner. She was one of those beings who keep all the promise of their beauty.

The pair briefly became the center of observation, but did not allow curiosity long to exercise itself. The Colonel and the Countess seemed to understand that accident had placed them in an awkward position. Martial, as they approached, hastened to join a group by the fireplace, watching Madame de Vaudremont with the jealous anxiety of a first flame of passion from behind a barrier of heads. A secret voice warned him that the success he prided himself on might be precarious. However, the coldly polite smile with which the Countess thanked Monsieur de Soulanges and her little bow of dismissal as she sat down by Madame de Gondreville relaxed the rigidity jealousy had made his face. Seeing Soulanges still standing near the sofa where Madame de Vaudremont sat, apparently unaware of the glance that conveyed they were both playing a ridiculous part, Martial knitted his brows and smoothed his curls to maintain composure, watching the faces of the Countess and Soulanges while still chatting with his neighbors. He then took the hand of Colonel Montcornet, who had just renewed their acquaintance, but he listened without hearing him; his mind was elsewhere.

Soulanges gazed calmly at the women, sitting four ranks deep around the immense ballroom, admiring the display of diamonds, rubies, gold, and shining hair, which almost outshone the blaze of waxlights, the cut glass of the chandeliers, and the gilding. His rival’s indifference unsettled the lawyer. Unable to control his impatience, Martial approached Madame de Vaudremont with a bow. Upon seeing the Provencal, Soulanges cast him a covert glance and impertinently turned away his head. A solemn silence reigned, curiosity peaking. Faces wore strange mixed expressions; everyone anticipated an outbreak that men of breeding carefully avoid. Suddenly, the Count’s pale face turned as red as the scarlet facings of his coat, and he fixed his gaze on the floor to hide his agitation. Spotting the unknown lady humbly seated by the pedestal of the candelabrum, he moved away with a melancholy air, passing in front of the lawyer, and took refuge in one of the cardrooms. Martial and the company thought Soulanges had publicly surrendered his position out of fear of the ridicule that follows a discarded lover. The lawyer proudly raised his head and looked at the strange lady; then, as he took his seat near Madame de Vaudremont, he listened so inattentively that he missed her words behind her fan:

“Martial, please don’t wear that ring you took from me. I have my reasons and will explain them when we leave. You must give me your arm to go to the Princess de Wagram’s.”

“Why did you come in with the Colonel?” asked the Baron.

“I met him in the hall,” she replied. “But leave me now; everybody is looking at us.”

Martial returned to the Colonel of Cuirassiers. It was then that the little blue lady became the object of curiosity that agitated the Colonel, Soulanges, Martial, and Madame de Vaudremont.

When the friends parted after their challenge, the Baron flew to Madame de Vaudremont and led her to a place in the most brilliant quadrille. Favored by the intoxication dancing produces in a woman and the turmoil of a ball, where men appear in all the trickery of dress that enhances their attractions as much as it does women’s, Martial thought he might yield to the charm that drew his gaze to the fair stranger. Though he managed to hide his first glances toward the lady in blue from the Countess’s watchful eyes, he was soon caught; and while he excused himself once for his absent-mindedness, he could not justify the unseemly silence that followed the most insinuating question a woman can ask:

“Do you like me very much this evening?”

The more dreamy he became, the more the Countess pressed and teased him.

While Martial danced, the Colonel moved from group to group, seeking information about the unknown lady. After exhausting even the most indifferent, he resolved to take advantage of a moment when the Comtesse de Gondreville seemed at liberty to ask her the stranger’s name. He noticed a little space cleared between the pedestal of the candelabrum and the two sofas, which ended in that corner. The dance had left several chairs vacant, forming rows of fortifications held by mothers and middle-aged women; the Colonel seized the opportunity to make his way through this palisade of shawls. He began by making himself agreeable to the dowagers, and from one to another, through compliments, he finally reached the empty space next to the stranger. At the risk of catching on to the gryphons and chimeras of the huge candelabrum, he stood there, braving the glare and dripping wax, to Martial’s annoyance.

Too tactful to speak suddenly to his right, he began by addressing a plain woman seated on the left: “This is a splendid ball, madame! What luxury! What life! Every woman here is pretty! You’re not dancing—because you don’t care for it, no doubt.”

This vapid conversation aimed to induce his right-hand neighbor to speak; however, she, silent and absent-minded, paid no attention. He had prepared several phrases leading to: “And you, madame?”—a question from which he hoped for great things. But he was surprised to see tears in the strange lady’s eyes, which seemed wholly absorbed in gazing at Madame de Vaudremont.

“You are married, no doubt, madame?” he asked hesitantly.

“Yes, monsieur,” replied the lady.

“And your husband is here, of course?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

“And why, madame, do you remain in this spot? Is it to attract attention?”

The mournful lady smiled sadly.

“Allow me the honor, madame, of being your partner in the next quadrille, and I will ensure not to bring you back here. When so many are ready to ascend the throne, and Royalty is the mania of the day, I cannot imagine you will refuse the title of Queen of the Ball that your beauty may claim.”

“I do not intend to dance, monsieur.”

The curt tone of her replies was discouraging; the Colonel withdrew. Martial, guessing the officer’s request and refusal, smiled, stroking his chin and making the diamond on his finger sparkle.

“What are you laughing at?” asked the Comtesse de Vaudremont.

“At the poor Colonel’s failure, who just put his foot in it—”

“I begged you to take your ring off,” said the Countess, interrupting him.

“I did not hear you.”

“If you can hear nothing this evening, at least you see everything, Monsieur le Baron,” said Madame de Vaudremont, vexed.

“That young man is displaying a very fine diamond,” the stranger remarked to the Colonel.

“Splendid,” he replied. “The man is the Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon, one of my most intimate friends.”

“I thank you for telling me his name,” she said; “he seems an agreeable man.”

“Yes, but he is rather fickle.”

“He seems to be on the best terms with the Comtesse de Vaudremont?” asked the lady, looking inquiringly at the Colonel.

“On the very best.”

The unknown turned pale.

“Hallo!” thought the soldier, “she is in love with that lucky devil Martial.”

“I fancied Madame de Vaudremont had long been devoted to M. de Soulanges,” said the lady, recovering from the grief that had clouded her beauty.

“For a week past, the Countess has been faithless,” replied the Colonel. “But you must have seen poor Soulanges when he came in; he’s still trying to disbelieve in his disaster.”

“Yes, I saw him,” said the lady. Then she added, “Thank you very much, monsieur,” in a tone of dismissal.

At that moment, the quadrille was ending. Montcornet had only time to withdraw, telling himself, “She is married.”

“Well, valiant Cuirassier,” exclaimed the Baron, drawing the Colonel aside into a window-bay to breathe garden air, “how are you getting on?”

“She is a married woman, my dear fellow.”

“What does that matter?”

“Oh, deuce take it! I’m a decent sort of man,” replied the Colonel. “I have no intention of courting a woman I cannot marry. Besides, Martial, she expressly told me she did not intend to dance.”

“Colonel, I will bet a hundred napoleons to your gray horse that she will dance with me this evening.”

“Done!” said the Colonel, putting his hand in the coxcomb’s. “Meanwhile, I’m going to look for Soulanges; he might know the lady, as she seems interested in him.”

“You’ve lost, my good fellow,” cried Martial, laughing. “My eyes have met hers, and I know what they mean. My dear friend, you owe me no grudge for dancing with her after she has refused you?”

“No, no. Those who laugh last, laugh longest. But I’m an honest gambler and a generous enemy, Martial, and I warn you, she is fond of diamonds.”

With these words the friends parted; General Montcornet made his way to the cardroom, where he saw the Comte de Soulanges at a bouillotte table. Though no deep friendship linked the two soldiers beyond comradeship, the Colonel of Cuirassiers was alarmed to see the Colonel of Artillery, usually prudent, playing a game that could lead to ruin. Heaps of gold and notes showed the frenzy of play. A circle of silent men stood around the players. Occasionally a few words—pass, play, I stop, a thousand Louis, taken—were spoken, but the five motionless men seemed to converse only with their eyes. As the Colonel, alarmed by Soulanges’ pallor, approached, the Count was winning. Field-Marshal the Duc d’Isemberg, Keller, and a famous banker rose from the table, cleaned out of considerable sums. Soulanges looked gloomier than ever as he swept up a quantity of gold and notes; he didn’t even count it; his lips curled with bitter scorn, defying fortune rather than thanking her.

“Courage,” said the Colonel. “Courage, Soulanges!” He thought to help by pulling him from the card-table. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“What is that?” asked Soulanges.

“That you will answer a question.”

The Comte de Soulanges rose abruptly, gathering his winnings into his handkerchief; his fierce look discouraged protest—players seemed relieved to see him leave.

“Those soldiers are always as thick as thieves at a fair!” remarked a diplomat who took his place. A single tired face turned to the newcomer and said, “When we say military men, we do not mean civil, Monsieur le Ministre.”

“My dear fellow,” Montcornet said to Soulanges, leading him aside, “the Emperor praised you this morning, and your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

“The Master does not love the Artillery.”

“No, but he adores the nobility, and you are an aristocrat. He said that men who married in Paris during the campaign are not in disgrace. Well?”

Soulanges looked bewildered.

“And now I hope you will tell me if you know a charming woman sitting under a huge candelabrum----”

At this the Count’s face brightened; he seized the Colonel’s hand. “My dear General,” he said, voice changed, “if anyone else had asked me that, I would have cracked his skull with this gold. Leave me, I beg you. I feel more like blowing out my brains tonight than— I hate everything I see. I am going. This gaiety, this music, these stupid faces, all laughing, are killing me!”

“My poor friend!” Montcornet replied gently, squeezing his hand. “You are too vehement. What would you say if I told you that Martial thinks so little of Madame de Vaudremont that he is quite smitten with that little lady?”

“If he speaks to her,” Soulanges shouted, trembling with rage, “I will thrash him flat, even if he’s in the Emperor’s lap!”

He sank into an easy chair. Montcornet slowly left, seeing Soulanges too furious for consolation.

Back in the ballroom, Montcornet noticed Madame de Vaudremont, agitated, and sat beside her.

“I wager something has vexed you?” he said.

“A mere trifle, General. I want to go to the Grand Duchess of Berg’s ball, but first to the Princesse de Wagram’s. Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon amuses himself by flirting with the dowagers.”

“That’s not the whole secret. I bet a hundred louis you stay here all evening.”

“Impertinent man!”

“You are afraid of seeing Martial at the feet—”

“Of whom?” she exclaimed feigning surprise.

“Of that candelabrum,” he replied, glancing at the fair stranger and scrutinizing the Countess.

“You have guessed it,” she said, hiding behind her fan. “Old Madame de Lansac, as malicious as an old monkey, told me Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon is flirting with that stranger, who sits here like a skeleton at a feast. I would rather see a death’s head than that face—so cruelly beautiful and pale. She is my evil genius. Madame de Lansac, who only goes to a ball to watch while pretending to sleep, has made me miserable. Martial shall pay dearly. Urge him, since he is your friend, not to make me unhappy.”

“I just left a man who promises to blow his brains out if he speaks to that lady. And he is a man to keep his word. But I know Martial; such threats encourage him. Besides, we have wagered—” The Colonel lowered his voice.

“Can it be true?” asked the Countess.

“On my word of honor.”

“Thank you, my dear Colonel,” Madame de Vaudremont replied, her glance inviting.

“Will you do me the honor of dancing with me?”

“Yes; but the next quadrille. During this one, I want to see what will come of this intrigue and learn who the little blue lady is; she looks intelligent.”

Seeing the Countess wished to be alone, the Colonel retired, pleased to have made a good start.

At such entertainments, women like Madame de Lansac gather like old sailors watching younger mariners. She easily guessed the Countess’s agitation. Madame de Vaudremont might fan and smile, hide her emotion, but the Duchess, a sharp-eyed, mischievous figure, read her heart. She detected the slightest movements that revealed feeling. From her large armchair, the coquette of a bygone generation admired the younger woman’s attempts to disguise annoyance and grief. The Countess had believed Martial a man of talent who could raise her to power; now she realized her error—an instant passion that wounded her pride as if an unblemished limb were amputated.

The Duchess, eager to speak to the Countess, dismissed her Ambassador; in a lover’s quarrel every other interest paled. Madame de Lansac shot a sardonic glance at the younger woman, making her fear the dowager’s influence. There are looks between women that strike like torches in a drama; none who did not know the Duchess could appreciate the terror her gaze fired in the Countess.

Madame de Lansac was tall; people said, “That must have been a handsome woman!” She rouged her cheeks, wore diamonds, and dressed with taste. Her sharp nose promised epigrams; her polite manners softened mischief, making direct spite unlikely. Her eyes lit triumphantly; a smile seemed to say, “I promised you as much!” A blush of hope crossed the pale cheeks of the young woman under the chandelier. The alliance between Lansac and the stranger did not escape Madame de Vaudremont, who sensed a mystery and sought to uncover it.

At this moment the Baron de la Roche-Hugon, having questioned the dowagers in vain about the blue lady’s name, turned in despair to the Comtesse de Gondreville, who answered curtly, “A lady whom the ‘ancient’ Duchesse de Lansac introduced to me.”

By chance the Baron met the Duchess’s intelligent glance toward the stranger. Despite bad terms between them, he decided to speak. The Duchess, seeing him approach, smiled with sardonic irony and looked at Madame de Vaudremont, causing Montcornet to laugh.

“If the old witch pretends to be friendly,” thought the Baron, “she will play me some spiteful trick.” He said, “Madame, I am told you have undertaken the charge of a very precious treasure.”

“Do you take me for a dragon?” the old lady returned. “But of whom are you speaking?” she added sweetly, reviving Martial’s hopes.

“Of that little lady, unknown to all, whom the jealousy of these coquettes has imprisoned in that corner. You, no doubt, know her family?”

“Yes,” said the Duchess. “But what concern have you with a provincial heiress, married some time ago, a woman of good birth, whom you men do not know; she goes nowhere.”

“Why does she not dance? She is such a pretty creature. May we conclude a treaty of peace? If you will tell me all I want to know, I promise to urge a petition for the restitution of the woods of Navarreins by the Commissioners of Crown Lands on the Emperor.”

The younger branch of Navarreins bears the arms of Lansac; the Duchess’s liaison with Louis XV had secured her husband a dukedom. The Baron's suggestion to petition for an estate belonging to the elder branch was transparent trickery.

“Monsieur,” the old woman said with deceptive gravity, “bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to me. I promise to reveal the mystery of the interesting unknown. You see, every man in the room shares your curiosity. All eyes involuntarily turn to the corner where my protégée sits modestly; she reaps the homage the women wished to deprive her of. Happy the man she chooses!” She paused, fixing Madame de Vaudremont with a look that plainly meant, “We are talking of you.” “I imagine you would rather learn the stranger’s name from your handsome Countess than from me.”

There was such marked defiance that Madame de Vaudremont rose, approached, and took the chair Martial placed for her. Without acknowledging him, she said, “I can guess, madame, that you are talking of me; but I admit my want of perspicacity; I do not know whether it is for good or evil.”

Madame de Lansac pressed the young woman’s hand in her dry, wrinkled fingers and replied in a low, compassionate tone, “Poor child!”

They exchanged glances. Madame de Vaudremont understood Martial was in the way and dismissed him with an imperious “Leave us.”

The Baron, displeased to see the Countess in the sibyl’s thrall, cast one of those looks that might sway a blinded heart but seemed ridiculous to a woman beginning to suspect the man who attracted her.

“Do you think you can play the Emperor?” said Madame de Vaudremont, turning to give an ironical sidelong glance at the lawyer.

Martial, a man of the world with wit and acumen, would not risk breaking with a woman favored at Court, whom the Emperor wished to see married. He relied on the jealousy he intended to provoke to learn the reason for her coolness and withdrew willingly as a new quadrille began.

Making room for dancing, the Baron leaned against a marble console, folded his arms, and watched the two women talk. He followed their glances to the stranger. Comparing the Countess to this new beauty, whose mystery enhanced her charm, he succumbed to the common self-interest of adventurous seducers—torn between a fortune within reach and indulging caprice. Light highlighted his anxious, sullen face against white silk hangings, making him seem an evil genius. Observers likely thought, “There is another poor wretch who seems to be enjoying himself!”

Meanwhile the Colonel, leaning between the ballroom and the cardroom, laughed undetected under his moustache. He enjoyed the dance’s turmoil, reading agitation in the Countess and Martial, pondering Soulanges’ gloom and the fair unknown’s expression, which shifted between hope and dread. Montcornet stood like the feast’s king, receiving smiles from many women; as a Colonel of the Imperial Guard he was one of the best matches in the army.

It was nearly midnight. Conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries, and scheming had reached fervor; a young man exclaimed, “A fine ball!”

“My sweet little angel,” Madame de Lansac said to the Countess, “you are now at an age when I made many mistakes. Seeing you are enduring a thousand deaths, I thought I might give charitable advice. To go wrong at two-and-twenty spoils your future; it is like tearing the gown you must wear. Later we learn to go about in it without crumpling it. Go on, making clever enemies and foolish friends, and you will see what a pleasant life you will lead!”

“Oh, madame, it is very hard for a woman to be happy, do you not think?” the Countess eagerly asked.

“My child,” the Duchess replied, “at your age you must choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is not wise enough to be a good husband, nor passionate enough to remain a lover. He is in debt, my dear; he is the man to squander your fortune. That would be nothing if he could make you happy. Do you not see how aged he is? He must have been ill; he is making the most of what is left. In three years he will be a wreck. Then he will be ambitious; perhaps he may succeed—I do not think so. What is he? A man of intrigue, with gossip and presumption but no real merit; he will not go far. Besides—look at him. Is it not written on his brow that, at this moment, what he sees in you is not a young and pretty woman but the two million francs you possess? He does not love you, my dear; he computes you as an investment. If you must marry, find an older man with an assured position who is halfway in his career. A widow’s marriage should not be a trivial love affair. Is a mouse to be caught twice in the same trap? A new alliance should be a good speculation; in marrying again you ought at least to hope to be addressed as Madame la Maréchale!

As she spoke, both women naturally looked at Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

“If you prefer to flirt rather than marry again,” the Duchess said bluntly, “then you, better than any woman, know how to stir up trouble and then calm it. But please, don’t disturb families or ruin wives’ happiness. I’ve played that dangerous game. For a moment of vanity, a virtuous woman can be destroyed—there are truly virtuous women, dear—and we can become mortally hated. I learned too late that, as the Duc d’Albe said, one salmon is worth a thousand frogs! Genuine affection brings far more happiness than fleeting passions. I came here to preach to you; you are the reason for my visit to this lower-class house. Have I not just seen actors here? We used to receive them in our boudoir, but never in the drawing-room! Why are you looking at me in amazement? If you want to play with men, toy only with those whose lives are unsettled and who have no duties. The others won’t forgive us for the errors that make them happy. Take this advice from my long experience. Look at Soulanges, for instance, whom you’ve turned into a lovesick fool for the past fifteen months. Do you realize what you’ve done? You’ve affected his entire life. He’s been married for two years and is adored by a charming wife whom he loves but neglects; she lives in tears. Soulanges has experienced remorse far worse than any pleasure you’ve given him. And you, you little minx, have abandoned him. Come and see the result of your actions.”

The old lady took Madame de Vaudremont’s hand, and they rose.

“There,” said Madame de Lansac, indicating a pale, trembling figure under the candlelight, “that is my grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges. Today she finally yielded to my persuasion and left the sorrowful room where her child gives her little consolation. You think her charming? Imagine how she must have looked when happiness and love illuminated that now-blighted face.”

The Countess looked away, lost in sad thoughts.

The Duchess led her to the card-room door, then, scanning the room, said, “And there is Soulanges!”

The Countess shuddered at the sight of Soulanges, pale and rigid in an easy chair. His indifference and the tension in his brow revealed his suffering. Players passed him by, ignoring him as if he were dead. The image of the tearful wife and the morose husband, separated amid festivity, struck the Countess with foreboding about her own future. Though her heart was not completely hardened, she pressed the Duchess’s hand, thanking her with a childlike smile.

“My dear child,” the old lady whispered, “remember that we can repel a man’s attentions just as easily as we can attract them.”

“She is yours if you’re not a fool,” Madame de Lansac whispered to Colonel Montcornet while the Countess remained absorbed in compassion for Soulanges, still loving him enough to wish to restore his happiness.

“Oh! I will talk to him!” she declared to Madame de Lansac.

“Do nothing of the sort!” the old lady exclaimed as she returned to her chair. “Choose a good husband and shut your door to my nephew. A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back. By bringing my niece here, I believe I’ve given her a chance to regain her husband’s affection. All I need from you is to play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet, and the Countess smiled.

“Well, madame, do you finally know the name of the unknown?” Martial asked the Countess when they were alone.

“Yes,” replied Madame de Vaudremont, her expression playful.

Her smile and bright eyes were like a will-o’-the-wisp leading travelers astray. Martial, believing she still loved him, adopted a charming demeanor. “Will you be annoyed if I seem to care about that name?”

“Will you be annoyed if a remnant of affection prevents me from telling you? And if I forbid you to approach that young lady? It could be dangerous.”

“To lose your favor would be worse than losing my life.”

“Martial,” the Countess said sternly, “she is Madame de Soulanges. Her husband would kill you if he knew.”

“Ha! So the Colonel can leave the man in peace who has taken his love, yet would fight for his wife! What a reversal of principles! I beg you to let me dance with her. You’ll see how little love that icy heart could feel for you; if the Colonel allows me to dance with his wife after letting me—”

“But she loves her husband.”

“An even greater obstacle I shall enjoy overcoming.”

“But she is married.”

“A whimsical objection!”

“Ah!” the Countess said with a bitter smile, “you punish us for both our faults and our repentance!”

“Do not be angry!” Martial exclaimed. “Forgive me, I beseech you. I will think no more of Madame de Soulanges.”

“You deserve that I should send you to her.”

“I’m off then,” he laughed, “and I’ll return more devoted to you than ever. You’ll see that the prettiest woman cannot capture the heart that is yours.”

“Meaning you want to win Colonel Montcornet’s horse?”

“Ah! Traitor!” he said, playfully threatening his friend. The Colonel smiled and joined them; the Baron gave him a seat near the Countess, saying with a sardonic tone, “Here, madame, is a man who boasted he could win your favor in one evening.”

He left, thinking he had cleverly piqued the Countess’ pride and done Montcornet a disservice, unaware of the irony in Madame de Vaudremont’s words, as both were unconsciously moving toward each other.

At that moment, the lawyer approached Madame de Soulanges, who sat pale and timid, seemingly alive only in her eyes. Her husband entered the ballroom, his eyes flashing with anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, rushed to her nephew, asking for his arm to find her carriage, feigning boredom to prevent a scene. Before leaving, she shot a knowing glance at her niece, signaling her to take revenge.

Madame de Vaudremont noticed the exchange and felt a sudden realization; she feared being the target of the Duchess’s cunning.

“That duplicitous Duchess,” she thought, “may be preaching morality while plotting against me.”

This thought stirred her pride more than curiosity to unravel the intrigue. In her distraction, she lost control. The Colonel, misreading her embarrassment, became more ardent. Old diplomats, entertained by the unfolding drama, had never witnessed so many simultaneous intrigues. The passions of the two couples were evident in the crowded rooms, reflected in the faces around them, amplifying their own feelings of impotence.

Finally, the Baron found a seat beside Madame de Soulanges. He admired her beauty up close, captivated by her fresh neck and graceful figure. At that time, women wore sashes tied under the bosom, a style that flattered those with perfect figures. As he stole glances at the Countess, Martial was enchanted by her perfection.

“You haven’t danced once this evening, madame,” he said in soft, flattering tones. “Not for lack of a partner, I presume?”

“I never go to parties; I’m quite unknown,” Madame de Soulanges replied coldly, unaware of her aunt’s signal to attract the Baron.

Martial, adjusting the diamond on his hand, felt a spark of hope as he caught the Countess’s eye. She blushed and looked at him with an indefinable expression.

“Do you enjoy dancing?” he asked to continue the conversation.

“Yes, very much, monsieur.”

Their eyes met, and the earnestness in her voice stirred something in him. “Then, am I too bold in offering to be your partner for the next quadrille?”

Artless confusion colored her cheeks.

“But, monsieur, I have already refused one partner—a military man—”

“Was it that tall cavalry colonel over there?”

“Precisely.”

“Oh! He’s a friend of mine; don’t worry. Will you grant me the favor I hope for?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

Her tone revealed a new and deep emotion that touched Martial’s world-weary heart. Overcome with shyness, he lost his confidence, and his southern charm ignited. He tried to speak, but his words felt clumsy compared to Madame de Soulanges’ bright replies. Fortunately, the quadrille was forming, and standing beside her eased his nerves. For many men, dancing is a way to express themselves, believing they can win a woman’s heart through their physical grace. Martial likely intended to showcase all his charms, judging by his pretentious movements and gestures.

He led his conquest to the quadrille, where the most brilliant women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra played, the Baron felt immense pride surveying the ladies forming the square. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled even Madame de Vaudremont’s, who stood opposite him and the lady in blue. All eyes briefly turned to Madame de Soulanges; a flattering murmur showed she was the topic among the men. The admiration and envy made the young woman blush, enhancing her charm. When she raised her eyes, she looked at her partner as if wishing to transfer the glory of admiration to him, showing she valued his opinion above all others. She blended innocence and vanity, displaying the budding affection youthful hearts possess. Though modest and inexperienced in ballroom arts, she knew how to glance at him at just the right moments.

When a new figure, invented by a dancer named Trenis, brought Martial face to face with the Colonel, he joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial dismissed the concern: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

After the quadrille, whispers circulated. The less attractive women made moral comments about Martial and the Comtesse, while the more beautiful marveled at her apparent ease in surrendering to him. The men were baffled by the Baron’s luck, not finding him particularly charming. A few sympathetic women warned against judging the Countess harshly, suggesting young wives would be ruined if a mere glance or graceful dance could compromise a reputation.

Only Martial understood his happiness. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand, feeling her response through the thin glove. “Madame,” he said as the quadrille ended, “do not return to that corner where you’ve been hiding. Is admiration the only benefit you gain from your jewels? Come and enjoy the scene and yourself.”

Madame de Soulanges yielded to his charm, and they walked through the crowded rooms. She hesitated at each door, peering in before entering, only reassured when he said, “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery, where a lavish collation awaited three hundred guests. As supper approached, Martial led the Countess to an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, draped with flowers and blue hangings. The noise faded. Initially startled, the Countess resisted following him but eventually sat on an ottoman after glancing in a mirror, perhaps reassured they could be seen.

“This room is charming,” she remarked, admiring the decor. “All here is love and delight!” Martial, deeply moved, noticed her face in the dim light: a mix of unease, modesty, and eagerness that captivated him. She smiled, which seemed to quell her internal struggle. Flirtatiously she took his left hand and removed the ring he wore, exclaiming, “What a fine diamond!”

Martial, intoxicated by her touch, looked at her with eyes as bright as the gem. “Wear it,” he said, “in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He trailed off, captivated by her rapture. She asked, “You give it to me?” with surprise. “I wish I had the whole world to offer you!” he replied. “Will you accept only my diamond?” She pressed, “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured her.

She placed the ring on her finger, and as Martial prepared to embrace her, she suddenly stood and said clearly, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.” The Baron was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it.”

“You are mistaken, madame,” Martial said, irritated. “It was given to me by Madame de Vaudremont.” “Precisely,” she replied with a smile. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gifted it to you. This ring has made a little journey, that is all. It may reveal secrets to me.” She touched a spring in the ring, revealing a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair, then fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded. He noticed the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughing nearby, and the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial took their banter in stride, grateful for their silence regarding the evening’s events, as he exchanged his horse for a young wife.

As the Comtesse de Soulanges drove across Paris to her home in Faubourg Saint-Germain, she was consumed by anxiety. Before leaving the Hotel Gondreville, she searched for her aunt and husband, who had departed without her. Disturbing suspicions plagued her mind. A silent witness to her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont had ensnared him, she had hoped for his repentance. Now she feared she had made a grave mistake by agreeing to her aunt’s scheme.

The evening darkened her innocent spirit. Initially alarmed by her husband’s suffering, she grew more distressed upon seeing her rival’s beauty, feeling the weight of societal corruption. As she crossed the Pont Royal, she discarded the hair from the diamond, a token of pure affection. Tears flowed as she recalled her long-standing grief, and she shuddered at the thought that a woman seeking peace must bury her pain without complaint.

“Alas!” she pondered, “What can women do when they do not love? What sustains their devotion? I cannot believe, as my aunt claims, that reason alone is enough.”

Still sighing, she arrived home and rushed upstairs, startled to find her husband by the fire. “How long has it been since you attended balls without telling me?” he asked, his voice strained. “You know a woman is out of place without her husband. You compromised yourself by hiding in the dark corner.”

“Oh, my dear Leon,” she coaxed, “I couldn’t resist the joy of seeing you without your knowing. My aunt took me to the ball, and I was very happy there!”

Her words softened the Count’s stern demeanor, for he had been dreading her return, fearing she would learn of his infidelity. In a bid to deflect her anger, he had tried to be the first to criticize her. Delighted to see him smile and grateful for his presence in a room he had frequented less often, the Countess looked at him tenderly, blushing and lowering her eyes. Her kindness further enchanted Soulanges, especially after the misery he had endured at the ball. He took her hand and kissed it gratefully; gratitude often accompanies love.

“Hortense, what is that on your finger that hurt my lip?” he asked, laughing. “It’s my diamond, which you said you lost, and which I have found.”

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont, despite their brief understanding, as she became one of the victims of the tragic fire that marred the ball hosted by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to the daughter of Emperor Joseph II.
