Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, married a wealthy heiress from Bordeaux and became a Gascon after selling his estate. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. Upon returning, he found his estate well-managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville's death in 1810, Monsieur de Manerville grew increasingly miserly, depriving his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul de Manerville, returning from college in 1810, lived under his father's strict control for three years. This oppressive environment stunted his emotional growth, making him timid and unable to assert himself. He felt trapped in his father's mansion, unable to socialize with peers due to financial constraints. His father took him to royalist gatherings, where Paul felt out of place among the aging nobility. To cope, he engaged in physical activities like fencing and horseback riding, while his intellectual pursuits were limited to novels.

After his father's death, Paul inherited a substantial fortune and estates but was disillusioned with Bordeaux and Lanstrac. He spent six years traveling Europe, eventually exhausting his capital and relying on his estate's revenues. Realizing he needed to settle down, Paul decided to return to Bordeaux, manage his affairs, and marry, believing that his noble title would attract a suitable partner.

Now a count, Paul recognized the societal value of marriage. He had gained worldly knowledge through his experiences, which he believed would enhance his prospects. Despite his extravagant lifestyle, he maintained a reputation for integrity and elegance, avoiding gambling and deceit.

One morning, Paul confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry and live as a country gentleman. De Marsay warned him against marriage, arguing it would lead to a loss of freedom and happiness. He criticized the institution, suggesting it primarily benefits children, and questioned whether Paul truly wanted such responsibilities.

De Marsay painted a bleak picture of marriage, emphasizing societal expectations and potential ridicule. He advised Paul to enjoy his bachelorhood and consider marrying later, perhaps a widow, to avoid the complications of a young bride.

Paul defended his desire for a family, longing for companionship and stability. He envisioned a life where he could share joys and secrets with a wife, rejecting de Marsay's cynical view. He believed he had the courage to be a good husband and father, seeking a deep connection with a partner.

De Marsay remained skeptical, arguing that Paul’s romanticized view of marriage was naive. He warned that love dynamics change post-marriage, often leading to disappointment. He cautioned Paul about the legal and emotional complexities, suggesting that a husband must exert control over his wife, which Paul might not be capable of.

Despite de Marsay's warnings, Paul remained resolute in his desire for a family, dismissing the notion that he would become a mere shadow of himself in marriage. He yearned for emotional connections, rather than the superficiality he associated with bachelorhood.

De Marsay continued to challenge Paul’s perspective, arguing that the realities of marriage often clash with ideals of love. He suggested that Paul’s experiences with women might lead to misconceptions about marital relationships, where initial excitement can fade into disillusionment.

Ultimately, Paul’s determination to marry stemmed from a desire for genuine companionship and a stable family life, contrasting sharply with de Marsay’s cynical outlook. The conversation highlighted the tension between societal expectations and personal desires, as Paul sought to navigate the complexities of love and commitment in a changing world.

In the winter of 1821, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux, intent on restoring his family mansion and maintaining his reputation for elegance. His connections within royalist society quickly elevated him to prominence, earning him the nickname “the pink of fashion.” His charm and Parisian sophistication captivated the local elite, allowing him to navigate social interactions with ease.

Paul de Manerville's success in provincial society stemmed from his attention to appearance and gentle demeanor, endearing him to both men and women. However, he soon recognized that marriage was essential for a fulfilling life in the provinces. Although too young for the mundane pursuits of provincial life, he longed for companionship, particularly with Mademoiselle Evangelista, the celebrated beauty of Bordeaux.

Mademoiselle Evangelista was the daughter of a wealthy Spanish merchant who had settled in Bordeaux. After her father's death, Madame Evangelista maintained a lavish lifestyle, raising her daughter in luxury. By the time Paul returned, Natalie was a stunning young woman, yet rumors about her extravagant upbringing made potential suitors hesitant, with many believing only a prince could afford to marry her.

When Paul and Natalie first met, they were immediately drawn to each other, each secretly envisioning a future together. Paul was aware of the Evangelista wealth and felt no need for inquiries that would typically deter a suitor. He dismissed warnings about the family's extravagance, confident in his ability to manage the financial implications of marrying Natalie.

As Paul frequented the Evangelista household, he became increasingly enchanted by both mother and daughter. Madame Evangelista, at forty, was still strikingly beautiful and adept at navigating social circles, harboring ambitions for her daughter to rise in Parisian society. Paul, appreciating the warmth of the Evangelistas, found himself spending more time with them, leading society to speculate about his intentions toward Natalie.

Despite the growing attention, neither Paul nor Natalie openly acknowledged their feelings. Natalie maintained a reserved demeanor, intriguing Paul, while Madame Evangelista subtly encouraged their connection. Paul began to view marriage as a means to secure his social standing and fulfill his ambitions, believing that wealth would ease the challenges of married life.

As their relationship developed, Paul idealized Natalie, overlooking potential flaws in her character. Captivated by her beauty and charm, he failed to recognize the complexities that might arise in their future together. Societal pressures surrounding their union added to the allure, as both families seemed to endorse the match.

However, beneath the surface, Natalie’s upbringing and her mother’s ambitions posed potential challenges. While Paul was drawn to the idea of a life with Natalie, he remained unaware of the deeper dynamics at play, including Madame Evangelista’s desire for power through her daughter's marriage.

As Paul contemplated marriage, he grappled with the realities of commitment and potential conflict, caught between romantic ideals and the practicalities of a life intertwined with Natalie and her mother. This chapter foreshadows the complexities of their impending union, highlighting the societal expectations and personal ambitions that would shape their lives, revealing the intricate dance of desires and challenges that accompany the pursuit of love and status in a society governed by appearances.

In the winter of 1822, Paul de Manerville sought to marry Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista with the assistance of his great-aunt, Baronne de Maulincour. After consulting Madame Evangelista, the baroness informed Paul that the marriage was likely to succeed but warned him about the dowry: Natalie’s fortune was tied to her father’s patrimony, and her mother would not contribute.

The baroness advised Paul to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to ensure the marriage contract protected his interests for future children, leaving Paul anxious about potential deceit from his future mother-in-law.

Following this advice, Paul approached Madame Evangelista to discuss the marriage contract. She suggested that both families’ notaries meet to negotiate terms. Nervous, Paul agreed, relieved that the notaries would handle the details.

Madame Evangelista faced pressure as she owed her daughter a significant sum from her late husband’s estate, which she could not pay. Fearing Paul might withdraw if he learned the truth, she resolved to manipulate the situation.

The next day, she summoned her ambitious notary, Maitre Solonet, who assured her they could navigate negotiations favorably. Madame Evangelista aimed to maintain control over her daughter’s fortune while ensuring a good match for Natalie.

As evening approached, both notaries met to discuss the marriage contract. Solonet proposed terms benefiting Madame Evangelista, while Mathias insisted on protecting Paul’s interests. Their discussions highlighted stark differences: Mathias represented traditional values, while Solonet embodied modern, opportunistic strategies.

During dinner, Paul and Natalie shared tender moments, but the tension regarding the marriage contract loomed. Paul was enamored with Natalie, but Mathias warned him about the financial implications of marrying into a family that had squandered much of its wealth.

As negotiations continued, Mathias expressed concern over the potential ruin of Paul’s fortune if he accepted unfavorable terms. He urged caution, but Paul, blinded by love, was eager to proceed.

Ultimately, a compromise was reached where Madame Evangelista would secure her daughter’s inheritance while ensuring her own financial stability. The notaries agreed on terms that would protect Paul’s interests and provide for future generations. As the evening concluded, both families felt relief, believing they had successfully navigated the complexities of the marriage contract, though underlying tensions hinted at challenges ahead.

The next day, Elie Magus, a jeweler in Bordeaux, was summoned by Madame Evangelista to discuss jewelry for the upcoming marriage of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie. Instead, she sought an appraisal of her mother’s diamonds, crucial for the marriage contract. Magus valued the necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000 francs but offered only 75,000 francs for the diamond and 160,000 for the jewelry, citing depreciation.

This conversation sparked rumors among the household staff and community about the marriage's stability. Speculation arose that Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the marriage might not happen. Despite notaries denying these claims, public skepticism persisted, fueled by jealousy of the Evangelista family's wealth.

As rumors spread, Paul organized a hunting party, further igniting speculation. Madame de Gyas, eager to confirm the gossip, visited Natalie and her mother, feigning concern while relaying the circulating rumors. Aware of the motivations behind the gossip, Madame Evangelista dismissed the concerns, asserting that the marriage contract was a private matter.

To counter the rumors, Madame Evangelista planned an extravagant celebration called the "fete of the camellias," intending to showcase her wealth. Preparations took over a month, during which the legalities of the marriage contract were finalized, and the community buzzed with anticipation.

On the day of the contract signing, Madame Evangelista had a moment of realization regarding the implications of the contract. Recalling her earlier discussions with the notaries, she feared her daughter's fortune might be compromised. Despite her initial confidence, she resolved to demand clarity on the contract's terms.

Lavish preparations transformed the courtyard into a tent filled with camellias, creating an opulent atmosphere. As guests arrived, excitement heightened, but the signing of the contract loomed ominously. Paul and Natalie, blissfully unaware of the underlying tensions, engaged in playful banter while the notaries read the contract details.

Madame Evangelista listened intently, and when the entailment clause was explained, she realized the potential financial ruin for her daughter. The entail would limit Natalie’s inheritance, leaving her vulnerable should Paul die first. This revelation struck Madame Evangelista deeply, leading her to conclude that her daughter was at risk of losing her fortune.

Despairing, she recognized that breaking off the marriage was no longer an option; the public nature of the preparations made it impossible. She felt trapped, realizing her earlier manipulations had backfired, jeopardizing her daughter’s future.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista's emotions boiled over. She confronted the notaries, expressing her anger and frustration. Solonet, one of the notaries, suggested delaying the signing, but Madame Evangelista felt that would worsen their reputation.

Ultimately, she decided to proceed with the marriage, vowing to protect her daughter’s interests. In a moment of clarity, she resolved to regain control over her daughter’s fortune, planning to invest wisely for Natalie’s security.

The signing of the contract marked a turning point, with Madame Evangelista determined to navigate her new reality. As the festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, knowing her daughter’s happiness was intertwined with her own ambitions and fears. The night unfolded with dancing and celebration, but beneath the surface, tensions simmered, setting the stage for future conflicts.

As Paul prepared for his marriage, he was troubled by doubts, particularly regarding his mother-in-law, Madame Evangelista. While he reassured himself about their financial arrangements and his union with Natalie, he remained cautious, mindful of Mathias's warnings.

Unbeknownst to Paul, the dynamics had shifted. Madame Evangelista, once supportive, was now contemplating withdrawing her interests from their lives. Paul struggled to understand her complex Creole nature, which combined intellect and passionate volatility, now stirred by the prospect of losing her daughter.

The next day, Madame Evangelista and Natalie reminisced about their harmonious life. She expressed sorrow over Natalie’s marriage, warning that love could fade if not managed carefully. She advised Natalie to establish her authority as a wife, emphasizing the importance of appearing obedient while subtly maintaining control.

Madame Evangelista cautioned against the dangers of constant proximity in marriage, suggesting social engagements to maintain allure and keep Paul interested. She stressed that a woman must never fully surrender her power, advising Natalie to cultivate mystery and reserve.

As the wedding approached, Madame Evangelista revealed her plan to sell her jewelry to support Natalie, proposing an exchange that would benefit her daughter. Paul, moved by this sacrifice, agreed, unaware of the underlying tensions.

When Paul arrived, he seemed troubled, leading Madame Evangelista to suspect Mathias's influence. She reiterated the importance of a woman's vanity in maintaining happiness and proposed selling her jewelry to ensure Natalie had valuable pieces. Paul accepted, and they discussed selling their property, with Natalie showing signs of understanding business matters.

Guests noted the unusual circumstances of the wedding, with some criticizing Paul for excluding Madame Evangelista. Meanwhile, Madame Evangelista, having played her part, left Natalie with a final piece of advice: to be a wife, not a mistress.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville visited his old friend, Monsieur Mathias, in Bordeaux. Mathias, now retired, was absent, but his housekeeper led Paul to Madame Mathias's room. Exhausted from his journey, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned, he found Paul changed but calm. They shared a meal, during which Mathias expressed sorrow over the public notices of Paul's financial troubles.

Mathias lamented the loss of Paul's estate, feeling personally shamed by the public seizures. Paul revealed he had left his wife, Natalie, without her knowledge, planning to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune under the name Camille. Mathias was shocked by Paul's decision to enter commerce, believing it beneath his dignity.

Paul explained that he had incurred massive debts due to lavish spending and the costs of his marriage. He had given Natalie expensive gifts and sold her investments to maintain their lifestyle. Mathias was incredulous at the scale of Paul's debts, which exceeded a million and a half francs. Paul insisted he could not burden Natalie with their financial troubles and preferred to separate their assets.

Mathias suggested Paul could live off Natalie's income while recovering, but Paul rejected this, wanting to work for his fortune rather than rely on her. He believed women should not be involved in financial matters, while Mathias felt a wife should share both joys and burdens of marriage.

The next day, Mathias received a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay, a friend of Paul’s. Despite his concerns about Paul's plans, Mathias ultimately accepted Paul's determination to leave for India. The ship, "Belle-Amelie," was set to sail, and Mathias accompanied Paul to the quay, where they faced curiosity and gossip about Paul's downfall.

As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with a handkerchief, indicating he had important letters. However, the sailor delivering the letters did not convey their urgency. Paul, wanting to avoid being swayed by their contents, pocketed the letters unread.

Once at sea, Paul reflected on his life and the choices that led him here. Overcome by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin contemplating his situation. Eventually, he opened the letters from Natalie and de Marsay.

Natalie's letter expressed her anguish over Paul's departure and her desire to support him. She felt betrayed by his decision to leave without consulting her and vowed to economize to pay off his debts. She also revealed she believed she was pregnant, filling her with hope for their future.

In contrast, de Marsay's letter detailed the extent of Paul's financial ruin, revealing that Natalie's mother had orchestrated much of the situation. De Marsay warned Paul of a conspiracy against him and urged him to return to Paris instead of going to India, suggesting he could regain his honor by confronting his rivals.

As Paul absorbed the harsh realities of his situation, he felt anger and despair. He questioned what he had done to deserve such betrayal and turmoil. Ultimately, he resolved to confront his challenges head-on, determined to rebuild his life and restore his honor.
