Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, married a wealthy Bordeaux heiress and became a Gascon, drawn to her estate, the château de Lanstrac. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique in 1790. On his return he found Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a notary’s clerk who embraced new ideas. After Madame de Manerville's death in 1810, the old man grew miserly and deprived his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul returned from college in 1810 and endured three years of strict paternal control that stifled his character. Physically courageous, he lacked the will to oppose his father and thus lost moral courage; repressed feelings made him timid in society, trapped by the old mansion and unable to engage with peers. His father took him to royalist gatherings where Paul felt out of place among the remnants of nobility, bored and unaware of potential social connections.

To escape monotony, Paul practiced activities his father deemed essential for a gentleman—fencing, horseback riding—and read novels in his free time. His father’s death liberated him, leaving him a substantial inheritance and well-maintained estates. Yet he harbored a deep aversion to Bordeaux and Lanstrac, where he had spent summers hunting.

Eager for a new life, Paul invested in consols and traveled across Europe, serving at various embassies. Years of indulgence and poor management eroded much of his fortune; he then decided to return to Bordeaux to manage his affairs and settle down as a country gentleman. Conscious of his rank, he felt compelled to marry well, believing his foreign experience had given him the knowledge and elegance for a successful match.

Despite extravagance, Paul had harmed no woman and kept a reputation for integrity. He maintained a refined household with well-dressed servants and a fashionable bachelor establishment. One day he confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry and settle. De Marsay cautioned him, arguing marriage would invite ridicule and responsibilities that would diminish his happiness.

De Marsay dismissed marriage as chiefly for securing money or heirs, warning it would not guarantee affection and that children often resent parental authority. He believed marriage mortifies individuality, confines people to mediocrity, and provokes the hatred it seeks to prevent. He urged Paul to wait until older and more established, doubting a young wife would share Paul’s ideals or disdain for social expectations.

Paul, however, sincerely desired an affectionate companion—emotional support and domestic happiness rather than social advantage. He rejected the notion that marriage must stifle him and insisted he was ready to embrace family responsibilities. The conversation highlighted de Marsay’s cynicism versus Paul’s earnest longing; despite warnings, Paul remained resolute, accepting the risks in pursuit of fulfillment.

Despite earlier conversations, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821 resolved to implement his plans. Restoring and furnishing the family mansion reinforced his reputation for elegance. Reintroduced to Bordeaux’s royalist society, he quickly rose to fashionable prominence, bringing Parisian manners and savoir-faire. An old marquise dubbed him “the pink of fashion,” a phrase used satirically by liberals and in earnest by royalists.

Paul de Manerville welcomed the title and prospered on attention. Combining charm and wit, he was respectful yet engaging with women, attentive to appearance without mere foppery. Medium height, healthy complexion, and graceful movements added to his appeal. More inclined to receive happiness than bestow it, he wanted understanding and encouragement—qualities that made him well suited for marriage. Conscious that provincial life required a household, Paul—still young and averse to mundane pursuits—saw marriage as a source of variety. His inclination intensified by a secret love for the beauty of Bordeaux, Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Around the turn of the century a wealthy Spaniard named Evangelista had settled in Bordeaux and, by fortune and connections, entered noble salons. His wife, a Creole from an illustrious Spanish family, maintained their status with extravagant tastes, indulging whims without heed to expense, backed by her devoted husband. After Evangelista’s death in 1813, Madame Evangelista, a widow with a fortune and a beautiful daughter, continued to live lavishly even as her wealth diminished.

By Paul’s return, Natalie was striking, yet no proposals had been made—largely because her mother’s extravagance discouraged suitors. Rumor held that only a prince could afford to marry her, a notion fomented by jealous mothers and rivals. Natalie, accustomed to luxury and adoration, remained largely unaware of life beyond privilege.

Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and immediately recognized each other’s charm. Madame Evangelista perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul imagined Natalie as his future wife. He had known of the Evangelista fortune since childhood, which eased his approach. Warnings about the family’s extravagance were dismissed by Paul, whose Parisian experience let him navigate provincial gossip. Madame began inviting him to formal dinners, and he found himself drawn into the prospect of marriage.

As he spent time with them he felt pride in their company. Society began to view him as Natalie’s suitor, and he enjoyed the attendant attention. Royalist circles flattered him, suggesting a match would be prudent given Natalie’s beauty and her mother’s intelligence and connections. Paul frequently visited their home and was captivated by Madame Evangelista’s beauty and charm; at forty she retained a striking presence, and her reputation for virtue enhanced her allure. Their relationship carried a mutual understanding: Madame saw Paul as a means to raise her social standing in Paris.

Paul was enchanted by the mother-daughter dynamic, which blended friendship with familial affection, and he flattered himself on the intimacy. Yet Madame Evangelista harbored ambitions of her own and hoped to use Paul to further them. Flattered, he increased his visits.

A year after his return, Paul’s attentions to Natalie led society to conclude he was courting her, though neither mother nor daughter seemed actively to pursue marriage. Natalie’s charming reserve intrigued Paul; he valued her ability to engage without familiarity, a restraint that allowed him to imagine a future together and to see marriage as desirable. He rationalized that marriage would bring him happiness, especially given his financial means and the compatibility of her upbringing in luxury with his lifestyle. He focused on these benefits and brushed aside concerns about potential difficulties.

Contemplating their future, Paul saw Natalie’s beauty and standing as enhancements to his own—an alliance that would secure his position and elevate his social status. He remained unaware of the complexities ahead, idealizing the relationship without grasping the realities of marriage. Natalie, sheltered and naive, was attracted to Paul’s charm but lacked experience of married life. Paul, though enamored, was cautious about possible pitfalls and whether he had the strength to manage a relationship with a woman who might not fully appreciate him.

The narrative then widens to the broader theme of marriage in French society, notably the negotiations over marriage contracts. Often ignored in literature, these contracts are crucial in shaping couples’ lives by binding personal desires to societal expectations. The impending discussions about the marriage contract will ultimately influence Paul and Natalie’s future, foreshadowing the complexities to come.

At the beginning of winter 1822, Paul de Manerville formally asked for Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand through his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour, who stayed in Médoc to assist him. After discussing the dowry with Madame Evangelista, the baroness reported the marriage was likely: Natalie’s dowry was her patrimony, and her mother would contribute nothing. The baroness advised Paul to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to ensure the marriage contract protected him.

Anxious, Paul visited Madame Evangelista to discuss the contract. She proposed that both families’ notaries meet to negotiate terms, but privately confessed she owed her daughter a large sum from her late husband’s estate and could not pay. Trapped by this debt, she feared that if Paul withdrew, Natalie’s prospects would be ruined.

The next day Madame Evangelista summoned her notary, Maitre Solonet, and revealed her troubles. Solonet, confident and pragmatic, assured her they could craft a contract that concealed the financial difficulties while favoring their side, and believed they could negotiate without exposing her debt.

That evening both notaries met. Mathias, representing Paul, was cautious and traditional, intent on shielding his client from risk. Solonet was more modern and opportunistic. During dinner Paul and Natalie shared tender moments while the lawyers considered the marriage’s financial implications. Mathias warned of the imbalance: Natalie’s family had squandered wealth, and marrying into such a household could imperil Paul.

Madame Evangelista and Solonet proposed she sell her house and use the proceeds to pay debts, thus improving Natalie’s dowry. Mathias doubted this would suffice and feared it still exposed Paul to vulnerability. Tension rose as the notaries debated contract terms; Mathias insisted on safeguards, Solonet pushed for arrangements benefiting Madame Evangelista, and their discussion grew heated as both underscored the marriage’s financial stakes.

Ultimately they compromised. Madame Evangelista agreed to create an entail on her property to secure her daughter’s future while protecting Paul’s interests. This arrangement allowed the marriage to proceed without immediate financial threat to either party.

As the evening ended, both families felt relieved—Paul optimistic about his future with Natalie, Madame Evangelista satisfied with the negotiation—yet underlying tensions and potential conflict lingered. The first day of negotiations revealed the complexities of love, ambition, and money, setting the stage for challenges ahead.

The following day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, answered Madame Evangelista’s summons expecting to discuss jewels for the bride, Comte Paul’s fiancée Mademoiselle Natalie. Instead he was asked to appraise the mother-in-law’s property, including her diamonds, for the marriage contract. Aware of differences between Asian and Brazilian stones, Magus valued Madame Evangelista’s necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000 francs. When she asked about selling them he warned of the heavy loss she would incur.

That conversation sparked staff rumors and speculation about the marriage’s stability. Society seized on the drama: gossip claimed Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the marriage was in jeopardy. Notaries denied the claims, but public opinion remained skeptical, fueled by envy of the Evangelistas’ wealth and rank.

Paul organizing a hunting party only intensified the talk. Madame de Gyas, eager to confirm the story, visited Natalie and her mother under a pretense of concern and relayed the circulating tales. The two women dismissed the gossip, recognizing its motives—especially the scheming of mothers pushing daughters into advantageous matches.

Preparing for the wedding, Madame Evangelista planned a grand fête of the camellias to display her wealth and counter rumors. The fête required lavish decorations and the finalization of the marriage contract. As the day approached she had a moment of doubt about the contract’s implications, suspecting her daughter’s fortune might be at risk.

On the day of signing the atmosphere was festive; guests arrived for the celebration. Natalie, radiant in bridal attire, and Paul, equally enamored, paid little heed to the legal discussions. Madame Evangelista concentrated on the contract, which spelled out financial arrangements and an entailment of property.

When the notaries explained the entail, Madame Evangelista understood that her daughter’s financial security was compromised: if Paul died first, Natalie would inherit only a fraction of the expected fortune. The revelation struck her deeply; she felt her daughter was being ruined.

Although she had once intended to manipulate the arrangement, Madame Evangelista found herself constrained by the public nature of the wedding preparations and the expectations they created. She resolved to ensure her daughter’s happiness even if it meant sacrificing some of her own interests.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista’s emotions surged. She vowed to protect her daughter’s future while nursing resentment toward Paul. The signing marked a turning point, cementing the marriage and the financial ties that bound the families.

After the festivities, Madame Evangelista resolved to reclaim her daughter’s fortune. Determined to invest wisely and regain control, she transformed from a careless spender into a shrewd strategist, driven by a desire for vengeance against Paul and setting the stage for future conflicts.

The fête briefly distracted Paul from his anxieties, but they returned that night. He feared Madame Evangelista might have deceived him without Mathias's intervention, though he comforted himself that in two days Natalie would be his and their finances seemed settled. Still, he resolved to stay vigilant, uncertain whether Mathias’s worries were justified.

Unbeknownst to Paul, his future had shifted. The more astute woman in his life had become an adversary, considering withdrawing her interests from their shared future. He failed to see the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law—her blend of European intellect, tropical passion, and Eastern reserve—dormant during a happy marriage but now inflamed by the prospect of losing her daughter. With only a day until the wedding, Madame Evangelista realized she could shape the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her implicitly, and a single conversation could bend social expectations.

The next morning she sat on Natalie’s bed, recalling their harmonious life and mourning the idea of Natalie bound to a man she might have to obey. Natalie dismissed obedience, expecting love to prevail, while Madame Evangelista warned that conjugal love can fade without careful cultivation and urged Natalie to establish authority from the outset.

She proposed that her own presence could complicate Natalie’s new role and suggested leaving to avoid conflict, counseling Natalie to appear obedient while subtly asserting dominance. Their talk exposed marital complexities: Madame Evangelista advocated maintaining a social life to keep Paul interested, warning that constant proximity breeds indifference. She advised Natalie to preserve her allure, balance affection with independence, and keep Paul entertained so their relationship stayed vibrant.

Overwhelmed, Natalie sought guidance on navigating her new life. Madame Evangelista reassured her they would remain in contact and that she would visit, but insisted separation was necessary for Natalie’s happiness and future.

When Paul arrived he attempted to hide his unease; Madame Evangelista noticed and offered to sell her jewelry to support the couple, framing it as a sacrifice for Natalie’s happiness and vanity. Paul, touched, accepted.

Soon Solonet, the notary, arrived with news of potential buyers for Madame Evangelista’s property, which could provide a substantial financial boost. Natalie consented to the sale, acknowledging the need for prudent management of their affairs.

Determined to secure her daughter’s prospects, Madame Evangelista decided to step back from their Parisian life, believing her presence might impede the marriage. She would support them from a distance, allowing Natalie and Paul to establish their own dynamic.

As the wedding neared, preparations for the midnight ceremony carried an air of foreboding; guests speculated about Madame Evangelista’s absence and its implications. After the ceremony she adopted the pose of a grieving mother, extracting promises from Paul that ensured her continued influence and sending the newlyweds to Paris satisfied that her plans were unfolding. Alone with Paul, Natalie began to assert her authority, setting the stage for the complex dynamics of their marriage.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at the home of his old friend Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux. The retired notary, too old for business and living quietly after selling his practice, was absent; his housekeeper led Paul to the room of the late Madame Mathias, who had died a year before. Exhausted, Paul slept until evening. When Mathias returned, he found Paul asleep and, noting it was the anniversary of his wife's death, expressed concern.

Mathias lamented the public notices of Paul's financial troubles, feeling personal shame at seeing the seizure announcements. He reflected that since Paul's marriage to Natalie Evangelista, things had gone badly, blaming that union for Paul's ruin. Despite his own difficulties, Mathias was relieved to have saved the titles to Paul's estates, though the usufruct had been lost.

When Paul awoke, he seemed calm despite his altered appearance. They shared a meal, and Mathias voiced sorrow over the seizure of Paul's properties. Paul revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and was sailing to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. Mathias was shocked that a count would enter commerce, but Paul insisted he was no longer a count and was determined to succeed under another name.

Paul outlined his financial situation, admitting massive debts incurred through lavish spending and gifts to Natalie. Mathias was incredulous at the scale of liabilities, including large sums owed to usurers. Paul acknowledged he had been forced to sell assets and take loans to maintain their lifestyle, culminating in the current catastrophe.

Despite Mathias's concerns, Paul was resolute about seeking fortune abroad. He believed he could return wealthy by forty. Mathias doubted the plan's feasibility and the reliability of de Marsay, who was supposed to send money.

The next day Mathias received a bill of exchange from de Marsay, confirming funds would arrive. Paul, however, remained determined to leave for India, convinced it was his only chance to recover. As he prepared to depart, Mathias lamented losing his friend, and Paul boarded the ship, waving goodbye.

Onboard, Paul was consumed by thoughts of his past and his love for Natalie. He felt a mix of regret and determination facing an uncertain future. Overcome by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin, reflecting on his choices and the affection binding him to his wife.

Once better, Paul opened letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie's letter revealed anguish at his departure and a resolve to support him financially—she pledged her jewels to pay his debts and vowed to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize to help him. She expressed love and commitment, promising practical sacrifices.

Paul was moved by her words, torn between love and despair. He recognized the depth of their bond yet was haunted by doubts about their future. De Marsay's letter laid out the extent of Paul's ruin and the machinations of his mother-in-law, who had conspired against him. It painted a grim picture: Paul's wife and her mother profited from his downfall.

As Paul processed betrayal and reality, he felt anger and helplessness. He realized he had been naive in trust and love; the weight of his decisions pressed on him. In the end, Paul was left to grapple with the consequences of his choices, the love he had for Natalie, and the uncertain path ahead as he sailed away from France, contemplating what the future might bring.
