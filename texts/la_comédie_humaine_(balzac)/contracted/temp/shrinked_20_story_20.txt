Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman who married a wealthy Bordeaux heiress, grew attached to her Gascon estate, the château de Lanstrac. He avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique in 1790 and returned to find Lanstrac well managed by Mathias, a progressive notary’s clerk. After Madame de Manerville’s death in 1810, the old man became miserly and deprived his only son, Paul, of resources.

Paul returned from college in 1810 and endured three years of strict paternal control that stifled him. Though brave, he lacked the will to oppose his father and thus lost moral courage; his suppressed feelings made him timid in society. Confined to his father’s mansion, he failed to connect with peers and felt out of place at royalist gatherings of the old nobility, where he was bored and isolated.

To escape monotony, Paul practiced fencing and riding—accomplishments his father demanded of a gentleman—and read novels. His father’s death finally freed him: he inherited substantial estates and well-kept property but retained an aversion to Bordeaux and Lanstrac, scenes of his constrained youth. Seeking a new life, Paul invested in consols and traveled across Europe, serving at several embassies. Years of indulgence and poor management depleted much of his fortune, and he resolved to return to Bordeaux to settle as a country gentleman. Conscious of rank, he felt compelled to marry well, believing his foreign experience had refined him for a successful match.

Despite extravagance, Paul had wronged no woman and kept a reputation for integrity; his household remained tasteful. He told his friend de Marsay of his wish to marry. De Marsay warned that marriage brings ridicule, responsibility, and loss of individuality. Paul insisted he sought emotional companionship, not social advantage, though de Marsay remained skeptical.

Despite earlier doubts, the Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux in the winter of 1821 determined to pursue his plans. Restoring and furnishing the family mansion burnished his reputation for elegance, and his Parisian manners quickly won him a leading place in Bordeaux’s royalist society. An old marquise called him “the pink of fashion,” a phrase liberals satirized and royalists admired.

Paul de Manerville enjoyed the attention. Charming, witty, and attentive to women, he mixed a touch of foppery with a genuine concern for his appearance. Of medium height, healthy complexion, and graceful movements, he preferred receiving happiness to bestowing it and sought the understanding and encouragement he believed marriage would provide. Young and averse to mundane pursuits, he nonetheless accepted that provincial life required matrimony and domestic variety. His inclination was sharpened by a secret love for Mademoiselle Evangelista.

Years earlier an affluent Spaniard, Evangelista, had settled in Bordeaux and, by fortune and connections, entered noble salons. His Creole wife, from an illustrious Spanish family, upheld their social position with extravagant tastes, indulging whims regardless of cost and backed by her devoted husband. After Evangelista’s death in 1813, Madame Evangelista—now a wealthy widow with a beautiful daughter—continued living lavishly even as her fortune waned.

By Paul’s return Natalie was a striking young woman, yet proposals had not come, largely because her mother’s extravagance discouraged suitors. Gossip exaggerated the situation, claiming only a prince could afford her. Accustomed to luxury and adulation, she remained largely ignorant of life beyond privilege.

Six months after Paul’s arrival they met at a high-society event and found mutual charm. Madame Evangelista perceived his interest and hoped he would become her son-in-law; Paul began to picture Natalie as his future wife. He had known of the Evangelista fortune since childhood, which eased his approach. Dismissing warnings about their extravagance with Parisian disdain, he navigated provincial gossip without deterrence. Madame invited him to formal dinners, and his appetite for marriage deepened.

Spending time with the Evangelistas, he felt proud of their company, and society soon treated him as Natalie’s suitor. Royalist circles called the match prudent given Natalie’s beauty and her mother’s intelligence and connections. Madame, forty and still striking, retained a reputation for virtue that enhanced her allure. Their relationship contained mutual understanding: Madame saw Paul as a means to advance socially in Paris; Paul felt flattered.

A year after his return people concluded he was courting Natalie, though neither mother nor daughter actively pressed for marriage. Natalie’s charming reserve intrigued him; he rationalized that marriage would bring happiness and secure his position, brushing aside potential difficulties. Sheltered and naive, Natalie was attracted to Paul’s charm but inexperienced in married life; Paul, enamored yet cautious, wondered if he could manage someone who might not fully appreciate him.

The narrative widens to note that in French society marriage is often governed by negotiations over marriage contracts—frequently overlooked in literature but crucial in shaping couples’ lives. Those impending discussions would influence Paul and Natalie’s future, foreshadowing social and personal complexities ahead.

In winter 1822 Paul de Manerville asked for Mademoiselle Natalie Evangelista’s hand through his great-aunt, the Baronne de Maulincour of Médoc. After discussing the dowry with Madame Evangelista, the baroness reported the marriage probable: Natalie’s dowry would be her patrimony; her mother would contribute nothing. She urged Paul to consult Maître Mathias to protect his interests in the contract.

Paul, anxious, visited Madame Evangelista, who proposed a joint meeting of the families’ notaries but privately admitted she owed her daughter a large sum from her late husband’s estate and could not pay. Fearing Paul might withdraw and harm Natalie’s prospects, she summoned Maître Solonet, who promised they could conceal the difficulty and craft a favorable contract.

Mathias and Solonet met that evening. Mathias, cautious and traditional, sought to safeguard Paul; Solonet favored practical, flexible solutions. While Paul and Natalie exchanged tender moments at dinner, the notaries debated the financial risk: Mathias warned that Natalie's family had squandered wealth; Madame Evangelista and Solonet suggested selling her house to cover debts. They compromised reluctantly: Madame Evangelista would place an entail on her property to protect Natalie while limiting Paul’s exposure. The marriage could proceed, but tensions remained, foreshadowing further tests of love, ambition, and money.

The next day Elie Magus, then in Bordeaux, answered Madame Evangelista’s summons expecting to discuss jewels for Comte Paul’s fiancée. Instead he appraised the mother-in-law’s possessions, valuing her necklace and earrings at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” at 100,000 francs, warning that selling them would incur heavy loss.

That conversation ignited staff rumors and society gossip about the marriage’s stability: whispers that Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the union might be in jeopardy. Notaries denied the claims, but public skepticism—fueled by envy of the Evangelistas’ wealth—persisted. Paul organizing a hunting party only heightened speculation. Madame de Gyas, feigning concern, visited Natalie and her mother to relay the tales; they dismissed the gossip, recognizing its self-interested origins among mothers eager to marry off daughters advantageously.

To counter the rumors and display her resources, Madame Evangelista planned a grand fête—the fête of the camellias—combined with finalizing the marriage contract. As the day neared she grew uneasy about the terms; at the signing she learned that if Paul died first Natalie would inherit only a fraction of her expected fortune. Struck and alarmed, Madame Evangelista resolved, despite personal cost, to protect Natalie’s future, shifting from careless spender to shrewd strategist bent on reclaiming the family fortune and influencing Paul—setting the stage for future conflict.

The fête had briefly eased Paul’s anxieties, which returned that night. He feared Madame Evangelista might have deceived him without Mathias’s intervention but comforted himself that in two days Natalie would be his and their finances settled. He did not realize his future had already shifted: the shrewd woman in his life had become an adversary, considering withdrawing her interests from their shared future. He failed to see the cunning of his Creole mother-in-law—part European intellect, part tropical passion, part Eastern indifference—awakened at the thought of losing her daughter. With a day until the marriage, Madame Evangelista perceived she could shape the couple’s life: Natalie trusted her implicitly, and a single conversation might alter their fate.

The next morning, seated on Natalie’s bed, Madame Evangelista recalled happier times and bewailed the idea of her daughter bound to a man she might have to obey. Natalie, confident that love would govern, brushed aside warnings of obedience. Madame countered that conjugal love can fade if not carefully cultivated and urged Natalie to establish authority from the start. She counselled leaving to avoid complicating the new household and to feign obedience while subtly asserting dominance.

Their talk produced practical marital strategies: keep an active social life to hold Paul’s interest; avoid constant proximity that breeds indifference; preserve allure and independence; and keep him entertained so the relationship remains vibrant. Overwhelmed, Natalie sought guidance. Madame promised continued contact and visits but insisted some separation was necessary for Natalie’s happiness and prospects.

When Paul arrived he tried to conceal his unease; Madame noticed and offered to sell her jewelry to support the couple, framing it as a sacrifice for Natalie’s vanity and future. Paul, moved, accepted. Solonet, the notary, brought news of potential buyers for Madame Evangelista’s property, which could provide a substantial financial boost; Natalie agreed to the sale.

Determined to secure her daughter’s prospects, Madame resolved to step back from their Parisian life and support them from afar so Natalie and Paul could form their own dynamic. As the midnight wedding neared, preparations carried a foreboding air and guests noted her absence. After the ceremony she played the grieving mother, extracted promises from Paul to secure her influence, and sent the newlyweds to Paris. Once alone with Paul, Natalie began to assert her authority, setting the stage for the complex dynamics of their marriage.

Five years later, in November, Comte Paul de Manerville, cloaked and somber, arrived at the house of his old friend Monsieur Mathias in Bordeaux. The retired notary was absent; his housekeeper led Paul to the late Madame Mathias’s room, where he stayed until evening. When Mathias returned and found Paul there on the anniversary of his wife’s death, he showed concern and embarrassment over public notices of Paul’s financial ruin. Mathias blamed Paul’s marriage to Natalie for the downfall, though he was relieved to have preserved the estate titles even as the usufruct was lost.

Paul appeared altered but calm. Over a meal, Mathias lamented the seizure of Paul’s properties. Paul then revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge and intended to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune. Mathias, shocked that a count would enter commerce, was told Paul no longer considered himself a count and planned to succeed under another name. Paul detailed enormous debts from lavish spending and gifts to Natalie; he admitted selling assets and borrowing heavily from usurers to sustain their lifestyle, which produced the present catastrophe.

Despite Mathias’s doubts and skepticism about de Marsay—who was meant to send funds—Paul insisted India was his only chance and vowed to return wealthy by forty. The next day Mathias received de Marsay’s bill of exchange confirming some funds would arrive, but Paul remained determined to leave. Mathias lamented losing his friend as Paul boarded the ship and waved goodbye.

At sea, Paul was consumed by memories and his love for Natalie, torn between regret and resolve. Struck by seasickness, he spent days in his cabin reflecting on his choices. When he recovered, he opened letters from Natalie and de Marsay. Natalie’s letter revealed anguish and a vow to support him: she pledged her jewels to pay his debts and promised to live at Lanstrac with her mother and economize. De Marsay’s letter detailed Paul’s ruin and exposed the mother-in-law’s profiteering. Confronted with love and treachery, Paul felt anger, helplessness, and remorse as he sailed away from France uncertain of the future.
