In Saumur, Monsieur Grandet, a wealthy man who amassed his fortune during the Revolution, lives in a cold house with his wife, Madame Grandet, and daughter, Eugenie. By 1816, his estate is valued at nearly four million francs, and he is focused on marrying Eugenie amidst competition from local suitors. The sale of Froidfond heightens this rivalry.

On Eugenie's birthday in November 1819, her cousin Charles arrives from Paris, charming her despite Grandet's sternness. News of Grandet's brother's bankruptcy and suicide complicates family dynamics, with Eugenie developing feelings for Charles while Grandet dismisses the idea of their union. 

Grandet's greed surfaces as he prioritizes wealth over family, scolding Eugenie for wanting to help Charles. Despite the family's mourning, Eugenie secretly comforts Charles, deepening their bond. As Charles prepares to leave for the Indies, Grandet arranges for his belongings to be sent away, leaving Eugenie heartbroken and transformed by love.

Two months later, Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, were anxious about a secret involving her cousin Charles. As New Year’s Day approached, Madame Grandet feared facing Grandet, who expected to see Eugenie's gold. On January 1, 1820, Grandet, in good spirits from financial success, demanded Eugenie's gold after breakfast. When she claimed she no longer had it, he became furious, accusing her of deceit.

Eugenie, desperate to protect her mother, faced Grandet alone as Madame Grandet fell ill. Months passed, and townspeople speculated about Eugenie's treatment while she remained devoted to Charles. Madame Grandet sought help from notary Cruchot, who warned Grandet that Eugenie would inherit her estate if her mother died. This prompted Grandet to reconcile with Eugenie, but he later attempted to seize gold from her, leading to a confrontation where she threatened self-harm.

After her mother’s death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her home, but Grandet pressured her to renounce her inheritance. Years later, after Grandet's death, Eugenie learned of her fortune but remained consumed by thoughts of Charles, who had distanced himself. When she learned of his engagement, she felt betrayed and ultimately severed ties with him, choosing to live modestly and dedicate herself to charity.
