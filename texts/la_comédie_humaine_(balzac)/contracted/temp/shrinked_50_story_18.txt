I have always wanted to tell a simple true story that would terrify two young lovers and drive them to seek refuge in each other’s hearts, like children clinging together at the sight of a snake. Saying so may seem vain.

I played a part in this almost ordinary tragedy, so if it fails to engage you, blame me and the mundane nature of reality. In 1819 I was traveling from Paris to Moulins and, for want of money, took an outside seat on the coach. An Englishman, seemingly better off, joined me and we quickly bonded over fresh air and the landscape. Conversation turned to women and love; oddly we agreed a birth certificate meant little—some women at thirty-five to forty were younger in spirit than girls at twenty. We celebrated “the woman of a certain age.” He confessed his mistress was thirty-eight; I loved a woman of forty. Freed by mutual admiration, we traded romantic follies: one had traveled six hundred miles for a brief meeting; the other had risked danger for secret trysts. His countess had once lighted a cigar for him; mine made chocolate and wrote daily. Both were adored by their husbands, whose simplicity lent danger to our pleasure.

At Pouilly I observed my new friend closely: well proportioned, dark-haired, blue-eyed, a pale, ill look, shapely hands—a Vicomte with a respectable income. About a league out of Pouilly the coach overturned. He jumped, miscalculated, and was crushed beneath the vehicle. We carried him to a peasant’s cottage where, in agony, he entrusted me with a sacred task: deliver the news of his death to his mistress so she would not learn it from a newspaper. He asked me to retrieve a key worn round his neck; I found it embedded in his flesh. He gave directions to La Charite-sur-Loire for the love letters, then died.

At La Charite I took the sealed letters; an old servant fainted. The Countess’s chateau lay eight leagues beyond Moulins and I had little money, yet youthful resolve made me walk fast, eager to reach her before anyone else. Nearing the Chateau de Montpersan, open doors, barking dogs and a servant-maid unsettled me. A playful little girl then led me, calling for her mother, through winding park paths.

I paused behind a shrub on the avenue, adjusting my shabby clothes so no one would mistake me for a local taxman. I spotted Juliette and her husband among the flowers; at her child's remark her pace quickened. Surprised, she regarded me coolly, disappointment visible. My prepared phrases failed me and her husband approached. I asked if they were M. le Comte and Mme. la Comtesse de Montpersan to size them up. He was a provincial nobleman: large, self-important, badly mannered. The Countess was the opposite: petite, graceful, in white muslin with rose accents, youthful and lively — the very Romantic ideal.

I chose diplomacy, took the Count aside with an air of mystery, and requested a private word. He obliged, confident his wife would learn his secrets, and left her to wander. I told him of my traveling companion’s death and of his sincere attachment to Juliette. Concerned for his wife, the Count urged caution; I said I could not approach her without first informing him and stressed the sacredness of the message. Flattered, he allowed me to proceed.

As we neared the house Juliette glanced at us with curiosity and disdain — likely at my appearance and our unexpected presence. I recalled her beauty in the sunlit alley and sighed. “Alas, madame, I have made a difficult journey solely for you,” I began; her face turned pale. She asked if he was ill; I said he was but urged her to control herself and revealed he had entrusted me with secrets about her. Pressing for details, she demanded whether he still loved her. I could only nod. Distress overwhelmed her; she cried out and leaned against a tree.

“Your husband is coming!” I warned.

“Do I have a husband?” she fled.

The Count called us to dinner; the Countess was absent. A maid soon reported her missing. Alarmed, the canon of Saint-Denis, the maid and I searched the garden and park. At last we heard a muffled cry from a granary and found Juliette buried in hay, sobbing. The maid and I helped her to her room; I told the servants she had a headache and should not be disturbed. Returning to the dining room, I was surprised the Count still ate: a serious medical condition had left him ravenous, which explained his apparent indifference. The evening ended in quiet; we retired early.

Later that night I was awakened by curtain rings. The Countess sat at my bed, pale and drawn. “Is it true?” she asked, trembling. “I don’t know how I can live after this blow, but I want to know everything.” I related the accident briefly and handed her the letters the dying man had entrusted to me. She lamented having burned all his other letters but produced a lock of his hair as a token. I returned it; she wept on my hands, kissed the lock, and fled, crying, “Ah! You love! May you always be happy.”

The next morning felt unreal. I searched under my pillow for the letters but found nothing. I spent the day with Juliette, seeing her noble spirit and selflessness. That evening the Comte de Montpersan accompanied me to Moulins and asked me to carry a rouleau of twenty-five louis d’or to a Paris contact. I agreed; the money covered my return. On arrival I appreciated how delicately Juliette had arranged the loan — proof of her deep, thoughtful love. What joy to share this adventure with a woman who cherished me: “Oh, my dear, not you! You must not die!”
