Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that let historians reconstruct Paris. Dilapidated walls bore timber-and-plaster patterns. A triangular roof, warped by weather, jutted over the street to shelter the entrance and dormer window. The upper story used overlapping planks so as not to overburden the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning, a young man in a cloak stood beneath a shop awning across from the house, studying it with the zeal of an antiquary. The relic of sixteenth-century civic life displayed many peculiarities. He ignored the first floor’s narrow, boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows, poorly made and dimmed by green-tinted glass. Occasionally he glanced down at the shop, where small details amused him.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore layers of paint and a comical depiction of a cat playing racket, which made him smile. The caricatured cat, racket in paw and an oversized ball, had grown more absurd with age. The faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs lured customers.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if he had just come from a wedding or ball; white gloves and disheveled hair hinted at recent festivities. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street held a rare stillness. His striking features, a blend of passion and calm, drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.” His pale face set off a dazzling white cravat, and his expressive eyes suggested many moods.

Suddenly, three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him. They playfully sprinkled him with shaving cream; he shook off the annoyance with indifference. Then a young girl’s face appeared at the window, her beauty like a Raphael Virgin—innocent and unembarrassed. On noticing the young man she withdrew and the window slammed shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung a cloth sign reading “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” The shop’s exterior, guarded by iron bars, concealed packages wrapped in brown linen. Despite its old-fashioned look, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked establishment among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty. He knew how to secure profits without flattery or costly gifts, though fellow traders warned against his notary, notorious for heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold on the Rue Saint-Denis, waking as if from a long slumber. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, watched a stranger who seemed overly curious about his business. His patient, shrewd face bore the marks of experience. A staunch traditionalist, he lamented the loss of old customs and enforced a strict regime for his three apprentices, who feared his constant vigilance.

As the apprentices began the day, Guillaume noted the stranger’s anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest, curious, stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine, Guillaume’s daughter, leaving hastily; he hailed a cab, which eased the apprentices’ worries about their prank on Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how he would have worked far more in his youth. Though the apprentices came from reasonably comfortable backgrounds, he demanded a rigorous work ethic, contrasting his order and economy with modern shops where young men expected early wealth. The shop itself was meticulously kept, a testament to his values.

Though the apprentices paid for their board, Madame Guillaume’s frugality kept them from joining the family at dessert. They attended Mass with the family, and the eldest apprentice, Joseph Lebas, after years of service, earned a modest salary. Guillaume regarded him as a fitting husband for his elder daughter, Virginie, even as Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

The household ran on strict traditions. Virginie and Augustine had been raised under their mother’s austere rule: Virginie resembling her mother in piety and stern demeanor; Augustine, at eighteen, delicate and charming yet constrained by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little time outside, so family gatherings and rare visitors were their pleasures.

Augustine yearned for more than this cloistered existence, often lost in thoughts of passion; her secret reading of romantic novels likely fed that longing. That yearning deepened Joseph’s affection for her, while Virginie, despite lacking suitors, found herself drawn to Joseph too. Their feelings developed quietly amid the shop’s monotony, and Monsieur Guillaume, aware of these shifting dynamics, mused about his daughters’ futures in a world where suitors might grow scarce.

From that day the old merchant, saddened by his eldest daughter's decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying his daughter would settle a sacred debt—repaying an orphan for a past kindness. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, felt the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder daughter marry first keenly. The warm-hearted, tall assistant suffered in silence.

To understand, rewind months before the story begins. One evening a young painter paused outside the darkened shop of the Cat and Racket, struck by a scene any artist would cherish. The unlit shop looked like a cave; a dining room, lit by a hanging lamp, cast a warm Dutch-like glow. The family—Augustine and a chubby maid among them—formed a modest, striking tableau. Fresh from Rome, the painter hungered for simple nature after Italian grandeur. The living sight before him stirred deep admiration for Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food. The lamp made her face seem almost angelic. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio, intent on capturing that magic. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, sometimes in disguise, to study Augustine. For eight months he devoted himself to love and art, neglecting friends and former pursuits. One morning his friend Girodet pierced his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet embraced him, recognizing the depth of feeling.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great work springs from such passion. Girodet warned against showing the pieces at the Salon, doubting the public would grasp their depth. Still, the works were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior sparked a genre shift, while the portrait became unforgettable, earning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These public events felt remote from the monastic life on Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition, rousing Augustine’s curiosity. With her cousin’s help she obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled: it was herself, and she felt terror mixed with excitement. The artist stood nearby and whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Desperate to escape the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were carried toward the celebrated painting. Overwhelmed, she cried at the sight. In a brief, silent exchange she gestured to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. That moment ignited guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos and the painter's gaze intoxicated her; new emotions surged where none had been before.

As they left, the painter glowed with joy; Augustine, caught in the whirlwind, realized she was loved. His words echoed in her mind, intensifying her turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, yet her mother’s plans to visit the Salon filled her with dread. Augustine’s heart raced as she contemplated the implications of these feelings.

That night she reflected, feeling a void in her mundane life and longing for the elegance she had always desired. The thought of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope despite her modest upbringing. Suddenly in love, her heart brimmed with dreams of a brighter future. The next day her parents returned from the Salon disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Still, news of the paintings’ disappearance and the lost shawl revealed to Augustine a sensitivity she had not known she possessed.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had captured Augustine's heart, found it hard to declare his love because of the strict household watch. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, closely observed by her mother, which made approach difficult. Theodore feared rivals among the assistants and, driven by passion, bribed a maid so they could exchange notes and arranged a discreet meeting during Mass at Saint-Leu.

Meanwhile the Cat and Racket household was absorbed in stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume overseeing inventory and accounts. The family busied themselves verifying goods, oblivious to Augustine’s inward turmoil; her absent-mindedness passed unnoticed. After weeks of labor the stock-taking ended, giving the family a rare evening of relaxation; they went to a performance of Cendrillon while the apprentices took the night out.

The next morning Monsieur Guillaume resumed his routine, reviewed the successful accounts, and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to discuss results showing significant profits. Guillaume proposed making Joseph a partner in recognition of his work. Overwhelmed, Joseph tried to answer but confessed a love for Augustine; Guillaume mistakenly thought he meant Virginie. Once corrected, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s feelings and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame Guillaume, unaware of Augustine’s attachment to Theodore, chided her for inattention during church. After the service she ordered Augustine to her room, prompting a heated debate between the parents about their daughter’s future.

Summoned before them, Augustine openly declared her love for Theodore de Sommervieux, emphasizing his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced old suspicions about artists, but Augustine defended Theodore as well-off and of good family, which piqued her father’s interest. At that moment Madame Roguin arrived with news that Theodore had recently received honors and gained wealth, and she urged the match. Her persuasive account began to sway both parents, who had earlier resisted a union above their station.

As the day progressed the household mood shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume informed Joseph of the change. By evening the family gathered to celebrate the prospective union, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket as a symbol of shared happiness, and Guillaume, still cautious, consented to the marriage while advising financial prudence. The lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household embraced the prospect of a joyful future.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu hosted two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore radiated happiness, while Virginie, dressed simply, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume tried to have Virginie married first, but the priests focused on the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie for making a sensible match, while others envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance would lead to ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to marriage settlements. After a splendid ball and supper, Monsieur and Madame Guillaume returned home, while Monsieur and Madame Lebas returned to their old residence. The artist, filled with joy, carried Augustine to their beautifully adorned apartment.

The passion between Theodore and Augustine made their first year together blissful and unclouded. Theodore showered her with affection, and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him. She remained blissfully unaware of the world around her, speaking in the language of love, though she occasionally revealed her naivety. As the year progressed, Theodore felt the need to return to his work, especially with a child on the way. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano's gatherings, seeking the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed the social scene but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared to Theodore's artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was genuine but superficial, and a growing disconnect emerged. Theodore, once captivated by her, began to feel the strain of their differing intellectual realms. He hoped the artistic environment would inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt to that lifestyle. Her discomfort showed during gatherings, where her innocence clashed with the more liberated attitudes of artists.

Over time Theodore’s affection waned, and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite her efforts to fulfill her duties as a wife, she sensed a growing distance. Three years into their marriage, Augustine, once vibrant, became consumed by sorrow. She tried to adhere to responsibilities and to rekindle Theodore’s love, but her efforts were in vain. The artist's enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano only deepened her despair, as she feared being replaced by a more experienced woman.

Augustine’s heartache forced her to confront her shortcomings and the barriers between them. She resolved to educate herself, believing that learning poetry and art would bridge the gap. But her attempts were met with frustration: she could not develop the natural wit and charm Theodore admired, and her earnestness often backfired, leaving her feeling increasingly inadequate in his presence.

In desperation, Augustine sought solace with her family, returning to the home of her childhood. She found her sister Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a stability that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Despite their mundane happiness, Augustine felt a pang of envy as she shared her struggles. Joseph offered practical, commerce-minded advice, but it only left her more conflicted.

Ultimately Augustine returned home, burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she once shared with Theodore, trying every remedy in hopes of healing their fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter Augustine warmly; her visit brightened their monotonous lives. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures, notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and traded memories with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, now on the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, often tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent experiences, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed recent achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about life with Theodore, Augustine's husband, a painter. Augustine mentioned his late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother’s disapproval of his lifestyle and profession. The couple’s incredulity grew as Augustine defended Theodore, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine hinted at divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume was ready to support her. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation, realizing her pain could scarcely be understood by them. She felt isolated, convinced that only those who had shared similar experiences could truly empathize.

Resolved to win back Theodore’s affection by different means, Augustine decided to consult the Duchess de Carigliano—not to reclaim his heart outright but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt both envy and admiration for its elegance. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by the woman’s beauty and charm but also sensed rivalry.

The Duchess responded with a mix of sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the importance of a composed facade and control when dealing with men of genius. She advised cultivating mystery and not revealing the full measure of one’s affections, framing such guardedness as strategy rather than mere manipulation. Initially disheartened, Augustine absorbed the Duchess’s lessons, recognizing that her love for Theodore might require a different approach.

The Duchess gave Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Filled with hope, Augustine resolved to embody the image in the painting. When Theodore returned, she presented the portrait; his reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a tumultuous confrontation.

The quarrel escalated, exposing the depths of Theodore’s passion and insecurity and culminating in a violent outburst. The aftermath left Augustine shattered; her dreams of mending their marriage lay in ruins. The next morning, her mother found her consumed by despair amid the remnants of the confrontation.

Augustine’s tragic fate was sealed as she succumbed to the pressures of her turbulent life, dying young. Her story stands as a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects that tragedy, prompting those who knew her to ponder the challenges of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
