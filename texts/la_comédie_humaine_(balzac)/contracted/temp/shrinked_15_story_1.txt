Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis, near the Rue du Petit-Lion, stood a charming old house that let historians reconstruct old Paris. Dilapidated walls bore hieroglyphic patterns of timbers and plaster cracks. The triangular roof, warped by weather, jutted over the street, sheltering the entrance and dormer. The upper story used overlapping planks to avoid overburdening the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man in a cloak stood beneath a shop awning across from the house, studying it with the zeal of an antiquary. The relic of sixteenth-century civic life displayed many peculiarities. He ignored the first floor’s narrow, boarded windows and focused on the third floor’s modest sash windows, poorly made and dimmed by green-tinted glass, occasionally glancing down at the shop where small details amused him.

A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore layers of paint and a comical depiction of a cat playing racket, which amused the young man. The caricatured cat, racket in paw with an oversized ball, had grown more absurd with age. A faded sign above it read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel,” a remnant of a whimsical past when such signs lured customers.

The young man, in a stylish cloak and white silk stockings, looked as if he had just come from a wedding or ball; white gloves and dishevelled hair hinted at recent festivities. Despite market gardeners hurrying by, the street held a rare stillness. His striking features — a blend of passion and calm — drew the attention of the shopkeepers at the “Cat and Racket.” His pale face set off a dazzling white cravat, and his expressive eyes suggested many moods.

Suddenly three apprentices appeared at the dormer window, laughing and teasing him; they playfully sprinkled him with shaving cream and he shook it off with indifference. Then a young girl’s face appeared at the window, her beauty like a Raphael Virgin — innocent and unembarrassed. On noticing the young man she withdrew and the window slammed shut, leaving him disappointed.

Meanwhile the shutters of the “Cat and Racket” opened and a manservant hung a cloth sign repeating “Guillaume, successor to Chevrel.” Behind iron bars the shop’s exterior concealed packages wrapped in brown linen. Though old-fashioned in appearance, Monsieur Guillaume was known for having the best-stocked establishment among Parisian cloth merchants and a reputation for honesty. He knew how to secure profits without flattery or costly gifts, yet fellow traders often warned against dealing with his notary, notorious for heavy discounts.

The old merchant stood at his shop threshold, watching the Rue Saint-Denis as if waking from slumber. Monsieur Guillaume, in traditional attire, scrutinized a stranger overly curious about his business. His patient, shrewd face reflected years of experience. A staunch traditionalist, he lamented lost customs and kept a strict regime for his three apprentices, who feared his vigilance.

As the apprentices began the day, Guillaume noticed the stranger's anxious glances and suspected ulterior motives. The eldest stepped outside and thought he saw Mademoiselle Augustine leaving hastily; he hailed a cab, easing the apprentices' worries about their prank on Augustine.

Guillaume reprimanded them for idleness, recalling how he would have worked far harder in his youth. Though the apprentices came from reasonably comfortable backgrounds, he demanded rigorous industry, contrasting his order and economy with modern shops where apprentices expected early wealth. The shop itself was meticulously kept, a testament to his values.

Although the apprentices paid for their board, they were not allowed to join the family at dessert—an indication of Madame Guillaume's frugality. They attended Mass with the family, and the eldest, Joseph Lebas, earned a modest salary after years of service. Guillaume regarded him as a fitting husband for his elder daughter, Virginie, though Joseph secretly loved the younger daughter, Augustine.

The household was governed by strict traditions. Virginie and Augustine had been raised under their mother's austere rule. Virginie resembled her mother in piety and sternness; Augustine, eighteen, was delicate and charming but often overshadowed by maternal expectations. Their lives centered on domestic duties with little time outside, so they took pleasure in family gatherings and rare visitors.

Madame Guillaume's rigid schedule dictated their days: they dressed early and followed routine. Augustine, however, yearned for more than their cloistered existence, often lost in thoughts of a more passionate life; secret readings of romantic novels fed her longing. That longing deepened Joseph's affection for Augustine while Virginie found herself drawn to him. Their feelings developed quietly amid the shop's monotony, and Monsieur Guillaume, aware of shifting dynamics, mused about his daughters' futures in a world where suitors might be scarce.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, remembered marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas and Virginie. Marrying his daughter would settle a sacred debt—repaying an orphan for a past kindness. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, noted the fifteen-year age gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s strict rule that the elder daughter marry first. The lovesick assistant, warm-hearted and tall, suffered in silence.

To understand, rewind months before. One evening a young man paused outside the darkened Cat and Racket shop, captivated by a scene any painter would prize. The unlit shop resembled a cave; a dining room, lit by a hanging lamp, cast a warm, Dutch-like glow. The family—Augustine and a chubby maid among them—made a modest, striking tableau. The painter, just returned from Rome, hungered for simple nature after Italian grandeur. His heart, filled with Italian passion, sought the meditative maiden he had only seen in paintings. The living scene stirred deep admiration: Augustine, pensive and scarcely touched by food, looked almost angelic in the lamp’s light. Overwhelmed, he left unable to eat or sleep.

The next day he retreated to his studio to capture that light. He visited the Cat and Racket repeatedly, sometimes in disguise, studying Augustine closely. For eight months he devoted himself to love and art, neglecting friends and former pursuits. One morning his friend Girodet pierced his isolation to ask about his Salon submission. The artist revealed two masterpieces; Girodet embraced him, recognizing the depth of his friend’s feeling.

“You are in love?” Girodet asked; the artist nodded. They agreed great work springs from such passion. Girodet warned against showing the pieces at the Salon; he doubted the public would grasp their depth. Still, the works were exhibited and caused a sensation. The Interior sparked a genre shift, while the portrait—Augustine—became unforgettable, earning acclaim and offers the artist refused.

These events, though public, felt remote from the monastic life on Rue Saint-Denis. During a visit to Madame Guillaume, the notary’s wife mentioned the exhibition, arousing Augustine’s curiosity. With her cousin’s help she obtained permission to visit the Louvre. Seeing the portrait, she trembled: it was herself; terror mixed with excitement. The artist stood nearby and whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” leaving her stunned.

Desperate to escape the crowd, Augustine urged her cousin to leave, but they were carried toward the celebrated painting. Overcome, she wept. In a brief silent exchange she gestured to the artist; he acknowledged her with a nod. That moment ignited both guilt and exhilaration. The Salon’s chaos and the painter’s gaze intoxicated her; new emotions surged where none had been before.

As they left, the painter glowed with joy; Augustine, caught in the whirlwind, realized she was loved. His words echoed in her mind, intensifying her turmoil. She feigned a headache to avoid discussing the exhibition, yet her mother’s plans to visit the Salon filled her with dread. That night she reflected, feeling a void in her mundane life and longing for the elegance she had always desired. The thought of being the wife of a genius filled her with hope despite her modest upbringing. The next day her parents returned from the Salon disappointed: the painter had removed the two pictures, and Madame Guillaume had lost her shawl. Still, news of the paintings’ disappearance and the lost shawl revealed to Augustine a sensitivity she had not known she possessed.

On the morning after a ball, Theodore de Sommervieux, who had won Augustine’s heart, found it hard to declare his love because the household kept her under close watch. Seated in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, Augustine was constantly observed by her mother, which stifled Theodore’s approach. Fearing a rival among the assistants, he bribed a maid to facilitate secret exchanges and arranged to meet Augustine discreetly during Mass at Saint-Leu.

Meanwhile the Cat and Racket household was absorbed in stock-taking, with Monsieur Guillaume supervising inventory and accounts while the family verified goods, oblivious to Augustine’s turmoil. After weeks of labor the inventory ended, and the family enjoyed a rare evening out to see Cendrillon while the apprentices celebrated elsewhere.

The next morning, Monsieur Guillaume resumed business as usual and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas to review the profitable results. Guillaume proposed making Joseph a partner to reward his work. Overwhelmed, Joseph confessed his love for Augustine; Guillaume initially misunderstood, thinking Joseph meant Virginie. Once corrected, Guillaume agreed to consider Joseph’s attachment and suggested he accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose as Madame Guillaume and Virginie exchanged glances at a nervous Joseph. Madame, unaware of Augustine’s romance, reproached her for inattentiveness in church and sent her to her room, prompting a heated discussion between the parents about their daughter’s future.

Summoned before them, Augustine declared her love for Theodore and emphasized his respectable background. Monsieur Guillaume voiced the usual doubts about artists, but Augustine defended Theodore as well-off and from a good family, which piqued her father’s interest. Madame Roguin then arrived with news of Theodore’s recent honors and fortune and advocated the match, beginning to sway the parents who had been resistant to their daughter marrying above her station.

As the day progressed the mood shifted from conflict to acceptance. Monsieur Guillaume informed Joseph of the developments, and by evening the family gathered to celebrate the prospective union. Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket as a token of shared happiness. Guillaume, still cautious, consented to the marriage while stressing financial prudence and advising Augustine to consult him on money matters. In the end the lovers found solace in mutual affection, and the household, once tense, embraced a hopeful future together.

A few months after a memorable Sunday, the high altar of Saint-Leu saw two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore were radiant; Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had tried to have Virginie married before Augustine, but the priests favored the more elegant bride. Neighbors commended Virginie for a sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank. An old fan-maker warned that such extravagance could bring ruin, and Monsieur Guillaume felt justified in his cautious approach to settlements. After the ball and supper, the Guillaumes returned home, the Lebas family to their old residence, and the joyful artist carried Augustine to their well-adorned apartment.

Their first year together was blissful. Theodore adored Augustine, who, lost in love, believed her devotion would always charm him; she spoke the language of love and revealed a naive simplicity. But as a child approached, Theodore had to return to work. He reconnected with friends and began frequenting the Duchesse de Carigliano’s gatherings, seeking the social validation of being seen with a beautiful wife.

Augustine enjoyed society but soon realized her lack of sophistication compared to Theodore’s artistic circle. Her admiration for his work was sincere yet superficial, and a growing disconnect emerged. Theodore, once captivated, felt the strain of their different intellectual worlds. He retreated to his studio, hoping the environment would inspire Augustine, but she struggled to adapt to the lifestyle. At gatherings her innocence clashed with the freer attitudes of artists, and her discomfort showed.

Over time Theodore’s affection cooled, and Augustine felt the weight of his indifference. Despite trying to meet her duties as a wife and rekindle his interest, she sensed a widening distance. Three years into their marriage, vibrant Augustine was consumed by sorrow. The artist’s enthusiasm for the Duchesse de Carigliano and for more experienced women deepened her despair; she feared being replaced.

Her heartache prompted Augustine to examine her shortcomings and the barriers between them. She resolved to educate herself, believing that understanding poetry and art would bridge the gap. Yet her attempts to change only brought frustration: she could not manufacture the natural wit and charm Theodore admired, and her earnest efforts often backfired, making her feel inadequate in his presence.

In desperation, Augustine sought refuge with her family, returning to her childhood home. There she found Virginie and brother-in-law Joseph Lebas managing their drapery business with a practical stability that contrasted sharply with her turmoil. Joseph offered straightforward, businesslike counsel, but it left her more conflicted than comforted.

Ultimately Augustine returned home burdened by uncertainty and longing for the love she once shared with Theodore, like a desperate patient trying every remedy to heal a fractured marriage.

The elderly couple welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit brightened their monotonous lives. For four years they had drifted, dwelling on past business successes and failures, notably the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and traded memories with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, now on the Army's clothing consulting board thanks to her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, often tasteless decor. Their nostalgia and stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her own turbulent experiences, leaving her both proud and appalled at their vacuous existence.

Despite her inner turmoil, Augustine displayed her recent achievements and aired marital grievances, which her parents eagerly absorbed. Madame Guillaume was especially curious about life with Theodore, Augustine's husband, a painter. Augustine mentioned his late nights and unconventional habits, prompting her mother’s disapproval of his lifestyle and profession. The couple’s incredulity grew as Augustine defended Theodore, sparking a heated debate about genius and marital responsibility.

When Augustine hinted at divorce, her parents were shocked into action; Monsieur Guillaume was ready to support her. Overwhelmed by their concern and unable to convey the depth of her suffering, Augustine rejected separation, realizing her pain could scarcely be understood by them. She felt isolated, convinced that only those who had shared similar experiences could truly empathize.

Resolved to win back Theodore’s affection by other means, Augustine decided to consult the Duchess de Carigliano—not to reclaim his heart outright but to learn how to captivate him. Approaching the Duchess’s opulent home, she felt both envy and admiration for its elegance. Meeting the Duchess, Augustine was struck by the woman’s beauty and charm but also sensed rivalry.

The Duchess responded with a mix of sympathy and shrewd counsel, stressing the importance of a composed facade and control when dealing with men of genius. She advised cultivating mystery and not revealing the full measure of one’s affections, framing such guardedness as strategy rather than mere manipulation. Initially disheartened, Augustine absorbed the Duchess’s lessons, recognizing that her love for Theodore might require a different approach.

The Duchess gave Augustine a portrait of herself painted by Theodore, believing it could rekindle his interest. Filled with hope, Augustine resolved to embody the image in the painting. When Theodore returned, she presented the portrait; his reaction was fury and jealousy, igniting a tumultuous confrontation.

The quarrel escalated, exposing the depths of Theodore’s passion and insecurity and culminating in a violent outburst. The aftermath left Augustine shattered; her dreams of mending their marriage lay in ruins. The next morning, her mother found her consumed by despair amid the remnants of the confrontation.

Augustine’s tragic fate was sealed as she succumbed to the pressures of her turbulent life, dying young. Her story stands as a poignant reminder of the struggles women face pursuing love and fulfillment, especially when entangled with the complexities of artistic genius. The inscription on her grave reflects that tragedy, prompting those who knew her to ponder the challenges of balancing passion and stability in relationships.
