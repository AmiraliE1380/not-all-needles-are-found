**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work expresses my gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. I often reflect on our cherished moments in Milan and hope this book serves as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse are distressed. Raised by strict parents, they entered loveless marriages without understanding love. Madame du Tillet feels trapped in her marriage to banker Ferdinand du Tillet, while Madame de Vandenesse enjoys a more affectionate relationship with Comte Felix but still feels unfulfilled. Their upbringing left them innocent yet naive, leading to a yearning for deeper fulfillment.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her unhappiness to her sister, expressing the emptiness of her luxurious life and feeling trapped by societal expectations. Despite her own struggles, Madame du Tillet tries to comfort her, but both lament the lack of genuine love in their marriages.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix marries Madame de Vandenesse, hoping to provide her with a loving home. Initially happy, she soon feels monotony and becomes aware of her desires, complicating her feelings for her husband.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, captures Madame de Vandenesse's attention. Their interactions are filled with tension, but Nathan struggles with his ambitions and his relationship with Florine, an actress who has supported him.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine relies on Nathan for success while feeling possessive. Their relationship strains as Nathan's ambitions grow, leading to jealousy, especially as he becomes more involved with Madame de Vandenesse.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's relationship deepens, filled with passion. The countess is captivated by Nathan's artistic spirit, while he is drawn to her innocence. Their love blossoms in secret, complicated by societal pressures.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's debt leads him to contemplate suicide, believing it his only escape. Unaware of his despair, the countess nurtures her feelings for him. When she learns of his plight, she rushes to save him, demonstrating her love.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess seeks financial assistance for Nathan, facing societal judgment. Despite her efforts, Nathan's situation remains precarious, testing her love as she grapples with the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
The count, aware of the bond between his wife and Nathan, seeks to protect his family's honor while understanding their connection. The countess confronts her feelings for Nathan, navigating her responsibilities and societal expectations, leading to difficult choices.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, illustrating the interconnectedness of his literary universe. Each character's journey reflects the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
