A START IN LIFE

CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS

Railroads will soon change travel to and from Paris, making certain small enterprises seem antiquarian. The picturesque “coucous” that once ran from the Place de la Concorde and the Cours-la-Reine, common a century earlier and still numerous in 1830, were scarce by 1842 except as suburban curiosities. In 1820 the “Environs of Paris” did not all enjoy regular stage-coach service.

The Touchards had established a successful messagerie from the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis, but they faced persistent competition from coucous on shorter routes. Parisian taste for country trips sustained local operators—often innkeepers along the road—who won custom by familiarity, lower charges, and accommodating practices that the Touchards’ formal system could not match. These rivals sometimes ignored regulations, broke passenger-number laws, and kept the affection of the masses; travelers preferred a familiar coachman and would wait for an old coach even if it looked unpromising.

One much-contested line was Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise—a lucrative route that in 1822 had three competing enterprises. Touchard tried to monopolize it by lowering fares and improving coaches, but short-run lines serving towns and villages like Saint-Denis and Pierrefitte remained productive. On the main road toward England, a turning at a place called The Cave leads to the delightful Oise valley and the little town of Isle-Adam, once the seat of the extinct family of Isle-Adam and formerly a Bourbon-Conti residence. Isle-Adam lies near Nogent and Parmain, villages noted for quarries whose stone built many fine Paris structures. Though the region had fine sites and châteaux, it was reached in 1822 by only two coaches that generally worked in harmony.

A simple reason explains this exception: from the Cave the paved road made by the Princes of Conti turns six miles off the main line to Isle-Adam and ends there, a detour a speculator would not take. In 1822 the Conti highway was the only road to Isle-Adam, so Pierrotin and his colleague reigned on the route, beloved by the countryfolk and often preferred even by those on the main road as far as the Cave. The two proprietors maintained a friendly rivalry; when one left Isle-Adam the other returned from Paris, and in the capital they economized by sharing the same yard, hotel, stable, coach-house, office, and clerk. Their hotel, at the corner of the rue d’Enghien, was the Lion d’Argent, long accustomed to lodging coachmen and vehicles.

Although advertised as departing at fixed hours, Pierrotin and his rival were indulgent, a practice that won peasant gratitude and sometimes embarrassed strangers. The afternoon trip listed for four would often not go until half-past; the morning at eight seldom left before nine. In summer the rules relaxed for country customers; this elasticity sometimes let Pierrotin take two fares for one place if a countryman arrived early and sat in a seat booked by a late traveler. He and his colleague excused such practices by citing hard times, winter losses, the need for new coaches, or the imperative of keeping to printed tariffs—displays rarely shown unless a traveler demanded them.

Pierrotin, about forty and a father, had been discharged from the cavalry at the great demobilization of 1815 and succeeded his father, who had long driven a coucou between Paris and Isle-Adam. He married an inn-keeper’s daughter and expanded the business into a regular service noted for intelligence and a certain military precision. Active and decisive, Pierrotin’s vivacious face lent sly shrewdness to his weather-stained features. He had the facility of speech learned by seeing life; his rough voice could soften before bourgeois passengers. His work clothing—stout nailed boots from Isle-Adam, bottle-green velveteen trousers and waistcoat, and a blue blouse embroidered on collar, shoulder-straps, and cuffs—was practical; a visor cap shaded his head. His military schooling left him respect for social superiority and obedience to the upper classes; he mingled little with the lesser bourgeoisie and always respected women. By “trundling the world,” he came to regard his human passengers as parcels requiring less attention than cargo, the essential object of a coaching business.

Alert to postwar changes, Pierrotin refused to be outdone by rivals. Since the previous summer he had talked of a large coach ordered from Farry, Breilmann, and Company—renowned makers who had adopted square English springs—an addition prompted by an influx of travelers. Pierrotin’s establishment consisted of two vehicles. One, used in winter and the only one he reported to the tax-gatherer, was the coucou inherited from his father. Its rounded body provided two hard metallic seats that might, by stretching the law, hold six—the seats divided by a removable wooden bar Pierrotin called “a back,” a device passengers hated for its awkwardness. Though each seat was legally for three, it was not uncommon to see eight persons jammed onto the two seats. Pierrotin maintained travelers were more comfortable in a compact mass; when fewer occupied a seat they bumped each other and risked hitting their hats against the roof on jolting roads.

In front was a wooden bench for the driver, where three travelers—called “rabbits”—could perch; Pierrotin sometimes placed four rabbits on the bench and sat on a box beneath the coach-body, which he kept full of straw and parcels. The coucou was painted yellow with a barber’s-blue band and bore in silver “Isle-Adam, Paris” and “Line to Isle-Adam” across the back. On great occasions it could carry three more passengers in a square compartment under an awning—allowed only for regulars and then avoided near gendarmerie posts where overloads might be reported. Thus on busy nights Pierrotin’s coucou could “trundle” fifteen travelers, pairing Rougeot, his stout old horse, with Bichette, a small mare of which he was fond.

The other vehicle was a comical four-wheel coach holding seventeen though legally allowed fourteen. It rattled so that Isle-Adam folk would say, “Here comes Pierrotin!” when he had barely emerged from the forest. It had an “interior” for six, a front cabriolet called the “coupe” closed with awkward sashes, and an “imperial” hood holding six under leather curtains. Pierrotin sat on a nearly hidden seat below the coupe. He paid tax only on the coucou, rated for six, taking out special permits when he ran the four-wheeler—common practice then, for taxes on vehicles had been timidly enforced at first and proprietors often cheated.

Often in summer the two coaches ran together, carrying thirty-two passengers though Pierrotin paid tax on six. On lucky days the convoy left Faubourg Saint-Denis at half-past four and reached Isle-Adam by ten at night, proudly proclaiming “We went at a fine pace!” To do the twenty-seven miles in five hours he sometimes omitted stops at smaller villages like Saint-Brice, Moisselles, and La Cave.

The Lion d’Argent’s deep plot extends back through a long courtyard to the stables; its passage under the second floor could shelter several coaches. In 1822 the offices of all lines starting there were kept by the innkeeper’s wife, who received fares, booked passengers, and stowed packages in her vast kitchen—a patriarchal system travelers accepted. If early they waited beneath the kitchen chimney, in the passage, or in the nearby Cafe de l’Echiquier.

On an early autumn Saturday in 1822 Pierrotin stood with hands in his blouse beneath the Lion d’Argent porte-cochere, watching the courtyard and through it the stables. Daumartin’s diligence had just gone, and it was past eight. Pierrotin fretted: only four passengers booked on a fine Saturday. He told his hostler to harness anyway, lamenting no parcels and the hard fate of coachmen. The hostler noted there was scarcely room left but the cabriolet; Pierrotin reminded him of the new coach arriving the next day and insisted he needed at least eighteen passengers for it. He boasted it would make Touchard envious—painted red and gold and drawing three horses; he had bought a mate for Rougeot and planned a showy team.

Yet Pierrotin’s buoyant talk disguised anxiety. The coach-builders required a two-thousand-franc preliminary payment; Pierrotin had exhausted resources and credit—his wife, father-in-law, and friends had bled for him. He had paid two thousand already and owed another thousand plus five hundred for Rougeot’s mate and three hundred for new harnesses on three months’ credit. Driven by despair and vanity, he had publicly announced the coach would start the next day, hoping the builders would accept fifteen hundred instead of the two thousand five hundred still due. He considered asking Monsieur Moreau, steward at Presles, to take his note for six months.

At this moment a footman in livery, carrying a leather portmanteau and returning from the Touchard establishment where he had gone too late for places, asked whether Pierrotin would wait a quarter-hour to take his master. Pierrotin agreed and received the trunk—well buckled, with a brass plate bearing a coat of arms. He wrapped it in hay and put it in the boot, then invited the valet into the Cafe de l’Echiquier for two absinthes. The valet explained his master only came to the district once a year and preferred to travel in his own carriage; this time he was going to Presles to see Monsieur Moreau. Pierrotin recognized the name: Monsieur le Comte de Serizy.

“But listen!” said the footman. “There’s a special order. If you have any of the country neighbors in your coach you are not to call him Monsieur le comte; he wants to travel ‘en cognito,’ and will pay a handsome pourboire if not recognized.”

Pierrotin wondered whether the secret journey related to a recent affair for which Pere Leger, a farmer, had come to Paris. The valet mentioned a countermanded order: last night the Daumont carriage had been ordered to leave at seven for Presles, but Monsieur le Comte had changed his mind at the last minute; Augustin, his valet de chambre, attributed the switch to the visit of a lady who called the previous night and again that morning—perhaps from the country. Pierrotin speculated whether the lady had said anything against Monsieur Moreau, whom Pierrotin greatly admired as an honest and capable steward. He had done errands for the Moreaus, received fees, and even fetched and returned the children; every time he carried someone to the chateau he was allowed to drive up to the gate—a benefit he valued.

The valet, however, was more cynical. They said Monsieur Moreau had little when Monsieur le Comte made him steward of Presles; the valet hinted that masters were annoying and advised Pierrotin, “Every man for himself. Do what Monsieur le Comte asks, and you will be paid well.” He stretched his arm to indicate a generous tip.

This prudent counsel cooled Pierrotin’s ardor for borrowing from Moreau. The footman’s presence and his mention of the Comte and his steward introduced the social actors central to the drama about to unfold in Pierrotin’s vehicle. A rapid glance at the lives of the Comte de Serizy and his steward, Moreau, is necessary to understand what is to follow.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the celebrated président Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family arms are party per pale or and sable, with an orle and two lozenges counterchanged; the motto, “i, semper melius eris,” and the distaff supporters reflect the modest pride of bourgeois families elevated when magistrates held office—the pun on “eris,” with the initial “i” and final “s” of “melius,” giving Serisy, the family’s estate-name.

The present count’s father had been a parlement president before the Revolution. The count himself, at twenty-two became councillor of State at the Grand Council in 1787 and wrote admirable memoranda on delicate diplomatic matters. He did not emigrate during the Revolution, spending the years on his Serizy estate near Arpajon, where his father’s standing protected him. After tending his dying father in 1794, he was elected to the Council of Five Hundred and accepted legislative duties to divert his grief. After 18th Brumaire, like many old parliamentary families, Monsieur de Serizy accepted Napoleon’s overtures: appointed to the Council of State, he reformed a disorganized department and became useful to the regime.

He was soon called to a ministry, made count and senator by the Emperor, and served as proconsul to two kingdoms. In 1806, at forty, he married the sister of the former Marquis de Ronquerolles, a widow at twenty of General Gaubert, who had left her property. The marriage matched his rank, increased his fortune, and linked him to the former Marquis de Rouvre, later a count and chamberlain under the Emperor.

Weary and ill in 1814, the Comte de Serizy resigned, left his department, and came to Paris. Napoleon at first suspected defection but accepted the count’s illness as cause. Though never disgraced, Serizy felt wronged by Napoleon. When the Bourbons returned, Louis XVIII, whom the count regarded as his legitimate sovereign, trusted him with private affairs and made him a cabinet minister. On March 20 the count did not go to Ghent; he told Napoleon he remained faithful to the Bourbons, would not accept a peerage during the Hundred Days, and stayed at his estate.

After Napoleon’s second fall he resumed office as privy councillor, became vice-president of the Council of State, and acted as liquidator of claims and indemnities demanded by foreign powers. Unambitious personally but influential, little of importance occurred without his counsel; he avoided court and was seldom seen in salons. A life of incessant work had formed his habit: rising at four to work until mid-day, attending duties in the afternoon, and sleeping by nine. He held the grand cross of the Legion of Honor and multiple foreign orders. No man was less conspicuous or more useful. Public honors mattered little to him; but such devotion rarely lacks a serious motive. His conduct had a cruel cause.

He had loved his wife before marrying her; the passion endured through the secret unhappiness of wedding a self-possessed widow who used her liberty, while he treated her with indulgent deference. His constant toil shielded him from a pain he silenced with a diplomat’s care for secrets. He knew jealousy would seem ridiculous in a society that would not believe an old statesman’s conjugal passion. How his wife fascinated him, why he suffered without resistance, why he did not fight, and why he still hoped would require a long history; here they are only hinted at. One cruel fact was that his labor had caused a skin malady which repelled his wife. Kind and considerate, he permitted her to be mistress of herself and their home. She received all Paris, spent time in the country, and returned as though still a widow. He managed her fortune and supplied her luxury like a steward. The countess admired his mind and could please him by approving him; an hour’s talk in his study made him pliable. Like old seigneurs, he protected her so loyally that any disrespect toward her would have seemed an unforgivable injury. The world admired him; Madame de Serizy benefited by that admiration. She was ungrateful, yet mingled charm with ingratitude and sometimes soothed his wounds.

This explains the sudden incognito journey of a minister of State.

A rich farmer of Beaumont-sur-Oise named Leger leased and cultivated a farm whose fields projected into and troubled the Comte de Serizy’s Presles estate. The farm belonged to a burgher, Margueron. The lease made to Leger in 1799, before the agricultural boom, was about to expire, and Margueron refused Leger’s offers to renew. Monsieur de Serizy wanted the farm to stop the annoyance of enclosed fields and heard Margueron’s chief ambition was to have his only son, then a mere tax-gatherer, appointed special collector of finances at Beaumont. Farmer Leger, knowing he could sell parcels to the count at a high price, planned to buy and resell to profit.

Two days before our narrative, anxious to settle the matter, Monsieur de Serizy summoned his notary Alexandre Crottat and his lawyer Derville to examine the situation. Though Derville and Crottat distrusted the count’s steward—prompted by a disturbing letter from him—the count defended Moreau, who had served him faithfully for seventeen years.

“Very well,” Derville said, “then go to Presles yourself and invite Margueron to dinner. Crottat will take a deed drawn, leaving only necessary lines blank for property description and titles. Take part of the purchase money in a Bank of France check, and the appointment of the son to the collectorship. If you don’t settle this now the farm will slip through your fingers. You don’t know the trickery of peasants. Peasants against diplomat, and the diplomat succumbs.”

Crottat agreed, and, as suggested by his valet Pierrotin, the count adopted the plan. The preceding evening he had sent Moreau a line by diligence to Beaumont, telling him to invite Margueron to dinner so they might close the purchase.

Before this affair, the count had ordered the château of Presles restored and refurnished; for a year Grindot, an architect then in fashion, had made weekly visits. While concluding the purchase the count intended to examine restorations and new furniture as a surprise for his wife when he brought her to Presles; he had put some personal pride into the work. Why the count, who planned to drive to Presles in his own carriage in the evening, should set out early the next morning incognito in Pierrotin’s coucou needs explanation.

A few words on the steward Moreau are necessary.

Moreau was the son of a provincial attorney who became syndic-attorney at Versailles during the Revolution. In that post Moreau the elder almost saved the Serizys’ lives and property; he belonged to Danton’s party, was pursued by Robespierre, and was executed at Versailles. Moreau the son, inheriting his father’s doctrines and friendships, was implicated in a conspiracy against the First Consul. Monsieur de Serizy, wishing to repay debts, enabled Moreau, under sentence of death, to escape; in 1804 he sought his pardon, obtained it, offered him a government post, and finally made him private secretary.

Some time after his patron’s marriage, Moreau fell in love with the countess’s waiting-woman and married her. To avoid the annoyances of that position at court, Moreau asked to manage the Presles estate, where his wife could play lady and neither would suffer wounded pride. The count wanted a trustworthy man at Presles because his wife preferred Serizy, an estate only fifteen miles from Paris. For three or four years Moreau thus held the key of the count’s affairs; he was intelligent, had studied law in his father’s office, and the count granted his request.

“You can never advance in life,” the count told Moreau, “for you have broken your neck; but you can be happy, and I will take care that you are so.”

He gave Moreau a salary of three thousand francs, a lodge near the château, all the wood needed from estate timber, oats, hay, and straw for two horses, and a right to the gardens’ produce. A sub-prefect was not as well provided for.

For the first eight years Moreau managed the estate conscientiously; he took interest in it. The count, visiting now and then to examine property and purchases, admired Moreau’s loyalty and rewarded him liberally.

But after the birth of Moreau’s third child—a daughter—he felt secure and ceased to see his advantages as gifts. About 1816, the steward, who had until then taken only what he needed, accepted twenty-five thousand francs from a wood-merchant as inducement to lease timber cutting for twelve years. Moreau argued that he could have no pension, was father of a family, and the count owed him that sum as a gift after ten years’ management; with sixty thousand francs already saved, this would let him buy a farm worth 125,000 francs in Champagne, above Isle-Adam on the Oise. Political events prevented the count and neighbors from noticing this investment, made in Madame Moreau’s name as if she had inherited from an aunt.

Once he tasted possession, Moreau, while keeping an appearance of integrity, seized opportunities to increase his fortune clandestinely; the interests of his three children served as a poultice to his conscience. Nevertheless, by local custom he shared with the count in profits due to his management. This swelling of his fortune troubled his conscience. Alert and thoroughly understanding the count’s interests, Moreau watched for good purchases, gaining a larger percentage. Presles returned a net revenue of seventy thousand francs. The countryside said for thirty miles: “Monsieur de Serizy has a second self in Moreau.”

Prudent, Moreau invested yearly after 1817, piling up savings secretly. He often played the poor man so well that the count sent his sons to the school Henri IV. At present Moreau held 120,000 francs invested in the Consolidated thirds, paying five percent and quoted at eighty francs. These hidden 120,000 and his Champagne farm, enlarged by purchases, amounted to about 280,000 francs, giving an income of some 16,000 francs.

Such was the steward’s position when the Comte de Serizy wished to buy the Moulineaux farm—ownership indispensable to his comfort. The farm consisted of ninety-six parcels bordering Presles and often running into it, causing disputes about hedges, ditches, and tree-cutting. Any other than a cabinet minister would probably have faced lawsuits. Pere Leger wished to buy the property only to resell it to the count at a handsome advance. To secure that sum he sought an understanding with Moreau. Three days before the crucial Sunday he talked with the steward and showed that Moreau could make the count invest at two and a half percent and thus pocket forty thousand francs off Leger’s offer.

“I tell you what,” Moreau said to his wife as he went to bed, “if I make fifty thousand francs out of the Moulineaux affair—and I certainly shall, for the count will give me ten thousand as a fee—we’ll retire to Isle-Adam and live in the Pavillon de Nogent.”

This “pavillon,” built by the Prince de Conti for a mistress, was well furnished.

“That will suit me,” his wife replied. “The Dutchman there has put it in order and now returns to India; he might let us have it for thirty thousand francs.”

“We shall be close to Champagne,” said Moreau. “I hope to buy the farm and mill of Mours for a hundred thousand francs. That would give us ten thousand a year. Nogent is one of the most delightful residences in the valley; and we should still have an income of ten thousand from the Grand-Livre.”

“But why not ask for the post of juge-de-paix at Isle-Adam? That would give influence and fifteen hundred a year.”

“Well, I did think of it.”

With these plans, Moreau, hearing the count was coming to Presles and wished him to invite Margueron to dinner on Saturday, sent an express to the count’s head-valet enclosing a letter to his master, which the messenger failed to deliver before Monsieur de Serizy retired. Augustin, however, placed it on his master’s desk in the morning. In the letter Moreau begged the count not to come, but to trust him entirely; he added that Margueron was no longer willing to sell the whole at once and talked of cutting the farm into lots. It was necessary to circumvent this plan, and perhaps employ a third party to make the purchase.

Everybody has enemies. The steward and his wife had offended a retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, and his wife, who lived near Presles. From pin-prick speeches matters had advanced to dagger thrusts. Monsieur de Reybert sought vengeance; he aimed to make Moreau lose his situation and gain it himself. Reybert had spied on the steward for two years. The same conveyance that took Moreau’s letter to the count carried Madame de Reybert to Paris, whom her husband had despatched. There she so earnestly asked to see the count that although sent away at nine, she was ushered into his study the next morning at seven.

“Monsieur,” she said to the cabinet minister, “we cannot write anonymous letters, so I have come in person. I am Madame de Reybert, née de Corroy. My husband is a retired officer with a pension of six hundred francs, and we live at Presles, where your steward has offered us insult after insult, though we are persons of good station. Monsieur de Reybert, who is not intriguing, retired in 1816 after twenty years—always at a distance from the Emperor. You know how difficult it is for soldiers not under the eye of their master to obtain promotion; my husband’s integrity displeased superiors. He has watched your steward for three years, aware of his dishonesty and intending to have him dismissed. We have been frank with you. Moreau plans to get an extra hundred thousand francs out of you, to be divided between the notary, the farmer Leger, and Moreau. You have written Moreau to invite Margueron, and you are going to Presles today; but Margueron will be ill, and Leger is so certain of buying he is now in Paris with the money. If we have enlightened you, and you want an upright steward, take my husband; though noble, he will serve you as he has served the State. Your steward has made two hundred and fifty thousand francs from his place; he is not to be pitied.”

The count thanked Madame de Reybert coldly, giving the holy-water of courts, for he despised backbiting; but he remembered Derville’s doubts and felt shaken. He then saw Moreau’s letter and read it. In its assurances of devotion and blaming implied distrust, he read truth.

“Corruption has come to him with fortune—as it always does!” he said.

The count then questioned Madame de Reybert, less for information than to observe her, and wrote a short note to his notary telling him not to send his head-clerk to Presles as requested, but to come himself in time for dinner.

“Though Monsieur le comte,” Madame de Reybert concluded, “may have judged me unfavorably for acting without my husband, he ought to be convinced we obtained this information in a natural and honorable manner; the most sensitive conscience cannot take exception to it.”

So saying, Madame de Reybert, née de Corroy, stood erect. She presented a face seamed with smallpox, a flat spare figure, two light eager eyes, fair hair plastered on an anxious forehead, a small drawn bonnet of faded green taffetas lined with pink, a white gown with violet spots, and leather shoes. The count recognized the wife of some poor, half-pay captain: a puritan, earnest in virtue but mindful of comfort and eager for a good situation.

“You say your husband has a pension of six hundred francs,” he remarked.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“You were born a Corroy?”

“Yes, monsieur,—a noble family of Metz, where my husband belongs.”

“In what regiment did Monsieur de Reybert serve?”

“The 7th artillery.”

“Good,” said the count, writing the number.

He had thought of entrusting estate management to some retired officer, about whom he could obtain exact information from the minister of war.

“Madame,” he resumed, ringing for his valet, “return to Presles this afternoon with my notary, who is going down for dinner and to whom I have recommended you. Here is his address. I am going myself secretly to Presles, and will send for Monsieur de Reybert to come and speak to me.”

Thus Monsieur de Serizy’s journey by public conveyance and his valet’s injunction to conceal his name and rank had not needlessly alarmed Pierrotin. That worthy had forebodings of a danger about to swoop down upon one of his best customers.

As Pierrotin emerged from the Café de l’Échiquier after tending the valet, he saw in the gateway of the Lion d’Argent a lady and a young man he recognized as customers; the lady, neck outstretched and anxious, looked for him. She wore a dyed black-silk gown, a brown bonnet, an old cashmere shawl, raw-silk stockings, low shoes, and carried a straw bag. Once beautiful, she seemed about forty; her blue eyes, stripped of happiness’s fire, showed she had long renounced the world. Her dress and manner indicated a mother wholly devoted to home and son. The bonnet’s faded strings and worn shape betrayed its age; the shawl was fastened with a broken needle stuck into sealing-wax. She waited impatiently for Pierrotin, wishing to entrust her son—doubtless travelling for the first time—to his care.

The son completed the picture. If the mother mended gloves, the son wore an olive-green coat with sleeves too short, showing he had grown; his blue trousers, patched by his mother, revealed a brighter patch when the coat-tails parted.

“Don’t rub your gloves that way, you’ll spoil them,” she said as Pierrotin appeared. “Is this the conductor? Ah! Pierrotin, is it you?” she exclaimed, leaving her son and taking the coachman aside.

“I hope you’re well, Madame Clapart,” he replied, with a blend of respect and familiarity.

“Yes, Pierrotin, very well. Please take good care of my Oscar; he is travelling alone for the first time.”

“Oh! so he is going alone to Monsieur Moreau!” Pierrotin cried, to learn whether he was really going there.

“Yes,” said the mother.

“Then Madame Moreau is willing?” returned Pierrotin, with a sly look.

“Ah!” said the mother, “it will not be all roses for him, poor child! But his future absolutely requires that I should send him.”

This answer struck Pierrotin. He hesitated to reveal his fears for the steward to Madame Clapart, while she feared harming her son by asking for too much care. During this brief deliberation, covered by remarks on the weather, journey, and stopping places, we must explain the ties that united Madame Clapart with Pierrotin and authorized their remarks.

Often—three or four times a month—Pierrotin, on his way to Paris, would meet the steward near La Cave. As soon as the vehicle arrived, Moreau would signal a gardener, who, with Pierrotin’s help, would put on the coach one or two baskets containing fruits, vegetables, chickens, eggs, butter, and game. The steward always paid the carriage and Pierrotin’s fee, adding money for the toll at the barrière if the baskets contained dutiable goods. These parcels were never addressed. On the first occasion, the steward had given Madame Clapart’s address by word of mouth to the discreet Pierrotin, requesting he never deliver the packages to others. Pierrotin, imagining an intrigue between the steward and some pretty girl, went to number 7 rue de la Cerisaie and found Madame Clapart instead of the beauty he expected.

Drivers of public conveyances enter many homes and learn many secrets; social accident often places them in intimate proximity, but being without education and poor observers, they are not usually dangerous. Nevertheless, after months Pierrotin was puzzled to explain Moreau and Madame Clapart’s exact relation from what he saw of the household. Though lodgings were not dear in the Arsenal quarter, Madame Clapart lived on a third floor at the end of a courtyard in a house once belonging to a great family when the nobility inhabited the old Tournelles and Saint-Paul sites. Toward the late sixteenth century those grandeurs were divided into the present streets—Cerisaie, Beautreillis, des Lions, etc. Madame Clapart’s flat, panelled with ancient carvings, consisted of three rooms—dining-room, salon, and bedroom; above was the kitchen and Oscar’s bedroom. Opposite the entrance, on the square landing, was a door opening into a rough stone tower built with a well for the staircase; that back room was where Moreau slept when in Paris.

Pierrotin had seen in the first room, where he deposited the hampers, six wooden chairs with straw seats, a table, and a sideboard; the windows had discolored curtains. In the salon he noticed old Empire furniture now shabby; the bedroom matched the salon and dining-room. The coarsely painted woodwork blurred the mouldings; the floors, never waxed, had the gray tone of boarding-schools. When Pierrotin visited Monsieur and Madame Clapart at meals he noted that the china and glass betrayed the utmost poverty; yet the forks and spoons were silver.

Monsieur Clapart, in a shabby surtout and broken slippers, always wore green spectacles and, when he removed his shabby cap, showed a pointed skull with a few dirty filaments hardly to be called hair. Timorous yet tyrannical, he looked wan.

In that dreary north-facing apartment, with only a vine and a well to look on, Madame Clapart bore herself like a queen and moved like a woman unaccustomed to walking. Often, while thanking Pierrotin, she gave him glances to touch a sensitive observer and would slip a twelve-sous piece into his hand; her voice then became charming. Pierrotin had never seen Oscar because the boy was always at school when his business took him to the house.

Here is the sad story Pierrotin could not have discovered by asking the portress, who only knew the Claparts paid 250 francs a year, had no servant but a charwoman coming a few hours each morning, that Madame Clapart did some washing herself, and paid postage daily as she could not let money accumulate.

There seldom exists a criminal wholly criminal; nor a dishonest nature entirely dishonest. A man may cheat his master yet do good at times. If Moreau’s faults can be excused, it might be for his persistent kindness to a woman whose favours he once claimed and in whose house he had hidden when condemned to death in 1797.

This woman, celebrated under the Directory for a liaison with a powerful protector, married through that protection a purveyor named Husson, who made millions from government contracts but whom Napoleon ruined in 1802. Husson became insane and threw himself into the Seine, leaving Madame Husson pregnant. Moreau, intimately allied with her, was then condemned to death and could not marry her; forced to leave France, he watched over her. Madame Husson, at twenty-two, married a clerk named Clapart, aged twenty-seven, rumored to be a rising man. But Clapart, though coarse and handsome, had no intelligence. Expecting wealth, he feigned passion but could not supply the wants she had known. He filled a Treasury post badly, giving a salary of eighteen hundred francs, which was all the household had to live on. When Moreau returned as secretary to the Comte de Serizy he learned of Madame Husson’s plight and obtained, before his own marriage, an appointment for her as head waiting-woman to Madame Mère, the Emperor’s mother. Despite such protection, Clapart was never promoted; his incapacity showed.

Ruined in 1815 by the fall of the Empire, the brilliant Aspasia of the Directory had no resources other than Clapart’s clerk’s salary of twelve hundred francs from a job procured by the Comte de Serizy. Moreau, the only protector of a woman he had known in opulence, obtained a half-scholarship for her son, Oscar Husson, at the school of Henri IV, and sent her regularly, by Pierrotin, such supplies from the estate at Presles as he could decently offer to a household in distress.

Oscar was his mother’s whole life and future. The poor woman’s only fault was exaggerated tenderness for her boy—the bête-noire of his stepfather. Oscar, unfortunately, had a foolish overweening conceit his mother did not perceive despite the stepfather’s sarcasms. This conceit so worried Monsieur Moreau that he begged Madame Clapart to send the boy to him for a month so he might study his character and find a suitable career; Moreau thought of proposing Oscar as his successor to the count.

To give each thing its due, we should show the causes of Oscar’s silly vanity: he was born in Madame Mère’s household, dazzled in childhood by imperial splendors. His imagination retained those images and nursed hopes of regaining a golden past. The natural boastfulness of schoolboys, resting on these memories and on maternal tales of former splendour, developed excessively. Oscar, leaving school, had borne many humiliations as a scholarship pupil among paying students unless the scholar can impose respect by physical superiority.

This mixture of departed splendor, faded beauty, blind maternal love, and heroic suffering made the mother a pathetic figure in Paris.

Incapable of understanding Moreau’s real attachment to her, and of that woman’s for the man who saved her in 1797, Pierrotin did not communicate his suspicion of danger to Moreau. His thought, “We have enough to do in this world to look after ourselves,” returned to mind, along with his obedience to what he called the “chefs de file”—men of rank. Besides, Pierrotin’s head was full of his own stings like coins in a purse. So his “Very good, madame,” “Certainly, madame,” in reply to the poor mother showed his desire to escape her long instructions.

“You will be sure to place the packages so they can’t get wet if the weather changes.”

“I’ve a hood,” replied Pierrotin. “Besides, see with what care they are placed.”

“Oscar, don’t stay more than two weeks, no matter how much they may ask you,” continued Madame Clapart, returning to her son. “You can’t please Madame Moreau, whatever you do; besides, you must be home by the end of September. We are to go to Belleville, you know, to your uncle Cardot.”

“Yes, mamma.”

“Above all,” she said in a low voice, “never speak about servants; keep thinking all the time that Madame Moreau was once a waiting-maid.”

“Yes, mamma.”

Like all youths whose vanity is excessively ticklish, Oscar seemed annoyed at being lectured on the threshold of the Lion d’Argent.

“Well, now good-bye, mamma. We shall start soon; there’s the horse all harnessed.”

The mother, forgetting she was in the street, embraced Oscar and smiling took a little roll from her basket.

“Tiens! you were forgetting your roll and the chocolate! My child, don’t take anything at the inns; they’ll charge you ten times the value.”

Oscar would have liked his mother farther off as she stuffed the roll and chocolate into his pocket. Two young men a few years older than Oscar, better dressed and without a mother hanging on them—complete in independence—witnessed the scene.

“He said mamma!” cried one, laughing.

The words reached Oscar and drove him to say, “Good-bye, mother!” in a tone of terrible impatience.

Madame Clapart spoke too loudly and seemed desirous to show her tenderness. “What is the matter with you, Oscar?” she asked. “I don’t know what to make of you,” she added severely, then resumed in her tender voice, “Listen, my Oscar—do not talk and show off; keep your tongue in check. You are not advanced enough to judge persons; there is nothing more dangerous than speaking in public conveyances. Besides, in a diligence well-bred persons always keep silence.”

The two young men had walked to the other end of the establishment and returned with boot-heels tapping. To rid himself of his mother, Oscar took an heroic measure that showed vanity stimulating intellect.

“Mamma,” he said, “you are standing in a draught, and you may take cold. Besides, I am going to get into the coach.”

The lad touched a tender spot; his mother caught him to her bosom, kissed him as if he were starting on a long journey, and went with him to the vehicle with tears. “Don’t forget to give five francs to the servants when you come away,” she said; “write me three times at least during the fifteen days; behave properly, and remember all I have told you. You have linen enough; don’t send any to the wash. And above all, remember Monsieur Moreau’s kindness; mind him as you would a father, and follow his advice.”

As he got into the coach Oscar’s blue woollen stockings became visible as his trousers pulled up, and the new patch showed through the parting coat-tails. The smiles of the two young men, on whom these signs of respectable indigence were not lost, were fresh wounds to his vanity.

“The first place was engaged for Oscar,” said the mother to Pierrotin. “Take the back seat,” she said to the boy with a loving smile.

Oh how Oscar regretted that trouble and sorrow had destroyed his mother’s beauty and that poverty prevented her being better dressed! One young man, wearing top-boots and spurs, nudged the other to notice Oscar’s mother; the other twirled his moustache as if to say, “Rather a pretty figure!”

“How shall I ever get rid of mamma?” thought Oscar.

“What’s the matter?” asked Madame Clapart.

He pretended not to hear. Perhaps she lacked tact; all absorbing sentiments have much egoism.

“Georges, do you like children when travelling?” asked one young man of the other.

“Yes, my good Amaury, if they are weaned, and are named Oscar, and have chocolate.”

These remarks, uttered in half-tones, allowed Oscar to hear or not; his face would indicate how much fun they might make of him. Oscar chose not to hear and looked to see if his mother, weighing on him like a nightmare, was still there. He compared his companion’s dress with his own and felt that his mother’s toilet counted for much in the youths’ smiles. “If they would only take themselves off!” he thought.

Instead, Amaury tapped the coucou’s wheel and remarked to Georges, “And so, my friend, you are really going to trust your future to this fragile bark?”

“I must,” replied Georges, fatalistically.

Oscar sighed as he noted the jaunty hat of his companion, revealing blond hair; he had black hair cut close by order of his stepfather. The chubby healthy face of the vain lad contrasted with the long pale one of the other. The latter’s broad forehead and filled chest showed a cashmere waistcoat; his tight iron-gray trousers and frogged overcoat looked elegant; the sight galled Oscar like an ugly woman seeing a pretty one. The click of the stranger’s boot-heels echoed in his heart; he felt hampered by his clothes—which were probably his stepfather’s—while the other seemed at ease.

“That fellow must have heaps of francs in his trousers pocket,” thought Oscar.

The young man turned. Oscar saw a gold chain and a watch; from then on the young man assumed the proportions of a personage.

Living in rue de la Cerisaie since 1815, taken to and from school by his stepfather and seldom seeing anything but his mother’s poverty, Oscar had little comparison. Brought up strictly on Moreau’s advice, he seldom went to the theatre except to the Ambigu-Comique. His stepfather still wore his watch in the fob with a heavy gold chain and various ornaments; Oscar considered that old-fashioned finery the “ne plus ultra.” He was bewildered by the revelation of superior, negligent elegance: spotless gloves, a gold-headed switch cane twirled with grace.

Oscar had reached that last quarter of adolescence when little things cause immense joys and miseries—a period when youth prefers misfortune to a ridiculous suit and torments itself about neckcloths and the desire to appear a man. Then the lad swells; his swagger is portentous because it boasts about nothings. Yet a lad who envies a well-dressed fool may also admire talent. Such defects, unless rooted in the heart, prove only youthful exuberance. For a nineteen-year-old only child kept at home by poverty and adored by an indulgent mother to envy a twenty-two-year-old in a frogged surtout is a peccadillo common to all ranks.

But Oscar passed from peccadillo to ill feeling. He felt humiliated, angry with the youth he envied, and secretly desired to show he was as good.

The two young fellows continued walking up and down from the gate to the stables; each time they turned they looked at Oscar curled in his corner. Oscar, convinced their jokes concerned him, affected indifference. He began to hum a chorus lately in vogue among liberals ending, “‘Tis Voltaire’s fault, ‘tis Rousseau’s fault.”

“Tiens! perhaps he is one of the chorus at the Opera,” said Amaury.

This exasperated Oscar, who bounded up, pulled out the wooden “back,” and called to Pierrotin: “When do we start?”

“Presently,” said Pierrotin, whip in hand, gazing toward rue d’Enghien.

At this moment a young man arrived with a true gamin followed by a porter dragging a hand-cart. The young man spoke confidentially to Pierrotin, who nodded and called his porter. The porter helped unload the cart, which contained two trunks, buckets, brushes, oddly shaped boxes, and innumerable packages that the gamin stowed with such speed Oscar, then smiling at his mother across the street, noticed none of the paraphernalia that might reveal his new companion’s profession.

The gamin, about sixteen, wore a gray blouse buckled by a leather belt, a cap jauntily perched, curly brown hair on his shoulders, a black-silk cravat around a very white neck, bright gray eyes, and a vivacious, mischievous face—Figaro-like. His gestures and mocking eye showed a lively intellect born of early vocation. Unconcerned about costume, he looked at his unpolished boots as a quizzical effect. “I’m in style,” he said, shaking himself and addressing his companion.

The companion’s glance showed authority over the adept; a practised eye would call the gamin a “rapin.” “Behave yourself, Mistigris,” said his master.

The master was a slight pale young man with thick black hair worn fantastically. His abundant hair suited an enormous head with a vast forehead proclaiming precocious intellect. His strained, harassed face was hollowed as if from chronic malady, poverty, or recent grief. His shabby American-green surtout was clean and brushed; a black waistcoat buttoned to the throat almost concealed a scarlet neckerchief; black trousers flapped his thin legs; muddy boots showed he had come on foot. With a rapid look he seized the Lion d’Argent scene, then glanced at Mistigris’s satirical stare.

“Charming!” said Mistigris.

“Yes, very,” replied the other.

“We seem to have got here too early,” continued Mistigris. “Couldn’t we get a mouthful somewhere? My stomach, like Nature, abhors a vacuum.”

“Have we time for a cup of coffee?” the artist asked Pierrotin.

“Yes, but don’t be long,” answered Pierrotin.

“Good; that means a quarter of an hour,” said Mistigris.

The pair disappeared. Nine o’clock was striking in the hotel kitchen. Georges thought it reasonable to remonstrate with Pierrotin.

“Hey! my friend; with such wheels as these (striking the clumsy tires with his cane) one might expect punctuality. I have devilishly pressing business or I wouldn’t trust my bones to it. And that horse you call Rougeot doesn’t look fit to make up for lost time.”

“We are going to harness Bichette while those gentlemen take their coffee,” replied Pierrotin. “Go and ask,” he said to his porter, “if Père Léger is coming with us—”

“Where is your Père Léger?” asked Georges.

“Over the way, at number 50. He couldn’t get a place in the Beaumont diligence,” said Pierrotin, making no answer to his customer; then he disappeared in search of Bichette.

Georges, after shaking hands with his friend, got into the coach, handling an important portfolio which he placed beneath the seat cushion. He took the opposite corner to Oscar on the same seat.

“This Père Léger troubles me,” he said.

“They can’t take away our places,” replied Oscar. “I have number one.”

“And I number two,” said Georges.

Just as Pierrotin reappeared having harnessed Bichette, the porter returned with a stout man whose weight could not be less than two hundred and fifty pounds. Père Léger belonged to the farmer species—square back, protuberant stomach, powdered pigtail, little blue linen coat, white gaiters above the knee fastened over velveteen breeches with silver buckles, and hob-nailed shoes. He held a small polished reddish stick with a large knob and a leather thong.

“And you are called Père Léger?” asked Georges very seriously as the farmer attempted to step up.

“At your service,” replied the farmer with a face like Louis XVIII, fat rubicund cheeks, and a large nose; his smiling eyes sunken in rolls of fat. “Come, a helping hand, my lad!” he said to Pierrotin.

The farmer was hoisted in by Pierrotin and the porter amid cries of “Houp la! hi! ha! hoist!” uttered by Georges.

“Oh! I’m not going far; only to La Cave,” said the farmer, good-humoredly.

In France everyone takes a joke.

“Take the back seat,” said Pierrotin, “there’ll be six of you.”

“Where’s your other horse?” demanded Georges. “Is it as mythical as the third post-horse.”

“There she is,” said Pierrotin, pointing to the little mare coming along alone.

“He calls that insect a horse!” exclaimed Georges.

“Oh! she’s good, that little mare,” said the farmer, now seated. “Your servant, gentlemen. Well, Pierrotin, how soon do you start?”

“I have two travellers in there after a cup of coffee,” replied Pierrotin.

The hollow-cheeked young man and his page reappeared.

“Come, let’s start!” was the general cry.

“We are going to start,” replied Pierrotin. “Now, then, make ready,” he said to the porter, who removed the stones that stopped the wheels.

Pierrotin took Rougeot by the bridle and gave that guttural cry, “Ket, ket!” to urge the two animals; though stiff, they pulled the coach to the Lion d’Argent door. Then Pierrotin gazed up rue d’Enghien and disappeared, leaving the coach to the porter.

“Ah ca! is he subject to such attacks—that master of yours?” said Mistigris to the porter.

“He has gone to fetch his feed from the stable,” replied the porter, versed in tricks to keep passengers calm.

“Well, after all,” said Mistigris, “‘art is long, but life is short’--to Bichette.”

At that time the studios fancied mutilating or transposing proverbs; it was a triumph to change letters or words to keep the semblance while making a fantastic meaning.

“Patience, Mistigris!” said his master; “‘come wheel, come whoa.’”

Pierrotin returned bringing the Comte de Serizy, who had come through rue de l’Échiquier and with whom Pierrotin had doubtless conversed.

“Père Léger,” said Pierrotin, looking into the coach, “will you give your place to Monsieur le comte? That will balance the carriage better.”

“We sha’n’t be off for an hour if you go on this way,” cried Georges. “We shall have to take down this infernal bar, which cost such trouble to put up. Why should everyone move for the man who comes last? We all have a right to the places we took. What place has monsieur engaged? Come, find that out! Haven’t you a way-book, a register, or something? What place has Monsieur Lecomte engaged—count of what, I’d like to know.”

“Monsieur le comte,” said Pierrotin, visibly troubled, “I am afraid you will be uncomfortable.”

“Why didn’t you keep better count of us?” said Mistigris. “‘Short counts make good ends.’”

“Mistigris, behave yourself,” said his master.

Monsieur de Serizy was evidently taken by people in the coach for a bourgeois named Lecomte.

“Don’t disturb anyone,” he said to Pierrotin. “I will sit with you in front.”

“Come, Mistigris,” said the master to his rapin, “remember the respect you owe to age; you don’t know how shockingly old you may be yourself some day. ‘Travel deforms youth.’ Give your place to monsieur.”

Mistigris opened the leather curtain and jumped out with frog-like agility.

“You mustn’t be a rabbit, august old man,” he said to the count.

“Mistigris, ‘ars est celare bonum,’” said his master.

“I thank you very much, monsieur,” said the count to Mistigris’s master, next to whom he now sat.

The minister of State cast a sagacious glance round the coach interior, affronting both Oscar and Georges.

“When persons want to be master of a coach they should engage all the places,” remarked Georges.

Certain now of his incognito, the Comte de Serizy made no reply but assumed a good-natured bourgeois air.

“Suppose you were late, wouldn’t you be glad the coach waited for you?” said the farmer to the two young men.

Pierrotin still looked up and down the street, whip in hand, apparently reluctant to mount the hard seat where Mistigris fidgeted.

“If you expect someone else, I am not the last,” said the count.

“I agree to that reasoning,” said Mistigris.

Georges and Oscar began to laugh impertinently.

“The old fellow doesn’t know much,” whispered Georges to Oscar, who was delighted at this apparent union between himself and the object of his envy.

“Parbleu!” cried Pierrotin, “I shouldn’t be sorry for two more passengers.”

“I haven’t paid; I’ll get out,” said Georges, alarmed.

“What are you waiting for, Pierrotin?” asked Père Léger.

Whereupon Pierrotin shouted a certain “Hi!” in which Bichette and Rougeot recognized a definitive resolution and sprang toward the faubourg at a pace soon to slacken.

The count had a burning red face with inflamed portions highlighted by snow-white hair. These blotches injured his naturally noble air; careful examination was needed to find in his green-gray eyes the shrewdness of a magistrate, the wisdom of a statesman, and a legislator’s knowledge. His face was flat, the nose depressed into it. The hat hid the grace of his forehead. There was enough odd contrast—silvery hair, burning face, thick black brows—to amuse youths.

He wore a long blue overcoat buttoned military-fashion to the throat, a white cravat, cotton wool in his ears, a high shirt-collar making a white patch on each cheek. His black trousers covered his boots whose toes were barely seen. No decoration adorned him; doeskin gloves concealed his hands. Nothing betrayed a peer of France and one of the kingdom’s most useful statesmen.

Père Léger had never seen the count, who knew the farmer only by name. When the count entered the carriage he cast a glance about him that affronted Georges and Oscar but was truly looking for the head-clerk of his notary in case he had been forced, like himself, into Pierrotin’s vehicle, intending to caution him about his incognito. Feeling reassured by Oscar, Père Léger, and the quasi-military air, waxed moustaches, and adventurer look of Georges, he concluded his note had reached his notary Alexandre Crottat in time to keep the clerk from leaving.

“Père Léger,” said Pierrotin when they reached the steep faubourg Saint-Denis at rue de la Fidélité, “suppose we get out, hey?”

“I’ll get out, too,” said the count, hearing Léger’s name.

“Goodness! if this is how we go we shall do fourteen miles in fifteen days!” cried Georges.

“It isn’t my fault,” said Pierrotin, “if a passenger wishes to get out.”

“Ten louis for you if you keep the secret of my being here as I told you before,” said the count in a low voice, taking Pierrotin by the arm.

“Oh, my thousand francs!” thought Pierrotin as he winked at Monsieur de Serizy, meaning, “Rely on me.”

Oscar and Georges stayed in the coach.

“Look here, Pierrotin, since Pierrotin you are,” cried Georges when they were stowed away again, “if you don’t mean to go faster than this, say so! I’ll pay my fare and take a post-horse at Saint-Denis; I have important business.”

“Oh! he’ll go well enough,” said Père Léger. “Besides, the distance isn’t great.”

“I am never more than half an hour late,” asserted Pierrotin.

“Well, you are not wheeling the Pope in this old barrow,” said Georges, “so, get on.”

“Perhaps he’s afraid of shaking monsieur,” said Mistigris looking at the count. “But you shouldn’t have preferences, Pierrotin, it isn’t right.”

“Coucous and the Charter make all Frenchmen equals,” said Georges.

“Oh! be easy,” said Père Léger; “we are sure to get to La Chapelle by mid-day,”—La Chapelle being the village beyond the Barrière of Saint-Denis.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Those who travel in public conveyances know strangers do not at once begin to talk; there is first a period of mutual examination. Once each thinks he has sized up his companions, the most talkative starts and the conversation livens—especially in French stage-coaches, where everyone is eager to laugh and show wit.

A twenty-two-year-old, Georges, was tempted to display his cleverness. He had sized up his fellow passengers: the count as a second-rate manufacturer or chandler; the shabby young man with Mistigris as insignificant; Oscar as a ninny; Pere Léger as an excellent dupe. He resolved to amuse himself at their expense and, as the coucou descended from La Chapelle toward Saint-Denis, ran over possible personae—Etienne, Béranger, a Carbonaro, the son of Marshal Ney, a disguised Russian prince, Cousin, Lord Byron—finally choosing a swaggering, Eastern-adventurer role. Mistigris’s banter and Pierrotin’s exclamations opened the talk.

Georges assumed the air of someone just returned from the Levant. Though pale, he said he had been ill for three months and had only just left his bed after a “germ” of suppressed plague. This produced alarm and gibes. He plunged into tales: Egypt and Greece, service under Ali, pacha of Janina, a quarrel with him, and Oriental living that had disordered his liver.

When Pere Léger asked if he had been a soldier and how old he was, Georges answered twenty-nine and outlined a patched military history: enlisted as a private in 1813, present at Hanau, promoted to sergeant-major, made sub-lieutenant at Montereau and decorated by the Emperor. He hinted at service at Mont-Saint-Jean in 1815, expressed disgust with France, then said he went abroad with comrades to serve a pacha in Egypt. He declared he wouldn’t renounce his religion for Islam and told of receiving a thousand talari from the pacha.

The passengers were captivated. Georges boasted of being the grandson of the famous Czerni-Georges, who had warred on the Porte; his father had died in Paris leaving Georges’s mother pregnant. Their property had been seized; his mother had remarried a purveyor, Monsieur Yung, now dead; she had left Georges estranged from his stepfather. That explained why he had gone to war as a private in 1813. Back in Greece, Ali Tebelen welcomed him as descendant of Czerni-Georges; Georges claimed Ali gave him a harem—five wives and ten slaves—but the women were stupid and the harem little better than a herd of goats; he preferred French grisettes.

They were entering Saint-Denis and stopped at a tavern famed for its cheese-cakes. The count, suspecting the youth had left something in the coach, returned and found beneath the cushion a portfolio labeled “Maitre Crottat, notary.” He took out the deed of sale for the farm at Moulineaux, pocketed it, and went into the inn, concluding Georges was Crottat’s second clerk sent as head-clerk. Georges meanwhile had paid for cheese-cakes and wine for Pere Léger and Oscar, basking in their admiration, and tried to pick up Mistigris and his master.

When Pierrotin returned, Georges resumed his tales. He said he had been made commander of Ali’s cavalry and had distinguished himself against Chosrew pacha, cutting his line and receiving Ali’s gratitude and costly gifts—yataghans, carbines, scimitars. Back at Janina Ali proposed degrading acts—drowning a wife, renouncing his religion—but Georges refused and fled. He claimed Chosrew later captured Ali by sea with English help; Chosrew had become a naval commander and, when he recognized Georges at Smyrna, nearly executed him. Monsieur de Rivière, the French ambassador, intervened, saving him and recovering two thousand talari, though most treasures were taken by the admiral.

Georges said he had witnessed the execution of Smyrna’s governor and then gone to Spain, becoming aide-de-camp to Mina with the rank of colonel for the constitutional cause. The count reacted sharply, warning that the Court of Peers was then investigating conspiracies and punishing French soldiers who bore arms against France or engaged in foreign intrigues. He added that if he were a magistrate he would have ordered the gendarmes at Pierrefitte to arrest the supposed aide-de-camp and summon the coach’s occupants to testify. The remark silenced Georges as they passed the gendarmerie guard-house.

Conversation turned to cultivation in Eastern lands. Georges, with his habitual flippancy, described them as too busy cutting each other’s heads off to farm like the French: onions, rice, tobacco, dates, rugs at Smyrna, Latakia tobacco, opium from poppies—customs that left the fellahs barely enough after taxes. Pere Léger, puzzled, asked about sheep and fields; Georges offered evasive explanations about crops growing with little cultivation. He mocked bureaucracy and contrasted despotic plunder with French stamped papers. The count, drawing from this talk, advised landowners to sell, warning taxes in Italy were heavy too.

Mistigris and Schinner—the supposed painter—joined in. Schinner claimed to be a humble painter returned from Rome after winning the grand prix and sent to Italy at the government’s expense. He insisted he’d go merely as a decorator to a château. The count indulged this mock-importance of artists, arguing that great ceilings and the love that inspired them were worth sacrifices. Oscar, jealous and awkward, listened, eager to be somebody.

Pierrotin, the driver, joked about carrying sovereigns and the pourboires he expected, and fretted about a debt for a new coach. The passengers exchanged banter about ceilings, Venice, Rome, and a near-murder Schinner claimed to have endured there. Mistigris hinted Lord Byron had been involved; Schinner demurred, not wishing to speak of Byron.

Schinner then related a long account of Dalmatia, Zara, and a Greek beauty named Zena. He had stayed with an apothecary, seen Zena on a neighboring balcony, and, despite an ugly duenna and a jealous seventy-year-old husband—an Uscoque pirate with millions, a missing ear and eye—had exchanged looks and stolen walks with her. He detailed the dangerous delicacy of their flirtation: ramparts, the duenna who wouldn’t leave, and growing tension. He finally entered the pirate’s house and found Zena; they walked the ramparts together. One day he returned to find the street full of people; he was seized, garroted, gagged, and dragged before magistrates accused of murdering the pirate. He was stoned and nearly killed by a maddened populace.

He learned that Zena had poisoned the pirate with opium to free herself for walks with him; the duenna had overdone the dose, implicating him. He insisted he knew nothing of the murder. The mayor and Austrian commissary of police released him with orders to return to Rome, while Zena, who had let the heirs take most of the Uscoque’s fortune, was sentenced to two years’ seclusion in a convent; Schinner hoped to paint her portrait someday. Mistigris complained that Schinner had left him penniless in Venice, forcing him to paint cheap portraits to reach Rome.

Georges chimed in with comments on Dalmatian maraschino and the follies of youth. Pere Léger repeatedly asked about farming and crops, receiving brief, often evasive replies. The count maintained a guarded attitude, sometimes smiling at fashionable braggadocio and sometimes revealing surprising knowledge—for example, when Georges mentioned names tied to Smyrna that the count recognized, making the youth seem less sure of his invented recollections.

Throughout the journey the characters parried between bluster and information: Georges’s ostentatious Eastern adventures mixed fact and fabrication; the count, discreet but observant, suspected a clerk posing as a colonel; Schinner’s romance showed the dangers beneath exotic talk; Mistigris supplied cynical comments; Pierrotin kept an eye on profit; Pere Léger asked homely questions; Oscar gaped and yearned to be more. Conversation, prompted by the coach’s enforced intimacy and the French habit of talk, ran on—at times diverting, at times revealing—until they moved on toward Pierrefitte and beyond.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Pierrotin’s coach descended into the large village of Saint-Brice, where he usually stopped to rest his horses. It was about half-past one. At the innkeeper’s cry—“Here’s Pere Leger”—the travelers prepared for breakfast.

“Always once a day,” said the fat farmer. Georges, twirling his cane, provoked poor Oscar by producing a small straw case of cigars and lighting one on the threshold. Oscar, puffing and coughing, tried to look jaunty; Schinner and Georges showed him proper technique, while the painter and Georges mocked Oscar’s awkwardness. Mistigris, seizing Oscar’s cigar, smoked with relish. Oscar fumed inwardly at Georges’s spending—Alicante, cheesecakes, cigars—and thought his own shabby clothes and patched stockings betrayed him. Mistigris, sharing the embarrassment, revealed his own patched socks, and the count, standing apart beneath the porte-cochere, smiled at the youngsters’ folly while envying their easy gaiety.

In the kitchen, the innkeeper and Pere Leger spoke low of Les Moulineaux. The innkeeper guessed Pere Leger had arranged to buy it, and offered to lend money. Pere Leger answered calmly that he had done for the count. He explained his plan: having secured forty thousand “pictures on coin” from the King to present to Moreau—presumably a middleman—he would buy Les Moulineaux outright for 260,000 francs and then sell it to the Count de Serizy for 360,000, profiting handsomely while keeping appearances by letting the count propose him as tenant. The farm brought in 6,000 francs rent now; Pere Leger would take it at 7,500 for eighteen years and show it as a sound investment. The innkeeper approved; the count, listening unseen, felt he had found evidence he sought but wondered if it was only talk and whether Moreau, the steward, was implicated.

Pierrotin came out to water the horses. The count, fearing indiscretion, whispered to him to keep his name secret in Saint-Brice and Isle-Adam, and promised Pierrotin a thousand francs the next morning if he did. Pierrotin, flattered and pale with happiness, agreed and then, pretending he had a fish to deliver, refused the innkeeper’s invite to breakfast and urged Pere Leger to come on. Pere Leger accepted, planning to harness a horse the innkeeper wished to sell and overtake Pierrotin.

To the count’s surprise, Pierrotin rebridled, and Schinner and Mistigris walked on. When Pierrotin caught up with the others, Pere Leger and Georges arrived too, Pere Leger boasting that he was the farmer of Les Moulineaux. Conversation resumed: Georges puffed cigars and asked about careers. Oscar, indignant at being called “little young man,” declared himself destined for diplomacy. The others laughed; Georges praised his experience with cigars and travel, and Schinner tongue-in-cheek tried to coach Oscar in courtly manners. Oscar, swelling with pretension, boasted of his family, his private tutor—Abbe Loraux—and his acquaintance with the Comte de Serizy and his son, whom he rode with. The claim surprised Pere Leger and the others.

When the count, interested, asked Oscar for information about Serizy, Oscar obliged: the count was said to suffer from a hideous skin disease; his wife, who couldn’t endure him, was the true power; the count was in love with her but as pitiable as Arnolphe in Moliere—so Oscar. He advised seeking the marquis rather than the count. The companions laughed at Oscar’s intimate knowledge; the count, shocked at the gossip about the Comtesse de Serizy, interposed sharply: he was a friend of her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, and would not tolerate disparagement of the countess. He warned them against speaking ill of people and declared he knew many influential persons and even Monsieur Grindot, architect of Presles. Then, asking Pierrotin to stop at the next inn, he left the coach, taking a path through the woods toward what everyone perceived as the chateau.

Pierrotin drove through Moisselles and the little group fell quiet. The count drew Pierrotin aside in the inn-yard and asked about Oscar. Pierrotin explained the youth was the son of a poor woman from rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, who often received fruit and game from Presles. Pere Leger teased that Pierrotin thought Oscar of some noble house, and the three young men sat mortified, realizing how their boastful talk might have misled the count.

When the coach entered the road to Presles, conversation resumed in fits of bravado and rebuke: Georges warned Oscar that if the man they’d encountered was the Count de Serizy, he had cause to be ashamed. Oscar, remembering his mother’s counsel and feeling premonitions of trouble, tried to maintain composure. They approached the estate: the chateau of Presles rose, splendid in the valley. The count made a light farewell, wishing success to the colonel, the painter, and Oscar, and urged the colonel to reconcile with the King; he praised Schinner but forbade him from domestic visits, and lauded Oscar’s imagined knowledge of statesmen. Then he told Pierrotin to set him out and to fetch him the same time next day, and went along a wood-path, leaving his companions embarrassed.

Pere Leger, suddenly recognizing the path to Les Moulineaux, confronted Georges—who quickly slipped away so the farmer couldn’t find him. Pierrotin pulled into the iron gates and drove into Presles. Oscar declared he would go straight to the chateau and flung himself out in a narrow path, uncertain where to go. Pere Leger shouted directions, and Oscar, compelled, entered the grand courtyard. To Oscar’s astonishment, Mistigris and his master Schinner were to be installed at the chateau: Pierrotin had been instructed to deposit their packages and the youths’ luggage with the concierge. In ten minutes Pierrotin discharged the painter’s implements and Oscar’s effects, including a small leather portmanteau, which the concierge’s wife took in charge. Then Pierrotin drove out through the forest road to Isle-Adam, his face aglow with the thought of the thousand francs he would soon receive and of the profit from his shrewd secrecy. Nothing more was needed for his happiness: on the morrow he would have his thousand francs and could begin paying for a new coach.

Throughout, the youthful boastfulness, the farmer’s scheme, the count’s discreet surveillance, Pierrotin’s opportunism, and the social pretensions of the travelers intersected: the count’s secrecy and influence, Pere Leger’s plot against the count, Oscar’s bluster and folly, and Pierrotin’s double gain all came to light at Presles, leaving the coach’s occupants in varying states of triumph, confusion, and shame.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Oscar, abashed, skulked behind a clump of trees in the courtyard, watching what would become of his two traveling companions, when Monsieur Moreau appeared on the portico from the guard-room. He wore a long blue overcoat, yellowish breeches, top-boots, and carried a riding-whip.

“Ah! my boy, so here you are? How is the dear mamma?” he said, taking Oscar’s hand. “Good-day, messieurs,” he added to Mistigris and his master, who stepped forward. “You are, no doubt, the two painters Monsieur Grindot told me to expect.”

He whistled twice; the concierge came.

“Take these gentlemen to rooms 14 and 15. Madame Moreau will give you the keys. Go with them to show the way; make fires if necessary, and take up all their things. I have orders from Monsieur le comte,” he added addressing the young men, “to invite you to my table, messieurs; we dine at five, as in Paris. If you like hunting, you will find plenty to amuse you; I have a license from the Eaux et Forêts; and we hunt over twelve thousand acres of forest, not counting our own domain.”

Oscar, the painter, and Mistigris exchanged subdued glances; Mistigris murmured, “‘Veni, vidi, cecidi,—I came, I saw, I slaughtered.’”

Oscar followed the steward briskly through the park.

“Jacques,” said Moreau to one of his children they met, “run and tell your mother that little Husson has come, and say I must go to Les Moulineaux for a moment.”

The steward, about fifty, medium height and dark-complexioned, seemed abrupt but kind; his speech was decisive, his tone revealing the affectionate penetration that had always made Oscar feel small in his presence. Trained by his mother to magnify the steward, Oscar felt a new fear arriving at Presles, as if Moreau might harm him.

“Well, my Oscar, you don’t look pleased to be here,” said Moreau. “You’ll find plenty of amusement; you shall learn to ride and to shoot and hunt.”

“I don’t know any of those things,” Oscar replied stupidly.

“But I brought you here to learn them.”

“Mamma told me only to stay two weeks because of Madame Moreau.”

“Oh! we’ll see about that,” Moreau said, slightly wounded that his authority was doubted.

Moreau’s youngest son, an active twelve-year-old, ran up.

“Come,” said his father, “take Oscar to your mother.”

Moreau himself went by the shortest path to the gamekeeper’s house, between the park and the forest.

The steward’s pavilion, built a few years before the Revolution and attached to the estate when the count’s father bought it, was a neat Louis XV freestone house standing ten steps above ground. On the ground floor a salon opened into a bedroom, and a dining-room connected with a billiard-room; a staircase and peristyle hall divided two suites. Madame Moreau had turned an adjoining chamber into a boudoir. These rooms, furnished with pieces from the chateau’s old furniture, the salon hung with blue-and-white damask and a Persian rug beneath a cut-glass chandelier, the boudoir arranged in a tent style with a classic divan, suggested country luxury. The dining-room and billiard-room were mahogany-furnished. Around the house Madame Moreau had laid out a garden opening into the park, hiding offices and stables and improving the entrance with a handsome iron gateway.

The Moreaus cleverly concealed their dependence on the count: they appeared as rich, independent managers of a friend’s property. The count and countess never came to Presles to check them. Perquisites granted by Monsieur de Serizy let them live luxuriously—milk, eggs, poultry, game, fruits, flowers, forage, vegetables, wood—sufficient to keep horses and servants. They bought only butcher’s meat, wines, and colonial supplies. The poultry-maid baked bread; Moreau paid his butcher with farm pigs after reserving some for himself. He stacked three hundred tons of hay while accounting for only a hundred, farmed poultry and cattle at the estate’s expense, and used stable manure for the chateau gardens—small appropriations with ostensible pretexts.

Madame Moreau employed local girls as maid and cook; a discharged soldier handled heavy labor. In neighboring towns, people either didn’t know or pretended not to know her past as a waiting-maid. Moreau’s influence secured favors—appointments and decorations—that made the couple welcome at every bourgeois festivity. The rector and mayor of Presles played cards with them every evening; hospitality followed feathering one’s nest.

Estelle, Madame Moreau, a pretty, affected woman of about thirty-six, wore Paris fashions and expensive boots; she avoided walking except in fine weather. Though allotted but five hundred francs a year for her toilette, she spent it to appear like a princess, resenting being called “the steward’s wife at Presles” and aspiring to be taken for the chateau’s mistress. She patronized villagers in the tone of a great lady and depended on her husband to manage stewardship and fortune. Confident in their comfortable existence of seventeen years, she feared the count’s plan to restore the chateau and urged Moreau to partner with Léger about Les Moulineaux so they might retire to Isle-Adam—she did not want to return to servitude.

A rancorous enmity with the Reyberts grew from a slight Madame de Reybert had inflicted when Estelle presumed precedence on arriving at Presles, spreading her former station as a waiting-maid through the countryside. For four years the Reyberts, cut dead by Estelle, brooded on revenge.

The Moreaus, friendly with Grindot the architect, learned two painters were coming to finish chateau decorations—the principal paintings already done by Schinner. Schinner’s reputation had brought respect: he and his wife lived in the chateau by the count’s orders, and the neighborhood vied to pay them attention. To regain a little revenge, Madame Moreau resolved to receive and praise the artist she expected, presenting him as Schinner’s equal.

For two days Estelle prepared, reserving her finest dress for Saturday, certain the artist would arrive for dinner. She wore a pink-striped gown with a pink belt, velvet ribbons and bracelets, bronze kid shoes, and a superb Leghorn bonnet trimmed with moss roses—Parisian elegance made for display. After ordering a choice dinner and reviewing her rooms, she stood near a flower-bed in the chateau courtyard, holding a rose-colored parasol to be seen as mistress on the arrival of the Beaumont coach. When Pierrotin’s coach left only Mistigris’s packages with the concierge, she withdrew disappointed, feeling too fine to sit idle in her salon awaiting the expected coach, which left Paris at noon and passed an hour after Pierrotin.

The two artists, sent by Moreau to their rooms to dress for dinner, had “rigged themselves up,” putting on their best suits before walking to the steward’s lodge, piloted by Jacques Moreau.

“Mamma,” he said, “here are the two artists sent by Monsieur Schinner.”

Madame Moreau, agreeably surprised, rose and prepared to display her graces.

“This ‘you need not hurry’” for Oscar signaled his unimportance to their hostess; after seventeen years she knew her husband’s attachment to Madame Clapart and little Husson and hated both, so Moreau had rarely risked bringing Oscar to Presles.

“We are requested, my husband and myself,” she told the artists, “to do you the honors of the chateau. We both love art, and, above all, artists,” she minced. “Make yourselves at home. In the country, one should be at ease; life is too insipid otherwise. We have already had Monsieur Schinner with us.”

Mistigris glanced slyly at his companion.

“You know him, of course?” Estelle asked.

“Who does not know him, madame?” said the painter.

“Knows him like his double,” Mistigris added.

“Joseph Bridau,” the painter replied, wondering what sort of woman she was.

Mistigris, inwardly rebelling at her patronizing tone, waited for a cue—artists seize such phrases to sketch the ridiculous. Estelle’s clumsy manners and revealing words made amusement easy.

“You say you love art, madame; perhaps you cultivate it successfully,” Bridau said.

“No. My education was commercial; but I have so profound and delicate a sense of art that Monsieur Schinner always asked me, when he had finished a piece, to give my opinion,” she answered.

“Didn’t he propose to ‘croquer’ you?” Bridau asked. “Painters are eager after handsome women.”

“What may you mean by such language?” she replied.

“In the studios we say croquer for sketching,” Mistigris explained, “and we are always wanting to croquer beautiful heads. That’s the origin of ‘She is pretty enough to eat.’”

“I was not aware of the origin,” she said with a sweet glance.

“My pupil here,” Bridau added, “Monsieur Leon de Lora, shows talent for portraiture. He’d be happy to leave you a souvenir by painting your charming head, madame.”

Bridau signaled Mistigris: “Come, sail in.” Leon slid to the sofa beside Estelle and took her hand, which she allowed.

“Oh! madame, if you’d offer a surprise to your husband and give me secret sittings I would surpass myself,” Leon cooed. “You are so beautiful, so fresh, so charming!”

“We must paint your dear children in arabesques,” Bridau interrupted.

“I would rather have them in the salon; but perhaps I am indiscreet,” she coquetted.

“Beauty, madame, is a sovereign whom all painters worship,” Bridau declared.

“They are both charming,” thought Madame Moreau. “Do you enjoy driving? Shall I take you through the woods after dinner in my carriage?”

“Oh! oh! oh!” cried Mistigris ecstatically. “Why, Presles will be our terrestrial paradise.”

“With an Eve, a fair, young, fascinating woman,” added Bridau.

As Estelle bridled, Rosalie burst in.

“Madame! The count is at the chateau.”

“Has he asked for me?” said Estelle.

“No; but he wants his trunk and the key of his apartment.”

“Then give them to him,” she said impatiently, hiding her real trouble.

“Mamma! here’s Oscar Husson,” said the youngest son, bringing in Oscar, who turned red seeing the two artists in evening dress.

“Oh! so you have come, my little Oscar,” Estelle said stiffly. “I hope you will now go and dress,” she added contemptuously. “Your mother, I presume, has not accustomed you to dine in such clothes as those.”

“Oh!” cried the cruel Mistigris, “a future diplomatist knows that ‘two coats are better than none.’”

“How do you mean, future diplomatist?” exclaimed Madame Moreau.

Poor Oscar had tears in his eyes as he looked between Joseph and Leon.

“Merely a joke in traveling,” Joseph replied to spare him.

“The boy wanted to be funny like the rest of us, and he blagued, that’s all,” Mistigris said.

“Madame,” Rosalie returned, “his Excellency orders dinner for eight at six. What shall we do?”

During Estelle’s conference with her head-woman the two artists and Oscar watched in consternation.

“His Excellency! who is he?” said Joseph Bridau.

“Why, Monsieur le Comte de Serizy, of course,” replied Jacques.

“Could he have been the man in the coucou?” Leon asked.

“Oh!” Oscar cried, “the Comte always travels in his own carriage with four horses.”

“How did the Comte get here?” Joseph asked Madame Moreau when she returned, much discomfited.

“I cannot explain it,” she said. “I do not know what brought him—and Moreau not here!”

“His Excellency wishes Monsieur Schinner to come to the chateau,” said the gardener. “And he begs Monsieur Schinner to dine with him; also Monsieur Mistigris.”

“Done for!” cried the rapin laughing. “He whom we took for a bourgeois in the coucou was the count.”

Oscar nearly froze; his throat tightened.

“And you, who talked to him about his wife’s lovers and his skin diseases!” Mistigris turned on Oscar.

“What does he mean?” exclaimed Madame Moreau, puzzled, as the artists left laughing at Oscar’s expression.

Oscar remained dumb, confounded, hearing nothing though Madame Moreau questioned and shook him by the arm. Rosalie reappeared with orders; the household bustled; the master had fallen upon his house like a bombshell.

From the top of the hill near La Cave, where he left the coach, the count had taken the wood-path to the gamekeeper’s house. The keeper was amazed to see his master.

“Is Moreau here? I see his horse,” the count asked.

“No, monseigneur; he meant to go to Moulineaux before dinner and left his horse here while he went to the chateau.”

“If you value your place,” said the count, “take that horse and ride at once to Beaumont to deliver a note I shall write.”

He entered the lodge, wrote a line folded to prevent opening without detection, and gave it to the man as soon as he mounted.

“Not a word to anyone,” he said. “And as for you, madame,” he added to the keeper’s wife, “if Moreau comes for his horse, tell him I have taken it.”

The count crossed the park and entered the chateau through the iron gates. Worn by public life and emotions, he felt wounded by the idea that Moreau had deceived him; thinking on Pere Leger’s revelations and gossip, he judged Moreau less a conspirator than a traitor who had revealed secrets.

When Moreau arrived at the lodge and asked for his horse, the keeper’s wife said the count had taken it.

“Monsieur le comte!” cried Moreau. “Whom do you mean?”

“The Comte de Serizy, our master,” she replied. Unable to understand, Moreau ran to the chateau, where he learned the count was dressing.

“Seven persons invited to dinner!” Rosalie cried when she saw him.

Moreau went through the offices to his house. He met the poultry-girl in an altercation with a handsome young man.

“Monsieur le comte particularly told me a colonel, an aide-de-camp of Mina,” insisted the girl.

“I am not a colonel,” replied Georges.

“Isn’t your name Georges?” asked the girl.

“What’s all this?” said the steward, intervening.

“Monsieur, my name is Georges Marest; I am the son of a rich ironmonger in the rue Saint-Martin; I come on business to Monsieur le Comte de Serizy from Maître Crottat, a notary, whose second clerk I am.”

“And I,” said the girl, “am telling him that monseigneur said: ‘There’ll come a colonel named Czerni-Georges, aide-de-camp to Mina; he’ll come by Pierrotin’s coach; if he asks for me, show him into the waiting-room.’”

“Evidently,” said the clerk, “the count is the traveler we took for a bourgeois in the coucou; if it hadn’t been for the politeness of a young man he’d have come as a rabbit.”

“A rabbit in Pierrotin’s coucou!” exclaimed Moreau and the girl.

“I’m sure of it from what she says,” Georges added. “I blagué the travelers with stories of Egypt and Greece and Spain,” he explained. “Wearing spurs, I took myself out for a colonel: pure nonsense!”

“Tell me,” the steward said, “what did that traveler look like?”

“Face like a brick, snow-white hair, and black eyebrows.”

“That is he!”

“Then I’m lost!” cried Georges.

“Why?”

“Oh, I chaffed him about his decorations.”

“Pooh! he’s a good fellow; you probably amused him. Come at once to the chateau. I’ll go in and see his Excellency. Where did you say he left the coach?”

“At the top of the mountain.”

“I don’t know what to make of it!”

“After all,” Georges thought, “though I did blague him, I didn’t say anything insulting.”

“Why have you come here?” asked the steward.

“I’ve brought the deed of sale for the farm at Moulineaux, ready for signature.”

“Good heavens!” Moreau exclaimed, bewildered.

Moreau felt his heart beat painfully. After rapping on his master’s door he heard:—

“Is that you, Monsieur Moreau?”

“Yes, monseigneur.”

“Come in.”

The count, dressed in white trousers and thin boots, a white waistcoat and black coat, wore the grand cross of the Legion on the right breast and the Golden Fleece on the left. He had arranged his hair himself, likely to impress Monsieur Margueron.

“Well, monsieur,” he said, seated, leaving Moreau standing, “we have not concluded that purchase from Margueron.”

“He asks too much.”

“Why is he not coming to dinner as I requested?”

“Monseigneur, he is ill.”

“Are you sure?”

“I have just come from there.”

“Monsieur,” the count said sternly, “what would you do with a man you trusted if, after seeing you dress wounds you desired secret, he should reveal your misfortunes and laugh at your malady with a strumpet?”

“I would thrash him,” Moreau answered.

“And if he betrayed your confidence and robbed you?”

“I would detect him and send him to the galleys.”

“Monsieur Moreau, listen. You have spoken of my infirmities to Madame Clapart, laughed at her house and, with her, over my attachment to the Comtesse de Serizy; her son, little Husson, told many matters about my treatment in a public conveyance at my presence. He dared calumniate my wife. Besides, Pere Leger, who was in the coach, revealed the plan by the notary at Beaumont, you, and he, concerning Les Moulineaux. If you went to Monsieur Margueron it was to tell him to feign illness. He is so little ill that he is coming here to dinner. Now, monsieur, I could pardon you having made two hundred and fifty thousand francs from your position in seventeen years—I can understand that. You might each time have asked me and I would have given it; but that you, who knew my toil for our country and my love for Madame de Serizy—that you should gossip before a boy, holding up my secrets and affections to ridicule—”

“Monseigneur!”

“It is unpardonable. To injure a man’s interest is one thing; to stab his heart is another! Oh! you do not know what you have done!”

The count put his head in his hands and was silent for a while.

“I leave you what you have gained,” he said after a time, “and I shall forget you. For my sake, for my dignity, and for your honor, we will part decently; I cannot forget what your father did for mine. You will explain the stewardship duties to Monsieur de Reybert, who succeeds you. Be calm, as I am. Give no opportunity for fools to talk. Above all, no recrimination or petty meanness. Though you no longer possess my confidence, behave with the decorum of well-bred persons. As for that miserable boy who wounded me to death, I will not have him sleep at Presles; send him to the inn; I will not answer for my temper if I see him.”

“I do not deserve such gentleness, monseigneur,” Moreau answered with tears. “Yes, you are right; if I had been utterly dishonest I should now be worth five hundred thousand francs instead of half. I offer to give you an account of my fortune with all details. But let me tell you what actually took place. It was from heart to heart that I spoke of you to Madame Clapart. As for my wife, I never said one word of these matters—”

“Enough,” said the count, conviction complete. “All is irrevocable. Put your affairs and mine in order. You can stay in the pavilion until October. Monsieur and Madame de Reybert will lodge in the chateau for now; endeavor to keep on terms with them, like well-bred persons who hate each other, but maintain appearances.”

They went downstairs; Moreau white as the count’s hair, the count calm and dignified.

During this interview the Beaumont coach, which left Paris at one, had stopped before the chateau gates and deposited Maître Crottat, the notary, who, per the count’s orders, was shown into the salon where he found his clerk, subdued, and the two painters, all painfully self-conscious. Monsieur de Reybert, crabbed and fifty, was there with old Margueron and the notary of Beaumont holding a bundle of deeds.

Seeing the count in evening dress and orders, Georges Marest felt a twinge, Joseph Bridau quivered, but Mistigris, conscious of Sunday clothes and with nothing on his conscience, remarked loud enough:—

“Well, he looks a great deal better like that.”

“Little scamp,” said the count, catching him by the ear, “we are both in the decoration business. I hope you recognize your own work, my dear Schinner,” he added, pointing to the salon ceiling.

“Monseigneur,” the artist replied, “I did wrong to take such a celebrated name out of mere bravado; but this day will oblige me to do fine things for you, and so bring credit on my own name of Joseph Bridau.”

“You took up my defense,” said the count hastily; “I hope you will dine with me, as well as my lively friend Mistigris.”

“Your Excellency doesn’t know to what you expose yourself,” the saucy rapin said; “‘facilis descensus victuali,’ as we say at the Black Hen.”

“Bridau!” the minister cried struck by a thought. “Are you related to one of the devoted toilers under the Empire, the head of a bureau who fell a victim to his zeal?”

“His son, monseigneur,” replied Joseph, bowing.

“Then you are most welcome,” the count said, taking Bridau’s hand. “I knew your father; count on me as on—on an uncle in America,” he laughed. “But you are too young to have pupils of your own; to whom does Mistigris belong?”

“To my friend Schinner, who lent him to me,” Joseph answered. “Mistigris’ name is Leon de Lora. Monseigneur, if you knew my father, will you think of his other son, accused of plotting against the State and soon to be tried before the Court of Peers?”

“Ah! that’s true,” the count said. “Yes, I will think about it. As for Colonel Czerni-Georges, the friend of Ali Pacha, and Mina’s aide-de-camp—” he continued, walking up to Georges.

“He! that’s my second clerk!” cried Crottat.

“You are mistaken, Maître Crottat,” the count assumed sternness. “A clerk who intends to be a notary does not leave important deeds in a diligence at the mercy of travelers; nor spend twenty francs between Paris and Moisselles; nor expose himself to be arrested as a deserter—”

“Monseigneur,” said Georges, “I may have amused myself with the bourgeois in the diligence, but—”

“Let his Excellency finish,” said the notary, digging his elbow into his clerk’s ribs.

“A notary,” the count continued, “should practice discretion and caution; he should be incapable of such a blunder as taking a peer of France for a tallow-chandler—”

“I am willing to be blamed for my faults,” Georges said; “but I never left my deeds—”

“Now you are committing the fault of contradicting a minister, a gentleman, an old man, and a client,” the count said. “Give me that deed of sale.”

Georges turned papers in his portfolio.

“That will do; don’t disarrange those papers,” the count said, taking the deed from his pocket. “Here is what you seek.”

Crottat turned it back and forth, astonished to receive it from his client’s hands.

“What does this mean, monsieur?” he asked Georges.

“If I had not taken it,” said the count, “Pere Leger—who showed by his agricultural questions he minds his business—might have seized that paper and guessed my purpose. You must dine with me on condition you describe the execution of the Muslim of Smyrna, and finish the memoirs of some client you know so well.”

“Schlague for blague!” Leon whispered to Bridau.

“Gentlemen,” the count said to the two notaries and Messieurs Margueron and de Reybert, “let us go into the next room and conclude this business before dinner, because, as my friend Mistigris would say: ‘Qui esurit constentit.’”

“Well, he is very good-natured,” Leon said to Georges after the count left the room.

“Yes, he may be,” Georges replied, “but my master isn’t; he’ll request me to blaguer elsewhere.”

“Never mind, you like travel,” said Bridau.

“What a dressing that boy will get from Monsieur and Madame Moreau!” cried Mistigris.

“Little idiot!” said Georges. “If not for him the count would have been amused. Well, the lesson is good; if ever again I’m caught bragging in a public coach—”

“It is stupid,” Joseph said.

“And common,” Mistigris added. “‘Vulgarity is the brother of pretension.’”

While the sale was settled between Monsieur Margueron and the Comte de Serizy, assisted by their notaries and Monsieur de Reybert, the ex-steward walked slowly to his house, entered the salon, and sat down without noticing anything. Little Husson slipped into a corner, frightened by the steward’s livid face.

“Eh! my friend!” Estelle said, coming in tired. “What is the matter?”

“My dear, we are lost—lost beyond recovery. I am no longer steward of Presles, no longer in the count’s confidence.”

“Why not?”

“Pere Leger, who was in Pierrotin’s coach, told the count all about Les Moulineaux. But that is not what cost me his favor.”

“What then?”

“Oscar spoke ill of the comtesse, and told of the count’s diseases.”

“Oscar!” cried Madame Moreau. “Ah! your sin is found out. It was well worth while to warm that young serpent in your bosom. How often I have told you—”

“Enough!” Moreau said in a strained voice.

At that moment they discovered Oscar cowering. Moreau swooped on the lad, seized him by the collar, dragged him to a window. “Speak! what did you say to monseigneur in that coach? What demon let loose your tongue, you who keep a doltish silence whenever I speak to you? Why did you do it?” he cried violently.

Too bewildered to weep, Oscar was dumb and motionless.

“Come with me and beg his Excellency’s pardon,” said Moreau.

“As if his Excellency cares a little for a toad like that!” cried the furious Estelle.

“Come, I say, to the chateau,” repeated Moreau.

Oscar collapsed like an inert mass.

“Come!” cried Moreau, his anger rising.

“No! no! mercy!” Oscar cried, unable to submit to a torture worse than death.

Moreau seized the lad by his coat and dragged him through the yards, which rang with Oscar’s outcries and sobs. He hauled him up the portico and, with an arm made powerful by fury, flung him, bellowing and rigid, into the salon at the very feet of the count, who, having completed the purchase, was about to leave the salon for the dining-room with his guests.

“On your knees, wretched boy! and ask pardon of him who gave food to your mind by obtaining your scholarship.”

Oscar, face to the ground, foamed with rage and said nothing. The spectators were shocked. Moreau seemed out of his senses; his face crimson and bloodshot.

“This young man is a mere lump of vanity,” the count said after waiting for Oscar’s excuses. “A proud man humbles himself because he sees grandeur in a certain self-abasement. I fear you will never make much of that lad.”

So saying, his Excellency passed on. Moreau took Oscar home and ordered the horses immediately put to Madame Moreau’s caleche.

################################################
While the horses were being harnessed, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart:

My dear,—Oscar has ruined me. During his journey in Pierrotin’s coach he spoke of Madame de Serizy’s behavior to his Excellency, who was travelling incognito, and actually told, to himself, the secret of his terrible malady. After dismissing me from my stewardship, the count told me not to let Oscar sleep at Presles, but to send him away immediately. Therefore, to obey his orders, the horses are being harnessed at this moment to my wife’s carriage, and Brochon, my stable-man, will take the miserable child to you to-night.

We are, my wife and I, in a distress of mind which you may perhaps imagine, though I cannot describe it to you. I will see you in a few days, for I must take another course. I have three children, and I ought to consider their future. At present I do not know what to do; but I shall certainly endeavor to make the count aware of what seventeen years of the life of a man like myself is worth. Owning at the present moment about two hundred and fifty thousand francs, I want to raise myself to a fortune which may some day make me the equal of his Excellency. At this moment I feel within me the power to move mountains and vanquish insurmountable difficulties. What a lever is such a scene of bitter humiliation as I have just passed through! Whose blood has Oscar in his veins? His conduct has been that of a blockhead; up to this moment when I write to you, he has not said a word nor answered, even by a sign, the questions my wife and I have put to him. Will he become an idiot? or is he one already? Dear friend, why did you not instruct him as to his behavior before you sent him to me? How many misfortunes you would have spared me, had you brought him here yourself as I begged you to do. If Estelle alarmed you, you might have stayed at Moisselles. However, the thing is done, and there is no use talking about it.

Adieu; I shall see you soon.

Your devoted servant and friend,

Moreau

At eight that evening Madame Clapart, just returned from a walk with her husband, was knitting winter socks for Oscar by candlelight. Monsieur Clapart expected Poiret, a friend who often came to play dominoes; he avoided cafés. In spite of small means, he prided himself on that habit.

“I’m afraid Poiret came while we were out,” said Clapart.

“No, the portress would have told us,” replied his wife.

“She may have forgotten it.”

“It wouldn’t be the first time,” said Clapart.

“Well,” the poor woman said to change the subject, “Oscar must be at Presles by this time. How he will enjoy that fine house and the park.”

“Oh! yes,” snarled Clapart, “you expect fine things of him; but there’ll be squabbles wherever he goes.”

“Will you never cease to find fault with that poor child?” she cried. “What has he done to you? If some day we should live at our ease, we may owe it all to him; he has such a good heart—”

“Our bones will be jelly long before that fellow makes his way,” said Clapart. “You don’t know your own child; he is conceited, boastful, deceitful, lazy, incapable of—”

“Why don’t you go to meet Poiret?” said the mother, wounded.

“A boy who has never won a prize at school!” continued Clapart, equating prizes with future success.

“Did you win any?” asked his wife. “Oscar stood second in philosophy.”

This silenced Clapart for a moment, then he resumed his complaints, including fear Moreau would set his wife against Oscar. “You don’t know what you’re doing,” he began—until the cracking of a postilion’s whip and the noise of a carriage stopping before the house stirred the street. Clapart looked out.

“They have sent Oscar back to you in a post-chaise,” he cried, partly satisfied, partly uneasy.

“Good heavens! what can have happened to him?” Madame Clapart trembled.

Brochon entered, followed by Oscar and Poiret.

“What has happened?” she asked the stable-man.

“I don’t know, but Monsieur Moreau is no longer steward of Presles, and they say your son has caused it. His Excellency ordered that he should be sent home to you. Here’s a letter from poor Monsieur Moreau, madame, which will tell you all. You never saw a man so changed in a single day.”

“Clapart, two glasses of wine for the postilion and for monsieur!” cried the mother, and flung herself into a chair to read the fatal letter. “Oscar,” she said, staggering toward his bed, “do you want to kill your mother? After all the cautions I gave you this morning—”

She fainted from distress. When she recovered, her husband was shaking Oscar by the arm.

“Will you answer me?” he demanded.

“Go to bed, monsieur,” she said to her son. “Let him alone, Monsieur Clapart. Don’t drive him out of his senses; he is frightfully changed.”

Oscar had slipped away to bed the instant he got the order. After a day of such emotion, despite his faults, he slept the sleep of the just. The next day the world seemed less changed; hunger replaced despair, and the violent mental suffering of the night had faded. Youth turns quickly from one impression to another; corporal punishment might have fixed the lesson, but discretion in administering it is crucial.

Madame Clapart sent her husband out so she might be alone with Oscar. She was pitiable: eyes worn with tears, a face weary from sleeplessness, a feeble voice—everything showed suffering beyond what she could bear.

When Oscar entered she motioned him to sit beside her and spoke in a gentle, grieved voice of the benefits they had received from Monsieur Moreau. For six years they had lived on his delicate charity; Monsieur Clapart’s salary and Oscar’s “demi-bourse” scholarship were due to the Comte de Serizy. Most of this would now cease. Clapart had no claim to a pension; when he could no longer keep his place, what would become of them?

“For myself,” she said, “I could nurse the sick or be a housekeeper and support Monsieur Clapart; but you, Oscar, what could you do? You must earn your living. There are four careers for a young man like you—commerce, government employment, the professions, or the military. Commerce needs capital, and we have none. Public employment requires influence and a long probation—you have alienated our only protector. The professions demand years and fees, and again money for your maintenance while you learn. You are eighteen and strong; enlist in the army; it seems the only way you can earn your bread.”

Oscar, ignorant of life and of earning a living, listened submissively. The word “army,” his mother’s tears, and her admonitions finally made him cry. Seeing his tears, Madame Clapart softened, and gave the familiar peroration mothers use in such scenes.

“Well, Oscar, promise me you will be more discreet—repress your silly vanity,” she urged.

Oscar promised, and she kissed him to console him.

“In future,” she added, “you will follow my advice. We will go see your uncle Cardot; he owed much to your father, who gave him his sister Mademoiselle Husson with an enormous dowry, enabling him to make a fortune. Perhaps he might place you with Monsieur Camusot, his successor and son-in-law, in the rue des Bourdonnais. But your uncle has many claims on him—four children, the Cocon d’Or given to his eldest daughter, and other connections. He scarcely knows of our existence. Still, to-morrow we will go and see him; he is our last hope.”

Monsieur Jean-Jerome-Severin Cardot had been a widower six years. As head-clerk of the Cocon d’Or, one of Paris’s oldest firms, he had bought the establishment in 1793 with the dowry money of Mademoiselle Husson, and made a large fortune. To provide for his children he had bought an annuity for himself and divided his capital among them; thus he lived on a thirty-thousand-franc annuity and had the comfort of seeing his children well provided.

Uncle Cardot lived at Belleville, in a first-floor apartment with a southern exposure and a garden for a thousand francs a year. He lived simply, served by an old cook and a former maid of Madame Cardot, who expected small legacies. His apartment remained as his late wife had furnished it. He dined in Paris five days a week and returned home at midnight in a hackney-coach. He was a little old man, neatly dressed, polite to women, and fond of pleasure. He professed epicureanism, admired Voltaire and Beranger, and affected decorum; to his children he appeared grave and attentive, though he privately indulged pleasures and gaiety.

He had not spoken of his income to his children, who supposed he had deprived himself for them, and thus redoubled their attentions. Only Camusot, his son-in-law, knew of the annuity; he considered Cardot’s conduct a generous blending of family duty and later enjoyment.

Madame Clapart did not trouble the Cardots with complaints, seeing them only rarely. She had always clung to Uncle Cardot as a hope and took Oscar to see him during vacations. Cardot sometimes gave Oscar a dinner and a Gaiete visit and once had provided a school-suit and a silver cup and fork. Madame Clapart reminded him of these favors and of his nephew’s gratitude, though Cardot, shrewd and aware that Oscar was a posthumous child with no claim, had not been deeply moved.

“Your son bears the name of Husson,” said Cardot when Madame Clapart and Oscar visited. “If my dear deceased wife were living she would wish to do something for the name of her father and of her brother—”

“She loved her brother,” said Oscar’s mother.

“All my fortune is given to my children,” he continued, “and I have now only an annuity; but I know the path on which you ought to start this young fellow. Let him study law; I’ll pay the costs. Put him in a lawyer’s office and let him learn the business; if he does well, and if I am still alive, each of my children shall lend him a quarter of the cost of a practice; I will be security for him. You will only have to feed and clothe him. Discretion, honesty, and work—live at home, go straight to the law-school, from there to a lawyer’s office; drudge night and day, and study at home. Become, by twenty-two, a second clerk; by twenty-four, head-clerk; be steady, and you will win all. If you don’t like the profession you might enter my son the notary’s office. Work, patience, discretion, honesty—those are your landmarks.”

“God grant that you may live thirty years longer to see your fifth child realizing all we expect from him,” cried Madame Clapart, seizing Cardot’s hand.

“Now come to breakfast,” replied the kind old man, leading Oscar by the ear.

During the meal Cardot noted Oscar’s inexperience. “Send him here now and then,” he said to Madame Clapart as they parted; “I’ll form him for you.”

This visit calmed the poor mother’s anxieties. For the next fortnight she took Oscar walking every day and watched him closely. By the end of October, while the household breakfasted on a humble meal, the formidable ex-steward entered and surprised this scene of poverty.

“We are now living in Paris—but not as we lived at Presles,” said Moreau, explaining the change in their relations caused by Oscar’s folly. “I shall seldom be here myself; for I have gone into partnership with Pere Leger and Pere Margueron of Beaumont. We are speculating in land, and we have begun by purchasing the estate of Persan. I am the head of the concern, which has a capital of a million; part of which I have borrowed on my own securities. When I find a good thing, Pere Leger and I examine it; my partners have each a quarter and I a half in the profits; but I do nearly all the work, and for that reason I shall be constantly on the road. My wife lives here, in the faubourg du Roule, very plainly. When we see how the business turns out, if we risk only the profits, and if Oscar behaves himself, we may, perhaps, employ him.”

“Ah! my friend, the catastrophe caused by my poor boy’s heedlessness may prove to be the cause of your making a brilliant fortune; for, really, you were burying your energy and your capacity at Presles.”

Madame Clapart related her visit to Cardot to show Moreau that she and her son need no longer be a burden.

“He is right, that old fellow,” said the ex-steward. “We must hold Oscar on that path with an iron hand, and he will end as a barrister or a notary. But he mustn’t leave the track; he must go straight through with it. Ha! I know how to help you. The legal business of land-agents is quite important, and I have heard of a lawyer who has just bought what is called a ‘titre nu’; that means a practice without clients. He is a young man, hard as an iron bar, eager for work, ferociously active. His name is Desroches. I’ll offer him our business on condition that he takes Oscar as a pupil; and I’ll ask him to let the boy live with him at nine hundred francs a year, of which I will pay three hundred, so that your son will cost you only six hundred francs, without his living, in future. If the boy ever means to become a man it can only be under a discipline like that. He’ll come out of that office, notary, solicitor, or barrister, as he may elect.”

“Come, Oscar; thank our kind Monsieur Moreau, and don’t stand there like a stone post. All young men who commit follies have not the good fortune to meet with friends who still take an interest in their career, even after they have been injured by them.”

“The best way to make your peace with me,” said Moreau, pressing Oscar’s hand, “is to work now with steady application, and to conduct yourself in future properly.”

################################################
Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a twenty-six-year-old solicitor newly established in the rue de Béthisy in a large apartment at the end of a narrow courtyard, for which he paid little.

Born to poor parents and brought up sternly, Desroches knew of Oscar’s condition and felt interest in him, though his apparent harshness limited the help he could show. Oscar, gaunt, with muddy skin and hair cropped like a clothes-brush, who spoke curtly and had a piercing eye and a gloomy vivacity, was terrified by the sight of his new master.

“We work here day and night,” said the lawyer from his armchair behind a table piled with papers. “Monsieur Moreau, we won’t kill him; but he’ll have to go at our pace.” Though it was Sunday, the head-clerk appeared, pen in hand.

“Monsieur Godeschal, here’s the pupil I told you of. Monsieur Moreau takes the liveliest interest in him. He will dine with us and sleep in the small attic next to your chamber. You will allot the exact time it takes to go to the law-school and back, so he does not lose five minutes on the way. See that he learns the Code and is proficient; after his work here you will give him authors to read. He will be under your immediate direction, and I shall keep an eye on him. They want to make him what you have made yourself, a capable head-clerk, so he can take such a place himself. Go with Monsieur Godeschal, my young friend; he’ll show you your lodging, and you can settle down.”

Desroches praised his clerks—youths with only their ten fingers to rely upon—working as hard as a dozen ordinary fellows. He had taken Godeschal from Derville when the latter was only a second clerk, paid him a thousand francs a year with board, and admired his indefatigable honesty. “For the slightest fault of that kind a clerk leaves my office,” he declared. Moreau thought Oscar was in a good school.

For two years Oscar lived in the rue de Béthisy, a den of petty law. Under this strict and capable supervision he kept rigid hours; his Paris life resembled that of a monk.

At five in the morning, in all weathers, Godeschal woke and took Oscar to the office, where their master was already at work. Oscar ran errands and prepared law-school lessons; Godeschal and often Desroches pointed out authors and difficulties. He was not allowed to leave a single section of the Code until he had mastered it; Godeschal gave him preliminary examinations more searching and longer than those of the law-school. After brief classes he returned to the office; occasionally sent to the Palais, but always under Godeschal’s thumb, until dinner. Dinner was modest—one dish of meat, one of vegetables, and a salad; dessert, a piece of Gruyère cheese. After dinner Godeschal and Oscar returned to work until night. Once a month Oscar breakfasted with his uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother. From time to time Moreau, when in Paris, took Oscar to dine in the Palais-Royal or to the theater in the evening. Oscar had been so snubbed by Godeschal and Desroches for attempts at elegance that he no longer gave thought to his clothes.

“A good clerk,” Godeschal told him, “should have two black coats, one new, one old, a pair of black trousers, black stockings, and shoes. Boots cost too much; you can’t have boots till you are called to the bar. A clerk should never spend more than seven hundred francs a year. Good stout shirts of strong linen are what you want. Ha! when a man starts from nothing to reach fortune, he has to keep down to bare necessities. Look at Monsieur Desroches; he did what we are doing, and see where he is now.”

Godeschal preached by example. He practised strict principles of honor, discretion, and honesty without ostentation; such conduct was natural to him. Eighteen months after Oscar’s installation the second clerk was, for the second time, slightly wrong in his accounts. Godeschal said to him before the others:

“My dear Gaudet, go away of your own free will, that it may not be said that Monsieur Desroches has dismissed you. You have been careless or absent-minded, and neither of those defects can pass here. The master shall know nothing about the matter; that is all that I can do for a comrade.”

At twenty Oscar became third clerk in the office. Though he earned no salary, he was lodged and fed while doing the work of the second clerk. Desroches employed two chief clerks, and the second’s work was unremitting toil. By the end of his second year in the law-school Oscar knew more than most licensed graduates; he did work at the Palais intelligently and argued some cases in chambers. Godeschal and Desroches were satisfied. Yet Oscar still showed, from time to time, a hankering after pleasure and a suppressed desire to shine.

Moreau, satisfied with Oscar’s progress, relaxed his watchfulness; in July 1825, when Oscar passed his examinations with a spotless record, the land-agent gave him money to dress elegantly and Madame Clapart prepared his outfit splendidly.

In November, when the courts reopened, Oscar occupied the chamber of the second clerk and did that work wholly, with a salary of eight hundred francs plus board and lodging. Uncle Cardot, who had privately inquired at Desroches about his nephew, promised Madame Clapart to look out for a practice for Oscar if he continued to do well.

Despite these virtuous appearances, Oscar endured great inward strife. At times he thought of quitting a life so contrary to his tastes; he fancied galley-slaves happier. Galled by the collar of this iron system, wild desires seized him when he compared himself in the street with well-dressed young men. Sometimes he was driven toward women; then he resigned himself, only to fall into deeper disgust for life. Impelled by Godeschal’s example, he was forced rather than led into the rugged way.

Godeschal watched Oscar and, by principle, kept him from temptation. Generally the young clerk had so little money he could not give way to excess. During the last year Godeschal had made five or six pleasure parties with Oscar, defraying expenses, feeling the rope by which he tethered the young kid must be slackened. These “pranks,” as he called them, helped Oscar endure existence, for there was little amusement in breakfasting with Uncle Cardot or seeing his mother, who lived even more penuriously than Desroches. Moreau could not be as familiar with Oscar as Godeschal could; perhaps Godeschal, sincere friend to young Husson, had been behind Moreau in safe efforts to initiate the poor youth into life’s mysteries. Oscar, grown prudent, had come to see the extent and character of the fault committed on that luckless journey; but his repressed fancies and youthful follies might still get the better of him. Nevertheless, the more knowledge he got of the world and its laws, the better his mind would form itself, and, provided Godeschal never lost sight of him, Moreau flattered himself that between them they could bring the son of Madame Clapart through in safety.

“How is he getting on?” asked the land-agent of Godeschal on his return from a journey.

“Always too much vanity,” replied Godeschal. “You give him fine clothes and fine linen, he wears the shirt-fronts of a stockbroker, and so my dainty coxcomb spends his Sundays in the Tuileries, looking out for adventures. What else can you expect? That’s youth. He torments me to present him to my sister, where he would see actresses, ballet-dancers, elegant young fops, spendthrifts wasting fortunes! His mind, I’m afraid, is not fitted for law. He can talk well, though; and if we could make him a barrister he might plead cases that were carefully prepared for him.”

In November 1825, soon after Oscar had taken his new clerkship and was about to pass his licentiate examination, a new clerk arrived to fill the place vacated by Oscar’s promotion.

This fourth clerk, Frédéric Marest, intended to enter the magistracy and was in his third year at the law school. A fine young man of twenty-three, he inherited about twelve thousand francs a year from a bachelor uncle, and was the son of Madame Marest, widow of a wealthy wood-merchant. Desiring to understand procedure and train to be a head-clerk in two years, he had placed himself in Desroches’ office to study; by thirty he hoped to be “procureur du roi.” Though Frédéric was cousin-german to Georges Marest, Oscar did not connect him with the grandson of Czerni-Georges.

“Messieurs,” said Godeschal at breakfast, “I announce the arrival of a new jurisconsult; and as he is rich, rishissime, we will, I hope, make him pay a glorious entrance-fee.”

“Forward, the book!” cried Oscar, nodding to the youngest clerk, “and pray let us be serious.”

The youngest clerk climbed to the top shelf and produced a register covered with dust. We must explain this perennial joke of legal offices. In a clerical life where work is the rule, amusement is treasured; a hoax is a delightful rarity. The gravest clerk, at times, craves fun and quizzing, and a set of clerks will seize any hoax with keen appetite. This instinct explains Georges Marest’s behaviour in the coucou; the denizens of a studio and of a lawyer’s office are in this line superior to comedians.

When Desroches bought a practice without clients he began a new dynasty, breaking the usual customs of receiving new-comers. Having taken an office where legal documents had never been scribbled, he bought new tables and white boxes edged with blue. His staff, clerks from other offices without mutual ties, were surprised to find themselves together. Godeschal, who had apprenticed under maître Derville, would not allow the precious tradition of the “welcome” to be lost. This “welcome” is a breakfast every neophyte gives to the ancients of the office.

About the time Oscar arrived, during the first six months of Desroches’ installation, on a winter evening when work had been finished early and the clerks warmed themselves before the fire, Godeschal conceived and composed a Register “architriclino-basochien,” of great antiquity, contrived to appear genuine and saved from the fires of the Revolution. The work, approved by the clerks, began with a search through dealers in old paper for a proper register—paper with an eighteenth-century mark, bound in parchment, stamped like an order in council. The book was left about to gather mold and wear; the gilt edges were tarnished with surprising perfection. Then entries were forged. The first sixty pages were filled with fictional reports of receptions in an eighteenth-century legal style, as though the clerical guild of Basoche had been continuous since 1525.

The Register opened with a mock solemnity: an eighteenth-century-styled invocation to Sainte-Geneviève, an assertion that since 1525 the clerks of this Practice had existed under her protection, and a declaration by clerks of a predecessor’s office that they renewed and continued the register and the archives. Names and dates—Malin, Grévin, Athanase Féret, Jacques Héret, Regnault de Saint-Jean-d’Angély, Bedeau—were appended, and an account of a long breakfast at Courtille that lasted until seven the next morning.

Expertly engrossed, it contained twenty-seven reports of receptions of neophytes, the last in 1792. After a blank of fourteen years the register began again in 1806 with Bordin’s appointment as attorney before the first Court of the Seine. A deed proclaimed the reconstitution of the kingdom of Basoche, crediting Sainte-Geneviève and maître Bordin for the miraculous preservation of the archives, and recording clerks’ gratitude with a breakfast and theatre visit. The entries mixed legal formality with rollicking conviviality—detailed praise for dinners, wines, and the master’s generosity—creating the illusion of continuous clerical tradition.

Under date June 1822, when Desroches took the oath, Godeschal’s declaration appeared, boasting of recovering the famous archives architriclino-basochien, seeking out a document dated 1786 connected with other proofs going back to 1525 and celebrating the “worship” of the Goddess Bottle and good living. Godeschal recorded a breakfast at the “Cheval Rouge,” recounting twelve bottles of twelve different wines, melons, and dishes, and thanked his sister Mariette, a celebrated dancer, for providing orchestra seats. The page bore stains of wine, pâtés, and candle-grease.

Oscar’s own pretended reception, recorded with mock solemnity, lampooned his humble home repast with exaggerated praise: radishes, gherkins, anchovies, butter and olives for hors-d’œuvre; a “succulent soup of rice” credited to maternal solicitude and fine stews; a tongue of beef with tomatoes; a compote of pigeons; a timbale of macaroni with chocolate custards; and a dessert of eleven dishes, all washed down with sixteen bottles of choicest wines, Roussillon and Rhône outdoing Champagne and Burgundy. The statutes of their Order were parodied too: candidates must limit the magnificence of their welcome to the length of their purse; Madame Clapart’s liberality merited hurrahs. The entry purported to be signed by all the clerks.

Three clerks had already been deceived by the Book, and three real “receptions of welcome” were falsely recorded in it. After each arrival the little sub-clerk placed the Archives Architriclino-Basochiennes on the new-comer’s desk; the clerks enjoyed the look on his face as he studied its facetious pages. Inter pocula each candidate learned the secret of the farce, and the revelation inspired him to hoax his successor.

We see why Oscar, now a participator in the hoax, cried, “Forward, the book!”

Ten minutes later a handsome young man with a fine figure and pleasant face presented himself, asked for Monsieur Desroches, and gave his name without hesitation.

“I am Frédéric Marest,” he said, “and I come to take the place of third clerk.”

“Monsieur Husson,” said Godeschal, “show monsieur his seat and tell him about the customs of the office.”

The next day the new clerk found the register on his desk. He read a few pages, began to laugh, said nothing to the assembled clerks, and laid the book down again.

“Messieurs,” he said at five o’clock, “I have a cousin who is head-clerk of the notary Maître Léopold Hannequin; I will ask his advice as to what I ought to do for my welcome.”

“That looks ill,” cried Godeschal when Frédéric had gone, “he hasn’t the cut of a novice, that fellow!”

“We’ll get some fun out of him yet,” said Oscar.

The next day at two a young man entered the office whom Oscar recognized as Georges Marest, now head-clerk to the notary Hannequin.

“Ha! here’s the friend of Ali Pacha!” cried Oscar.

“Hey! you here, Monsieur l’ambassadeur!” answered Georges, recognizing him.

Godeschal asked if they knew one another.

“We got into a scrape together about two years ago,” Georges said. “I had to leave Crottat and go to Hannequin.”

“What was it?” asked Godeschal.

“Oh, nothing,” replied Georges at Oscar’s signal. “We tried to hoax a peer of France and he bowled us over. So you want to jockey my cousin, do you?”

“We jockey no one,” said Oscar with dignity, producing the famous register and pointing to a sentence of banishment passed on a refractory in 1788.

Georges laughed, leafed through the archives, and then announced a fête. “Tomorrow (Sunday) you are bidden to the Rocher de Cancale at two. Afterwards I’ll take you to spend the evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, where we’ll play cards and you’ll see the elite of the women of fashion. Therefore, gentlemen of the lower courts,” he added with notarial airs, “you will have to behave yourselves, and carry your wine like the seigneurs of the Regency.”

The office cheered. Desroches emerged from his private room.

“What’s all this about?” he asked. Seeing Georges, he suspected a plan to demoralize his clerks. He opened his cash-box and gave Oscar five hundred francs. “Go to the Palais and get from the registrar a copy of the decision in Vandernesse against Vandernesse; it must be served to-night if possible. I promised Simon twenty francs. Wait if the copy is not ready. Don’t let Derville stick a spoke in our wheel; Count Félix de Vandernesse is more powerful than his brother, our client, the ambassador. Keep your eyes open; if there’s the slightest hitch come straight back.”

Oscar set off resolved to distinguish himself in his first entrusted affair as second clerk.

After Georges and Oscar left, Godeschal probed the new clerk about the supposed marquise. Frédéric, cool and grave like a king’s attorney, sustained his cousin’s hoax. By his manner he convinced the office that the marquise might indeed be a Mexican-born widow of a Spanish grandee, courted by rich Georges. “She loves to laugh, sing, drink like me,” he whispered, quoting Béranger. “Georges is very rich: he inherited eighteen thousand francs a year and with twelve thousand from an uncle he has thirty thousand. He pays his debts and gives up the law. He hopes to be Marquis de las Florentinas; the young widow can give her title to her husband.”

Though some clerks remained skeptical, the promise of a Rocher de Cancale breakfast and a fashionable evening put them in high spirits.

In truth the Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos was simply Mademoiselle Agathe‑Florentine Cabirolle, first danseuse at the Gaîté, whom uncle Cardot had taken up. After Madame Cardot’s death Cardot encountered Florentine leaving Coulon’s dancing-class when she was about thirteen. Charmed, he followed her home, learned she was a portress’s daughter, and soon installed mother and daughter in a modest apartment in rue de Crussol with mahogany furniture, carpets, a proper kitchen, and a monthly pension. Cardot, the generous patron, sent her to Vestris and watched her debut at sixteen in a melodrama’s ballet. He raised her stipend to five hundred francs; though no longer adored as an angel, he remained a “friend for life,” a second father.

From 1820 to 1823 she moved among celebrated dancers—Mariette, Tullia, Florine, and Coralie. Cardot, now older, indulged a semi‑paternal devotion; he kept her close so he could still sing “Mère Godichon.” Over the years he saved eighty thousand francs. Expecting that Florentine would soon need the luxuries of a theatrical star, he had recently spent forty‑five thousand francs fitting up the former apartment of the late Coralie for her. The dance star’s lodgings on rue de Vendôme were magnificently furnished; she began to give dinners, spent three hundred francs a month on dress, kept a carriage, and had servants. An engagement at the Opera was in prospect. Cardot, who had been master of his household, now found himself under a capricious rule, but he loved his charge and intended to leave her a hundred thousand francs.

Georges Marest, with thirty thousand francs a year and a handsome face, courted Florentine. A danseuse always wants a young man to take her out—dinners, boxes, drives, wines; the passion for such a star costs trifles to the favored mortal. Georges, newly wealthy after his uncle’s death, stopped thinking of becoming a notary. Frédéric, by contrast, persisted in pursuing public office.

The clerks, sons of poor parents unused to the great world, dressed up to attend Georges’ breakfast and the promise of meeting the Mexican marquise. Oscar felt a mix of resentment toward Georges for a prior slight—he still recalled Moreau having flung him at the count’s feet—but he eagerly prepared to display his new clothes. “What luck,” he told Godeschal, “my mother had just made me a new coat, trousers, waistcoat; I have six frilled shirts!” Madame Clapart appeared with cravats and praised Godeschal as a model. Godeschal warned Oscar not to blunder as he had the previous night; Desroches had been furious that the judgment was not obtained. Godeschal had gone to the Palais himself at six that morning and secured a promise the copy would be ready by seven.

Madame Clapart, proud of her son’s appointment and savings, had given him a gold watch and told him Cardot promised money for a conscript substitute. She also brought a pocket‑book from their friend Moreau. Oscar insisted on carrying the five hundred francs Desroches gave him to fetch the judgment; his mother, alarmed, urged leaving it with Godeschal, but the latter had already gone.

Oscar lounged on the boulevards until Georges’ breakfast. Proud in his new clothes—blue waistcoat with palm pattern, black cashmere trousers, black coat, and a saved gilt‑top cane—he enjoyed prospects of the gay world. He tried to keep his aversion for Georges in check—an instinct to avoid those who had once injured him—but when at half‑past two he entered the Rocher de Cancale salon he forgot it. Present were an old captain of dragoons named Giroudeau, Finot the journalist who might secure Florentine’s Opera engagement, and du Bruel the author and friend of Tullia. Georges made himself charming.

“You’ve taken to private diplomacy,” he said. “What difference between a lawyer and an ambassador? Ambassadors are attorneys of peoples. If I can ever be useful to you, let me know.”

Oscar confessed Georges had once done him great harm. “Pooh!” said Georges. “It was Monsieur de Sérizy who behaved badly. His wife—I wouldn’t have her; and the count, Minister of State and peer of France, has a small mind. I don’t care a fig for him.” Oscar welcomed such slurs, which diminished his own fault in his eyes.

The feast stretched long; courses took hours, dessert did not appear until eight. The wines were excellent. The clerks ate and drank like men newly freed from privation. Godeschal, after signing the report of the meal, left them to the wines and liqueurs. By half‑past ten Oscar was so intoxicated Georges packed him into a coach and sent him home, but ten others, also drunk, determined to walk to Florentine’s house. Only Georges, Giroudeau, du Bruel, and Finot could keep pace; the rest were carried by wines and carriages Georges sent for, and after an hour’s drive round the boulevards they returned by Bercy and the quays to rue de Vendôme.

Florentine’s salon sparkled with stage princesses who, knowing of Frédéric’s joke, imitated the women of good society as they ate ices. Wax candles burned in candelabra; footmen in full livery served dainties on silver; Lyonnaise hangings and carpets dazzled the newcomers. Through Georges’ artful presentation and their own intoxication, the clerks believed in the Marquise de las Florentinas. Gold glittered on card‑tables in the bed‑chamber; in the salon women played vingt‑et‑un, kept by the celebrated Nathan.

Presented to Florentine by Georges, Oscar at first did not recognize the dancer he had seen at the Gaîté in this aristocratically décolletée and lace‑swaddled figure. She received him with gracious manners, then took him to a vingt‑et‑un table and introduced him to the pretty Fanny Beaupré, who had just succeeded at the Porte‑Saint‑Martin.

“My dear,” said Florentine, “allow me to present a charming youth whom you can take as a partner in the game.”

“Ah! that will be delightful,” smiled the actress. “I am losing. Shall we go shares, monsieur?”

Oscar sat, ashamed to mix his small silver coins with the actress’s gold. She put up five gold pieces; he produced his hundred francs in silver. In ten throws the actress lost the two hundred francs. She became banker; Florentine lent her five hundred francs obtained from Georges. Nathan had won twelve hundred francs, and the actress whispered to Oscar to bring them luck.

Tempted by the opportunity and the atmosphere, Oscar took out the five hundred francs Desroches had given him. The first throws brought gains—two thousand francs—but a few turns later the gains vanished. Oscar wanted to feign illness and escape, but “honor” held him. Three more throws lost their stake. He drank iced punch; then, in a fit of desperation and hope, he accepted Florentine’s offer when she lent him a thousand francs to win back what he’d lost, advising him not to risk more than five hundred. She gave him the money to help him, yet the brigands Finot and Giroudeau won it from him. The company laughed over the “poor innocent.”

By dawn, after “character dancing,” Florentine, tired, went to bed, oblivious to Oscar still asleep in the boudoir.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About eleven the next morning a tremendous noise woke the unfortunate clerk. Hearing his uncle Cardot’s voice, he feigned sleep and buried his face in the yellow velvet cushions where he had passed the night.

“Really, my little Florentine,” said the old gentleman, “this is neither right nor sensible; you danced last evening in ‘Les Ruines,’ and you have passed the night in an orgy. That’s deliberately going to lose your freshness. Besides, it was ungrateful to inaugurate this beautiful apartment without even letting me know. Who knows what has been going on here?”

“Old monster!” cried Florentine. “Haven’t you a key that lets you in at all hours? My ball lasted till five in the morning, and you have the cruelty to come and wake me at eleven!”

“Half-past eleven, Titine,” Cardot corrected meekly. “I came out early to order a dinner fit for an archbishop at Chevet’s. Just see how the carpets are stained! What sort of people did you have here?”

“You needn’t complain, for Fanny Beaupré told me you were coming to dinner with Camusot, and to please you I’ve invited Tullia, du Bruel, Mariette, the Duc de Maufrigneuse, Florine, and Nathan. So you’ll have the four loveliest creatures ever seen behind the foot-lights; we’ll dance you a ‘pas de Zéphire.’”

“It is enough to kill you to lead such a life!” cried Cardot. “And look at the broken glasses! What pillage! The antechamber actually makes me shudder—”

He stopped, magnetized. Through the open door of the boudoir he saw the outline of a man in a black coat.

“Ah, Mademoiselle Cabirolle!” he said.

“Well, what?” she asked.

Florentine’s eyes followed Cardot’s; then, seeing the clerk, she burst into fits of laughter that confused Cardot, and Oscar was compelled to rise; Florentine took him by the arm, still pealing at the conscience-stricken faces of uncle and nephew.

“You here, nephew?”

“Nephew? so he’s your nephew?” cried Florentine, with another burst of laughter. “You never told me about him. Why didn’t Mariette carry you off?” she said to Oscar, who stood petrified. “What can he do now, poor boy?”

“Whatever he pleases!” said Cardot sharply, starting toward the door as if to go.

“One moment, papa Cardot. You will be good enough to get your nephew out of a scrape into which I led him; for he played his master’s money and lost it, and I lent him a thousand francs to win it back, and he lost that too.”

“Miserable boy! you lost fifteen hundred francs at play at your age?”

“Oh, uncle, uncle!” cried Oscar, plunged into despair, dropping on his knees, hands clasped. “It is twelve o’clock! I am lost, dishonored! Monsieur Desroches will have no pity! He gave me the money for an important affair, in which his pride was concerned. I was to get a paper at the Palais in the case of Vandernesse versus Vandernesse! What will become of me? Oh, save me for the sake of my father and aunt! Come with me to Monsieur Desroches, and explain it to him; make some excuse—anything!”

These words burst out through sobs that might have moved even a sphinx.

“Old skinflint!” said the danseuse, crying. “Will you let your own nephew be dishonored—the son of the man to whom you owe your fortune? His name is Oscar Husson. Save him, or Titine will deny you forever!”

“But how did he come here?” asked Cardot.

“Don’t you see that the reason he forgot to go for those papers was because he was drunk and overslept? Georges and his cousin Frédéric took all the clerks in his office to a feast at the Rocher de Cancale.”

Père Cardot looked at Florentine and hesitated.

“Come, come,” she said, “you old monkey, wouldn’t I have hidden him better if there had been any other business?”

“There, take your five hundred francs, you scamp!” said Cardot to his nephew, “and remember, that’s the last penny you’ll ever get from me. Go and make it up with your master if you can. I’ll return the thousand francs which you borrowed of mademoiselle; but I’ll never hear another word about you.”

Oscar, not wishing to hear more, disappeared. Once in the street, he knew not where to go.

Chance which destroys men and chance which saves them were both at work that morning. When Mariette reached home that night, alarmed at what might happen to the youth in whom her brother took interest, she wrote a hasty note to Godeschal, telling him what had happened to Oscar and enclosing a bank bill for five hundred francs to repair his loss. She charged her maid to carry the note to Desroches’ office before seven o’clock. Godeschal, rising at six and finding Oscar absent, guessed what had occurred. He took the five hundred francs from his own hoard and rushed to the Palais, where he obtained a copy of the judgment and returned in time to lay it before Desroches by eight o’clock.

Desroches, who rose at four, was in his office by seven. Mariette’s maid, not finding her mistress’s brother in his bedroom, came down to the office and met Desroches, to whom she naturally offered the note.

“Is it about business?” he said; “I am Monsieur Desroches.”

“You can see, monsieur,” replied the maid.

Desroches opened the letter and read it. Finding the five-hundred-franc note, he went into his private office, furious with his second clerk. About half-past seven he heard Godeschal dictating to the second head-clerk a copy of the document in question, and a few moments later the good fellow entered his master’s office with an air of triumph.

“Did Oscar Husson fetch the paper this morning from Simon?” inquired Desroches.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“Who gave him the money?”

“Why, you did, Saturday,” replied Godeschal.

“Then it rains five-hundred-franc notes,” cried Desroches. “Look here, Godeschal, you are a fine fellow, but that little Husson does not deserve such generosity. I hate idiots, but I hate still more the men who will go wrong in spite of the fatherly care which watches over them.” He gave Godeschal Mariette’s letter and the banknote which she had sent. “You must excuse my having opened it,” he said, “but your sister’s maid told me it was on business. Dismiss Husson.”

“Poor unhappy boy! what grief he has caused me!” said Godeschal. “That tall ne’er-do-well of a Georges Marest is his evil genius; he ought to flee him like the plague; if not, he’ll bring him to some third disgrace.”

“What do you mean by that?” asked Desroches.

Godeschal briefly related the affair of the journey to Presles.

“Ah! yes,” said the lawyer, “I remember Joseph Bridau told me that story about the time it happened. It is to that meeting that we owe the favor Monsieur de Sérizy has since shown in the matter of Joseph’s brother, Philippe Bridau.”

At this moment Moreau, to whom the Vandernesse case was important, entered the office. The marquis wished to sell the land in parcels and the count opposed such a sale. The land-agent therefore bore the first fire of Desroches’ wrath against his ex-second clerk and all the threatening prophecies which he fulminated against him. The result was that this sincere friend and protector of the unhappy youth came to the conclusion that his vanity was incorrigible.

“Make him a barrister,” said Desroches. “He has only his last examination to pass. In that line, his defects might prove virtues, for self-love and vanity give tongues to half the attorneys.”

Meanwhile Clapart, who was ill, was being nursed by his wife—a painful, unrewarded duty. The sick man tormented her; impoverished and crafty, he stung his wife with malicious teasing. By the way she boasted of Oscar whenever he obtained the slightest success, Clapart knew the extent of her secret uneasiness and took pains to rouse it whenever he could.

“Well, Madame,” Clapart would say, “Oscar is doing better than I even hoped. That journey to Presles was only a heedlessness of youth. Where can you find young lads who do not commit such faults? Poor child! he bears his privations heroically! If his father had lived, he would never have had any. God grant he may know how to control his passions!”

While these events occurred in the rue de Vendôme and the rue de Béthisy, Clapart, wrapped in an old dressing-gown, watched his wife in their bedroom as she prepared the family broth, Clapart’s “tisane,” and her own breakfast.

“Mon Dieu! I wish I knew how the affair of yesterday ended. Oscar was to breakfast at the Rocher de Cancale and spend the evening with a marquise—”

“Don’t trouble yourself! Sooner or later you’ll find out about your swan,” said her husband. “Do you really believe in that marquise? Pooh! A young man who has senses and a taste for extravagance like Oscar can find such ladies as that on every bush—if he pays for them. Some fine morning you’ll find yourself with a load of debt on your back.”

“You are always trying to put me in despair!” cried Madame Clapart. “You complained that my son lived on your salary, and never has he cost you a penny. For two years you haven’t had the slightest cause of complaint against him; here he is second clerk, his uncle and Monsieur Moreau pay all expenses, and he earns, himself, a salary of eight hundred francs. If we have bread to eat in our old age we may owe it all to that dear boy. You are really too unjust—”

“You call my foresight unjust, do you?” replied the invalid, crossly.

Just then the bell rang. Madame Clapart ran to open the door, and remained in the outer room with Moreau, who had come to soften the blow which Oscar’s new folly would deal to the poor mother’s heart.

“What! he gambled with the money of the office?” she cried, bursting into tears.

“Didn’t I tell you so, hey?” said Clapart, appearing like a spectre at the salon’s door where curiosity had drawn him.

“Oh! what shall we do with him?” said Madame Clapart, whose grief made her impervious to Clapart’s taunts.

“If he bore my name,” replied Moreau, “I should wait composedly till he draws for the conscription, and if he gets a fatal number I should not provide him with a substitute. This is the second time your son has committed a folly out of sheer vanity. Well, vanity may inspire fine deeds in war and may advance him in the career of a soldier. Besides, six years of military service will put some lead into his head; and as he has only his last legal examination to pass, it won’t be much ill-luck for him if he doesn’t become a lawyer till he is twenty-six; that is, if he wants to continue in the law after paying, as they say, his tax of blood. By that time, at any rate, he will have been severely punished, he will have learned experience, and contracted habits of subordination. Before making his probation at the bar he will have gone through his probations in life.”

“If that is your decision for a son,” said Madame Clapart, “I see that the heart of a father is not like that of a mother. My poor Oscar a common soldier!--”

“Would you rather he flung himself headforemost into the Seine after committing an dishonorable action? He cannot now become a solicitor; do you think him steady and wise enough to be a barrister? No. While his reason is maturing, what will he become? A dissipated fellow. The discipline of the army will, at least, preserve him from that.”

“Could he not go into some other office? His uncle Cardot has promised to pay for his substitute; Oscar is to dedicate his graduating thesis to him.”

At that moment carriage-wheels were heard, and a hackney-coach containing Oscar and all his worldly belongings stopped before the door. The luckless young man came up at once.

“Ah! here you are, Monsieur Joli-Cœur!” cried Clapart.

Oscar kissed his mother and held out a hand to Moreau, which Moreau refused. Oscar replied with a reproachful look the boldness of which he had never shown before. Then he turned on Clapart.

“Listen to me, monsieur,” said the youth, transformed into a man. “You worry my poor mother devilishly, and that’s your right, for she is, unfortunately, your wife. But as for me, it is another thing. I shall be of age in a few months; and you have no rights over me even as a minor. I have never asked anything of you. Thanks to Monsieur Moreau, I have never cost you one penny, and I owe you no gratitude. Therefore, I say, let me alone!”

Clapart, hearing this apostrophe, slunk back to his sofa. The reasoning and inward fury of the young man, who had just received a lecture from his friend Godeschal, silenced the imbecile mind of the sick man.

“A momentary temptation, such as you yourself would have yielded to at my age,” said Oscar to Moreau, “has made me commit a fault which Desroches thinks serious, though it is only a peccadillo. I am more provoked with myself for taking Florentine of the Gaieté for a marquise than I am for losing fifteen hundred francs after a little debauch in which everybody, even Godeschal, was half-seas over. This time, at least, I’ve hurt no one but myself. I’m cured of such things forever. If you are willing to help me, Monsieur Moreau, I swear to you that the six years I must still stay a clerk before I can get a practice shall be spent without—”

“Stop there!” said Moreau. “I have three children, and I can make no promises.”

“Never mind, never mind,” said Madame Clapart to her son, casting a reproachful glance at Moreau. “Your uncle Cardot—”

“I have no longer an uncle Cardot,” replied Oscar, relating the scene at the rue de Vendôme.

Madame Clapart, feeling her legs give way, staggered to a chair and fell as if struck by lightning.

“All the miseries together!” she said, and fainted.

Moreau took her in his arms and carried her to bed. Oscar remained motionless, as if crushed.

“There is nothing left for you,” said Moreau when he returned, “but to make yourself a soldier. That idiot of a Clapart looks to me as though he couldn’t live three months, and then your mother will be without a penny. Ought I not, therefore, to reserve for her the little money I am able to give? It was impossible to tell you this before her. As a soldier, you’ll eat plain bread and reflect on life such as it is to those who are born into it without fortune.”

“I may get a lucky number,” said Oscar.

“Suppose you do, what then? Your mother has well fulfilled her duty towards you. She gave you an education; she placed you on the right road, and secured you a career. You have left it. Now, what can you do? Without money, nothing; as you know by this time. You are not a man who can begin a new trade by taking off your coat and going to work in your shirt-sleeves with the tools of an artisan. Besides, your mother loves you, and she would die to see you come to that.”

Oscar sat down and no longer restrained his tears. At last he understood this language, so completely unintelligible to him since his first fault.

“Men without means ought to be perfect,” added Moreau, not suspecting the profundity of that cruel sentence.

“My fate will soon be decided,” said Oscar. “I draw my number the day after to-morrow. Between now and then I will decide upon my future.”

Moreau, deeply distressed in spite of his stern bearing, left the household in the rue de la Cerisaie to its despair.

Three days later Oscar drew the number twenty-seven. In his interest the former steward of Presles had courage to go to the Comte de Sérizy and ask for his influence to get Oscar into the cavalry. It happened that the count’s son, having left the École Polytechnique rather low in his class, had been appointed, as a favor, sub-lieutenant in a regiment of cavalry commanded by the Duc de Maufrigneuse. Oscar was thus, through the Comte de Sérizy’s intercession, drafted into that regiment, with the promise of promotion to quartermaster within a year. Chance had placed the ex-clerk under the command of the son of the Comte de Sérizy.

Madame Clapart, after languishing for several days, became a victim to remorse. She attributed the sorrows of her second marriage and the misfortunes of her son to retribution for the pleasures of her youth. This belief hardened into conviction. The poor woman went, for the first time in forty years, to confess herself to the Abbé Gaudron, vicar of Saint-Paul’s, and took up devotion. But so ill-used and loving a soul as Madame Clapart’s could never be anything but simply pious. The Aspasia of the Directory wanted to expiate her sins in order to draw down God’s blessing on Oscar, and she henceforth vowed herself to works of the purest piety. She believed she had won heaven’s attention when she saved the life of Monsieur Clapart, who, thanks to her devotion, lived on to torture her; yet she chose to see his tyranny as a trial inflicted by One who loveth while He chasteneth.

Oscar meantime behaved so well that by 1830 he was first sergeant of the company of the Vicomte de Sérizy, which gave him the rank of sub-lieutenant of the line. Oscar Husson was by that time twenty-five. As the Royal Guard, to which his regiment was attached, was always in or near Paris, he came to see his mother from time to time and tell her his griefs; he had sense enough to see he could hardly become an officer as matters then were. The cavalry grades were being taken up by the younger sons of noble families, and men without titles found promotion difficult. Oscar’s sole ambition was to leave the Guards and be appointed sub-lieutenant in a regiment of the line. In February 1830 Madame Clapart obtained this promotion for her son through the influence of Madame la Dauphine, granted to the Abbé Gaudron, now rector of Saint-Pauls.

Although Oscar outwardly professed devotion to the Bourbons, in the depths of his heart he was a liberal. Therefore, in the struggle of 1830, he went over to the side of the people. This desertion, significant because of the crisis, brought him into public notice. During the triumphal excitement in August he was promoted lieutenant, received the cross of the Legion of Honor, and was attached as aide-de-camp to La Fayette, who made him captain in 1832. When La Fayette was removed from command of the National Guard, Oscar, whose devotion to the new dynasty amounted to fanaticism, was appointed major of a regiment sent to Africa at the time of the Prince-Royal’s first expedition. The Vicomte de Sérizy chanced to be lieutenant-colonel of this regiment. At the affair of the Makta, where the field had to be abandoned to the Arabs, Monsieur de Sérizy was left wounded under a dead horse. Oscar, discovering this, called to the squadron:

“Messieurs, it is going to death, but we cannot abandon our colonel.”

He dashed upon the enemy, and his electrified soldiers followed him. The Arabs, astonished by this furious return, allowed Oscar to seize the vicomte, whom he flung across his horse and carried off at full gallop, receiving two slashes from yataghans on his left arm.

Oscar’s conduct was rewarded with the officer’s cross of the Legion of Honor and promotion to lieutenant-colonel. He took most affectionate care of the Vicomte de Sérizy, whose mother met him on the regiment’s arrival at Toulon, where, as we know, the young man died of his wounds.

The Comtesse de Sérizy had not separated her son from the man who had shown him such devotion. Oscar himself was so seriously wounded that the surgeons whom the countess had brought from Paris deemed it best to amputate his left arm.

Thus the Comte de Sérizy was led not only to forgive Oscar for his painful remarks on the journey to Presles, but to feel himself indebted to him on behalf of his son, now buried in the chapel of the château de Sérizy.

Years after the Makta affair, on a May morning beneath the porte-cochere of the Lion d’Argent in the rue de Faubourg Saint-Denis, an old lady in black, leaning on a man of about thirty-four—bronzed, missing an arm, wearing a Legion of Honor rosette as a retired officer—waited for Pierrotin’s diligence. Pierrotin, master of the Oise line, would scarcely have recognized in this soldier the Oscar Husson he once escorted to Presles. Madame Husson, now a widow and transformed by piety, was hardly recognizable either. Clapart, the idle lounger whose presence had led to the earlier disaster, was dead; his death had, in a way, served his wife by removing his evil influence. She now received a pension of fifteen hundred francs a year.

The coach, well kept and roomy, could hold nineteen passengers; Pierrotin, still brisk though about fifty-six, supervised porters as they stowed luggage. Oscar told Pierrotin two places in the interieur were taken under the name of his servant Bellejambe, whom he said had reserved them. Pierrotin guessed he was the new collector of Beaumont, replacing Monsieur Margueron’s nephew. The officer, wishing temporarily to remain unknown, pressed his mother’s arm to forestall her speaking.

A voice from the street made Oscar start: Georges Marest hailed, asking if there was a seat. Pierrotin rebuked him for his familiar tone. By voice alone, Oscar would not have recognized Georges. Once elegant, Georges was almost bald, with tufts above his ears, a pear-shaped belly, blotched skin and dull, vinous eyes; his clothes were shabby if pretentiously arranged, and a battered cane and mismatched finery hinted at a desire to display former rank despite decay.

Georges asked about seats; Pierrotin answered the coupe was taken by a peer of France, the Baron de Canalis, his wife, and mother-in-law, leaving only one place in the interieur. Georges, recalling past adventures with the Comte de Serizy, accepted the interieur. He glanced at Oscar and his mother but did not recognize them: Oscar’s African bronzing, pronounced moustache and whiskers, strict dress, missing arm and Legion rosette would have concealed his identity; Madame Clapart’s religious austerity hid the Aspasia of 1797.

Pere Leger, an enormous old man in plain but good clothes and clearly a millionaire, came along and received Pierrotin’s respectful greeting. Georges jeered at his own past follies; Leger corrected his address and the two traded remarks about change and fortune. An old man named Reybert joined, followed by Joseph Bridau, the celebrated painter, whose fame and bearing were now evident in his dress and bearing; neither Georges nor Oscar recognized him at first.

A clerk read the passenger list: the Canalis party in the coupe; Bellejambe, two places in the interieur; de Reybert, three; Georges gave his name lowly at the clerk’s request. The rotunde filled with nurses, country folk, and shopkeepers; four young men were sent to the imperiale. The coach moved off, climbing the faubourg Saint-Denis, racing to Saint-Denis, then taking the road through the valley of Montmorency.

At the turn into Montmorency, Georges broke the silence, joking about speed and showing a silver watch. Leger corrected his title; Georges boasted of having “made the revolution of July,” admitting that it ruined him. Joseph Bridau teased him about new campaigns; conversation turned to familiar names and fortunes. Leger noted Pierrotin had become a major entrepreneur of the Oise coaches, owning a hotel where all diligences stopped and a helpful wife and daughter.

They spoke of the old circle: Mistigris, now famed as Leon de Lora; the youth who had offended the count with foolish talk; the count himself, driven yet fragile—Bridau feared the count’s last trip would be from Presles to Isle-Adam for Bridau’s marriage. Reybert reported the count’s wife visited once a month and had recently married her niece to a rich Pole, the Comte Laginski. Madame Clapart asked who would inherit Monsieur de Serizy’s property; Georges said the count’s wife would take it and bury him. Leger teased Georges about illusions; Georges defended the countess’s elegance.

Conversation passed to Moreau, the steward the count had dismissed, now deputy from the Oise and owner of the estate of Pointel between Presles and Beaumont, something Georges called “bad taste.” Monsieur de Reybert warned Georges to speak softly: Madame Moreau and her daughter, the Baronne de Canalis, were in the coupe. Georges speculated on what dowry could have induced the great orator to marry Moreau’s daughter; Leger named “something like two millions.” Georges sneered that the orator always liked millions.

Oscar interrupted to reproach Georges for speaking ill in a public conveyance. Joseph Bridau glanced at Oscar, then recognized his rosette.

“Monsieur is not an ambassador, but his rosette tells us he has made his way nobly,” Bridau said, noting that he and General Giroudeau had named him in their reports. Georges then recognized Oscar.

“Faith! if it hadn’t been for your voice I should never have known you,” Georges exclaimed. Reybert recalled that Oscar had rescued Vicomte Jules de Serizy from the Arabs and that the count had obtained for him the collectorship at Beaumont as a stepping stone to Pontoise. Oscar confirmed this. Bridau invited Oscar to his marriage at Isle-Adam; Oscar accepted, asking whom Bridau would marry. Bridau answered: Mademoiselle Leger, Pere Leger’s granddaughter, the marriage arranged by the count to secure the painter’s future.

Georges, eager for assistance, declared himself fortunate to have chosen that day to traverse the valley; he announced he worked for a recently formed company called “l’Espérance,” whose statutes were approved by royal ordinance. He explained it provided dowries to girls after ten years, annuities to old men, education for children—handling fortunes and appointing agents across France. Leger wryly likened it to an insurance company; Georges corrected him, calling himself the inspector-general charged with finding honest correspondents and agents, a delicate and difficult task.

Pierrotin and Oscar joked; Georges curtly replied to Oscar’s question about how he’d lost his thirty thousand a year with, “As you lost your arm,” to which Oscar returned a bitter, sarcastic remark. Georges admitted he had too many shares to sell.

They halted at Saint-Leu-Taverny to change horses and for passengers to disembark briefly. Oscar admired Pierrotin’s agility as he unhooked traces and tended the horses. Oscar introduced his mother to Pierrotin, who confessed he wouldn’t have known them and remarked that Africa darkens the skin. Madame Moreau de l’Oise, alighting from the coupe, heard the name and glanced disdainfully at Oscar and his mother. Oscar’s attempt to be gracious to Pierrotin was one of the last blunders vanity would lead him to commit: two months after settling in Beaumont-sur-Oise, Oscar began courting Pierrotin’s daughter, Georgette, whose dowry was one hundred and fifty thousand francs, and he married her in late winter, 1838.

The narrative remarks that the Presles journey had taught Oscar discretion; his disaster at Florentine’s card-party had strengthened his honesty; military hardships taught him the social hierarchy and the acceptance of his lot. He became prudent and capable, happy enough. The Comte de Serizy, before his death, secured him the collectorship at Pontoise; with the influence of Monsieur Moreau de l’Oise, the Comtesse de Serizy and the Baron de Canalis, he later obtained a receiver-generalship, and the Camusot family recognized him as a relation.

Oscar is described finally as a commonplace, gentle, modest man who keeps to a middle course like his government, exciting neither envy nor contempt: the modern bourgeois.
