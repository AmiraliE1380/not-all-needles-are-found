DEDICATION

To Laure.

A START IN LIFE

CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS

Railroads will soon make certain trades and travel habits obsolete; the Scene’s elements will become relics, and future generations will be curious about the old social fabric. The once-popular “coucous” of 1830 were already nearly extinct by 1842, surviving only at occasional suburban spectacles. Even in 1820 many celebrated districts around Paris lacked regular stage-coach services.

The Touchards, father and son, held a near-monopoly on travel within a forty-five-mile radius from their base in the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis. Despite resources and legal rights, they faced fierce competition from local coucous on short routes. Parisians’ fondness for the countryside allowed small local enterprises—often run by innkeepers intimately familiar with their districts—to thrive. These proprietors offered rapid, convenient service and lower prices, bending or ignoring permits and passenger limits; travelers preferred familiar coachmen and would delay journeys to ride with them. Eventually omnibuses, carrying more passengers more efficiently, would supplant many coucous.

One contested line ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, lucrative because of stops at Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice; in 1822 it supported three competing services. The Touchards lowered fares and improved coaches, but rivals persisted, extending to places like Chambly and Beauvais. By contrast, the road to Isle-Adam—famous for quarries and history—was less speculative in 1822 and served by only two coaches. Its terminus discouraged competition, granting Pierrotin and his partner a virtual monopoly. Even travelers on the main road to the Cave preferred their familiar vehicles to the often-overcrowded Beaumont coaches.

Pierrotin, in his forties, had taken over his father’s coucou service after being discharged from the cavalry in 1815. He married an innkeeper’s daughter, expanded the business, and combined intelligence with a military precision that shaped his conduct. His appearance suited his trade: sturdy boots, bottle-green trousers, and a blue blouse with bright embroidery. Habit made him respect social order, but his work caused him to regard passengers chiefly as cargo.

Aware that the industry was changing, Pierrotin sought to modernize. He ordered a larger coach from a respected maker to meet rising demand. His existing fleet included an inherited yellow-and-blue coucou that officially seated six but often carried more, and a four-wheeled coach that could hold seventeen though legally limited to fourteen. The four-wheeler was noisy and instantly recognizable, announced by locals long before it arrived. Pierrotin paid taxes only on the coucou, skirting regulation via a special permit for the four-wheeler.

On a Saturday morning in autumn 1822, Pierrotin stood outside the Lion d’Argent watching other coaches depart and fretting over the lack of passengers and parcels. He lamented his financial strain: he still owed a thousand francs to finish paying for the new coach. Though hopeful about its promise, he considered asking Monsieur Moreau, the steward at Presles, for a loan.

A footman arrived seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. Pierrotin seized the chance, hoping the fare would ease his debts. The footman explained the count had countermanded a carriage for reasons not given, implying secrecy. Pierrotin sensed the importance of the visit, felt both excitement and concern for Moreau—whom he respected—and, urged by the footman’s admonitions to conceal the count’s identity, resolved to ensure a smooth, discreet journey despite his uneasy finances and the uncertain fate of his new coach.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descended from the notable President Huguet, ennobled under François I, and bore a coat of arms split gold and black with a motto that played on modesty and the estate’s name. His father had been a president of parliament before the Revolution. The count himself became a councillor of State at twenty-two, noted for diplomatic memoranda. He did not emigrate during the Revolution but remained at Serizy, protected by his father’s reputation. After his father’s death in 1794 he joined the Council of Five Hundred to distract himself from grief. Following 18th Brumaire he gained the First Consul’s favor, rejoined the Council of State, and reorganized a disordered department, helping shape the administrative framework under Napoleon.

In 1806, at forty, he married the widow of a prominent Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. By 1814, worn by relentless labor and ill health, he resigned; though Napoleon viewed him with some suspicion, he retained status and later won Louis XVIII’s trust, who made him a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days he remained loyal to the Bourbons and stayed at Serizy. After Napoleon’s final fall he resumed duties as privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing public affairs without seeking praise. He rose early, worked tirelessly, and received many honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor. Despite public recognition, he led a life of quiet dedication, marked by a deeper, unspoken sorrow.

The count had long been passionately in love with his wife; the feeling persisted despite marital strains. His constant work distracted him from heartache, and he gave her considerable freedom, treating her with respect and admiration. She appreciated some of his qualities but was sometimes ungrateful, which wounded him; only occasionally did she soothe his pain.

A pressing matter then confronted him: a farm encroaching on the Serizy estate, Presles. The farm, leased by a tenant named Léger from a local burgher, Margueron, was coming up for sale as the lease expired. The count wanted to buy it to end ongoing disputes and instructed his notary and lawyer to prepare for quick negotiations, stressing prompt action to avoid losing the opportunity.

His steward, Moreau, had served faithfully for seventeen years. Moreau’s father had saved the Serizys during the Revolution, and in gratitude the count had helped Moreau avoid a death sentence. Moreau had since married and asked to manage the Presles estate, where he could live comfortably. Initially diligent, Moreau’s loyalty faded after securing his post; he began accepting bribes and engaging in questionable dealings.

As the count prepared to visit Presles, Moreau plotted to profit from the sale, having already arranged a lucrative deal with Léger to secure funds for his family. Moreau’s conduct, however, provoked the ire of a neighboring retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, who sought revenge for perceived slights. Madame de Reybert approached the count, alleging Moreau’s dishonesty and an attempt to defraud him in the purchase, and provided particulars. Though the count at first suspected Madame de Reybert’s motives, his doubts about Moreau mounted. He decided to investigate, instructing his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert back to Presles and to meet him there. Thus the count’s incognito journey, meant as a straightforward purchase, became charged with intrigue and the threat of betrayal by those he trusted.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier, he saw a woman and her son at the Lion d’Argent looking for him. The woman, in a faded black gown and an old shawl, was anxious—a devoted mother sending her son Oscar out alone for the first time. Her attire suggested sacrifice; she was eager to entrust him to Pierrotin’s care.

Oscar, in a short-sleeved coat and patched trousers, was eighteen and still under his mother’s sway. Madame Clapart fretted that he might soil his gloves and worried aloud about his trip to Monsieur Moreau. Pierrotin knew the family; he often carried goods for Moreau, who maintained a discreet, intimate connection with Madame Clapart, though Pierrotin did not know all the particulars.

They lived modestly on the third floor amid old furniture and signs of poverty, but Madame Clapart retained a regal air. Her husband, Clapart, a government clerk, earned little; the household subsisted partly on Moreau’s occasional help. Oscar was the center of her world, and she remained blind to his faults, which his stepfather’s neglect only worsened.

Moreau had once been close to Madame Husson—Madame Clapart in her better days. After misfortunes, including her first husband’s suicide, she married Clapart, who failed to fulfill her hopes. When Moreau returned to France he secured a scholarship for Oscar, yet the family’s condition stayed precarious.

Reared on memories of his mother’s past glories, Oscar developed vanity and entitlement. Unaware of his own limits, he worried Moreau, who suggested the young man spend time with the steward as a test of his potential. Madame Clapart’s constant reminders about propriety intensified Oscar’s discomfort as he prepared to leave.

At departure she fussed over him, packing food and offering last-minute counsel. He felt humiliated by her attentiveness, especially before two young men who mocked him. Their laughter stung; he longed to free himself from his mother’s affection.

Those two, better dressed and more confident, deepened his insecurity. Boarding the coach, Oscar tried to ignore their jests while grappling with feelings of inferiority.

A shabby young artist and his lively apprentice, Mistigris, arrived and enlivened the scene; their banter and ease contrasted with Oscar’s self-consciousness and deepened his sense of isolation.

The Comte de Serizy’s entrance caused a stir. Disguised as a bourgeois, the count sought anonymity while navigating the coach’s social order. Pierrotin, eager to please him, rearranged seating and drew complaints from other passengers. Tension charged the compartment as they waited.

When the coach set off, the passengers’ interactions revealed varied origins and motives. Beneath the surface, Oscar’s struggle with identity and self-worth continued to simmer, setting the stage for the journey ahead.

Those who travel in public conveyances often remain silent at first, using the time to observe and settle in; conversation usually breaks only when the most talkative person speaks, especially in France where social interaction is encouraged. The text sketches national types: Englishmen reserved, Germans somber, Italians cautious, Spaniards rarely using public transport, Russians hampered by poor roads. In France the atmosphere is livelier, and talk flows freely among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever, lively young man, quickly sized up his fellow passengers, judged himself superior, and set out to amuse himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis, he experimented with identities and chose to present himself as a disguised Russian prince or a soldier returned from the East. When Mistigris complained about dust, Georges joked and steered the conversation toward supposed travels in the Levant, claiming to have served under Ali, pacha of Janina, and inventing a scare about a plague that alarmed the count, who asked the driver to stop. He added that he had fought in the 1813 campaign and at Waterloo, where he said he was promoted to captain.

The count asked about decorations; Georges answered he did not wear them in travel. When the count produced his own honors, Georges professed admiration for the Emperor and continued with tales of Egypt and an alleged harem presented to him by Ali Tebelen, though he dismissed harem life as uninteresting. Arriving at Saint-Denis, the group disembarked at a tavern famed for its cheese-cakes, where the count, suspicious, discovered in a portfolio that Georges was actually Crottat’s second clerk. This revelation made the count doubt Georges’s narratives and motives.

Meanwhile Georges impressed others—especially Pere Léger and Oscar—by paying for their treats and ordering drinks, cultivating the pose of someone important. Mistigris, a modest painter, introduced himself. The count observed how easily Georges bought admiration and tried to elevate his status.

As the journey resumed, Georges continued spinning accounts of Eastern oddities: lack of police, peculiar farming practices, heavy taxation under the pacha that left little for peasants, and farmers bent on subsistence rather than cultivation. The count and Mistigris discussed the implications and drew parallels to French conditions, while the count remained skeptical of Georges’s authenticity.

Georges’s stories grew more elaborate, culminating in a dramatic love affair with a beautiful Greek woman named Zena in Dalmatia. He recounted danger from her jealous husband, a pirate, and a narrow escape from execution after the husband was poisoned. The tale—full of peril and romance—captivated the group; Oscar was especially envious of Georges’s purported adventures. Mistigris added his own anecdotes, lending humor and variety.

Throughout, the coach’s atmosphere became lively, with laughter, banter, and storytelling warmed by drink. Georges’s confidence and charisma won admiration from many travelers, even as the count remained wary and alert to contradictions. The journey continued as a weave of tales, camaraderie, and intermittent skepticism, the passengers drawn together by shared narrative and the entertaining performance of identity.

Pierrotin’s coach came down into Saint-Brice around half past one, where he usually stopped an hour to rest his horses. The innkeeper greeted them. Père Léger, the fat farmer, said he would break a crust, while Georges, ever theatrical, twirled his cane and ordered a good breakfast. Georges then produced a straw case and lit a pale cigar, asking if anyone smoked. Oscar, puffing to appear at ease, accepted, though he coughed and showed he did not know how to smoke. Schinner sniffed at the cheap cigars—“ten-sous cigars”—and Georges explained they were remnants from Spain. Schinner, who had already eaten at the Lion d’Argent, declined breakfast.

Oscar, inwardly calculating how little his clothes and carriage matched his ambitions, felt uneasy as Georges showed off his extravagances—Alicante cheese, expensive cigars, likely a lavish breakfast. Mistigris joked that Georges would spend ten francs at least, and that “sharp stomachs make short purses.” Georges proposed a bottle of Bordeaux with Père Léger; Oscar, noting the expense, muttered about the cost and sat on a stone post, unaware that his trousers exposed mismatched stockings. Mistigris noticed and teased; Schinner displayed a similar mismatch, and the count laughed, envying their casual air.

Conversation turned to Les Moulineaux and finances. The innkeeper asked Père Léger if he intended to buy it after going to Paris for the money; Léger answered quietly, “I’ve done for him.” The innkeeper offered to lend twenty thousand francs if needed and reported that Monsieur Margueron had been invited to dine with the Comte de Serizy at Presles. Père Léger laughed that they had ways to thwart such plans. He explained that if the count secured the ministry he might appoint Monsieur Margueron’s son, but Léger had the King—he would give Moreau forty thousand of his pictures on coins of the realm, which would enable him to buy Les Moulineaux outright for two hundred sixty thousand francs in cash before the count could act. The innkeeper and Pierrotin argued the farm’s value: Les Moulineaux brought in six thousand francs and at a lease of seven thousand five hundred for eighteen years it was a solid investment. If Moreau received ten thousand francs from the count, he could make fifty thousand; the count, they reasoned, would not mind since he was rich and perhaps intended to live at Presles if he was spending two hundred thousand restoring it.

Pierrotin came out to water the horses, and the count, fearing gossip, drew him aside. He reminded Pierrotin of the ten louis he had promised to secure his silence and added an inducement: a thousand francs the next day for a new coach if he continued to conceal the count’s name and avoided going into the inn. Pierrotin promised compliance, assuring the count it concerned Père Léger. Pierrotin urged the farmer to hurry so they would not be late; he had a fish to deliver by three to Stors for a great dinner and could not delay customers. Père Léger suggested harnessing the horse Pierrotin wanted to sell into the cabriolet so they could breakfast and then overtake Pierrotin. To everyone’s surprise, Pierrotin rebridled his horses and drove off; Schinner and Mistigris had already walked on.

As the coach rolled toward Moisselles, Georges praised the landscape with theatrical enthusiasm. Schinner, who had traveled more widely, dismissed such provincial charm. Georges offered his remaining cigars to be finished by Schinner; Père Léger and the count stayed silent but consenting. Oscar, offended at being referred to as a “little young man,” declared he was not an aide-de-camp and that he would likely travel to the East in time, though the career his family intended for him would spare him certain annoyances before then. The others mocked him; Mistigris crowed and made his indignation seem absurd.

When Georges asked Oscar what career his family had destined for him, Oscar replied “diplomacy.” This provoked laughter from Schinner and Mistigris, and even the count smiled at the conceit. Georges observed that Oscar’s mother apparently did not fit the figure of an ambassadress; Oscar corrected him: his mother ran their household. The group capitalized on Oscar’s pretension to amuse themselves. Schinner teased that well-bred people spoke of “households,” and the count praised the company ironically: a famed painter, a future general, and a young diplomat destined to recover Belgium for France. Oscar tried to assert dignity with a proverb but was counseled that proverbs alone would not make a diplomat.

The conversation turned to Oscar’s upbringing. He named his tutor, the Abbé Loraux, now vicar of Saint-Sulpice, and boasted that his family would further the fortunes of clerical acquaintances like the Abbé Frayssinous. The count asked about the abbé and then about Oscar’s destination. Oscar said he was going to the Château de Presles to see the Comte de Serizy, claiming to be a comrade of the count’s son and frequently riding with him. Surprise rippled among the others: Père Léger wondered whether the count was at Presles, Schinner asked how often Oscar saw him, and Mistigris muttered witticisms. The count, hearing this, said he wanted information about Monsieur de Serizy for a matter that would cost little; he hoped Oscar could provide it.

Oscar, eager to display insider knowledge, advised the count not to approach the Comte de Serizy for help but his wife, who, he claimed, was the count’s real power because the count was disfigured by a stubborn skin disease. He described treatments the count took—sulphur and steam baths, an iron box fashioned by a valet—mocking the count’s brittle, parchment-like condition and suggesting that no one could blame the countess for finding diversions elsewhere. He proposed that the Marquis d’Aiglemont, a former admirer of Madame de Serizy, would be the man to approach to secure both her and her husband’s favor. The young man’s talk grew increasingly intimate and indiscreet. Schinner and Mistigris chided him for speaking so publicly of someone’s private life.

The count, hearing his name and the countess’s, interrupted sharply and forbade any word against the Comtesse de Serizy. He declared himself a friend of her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, and warned that anyone who disparaged the countess must answer to him. Then, with a certain hauteur, he insisted that he knew people—ministers, architects, and court officials—and that he prevented unworthy men from receiving honors. He added, with a glance toward Georges, that he did not wear decorations but ensured they went properly. He then asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn so he could step out.

Pierrotin hurried through Moisselles. The short distance to the next stop passed in silence. Drawing Pierrotin aside in the inn-yard, the count asked about the lad who claimed intimacy with Presles. Pierrotin disclosed that the young man was the son of a poor woman in the rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, for whom Pierrotin often delivered the game and fruit from Presles. The count learned that the youth was not a noble at all but a steward’s or concierge’s son. He was going to be left on the road near Maffliers, telling Pierrotin he did not wish to go on to Isle-Adam.

The others, overhearing this, felt exposed as though caught in a lie; they dared not meet each other’s eyes. Mistigris mocked the predicament as “suffering for licence’s sake.” Oscar, mortified, insisted he did know the count; Georges dryly replied that even if he had, Oscar would never make an ambassador because people must learn to talk without saying anything when in public conveyances—words meant to impress but not to reveal realities.

The coach continued in silence, then passed through the Carreau woods. Mistigris muttered proverbs in mock consolation. Oscar asked about the Château de Franconville and was scolded for ignorance; the count teased that Oscar knew men, not castles. Oscar, eager for recognition, demanded his name be remembered: “I am Oscar Husson, and ten years hence I shall be famous,” he declared, then flopped back, embarrassed. The count, amused, explained with a touch of irony the family’s stamped name—Husson de la Cerisaie—suggesting noble pretensions. The hill of La Cave descended toward the great château of Presles.

Before they reached the chateau, the count gave his friends parting comments. He wished the colonel reconciliation with the restored Bourbon monarchy, praised Schinner’s rising fame while warning of his domestic perils, and congratulated Père Léger on his enterprise in taking the Comte de Serizy’s property, urging him to be resolute. He paid a civil, slightly condescending compliment to Oscar: he had secrets of statesmen and could make them tremble. Then the count stepped down at an iron gate and told Pierrotin to set him out and to pick him up at the same place the next day. He left the coach amid the company’s confusion.

Père Léger and the others speculated that the count had hired Franconville, noting the path to it. Schinner swore never again to blague in a public coach, blaming Mistigris, who shrugged that fortune mocked him as usual. Georges warned Oscar privately that he would not envy him if the passenger had indeed been the Comte de Serizy, for Oscar’s indiscreet talk could come back to haunt him. Oscar, remembering his mother’s cautions, turned pale.

Pierrotin announced their arrival at a fine iron gate. The others feigned either knowledge or surprise; Georges quickly claimed he was actually going on to Les Moulineaux to avoid looking foolish. Père Léger revealed he was the farmer at Les Moulineaux, welcoming the colonel to his house. Oscar told Pierrotin to leave his things at the steward’s; he intended to go straight to the chateau, plunging down a narrow path until Père Léger called out that he must go through the little gate to reach the forest entrance properly. Reluctantly directed, Oscar entered the grand courtyard.

Père Léger watched as Georges slipped away when he realized Père Léger occupied Les Moulineaux, in effect revealing that Georges had been heading there after all. At Pierrotin’s request the iron gates opened, and he proudly drove in to deliver Schinner’s parcels. Oscar was surprised to learn that Mistigris and his master would actually be staying in the chateau. In ten minutes Pierrotin had unloaded the painter’s packages, Oscar’s bundles, and a small leather portmanteau, which he handed to the concierge’s wife. He then drove off, cracking his whip, beaming with the sly satisfaction of a peasant who has calculated his profit: the next day he expected a thousand francs from the count and, with that, a magnificent new coach.

Oscar, uneasy, hid behind trees in the courtyard and watched Monsieur Moreau emerge from the guard-room in a blue overcoat with a riding whip. Moreau greeted him warmly and introduced himself to Mistigris and his master, the two painters sent by architect Monsieur Grindot. He told the concierge to show them to their rooms, invited them to dinner at five, and mentioned hunting on the estate.

As Moreau led Oscar through the park, he sent his son Jacques ahead to tell Madame Moreau of their arrival. Stern in appearance but kindly, Moreau impressed Oscar, who felt small beside him. Moreau urged Oscar to learn to ride and hunt despite his inexperience. Oscar’s mother had permitted only a two-week stay because Madame Moreau was present, yet Moreau seemed intent on extending it.

The steward’s house, built before the Revolution, stood in a large garden and adjoined the chateau. It contained a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir furnished to Madame Moreau’s taste. The Moreaus lived comfortably on estate provisions with little expense. Madame Moreau, who had once been a maid, kept an air of elegance and worked to be seen as mistress of the chateau, often patronizing villagers.

Tension with the Reyberts stemmed from a past slight when Madame de Reybert had reminded Madame Moreau of her former status. The Moreaus had advance notice of the painters’ arrival, and Madame Moreau was eager to impress them, hoping the artist she hosted would raise her social standing.

On the day the painters were due, Madame Moreau dressed elegantly, but the Paris coach brought only Mistigris’s packages, leaving her disappointed. The two artists—Mistigris and Bridau—eventually arrived; Madame Moreau welcomed them and professed a love of art. The painters quickly recognized her pretentiousness and decided to humor her, engaging in conversation about art.

Their talk was interrupted when a maid announced the count’s arrival, and Madame Moreau grew flustered. Oscar, feeling out of place, was summoned by his younger brother and chastised for his appearance. Mistigris made a joke about Oscar’s future, heightening the awkwardness. The maid returned saying the count wanted dinner promptly, adding to the household’s bustle.

The count had arrived unexpectedly and was distressed to learn Moreau was absent. He had overheard remarks suggesting Moreau’s disloyalty, prompting a confrontation. The count expressed disappointment that Moreau had discussed private matters, especially in front of Oscar. Moreau tried to explain but ultimately faced the consequences of his indiscretions.

As the count prepared for dinner, guests including notaries and the Reyberts arrived, sensing tension and the count’s displeasure. Mistigris’s light remark about the count’s appearance briefly eased the mood.

Moreau returned home distraught over the loss of his position and the implications of Oscar’s careless words. Estelle, his wife, was furious and grasped the gravity of their predicament. Moreau confronted Oscar, demanding an explanation; Oscar remained silent, overwhelmed by fear and shame. In anger, Moreau dragged him back to the count to apologize. The count dismissed Oscar’s behavior and doubted his worth. Humiliated, Moreau took Oscar home to contemplate the consequences and the uncertain future of their family.

While the horses were being harnessed, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart in distress about Oscar’s conduct. During a journey Oscar had disclosed sensitive information about Madame de Serizy to a count traveling incognito, which led to Moreau’s dismissal as steward of Presles. The count ordered Oscar sent home immediately, a decision that terrified Moreau and his wife because it threatened the family’s future and Moreau’s hopes of improving their fortunes.

That evening Madame Clapart was knitting when Monsieur Clapart mentioned a friend’s forthcoming visit and voiced doubts about Oscar’s prospects, criticizing the boy’s character. Madame Clapart defended Oscar, insisting on his good nature and potential. Their conversation was interrupted by Brochon’s arrival, who brought Oscar back and delivered a letter from Moreau explaining the situation. Overwhelmed, Madame Clapart fainted on reading the letter, stricken with panic about her son’s prospects.

The next day Madame Clapart sought Oscar alone, her appearance revealing her distress. She reminded him of Moreau’s support and how precarious their situation had become now that he had lost the stewardship. She outlined the few realistic career options remaining and stressed the need for discretion, hard work, and prudence. Oscar remained largely indifferent to the gravity of their circumstances, which frustrated her entreaties.

Madame Clapart pressed Oscar to promise greater discretion and to heed her advice, and proposed visiting Uncle Cardot as a last hope. Uncle Cardot, a self-made businessman who had amassed a fortune and was known for generosity to his children, lived modestly and was unaware of their struggles.

At Uncle Cardot’s he greeted them warmly and, while pleased to hear of Oscar’s academic achievements, remarked that the boy still had much to learn. Moved by Madame Clapart’s account of their hardship, Cardot offered to support Oscar’s legal education and to pay the costs. He urged Oscar to apply himself, maintain integrity, and cultivate discipline, convinced that with effort he could succeed.

Afterward Madame Clapart felt renewed hope, though the family still faced financial strain. Moreau later visited to say he had begun land speculation and could help place Oscar as a pupil in a law office, again stressing that discipline and hard work were essential to repair his reputation and secure his future. Madame Clapart was grateful and urged Oscar to express his gratitude; Moreau pressed him to focus on studies and behavior as the path to improvement.

Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a young solicitor in the rue de Béthisy who had recently set up in a spacious but inexpensive office. Desroches, twenty-six, himself raised in poverty, recognized Oscar’s situation; his gaunt appearance and curt manner intimidated the youth. Seated amid a mountain of papers, he told them, “We work here day and night,” and instructed his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, to supervise Oscar’s training so he would not lose time on the way to law school and would master the Code. Oscar was to live in a small attic next to Godeschal’s room; Desroches declared his ambition to make him a capable clerk.

For two years Oscar lived in the office, surrounded by petty but diligent work. Under Godeschal’s strict eye he kept a monk-like routine: rising at five, running errands, studying the Code under rigorous examination, and returning after classes to more tasks. Meals were plain. His social life was meager — monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot and Sundays with his mother — and although Moreau sometimes took him out, Oscar’s attempts at elegance met the disapproval of his superiors.

Godeschal counseled modest dress and frugality, insisting a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year. He practiced these principles himself, combining honesty and integrity with no pretense. When another clerk failed in his duties, Godeschal discreetly urged him to leave rather than endure dismissal. He sought to shield Oscar from temptation, even occasionally covering the costs of outings to give him some enjoyment, since Oscar’s home life lacked interest. Moreau was supportive but less able than Godeschal to connect with the young man. Both hoped to guide him safely through his formative years.

By twenty, Oscar had become the third clerk, performing the second clerk’s duties without salary but receiving room and board. His knowledge surpassed that of many licensed graduates, and he began to argue cases in chambers. Yet he felt trapped in a life at odds with his desires: he envied the well-dressed young men in the streets and yearned for freedom and pleasure, feelings he often suppressed. Godeschal noticed these tendencies and worried they might lead Oscar astray; he thought Oscar’s talents might suit a barrister better than a solicitor’s clerkship. “Too much vanity,” he commented when Moreau asked how Oscar was getting on.

In November 1825, shortly after Oscar’s promotion, a new clerk arrived: Frédéric Marest, a well-off young man with ambitions for the magistracy who wished to learn practical legal work. Godeschal announced him, hoping Marest would join the office camaraderie. The clerks enjoyed practical jokes; Godeschal maintained a humorous tradition — a fictitious register of receptions for new clerks filled with absurd entries — as a source of amusement and bonding. Desroches’ recently established office lacked such a welcome, but Godeschal was determined to uphold it.

Oscar, settling into his role, became part of the jest and called for the register when Marest arrived. Marest read it and laughed privately, then said he would consult his cousin, a head clerk, about the welcome he should extend — a remark that aroused Godeschal’s suspicions about Marest’s novice status. Despite the lighthearted atmosphere and clerkly camaraderie, Oscar’s internal conflict persisted as he navigated professional pressures and the expectations placed upon him.

The next day at two o’clock Oscar recognized Georges Marest, now head-clerk of notary Hannequin, who entered the office. Georges greeted Oscar, recalling past mischief involving a peer of France that had led to Georges’ transfer to Hannequin. He then invited Oscar and his colleagues to a gathering at the Rocher de Cancale and later to spend the evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, promising a night of high society.

Desroches, the office head, interrupted to instruct Oscar to obtain a crucial legal document from the Palais and warned him to be careful of Derville, who might obstruct their efforts. Oscar was keen to prove himself in this first assignment as second clerk.

After Georges and Oscar left, Godeschal tried to learn more about the Marquise, but Frederic, keeping a serious demeanor, sustained the ruse, suggesting she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. The clerks, excited by the promise of a grand breakfast and fashionable festivities, eagerly anticipated meeting the Marquise.

In truth, the “Marquise” was Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse at the Gaîté, supported by Père Cardot, a merchant who had taken her and her mother under his wing. Cardot had invested in Florentine’s career; she became a celebrated star, living comfortably and preparing for a possible engagement at the Opera.

Georges, with new prosperity, actively courted Florentine, and the clerks, keen to impress, dressed in their best. Oscar’s mother, Madame Clapart, voiced hopes for her son’s advancement and warned him about Georges, whom she blamed for Oscar’s earlier misfortune.

On the day of the gathering, Oscar felt both excitement and apprehension, resolved to enjoy the day while mindful of his mother’s and Godeschal’s advice. After a lavish, boisterous breakfast the clerks, now intoxicated, went on to Florentine’s salon, greeted by dazzling guests and opulence.

As the evening progressed, Oscar was introduced to Florentine, who charmed him with grace and beauty. He was drawn into a game of vingt-et-un with her and the actress Fanny Beaupré, quickly losing the money he had been given for the legal document. Despite reluctance, he gambled more and sank into desperation.

Florentine, noticing his distress, offered him a thousand francs to recover his losses, which he accepted, mistaking her aid for noble kindness. His luck failed; he lost everything and plunged into despair and drunken stupor.

The party continued into the early morning, Florentine and her friends reveling while Oscar remained oblivious to the wider chaos, asleep and unaware of the consequences his actions would bring.

The next morning a loud noise woke clerk Oscar Husson. Hearing his uncle Cardot's voice, he pretended to sleep as Cardot scolded him for late-night revelry and the company he kept. Florentine, a danseuse, laughed and revealed Oscar as Cardot's nephew and urged the uncle to help him. Oscar had lost gambling money that belonged to his employer, Monsieur Desroches. Desperate and ashamed, he begged Cardot for aid, fearing disgrace and the loss of his job.

Cardot reluctantly lent him five hundred francs, warning it would be the last help. Oscar left uncertain. Mariette, worried, sent a note and money; Godeschal, another clerk, rushed to prepare the papers for Desroches. Angered, Desroches resolved to dismiss Oscar despite Godeschal's defense. Godeschal recounted Oscar's past errors and blamed the influence of Georges Marest, a bad companion. Half in jest, Desroches suggested Oscar should try the law, thinking his vanity might fit the bar.

At home, Clapart, Oscar's father, tormented his wife with reminders of their son's failures. Madame Clapart defended Oscar while Clapart remained cynical. When Oscar returned, he confronted his father, asserted his independence, and protested his treatment, but his plea fell on deaf ears.

Determined to take responsibility, Oscar faced the prospect of military service, which Clapart believed would instill discipline. He drew a number for conscription and, despite his fears, hoped for a favorable outcome. Three days later, through the Comte de Serizy's influence, Oscar was assigned to the cavalry. Overwhelmed by their son's misfortunes, Madame Clapart turned to religion, believing past sins had brought trouble and seeking redemption through piety. Oscar applied himself in the army and rose steadily, eventually becoming a lieutenant-colonel.

Years later, a decorated Oscar stood with his mother outside the Lion d’Argent, waiting for a coach. Both were unrecognizable from their former selves. Though haunted by past mistakes, Oscar had achieved success. As they prepared to board, they encountered Georges Marest, now a shadow of his former self, who failed to recognize Oscar. Poorly dressed, Georges tried to preserve dignity despite his decline. Oscar reflected on how far they had diverged since youth.

On the journey, Georges' attempts to impress others with tales of past exploits fell flat. Oscar, confident and composed, defended his achievements and credited his military career for his stability. He was invited to a wedding, signaling his integration into respectable society. He married Georgette Pierrotin, the coach proprietor’s daughter, cementing his social standing. His earlier errors became instructive lessons, guiding him to discretion, honesty, and steady work. Ultimately, discipline, connections, and changed character led to a secure, respected career in a world that once seemed precarious.
