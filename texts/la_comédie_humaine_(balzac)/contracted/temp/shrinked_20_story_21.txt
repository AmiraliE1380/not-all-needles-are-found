A START IN LIFE

CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS

Railroads will soon render certain industries obsolete and alter transportation methods around Paris. The elements of this Scene will soon be seen as archaeological remnants of a bygone era. Our descendants will be intrigued to learn about the social fabric of what they will call the “olden time.” The once-popular “coucous” that flourished in 1830 are nearly extinct by 1842, surviving only during special suburban events like the Grandes Eaux of Versailles. In 1820, many celebrated areas around Paris lacked regular stage-coach services.

The Touchards, father and son, monopolized travel and transportation to towns within a forty-five-mile radius, establishing a significant business in the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis. Despite their established rights and resources, the Touchard coaches faced fierce competition from local coucous for shorter distances. The Parisian love for the countryside allowed local enterprises to thrive against the Touchards, leading to a proliferation of rapid, convenient transport options that sparked intense competition. Ultimately, the coucous struggled against omnibuses, which could carry more passengers efficiently. Today, any remaining coucous might be subjects of scholarly study akin to Cuvier’s research on extinct species.

These smaller enterprises, which had been competing against the Touchards since 1822, often gained favor from local residents. The proprietors were typically innkeepers familiar with the area, allowing them to provide better service and lower prices than the Touchard coaches. They often operated without the need for permits and were willing to bend the rules regarding passenger limits. This earned them the loyalty of the community, leading travelers to delay their journeys to ride with familiar coachmen, even if their vehicles were less reliable.

One of the most contested routes was from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, a lucrative line with three competing services in 1822. Despite the Touchards’ efforts to lower prices and improve their coaches, competition persisted due to the profitability of the route, which included towns like Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice. The Touchards eventually extended their route to Chambly, but competition remained fierce, with rival services reaching as far as Beauvais.

The road to Isle-Adam, a charming town known for its quarries and historical significance, was less competitive in 1822, served by only two coaches. This lack of rivalry stemmed from the road’s geography; no speculative enterprise would divert to Isle-Adam, as it was the terminus of the route. Consequently, Pierrotin and his colleague enjoyed a monopoly on this line, beloved by locals. Their vehicles were well-known, and even those living along the main road to the Cave preferred them over the often-full Beaumont coaches.

Pierrotin, a man in his forties, had taken over his father’s coucou service after being discharged from the cavalry in 1815. He married the daughter of a small innkeeper, expanded the business, and became known for his intelligence and military precision. His appearance reflected his profession: sturdy boots, bottle-green trousers, and a blue blouse adorned with colorful embroidery. His military background instilled in him a respect for social hierarchy, yet his experiences led him to view passengers as mere cargo.

Aware of the changes in his industry, Pierrotin sought to modernize his service. He had ordered a new, larger coach from a reputable manufacturer, necessitated by an increase in travelers. His current fleet included a coucou inherited from his father, which could uncomfortably accommodate more passengers than legally allowed. Despite its limitations, Pierrotin believed that a full vehicle provided a better experience than a half-empty one.

The coucou, painted yellow with blue accents, could carry up to six passengers on its seats, but Pierrotin often squeezed in more. He also had a four-wheeled coach that could hold seventeen, though it was officially limited to fourteen. This vehicle was noisy and easily recognizable, prompting locals to announce his arrival long before he was visible. Pierrotin paid taxes only on the coucou, cleverly circumventing regulations by using a special permit for the four-wheeler.

On a Saturday morning in the autumn of 1822, Pierrotin stood outside the Lion d’Argent, observing the bustling activity as other coaches departed. He was anxious about the lack of passengers and parcels, lamenting his financial struggles. Despite his optimism about the new coach, he faced a pressing debt of a thousand francs needed to complete the payment for it. His desperation led him to consider appealing to Monsieur Moreau, the steward at Presles, for a loan.

Just then, a footman approached, seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. Pierrotin seized the opportunity, hoping to secure a fare that would alleviate his financial woes. The footman revealed that the count had countermanded a carriage for reasons unknown, hinting at a secretive purpose for his journey. Pierrotin, recognizing the potential significance of the count’s visit, felt a mix of excitement and concern for the steward, whom he respected.

As the conversation unfolded, Pierrotin learned more about the count’s intentions and the potential implications for Moreau. The footman’s advice to keep the count’s identity hidden only heightened Pierrotin’s sense of responsibility. He resolved to ensure a smooth journey for the count while grappling with his own financial anxieties and the fate of his new coach.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the notable president Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family’s coat of arms features a party per pale or and sable, with a motto reflecting modesty and a pun on “Serisy,” the estate's name.

The current count's father was a president of parliament before the Revolution. The count himself became a councillor of State at just twenty-two, known for his diplomatic memoranda. He did not emigrate during the Revolution, instead remaining on his estate, Serizy, where his father's reputation shielded him from danger. After his father's death in 1794, he was elected to the Council of the Five Hundred, seeking distraction from his grief. Following the 18th Brumaire, he became a favored figure of the First Consul, appointed to the Council of State and tasked with reconstructing a disorganized department. His active role contributed to the administrative framework established under Napoleon.

By 1806, at age forty, he married the widow of a prominent Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. In 1814, after years of hard work, he resigned due to health issues, and although initially viewed with suspicion by Napoleon, he maintained his status and was later trusted by Louis XVIII, who appointed him a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days, he remained loyal to the Bourbons, spending that time at Serizy.

After Napoleon's second fall, he resumed his role as a privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing public affairs without seeking personal recognition. His life was marked by relentless work, rising early and retiring early, earning numerous honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor. Despite his contributions, he led a life of quiet dedication, driven by a deeper, unspoken sorrow.

The count had long been in love with his wife, a passion that persisted through the challenges of their marriage. His constant labor served as a distraction from his heartache, and he allowed her considerable freedom, treating her with respect and admiration. While she appreciated his qualities, her ingratitude sometimes stung, yet she occasionally soothed his wounds.

The narrative shifts to a pressing matter involving a farm that encroached upon the count's estate, Presles. The farm, leased by a farmer named Leger from a local burgher, Margueron, was up for sale as the lease was expiring. The count sought to purchase it to eliminate ongoing disputes. He had previously instructed his notary and lawyer to prepare for negotiations, emphasizing the need for a swift resolution to avoid losing the opportunity.

The count's steward, Moreau, had served him faithfully for seventeen years. Moreau's father had saved the Serizys during the Revolution, and in gratitude, the count had helped Moreau escape a death sentence. Moreau had since married and requested to manage the Presles estate, where he could live comfortably. Initially diligent, Moreau's loyalty waned after he became secure in his position, leading him to accept bribes and engage in questionable dealings.

As the count prepared to visit Presles, Moreau plotted to profit from the farm sale, having already discussed a lucrative deal with Leger. He hoped to make a significant sum from the transaction, which he intended to use to secure a better life for his family.

However, Moreau's actions had drawn the ire of a neighboring retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, who sought revenge for perceived slights. Madame de Reybert approached the count, claiming that Moreau was dishonest and attempting to defraud him in the farm purchase. She provided details about Moreau's alleged corruption, which made the count reconsider his trust in the steward.

Despite his initial skepticism towards Madame de Reybert's motives, the count's doubts about Moreau grew. He decided to investigate further, instructing his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert back to Presles and to meet with him there. This decision set the stage for a confrontation that would reveal the true nature of Moreau's dealings and the potential danger to the count's interests. 

Thus, the count's incognito journey to Presles, initially intended for a straightforward purchase, became fraught with intrigue and the threat of betrayal from those he trusted.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier, he spotted a woman and her son at the Lion d’Argent, clearly looking for him. The woman, dressed in a faded black gown and an old shawl, appeared anxious and was likely a devoted mother concerned for her son, Oscar, who was traveling alone for the first time. Her attire suggested a life of sacrifice, and she was eager to entrust her son to Pierrotin’s care.

Oscar, dressed in a short-sleeved coat and patched trousers, was a typical young man of eighteen, still under his mother’s influence. As Pierrotin approached, the mother urged Oscar not to spoil his gloves and expressed her worries about his journey to Monsieur Moreau. Pierrotin, familiar with the family, had often transported goods for Moreau, who had a secretive relationship with Madame Clapart, Oscar’s mother. Their exchanges hinted at a deeper connection, though Pierrotin remained unaware of the full story.

Madame Clapart lived modestly in a third-floor apartment, filled with old furniture and signs of poverty, yet she maintained a regal demeanor. Her husband, Clapart, was a government clerk with a meager salary, and their life was sustained by Moreau’s occasional assistance. Oscar was the center of her world, and despite her love, she was blind to his shortcomings, which were exacerbated by his stepfather’s disdain.

Moreau had once been close to Madame Husson, Oscar’s mother, during her more prosperous days. After a series of misfortunes, including her husband’s suicide, she married Clapart, who failed to provide for her as she had hoped. Moreau, upon returning to France, helped her secure a scholarship for Oscar, but the family remained in dire straits.

Oscar’s upbringing was marked by his mother’s past glories, which fueled his vanity and sense of entitlement. He was unaware of his own limitations, and Moreau’s concern for him led to a suggestion that he spend time with the steward to assess his potential. Meanwhile, Madame Clapart’s overprotectiveness and her reminders about propriety only added to Oscar’s embarrassment as he prepared to leave.

As they prepared to depart, Madame Clapart fussed over Oscar, packing him food and giving him last-minute advice. Oscar, eager to escape his mother’s clutches, felt humiliated by her attentiveness, especially in front of two young men who mocked him. Their laughter stung, and he wished for independence from his mother’s affection.

The two young men, better dressed and seemingly more confident, further highlighted Oscar’s insecurities. He envied their appearance and felt a deep sense of inadequacy. As they boarded the coach, Oscar’s discomfort grew, and he tried to ignore their jests while grappling with his own feelings of inferiority.

The arrival of a young artist and his apprentice, Mistigris, added to the lively atmosphere. The artist, dressed in shabby yet artistic clothing, and his companion, full of youthful exuberance, contrasted sharply with Oscar’s self-consciousness. Their banter and carefree attitude only deepened Oscar’s sense of isolation.

As the coach finally prepared to leave, the Comte de Serizy arrived, causing a stir among the passengers. His presence shifted the dynamics, and the young men’s mockery turned to curiosity. The count, disguised as a bourgeois, sought to maintain his anonymity while navigating the social hierarchy within the coach.

Pierrotin, eager to please the count, attempted to balance the seating arrangements, leading to further complaints from the other passengers. The atmosphere was charged with tension as they all awaited departure, each passenger’s personality and background contributing to the unfolding drama.

As the coach finally set off, the interactions among the passengers revealed their diverse backgrounds and motivations. Oscar’s internal struggle with his identity and self-worth continued to simmer beneath the surface, setting the stage for the journey ahead.

Those who travel in public conveyances often remain silent at first, using the time to observe one another and settle in. This silence typically ends when the most talkative member of the group initiates conversation, especially in France, where social interaction is encouraged. In contrast, other cultures have different customs; for instance, Englishmen rarely speak, Germans are somber, Italians are cautious, Spaniards lack public transport, and Russians have poor roads. In France, however, the atmosphere is lively, and conversation flows freely, especially among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever and lively young man, quickly assessed his fellow passengers. He deemed himself superior and decided to entertain himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis, he contemplated various identities he could assume to amuse himself, ultimately deciding to present himself as a disguised Russian prince or a soldier with tales of the East.

During this time, Mistigris, one of the passengers, commented on the dust, prompting Georges to make a joke about it. The conversation shifted to Georges's supposed travels in the Levant, where he claimed to have served under Ali, pacha of Janina. He fabricated a story about having contracted a form of the plague, which alarmed the count, who asked the driver to stop. Georges continued his tale, claiming to have been a soldier in the campaign of 1813 and to have fought at Waterloo, where he was promoted to captain.

The count, intrigued, asked about Georges's decorations, to which Georges replied that he did not wear them while traveling. The count then revealed his own impressive collection of honors, and Georges expressed his admiration for the Emperor Napoleon, claiming to have served under him. The conversation continued with Georges recounting his experiences in Egypt, where he claimed to have been given a harem by Ali Tebelen, though he dismissed the idea of harem life as uninteresting.

As they entered Saint-Denis, the group disembarked at a tavern known for its cheese-cakes. The count, suspicious of Georges's stories, discovered a portfolio belonging to Georges that indicated he was actually Crottat’s second clerk. This revelation led the count to suspect that Georges was not who he claimed to be.

Georges, meanwhile, continued to impress the other passengers, particularly Pere Leger and Oscar, by paying for their treats and ordering drinks. He posed as a significant figure, while Mistigris, a painter, introduced himself modestly. The count, observing the interactions, noted the dynamics among the group, particularly Georges's attempts to elevate his status.

As the journey resumed, Georges continued to weave tales of his adventures, including his time in the East and his encounters with Ali Tebelen. He described the peculiarities of Eastern life, including the lack of law enforcement and the unique farming practices of the region. Georges's stories captivated the group, though the count remained skeptical of their authenticity.

The conversation shifted to the nature of farming in the East, with Georges explaining that the locals were more focused on survival than agriculture. He described the heavy taxation imposed by the pacha, which left little for the farmers. The count and Mistigris engaged in a discussion about the implications of such a system, drawing parallels to their own experiences in France.

As the journey continued, Georges's tales became increasingly elaborate, including a dramatic story about a love affair with a beautiful Greek woman named Zena in Dalmatia. He recounted the dangers he faced from her jealous husband, a pirate, and how he narrowly escaped execution after the husband was poisoned. The group listened intently, with Oscar particularly envious of Georges's adventurous life.

Mistigris interjected with his own experiences, adding humor to the conversation. The count, amused yet skeptical, questioned the plausibility of Georges's stories, while Georges maintained his bravado, insisting that such adventures were not uncommon for someone of his background.

As the travelers shared their stories, the atmosphere in the coach became lively, with laughter and banter flowing freely. Georges's confidence and charisma won over the admiration of his fellow passengers, even as the count remained wary of his true identity. The journey continued with a mix of storytelling, camaraderie, and the occasional skepticism, creating a rich tapestry of interactions among the diverse group.

CHAPTER V. THE DRAMA BEGINS

Pierrotin’s vehicle descended the steep incline into the village of Saint-Brice, where he typically stopped for an hour to rest his horses. It was around half-past one.

“Ha! here’s Pere Leger,” called the innkeeper as the coach arrived. “Do you breakfast?”

“Always once a day,” replied the fat farmer, “and I’ll break a crust here.”

“Give us a good breakfast,” shouted Georges, twirling his cane, which impressed poor Oscar.

Oscar’s admiration turned to jealousy when Georges pulled out a small straw case and lit a light-colored cigar on the inn's threshold.

“Do you smoke?” Georges asked Oscar.

“Sometimes,” Oscar replied, puffing out his chest.

Georges offered the case to Oscar and Schinner.

“Phew! ten-sous cigars!” Schinner remarked.

“The remains of those I brought back from Spain,” Georges said. “Do you breakfast here?”

“No,” Schinner replied. “I’m expected at the chateau. I had something at the Lion d’Argent before starting.”

“And you?” Georges asked Oscar.

“I have breakfasted,” Oscar said, wishing for better clothes. He coughed and grimaced as he tried to smoke.

“You don’t know how to smoke,” Schinner said, demonstrating with ease.

Georges imitated Schinner but failed to exhale properly, making Oscar feel even more inadequate.

Mistigris, noticing Oscar’s discomfort, took his cigar, joking, “You haven’t any contagious diseases, I hope.”

Oscar, annoyed, thought about how Georges spent money extravagantly. “Eight francs for Alicante and the cheese-cakes; forty sous for cigars; and his breakfast will cost him—”

“Ten francs at least,” Mistigris replied. “But that’s how it is. ‘Sharp stomachs make short purses.’”

“Come, Pere Leger, let’s drink a bottle of Bordeaux together,” Georges suggested.

“Twenty francs for his breakfast!” Oscar exclaimed, feeling overwhelmed by the expenses.

Feeling inferior, Oscar sat on a stone post, lost in thought, unaware that his trousers were revealing his mismatched stockings.

“We are brothers in socks,” Mistigris joked, pulling up his own trousers to show a similar situation.

The count, overhearing, laughed at their antics, envying their carefree nature.

“Are you to have Les Moulineaux? I know you went to Paris for the money,” the innkeeper asked Pere Leger, who was inspecting a horse.

“I’ve done for him,” Pere Leger replied quietly.

“Good! I like to see those nobles fooled. If you need twenty thousand francs, I’ll lend them to you,” the innkeeper offered.

“Francois, the conductor of Touchard’s six o’clock coach, told me that Monsieur Margueron was invited by the Comte de Serizy to dine with him today at Presles,” the innkeeper added.

“That was the plan, but we had our own ways of thwarting it,” Pere Leger laughed.

“The count could appoint Monsieur Margueron’s son, and you haven’t any place to give,” the innkeeper reminded him.

“Of course, but if the count has the ministry on his side, I have King Louis XVIII.,” Pere Leger said. “Forty thousand of his pictures on coin of the realm given to Moreau will enable me to buy Les Moulineaux for two hundred and sixty thousand, money down, before Monsieur de Serizy can do so.”

“Well done, bourgeois!” the innkeeper exclaimed.

“Besides, the farm is worth that to him,” Pere Leger added.

“Yes; Les Moulineaux brings in six thousand francs in rental. I’ll take another lease at seven thousand five hundred for eighteen years. It’s an investment at more than two and a half percent. The count can’t complain about that.”

“How much will Moreau make?” the innkeeper asked.

“If the count gives him ten thousand francs for the transaction, it will bring him fifty thousand,” Pere Leger replied.

“After all, the count doesn’t like Presles. He’s so rich, what does it matter what it costs him?” the innkeeper said.

“Nor I,” Pere Leger agreed. “But he must be intending to live there; why else spend two hundred thousand francs restoring the chateau?”

The count listened intently, skeptical about the conspiracy involving his steward.

Pierrotin came out to water his horses. The count, fearing indiscretion, approached him. “I promised you ten louis to keep my secret; if you continue to conceal my name, I’ll give you a thousand francs tomorrow for your new coach. Don’t go in there to breakfast; stay with your horses.”

“Monsieur le comte, I understand you; don’t worry! It relates to Pere Leger, of course,” Pierrotin assured him.

“Make yourself easy,” the count replied.

“Come, hurry,” Pierrotin said, urging the farmer. “We’re late. You can soon overtake me; it will do you good to walk a bit.”

“What a hurry you are in, Pierrotin!” the innkeeper remarked. “Can’t you stay and breakfast? The colonel pays for the wine at fifty sous and has ordered champagne.”

“I can’t. I have a fish to deliver by three o’clock for a great dinner at Stors; there’s no fooling with customers or fish,” Pierrotin insisted.

“Very good,” Pere Leger said. “You can harness that horse you want to sell me into the cabriolet; we’ll breakfast in peace and overtake Pierrotin.”

To the count’s surprise, Pierrotin himself rebridled the horses. Schinner and Mistigris had walked on. As Pierrotin drove down the narrow road to Moisselles, Georges continued to talk with the farmer about the beauty of the hostess at Saint-Brice.

“Upon my word, this landscape is not so bad, great painter, is it?” Georges exclaimed.

“Pooh! you who have seen the East and Spain can’t really admire it,” Schinner replied.

“I’ve two cigars left! If no one objects, will you help me finish them, Schinner? The little young man there seems to have found a whiff or two enough for him,” Georges said.

Pere Leger and the count remained silent, which was taken as consent.

Oscar, furious at being called a “little young man,” retorted, “I am not the aide-de-camp of Mina, monsieur, and I have not yet been to the East, but I shall probably go there. The career to which my family destines me will spare me the annoyances of traveling in a coucou before I reach your present age.”

“‘Et caetera punctum!’” Mistigris crowed, imitating a young cock, which made Oscar’s indignation seem even more absurd.

“Your family, young man, destines you to some career, do they?” Georges asked. “Might I ask what it is?”

“Diplomacy,” Oscar replied.

Laughter erupted from Mistigris, Schinner, and the farmer. Even the count smiled.

“By Allah!” Georges exclaimed, “I see nothing to laugh at in that. Though it seems to me, young man, that your respectable mother is not exactly in the social sphere of an ambassadress.”

“My mother, monsieur!” Oscar exclaimed indignantly. “That was the person in charge of our household.”

“‘Our household’ is a very aristocratic term,” the count remarked.

“Kings have households,” Oscar replied proudly.

Georges suppressed a laugh, signaling to Mistigris and Schinner to manage Oscar cleverly for amusement.

“Monsieur is right,” Schinner said. “Well-bred people always talk of their ‘households’; it is only common persons like ourselves who say ‘home.’”

“I congratulate myself on having traveled with three such distinguished men,” the count said, “a painter already famous, a future general, and a young diplomat who may one day recover Belgium for France.”

Oscar, feeling the need to assert himself, declared, “All is not gold that glitters.”

“That’s not it,” Mistigris corrected. “All is not old that titters. You’ll never get on in diplomacy if you don’t know your proverbs better than that.”

“I may not know proverbs, but I know my way—”

“It must be far,” Georges interjected, “for I saw that person in charge of your household give you provisions enough for an ocean voyage: rolls, chocolate—”

“A special kind of bread and chocolate, yes, monsieur,” Oscar retorted. “My stomach is much too delicate to digest the victuals of a tavern.”

“‘Victuals’ is a word as delicate and refined as your stomach,” Georges quipped.

“Ah! I like that word ‘victuals,’” Schinner chimed in.

“The word is all the fashion in the best society,” Mistigris added. “I use it myself at the cafe of the Black Hen.”

“Your tutor is, doubtless, some celebrated professor, isn’t he?—Monsieur Andrieux of the Academie Francaise, or Monsieur Royer-Collard?” Schinner asked.

“My tutor is or was the Abbe Loraux, now vicar of Saint-Sulpice,” Oscar replied, recalling his school confessor.

“Well, you were right to take a private tutor,” Mistigris said. “‘Tuto, tutor, celeritus, and jocund.’ Of course, you will reward him well, your abbe?”

“Undoubtedly he will be made a bishop some day,” Oscar said.

“By your family influence?” Georges inquired gravely.

“We shall probably contribute to his rise, for the Abbe Frayssinous is constantly at our house,” Oscar replied.

“Ah! you know the Abbe Frayssinous?” the count asked.

“He is under obligations to my father,” Oscar answered.

“Are you on your way to your estate?” Georges asked.

“No, monsieur; but I can say where I am going, if others cannot. I am going to the Chateau de Presles, to the Comte de Serizy.”

“Is Monsieur de Serizy at Presles?” Pere Leger asked, astonished.

“Apparently, as I am going there,” Oscar replied.

“Do you often see the count?” Schinner asked.

“Often,” Oscar said. “I am a comrade of his son, who is about my age, nineteen; we ride together nearly every day.”

“‘Aut Caesar, aut Serizy,’” Mistigris said sententiously.

Pierrotin and Pere Leger exchanged knowing glances.

“Really,” the count said to Oscar, “I am delighted to meet a young man who can tell me about that personage. I want his influence on a rather serious matter, although it would cost him nothing to oblige me. It concerns a claim I wish to press on the American government. I should be glad to obtain information about Monsieur de Serizy.”

“Oh! if you want to succeed,” Oscar advised, “don’t go to him, but go to his wife; he is madly in love with her; no one knows more than I do about that; but she can’t endure him.”

“Why not?” Georges asked.

“The count has a skin disease which makes him hideous. Doctor Albert has tried in vain to cure it. The count would give half his fortune for a chest like mine,” Oscar boasted. “He lives a lonely life; gets up very early and works from three to eight o’clock; after eight he takes his remedies—sulphur-baths, steam-baths, and such things. His valet bakes him in a sort of iron box—he hopes to get cured.”

“If he is such a friend of the King as they say, why doesn’t he get his Majesty to touch him?” Georges asked.

“The count has promised thirty thousand francs to a celebrated Scotch doctor who is coming to treat him,” Oscar continued.

“Then his wife can’t be blamed if she finds better—” Schinner began, but didn’t finish.

“I should say so!” Oscar resumed. “The poor man is so shriveled and old you would take him for eighty! He’s as dry as parchment, and, unluckily for him, he feels his position.”

“Most men would,” Pere Leger said.

“He adores his wife and dares not find fault with her,” Oscar continued, enjoying the attention. “He plays scenes with her that would make you die of laughing—exactly like Arnolphe in Moliere’s comedy.”

The count, horrified, looked at Pierrotin, who assumed Oscar was lying.

“So, monsieur,” Oscar concluded, “if you want the count’s influence, I advise you to apply to the Marquis d’Aiglemont. If you get that former admirer of Madame de Serizy on your side, you will win husband and wife at one stroke.”

“Look here!” Schinner said. “You seem to have seen the count without his clothes; are you his valet?”

“His valet!” Oscar exclaimed.

“People don’t tell such things about their friends in public conveyances,” Mistigris said. “As for me, I’m not listening to you; I’m deaf: ‘discretion plays the better part of adder.’”

“‘A poet is nasty and not fit,’ and so is a tale-bearer,” Schinner added.

“Great painter,” Georges said, “learn this: you can’t say harm of people you don’t know. Now the little one here has proved, indubitably, that he knows his Serizy by heart. If he had told us about the countess, perhaps—?”

“Stop! not a word about the Comtesse de Serizy, young men,” the count interrupted. “I am a friend of her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, and whoever disparages the countess must answer to me.”

“Monsieur is right,” the painter agreed. “No man should blague women.”

“God, Honor, and the Ladies! I believe in that melodrama,” Mistigris added.

“I don’t know the guerrilla chieftain, Mina, but I know the Keeper of the Seals,” the count continued, looking at Georges. “And though I don’t wear my decorations,” he added, glancing at the painter, “I prevent those who do not deserve them from obtaining any. And finally, let me say that I know so many people that I even know Monsieur Grindot, the architect of Presles. Pierrotin, stop at the next inn; I want to get out a moment.”

Pierrotin hurried his horses through the village street of Moisselles, arriving at the inn where all travelers stopped. This short distance was completed in silence.

“Where is that young fool going?” the count asked Pierrotin as he drew him into the inn-yard.

“To your steward. He is the son of a poor lady in the rue de la Cerisaie, to whom I often carry fruit and game from Presles. She is Madame Husson,” Pierrotin explained.

“Who is that man?” Pere Leger asked Pierrotin after the count left.

“Faith, I don’t know,” Pierrotin replied. “This is the first time I have driven him. I shouldn’t be surprised if he was that prince who owns Maffliers. He just told me to leave him on the road near there; he doesn’t want to go on to Isle-Adam.”

“Pierrotin thinks he is the master of Maffliers,” Pere Leger said to Georges when he returned to the coach.

The three young men felt as if they had been caught in a lie; they dared not look at each other, considering the consequences of their fibs.

“This is what is called ‘suffering for license sake,’” Mistigris said.

“You see I did know the count,” Oscar insisted.

“Possibly. But you’ll never be an ambassador,” Georges replied. “When people want to talk in public conveyances, they ought to be careful, like me, to talk without saying anything.”

“That’s what speech is for,” Mistigris concluded.

The count returned to his seat, and the coucou rolled on in silence.

“Well, my friends,” the count said as they reached the Carreau woods, “here we all are, as silent as if we were going to the scaffold.”

“‘Silence gives content,’” Mistigris muttered.

“The weather is fine,” Georges remarked.

“What place is that?” Oscar asked, pointing to the chateau de Franconville, which looked impressive against the backdrop of the Saint-Martin forest.

“How is it,” the count exclaimed, “that you, who say you go often to Presles, do not know Franconville?”

“Monsieur knows men, not castles,” Mistigris replied.

“Budding diplomatists have so much else to take their minds,” Georges added.

“Be so good as to remember my name,” Oscar said angrily. “I am Oscar Husson, and ten years hence I shall be famous.”

After that declaration, Oscar slumped back in his corner.

“Husson of what, of where?” Mistigris asked.

“It is a great family,” the count replied. “Husson de la Cerisaie; monsieur was born beneath the steps of the Imperial throne.”

Oscar blushed deeply, filled with dread.

They were about to descend the steep hill of La Cave, at the foot of which stood the magnificent chateau of Presles.

“Messieurs,” the count said, “I wish you every good fortune in your various careers. Monsieur le colonel, make your peace with the King of France; the Czerni-Georges ought not to snub the Bourbons. I have nothing to wish for you, my dear Monsieur Schinner; your fame is already won, and nobly won by splendid work. But you are much to be feared in domestic life, and I, being a married man, dare not invite you to my house. As for Monsieur Husson, he needs no protection; he possesses the secrets of statesmen and can make them tremble. Monsieur Leger is about to pluck the Comte de Serizy, and I can only exhort him to do it with a firm hand. Pierrotin, put me out here, and pick me up at the same place tomorrow,” the count added before leaving the coach, leaving his companions confused.

“He must be that count who has hired Franconville; that’s the path to it,” Pere Leger said.

“If ever again,” Schinner said, “I am caught blague-ing in a public coach, I’ll fight a duel with myself. It was your fault, Mistigris,” he said, tapping his friend on the head.

“All I did was to help you out, and follow you to Venice,” Mistigris replied. “But that’s always the way, ‘Fortune belabors the slave.’”

“Let me tell you,” Georges said to Oscar, “if, by chance, that was the Comte de Serizy, I wouldn’t be in your skin for a good deal, healthy as you think it.”

Oscar, recalling his mother’s warnings, turned pale.

“Here you are, messieurs!” Pierrotin called as they arrived at a fine iron gate.

“Here we are—where?” the painter, Georges, and Oscar asked simultaneously.

“Well, well!” Pierrotin exclaimed. “If that doesn’t beat all! Ah ca, messieurs, have none of you been here before? Why, this is the chateau de Presles.”

“Oh, yes; all right, friend,” Georges said, regaining his confidence. “But I happen to be going on to Les Moulineaux,” he added, not wanting his companions to know he was actually going to the chateau.

“You don’t say so? Then you are coming to me,” Pere Leger said.

“How so?” Georges asked.

“Why, I’m the farmer at Moulineaux. Hey, colonel, what brings you there?” Pere Leger inquired.

“To taste your butter,” Georges replied, pulling out his portfolio.

“Pierrotin,” Oscar said, “leave my things at the steward’s. I am going straight to the chateau.”

Oscar then plunged into a narrow path, unsure of his direction.

“Hi! Monsieur l’ambassadeur,” Pere Leger called. “That’s the way to the forest; if you really want to get to the chateau, go through the little gate.”

Thus compelled, Oscar entered the grand courtyard. While Pere Leger watched him, Georges, realizing the farmer was the current occupant of Les Moulineaux, slipped away unnoticed.

The iron gates opened at Pierrotin’s request, and he proudly drove in to deposit the various items belonging to Schinner. Oscar was stunned to discover that Mistigris and his master would be staying in the chateau itself. In ten minutes, Pierrotin had unloaded the painter’s packages, Oscar’s bundles, and a small leather portmanteau, which he handed to the concierge’s wife. Then he drove out of the courtyard, cracking his whip, his face beaming with the sly expression of a peasant calculating his profits. Nothing was lacking now to his happiness; tomorrow he would have his thousand francs, and consequently, his magnificent new coach.

Ten days later, Oscar was taken by Monsieur Moreau to Maitre Desroches, a young solicitor in the rue de Bethisy, who had recently set up in a spacious but inexpensive office. For two years, Oscar lived in the office, which was characterized by its petty yet diligent work ethic. He performed office errands and studied diligently, mastering the Code under Godeschal's rigorous examinations. After classes, he returned to the office for more work, with meals consisting of simple fare. His social life was limited to monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot and Sundays with his mother. Moreau occasionally treated him to outings, but Oscar's attempts at elegance were met with disapproval from his superiors.

Godeschal advised Oscar on maintaining a modest wardrobe and living frugally, emphasizing that a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year. By twenty, Oscar became the third clerk, performing the duties of the second without a salary but receiving room and board. His knowledge surpassed that of many licensed graduates, and he began to argue cases in chambers. Despite his progress, Oscar struggled internally, feeling trapped in a life that clashed with his desires. He envied the well-dressed young men he saw on the streets and felt a yearning for freedom and pleasure, which he often suppressed.

Godeschal, aware of Oscar's struggles, sought to shield him from temptation. He often covered the costs of outings to provide Oscar with some enjoyment, as his home life was less stimulating. Moreau, while supportive, could not connect with Oscar as Godeschal did. Oscar's repressed desires and youthful follies posed a risk, but Godeschal and Moreau hoped to guide him safely through his formative years.

“How is he getting on?” Moreau inquired of Godeschal after a trip away.

In November 1825, shortly after Oscar's promotion, a new clerk named Frederic Marest joined the office. Desroches' office, having recently been established, lacked the customary welcome tradition, but Godeschal was determined to uphold it.

Despite the lighthearted atmosphere, Oscar's internal conflict persisted as he navigated the pressures of his profession and the expectations placed upon him.

CHAPTER X. ANOTHER CATASTROPHE

The next morning, a loud noise woke the clerk, Oscar Husson. Recognizing his uncle Cardot's voice, he pretended to sleep, turning his face into the cushions. Cardot scolded him for his late-night revelry and the state of the apartment, expressing concern over the guests Oscar had entertained.

Florentine, a danseuse, laughed at the situation, revealing Oscar's identity as Cardot's nephew. She urged Cardot to help Oscar, who had lost money gambling with funds from his employer, Monsieur Desroches. Oscar, distraught, pleaded for his uncle's assistance, fearing dishonor and the loss of his job.

Cardot reluctantly gave Oscar five hundred francs, warning him it would be the last help he would receive. Oscar left, unsure of where to go, as chance seemed to conspire against him. Meanwhile, Mariette, concerned for Oscar, sent a note and money to help him, while Godeschal, another clerk, rushed to obtain the necessary documents for Desroches.

Desroches, angered by Oscar's actions, decided to dismiss him, despite Godeschal's defense of the young man. Godeschal recounted Oscar's previous misadventures, highlighting the influence of Georges Marest, a bad companion. Desroches suggested making Oscar a barrister, believing his vanity could serve him well in that profession.

At home, Clapart, Oscar's father, tormented his wife with reminders of their son's failures. Madame Clapart, distressed, defended Oscar, but Clapart remained cynical about his son's future. When Oscar returned home, he confronted Clapart, asserting his independence and expressing frustration over his father's treatment.

Oscar's plea for understanding fell on deaf ears, and he resolved to take responsibility for his actions. He faced the prospect of military service, which Clapart believed would instill discipline in him. Oscar drew a number for conscription, and despite his fears, he hoped for a favorable outcome.

Three days later, Oscar was drafted into the cavalry, thanks to the Comte de Serizy's influence. Madame Clapart, overwhelmed by her son's misfortunes, turned to religion, believing her past sins were the cause of their troubles. She sought redemption through piety, while Oscar excelled in his military career, eventually rising to the rank of lieutenant-colonel.

CHAPTER XI. OSCAR’S LAST BLUNDER

Years later, Oscar, now a decorated officer, stood with his mother outside the Lion d’Argent, waiting for a coach. They were unrecognizable from their former selves. Oscar's past mistakes haunted him, but he had found a measure of success. As they prepared to board, they encountered Georges Marest, now a shadow of his former self, who failed to recognize Oscar.

Georges, dressed poorly, attempted to maintain an air of dignity despite his circumstances. Oscar reflected on how far they had both fallen since their youth. The coach departed, and the passengers exchanged stories, revealing their changed lives. Oscar's military service had shaped him, and he had learned valuable lessons from his past.

As they traveled, Georges attempted to impress others with tales of his past, but his efforts fell flat. Oscar, now confident and composed, defended his achievements and expressed gratitude for his military career. He was invited to a wedding, marking a turning point in his life.

Oscar's journey from a reckless youth to a respected officer illustrated the impact of experience and maturity. He married Georgette Pierrotin, the daughter of the coach proprietor, solidifying his place in society. His past mistakes served as lessons, guiding him toward a more stable and honorable life.

Oscar's transformation was complete; he had learned the importance of discretion, honesty, and the value of hard work. His connections and experiences ultimately led him to a successful career, earning him respect and stability in a world that had once seemed so precarious.
