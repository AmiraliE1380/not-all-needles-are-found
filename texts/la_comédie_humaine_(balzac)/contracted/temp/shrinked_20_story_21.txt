**CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS**  

In the near future, railroads will render certain transportation industries around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son duo, monopolized travel within a 45-mile radius but faced stiff competition from local services, particularly the coucous, which thrived on shorter routes. Despite their efforts, the Touchards struggled against smaller competitors who enjoyed local goodwill.

One of the most contested routes was from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, where the Touchards faced three rivals in 1822. The competition was fierce due to the line's profitability, serving several towns and villages. Although the Touchards extended their route to Chambly, competition remained strong.

The road to Isle-Adam, known for its quarries and historical significance, was less competitive, with only two coaches operating harmoniously. Pierrotin, a well-liked coachman, and his rival coordinated their schedules to benefit customers. They operated from the same yard in Paris, the “Lion d’Argent,” where the innkeeper’s wife managed bookings and fares.

On a Saturday morning in 1822, Pierrotin stood outside the inn, anxious about the lack of passengers. He had ordered a new coach to expand his business but was short on funds. Despite his ambitions, he faced financial strain, having exhausted his resources to secure the new vehicle. He hoped to attract more customers with the new coach, designed to rival the Touchards’ offerings.

Pierrotin’s current vehicle, an inherited coucou, was cramped and often overloaded. He planned for a larger, more luxurious coach but needed to raise a thousand francs to complete the payment. As he contemplated his options, a footman approached, seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito.

The footman revealed that the count had countermanded a carriage for unknown reasons, hinting at potential intrigue. Eager to please and hoping for a generous tip, Pierrotin agreed to the arrangement, despite the footman’s warning to keep the count’s identity secret from other passengers.

As Pierrotin prepared for the journey, he reflected on the count’s steward, Monsieur Moreau, a man he respected and who often employed him for errands. The conversation with the footman left Pierrotin curious about the count’s motives for traveling discreetly and considering the implications for Moreau, whom he believed deserved protection.

This chapter sets the stage for a narrative exploring ambition, social dynamics, and the evolving transportation landscape in early 19th-century France, highlighting Pierrotin's struggles and aspirations as he navigates the complexities of his profession and relationships.

**CHAPTER II. THE STEWARD IN DANGER**

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the notable president Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family coat of arms features a party per pale or and sable, with the motto “i, semper melius eris,” reflecting the modesty of burgher families. The current count, a councillor of State at just twenty-two, distinguished himself during the Revolution by remaining on his estate, Serizy, where he was protected by his father's reputation. After his father's death in 1794, he was elected to the Council of the Five Hundred and later became a prominent figure under Napoleon, who appointed him to the Council of State and made him a count and senator.

In 1806, he married the widow of a Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. By 1814, due to health issues, he resigned from his posts and returned to Paris, maintaining loyalty to the Bourbons and refusing Napoleon's peerage during the Hundred Days. After Napoleon's second fall, he resumed his political duties, becoming vice-president of the Council of State and managing foreign claims without seeking personal glory. His life was marked by hard work, rising early and retiring early, earning numerous honors for his service.

Despite his public success, the count suffered privately from unfulfilled love for his wife, who enjoyed considerable freedom and social engagement. He respected her autonomy, allowing her to manage her affairs while remaining devoted to her, even as his own health deteriorated. The count's dedication to work masked his emotional pain, stemming from jealousy and the complexities of their marriage.

The narrative shifts to a pressing matter involving a farm encroaching upon the count's estate, Presles. The lease on this farm, held by a farmer named Leger, was expiring, and the owner, Margueron, refused to renew it. The count sought to purchase the farm to eliminate disputes. He consulted his notary and lawyer, who advised inviting Margueron to dinner to finalize the sale. The count planned to bring part of the purchase money and secure a government position for Margueron's son as part of the deal.

Before this meeting, the count had initiated renovations at Presles, intending to surprise his wife. However, he decided to travel incognito to avoid drawing attention.

Moreau, the steward of Presles, had a complex background. His father had saved the Serizys during the Revolution, and Moreau had been granted a position by the count after escaping a death sentence. Initially loyal and diligent, Moreau's character changed after becoming comfortable in his role, leading him to accept bribes and engage in dishonest dealings. He aimed to profit from the sale of the farm, planning to pocket a significant sum while maintaining the appearance of integrity.

As the count prepared for his trip, Moreau conspired with Leger to inflate the farm's price, hoping to benefit from the transaction. Meanwhile, a retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, and his wife, feeling wronged by Moreau, sought to expose his dishonesty to the count. Madame de Reybert approached the count, claiming that Moreau was scheming to defraud him in the farm purchase.

Though initially skeptical, the count began to doubt Moreau's integrity after reading a letter from the steward that contradicted his assurances. He decided to investigate further, instructing his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert back to Presles and to delay sending his clerk as previously planned.

Thus, the count's incognito journey and the warning from Madame de Reybert set the stage for a confrontation that could jeopardize Moreau's position and the count's interests.

**CHAPTER III. THE TRAVELLERS**

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier, he spotted Madame Clapart and her son Oscar at the Lion d’Argent. The mother, in a worn black gown and old shawl, anxiously searched for him, her appearance reflecting a once-beautiful woman now burdened by life and devoted to her son, who was traveling alone for the first time. Oscar, in a short olive-green coat and patched blue trousers, mirrored their modest means.

“Is this the conductor? Ah! Pierrotin, is it you?” she exclaimed, leaving her son to speak with him privately. She asked Pierrotin to take good care of Oscar, who was heading to Monsieur Moreau’s place. Pierrotin hesitated to voice his concerns about the challenges Oscar would face.

Pierrotin often transported goods for Moreau, who had a discreet arrangement with Madame Clapart, delivering baskets of food to her home, which puzzled him given their poverty. Madame Clapart lived in a modest apartment filled with old furniture but maintained a regal demeanor.

Monsieur Clapart, her husband, was timid yet tyrannical, and their life was marked by hardship. Despite their struggles, Madame Clapart’s unwavering love for Oscar blinded her to his shortcomings. Raised in past splendor, Oscar now faced harsh realities, compounded by his stepfather’s disdain.

Moreau, once close to Madame Clapart, had helped secure a scholarship for Oscar at a prestigious school. However, Oscar’s vanity and lack of self-awareness troubled Moreau, who wished to guide him toward a suitable career.

As they prepared for the journey, Madame Clapart fussed over Oscar, reminding him to avoid discussing servants and to be respectful. Oscar, embarrassed by his mother’s attentiveness, longed for independence and felt humiliated by the presence of two better-dressed young men nearby, who mocked him for his mother’s affection.

The young men, Georges and Amaury, engaged in playful banter, aggravating Oscar’s insecurities. He envied their confidence and style, feeling trapped by his circumstances. As the coach was readied, Pierrotin managed the logistics while the young men continued to tease Oscar, who tried to appear indifferent.

Eventually, the coach filled with passengers, including a stout farmer named Pere Leger and the Comte de Serizy, who arrived last, disguised as a bourgeois to maintain his incognito amidst the youthful mockery.

As the journey began, Georges expressed impatience with Pierrotin’s pace, while Mistigris, a young artist, made light of the situation. The count, overhearing their comments, remained composed, aware of the importance of keeping his identity hidden.

The group’s dynamics shifted as they traveled, with Oscar grappling with feelings of inadequacy and envy, while others engaged in light-hearted exchanges. The journey promised a mix of tension and camaraderie, as each character navigated their social standings and personal aspirations.

**CHAPTER IV. THE GRANDSON OF THE FAMOUS CZERNI-GEORGES**

In public transport, passengers often begin in silence, observing one another until conversation starts, typically initiated by the most talkative. This is particularly true in French stage-coaches, known for their lively banter, unlike the more reserved customs of other nations. Georges, a clever and lively young man, quickly assessed his fellow travelers, considering himself superior and planning to amuse himself at their expense.

As the coach descended from La Chapelle, Georges contemplated various identities to entertain himself, ultimately deciding to pose as a disguised Russian prince. He engaged in playful banter with Mistigris, who was traveling with a count, and the other passengers, spinning tales of his supposed adventures in the East, including serving under Ali, pacha of Janina.

Georges claimed to have been a soldier, embellishing his past with stories of battles and decorations that intrigued the other passengers. He recounted experiences in Egypt and Greece, painting a picture of a life filled with excitement. He mentioned being the grandson of Czerni-Georges, a historical figure who fought against the Ottoman Empire, and described his return to Greece, where he was welcomed by Ali Tebelen.

As the conversation flowed, Georges boasted about his time in a harem, downplaying its significance while expressing a preference for French women. Upon arriving in Saint-Denis, they stopped at a tavern known for its cheese-cakes. Enjoying the attention of his fellow travelers, Georges paid for their treats and continued weaving his elaborate tales.

The count, intrigued yet skeptical, suspected Georges was not who he claimed to be. He discovered a portfolio belonging to Georges, revealing him as Crottat’s second clerk, confirming his suspicions. Meanwhile, Georges continued to impress the others with his stories, even as the count remained wary of his true identity.

As they resumed their journey, Georges shared more about his supposed military exploits, claiming to have played crucial roles in battles and received lavish gifts from Ali Tebelen. He also recounted misfortunes, including losing treasures to a Turkish admiral after being captured.

The count, concerned about Georges’ reckless boasting, warned him about the consequences of speaking too freely about his past, especially regarding foreign intrigues. Undeterred, Georges entertained the group with tales of a dramatic encounter in Dalmatia involving a beautiful woman named Zena and a jealous pirate husband.

Schinner, a painter traveling with the group, chimed in with his own stories of love and danger, recounting complications from his infatuation with Zena. The narrative shifted between Georges and Schinner, both vying for the attention of their fellow travelers while weaving intricate tales of their pasts.

As the journey progressed, the count and others listened with a mix of skepticism and fascination, caught up in the drama and excitement of the stories. Georges and Schinner’s narratives intertwined, creating a tapestry of adventure, romance, and intrigue that captivated their audience, even as the count remained cautious about the truth behind their tales.

Ultimately, the journey served as a backdrop for exploring identity, storytelling, and the allure of adventure, with each character revealing their desires and aspirations through the stories they shared. The interplay of truth and fiction became a central theme as the travelers navigated their realities while being drawn into the fantastical worlds created by Georges and Schinner.

**CHAPTER V. THE DRAMA BEGINS**

Pierrotin's coach arrived in Saint-Brice around 1:30 PM for a routine stop to rest his horses. 

“Ah! Here’s Pere Leger,” the innkeeper called as the coach halted. “Do you breakfast?”

“Always once a day,” replied the farmer, eager to eat.

“Give us a good breakfast!” Georges exclaimed, twirling his cane, which impressed Oscar. However, Oscar's admiration turned to jealousy when Georges lit a cigar from a small case.

“Do you smoke?” Georges asked Oscar.

“Sometimes,” Oscar replied, trying to appear casual.

Georges offered the case to Oscar and Schinner, who noted the cigars were cheap. Schinner declined breakfast, having eaten earlier, while Oscar claimed he had too. He envied Georges's carefree spending, calculating their trip costs.

As they waited, Mistigris joked about their mismatched socks, prompting laughter from the count, who observed the young men with a mix of amusement and envy.

The innkeeper and Pere Leger discussed a potential land deal involving the Comte de Serizy, hinting at a scheme to outmaneuver him. The count overheard, suspecting a conspiracy involving his steward, Moreau.

Fearing indiscretion, Pierrotin promised the count to keep his identity secret in exchange for future payment. He decided not to join the others for breakfast, citing a delivery he needed to make.

Pere Leger suggested harnessing a horse to the cabriolet and having breakfast before catching up with Pierrotin. The count was intrigued by the farmer's plans and potential profits.

As Pierrotin drove away, Georges and the farmer discussed the landscape's beauty, while Oscar, feeling belittled, attempted to assert his future importance in diplomacy. His claims drew laughter from the others, who found his pretensions amusing.

Oscar insisted he was friends with the Comte de Serizy's son, surprising Pere Leger. The count, intrigued, asked Oscar for information about the count, leading to Oscar revealing personal details about the count's life and health, shocking the count.

The conversation turned to the countess, and the count interrupted, defending her honor. He then revealed his connections and left the coach at an inn, leaving the young men confused over their earlier bravado.

As they approached the chateau de Presles, Pierrotin revealed he would drop off the young men at the estate. Oscar, eager to assert his status, rushed ahead, unaware of the path he took. Pere Leger pointed him in the right direction, while Georges slipped away, realizing the farmer's connection to Les Moulineaux.

Pierrotin delivered the packages to the concierge, leaving the young men to contemplate their earlier claims and the reality of their situation. As Pierrotin drove away, he felt satisfied, knowing he would soon receive his payment and a new coach.

**CHAPTER VI. THE MOREAU INTERIOR**

Oscar, feeling shy, hid behind trees in the courtyard, observing his companions when Monsieur Moreau appeared on the portico, dressed in a long blue overcoat and carrying a riding whip. He greeted Oscar warmly and introduced himself to Mistigris and his master, the two painters sent by architect Monsieur Grindot. Moreau instructed the concierge to take them to their rooms and invited them to dinner at five, mentioning the hunting opportunities on the estate.

As they walked through the park, Moreau sent his son Jacques to inform Madame Moreau of Oscar's arrival. Moreau, a stern-looking man with a kind nature, encouraged Oscar to learn horseback riding and hunting, despite Oscar's reluctance. He noted that Oscar's stay was only supposed to be two weeks due to Madame Moreau's presence.

The steward's house, built before the Revolution, was situated in a large garden and connected to the chateau. It featured a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir for Madame Moreau, who had decorated it lavishly. The Moreaus lived comfortably, relying on the estate's resources while maintaining an appearance of independence. Madame Moreau, a former maid, enjoyed her status and often dressed elegantly, seeking to be seen as a lady of the chateau rather than a steward's wife.

Tensions existed between the Moreaus and the Reyberts, stemming from a past incident where Madame Reybert reminded the community of Madame Moreau's previous station. The Moreaus had cultivated relationships with local bourgeoisie, often hosting gatherings and playing cards with the rector and mayor.

Madame Moreau eagerly awaited the two painters, hoping to impress them and elevate her social standing. She dressed in her finest attire, anticipating their arrival. However, when the coach from Paris arrived, it only delivered Mistigris's packages, leaving her disappointed.

When the artists finally arrived, Madame Moreau welcomed them and expressed her love for art. She attempted to impress them with her knowledge and charm, but her past as a maid occasionally slipped through her facade. The artists, recognizing her pretensions, decided to play along, engaging her in conversation about art and flattery.

Just as the atmosphere became more relaxed, a maid interrupted, announcing the count's unexpected arrival. This news shocked Madame Moreau, who was concerned about the implications of the count's visit. The count, having overheard Oscar's comments about him during the journey, was furious. He confronted Moreau about the gossip and betrayal, revealing that he felt deeply hurt by the steward's actions.

Moreau, distressed, tried to explain that he had spoken of the count with affection, but the damage was done. The count decided to dismiss Moreau from his position, allowing him to keep his earnings but severing their relationship. He instructed Moreau to ensure a smooth transition to the new steward, Monsieur de Reybert.

As the count prepared for dinner, the atmosphere was tense. The arrival of various guests, including notaries and local dignitaries, added to the pressure. Mistigris, unaware of the gravity of the situation, made light-hearted comments, while Georges Marest, a clerk, found himself in trouble for his earlier antics.

Meanwhile, Moreau returned home, distraught over the loss of his position and the implications for his family. He confronted Oscar, blaming him for the situation and demanding an apology to the count. Oscar, overwhelmed with fear and shame, resisted, leading to a dramatic confrontation where Moreau forced him to apologize in front of the count.

The count, unimpressed by Oscar's behavior, dismissed him, leaving Moreau to grapple with the consequences of the evening's events. The chapter concludes with Moreau taking Oscar home, contemplating the fallout from the count's visit and the future of their family.

**CHAPTER VII. A MOTHER’S TRIALS**

While preparing for a journey, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, expressing distress over Oscar's recent actions. During a trip, Oscar had carelessly disclosed sensitive information about Madame de Serizy to a high-ranking official, leading to Moreau's dismissal as steward of Presles. Moreau decided to send Oscar home immediately, feeling the weight of his family's future and his own ambitions. He lamented Oscar's behavior and questioned his intelligence, wishing he had received better guidance.

That evening, Madame Clapart was knitting when her husband, Clapart, voiced concern that their friend Poiret might have visited while they were out. Clapart criticized Oscar, believing he would cause trouble at Presles. Madame Clapart defended her son, highlighting his good nature and potential, but Clapart dismissed her optimism, insisting Oscar was lazy and incapable.

Suddenly, a carriage arrived, bringing Oscar home. Brochon, the stable-man, informed them that Moreau had lost his position due to Oscar's actions. Distressed, Madame Clapart fainted upon reading Moreau's letter. When she regained consciousness, she urged Oscar to go to bed, while Clapart continued to berate him.

Despite the turmoil, Oscar slept soundly, unaware of the gravity of his situation. The next day, he felt hungry and surprised that life continued as normal. His mother, worried about their future without Moreau's support, explained the dire consequences of his actions. She emphasized the need for him to find a career, outlining limited options such as commerce, government work, or military service. She expressed fears about their financial instability and urged him to enlist in the army.

Oscar, still naive about life's challenges, listened to his mother but struggled to grasp their reality. Her tears moved him, and she implored him to promise to be more discreet in the future. They discussed seeking help from Uncle Cardot, who had prospered in business and might assist Oscar.

Uncle Cardot, a wealthy widower, lived modestly and had provided well for his children. Unaware of Madame Clapart and Oscar's struggles, he received them during their visit. She highlighted Oscar's achievements, hoping to gain his support. Cardot, while polite, was skeptical about Oscar's future and the family's needs.

Madame Clapart revealed their financial struggles, explaining her sacrifices for Oscar's education. Moved by her plight, Cardot offered to help Oscar pursue a legal career, promising to cover his educational costs if he showed promise. He advised Oscar to work hard and maintain integrity, emphasizing the importance of discretion in a big city like Paris.

After their visit, Madame Clapart felt hopeful for Oscar's future. Moreau later visited, having started a new venture in land speculation. He reassured her that Oscar could still have a successful career if he remained disciplined. Moreau proposed that Oscar work under a young lawyer named Desroches, who would provide the structure and guidance he needed.

Madame Clapart was grateful for Moreau's continued support and urged Oscar to express his thanks. Moreau emphasized that the best way for Oscar to redeem himself was through hard work and proper conduct moving forward.

**CHAPTER VIII. TRICKS AND FARCES OF THE EMBRYO LONG ROBE**

Ten days later, Oscar was introduced by Monsieur Moreau to Maitre Desroches, a young solicitor with a modest office on rue de Bethisy. Desroches, who had risen from poverty, took a keen interest in Oscar, recognizing his own past struggles, though his stern demeanor intimidated Oscar.

Desroches informed his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, that Oscar would be living in the attic next to Godeschal’s room and would need strict supervision to excel in law school and learn the Code thoroughly. Godeschal, also from humble beginnings, was tasked with guiding Oscar, emphasizing hard work and integrity.

For two years, Oscar lived a monk-like existence in the office, waking at five each morning to work alongside Godeschal. He performed errands, prepared for law school, and faced rigorous examinations from both Godeschal and Desroches. His daily routine included a simple dinner and continued work until nightfall. Oscar occasionally visited his uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother, but he was often reprimanded for his attempts at elegance.

Godeschal advised Oscar on maintaining a modest wardrobe and living frugally, citing Desroches as an example of success through hard work. Eighteen months into his tenure, Oscar became the third clerk, performing the duties of the second clerk without a salary but receiving room and board. By the end of his second year, he had gained significant legal knowledge and was performing well at the Palais.

Despite his progress, Oscar felt trapped in a life that clashed with his desires. He envied the well-dressed young men he saw on the streets and longed for freedom and pleasure, which he suppressed under Godeschal's guidance. Godeschal, aware of Oscar's struggles, occasionally took him out for leisure to help him cope with the monotony of his life.

In November 1825, after passing his exams, Oscar received funds from Moreau to upgrade his wardrobe and began working as the second clerk with a salary of eight hundred francs. His uncle Cardot promised to help him find a practice if he continued to excel.

However, Oscar's internal conflict persisted. He often contemplated leaving his rigorous life, feeling more like a galley slave than a budding lawyer. Godeschal remained vigilant, ensuring Oscar was not exposed to temptations, as he often lacked money for excesses. Godeschal's occasional outings provided some relief from Oscar's otherwise dreary existence.

When Godeschal returned from a trip, he reported to Moreau that Oscar was becoming vain, dressing too elegantly and seeking social adventures. He worried that Oscar's aspirations might not align with a legal career, despite his talent for conversation.

Shortly after Oscar's promotion, a new clerk named Frederic Marest joined the office. Marest, a wealthy young man aspiring to become a magistrate, sought to learn the intricacies of legal practice. Godeschal announced Marest's arrival, hoping he would contribute to the office's camaraderie.

The clerks engaged in a tradition of welcoming new members with a humorous register filled with fictitious cases and exaggerated accounts of past receptions. This playful spirit helped the clerks bond and relieve the monotony of their work.

As Oscar settled into his role, he participated in the office's lighthearted pranks, including the ongoing joke surrounding the register. When Marest arrived, he quickly caught on to the humor, indicating he was not easily fooled.

In summary, Oscar's journey through the legal world was marked by rigorous discipline, internal conflict, and camaraderie among his fellow clerks, all while navigating the challenges of ambition and societal expectations.

**CHAPTER IX: LA MARQUISE DE LAS FLORENTINAS Y CABIROLOS**

The next day, Georges Marest, now head-clerk of notary Hannequin, visited Oscar, whom he recognized from a past misadventure. He invited Oscar and his colleagues to a lavish gathering at the Rocher de Cancale and later to an evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, promising elegance and excitement.

Oscar's superior, Desroches, instructed him to retrieve a crucial legal document, warning him about Derville, who might obstruct their efforts. Eager to prove himself as a second clerk, Oscar accepted the challenge.

After Georges left, Godeschal questioned Frederic about the marquise, who continued the ruse, claiming she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. The clerks, excited by the prospect of mingling with high society, prepared for the event.

The Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse at the Gaiete, supported by her benefactor, Pere Cardot. Over the years, Florentine had risen in status, enjoying a luxurious lifestyle and courting Georges, who had recently inherited a fortune.

As the clerks anticipated the gathering, Oscar felt a mix of excitement and apprehension, particularly regarding Georges, who had previously caused him distress. Despite his reservations, he donned his new clothes and set out to enjoy the day.

At the Rocher de Cancale, Oscar met various guests, including an old captain and a journalist. Georges charmed Oscar, downplaying past grievances. The feast was extravagant, and the clerks indulged in food and drink, leading to intoxication.

Afterward, they headed to Florentine's salon, greeted by a dazzling array of women and opulence. Oscar was captivated by Florentine's transformation into a sophisticated marquise. He soon found himself drawn into a game of vingt-et-un with Fanny Beaupre, an actress, and lost significant money, including the five hundred francs entrusted to him by Desroches.

Overwhelmed by his losses, Oscar contemplated despair but was reassured by Florentine, who offered him a thousand francs to recover. He accepted, hoping to regain his standing. However, as the night progressed, he lost even more, succumbing to the thrill of gambling and drink.

By early morning, Oscar was exhausted and fell asleep on a sofa, oblivious to the ongoing festivities. Florentine and her friends discussed the events, unaware of the trouble Oscar faced due to his reckless gambling. The night ended with Oscar still asleep, caught in the consequences of his choices.

**CHAPTER X. ANOTHER CATASTROPHE**

The next morning, Oscar Husson was awakened by his uncle Cardot, who scolded him for his late-night revelry and the state of the apartment, expressing disappointment that Oscar hadn’t informed him about the gathering. Florentine, a dancer, defended herself, claiming she had invited guests to please Cardot.

As tensions escalated, Florentine revealed that Oscar had lost money gambling, prompting Cardot to chastise him for his recklessness. Distraught, Oscar begged Cardot for help in explaining the situation to his employer, Monsieur Desroches, fearing job loss. Florentine urged Cardot to assist Oscar, reminding him of their family ties.

Reluctantly, Cardot gave Oscar five hundred francs, warning it would be the last time he would help him. Oscar left, uncertain of his next steps as he faced the consequences of his actions. Meanwhile, Mariette, concerned for Oscar, sent him a note and money to help recover his losses.

Desroches, upon receiving the note, was furious with Oscar for his irresponsibility. Godeschal, Oscar's friend, defended him, suggesting that Oscar's vanity was leading him astray. Ultimately, Desroches decided to dismiss Oscar, believing he did not deserve further assistance.

At home, Oscar's mother, Madame Clapart, learned of her son's troubles and was devastated. Clapart, her husband, mocked her distress, but she remained hopeful for Oscar's future. When Oscar returned home, he confronted Clapart, asserting his independence and rejecting his father's authority. Clapart's harsh words deepened Madame Clapart's sorrow.

Feeling the weight of his mistakes, Oscar resolved to change his ways. He drew a military number, which determined his fate. Despite the challenges ahead, he hoped for a better future.

Three days later, Oscar drew number twenty-seven, leading to his enlistment in the cavalry. His fortunes changed when he was assigned to a noble regiment, thanks to the influence of the Comte de Serizy. Overwhelmed by her son's misfortunes, Madame Clapart turned to religion for solace, believing her past sins caused her family's suffering.

Oscar excelled in the military, rising through the ranks and earning honors for bravery during a battle in Africa. He saved the Vicomte de Serizy, solidifying his reputation and leading to further promotions.

**CHAPTER XI. OSCAR’S LAST BLUNDER**

Years later, Oscar, now a decorated officer, traveled with his mother to Beaumont. They encountered Georges Marest, a former acquaintance whose life had taken a downward turn. Georges, unrecognizable and in poor condition, attempted to engage with Oscar, who had changed significantly since their last meeting.

As they traveled, Oscar reflected on the changes in their lives. He had risen from his past mistakes, while Georges had fallen into obscurity. The journey brought back memories of their shared past, highlighting the stark contrast between their current circumstances.

Oscar's military career continued to flourish, and he eventually married Georgette Pierrotin, the daughter of the coach owner. This union marked a new chapter in his life, as he learned from his past blunders and embraced a more responsible path.

Through his experiences, Oscar matured into a respectable man, earning a position as a collector and later a receiver-general. His journey from a reckless youth to a responsible adult exemplified the lessons learned from his earlier mistakes, ultimately leading to a fulfilling life.
