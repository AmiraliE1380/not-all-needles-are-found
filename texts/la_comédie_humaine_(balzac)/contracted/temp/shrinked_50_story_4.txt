In October 1800 Bartolomeo di Piombo, a distraught sixty‑something foreigner, stood at the Tuileries with his wife and young daughter, handed his dagger to his wife, and said he would confront the Bonapartes. A soldier detained him until Lucien recognized him and led him to Napoleon. Bartolomeo sought asylum, confessing he had killed the Portas—the family that burned his vineyard and murdered his son—avenging his child by slaying all seven. Bitter at perceived Bonaparte indifference and noting he had once protected their family, he appealed for mercy. Bound by law, Napoleon could not condone private vengeance; he acknowledged Corsican vendettas, offered lodging and financial aid, and warned Paris would not tolerate private justice. Bartolomeo accepted and pledged loyalty. Lucien later found them penniless, helped them, and noted their Corsican property could not sustain life in Paris. Fifteen years later these choices proved significant.

Servin, a respected artist of about forty, was the first to open a studio specifically for young girls who wished to paint. Morally strict and devoted to art, he was married to a general’s daughter with no dowry. Initially mothers stayed with their daughters but soon left them alone, reassured by Servin’s principles. To avoid social disputes and maintain rigor, he limited pupils to young ladies from wealthy families and refused those intent on professional careers. His careful method made him a byword for feminine art—Servin as Herbault was for bonnets and Leroy for gowns—and any mother who suspected talent in her daughter recommended him.

The studio sat in an attic above his apartment, reached by a small spiral stair and sealed from below: a large, light-filled room with panes shaded by green linen, dark-gray walls hung with caricatures and plaster models, a purposeful mix of order and disorder.

On a bright July day the girls worked at their easels. Two cliques had formed—wealthy merchants’ daughters and aristocrats led by the vain Amelie Thirion, and the rest. Ginevra Piombo, modest and anxious, provoked their jealousy with her talent and beauty; Amelie pushed her easel aside amid whispers. Political divisions sharpened matters: Ginevra was a Bonapartist, scorned by royalist families. A faint sound revealed Servin was sheltering a fugitive—an officer of the Imperial Guard. Ginevra chose proximity to protect him. When Madame Servin arrived, hinting at her husband’s absence yet aware of the hidden man, Ginevra quietly hummed a Corsican song while sketching his head to mask his breathing. Servin praised her work; the others, including Amelie, were drawn in. Amelie’s probes failed, and the shared secret forged a tacit bond: Ginevra resolved to keep the officer hidden and help his escape as turmoil brewed outside.

When Servin and Ginevra thought themselves alone, Servin admitted a young Imperial officer with his arm in a sling. He recoiled at first until Servin introduced Ginevra as the daughter of a loyal friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo; recognizing her patriotism, the soldier relaxed. Ginevra noticed his wound, but cries from the street—“Condemned to death!”—made him pale at the name “Labedoyere.” He collapsed, anguished that Labedoyere faced death for their cause while he hid and felt useless. Ginevra urged him to wait out the danger, offered her savings and her father’s influence, and pressed him to consider escape and further service. Her maternal compassion moved him; he called her “an angel of goodness,” yet spoke of avenging Labedoyere and his devotion to Napoleon.

As sunset lit his face, Ginevra felt a sudden, near-magical bond. The next day he—now Luigi—recounted the retreats from Moscow and Waterloo. They spoke Corsican, deepening their intimacy, and Ginevra listened with growing admiration. Her absences from the studio sparked rumors: Mademoiselle Thirion withdrew her class and attendance fell. Laure, sent away by her mother because of the scandal, left saddened; Ginevra felt the loss.

When accused, Servin dismissed gossip and focused on his exhibition. Luigi arrived, jokingly claiming responsibility for the missing students; Servin teasingly suggested marriage. Their silence acknowledged mutual love. Servin encouraged them, and Ginevra pledged to use her father’s influence to seek Luigi a pardon.

Meanwhile her parents fretted over her late returns. Bartolomeo, relieved when she came home, embraced her; the family’s warmth underscored that Ginevra and Luigi’s love, forged in adversity, united them and embodied the resilience of their shared ideals.

“Here she is, my Ginevra… mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old baron, embracing his daughter. Ginevra protested, laughed it off, and reassured her mother. The baroness revived; Piombo and servants breathed with courtly satisfaction.

At dinner Piombo teased Ginevra about her studio hours, hinting jealousy of painting. The tone shifted when Ginevra, eyes downcast, confessed she loved a young man—no prince, but a man without fortune, a comrade of Labedoyere who had been proscribed and hidden by Servin. Piombo, affronted, declared a daughter should love only her father; Ginevra answered that love was not a matter of will. She reminded him she had not reproached his devotion to Napoleon and said she wanted a husband to protect her. Accusations followed: Piombo’s possessive pride versus her plea to choose. She insisted she would not break her word—she loved him truly.

Piombo raged that if the man truly loved her he would kill him; if not, Piombo himself would. Ginevra maintained filial and romantic love were not comparable; she felt both, perhaps the latter stronger. The baroness tried to soften things. Shaken, Piombo asked to think. Ginevra implored him to love her for herself and promised never to abandon him. Their tenderness cooled his anger; he forgave her, and they briefly returned to earlier affection.

Days later Ginevra grew more solicitous, singing and saying their nocturnes needed a man’s voice. A week on, her mother signaled triumph: she had persuaded Piombo to receive the suitor. That day Ginevra arrived supported by Luigi, an officer who had reappeared after hiding and been restored to the list thanks to Ginevra’s appeals to the Duc de Feltre. Luigi’s pale earnestness deepened her love. Piombo sat cold and stern.

Ginevra presented Luigi as a soldier who had fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean. Piombo inspected him and sarcastically noted he bore no decoration; Luigi said he no longer wore the Legion of Honor. The baroness remarked Luigi resembled someone from Corsica—Nina Porta. Luigi answered Nina had been his sister and, when pressed, confirmed he was Luigi Porta.

The name staggered Piombo. He and the baroness left in stunned silence; Ginevra stood pale and speechless. The revelation unmasked a vendetta: the Portas had allegedly killed Ginevra’s brother and burned her house; Piombo had massacred the Portas in revenge. Luigi, bewildered, recounted being bound in the burning house, then taken to Genoa and raised by an old man named Colonna, who warned of enemies and told him to assume the name Luigi. How he survived remained unclear.

Horrified, Ginevra told Luigi he was safe for the moment in her father’s house but warned that once he left he would be hunted by Corsicans in Piombo’s service. She smiled sadly and said their love must be pure and resolute to endure what lay ahead. He squeezed her hand; they accepted their sealed destiny. She escorted him to his modest lodging, determined rather than cowed.

Back home a chill fell. Piombo fumbled for words and ordered a fire; both father and daughter faced private torment they could not bridge. On Ginevra’s birthday, the baroness summoned them to reconcile in Piombo’s study. Two notaries arrived with witnesses to fulfill the law regarding Ginevra’s marriage to Luigi Porta. Piombo bristled, furious at what he saw as a violation of paternal authority and demanded whether French laws could tear a daughter from her father.

When the notary persisted, Piombo seized a dagger and rushed toward Ginevra; witnesses restrained him. Ginevra knelt and begged for mercy. The sight unnerved him; he flung the dagger away and, ashamed, pushed her aside. Then he pronounced his cruelest judgment: “Go. The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here.” He renounced her publicly and irreversibly.

Ginevra fled to Luigi’s humble home. She confessed the loss of parental blessing and their lack of fortune; Luigi flung aside material cares. “Then I am richer than kings!” he exclaimed, vowing to love her as her parents no longer would. Thus remained: Ginevra’s candid love, Piombo’s possessive rage and violent renunciation, Luigi’s return through Ginevra’s appeals, and the catastrophic revelation that Luigi was a Porta—binding them to a vendetta that had destroyed Ginevra’s family and left her only Luigi as shelter.

The day after Ginevra was expelled from her father’s house she took refuge with Madame Servin, who received her coldly and blamed her husband for the trouble. Shocked but proud, Ginevra rented a room near Luigi, who devoted himself to her and painted a bright future.

One morning trunks from her mother arrived with a sorrowful letter pleading reconsideration. The letter moved her briefly, but Luigi’s presence dissolved her resolve to go back; she buried her grief for their love.

On the wedding day she was essentially alone: Luigi brought ordinary men as witnesses while joyful processions passed, underlining their different circumstances. At the mayor’s office the clerk noted her parents’ absence and the ceremony felt like mere bureaucracy. After signing the registers they left with mixed relief and sadness. An old priest married them in a modest chapel; his remarks about absent parents stung, but a sergeant’s simple blessing comforted Ginevra.

They moved into a modest apartment Luigi called their true wedding, thoughtfully arranged with artistic supplies. They soon worked: Ginevra copied paintings and gained clients; Luigi parlayed his handwriting into a copying business and hired assistants. Love blended with labor became their happiest period. Evenings were shared; though she sometimes regretted her old family life, she marked an anniversary with a self-portrait for Luigi and passed another contented year.

By early 1819 picture-dealers urged Ginevra to produce originals instead of copies as competition grew. Madame Porta regretted not turning earlier to genre painting, and though Ginevra tried portraiture she faced stiff rivalry. Despite modest savings, Luigi and Ginevra grew anxious about their future.

That winter Luigi labored relentlessly in a crowded, less lucrative writing market. He could no longer afford help and had to work longer for the same pay. Ginevra painted commendable works, but dealers shunned lesser-known artists and low prices brought few sales.

Their household became dire; love and happiness were overshadowed by poverty. They concealed worries from one another: Ginevra lavished affection while Luigi hid his fears. One night Luigi left to work late and Ginevra, moved, resolved to help by coloring lithographs; they feigned sleep at dawn to spare each other distress. On another night, feverish Luigi saw Ginevra’s lamp across the courtyard and, confronting her, learned she could not idle while he suffered. They watched the dawn together with tentative hope.

Joy followed briefly with the birth of their son, Bartolomeo, but soon they sold possessions and jewels. Poverty stripped them of comfort and pride. Ginevra, frail, often starved herself to feed Luigi while tending the sickly child. Desperate, Luigi sold himself as a military substitute to get an advance. He returned home to find Bartolomeo cold and lifeless; Ginevra, delirious, professed her love and died in his arms. Her parents, awaiting news, were shattered—another life consumed by the same cycle of suffering, leaving only memories and grief.
