In October 1800, Bartolomeo di Piombo stood outside the Tuileries Palace with his family, determined to confront the Bonapartes despite their hardships. After a tense encounter with a soldier, Lucien Bonaparte recognized him and brought him to Napoleon. Bartolomeo revealed he had killed the Portas, responsible for his son’s death. Surprised, Napoleon offered him a chance to live in Paris, which he accepted, pledging loyalty to the Bonaparte family.

Fifteen years later, Bartolomeo's life in Paris revolved around themes of vengeance and loyalty. Servin, an artist, opened a studio for wealthy girls, including Ginevra Piombo, who faced ostracism but remained dedicated to her art. Despite Amelie's attempts to undermine her, Ginevra excelled. She discovered Luigi, a hidden officer, who shared his despair over his past. They formed a deep connection and devised a secret communication system. As rumors spread, Ginevra focused on her feelings for Luigi, while he remained loyal to his beliefs. When gossip drove her classmates away, she confided in Servin and proposed helping Luigi secure a pardon, highlighting loyalty, love, and societal judgment.

“Here she is, my Ginevra!” Piombo exclaimed, embracing his daughter. He noted her long hours at the studio, suggesting painting had become more important than family. Ginevra shocked her parents by confessing her love for Luigi Porta, a comrade of Labedoyere, who had been proscribed. Piombo insisted she should love no one but him, but Ginevra argued that love is not a choice.

Piombo lamented her lack of faithfulness, yet Ginevra reminded him of his own absences for political missions. As Piombo's anger flared, Ginevra pleaded for understanding, asserting her love would not diminish her affection for him. She declared, “I am Ginevra Piombo, and I will be the wife of Luigi Porta.” Despite her father’s threats, Ginevra remained resolute. The next day, she found herself confined at home, communicating with Luigi through a chambermaid. On her birthday, tensions escalated when notaries arrived to discuss her marriage. Ginevra knelt before Piombo, pleading for his consent, but he cast her out.

Ginevra entered Luigi’s modest lodging, stating, “My father and my mother have cast me off.” They married, but financial strain grew as Ginevra struggled to sell her paintings. After the birth of their son, Bartolomeo, their situation worsened. In despair, Luigi decided to sell himself as a military substitute for money. Rushing home, he found Ginevra holding their lifeless child. Despite his pleas, Ginevra succumbed to exhaustion and died in his arms, leaving him devastated. Meanwhile, Ginevra's parents were haunted by memories of their daughter, and when Luigi revealed her fate, it shattered them, leading to profound grief over their loss.
