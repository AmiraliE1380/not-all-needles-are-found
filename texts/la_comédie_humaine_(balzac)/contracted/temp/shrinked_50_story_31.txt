Dedication: To David, Sculptor—Balzac thanks sculpture’s permanence compared with other fame.

In autumn 1826 Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s and hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral. He hated wet shoes but was in high spirits after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and ambitious for a long‑desired canonry, so absorbed that he neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, now the narrow Rue de la Psalette, held one house owned by the devout old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, who kept it through the Restoration by boarding priests. She took only two lodgers: for twelve years Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the rooms—succeeded him. Chapeloud’s death left him both bereaved and secretly relieved: he inherited the tasteful library and furniture he had envied. Becoming Gamard’s boarder and securing the canonry seemed to promise the comforts and status he desired. Settled in, he enjoyed these attentions, but his obsession with preferment returned.

One rainy evening he found the door locked; his servant Marianne explained Gamard had assumed he was already inside. Small omissions—no slippers, no fire—pricked his vanity and felt like disrespect. Simple and lacking worldly tact, he failed to see these lapses as deliberate. Chapeloud had handled Gamard’s temperament with shrewdness; Birotteau’s guilelessness made him an easy target. Over eighteen months Gamard’s hopes to cultivate a salon were thwarted by his indifference and evenings out; she grew quietly vengeful, using coolness and petty neglects as the first signs. Absorbed by hopes of preferment, Birotteau confronts symptoms, not causes—mistaking personal slights for mere oversight. The contrast between the two priests shows how small domestic grievances, unacknowledged ambitions, and provincial social maneuvering escalate into a deeper conflict, a prologue to a fuller study of pride, naiveté, and intrigue.

The next morning Birotteau woke absorbed in thoughts of a possible canonry, briefly putting last night’s ill omens aside. He rang for Marianne, who did not appear; before he could ring again Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected. Troubert noted the absent fire, called for Marianne, and asked after Birotteau’s health and the canonry. Unaware Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him, Birotteau described his efforts to obtain the post.

The two abbés contrasted strikingly: Troubert was tall, thin, serious, with a wrinkled, sarcastic face; Birotteau plump, round-faced, and cheerful. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dulled intellect some blamed on subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau’s joviality made him popular. Neither seemed likely to rise far—Troubert had smoothed his ambitions into piety and, at fifty, showed failing health that might still win vicar-general; Birotteau’s amiability kept him comfortable. Abbé Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert, who nonetheless treated him respectfully.

Troubert borrowed the Register of Bishops. While carrying the heavy books downstairs they met Mademoiselle Gamard, who sharply scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room. Breakfast was tense; Gamard complained of being disturbed, and Birotteau, oppressed by her bitter, abrupt manner, escaped to the garden and then to church. On returning he found he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty protection of her propriety.

Eight days after the affair began, Abbé Birotteau discovered that Mademoiselle Gamard’s hostility—visible since the candlestick-and-clock incident—was a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he felt suffocated by her malice and, absorbed in minor worries, confided in no one.

His life became torment. Anxiety damaged his health, so he turned to Abbé Troubert. Embarrassed to complain, he described Gamard’s provocations; Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised indulgence, blaming her on ill health.

Seeking respite, Birotteau stayed at Madame de Listomère’s country house, unaware Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Gamard’s lawyer Caron reported she was alarmed by his leave and demanded clarification to avoid disputes. Back among friends, speculation grew: Listomère hinted Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment; an elder warned this was only the start; Mademoiselle Salomon said the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed. Friends urged Birotteau to yield if it aided advancement.

Caron pressed him to sign away his residence; under pressure, he signed. He later learned he had surrendered goods Gamard claimed as compensation; she coldly produced the agreement. Crushed, he fled; friends intervened. Monsieur de Bourbonne found the deed exploitative and urged suit. After debate they agreed to support legal action, though Bourbonne warned many would desert a bitter fight. An appraisal exposed Gamard’s fraud, and as proceedings opened gossip and political divisions intensified against the powerful alliance of Gamard and Troubert.

A few days after the quarrel between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning of possible retirement. Alarmed, he rushed to Paris; the naval minister dismissed his fears, but inquiries produced a document confirming the retirement, pending the director’s signature. Desperate, the baron consulted his deputy uncle, who explained that the minister believed the baron had sided with Radicals in Tours and offended the influential Abbé Troubert. His uncle urged reconciliation with Troubert as vital to the baron’s career.

Fearing political fallout, the family followed Monsieur de Bourbonne’s counsel to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau. The baroness pressed Birotteau to withdraw his suit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house; he agreed and asked for Chapeloud’s portrait. Gamard fell ill amid gossip blaming the suit. The baroness’s meeting with Troubert was a duel of insinuations; he later returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait, which the baroness accepted.

Gamard died, leaving her fortune to Troubert, who rose to Bishop of Troyes. Birotteau mourned alone, later exiled to a distant parish—his fate exposing the moral costs of ambition and clerical power.
