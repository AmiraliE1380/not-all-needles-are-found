De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, highlighting the lasting nature of David's art compared to his own transient writing. 

In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a corpulent priest nearing sixty, hopes for a canon nomination at Saint-Gatien Cathedral. He lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests, and envies the apartment of his late friend, Abbe Chapeloud. Initially grateful for Gamard's care, Birotteau soon feels neglected and suspects her ill will, blinded by his ambition.

The next morning, he forgets their discord. When his servant Marianne is absent, Abbe Troubert visits, unaware of Birotteau's naivety regarding his ambitions. Tensions rise as Gamard scolds Birotteau for bringing books to breakfast, revealing her vindictiveness.

After discovering Gamard's plot against him, Birotteau feels tormented and seeks help from Troubert, who dismisses him. Despite warnings, he naively signs a document relinquishing his belongings, allowing Gamard to claim them. Confronting her, he realizes Troubert has taken over his apartment. Devastated, he seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, but his situation worsens when Gamard dies, leaving her fortune to Troubert, who consolidates his power while Birotteau's life deteriorates amid illness and despair.
