De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, highlighting the lasting nature of David's art compared to the fleeting permanence of his own writing.

In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain while returning home, hopeful about a canon nomination at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien. He lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests, and envies the apartment of his deceased friend, Abbe Chapeloud, from whom he inherited a library and furniture.

Initially enjoying Gamard's care, Birotteau soon senses neglect, leading him to suspect her ill will. Despite his good nature, he struggles to understand her hostility, reflecting on their relationship and realizing he has taken her attentions for granted. Gamard, feeling slighted by his social engagements, withdraws her kindness, creating tension.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry blinds him to the conflict with Gamard, whose resentment stems from his ambitions. The narrative explores themes of envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, illustrating how misunderstandings can lead to conflict.

The next morning, preoccupied with thoughts of the canonry, Birotteau forgets the previous night's signs. When his servant Marianne does not come, Abbe Troubert enters, expressing concern over Birotteau's health, unaware of Birotteau's naivety regarding Troubert's ambitions.

As tensions rise, Gamard scolds Birotteau for bringing books to breakfast, souring the atmosphere. Oblivious to her malice, he tries to please her. After returning home late for dinner, he discovers Gamard manipulated the clock to ensure he missed the meal, exemplifying her vindictiveness.

Eight days in, Birotteau uncovers Gamard's plot against him, realizing he is under her scrutiny. His peaceful life descends into despair as he perceives her actions as personal attacks. Isolated and ashamed, he seeks help from Troubert, who dismisses his concerns, deepening Birotteau's helplessness. Planning a stay at Madame de Listomere's country house, he is warned by Gamard's lawyer that his absence could lead to legal complications.

Despite friends urging him to resist Gamard, Birotteau's naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing his rights to his belongings, sealing his fate. Confronting Gamard, he realizes Troubert has taken over his apartment, and Gamard claims his furniture. Devastated, he seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, where friends promise to help him reclaim his dignity.

As Birotteau's life deteriorates amid illness and despair, Troubert's influence grows unchecked, highlighting the consequences of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France.
