De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, highlighting the lasting impact of David's work compared to the uncertain permanence of his own writing.

In autumn 1826, Abbe Birotteau, a short, corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain while returning home, feeling uplifted by the prospect of being nominated as canon at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien. He lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an old maid who boards priests, and envies the beautiful apartment of his deceased friend, Abbe Chapeloud, from whom he inherited a library and furniture.

Initially enjoying Gamard's care, Birotteau soon notices signs of neglect, leading him to suspect her ill will. Despite his good nature, he struggles to understand her hostility, reflecting on their relationship and realizing he has taken her attentions for granted. Gamard, feeling slighted by his social engagements, withdraws her kindness, creating tension between them.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry blinds him to the conflict with Gamard, whose resentment stems from his social aspirations. The narrative explores themes of envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, illustrating how misunderstandings can lead to personal conflict.

The next morning, Birotteau, preoccupied with thoughts of the canonry, forgets the previous night's signs. When his servant Marianne does not come, Abbe Troubert enters. Troubert, a serious man, contrasts with Birotteau's cheerfulness and expresses concern over Birotteau's health and prospects, unaware of Birotteau's naivety regarding Troubert's ambitions.

As tensions rise, Gamard scolds Birotteau for bringing books to breakfast, souring the atmosphere. Despite her bitterness, Birotteau remains oblivious to her malice, caught in a cycle of trying to please her. After returning home late for dinner, he discovers that Gamard manipulated the clock to ensure he missed the meal, exemplifying her vindictive nature and overshadowing his life with her petty tyranny.

Eight days into the story, Abbe Birotteau uncovers a plot against him by Mademoiselle Gamard, who has harbored resentment for months. Initially unaware of her malicious intentions, he becomes tormented as unsettling incidents reveal he is under her scrutiny. His once peaceful life descends into despair as he perceives Gamard's actions as personal attacks.

Feeling isolated and ashamed to confide in friends, Birotteau seeks help from Abbe Troubert, who dismisses his concerns as trivial, attributing Gamard's behavior to health issues, deepening Birotteau's helplessness. To escape, he plans a short stay at Madame de Listomere's country house, unaware this plays into Gamard's hands. At the Alouette, he is confronted by Monsieur Caron, Gamard's lawyer, who warns that his absence could lead to legal complications regarding his living arrangements. Confused, Birotteau returns to his friends, who urge him to resist Gamard.

Madame de Listomere and others suspect Troubert covets Birotteau's apartment and encourage him to stand firm. However, Birotteau's naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing his rights to his belongings in Gamard's house, believing it a mere formality. This act seals his fate, as he later learns Gamard intends to keep his possessions as compensation for board differences.

Upon confronting Gamard, Birotteau realizes the extent of his loss: Troubert has taken over his apartment, and Gamard claims his furniture. Devastated, he seeks solace at Madame de Listomere's, where friends promise to help him reclaim his dignity. Mademoiselle Salomon takes him back to the Alouette, where he struggles with betrayal. His friends strategize a legal response against Gamard, but the local lawyer advises against it, fearing Birotteau may be seen as ungrateful.

Despite this, Birotteau's friends remain committed to supporting him, gathering evidence of the value of his possessions. Meanwhile, Baron de Listomere faces potential retirement from the Navy and seeks help from his uncle, who warns that his political views have made him unpopular, particularly with Troubert. The baron consults Monsieur de Bourbonne, who suggests distancing from Birotteau to appease Troubert.

The baroness then asks Birotteau to withdraw his lawsuit against Gamard for family interests. Distressed, Birotteau reluctantly agrees but insists on retrieving a portrait of Abbe Chapeloud. Madame de Listomere promises to negotiate for the portrait. Shortly after, Gamard dies, leaving her fortune to Troubert, leading to gossip blaming Birotteau for her death. Madame de Listomere visits Troubert to negotiate peace, but he refuses to part with the portrait.

Gamard's death further isolates Birotteau, who is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien, effectively exiling him. Troubert ascends to Bishop of Troyes, consolidating his power. After Madame de Listomere's death, Troubert contests Birotteau's annuity, threatening the baron's career. Ultimately, Birotteau's life deteriorates amid illness and despair, while Troubert's influence grows unchecked, highlighting the consequences of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France.
