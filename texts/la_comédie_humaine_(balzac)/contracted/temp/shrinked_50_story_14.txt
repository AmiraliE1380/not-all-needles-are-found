**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work expresses my gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. I often reflect on our cherished moments in Milan and hope this book serves as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse feel trapped in loveless marriages due to their strict upbringing. Madame du Tillet is unhappy with banker Ferdinand du Tillet, while Madame de Vandenesse, though loved by her husband, feels unfulfilled.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her emptiness to her sister, lamenting societal expectations and the lack of genuine love in their lives. Despite her own struggles, Madame du Tillet tries to comfort her.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix de Vandenesse marries Madame de Vandenesse, initially providing happiness, but she soon feels monotony and unrecognized desires.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, captivates Madame de Vandenesse, creating tension as he balances his ambitions and his relationship with actress Florine.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine relies on Nathan for success, leading to jealousy as he becomes more involved with Madame de Vandenesse.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's secret relationship deepens, filled with passion but complicated by societal pressures.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan, burdened by debt, contemplates suicide. Unaware of his despair, the countess nurtures her feelings for him and rushes to save him upon learning of his plight.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess seeks financial help for Nathan, facing societal judgment while grappling with the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
The count, aware of his wife's bond with Nathan, seeks to protect his family's honor. The countess confronts her feelings, navigating her responsibilities and societal expectations, leading to difficult choices.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, reflecting the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
