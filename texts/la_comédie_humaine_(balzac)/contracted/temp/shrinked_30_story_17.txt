**La Grenadière** by Honoré de Balzac, translated by Ellen Marriage, depicts a charming house on the Loire River's right bank, near Tours, surrounded by cliffs, white stone houses, gardens, and vineyards, all benefiting from a favorable climate for fruit cultivation.

Situated near the village church of Saint-Cyr, La Grenadière is perched halfway up a hillside, with steps leading to an embankment. The property features terraced gardens, trellises, and a variety of flowers, creating a vibrant atmosphere. A Gothic archway opens into a rose-filled garden with a wooden summer-house covered in climbing plants.

The rustic house has large windows, a slate roof, and yellow-washed walls with green shutters. Inside, a crooked staircase leads to a dining room with tiled floors and a sitting room adorned with saffron-colored wallpaper. The first floor contains two simple bedrooms, and a modern kitchen connects to the main house, indicating it was once a retreat for townsfolk from Tours.

The estate includes a small vineyard and a vinedresser’s cottage, surrounded by stone walls and fruit trees. The terrace offers stunning views of the Loire, embodying a humble yet majestic dwelling rich in natural beauty.

In the spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, choosing La Grenadière for its distance from town. Her simple furnishings reflected her modest lifestyle, leaving her social status ambiguous. Tall and slender, with delicate features and a melancholic expression, she captivated local residents during her evening walks along the Loire.

Mme. Willemsens ventured into Tours only twice, sparking curiosity about her background. The landlord referred to her as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, suggesting nobility. Her life remained a mystery, marked by grace and a deep bond with her children, Louis-Gaston and Marie-Gaston. Their close relationship and daily prayers illustrated the power of love and familial bonds in their idyllic yet isolated life.

Dedicated to her children, Mme. Willemsens created a nurturing environment filled with lessons and play. She was present for their studies from ten to three, enjoying lunch in the summer-house and relaxing afterward while they explored the orchard. During lessons, she embroidered quietly, focusing on Louis and discussing his progress with tutors, instilling values of moderation and resilience in both children.

Her joy in watching them play often brought tears to her eyes, representing her ideal happiness. In the evenings, she read biographies to Louis, teaching him perseverance, but her happiness was overshadowed by the fear of her illness and its impact on their future. One evening, she confided in Louis about her condition, urging him to be strong and promising to guide him in caring for Marie.

As summer progressed, her health declined, and the atmosphere at La Grenadière grew somber. The children adapted to her condition, with Louis focusing on his studies and making remarkable progress. Despite her illness, Mme. Willemsens cherished time with them, often walking in the gardens. Unbeknownst to her, Louis worked diligently at night, preparing for the entrance examination to the Ecole polytechnique, encouraged by his mother to balance studies with physical activity.

In October, she enjoyed warm moments with Louis and Marie, who brought her flowers, creating a serene yet melancholic scene. Eventually, the doctor confined her to her room, where she played the piano for the last time and expressed fears about their uncertain future. As her health worsened, she called Louis to her side, revealing her impending death and instructing him to retrieve important papers, including a letter to her estranged husband, Lord Brandon.

She shared her limited financial resources with Louis, urging him to care for Marie and pursue a career at sea. Finding solace in his determination, she felt peace before passing away surrounded by her children. After her funeral, Louis and Marie prepared for their new lives, with Louis taking on the responsibility of caring for Marie as he left for a warship apprenticeship, resolved to honor his mother’s memory and embody the resilience she instilled in him.
