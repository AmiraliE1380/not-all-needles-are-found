La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, runs between cliffs topped by white-stone country houses, gardens and terraced vineyards warmed by southern exposure. A church spire marks Saint-Cyr; further on the Choisille joins the Loire.

Halfway up the hillside and a hundred paces from the church, La Grenadiere—an old Touraine house of two to three centuries—is reached by a fissure in the rock with steps down to the dike of the Paris–Nantes causeway. A gate opens onto a narrow footpath between terraces; trees partly conceal the house until the river view widens. A crumbling Gothic arch leads into a small garden hewn from rock beside a wooden summer-house draped in jessamine and honeysuckle. Vine-clad steps lead to an arched doorway into cellars carved in the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees frame the yellowwashed front with two large windows, a rustic door, three dormers in a steep slate roof, and green shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby; to the right a large dining room with white tile floors, to the left a sitting room with saffron walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor has two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most openings face south; a later timber-framed kitchen with a pump and well suggests the house began as a vintner’s retreat.

Two acres of vineyard, a steep back slope and a vinedresser’s cottage adjoin the western gable. Stone walls and espaliers maximize the land while neglected corners fill with figs, wildflowers and strawberries. Modest yet dignified, La Grenadiere yields grapes, figs, peaches and melons; Spanish broom and Italian oleanders add exotic notes. With the Loire below and Tours and its châteaux in view, it remains a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers, where river sounds invite quiet contemplation among perennial flowers and fruit.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer he pays a thousand francs for six months; orchard fruit doubles the rent, and the vintage doubles it again. With a stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, well-head and a view over three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers, its health and beauty seem beyond price. Unsold since 1690 when it fetched forty thousand francs, it has remained a cherished family possession.

In a bright Restoration spring Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, thirteen and eight, and chose La Grenadiere—perhaps because it lay some distance from town. She turned the drawing-room into her bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and had the housekeeper sleep in a closet. The dining-room served as sitting and drawing room, simply furnished in plain walnut; the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens resisted easy classification: her plain dress invited assumptions while polished manners suggested higher rank. Tall and slender with delicate features and a melancholy expression, her dark eyes sometimes revealed secret anguish and premature wrinkles. She always wore a long black gown with a silk ribbon and an English-style bonnet, a sign of mourning. Her reserve provoked local curiosity; she visited Tours only twice—to inquire about teachers and arrange lessons—and her rare appearances on the bridge attracted attention. The owner said she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, implying her husband’s name; the remark circulated.

Natural manners and a sweet voice charmed the local elite, but her sadness and beauty kept her untouchable. Several young men fell in love yet kept away; she burned all letters, underscoring her wish for solitude.

The three tutors admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys resembled their mother—Louis-Gaston dark-haired and robust, Marie-Gaston almost golden and delicate—with contrasting temperaments; Louis was protective. Well-behaved and attentive, they kept a scrupulous routine of rising early and prayer. Their interactions with their mother were affectionate and intuitive, though Louis often noticed small signs of her declining strength and remained watchful.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during her children’s early breakfast and play to be a pleasing sight for them. From ten until three she listened to their lessons, breaking for a midday meal in the summer-house where they all ate together. After lunch the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

They ranged through the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and studying plants and insects, often returning with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during lessons but followed them attentively, encouraging her eldest, Louis, and taking little notice of little Marie. She walked tutors to the gate and frankly queried Louis’ progress; they reported his strengths and weaknesses honestly.

Their days balanced work and play, leaving little room for quarrels. Her steady love smoothed difficulties and taught her sons moderation and submission. She often wept for joy watching them, treasuring the laughter in the garden-house. Thanks to their English nurse they spoke both languages, and their mother guided their learning with engaging biographies of great figures to teach ambition and success.

Little Marie often fell asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens would grow pensive and tearful; one evening she confessed fears of leaving them alone and without resources. Louis, puzzled, promised to care for Marie. She reassured him that love sustained her and hoped Touraine’s air might aid recovery.

As she spoke more of possible death, Louis adjusted the household to her melancholy. The lively home grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined. By August she remained beautiful but frail; in autumn she spent nearly all her time with the children, while Louis, encouraged by his mother, prepared for the École Polytechnique, listened to a retired naval officer’s tales, walked, climbed and learned to swim, his sunburned face showing renewed purpose.

When October came, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth, likened to the Bay of Naples and advised by the faculty, softened her days. At midday she sat in the shade with her two children, Louis and Marie, who never left her. After lessons the elder lay beside her while restless Marie gathered flowers. With tired eyes and languid movements, she smiled at them—an autumnal, poignant scene.

The doctor soon confined her to her room, brightened daily by favorite flowers and her children at her side. One early November day she played the piano for the last time, fingers moving beneath a Swiss landscape painting; outside her children played. When ordered to stay in bed, she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for a final view of her homeland. She looked up at the clouds, then, anguished, clutched her children’s hands and worried about their future and how they might judge her. Seeing them kneel beside her, she returned to bed and never rose again.

Days blurred with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers—an ominous sign. One quiet Sunday night she drew the curtain and told Louis tomorrow would be her last. She had him fetch important papers—birth certificates and a letter to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband—which she dictated, hesitating over her signature before choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She revealed twelve thousand francs saved and urged him to keep it safe, warning everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, send her to school, and apprentice at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes brightened at his resolve. Growing weak, she requested a priest and, surrounded by loved ones, received the sacrament. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she died.

Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body. Only a few mourners attended; Marie, too young to grasp finality, cried as they lowered the coffin marked by a simple cross. The children returned to La Grenadiere for a last look, then left with Annette to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with Marie’s birth certificate and the money, sent Marie to school, gave earnest advice, and departed to apprentice on a warship. A month later, watching the French coast fade, he embraced life at sea, resolved to honor his mother’s memory and comforted by an old sailor’s words.
