**Summary of "Two Poets" by Honoré De Balzac**

*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy that follows Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in the provincial town of Angoulême. The narrative begins in a time when traditional printing methods were still prevalent, with Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman, now running a small printing establishment. Despite his lack of formal education, Séchard has managed to acquire a printing license during the tumultuous period of the French Revolution, allowing him to operate his business.

Séchard's journey began when he inherited the printing house after the death of its previous owner. He cleverly navigated the political landscape by collaborating with a nobleman, M. le Comte de Maucombe, to print decrees while avoiding the dangers of the time. As the years passed, Séchard transitioned from a struggling pressman to a shrewd businessman, driven by greed and a desire to secure a future for his only son, David.

David Séchard excelled in his studies and was sent to Paris to learn the trade of printing. His father, however, harbored a deep-seated contempt for book learning, believing that practical experience was more valuable. David returned home in 1819, ready to take over the family business, which had enjoyed a monopoly on official printing in the region. However, competition arose when the Cointet Brothers sought a second printing license, threatening Séchard's established business.

Jérôme-Nicolas, now an aging and increasingly greedy man, was eager to pass the business onto David but was also determined to profit from the transaction. He viewed his son not just as an heir but as a rival in business. Their relationship was strained, with Séchard's cunning overshadowing paternal affection. He prepared to negotiate the sale of the business, fully aware that he intended to sell at a high price while David aimed to buy low.

The printing house itself was a reflection of Séchard's character—chaotic yet functional, with a layout that often led to accidents for unsuspecting visitors. The premises included a workshop filled with the tools of the trade, a kitchen, and living quarters that were stark and unkempt, indicative of Séchard's avarice.

As the story unfolds, the dynamics between father and son become increasingly complex, highlighting themes of ambition, familial duty, and the harsh realities of business. Séchard's obsession with profit and control ultimately complicates their relationship, setting the stage for conflict as David seeks to assert his independence and navigate the challenges of the printing industry.

In summary, *Two Poets* explores the struggles of a father-son relationship against the backdrop of a changing society, emphasizing the tension between personal ambition and familial loyalty. The characters are shaped by their environment, and their interactions reflect broader societal themes of competition, greed, and the pursuit of success.

The sitting-room of the late Mme. Séchard was only partially modernized, featuring powder blue wainscoting and Oriental wallpaper, but lacking essential decor. Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, inebriated, brought his son David to review a valuation of the family printing business. David was shocked to find the equipment overvalued at sixteen hundred francs, deeming the old presses unworthy. Séchard insisted that the presses were reliable and had served him well for twenty years, dismissing David's preference for modern machinery.

As they moved to the workshop, Séchard demonstrated the presses' functionality, boasting about their durability and the quality of the type. David, however, recognized the equipment's outdatedness and questioned the valuation, particularly the type's worth, which he believed was inflated. Despite Séchard's insistence on the quality of the tools and the potential profits, David felt trapped in a negotiation that seemed unfair.

Old Séchard, sensing David's hesitation, grew anxious, believing that a lack of argument indicated acceptance. He highlighted the value of the business license and the income from advertisements, urging David to see the potential for profit. David ultimately agreed to the terms, which included a partnership agreement that would allow Séchard to retain a room in the building and share profits until the purchase was fully paid.

After moving in, David found himself financially strained, as Séchard refused to contribute to operational costs, insisting that David should manage on his own. This led to a painful realization for David about his father's greed and lack of support. Séchard's fixation on the business and his own financial gain overshadowed any paternal concern.

Meanwhile, Séchard, now living in the countryside, became increasingly anxious about the business's success. He frequently visited David, who was struggling to keep the printing house afloat amidst competition from the Cointet Brothers, who were gaining market share by slandering David and monopolizing local contracts.

David's friendship with Lucien Chardon, a talented but impoverished young man, blossomed as they shared aspirations and dreams. Lucien's family had fallen into poverty after his father's death, and he was eager to escape his circumstances. David offered him a position in the printing house, hoping to help him find a path to success.

As their friendship deepened, they discussed innovative ideas for the printing business, but their intellectual pursuits distracted them from the practicalities of running the establishment. The Cointets, recognizing David's lack of business acumen, allowed his operation to continue in a diminished capacity, ensuring it wouldn't become a threat to their own growing enterprise.

The physical state of the printing house reflected Séchard's avarice, as he neglected repairs and maintenance. The building was dilapidated, and the workshop was cluttered and grimy, indicative of the struggles faced by David and Lucien as they attempted to navigate the challenges of their respective ambitions while contending with familial and financial pressures. 

In summary, the narrative explores themes of familial obligation, the clash between tradition and modernity, and the struggles of young men trying to forge their paths in a world marked by competition and economic hardship. David's relationship with his father is fraught with tension, as he grapples with the burden of inherited expectations and the desire for independence, while his friendship with Lucien offers a glimmer of hope amidst adversity.

In May 1821, David and Lucien, two aspiring poets, sat in a yard, escaping the oppressive atmosphere of their printing office. David, a robust yet melancholic figure, contrasted sharply with Lucien, whose delicate beauty and charm resembled that of a youthful Bacchus. While David embodied the struggles of a man born to obscurity, Lucien, with his Gascon temperament, was ambitious and adventurous, often contemplating the darker aspects of society.

Their friendship flourished over three years as they immersed themselves in literature and art, sharing dreams of future fame. David admired Lucien's brilliance, while Lucien sought to elevate both of them socially. One day, David excitedly shared a book of poetry by André de Chénier, which deeply moved both friends, igniting their aspirations.

As they reveled in their artistic pursuits, a stranger arrived with a manuscript for printing, introducing himself as an author sent by Mme. de Bargeton, a woman Lucien was infatuated with. Lucien's feelings for her were intense, yet he recognized the social barriers between them. Despite this, he vowed to not pursue her unless David was welcomed too, demonstrating his loyalty to his friend.

After Lucien left, David reflected on their bond, feeling a mix of admiration and concern for Lucien's romantic ambitions. Lucien walked through Angoulême, contemplating the societal divides between the upper town, where the nobility resided, and L’Houmeau, the bustling suburb where he lived. The old city, perched on a crag, was stagnant, while L’Houmeau thrived with commerce, creating a deep-seated rivalry between the two areas.

The upper town was home to old families who maintained strict social boundaries, viewing outsiders with disdain. This exclusivity intensified during the Restoration, further alienating the bourgeoisie from the nobility. Lucien's aspirations to rise above his humble origins were complicated by the entrenched class prejudices of Angoulême, where even a connection to a woman like Mme. de Bargeton could be revolutionary.

Mme. de Bargeton, an admirer of literature and the arts, had a significant influence on Lucien's ambitions. Her interest in him was both a blessing and a curse, as it placed him at the center of a social conflict that could either elevate or destroy him. The narrative sets the stage for Lucien's struggle against societal constraints, driven by his passion for poetry and love for Mme. de Bargeton, while David remains a steadfast friend, embodying the virtues of loyalty and sacrifice.

M. de Bargeton, a descendant of a Bordeaux alderman ennobled under Louis XIII, saw his family fortunes decline due to the extravagance of his grandfather, M. de Bargeton the Waster. By 1789, the family retained only seignorial rights to rents from their Angoumois lands, amounting to ten thousand francs annually. M. de Bargeton married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman from an old family, but one that had fallen into obscurity. Her father, M. de Nègrepelisse, was a miser who lived off his wife’s small estate, while Anaïs received a broad education from Abbé Niollant, who instilled in her a love for music and literature.

Anaïs, or Naïs, developed a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for those around her, leading to a haughty demeanor. Her education, while enriching, also fostered a sense of superiority that made her ill-suited for provincial life. After the Abbé's death in 1802, Naïs faced her father's miserly nature and her own rebellious spirit against the constraints of marriage. M. de Nègrepelisse sought a suitable husband for her, ultimately settling on M. de Bargeton, who was older and perceived as lacking intellect but manageable.

Naïs married M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom and social connections in Paris, but their age difference and limited income constrained their lives in Angoulême. As time passed, Naïs's once-vibrant qualities became absurdities, and her artistic inclinations turned into exaggerated expressions of emotion. She became increasingly isolated, longing for the excitement of life beyond her provincial existence.

Her marriage, marked by disappointment, left her yearning for love and connection. The rise of the Empire rekindled her hopes, leading her to fall for a young officer, whose death in battle deepened her sorrow. This loss cast a shadow over her life, and as she faced the reality of aging without fulfillment, her pride kept her from engaging in the mundane affairs of provincial men.

Mme. de Bargeton’s life became a cycle of unfulfilled dreams and artistic aspirations, leading her to seek solace in literature and the arts. Despite her intellectual gifts, she remained trapped in a narrow existence, her dignity morphing into affectation. The arrival of M. du Châtelet, a charming but superficial man, introduced a new dynamic to her life. He ingratiated himself into her circle, recognizing her potential as a social asset, while the local bourgeoisie disapproved of his presence in their elite society.

M. du Châtelet’s background and experiences made him an intriguing figure, and he sought to cultivate a relationship with Mme. de Bargeton, seeing in her both a source of personal gain and a means to elevate his own status. His arrival disrupted the social order, as he was an outsider welcomed into a space that had previously been exclusive. The contrast between his ambitions and the provincial norms highlighted the tensions within Angoulême society, where intellectual poverty coexisted with the remnants of decayed gentility. 

In summary, Mme. de Bargeton’s life reflects the struggles of a woman caught between her aspirations and the limitations imposed by her environment, ultimately leading to a profound sense of isolation and unfulfilled potential.
