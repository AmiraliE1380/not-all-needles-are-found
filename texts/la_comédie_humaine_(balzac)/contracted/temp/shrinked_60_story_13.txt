**LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES**

**FIRST PART**

**I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.**  
My dearest Renee, I am free! I hope you have escaped the convent too. My release came from my aunt's concern for my health after your departure. In Paris, I feel our souls are intertwined, but without you, I felt trapped. My aunt cannot understand my longing for life outside the convent.

Upon arriving, I found no one waiting for me, and the emptiness of my grandmother's home overwhelmed me. Philippe assured me my inheritance was intact, but my father's failure to restore our family home filled me with despair.

As I settled in, I found comfort in memories and resolved to make this space my own. I long to hear about your life.

**October 19th.**  
I met my mother for the first time since my return. She treated me as an equal, expressing her desire for friendship. My father was affectionate, and I felt a sense of belonging. I am preparing for my debut into society with a generous allowance and a governess, Miss Griffith.

**II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.**  
I am starting to feel at home. My father’s allowance allows me to acquire new clothes for my social debut. Miss Griffith is a good companion, and my family seems busy, leaving me to discover my path.

**III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.**  
I am ready to enter society! After much preparation, I feel confident in my appearance. I plan to present myself as charming and innocent. Despite my excitement, I regret my past life in the convent. The transition is overwhelming, and my family remains distant. I hope to uncover the mysteries of my family and the society I am about to enter.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On December 15th, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and Bois de Boulogne, feeling unnoticed despite my efforts to look appealing. My parents were the only ones who acknowledged me, while Griffith observed the crowd without interest. I noted that many men lacked elegance, particularly in their hats, which Miss Griffith defended as cultivated.

At an evening ball, I stayed close to my mother, who drew all the attention while I felt overshadowed. My dress, which I thought beautiful, was outshone by the married women’s attire. The men seemed worn and lacked the dignity of their ancestors, except for one who, despite his beauty and intellect, appeared self-absorbed.

Determined to enjoy myself, I focused on dancing. Afterward, my mother advised me to be discreet about my feelings, highlighting the complexities of feminine duplicity.

Two weeks later, at the Castle of Maucombe, my family dined with M. de l'Estorade, whose son returned after evading conscription. My parents support a match between me and Louis de l'Estorade, and I agreed to marry him, hoping to improve his estate.

Meanwhile, Don Felipe Henarez reassures his brother of his safety in Spain's chaos, reflecting on their family's decline and urging Fernand to claim his titles. He plans to keep his mother’s diamonds for independence and offers a wedding gift from his limited savings. Felipe finds solace in teaching Spanish in France and emphasizes the need to keep his identity secret from Marie.

Louise de Chaulieu writes to Renee de Maucombe, expressing disbelief at her quick engagement and sharing her disillusionment with society while remaining hopeful for love. In her second letter, she reveals her interest in Don Henarez, grappling with her emotions and societal expectations.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mlle. de Chaulieu worries that her friend Renee's marriage will suppress her spirit, urging her to seek true passion over societal expectations. She is intrigued by M. Henarez, a melancholic Spanish teacher, despite warnings from Mme. de L'Estorade about his dangerous nature and her own marital issues. 

In February, Mlle. de Chaulieu grapples with her father's political ambitions and her feelings for M. Henarez, appreciating his charm and intellect. She critiques Mme. de L'Estorade's pragmatic views on marriage, insisting love should guide choices. After a romantic encounter with M. Henarez, she burns a letter from him, symbolizing her emotional conflict. 

As she prepares for an important evening, she reflects on love's power and her connection with M. Henarez. Excited about a new carriage, she feels empowered by love, while Mme. de L'Estorade stresses the need for responsibility in marriage. Mlle. de Chaulieu experiences emotional turmoil regarding Felipe, longing for clarity in her complex feelings about love and respect.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Renee expresses dissatisfaction with marriage, feeling it subjugates women and turns them into possessions. She yearns for a love beyond societal norms and hopes motherhood will bring fulfillment, while her husband, Louis, remains unaware of her struggles. 

Louise, conflicted about her feelings for Felipe, plans a secret meeting to test his character, wishing for Renee's company. Their encounter is filled with unspoken emotions, as Louise voices concerns about their relationship while Felipe vows to prove himself. 

Louise also shares her disappointment in Felipe's lack of appreciation for Racine's _Berenice_, warning that diminished passion would lead to despair. Later, she joyfully announces her engagement to Felipe, who has accepted her family's wishes. Despite her worries, Renee supports Louise's happiness, and their letters explore themes of love, loyalty, and societal expectations.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Dear friend, it has been nearly eight months since our marriage, and your silence is inexcusable.

We traveled to the Castle of Chantepleurs, purchased by Macumer in Nivernais, and arrived the following evening. I slept during the journey, comforted by Felipe's presence. His affectionate gestures deeply touched me, and I awoke to find his passionate gaze unchanged. Despite his calm demeanor, he later revealed intense emotions he had kept hidden.

As we settled into our new life, I found marriage less daunting than expected. Felipe reassured me of his fidelity, and I began to understand the depth of my feelings for him. True happiness in marriage is often unspoken, overshadowed by duty. I admire your strength, Renee, and see that those who marry for love experience a different reality than those who marry for convenience.

We spent seven and a half blissful months at Chantepleurs, but I often thought of you. Now, in Paris, I feel more beautiful and content than ever. I wish to understand your heart, as I suspect you hide your wounds. I pray for your happiness with Louis and suggest encouraging him to pursue a political career, which could lead to a new beginning for both of us.

In January 1826, I write to you, excited about my upcoming visit to Chantepleurs and my role as godmother to your first child. I reflect on our different lives—your motherhood and my childlessness—finding joy in our roles despite my desire for motherhood. 

You respond nearly five months later, sharing your emotional journey during childbirth and the overwhelming love for your son, Armand. You highlight the bond created through nursing and express gratitude for Louis. Your reflections on motherhood encourage me to embrace my future, believing it will enhance my happiness. Our letters illustrate our contrasting yet complementary experiences navigating love and motherhood.

**Summary of Correspondence Between Mme. de Macumer and Mme. de L'Estorade (March - July 1826)**

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (March 1826)**  
I apologize for my late reply, assuming your silence meant consent about the luncheon service en route to Marseilles. Life in Paris is overwhelming, but my love for Felipe is my refuge. My reputation has improved, pleasing Felipe, while my family secures our legacy through my brother’s marriage. Society's initial criticism of my marriage has shifted to admiration, though the attention can be intoxicating. I long for a true friend and look forward to seeing you in May.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Motherhood keeps me busy with my son, Armand-Louis. I look forward to your visit and hope you find my home welcoming despite the challenges of a teething baby.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (April 1826)**  
I’m pleased to inform you that your father-in-law is now Comte de l'Estorade.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (July 1826)**  
I left abruptly due to jealousy over Felipe’s attention to you, which highlighted my insecurities. I’m returning to Chantepleurs, hoping to find fulfillment.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Your departure surprised me; you had no reason to feel jealous. I urge you to return, as Felipe respects you deeply. Embrace your role as a supportive wife to maintain his dignity and seek true happiness. 

The Vicomtesse de L'Estorade mentions a reply waiting for her at Chantepleurs and expresses concern over the government's impending dissolution, concluding with affectionate remarks about the Baronne's influence in Italy.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
I received your distressing letter, forwarded by my steward. Renee, I won’t dwell on my hurt feelings but will share the impact of your words.

We had just returned from a lovely reception hosted by the ambassador, where I felt radiant, and Macumer was swept away in love. Upon reading your harsh response, I sobbed. Felipe, my dear Arab, insisted that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade our moments together for a mundane existence.

While he whispered sweet nothings, I fainted but am well now. This morning, I confessed my fears to Felipe, asking him to guide me if I ever strayed from his desires. Your letter frightened me, for you are a true friend.

Thank you, Renee. Your words revealed the depth of Macumer's love. Rome is the city of love, where passion thrives alongside art and faith.

We will soon meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Venice. If you write, please address it to Paris, as we leave Rome shortly.

Your letter unsettled me. You and Felipe seem like children. His love for you may resemble a man’s affection for a courtesan—lavish yet knowing she is unfaithful. 

Louis received the cross of the Legion of Honor upon his election to the provincial Council. My father has requested the rank of Officer of the Legion for him, and I hope you can assist.

Ah, Louise, I have endured hell. My son Armand was ill, and after four days of fear, he began to recover, but I remain troubled by the cause of his illness and fear for my unborn child.

Dear Renee, Macumer and I forgave your earlier naughtiness. I write to inform you that Louis has been promoted to Officer of the Legion. 

My brother married Mlle. de Mortsauf, and my parents visited for the wedding. I plan to retreat to Chantepleurs after March to economize, as our Italian tour was costly. 

My daughter, Jeanne-Athenais, is two months old, with my mother and Louis' grand-uncle as her godparents. Your godson can now say your name, calling it _Matoumer_. I worry about him while I recover from childbirth. Please write me a long letter.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For the first time, I find myself alone and crying under a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh. Married for nearly three years without a child, I yearn for motherhood, imagining the joy of a child like your Athenais. Each disappointment deepens my melancholy, prompting me to dream of motherhood—embroidering caps, choosing baby clothes, and hearing "Mamma" from my own child. My home feels desolate without children, and I feel unnatural as a childless woman. I long to express my sorrow, and even my Moor was moved to tears by my plight. I plan to consult a doctor about my situation next winter.

A year has passed without a letter from you, which pains me. Your husband Louis visits often, but I miss your thoughts. Your seclusion worries me, and I hope you will come soon to share more about your life.

You complain of my silence, but my children keep me busy. I didn’t want to bring them to Paris while expecting another child. I plan to buy a house in Paris with money from Louis' uncle and my mother. Motherhood requires self-denial; I cannot trust anyone else to care for them as I do.

I am overwhelmed with grief after losing Felipe. I regret the jealousy I placed on him and live in sorrow, questioning if our passion is destined to be barren. I invite you to visit me for comfort.

Dear Renee,

By the time you read this, I will be near you. Louis must remain in Provence for the elections. I come not to comfort but to share my heart. I have sold my house and properties, leaving me with about twelve hundred thousand francs. I invested a million in the Three per Cents, yielding an income of sixty thousand francs.

The real reason for my actions is love. I am to be married soon in a secret ceremony near Paris. My beloved, Marie Gaston, has been courting me for nearly two years. Despite our age difference and his financial struggles, I am ready to end our long restraint. 

I plan to keep our marriage private and have purchased a secluded property near Ville d'Avray. In two days, I will become Madame Gaston. Please address future correspondence to me as Madame Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles. Farewell, my dear friend. The pain of losing you is profound.

Dear Daniel,

I need you and Joseph Bridau as witnesses for my wedding tomorrow. My future wife prefers seclusion, which is why we’ve seen little of each other. After the wedding, we will be apart for a while, but I trust our friendship can endure this absence. She has sacrificed her closeness with a childhood friend for me, and I must do the same.

Our love is profound and genuine, built on mutual understanding. However, I feel a burden of shame; she is wealthy, while I am not. Your support has been crucial during my struggles, and I feel humiliated by our circumstances. This shame reflects my love, as I willingly accept it.

Despite this, my dreams are fulfilled. She is noble, intelligent, and beautiful, embodying the ideal woman. At twenty-seven, she is a widow without children, possessing both energy and moments of melancholy. Our love is pure and has flourished without succumbing to physical desire, reflecting our mutual respect. We have both faced disappointments, and she acknowledges her previous marriage as a mistake.

You, my friend, are a giant among men, and I hope you can appreciate the happiness that awaits me.

---

Louise, are you truly planning a life of solitude after your first marriage? You seem to be inviting misery upon yourself. It appears you have found someone who has changed your mind about remarrying, but have you forgotten your previous criticisms of men?

I cannot blame you for seeking happiness, though I fear for you. Absolute happiness often invites disaster, and I sense your joy may be too heavy to bear. I hope you will share more about this man you love.

I have not yet told Louis about your marriage plans, as it would only invite his ridicule. You are brave to face winter alone, but I will support you as your discreet friend.

---

What has happened to you, dear? After two years of silence, I worry about Louise. Is this what love does? It seems to overshadow our friendship. I miss your letters and your presence.

Regarding our situation, we have chosen to remain in the Audit Department for our children's future. Louis has risen in politics, and I refused an embassy offer to stay in Paris for our children's education.

I am a happy mother, but I worry about Nais's future and the uncertainties of marriage. Your silence has reminded me of these fears. Please write back soon; I miss you dearly.

After two years, I feel compelled to share my happiness with you. Our love has flourished, bringing us daily joy. Gaston, my beloved, has a well-proportioned figure and natural grace. He is free from vices, and his smile brings me joy.

Despite my happiness, I struggle with jealousy and the absence of children weighs heavily on me. As I approach thirty, I feel the pressure of time. I hope we won’t let years pass without communication again. 

With all my love,  
Your friend.

Dear Louise,

Your recent letter reveals a naive view of love that concerns me. True love requires commitment and sacrifices from both partners, focusing on the responsibilities of family life rather than fleeting desires.

You seek adoration, but as a mature woman, you should prioritize the duties of wifehood and motherhood. I urge you to return to Paris; it is not misfortune to fear, but the pursuit of happiness that may lead you astray. Life's simple pleasures sustain us, and while passionate love can be intoxicating, it is ultimately exhausting. I chose self-sacrifice, and despite its challenges, I would do so again. Your romantic notions seem selfish, and I hope you reconsider your path.

Life is about balance; excess leads to suffering. You are unwell because you cling to a passion that marriage should temper. The true glory of home lies in calmness and shared experiences, which you overlook. You risk degrading marriage by pursuing impulses without restraint.

If only you could experience family life, you might understand the lasting happiness it brings. I despair that my attempts to share my happiness with you have highlighted your selfishness. Your love for Gaston seems more about your needs than his true self.

I am troubled by my discovery of Gaston’s mare, Fedelta, covered in mud, leading me to suspect he went to Paris without telling me. Jealousy consumed me, and when Gaston reassured me of his love, I remained unconvinced. I felt trapped in a hell of possibilities.

Days passed, and I noticed Gaston was secretive. I followed him to the post office, where I learned of a play he had written. My suspicions grew, and I discovered he was supporting his late brother's widow and children, not a rival. 

You should find comfort in this revelation and consider embracing this family. Plan a surprise for him, and let this understanding bring you peace.

Oh, dear friend, it is too late for me. I am tormented by my feelings and cannot bear the thought of losing Gaston. Come to me, for I need your support. 

The Comtesse de L'Estorade explains her decision to stay with Louise, who is suffering from advanced consumption. Louise wants to keep her illness secret from Gaston, believing it will help her maintain dignity. Despite her illness, she retains her charm, even joking about her appearance in death. On August 26th, the Comtesse witnesses Louise's final moments, reflecting on the profound loss and deep connections formed through love.
