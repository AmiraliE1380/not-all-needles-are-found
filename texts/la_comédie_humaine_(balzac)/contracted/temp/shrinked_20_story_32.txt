DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy, etc.

Dear Nodier, this book exposes actions hidden behind domestic doors, where divine intervention often replaces human justice. Its moral lessons concern families and motherhood and warn of the consequences when paternal authority weakens—a tribunal historically central to domestic order. A loving mother cannot wholly replace patriarchal authority. The narrative upholds the indissoluble marriage bond and denounces unchecked selfishness. In a society that idolizes wealth and success at any cost, the law is impotent; perhaps a return to Catholicism and a different education could restore moral bearings.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will celebrate noble self-sacrifice while showing how war corrupts those who live domestically as if on a battlefield.

You have keenly observed our times and offered valuable reflections on political chaos. I entrust this story to your name, which may shelter it from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the townspeople of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget. Rumor said he made his beautiful wife unhappy, though the household’s true life remained concealed. Rouget, stern and concerned with masculine authority, believed men must show strength—a conviction causing much female suffering. His wife, a former demoiselle Descoings, had been frail in youth. They had two children: a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

The Descoings were wealthy wool merchants turned moneyed, miserly investors; Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris and failed as a grocer, later captivated by a beautiful widow. By 1792 Descoings seemed prosperous, while his parents had shifted their profits into forfeited estates. Expecting his wife’s death, Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to live with the Descoings, hoping she’d secure the family’s favor. Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun and resembling her mother, sowed discord between Rouget and his friend Monsieur Lousteau, who left town after a quarrel.

Madame Rouget, hearing of her daughter’s departure, foretold she would never see Agathe again—a prediction that proved true. Her health then declined, worsened by her son’s neglect. Jean-Jacques, influenced by his father, offered little comfort.

Agathe’s arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings’ political misstep. He imprudently commented on the famine, angering powerful men; his wife’s attempts to secure his release failed, and he was executed alongside the poet André Chénier. The episode made local grocers wary of politics for years and affected the community more domestically than Chénier’s death did historically.

After Descoings’ death Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Dr. Rouget’s approval—Rouget hoping to profit from the Descoings’ inheritance. Bridau, taken by Agathe’s beauty, married unaware of Rouget’s manipulations. Once the Descoings’ wealth passed on, Rouget sank into debauchery and died in 1805, leaving Jean-Jacques, who remained a bachelor under his father’s lasting influence. Agathe, resembling her mother, became a devoted wife and mother, but her ignorance of the world left her vulnerable.

Bridau, an ardent Bonapartist, was promoted in 1804 but worked himself to death; in 1808, on the cusp of a further promotion the Emperor intended for him, he died from overwork. The Emperor recognized Bridau’s dedication too late. After Bridau’s death, no one in Issoudun corresponded with Agathe. She lost her mother shortly before bearing her second son; her father’s death coincided with the Emperor’s coronation, which distracted Bridau. Her brother had not contacted her since she left home; she gradually accepted the family’s indifference.

Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, warned Agathe she would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother. Agathe hesitated to burden him; Bridau, indifferent or unaware, did not contest the arrangement, leaving them financially exposed.

Concerned, Bridau had prudently invested their remaining funds to secure a modest income. After his death Agathe and Madame Descoings pooled resources to live on a joint income of twelve thousand francs a year—an arrangement that masked underlying strains.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, sixty-five yet still charming and fond of theater and good food, had a secret: her son Bixiou, a major in the army, had a son being educated with Agathe’s children. Hoping to increase the family’s fortune, Madame Descoings secretly gambled on lotteries and accumulated heavy debts. When she confessed, Agathe—without reproach and devoted to duty—sold possessions and liquidated investments to pay them off, marking a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches in Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, overshadowed by the high gray walls of the college and library given by Cardinal Mazarin. There, on the third floor of a damp, cold building, lived a poor widow, Madame Bridau (Agathe). Opposite were the Institute buildings where aspiring artists, the rapins, strove amid examination chaos to win prizes; that artistic hub later moved to the Palais des Beaux-Arts.

From her windows Agathe watched the students’ cages below and tried to brighten her room with boxes of earth and small gardens despite police rules. The shallow house, reached by a winding staircase, held three rooms—a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom—plus a kitchen and two single rooms. She had chosen the apartment because it was affordable (four hundred francs a year), near her sons’ school, and in a neighborhood she knew.

The interior matched the building’s general dreariness. The dining room, with yellow wallpaper and unglazed tiles, held only essentials. The salon contained an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; above a card table hung a vivid painting of Napoleon.

After her husband’s death Agathe took up keeping two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and became absorbed in grief. Her room collected cat hair, millet, and bits of chickweed: signs of neglect and of her clinging to the past. She preserved everything that had belonged to Bridau, even wrapping his last pen with a note. She seemed to lose her sense of womanhood, devoting herself wholly to her children and to saving for their future.

Agathe now lived for her sons and fretted over money. Since moving to rue Mazarin a melancholy settled on her face. She looked for support from the Emperor, who provided three hundred francs a year for each child, along with scholarships. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, on the second floor, had assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe; the Descoings themselves now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores, and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums.

Driven by maternal devotion, Agathe kept expenses low and gave up small pleasures. Distrustful of the future, she saved three thousand francs annually from her pension, hoping to secure a modest inheritance for her children. Thus both widows moved from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty—each for different reasons.

The view from her windows, the students’ tumult, and her husband’s portrait formed the environment that would shape her son Joseph Bridau into a notable painter of the modern French school. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother and had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage; Agathe hoped his physical likeness indicated moral similarity. At fifteen Philippe’s charm reinforced her hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways: untidy, often scolded, and clearly the less favored son.

Joseph’s artistic inclination began during a visit to the Tuileries where a caricature on an Institute wall captivated him. He began to draw, sneaking into the Institute courtyard where older students at first teased him; his persistence eventually earned their respect and encouragement. He began visiting the atelier of the sculptor Chaudet, who recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared for Joseph’s future and tried to dissuade him, believing art would mean hardship; Chaudet defended the vocation passionately, insisting true talent is a divine calling.

As Joseph pursued art, Agathe’s worries grew, particularly when Philippe sought the military life. Enchanted by soldiering, Philippe secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist; within days he was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen. Meanwhile Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn and he sought to earn a living through art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Despite Agathe’s concerns about his health, Joseph thrived in the artistic environment, forming close bonds with fellow students and mentors.

As politics shifted, Philippe returned home a decorated officer while Joseph faced uncertainty in his career. Agathe’s preference for Philippe became more apparent as she admired his military achievements and sacrifices for the Emperor. Joseph, less celebrated, remained devoted to his mother, who struggled to understand his artistic aims. Despite their different paths, both brothers navigated the challenges of their careers, with Agathe’s love evident in her relentless support and concern for their futures.

In 1816 Joseph persuaded his mother to let him convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, partly financed by Madame Descoings. Where the two widows regarded painting as a trade, Joseph embraced it passionately, installing a skylight and chocolate-colored walls while continuing studies under Gros and Schinner.

Political unrest simmered in France: conspiracies and riots surrounded the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, implicated in plots, was arrested and released for lack of evidence; his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. The widows’ friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad for safety; she resisted his serving a foreign power but he ultimately joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile, an enterprise that failed. Agathe equipped him with ten thousand francs, and by late 1817 she had adjusted to reduced means. Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his talent developed.

In 1818 Roguin, the notary, absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite their own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support her.

Desperate for funds, Joseph turned to Gerard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII. Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt. When Philippe returned from America, Agathe welcomed him without realizing how much he had changed.

Time abroad had hardened him. He became self-centered, indulged in drinking and gambling, and adopted a swaggering arrogance. Agathe, blinded by maternal love, excused his faults, seeing him as a victim of circumstance. The family’s finances deteriorated, and friends discussed selling possessions to help Philippe.

Philippe’s conduct alarmed acquaintances: he refused to serve the Bourbons, which limited employment prospects, yet lived as if supported by others. He frequently disappeared for days, maintained a lavish lifestyle, favored cafés and gaming halls, deepened ties with former comrades, and showed increasing disregard for family responsibilities.

Still, Philippe sought to impress Mariette, a young dancer. Giroudeau encouraged the pursuit and promised to help him enter journalism, proposing he exploit his experiences. Philippe’s ambitions clashed with reality; he struggled to adapt to civilian life, grew more hostile to the Liberal party, and nursed a desire for revenge against those he felt had wronged him.

When a potential post—as cashier at a newspaper—materialized, Agathe and Madame Descoings breathed easier and secured it despite past sacrifices. The role brought temporary relief but did little to reform him. His arrogance continued to grow, and Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated.

By the end, Philippe’s return and altered behavior strained relationships and exposed fault lines in loyalty, ambition, and judgment. The once close-knit household faced tests that revealed the consequences of choices made in pursuit of dreams, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between personal aspiration and familial duty.

Three months later the colonel, who ate as if for four men, still criticized the food while the widows, reluctant to press him for payment, continued to spend lavishly on his account. His mother and Madame Descoings hesitated to remind him of his promise. A year passed without any of the “tigers with five claws,” as Leon Gozlan dubbed such men, contributing a cent to the household. The colonel assuaged his conscience by seldom dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother observed, “he has a place.”

Mariette, encouraged by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, made her debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin, triumphing alongside the celebrated Begrand. A wealthy general, enchanted with an actress and acquainted with Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario and arranged her first appearance—an example of the solidarity that binds those enchanted by folly.

Bixiou soon told his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the famed dancer. The widows were shocked; Agathe’s piety led her to regard all stage women as morally corrupt, and both assumed they lived extravagantly.

“Do you think my brother is foolish enough to spend money on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph protested. Bixiou reassured them, noting Mariette’s rising prospects—she was likely to be engaged at the Opera and attract diplomats. “Don’t worry, Madame Bridau; the diplomatic body often visits the Porte-Saint-Martin, and that beautiful girl won’t stay long with your son. I heard an ambassador is madly in love with her.”

Philippe, driven partly by financial motives, proposed marriage to Mariette, but she refused—either suspecting his motives or valuing her independence, especially with prospects at the Grand Opera. For the next year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, passing his time at the office, the theatre, and with Mariette. He mixed with entertainers like Tullia, a celebrated singer; his life became a carnival of pleasure that lasted eighteen months but was not without consequence.

After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette quickly caught the attention of a distinguished duke. Philippe, consumed by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed, taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette then left for London to cultivate patrons while preparations were made for a permanent Opera house. Despite her infidelities and use of him as a stepping stone, Philippe remained deeply attached.

Forced to let Mariette go, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, crushed not merely by the financial deficit but by the thought of life without her company. Suicide crossed his mind. He entered Joseph’s studio, where Joseph was copying a picture for a dealer and discussing his modest earnings. Pale and subdued, Philippe asked how long it would take to paint his portrait, saying he wished to leave a likeness to his mother. When Joseph asked if he planned to leave again, Philippe answered, “I am going never to return.”

Pressed, Philippe confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph told him he would not be dishonored if he repaid the money, but Philippe, despairing, rushed out, leaving Joseph anxious.

Joseph informed Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe, learning the truth, was devastated, fearing for Philippe’s life and their family honor. “He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!” she cried. Madame Descoings urged hope, attributing Philippe’s fall to a bad woman; Agathe, however, lived in terror for her son.

That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, inventing a story that he had merely held the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house where he first won but ultimately lost everything. Returning home, he found concern and affection from his family, which comforted him. “Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, recognizing the tactic had worked, feigned gratitude and affection; they shared dinner, and he felt temporarily relieved.

The next day Agathe appeared older and more anxious, aware their financial situation was grave. Madame Descoings, anxious about the family’s reputation, suggested consulting Desroches. Desroches, fulfilling his diplomatic role, advised Agathe to sign the transfer of funds—effectively arranging repayment—and to petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army. He assured them Philippe would soon regain his rank, easing some worry. Still, Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant fear of his possible downfall.

Philippe resumed his life of leisure—gambling, drinking, appearing disheveled and indifferent to the family’s plight. His love for Mariette had embittered him; he treated women with contempt. Meanwhile Joseph concentrated on painting and gained recognition and support from Madame Descoings, who believed in his prospects.

As Madame Descoings pursued her lottery dreams she remained oblivious to growing tensions. Joseph, noticing discrepancies in his finances, suspected Philippe. He confided in Madame Descoings and they devised a plan to catch Philippe in the act. When Joseph discovered that Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt bitterly betrayed. Despite his anger, he could not sever the bond of brotherhood and struggled with confronting Philippe. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his brother’s corrosive influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe's cautious approach to nightly gambling, he sometimes ended up "cleaned out." Needing his ten-franc stake, he began stealing from his family—taking from his brother Joseph and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a distressing dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe saw his decline and begged him to ask for money instead of stealing. Within two weeks her savings were gone and she took worsted work for a meager twenty sous a day. Her hidden anguish showed in sleepless nights spent waiting for Philippe and watching his drunkenness and mood swings tied to gambling.

When Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes, he snapped that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He had borrowed from Giroudeau, calling it a "debt of honor." Agathe defended him as out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe’s gambling worsened, and he grew more debauched, losing his former good looks.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then schemed to find Madame Descoings’ savings, guessing she would be out buying Christmas treats. He broke into her room and found twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress, which he took to gamble.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions and by the end of the night had amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs. Intoxicated by success, he pressed on and lost everything. The next morning he woke in despair, having ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe to return for Christmas dinner. When he failed to show they worried. Madame Descoings had pinned hopes on her lottery stake, but when she checked her savings she found the money gone and fainted. Agathe, horrified, confirmed with the concierge that Philippe had been in the apartment that day.

Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he had taken what was owed him. The confrontation escalated; Joseph and Philippe came to blows. As tensions peaked, Madame Descoings suffered a stroke and the family rushed to her side.

Afterward Joseph tried to help his mother with money, but she refused, clinging to the lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced, it became clear she had missed her chance; she declined further and soon died, leaving Agathe and Joseph to cope with Philippe's betrayal.

As Philippe recovered from his own illness, Agathe told him their situation had drastically changed and urged him to rejoin the army and support himself. He responded with indifference. Despite her tears she offered him money; he took it and left, seeking the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother’s reduced circumstances, Joseph resolved to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added tasteful decor and a double door, and took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense, Agathe, with her confessor’s help, accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

Agathe adapted to the routine, preparing Joseph’s breakfast and managing his household despite her lack of art knowledge. She mended his clothes, kept the studio tidy, and aided his work; their bond deepened through small acts of tenderness. They discussed Philippe, whose reckless life worried Agathe, but Joseph dismissed her fears, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine, but Joseph declined to prioritize his mother. When he later told Agathe about Philippe, she felt renewed hope. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared a special breakfast for Philippe’s portrait sitting. During the session she hid, overwhelmed as she watched the two sons together.

After posing, Philippe invited them to dinner, which made Joseph uneasy because of Philippe’s apparent wealth; Agathe eagerly accepted. The lavish meal heightened Joseph’s suspicions about Philippe’s finances. At the next sitting, Agathe learned more about Philippe’s social circle—journalists and actresses—and heard his boastful ambitions, though he remained evasive about money.

Joseph had to postpone another sitting because of a pressing commission. When Philippe visited the studio he mistakenly took a valuable copy of a Rubens, believing it the original, and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months later an old officer told Agathe of Philippe’s dire condition: ill and needing money for care. Working at the lottery office, she had little to spare and refused to help the son she had banished from her heart. Joseph agreed, insisting Philippe should go to the hospital.

Some time after, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street—destitute and recently discharged from the hospital. Maternal instinct overcame her; she gave him money despite knowing it would likely be squandered, then fell into despair over his future.

Agathe later learned Philippe had been involved in a conspiracy that led to his arrest; she fainted when she heard his name. Joseph consulted Desroches, who advised them to secure the family inheritance. Desperate, Agathe wrote to her godmother for help but received a discouraging reply about her brother’s unwillingness to assist.

Desroches urged quick action to protect the children’s rights, suggesting they gather evidence of undue influence over Philippe and announcing plans to visit Issoudun during the holidays to help. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences, and Agathe, reflecting on the town’s changes and her long estrangement from her brother, resolved to confront the situation on her return.

Issoudun, one of France’s oldest cities noted by Caesar and Rigord for its population and commerce, yielded a fifth-century basilica in recent excavations by Monsieur Armand Peremet, likely unique in France and built from stones of a Roman temple to Isis. The town’s name, from "Isis" with "dun" indicating a religious and military settlement, points to ancient Celtic origins.

In the Middle Ages Issoudun was a power center: Richard Coeur-de-lion built a tower above the basilica as part of its fortifications, and later the town served as a stronghold for Routiers and Cottereaux mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard. Its prominence has since faded. During the 1830 insurrection vine-growers resisted government measures, burned tax offices, and asserted autonomy, demonstrating the force of a community of about eight thousand vine-growers.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun housed sixteen to seventeen thousand people, prospering on the wool trade and producing blankets and gloves. By Louis XIV’s time it enjoyed some elegance and standing. Today the population is about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from nearby suburbs. The local economy, especially wine, suffers from outdated practices; growers oppose modernization, producing poor-quality wine that undermines potential prosperity.

This decline stems from a pervasive spirit of stagnation. When a proposal arose for a new road linking Paris and Toulouse through Issoudun, local leaders opposed it, fearing higher food prices; their resistance exemplifies a broader provincial tendency to favor tradition over progress.

Issoudun’s social fabric has also deteriorated. The nobility’s absence after historical upheavals removed a source of social competition and dynamism. The bourgeoisie, preserving routine social forms, have become complacent, contributing to stagnation in governance and community life. Administration is dominated by intertwined local figures whose familial ties produce ineffectual leadership and weak accountability. The clergy exert little influence, further diminishing civic vitality.

Against this background the town’s youth, feeling trapped, formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet. Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, was charismatic and adventurous. Raised in poverty and directionless, he enlisted in the military and distinguished himself in campaigns. Returning to Issoudun, he clashed with the bourgeoisie; their disapproval of his lifestyle and pranks only boosted his notoriety.

Max became a local hero after a violent confrontation with royalist officers, and his leadership of the Knights invigorated the town with mischief. The group staged pranks that entertained and alarmed townspeople, executing elaborate hoaxes and frightful stunts that revealed their creativity and camaraderie. While disruptive, these antics brought excitement to an otherwise stagnant community.

The local tavern, run by Père and La Cognette, served as the Knights’ secret meeting place where they planned escapades and shared meals; the couple, unaware of the full extent of the activities, supported them out of self-interest, ensuring their silence.

Issoudun’s archaeological significance and ancient grandeur contrast sharply with modern decline—economic backwardness, social complacency, and weak institutions—while the Knights, under Maxence Gilet, embody the youth’s frustration and thirst for excitement.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower one morning. When Maxence arrived at a Knights' rendezvous, they decided to haul Père Fario’s cart up onto the embankment. The tower of Issoudun, built by Richard Coeur-de-Lion on the ruins of a basilica, which itself lay over a Roman temple and a Celtic dun, forms a mound of layered history; its pedestal rises eighty feet on the town side. In an hour the cart was disassembled, hoisted piece by piece to the top, and reassembled, an operation likened to moving artillery. The Knights, hungry and thirsty after their labors, went to Mère Cognette’s to await the old Spaniard’s perplexed reaction the next morning.

Their nights were not always given to such pranks; they met for hunts, vintages, and skating, and the twenty youths formed a close band with varying ties. Maxence had become their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, grandsons of Madame Hochon, regarded Max as a cousin and relied on him for money and adventures denied by their grandfather.

That night, after twelve to fifteen bottles of wine at a dim table, Baruch warned Max, “You are threatened in your stronghold.”

“My grandmother received a letter,” he explained. “Madame Bridau, her goddaughter, is coming with her son. She’s preparing two rooms.”

Max asked, “What’s that to me?” downing his glass.

At thirty-four, Maxence was striking—soldierly, well-proportioned, with expressive eyes and a winning smile. Around the table someone observed that Madame Hochon’s goddaughter was Rouget’s sister and that her arrival might be to secure the property when Rouget died.

Max repeated, “What’s that to me?”

François suggested that Rouget might revoke a will favorable to Flore. Max snapped that he had “silenced those who meddle” on François’s behalf and bristled at a contemptuous nickname used for the woman he cared about. He had never before spoken so openly of Flore Brazier, Rouget’s servant-mistress, whom society disparaged; his friends had learned to tread carefully.

Urged on, Max explained plainly what they all suspected: yes, Flore—called the Rabouilleuse—aimed for Rouget’s property. He admitted that his dissipations and generosity might be thought to come from her, and his enemies believed he intended to marry her when Rouget died. He asked his friends for help against the Bridaus, within the limits of their fooleries—no murder or poison—content if the Parisians failed and Flore retained only what she actually stood to inherit. He declared he loved Flore and would prefer her even to Mademoiselle Fichet, the wealthy heiress courted by Goddet.

The Knights cheered him on with relaxed morality and ready loyalty: “We’ll stand by you!” “A fig for the Bridaus!” “Here’s to the beautiful Flore!” Their acclaims reflected Max’s leadership, which served his interests and bound them as his support. He toasted them in return and plotted: “Be in the market early; let me know when the old fellow comes for his cart.” They slipped away at half-past three, moving stealthily along the walls in soft shoes.

Max returned to the place Saint-Jean, concealing his worries. His time in Cabrera had taught him deep dissimulation. The forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s property was central to his attachment to Flore. Despite his outward confidence, the news of legitimate heirs had unsettled him: Rouget’s savings, amassed over seventeen years, remained in the doctor’s name, and a will favoring Flore might not secure those funds permanently. He complained that Flore had never mentioned her sister and nephews in seven years; seven hundred and fifty thousand francs divided among notaries could not be liquidated quietly in their gossiping town. His priority was to dispose of these relatives and secure the property. Exhausted, he entered Père Rouget’s house with a pass-key and went to bed, promising clearer thoughts in the morning.

The narrative then turns to how Flore Brazier acquired the nickname “Rabouilleuse” and became, in effect, mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household. As Doctor Rouget aged, he found his son inept and treated him harshly, trying to impose routine; this only prepared Jean-Jacques to submit easily to whatever authority arrived.

One September day in 1799, returning from rounds, the doctor found a little girl catching crabs by a brook. She emerged from the water in a tattered petticoat and makeshift hat—Flore, catching crabs for her uncle Brazier. The doctor, struck by her looks, asked where she came from; she said she belonged in Vatan and was fetching crabs for her uncle. The uncle’s sarcastic reply invoked the Republic, but ultimately the doctor offered to take the child in and pay wages. Despite the uncle’s initial reluctance, he agreed to leave Flore with Rouget.

Flore’s life changed. She moved into the doctor’s home, was well-fed and clothed, and received an education and gifts meant to encourage study. Yet her peasant upbringing had bred a deep aversion to books; the doctor eventually stopped sending her to school. The townspeople speculated about the nature of Rouget’s intentions; gossip and moral judgment swirled around the relationship, blurring truth with suspicion. For five years the doctor enjoyed educating Flore without some complications that might have arisen, while Flore, born in 1787 and shaped by the upheavals of the late eighteenth century, adopted a self-interested view of her circumstances rather than a scrupulous virtue.

By 1802, the Catholic Church was only slowly reestablishing itself and social norms had shifted. Flore was envied for her privileged life despite the moral implications, and the doctor’s affection for her was read variously through the lens of rumor.

As Rouget neared death, his notary urged him to make provisions for Flore. The doctor’s response—cynical and resentful—revealed a man who, chastened by age and disappointments, sought to take revenge on the object of his unfulfilled desires. He refused to leave anything to Flore, suggesting he believed her beauty would secure her future and that she, or others, should not expect his savings. Thus his bitterness and the town’s gossip shaped Flore’s precarious position: cared for and elevated by him, yet morally suspect and ultimately excluded from his testament.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son unhappy, especially after Jean-Jacques came of age in 1791, but he provided Flore with the material comforts valued by country folk. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her now that Monsieur was dead, Jean-Jacques’s dull face for the first time showed animation. “Leave the room,” he instructed Fanchette, who was clearing the table.

At seventeen, Flore retained the beauty that had attracted the doctor, though she was gaining the plumpness common among countrywomen. Jean-Jacques, feeling an urge to speak, hesitated—held back by his father’s recent death and by gratitude for his benefactions. Flore, unable to understand his silence, left without knowing what to think.

At his father’s death Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven, timid and submissive, resembling a child. His timidity sprang from a torpor of the faculties that a stronger influence might have roused. He had a passionate nature but lacked confidence; awkward looks and social awkwardness isolated him and drove him to solitude.

The doctor had, too late, recognized how damaging his son’s temperament was. Wishing to marry Jean-Jacques off yet fearing loss of control over his property, he searched for a suitable daughter-in-law and tried to instill avarice in his son to give him some financial instinct.

Despite this, Jean-Jacques was secretly in love with Flore, the only woman he could comfortably observe. He delighted in the education his father provided her, thinking it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for him to declare himself, but he remained silent.

Three weeks passed in uncertainty. One night Flore awoke to find Jean-Jacques crouched outside her door, which led her to believe he loved her. The next day she found him less unattractive. Their hesitant conversations continued: he asked if she wished to return to the fields, and she, curious about his intentions, assured him of her honesty. These exchanges revealed his growing affection though he struggled to articulate it.

After ten months Jean-Jacques transformed: his demeanor brightened, he attended to his appearance, and the townspeople noticed and speculated about their relationship. Fanchette, the cook, disapproved of Flore’s place in the household and eventually left. Anticipating this, Flore had learned to cook and manage the house; her culinary skill surpassed the former cook’s, and she took pride in keeping the home clean and orderly.

Over the next nine years Flore gained complete control over Jean-Jacques. She treated him with a mix of familiarity and authority, becoming the dominant figure while he, childlike and dependent, relied on her for everything and indulged her whims.

By 1815 Flore reached the height of her beauty, the buxom housewife. In 1816 she fell in love with Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer, and this new passion altered their household. Frustrated by what she felt was Jean-Jacques’s neglect, she confronted him about his wealth and the empty rooms in their home, urging him to invite Max. Jean-Jacques, fearing her loss, reluctantly agreed.

After Max moved in, Jean-Jacques found companionship and conversation he had lacked. Flore distanced herself from household duties, claiming they spoiled her hands; a new cook was employed and Flore adopted a more elegant appearance to impress Max. She bought a horse for him and welcomed a servant to assist him. The household dynamics shifted: Flore and Max became central while Jean-Jacques receded into the background, increasingly dependent on them.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become semi-vegetative. After Max’s financial aid, Flore raised Rouget’s standard of living, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen. Despite better food, he weakened physically and, utterly submissive, his devotion to Flore was his sole motive; he seemed older than his fifty-seven years.

Max’s apartment showed his devotion: provincial comforts, colored tiles, elegant wallpapers and mahogany furniture. A rush matting on the stairs muffled footsteps so Max could come home late without waking anyone, Rouget included, who remained ignorant of Max’s nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore, in a cotton gown, found Max asleep and noticed his late return. He told her a serious matter: Rouget’s family was coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution, asked for coffee and suggested they plan later. Anxious, Flore began to make coffee but was interrupted by Baruch, who announced that Fario was searching for his cart.

Max joined the crowd. Fario, a small peculiar man, claimed his cart had been stolen. Max led him to the tower embankment where the cart was inexplicably found at the top. Amid the crowd’s laughter, Max pretended to assist, theatrically tossing the cart down to Fario’s dismay.

The incident became Issoudun gossip: Fario’s anger and Max’s antics drew attention. Max grasped the implications and felt the need to manage the fallout, especially with the Bridaus’ arrival.

François informed Max that the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max decided they should not appear hostile; he proposed letting the Hochons drive them out instead. Meanwhile Flore, unable to contain her anger, punished Rouget by withdrawing her affection, leaving him confused and distressed.

Flore’s harshness struck Rouget hard; he depended on her emotionally. He asked what he had done, while Vedie urged him to placate Flore. Flore’s fury sprang from learning Rouget’s sister and nephew were coming, whom she saw as threats.

Rouget, bewildered, denied inviting them and blamed Madame Hochon for the visit. Max arrived and argued Rouget should welcome his kin, since public perception mattered. Flore, mollified, prepared breakfast. Rouget thanked Max, who counseled him to keep his will while being polite to his sister.

After breakfast Max and Flore discussed finances and plans to secure Rouget’s wealth while handling the incoming family. Flore admired Max’s cleverness; they plotted so the Hochons would help deal with the Bridaus.

The Hochon household, opposite Rouget’s, was similarly modest. Monsieur Hochon, a retired tax collector, was famed for extreme frugality. His wife, Madame Hochon, resolved to properly receive her goddaughter Agathe and prepared the house despite her husband’s reluctance. She cleaned and borrowed furniture, hoping to shield Agathe from Max and Flore’s influence. Her granddaughter Adolphine secretly hoped to win Joseph Bridau’s affection.

News of the Bridaus’ arrival split Issoudun into factions: the older bourgeoisie, who offered measured support from a distance, and the Knights of Idleness, more vehemently loyal to Max. The tension around the Bridaus foreshadowed a clash of interests in the small town.

Throughout, Max maneuvered socially and politically to protect Flore and Rouget’s position, exploiting public spectacle and allies. Flore used affection and withdrawal to control Rouget; the Hochons prepared to receive the intruding relatives as a potential counterweight. The Fario incident and the town’s divided sentiment underscored how fragile reputations and power were in Issoudun, and how a few calculated actions could shape the looming confrontation over inheritance and influence.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three o’clock. Though tired, Madame Bridau felt revived by familiar sights and memories of youth. News spread quickly; Madame Hochon greeted Agathe warmly, taking her in like a daughter after years of loss had left her clinging to affection.

Agathe met the uninviting Monsieur Hochon and the rest of the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy little Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. The miserly Hochon observed Joseph’s recent hospital stay and apparent hunger; the youths were intrigued by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said with maternal pride. Blunt as ever, Joseph remarked on his likeness to his father, which Madame Hochon interpreted as affection for Agathe. Baruch hurried off to spread the news, emphasizing Joseph’s striking appearance.

Townspeople soon gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and airing their disdain for artists. Curiosity about the Parisians grew; many planned to visit the Hochons that evening.

Settled into modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his artistic life in Paris with the drab provincial existence he now saw. Dinner exposed the Hochons’ frugality: plain bouilli, hard-boiled eggs, and meager sides. Accustomed to better fare, Joseph found the meal disappointing but tried to adapt. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches, and Madame Hochon braced for many visitors. Joseph noted the family’s strict ways and the limits of their hospitality.

After dinner the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories: Agathe recounted struggles and Madame Hochon offered hard-won wisdom about endurance. Their conversation was repeatedly interrupted by a stream of callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph remained detached, studying these visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy. Later, as the house quieted, Agathe and Madame Hochon resumed their heartfelt exchange, reflecting on the burdens they carried.

Elsewhere in town, the Knights of Idleness convened to plot mischief against the Parisians. Max, their leader, proposed a prank using rats to sabotage the grain supply of a disliked merchant, Fario. The gang welcomed the scheme and sought a way to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

The next morning at breakfast Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget, unrecognizable and disheveled, accompanied by Flore Brazier, a striking, conspicuous woman. Joseph’s artistic eye admired Flore’s beauty; Agathe feared her influence over Jean-Jacques. The family debated how to approach him; Joseph suggested he visit first to assess matters.

During breakfast a letter arrived from Jean-Jacques inviting Joseph to dine with him. The Hochons reacted with mixed feelings, suspecting Flore’s involvement and worrying about motives. They discussed strategies for handling the tangled family dynamics. Later, Gritte, the servant, reported the Rouget household’s ostentation and Flore’s extravagant display, prompting the Hochons to dress suitably.

Monsieur Hochon advised Joseph to be observant and to speak frankly with Jean-Jacques about his nephews, stressing the need to understand the situations with Max and Flore. With tensions simmering and rival schemes in motion, the stage was set for a complex family reunion promising confrontation and revelations.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. The table was set; Joseph greeted his uncle, Flore, and Max.

“We haven’t seen each other since I was born, dear uncle,” Joseph said cheerfully.

“You are very welcome,” Rouget answered dully.

Joseph complimented Flore. Rouget, brightening, introduced Maxence Gilet, a former Imperial Guardsman. Joseph noted Max’s good looks and thought the household a “triangular” arrangement: a dull uncle, an attractive woman, and a polished suitor.

Baruch and Francois arrived, and Flore suggested a walk to the tower of Issoudun. While she prepared, Joseph explored the house’s pictures. Rouget said they had come from the Descoings, bought during the Revolution. Joseph admired them; Max led him upstairs to see more. Back downstairs, Flore whispered to Rouget that he should give the paintings to Joseph, who “didn’t care for them.”

Rouget proposed giving the pictures if they would help Joseph, “but without the frames.” Joseph offered to make copies. Flore suggested paying him four thousand francs; Rouget agreed. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. The news of the transaction spread through Issoudun, boosting Flore’s reputation.

That night a splendid dinner was held, attended by local dignitaries. Joseph, somewhat tipsy, went home at eleven; Rouget was carried to bed after heavy drinking. Left alone, Max warned Flore it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them. They planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The following morning Joseph found the pictures leaning against the wall. His mother, Agathe, visited Rouget to thank him, urged by old Hochon, who mistrusted the Bridaus’ motives. “Your adversaries are cunning,” Hochon told Agathe. He advised her to cultivate Flore to gain access to Rouget. Agathe was warmly received by her brother, who remained ill from the night’s excesses. Flore feigned servitude; Agathe suggested Rouget marry her, but Flore insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

Meanwhile the local mischief-makers, called the Knights of Idleness, released rats and mice in Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. Agathe believed Rouget had not made a will and intended to question him about his intentions toward Flore. The Knights sought ways to drive the Parisians away.

After a week the Bridaus had made little formal progress. Hochon urged Agathe to stay longer, stressing the need for religious influence to counter Max and Flore. Agathe resisted using religion as manipulation, but Hochon insisted it was necessary.

Recognizing the paintings’ value, Joseph sent them to Desroches, convinced they were worth more than he had been given. Agathe warned him to keep their value secret, but word spread and searches for old artworks began in Issoudun. Max regretted his role, fearing the Church’s influence on Rouget.

Flore, following Max’s lead, claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage. They bought a shabby vehicle that drew attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney giving Max authority to manage his investments, while Flore handled local transactions.

Fario returned to find his grain store devastated by rats and pigeons and suspected Max’s involvement. Seeking vengeance, he started to gather evidence against the Knights.

Joseph received letters from Schinner and Desroches; Desroches advised keeping his mother in Issoudun and cautioned him about Max, suggesting Rouget might have made a will. The Bridaus’ potential inheritance depended on the larger contest between Flore and the Church.

Joseph decided to return to Paris, saying goodbye to Rouget, who seemed changed. Max and Flore exchanged glances, sensing Joseph’s departure fit their plan. Irritated by talk of the paintings’ worth and the implications, Joseph resolved to return them to Rouget, determined to succeed on his own merits.

The Hochon family, observing Joseph’s agitation, speculated about what had happened. Hochon concluded Joseph had been manipulated by Max and Flore, who knew the paintings’ value all along. Agathe defended Joseph’s choice to return the pictures, but Hochon warned that doing so might jeopardize their inheritance prospects.

Between midnight and one, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs. The expedition lasted until three, after which they went to Cognette’s for supper. At half-past four, as Max turned from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue, Fario, hiding in a recess, stabbed him and fled toward Vilatte, wiping the knife on his handkerchief. Fario washed the handkerchief in the Rivière forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his new watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, crying out and alarming Lousteau-Prangin, son of a judge, and young Goddet, who rushed up the street shouting for help. The townspeople, used to false alarms, stayed in bed. When they reached Max, he had fainted. He recognized Fario but, on regaining consciousness and realizing his wound was not fatal, decided to exploit the situation, declaring, “I think I recognized that cursed painter!”

Lousteau-Prangin hurried to inform his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others, while Goddet’s suspicions were piqued by Max’s words. He asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she said Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. Shocked, they entered Rouget’s house and found Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how the townspeople would perceive him, fearing they might suspect him of robbing his uncle. He wished to escape to Paris and confront Max. At dawn he walked to the poplar avenue leading to Tivoli to calm his nerves.

Meanwhile Max plotted revenge. After Goddet examined the wound and found it serious but not fatal, news of Max’s condition spread, drawing a crowd to the place Saint-Jean. Max whispered to Flore they could use the incident to rid themselves of the Parisians, suggesting they pretend he was dying to have Joseph arrested. Flore, feigning tears, spread the story that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. The enraged crowd demanded to find Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn. The mob grew increasingly hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, alerted by Gritte, locked himself inside, fearing for his safety and wealth. Madame Hochon, worried for their daughter Agathe, hoped she would sleep through the shock. Agathe, however, woke, learned of the accusations, and fainted at the sight of the gendarmes. Monsieur Hochon urged the magistrates to act quickly. The lieutenant of gendarmes reported a large crowd demanding justice for Max.

As the situation intensified, Joseph was spotted returning and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened, protecting him from the angry townspeople who believed he was the murderer. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety as the crowd threatened violence.

At the Palais, Joseph insisted on his innocence and provided an alibi, detailing his movements that morning. The magistrates were somewhat swayed by his calm demeanor and logical explanation but decided he should remain in custody while they verified his claims. Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free.

Meanwhile Max, recovering, remembered a detail that could exonerate Joseph: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. Max wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release. As the investigation progressed, witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi, and he was released. He returned home to his mother’s warm welcome; she was relieved to see him safe. The incident strengthened their bond, and Joseph felt grateful for the support he received.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians, viewing it as a victory. Max and Flore, however, were pleased with the outcome, having successfully rid themselves of what they saw as disruptive outsiders.

Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph’s spirits improved despite the recent turmoil. Agathe remained anxious about her son Philippe, whose trial was approaching. To escape the stress, Joseph traveled to the chateau of the Comte de Sérizy to focus on his work.

Philippe, meanwhile, faced consequences for his actions during the political upheaval. He was sentenced to five years of surveillance and exiled to Autun. Desroches, a family friend, intervened, securing a change of residence to Issoudun and urging him to reclaim his inheritance from their uncle Rouget. Motivated by the chance to restore the family’s fortunes, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun. Desroches advised caution and strategy in dealing with Max and the Rabouilleuse, who threatened their plans. Eager to act, Philippe resolved to confront the situation head-on and, after a brief dinner, departed for Issoudun to reclaim his family's legacy.

On November 2, All-Souls’ Day, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun police commissary and was advised to remain on rue l’Avenier. Word spread that a banished officer linked to the painter falsely accused in the affair had arrived, stirring the town. Maxence Gilet, now recovered, had converted all of Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. Monsieur Hochon consulted notary Monsieur Héron about the situation.

Héron observed that Rouget’s heirs ought to thank him for preventing Gilet from profiting from Rouget’s income. Hochon, long resentful of Gilet and fearful of losing his property, resolved to protect the Bridaus.

Gilet and Flore Brazier welcomed Philippe’s arrival, confident they could manipulate Rouget to secure his funds. The next day Philippe presented himself to his uncle in tattered clothes. Flore felt a chill at his appearance; Gilet detected a latent threat. Philippe’s disheveled state revealed recent hardships, and he made clear he would not beg from Rouget despite past family grievances. Flore urged that he should not live on a meager salary while his uncle had wealth, but Philippe declined her breakfast and refused to impose. His blunt, proud bearing left a strong impression on both Flore and Gilet.

Philippe then visited the Hochons, encountering a mix of curiosity and fear—Madame Hochon especially associated him with past tragedies. Philippe spoke candidly about living on limited means; his charm and honesty won some household members. Discreetly, he asked Monsieur Hochon for advice on navigating Issoudun, stressing secrecy. Hochon supplied names of other officers who opposed Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier. Philippe befriended them, gaining respect by revealing knowledge of the recent conspiracy against the Bourbons, a notable military plot.

Through strategic behavior and careful planning, Philippe integrated into the local military circle while concealing his true aims. He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, cultivated a respectable image among townspeople, and thus disarmed Gilet’s initial suspicions.

As he settled, Philippe took fencing lessons with Carpentier and practiced shooting, maintaining a façade of propriety. His disdain for Gilet became clear, though Gilet avoided direct confrontation and remained cautious.

Philippe’s inquiries into Gilet and Flore led him to Fario, who supplied useful information about the Knights of Idleness and their schemes. Philippe began to suspect that Gilet and Flore planned to secure Rouget’s income and flee. He started devising measures to protect his uncle.

Acting on this, Philippe insisted Rouget take a walk with him, troubled by Flore’s manipulative influence. After some resistance, Rouget consented; Philippe saw the outing as crucial to freeing him from Flore’s control.

Later Philippe met Hochon to discuss Rouget’s finances, revealing that Gilet was attempting to obtain a power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds. They agreed to monitor Rouget’s movements to prevent Gilet’s success.

Meanwhile, Flore and Gilet intensified their plotting: Gilet proposed they leave Issoudun for Vatan to secure the money and marry; Flore, fearful of Philippe’s influence, reluctantly agreed. Tensions rose as Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore their honor hardened, preparing the ground for a dramatic showdown in Issoudun.

As Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle along the boulevard Baron, he reflected on the coming confrontation between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet. Monsieur Hochon watched, intrigued by the struggle over Rouget's fortune.

Philippe bluntly told his uncle that although Rouget loved Flore, she wanted him out of the way to marry Max. He vowed to restore Rouget's “Rabouilleuse” to compliance, as she had been before Max appeared. Philippe proposed a brutal solution: he would kill Max like a dog — but only if Rouget agreed to one condition. Rouget must not sign the power of attorney before December third; the others wanted it only to sell his assets and flee to Paris for the wedding. Philippe added that Maxence would not act as proxy unless he killed Philippe first, and if Philippe killed Max the uncle would have to take Philippe in his place and Philippe would make Flore love him. Rouget recoiled at the thought of hurting Flore, but Philippe insisted that a man must be feared or respected to manage women.

They met Mignonnet and Carpentier, and Philippe explained his uncle’s predicament: either Bridau must kill Gilet or Gilet must kill Bridau. He asked them to arrange seating at the upcoming banquet so he could confront Gilet directly. They agreed to support him but warned of Gilet’s skill as a duelist. Philippe reassured Rouget that, after a period of mourning, he and Flore would live happily together.

Back home Rouget found Kouski weeping: Flore had left with Vedie, and Rouget’s frantic search yielded nothing. Gilet, meanwhile, was aware that the town resented him for his recent actions. Captain Potel confronted Gilet about the backlash and urged him to face Bridau, but Gilet remained focused on securing Rouget’s fortune before any duel.

Max plotted to transfer Rouget’s assets to Flore, hoping to keep the money out of Philippe’s reach; if he obtained the power of attorney he could manipulate the outcome. Philippe, aware of Gilet’s intentions and suspecting he would try to reconcile with Flore, prepared to counter. He sought to prevent any reconciliation and to block transfers of Rouget’s property.

When Philippe heard Flore had departed he rushed to his uncle’s house, but Rouget was not available. Philippe then devised a plan to intercept Gilet and Flore: use Baruch to secure the power of attorney while holding Gilet at bay, protecting Rouget’s interests and ensuring Flore returned. When Rouget finally came back, he began writing a letter threatening to revoke the will he had made in Flore’s favor if she did not return; Philippe encouraged this, believing it would bring her home.

As gossip swelled about the impending duel, Philippe remained focused on his plan and assured Rouget Flore would return and they would be happy. When Flore did return, Philippe confronted her and asserted control, warning that if she did not comply he had means to remove her from Rouget’s life. Shaken, Flore realized how precarious her position was; Rouget, emotionally vulnerable, clung to the hope she would come back.

The next morning the town speculated about the duel. Philippe and Gilet’s confrontation loomed; many hoped it would resolve the tensions. Confident in his strategy, Philippe prepared for the banquet, knowing its outcome would decide Rouget’s fortune and his own ambitions.

Around four o’clock the next day, officers of the old army gathered in the place du Marché in Issoudun, waiting for Colonel Philippe Bridau for a banquet celebrating the coronation. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him. An old sub-lieutenant said that if Max had taken up residence in Rouget’s house he should not have been dismissed without a fight. When Max arrived he received a cold welcome, sharply contrasting with the warm reception for Philippe and strengthening Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe proposed a toast to the Grand Army and was enthusiastically echoed by eleven soldiers, including Benjamin and Kouski. Mignonnet then toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max rose to make an ironic wish that everyone meet again next year. Tension spread as Max’s reputation made his words heavy with meaning. Philippe and Max exchanged barbs; Philippe remained calm while Max struggled to keep his composure. The meal continued tensely until dessert, and the duel was set for the next morning with sabres; Goddet, a former army surgeon, was to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon visited Philippe to urge him to pray before the duel. Madame Hochon offered a relic for protection, which he declined, though she insisted on praying for him. The next morning Max arrived at the duel site with his seconds and prepared for combat. Both men tried to appear composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance.

When the duel began Max launched an aggressive move but was quickly disarmed by Philippe, who paused to let Max recover his weapon. This courtesy increased the tension. When fighting resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but landed a fatal stroke. Max died, ending his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town’s relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated by Max’s death, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering from his wounds, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation. He observed that Flore’s illness prevented her from fleeing and intended to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823 Flore, urged by those around her and by Philippe’s suggestions that marriage would secure her future, began to consider marrying Rouget. Philippe manipulated the situation, presenting himself as a supportive figure while secretly arranging to benefit from the match. Flore, recovering, was persuaded to accept Philippe’s proposal, which he framed as a path to respectability.

The marriage contract gave Flore a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in her son’s apparent reform and the town’s admiration. But Desroches, the family lawyer, warned Agathe that Philippe’s cunning would likely prevent her and Joseph from inheriting Rouget’s property.

Once married to Flore, Philippe asserted control over her and the household, demanding a power of attorney from Rouget. He planned to use Flore to manage his uncle’s affairs while keeping a close watch on her. His ambitions extended beyond Issoudun: he sought to reestablish himself in the army and secure a future in Paris. In a letter to the minister of war Philippe professed loyalty to the new regime and requested reinstatement in the royal army, enclosing endorsements from local officials praising his conduct. These references ultimately led to his reinstatement as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle’s income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon absorbed in Parisian extravagance; Rouget succumbed to excess and died after a lavish supper, a ballet dancer named Lolotte being implicated, his death attributed to indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in this new life, but Philippe had Mariette watch her to prevent serious missteps. In October 1823 Philippe went to Issoudun to settle his uncle’s estate, rapidly amassing a fortune of sixteen hundred thousand francs, excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon. He entrusted the money to Mongenod and Sons for investment.

Philippe married, securing his wife’s fortune against childlessness. He bought her a furnished apartment and secretly acquired a grand hotel in the rue de Clichy, lavishing it with costly renovations and furnishings. The rise of Charles X favored the Duc de Chaulieu’s family; because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies, he was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment. His court connections produced promotion and ennoblement as Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought further honors—aiming for a post as aide-de-camp to the Dauphin, distancing himself from past acquaintances who might jeopardize his status. He planned to marry Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges and aspired to become a general, accumulating military distinctions to bolster his ambitions.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who managed a lottery office. Despite his talent, Joseph suffered financial hardship and criticism while Philippe’s success overshadowed him. Misled by appearances, Agathe convinced herself Philippe’s rise justified his neglect.

In desperation Agathe wrote to Philippe asking for financial help for Joseph. Philippe’s cold reply devastated her and hastened her decline. Near death she sought consolation from her confessor, who laid bare the painful contrast between her sons; her realization that she had misplaced her affections deepened her sorrow, and she died shortly after.

Joseph, heartbroken, buried his mother alone. Philippe remained indifferent, repelled by Joseph’s scathing letter. Bereft, Joseph found solace among friends who supported him through grief.

Later Joseph received a letter from Flore, Philippe’s wife, pleading for help as she faced destitution. Joseph and his friend Bixiou visited and found a shadow of her former self; they arranged medical care and witnessed the depths of Philippe’s cruelty and neglect.

As Flore’s health deteriorated, Joseph and his friends plotted to expose Philippe’s abandonment and secure justice for her, while Philippe continued to climb socially, blind to consequences. His fortunes collapsed during the July Revolution of 1830, precipitating his downfall. In the end Joseph inherited Philippe’s estate and found success in his art, becoming Comte de Brambourg, yet remained humble, often reflecting on his family’s tragic fate and the lessons of their tumultuous lives.
