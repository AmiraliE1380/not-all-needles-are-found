DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy, etc.

Dear Nodier, this book reveals actions hidden behind the closed doors of domestic life, where divine intervention often replaces human justice. The moral lessons within are significant for families and motherhood. We may one day realize the consequences of diminishing paternal authority, which historically served as the only tribunal for domestic crimes. A mother, no matter how loving, cannot replace the patriarchal role. This narrative illustrates the importance of the indissoluble marriage bond and the dangers of unchecked selfishness. May society, built on wealth, recognize the law's impotence in a system that glorifies success at any cost. I hope it returns to Catholicism for the moral uplift of its people through a different kind of education.

In the “Scenes from Military Life,” I will depict noble self-sacrifice while also highlighting the corrupting influence of war on those who act in domestic life as if on a battlefield.

You have keenly observed our times, and your reflections on the political chaos have been insightful. Thus, I entrust this story to your esteemed name, which may shield it from inevitable criticism.

Your sincere admirer,

De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792, the townspeople of Issoudun regarded Dr. Rouget as a man of great malice. Rumors suggested he made his beautiful wife unhappy, though she may have been somewhat foolish. Despite gossip, the true nature of their household remained hidden. Dr. Rouget was not well-liked, yet the townspeople treated him civilly. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, was frail in her youth, which some claimed was why he married her. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and a daughter, Agathe, born unexpectedly ten years later.

These details may seem trivial, but they are crucial to understanding Dr. Rouget's character. He followed the harmful belief that men should exhibit strength, a notion that has caused much suffering for women. The Descoings, Rouget's in-laws, were wealthy commission merchants in the wool trade, growing rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget's brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, where he failed as a grocer, driven by love for a beautiful widow.

In 1792, Descoings was thought to be thriving, while his parents had retired from the wool trade to invest in forfeited estates. Anticipating his wife's death, Dr. Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to stay with Descoings, hoping they would grow fond of her. Agathe, the most beautiful girl in Issoudun, bore a resemblance to her mother, which caused a rift between Dr. Rouget and his friend, Monsieur Lousteau, who left Issoudun after a falling out.

Madame Rouget, upon learning of her daughter's departure, predicted she would never see Agathe again, a sentiment that proved true. Her health declined, exacerbated by her son’s neglect. Jean-Jacques, like his father, was not a source of comfort for her.

Agathe's arrival in Paris coincided with Descoings' imprisonment due to political missteps. He had foolishly expressed his opinions about the famine, angering influential figures. His wife’s attempts to secure his release were misguided, leading to his execution alongside poet André Chénier. This event instilled fear in local grocers, who avoided politics for years.

Descoings' death had a greater impact than Chénier's, though history would later reveal the latter's loss was more significant. Following this, Agathe married Bridau, a secretary in the ministry of the interior, with Dr. Rouget's approval, eager to benefit from the Descoings' inheritance. Bridau, enamored with Agathe's beauty, married her without realizing the extent of Dr. Rouget's manipulations.

After inheriting the Descoings' wealth, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life until his death in 1805, leaving Jean-Jacques, who remained a bachelor for reasons tied to his father's influence. Agathe, resembling her mother, became a devoted wife and mother, yet her ignorance of life led to misfortunes.

Bridau, a fervent supporter of Napoleon, was appointed to a high position in 1804. He worked tirelessly, neglecting his health, and died in 1808 from overwork just as he was about to receive a significant promotion. The Emperor, recognizing Bridau's dedication, intended to reward him but was too late.

After Bridau's death, Agathe received no communication from Issoudun. She lost her mother shortly before giving birth to her second son, and her father’s death coincided with the Emperor's coronation, which kept Bridau occupied. Agathe's brother had not contacted her since she left home, and she gradually accepted her family's indifference.

Madame Hochon, Agathe's godmother, warned her that she would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother. Agathe hesitated to burden him, and Bridau, either unaware or accepting of the injustice, did not contest the inheritance arrangements. They tacitly accepted the situation, leading to financial difficulties.

Bridau, concerned for Agathe's future, invested their remaining funds wisely, ensuring she would have a modest income. After his death, Agathe and Madame Descoings combined their resources, living on a joint income of twelve thousand francs a year. This arrangement, while practical, masked underlying tensions.

In 1809, Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, retained her youthful appearance and charm, despite her age. She enjoyed good food and the theater, and her secret vice was gambling on lotteries. Despite her efforts to cheer Agathe, she harbored a significant secret about her own son, Bixiou, who was a major in the army and had a son being educated alongside Agathe's children.

Agathe, devoted to her children, chose not to remarry, believing it was her duty to be both mother and authority figure. However, her lack of intelligence and blind trust in Madame Descoings led to financial ruin. Madame Descoings, hoping to enrich her family through lottery winnings, accrued significant debt, ultimately confessing her situation to Agathe.

Agathe, without reproach, took charge of their finances, selling possessions and liquidating investments to pay off the debts, marking a significant turning point in their lives.

CHAPTER II

One of the bleakest areas in Paris is the section of rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, overshadowed by the high gray walls of the college and library gifted by Cardinal Mazarin. Here, a poor widow, Madame Bridau, took residence on the third floor of a damp, cold building. Opposite her were the Institute buildings, home to aspiring artists, known as rapins, who transformed into prize-winning talents amid the chaos of examinations. This artistic hub has since moved to the more elegant Palais des Beaux-Arts.

From her windows, Madame Bridau could see the dreary cages of the Institute. To brighten her space, she placed boxes of earth outside her windows to cultivate gardens, despite police regulations. The house was shallow, with a winding staircase leading to three rooms: a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom, along with a kitchen and two single rooms. She chose this apartment for its affordability at four hundred francs a year, its proximity to her sons’ school, and her familiarity with the neighborhood.

The interior reflected the house's overall dreariness. The dining room, with yellow wallpaper and unglazed tile flooring, contained only essential furniture. The salon featured an Aubusson carpet gifted to her, and a mahogany sofa adorned with a portrait of her late husband, Bridau, painted in pastel. The portrait captured his noble essence, while a vibrant painting of Napoleon hung above a card table.

Madame Bridau filled her home with two large birdcages, one with canaries and the other with Java sparrows, a hobby she took up after her husband's death. Her room became cluttered with cat hair, millet, and bits of chickweed, reflecting her grief and neglect of personal care. She preserved everything that belonged to Bridau, even wrapping his last used pen with a note. After his death, she lost all sense of her womanhood, consumed by sorrow and focused solely on her children.

Agathe, now living for her children, felt deep sadness over their financial struggles. Since moving to rue Mazarin, a melancholy shadow had settled on her face. She hoped for support from the Emperor, who provided three hundred francs a year for each child, along with scholarships.

Meanwhile, her aunt, Madame Descoings, lived on the second floor, having assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe. The Descoings, now living modestly on twelve hundred francs a year, employed a woman for morning chores and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, often indulging them with treats and small sums for their needs.

Madame Bridau, driven by maternal love, also kept her expenses low, cutting off her own small pleasures. Distrustful of the future, she saved three thousand francs annually from her pension, hoping to secure a modest inheritance for her children. Thus, both widows transitioned from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each driven by different motivations.

The view from Madame Bridau's windows, the tumult of the students below, and the presence of her husband's portrait all contributed to the environment that would shape her son, Joseph Bridau, into a prominent painter of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder son, resembled his mother but had a bold demeanor that often led others to mistake it for courage. His mother believed this physical resemblance indicated a moral similarity, expecting him to develop her sensitivity. At fifteen, Philippe's charm reinforced her hopes, while Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways. Joseph struggled with cleanliness and often received scolding compared to his brother, leading to a maternal preference that Agathe could not hide.

Joseph's artistic inclination emerged during a visit to the Tuileries, where he was captivated by a caricature drawn on the wall of the Institute. This sparked his desire to learn to draw, leading him to sneak into the Institute's courtyard, where he faced teasing from older students. However, his persistence won their respect, and he was encouraged to pursue his artistic ambitions.

Despite his mother's initial resistance, Joseph began visiting the atelier of sculptor Chaudet, who recognized his talent and encouraged him. Agathe, fearing for her son's future, sought to dissuade him from art, believing it would lead to a life of hardship. Chaudet, however, passionately defended the artistic vocation, asserting that true talent is a divine calling.

As Joseph continued to pursue art, Agathe's worries grew, especially when Philippe expressed a desire to join the military. Inspired by the spectacle of military life, Philippe secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. Within days, he was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and quickly rose through the ranks, becoming a captain by nineteen.

Meanwhile, Joseph's scholarship was withdrawn, and he sought to earn a living through art, encouraged by Madame Descoings. Despite his mother's concerns about his health, Joseph thrived in the artistic environment, forming close bonds with fellow students and mentors.

As the political landscape shifted, Philippe returned home as a decorated officer, while Joseph faced uncertainty in his artistic career. Agathe's preference for Philippe became more apparent, as she admired his military accomplishments and the sacrifices he made for the Emperor. Joseph, though less celebrated, remained devoted to his mother, who struggled to understand his artistic aspirations.

Despite the differences in their paths, both brothers navigated the challenges of their respective careers, with Agathe's love for them manifesting in her support and concern for their futures.

In 1816, Joseph secured his mother’s approval to convert the adjoining garret into an atelier, with financial support from Madame Descoings. The two widows viewed painting merely as a trade, but Joseph embraced it passionately, transforming the space with a skylight and chocolate-colored walls. Despite his dedication, he continued to work under Gros and Schinner.

During this time, political unrest brewed in France, with conspiracies and riots surrounding the Chamber of Deputies. Philippe, involved in these conspiracies, faced arrest but was released due to lack of evidence. His military pay was cut, and Agathe feared for his safety. The widows often hosted their friends, who advised Agathe to send Philippe abroad for his safety, but she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power.

Eventually, Philippe decided to join General Lallemand in the United States to help establish the Champ d’Asile, a failed venture. Agathe provided him with ten thousand francs to start, and by late 1817, she had adjusted to living on a reduced income. Joseph, aspiring to support his family, lived frugally, while his artistic talent grew.

In 1818, Joseph’s fortunes took a turn when Roguin, the notary, absconded with funds meant for Madame Descoings. Shortly after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for encouraging Philippe’s journey. Despite their financial struggles, Madame Descoings continued to support Agathe.

Desperate for funds, Joseph sought help from Gerard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII. Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt. Soon after, Philippe returned from America, and Agathe welcomed him with open arms, unaware of the changes in his character.

Philippe had become hardened and self-centered during his time abroad, indulging in drinking and gambling. Agathe, blinded by maternal love, failed to see his flaws, believing he was a victim of circumstance. The family’s financial situation worsened, leading to discussions about selling possessions to support Philippe.

Upon his return, Philippe’s demeanor alarmed family friends. He rejected the idea of serving the Bourbons, complicating his prospects for employment. Agathe, anxious about his future, accepted sacrifices to support him. Philippe assured them he would find a job, but he continued to indulge in a lavish lifestyle, often disappearing for days.

As Philippe settled back into Parisian life, he frequented cafes and gambling halls, neglecting his responsibilities. His drinking escalated, and he formed friendships with former comrades, further distancing himself from his family. Philippe’s behavior raised concerns among his family and friends, who noticed his growing arrogance and disregard for others.

Despite his flaws, Philippe sought to impress Mariette, a young dancer, and Giroudeau encouraged him to pursue her. Philippe’s infatuation with Mariette led him to believe he could secure a better life through her connections. Giroudeau promised to help Philippe find work in journalism, suggesting he leverage his experiences for personal gain.

Philippe’s ambitions clashed with his reality, as he struggled to adapt to a life outside the military. He became increasingly vocal against the Liberal party, blaming them for his misfortunes. His anger fueled his desire for revenge against those he felt had wronged him.

As Philippe’s situation improved with a potential job offer, Agathe and Madame Descoings were relieved, believing their troubles were over. They agreed to secure Philippe’s position, overlooking the sacrifices they had made. Philippe’s newfound role as a cashier in a newspaper brought temporary relief, but his character continued to deteriorate, leaving Joseph feeling overshadowed and unappreciated.

In the end, the family’s dynamics shifted as Philippe’s arrogance grew, and Joseph struggled to find his place amidst the chaos. The once close-knit family faced challenges that tested their bonds, revealing the complexities of loyalty, ambition, and the consequences of choices made in pursuit of dreams.

Three months later, the colonel, who consumed enough for four men, continued to criticize the food while the widows, feeling delicate about his payments, spent excessively on their meals, yet he had not contributed a single penny. His mother and Madame Descoings hesitated to remind him of his promise. A year passed without any of the “tigers with five claws” Leon Gozlan referred to finding their way from Philippe’s pocket to the household purse. The colonel soothed his conscience by rarely dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother remarked, “he has a place.”

Mariette, influenced by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, made her debut at the Porte-Saint-Martin, where she triumphed alongside the famous Begrand. Among the theatre's directors was a wealthy general officer enamored with an actress, who had become an impresario for her sake. This officer, familiar with Philippe and Giroudeau, arranged Mariette’s first appearance, showcasing the solidarity among those captivated by folly.

Bixiou soon revealed to his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. This news shocked the two widows; Agathe’s religious beliefs led her to view all stage women as morally corrupt, while both women believed such women lived extravagantly.

“Do you think my brother is foolish enough to spend money on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph remarked. Bixiou added, “They’re considering engaging Mariette at the Opera. Don’t worry, Madame Bridau; the diplomatic body often visits the Porte-Saint-Martin, and that beautiful girl won’t stay long with your son. I heard an ambassador is madly in love with her.”

Philippe, motivated by financial interests, proposed marriage to Mariette, but she, anticipating an engagement at the Grand Opera, declined, either sensing his ulterior motives or valuing her independence. For the rest of the year, Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending his time at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette. His friends saw him enjoying a life of pleasure, mingling with Tullia, a leading singer, and other entertainers.

This carnival lasted eighteen months but was not without troubles. Mariette quickly captured the attention of a distinguished duke after her Opera debut in January 1821. Philippe, driven by passion, began to dip into the funds he managed, taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette then left for London, seeking lords to support her while the temporary opera house was prepared. Philippe, despite her infidelities, found himself deeply in love with her, while she viewed him as a stepping stone in her career.

Forced to let Mariette go to London, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, reflecting on his inability to live without the pleasures he had enjoyed over the past year. The thought of suicide crossed his mind, not due to the financial deficit but because he could no longer endure life without Mariette’s company. He entered his brother’s studio, where Joseph was painting.

“Is that how pictures are made?” Philippe asked, initiating a conversation. Joseph explained he was copying a picture for a dealer and shared his earnings. Philippe, pale and subdued, inquired how long it would take to paint his portrait, revealing he wished to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he was going away again, Philippe replied, “I am going never to return.”

Joseph, concerned, pressed him for the reason. Philippe confessed he had taken money from the funds and could not live with the dishonor. Joseph reassured him that he wouldn’t be dishonored if he repaid the money, but Philippe, feeling hopeless, rushed downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious.

Joseph shared the news with Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe, upon learning the truth, was devastated, fearing for Philippe’s life. “He! to fail in honor! the son of Bridau to take the money that was trusted to him!” she lamented, trembling and crying. Madame Descoings urged hope, suggesting Philippe’s misfortune stemmed from a bad woman. Agathe, however, was distraught, fearing for her son’s safety.

As the day passed, Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, fabricating a story that he had retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house, where he initially won but ultimately lost everything. Returning home, he was met with concern and affection from his family, which he found comforting.

“Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, realizing the threat had worked, feigned gratitude and affection, embracing his mother. They shared a dinner, and Philippe, despite his earlier despair, felt a sense of relief.

The next day, Agathe appeared older and more anxious, realizing their financial situation was dire. Madame Descoings, concerned for the family’s honor, suggested they consult Desroches, who could help manage the situation. Agathe agreed, hoping to protect Philippe’s reputation.

Desroches, fulfilling his diplomatic role, advised Agathe to sign the transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army. He assured them that Philippe would soon regain his rank, alleviating some of their worries. However, Agathe’s faith in her son was shaken, and she lived in constant fear of his potential downfall.

Philippe, now back to a life of leisure, resumed his gambling and drinking habits, appearing disheveled and indifferent to his family’s struggles. His love for Mariette hardened his heart, and he treated women with contempt. Meanwhile, Joseph focused on his painting, gaining recognition and support from Madame Descoings, who believed in his future success.

As Madame Descoings pursued her lottery dreams, she remained oblivious to the growing tensions within the family. Joseph, noticing discrepancies in his finances, suspected Philippe was stealing from him. He confided in Madame Descoings, who shared his concerns. They devised a plan to catch Philippe in the act, hoping to confront him about his behavior.

When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed. Despite his anger, he recognized Philippe as his brother and struggled with the idea of confronting him. Madame Descoings, sympathetic to Philippe’s struggles, urged Joseph to be understanding, but Joseph remained wary of his brother’s influence on their family’s future.

CHAPTER V

Despite Philippe's careful approach to his nightly gambling, he occasionally found himself "cleaned out." Driven by the need for his evening stake of ten francs, he resorted to stealing from his family, taking money from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left around. Agathe, after a distressing dream where Philippe robbed her, realized the extent of his moral decline. She pleaded with him to ask for money instead of stealing, but within two weeks, her savings were depleted, prompting her to consider finding work. She began to take on worsted work, earning a meager twenty sous a day. The toll of her hidden anguish was evident in her deteriorating appearance; she spent sleepless nights waiting for Philippe to return, noting his drunkenness and mood swings based on his gambling outcomes.

Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes, but he retorted that his mother had no money and that he had already mortgaged his pension. He had borrowed money from Giroudeau, which he justified as a "debt of honor." Agathe defended Philippe, believing he was not in his right place, while Joseph criticized him for neglecting his appearance and responsibilities. Philippe's gambling habits worsened, and he became increasingly debauched, losing his former good looks.

One day, Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay off a debt to his tobacconist, but Joseph refused to give him any. Philippe then schemed to find out where Madame Descoings hid her savings, suspecting she would be out buying treats for Christmas. He managed to break into her room and discovered twenty napoleons hidden in her mattress. He took the money, planning to gamble it away.

Philippe played at the gambling house, winning substantial amounts over several sessions. By the end of the night, he had amassed a fortune of over one hundred fifty thousand francs. However, intoxicated by his success, he continued to gamble and lost everything. The next morning, he awoke in a state of despair, having brought ruin upon his family.

Meanwhile, Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe to return for Christmas dinner. When he failed to show, they grew worried. Madame Descoings had high hopes for her lottery stake, but when she went to check her savings, she discovered the money was gone. She fainted upon realizing Philippe had stolen it. Agathe, horrified, sought confirmation from the concierge, who revealed Philippe had indeed been in the apartment that day.

Agathe returned, pale and shaken, and the family confronted Philippe about the theft. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he had merely taken what was owed to him. The confrontation escalated, leading to a physical altercation between Joseph and Philippe. As tensions rose, Madame Descoings suffered a stroke, and the family rushed to her side.

Afterward, Joseph attempted to help his mother by offering her money, but Madame Descoings refused, clinging to the hope of her lottery win. When the winning numbers were announced, it became clear that she had missed her chance, leading to her further decline. She passed away shortly after, leaving Agathe and Joseph to deal with the aftermath of Philippe's actions.

As Philippe recovered from his own illness, Agathe informed him that their financial situation had changed drastically. She urged him to rejoin the army and support himself, but he responded with indifference, feeling abandoned by his family. Despite her tears, he took the money she offered and left, seeking the company of those he deemed true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son, resigning herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822, Madame Bridau had moved into the attic room above the kitchen of her former apartment, which had recently been occupied by her son Philippe. Joseph, seeing his mother’s reduced circumstances, was determined to make her comfortable. He rearranged the garret, adding tasteful decor and a double door to enhance its elegance. To alleviate her burdens, Joseph took Agathe to dine daily at a table-d’hote frequented by respectable patrons, costing him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense, Agathe, with the help of her confessor, secured a position worth seven hundred francs a year at a lottery office, which required her to work long hours.

Agathe adapted to her new routine, preparing breakfast for Joseph and managing his household needs, despite her lack of understanding of art. She supported Joseph in his work, mending his clothes and keeping the studio tidy. Their bond deepened through small acts of tenderness, even as they discussed Philippe, whose reckless lifestyle worried Agathe. Joseph, however, dismissed her concerns about Philippe’s suffering, believing he was thriving in New York.

One afternoon, Joseph visited Philippe, who had taken a job at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine with him, but Joseph declined, prioritizing his mother. When he later informed Agathe of Philippe’s situation, she felt a surge of hope. Inspired, she decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared a special breakfast for Philippe’s portrait sitting. During the session, Agathe remained hidden, overwhelmed with emotion as she witnessed her sons together.

Philippe, after posing, invited them to dinner, which made Joseph uneasy due to Philippe’s apparent wealth. Agathe, however, was eager to join. The dinner was lavish, raising Joseph’s suspicions about Philippe’s financial integrity. The following Sunday, Agathe was more involved in the sitting, learning about Philippe’s social circle, which included journalists and actresses. Philippe boasted of his ambitions but was evasive about his finances.

Joseph had to postpone Philippe’s next sitting due to a pressing commission. When Philippe visited Joseph’s studio, he mistakenly took a valuable copy of a Rubens painting, believing it to be the original, and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Upon discovering the theft, Joseph was devastated, and Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to his vices.

Two months later, an old officer visited Agathe, revealing Philippe’s dire condition. He was ill and needed financial support for medical care. Agathe, now working in a lottery office, had little to spare and refused to help Philippe, whom she had banished from her heart. Joseph echoed her sentiments, asserting that Philippe should go to the hospital.

As time passed, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, looking destitute and recently released from the hospital. Overcome with maternal instinct, she gave him money, despite knowing it would likely be squandered. This act of compassion left her in despair, fearing for his future.

Agathe later learned of Philippe’s involvement in a conspiracy that led to his arrest. She fainted upon hearing his name, and Joseph sought legal counsel from Desroches, who advised them to secure their family’s inheritance. Agathe, desperate to save Philippe, wrote to her godmother for financial assistance, but received a discouraging response about her brother’s unwillingness to help.

Desroches urged Agathe to act quickly to protect her children’s rights, suggesting they gather evidence of undue influence over Philippe. He planned to visit Issoudun during the holidays to assist them. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences of his actions.

As they traveled, Agathe reflected on the changes in Issoudun and the long-standing estrangement from her brother. The silence between them had been shaped by complex circumstances, and Agathe was determined to confront the situation upon her return.

CHAPTER VII

Issoudun, one of France's oldest cities, boasts a rich history, evidenced by references from Caesar and Rigord, who noted its significant population and commerce. Recent archaeological excavations led by Monsieur Armand Peremet revealed a fifth-century basilica, likely the only one in France, built from stones of a Roman temple dedicated to Isis. The name "Issoudun" itself, derived from "Isis," indicates its ancient Celtic roots, with "Dun" signifying a religious and military settlement.

Historically, Issoudun was a center of power during the time of Richard Coeur-de-lion, who constructed a tower above the basilica, using it as a foundation for fortifications. The city was also a stronghold for the Routiers and Cottereaux, mercenaries sent by Henry II against his son Richard during a rebellion.

Despite its historical significance, Issoudun's importance has waned. After the 1830 insurrection, the vine-growers of Issoudun revolted against the government, refusing military intervention and asserting their autonomy. They burned tax offices and threatened officials, demonstrating their strength as a community of around eight thousand vine-growers.

In the fourteenth century, Issoudun had a population of sixteen to seventeen thousand, thriving on the wool trade and producing goods like blankets and gloves. By the time of Louis XIV, it was known for its elegance and social standing. However, today, its population has dwindled to about twelve thousand, including vine-growers from surrounding suburbs. The local economy, particularly the wine industry, suffers from outdated practices, as vine-growers resist modernization, leading to poor-quality wine that could otherwise be a source of wealth.

The decline of Issoudun is attributed to a pervasive spirit of stagnation. When discussions arose about a new road connecting Paris and Toulouse through Issoudun, local leaders opposed it, fearing increased prices for provisions. This reluctance to change mirrors the broader provincial attitude, where tradition often trumps progress.

Issoudun's social fabric has deteriorated, lacking the class distinctions that once characterized its society. The absence of nobility, due to historical upheavals, has left the town without the vibrancy that comes from social competition. The local bourgeoisie, while maintaining routine social practices, have become complacent, leading to a stagnation that affects governance and community life.

The town's administration, dominated by local figures, has become ineffective, with officials often related to one another, resulting in a lack of accountability. The local clergy also hold little influence, further contributing to the town's decline.

Amidst this backdrop, the youth of Issoudun, feeling trapped by their circumstances, formed a group known as the "Knights of Idleness." This organization, led by Maxence Gilet, engaged in mischievous pranks that both entertained and terrorized the townspeople. Max, rumored to be the illegitimate son of a local official, was charismatic and adventurous, quickly rising to prominence among his peers.

Max's upbringing was marked by poverty and a lack of direction, leading him to enlist in the military, where he distinguished himself during campaigns. After returning to Issoudun post-war, he found himself at odds with the local bourgeoisie, who disapproved of his lifestyle and antics.

Despite his controversial reputation, Max became a local hero, especially after a violent confrontation with royalist officers. His leadership of the Knights of Idleness brought a new level of mischief to the town, as they executed elaborate pranks that kept the community in a state of unrest.

The Knights' antics included everything from frightening townsfolk to elaborate hoaxes, showcasing their creativity and camaraderie. Their activities, while often disruptive, provided a sense of excitement in an otherwise stagnant town.

The local tavern, run by Pere and La Cognette, served as a secret meeting place for the Knights, where they planned their escapades and enjoyed camaraderie over meals. The couple, while seemingly oblivious to the true nature of the Knights' activities, supported them out of self-interest, ensuring their silence and cooperation.

In summary, Issoudun's rich history contrasts sharply with its current state of decline, marked by social stagnation and economic challenges. The emergence of the Knights of Idleness, led by the charismatic Maxence Gilet, reflects the frustrations of the town's youth and their desire for excitement in a community that has lost its former vibrancy.

CHAPTER VIII

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orleans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness executed one of their clever pranks. An old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war, left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower one morning. Maxence, arriving at a Knights' rendezvous, was soon asked, “What are we to do tonight?”

“Here’s Pere Fario’s cart,” he replied. “Let’s get it up on the embankment first, and then decide.”

The tower of Issoudun, built by Richard Coeur-de-Lion, stands on the ruins of a basilica, which itself rests above a Roman temple and a Celtic Dun. This layered history forms a mound rich with archaeological significance. The tower, comparable in height to the obelisk of Luxor, has an eighty-foot-high pedestal on the town side. CHAPTER XIV

On November 2nd, All-Souls’ Day, Philippe Bridau visited the police commissary in Issoudun to register his arrival and was advised to stay on rue l’Avenier. News of his presence, as a banished officer related to the falsely accused painter, spread quickly, stirring excitement. Maxence Gilet, now fully recovered from his injury, had successfully converted all of Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, causing a stir in the town. Monsieur Hochon, concerned for the Bridau family, consulted notary Monsieur Heron about the situation.

Heron remarked that the heirs of Rouget should be grateful to him for preventing Gilet from benefiting from Rouget’s income. Hochon, who harbored resentment towards Gilet for past fears of losing his possessions, sought to protect the Bridaus.

Gilet and Flore Brazier were pleased with Philippe’s arrival, confident they could manipulate Rouget to secure the funds. The day after arriving, Philippe visited his uncle, presenting himself in tattered clothing. Flore felt a chill at his appearance, and Gilet sensed a latent threat from Philippe. Philippe’s disheveled state reflected his recent hardships, and he introduced himself to Rouget, expressing disdain for asking anything from him due to past grievances against his family.

Flore insisted Philippe should not live on a meager salary while his uncle had substantial wealth. Philippe, however, refused her invitation to breakfast, stating he would not impose on Rouget. After a curt exchange, Philippe left, leaving a strong impression on both Flore and Gilet.

Later, Philippe visited the Hochons, where he was met with a mix of curiosity and fear, especially from Madame Hochon, who associated him with past tragedies. Philippe introduced himself and discussed his plans to survive on his limited income, winning over some of the Hochon family with his charm and honesty about his past.

Philippe discreetly sought advice from Monsieur Hochon about navigating his situation in Issoudun, emphasizing the need for secrecy. Hochon provided him with the names of other officers who opposed Gilet, including Mignonnet and Carpentier, both of whom Philippe befriended. He quickly gained their respect by sharing insights about the recent conspiracy against the Bourbons, which had been a significant military plot.

Philippe’s strategic behavior and careful planning allowed him to integrate into the local military community while keeping his true intentions hidden. He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, maintaining a respectable image to win over the townspeople and disarm Gilet’s suspicions.

As Philippe settled into his new life, he began fencing lessons with Carpentier and practiced shooting, all while maintaining a facade of propriety. His disdain for Gilet became evident, yet Gilet remained cautious, avoiding direct confrontation.

Philippe’s investigation into Gilet and Flore’s activities led him to Fario, who provided valuable information about the Knights of Idleness and their schemes. Philippe suspected Gilet and Flore were plotting to secure Rouget’s income and escape, prompting him to devise a plan to protect his uncle.

One day, Philippe confronted Rouget, insisting they take a walk together, sensing Flore’s manipulative influence over him. After some resistance, Philippe’s determination led Rouget to agree to the walk, which Philippe saw as a crucial step in liberating his uncle from Flore’s control.

Philippe later met with Hochon to discuss the implications of Rouget’s financial situation, revealing that Gilet was attempting to secure a power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds. They devised a plan to monitor Rouget’s movements and prevent Gilet from succeeding.

Meanwhile, Flore and Gilet plotted to manipulate Rouget further, with Gilet suggesting they leave Issoudun for Vatan, where they could secure the funds and marry. Flore, fearing Philippe’s influence, reluctantly agreed to Gilet’s plan, setting the stage for a confrontation between the two men.

As tensions escalated, Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and reclaim their honor intensified, setting the stage for a dramatic showdown in the small town of Issoudun.

CHAPTER XV

As Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle along the boulevard Baron, he reflected on the impending confrontation between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet. Monsieur Hochon, observing them, was intrigued by the outcome of the struggle over Rouget's fortune.

"My dear uncle," Philippe began, "you love this girl, and rightly so; she is stunning! But she wants you out of the way to marry Max, whom she adores."

"I know that, Philippe, but I love her all the same."

"I've sworn to make your Rabouilleuse as compliant as my glove, just as she was before that scoundrel came into your life," Philippe declared.

"If you could do that!" Rouget replied.

"It's simple," Philippe insisted. "I'll kill Max like a dog, but only if you agree to one condition."

"What is that?" Rouget asked, confused.

"Don't sign that power of attorney they want before December third. They only want it to sell your assets and run off to Paris for their wedding."

"I'm afraid so," Rouget admitted.

"Promise me to delay signing until next week," Philippe urged. "Maxence will not be your proxy unless he kills me first. If I kill him, you must agree to take me in his place, and I’ll ensure Flore loves you."

"I could never allow that. A blow to Flore would break my heart."

"That's the only way to manage women," Philippe replied. "A man must be feared or respected."

As they walked, they encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, who Philippe informed about his uncle's predicament. He explained that either Colonel Bridau must kill Captain Gilet or vice versa. He asked them to arrange seating at the upcoming banquet so he could face Gilet directly.

Mignonnet and Carpentier agreed to support Philippe, but expressed concern about Gilet's reputation as a duelist. Philippe reassured his uncle that he and Flore would soon be living happily together after she mourned.

Later, Rouget returned home to find Kouski in tears, revealing that Flore had left with Vedie. The news devastated Rouget, who searched for her in vain. Gilet, meanwhile, was aware of the growing animosity against him in town due to his recent actions.

Captain Potel confronted Gilet about the backlash from the townspeople, urging him to confront Bridau. Gilet, however, was determined to secure Rouget's fortune before any duel.

Max plotted to transfer Rouget's assets to Flore, intending to keep the money out of Philippe's reach. He believed that if he could secure the power of attorney, he could manipulate the situation to his advantage.

Meanwhile, Philippe, aware of Gilet's intentions, prepared to counteract his plans. He suspected that Gilet would attempt to reconcile with Flore and sought to prevent that from happening.

As the evening approached, Philippe received word of Flore's departure and rushed to his uncle's house, only to be told that Rouget was unavailable. He then devised a plan to intercept Gilet and Flore.

Philippe's strategy involved using Baruch to secure the power of attorney while keeping Gilet at bay. He was determined to protect his uncle's interests and ensure that Flore would return to Rouget.

When Rouget finally returned home, he began writing a letter to Flore, threatening to revoke his will in her favor if she did not return. Philippe encouraged this move, believing it would bring Flore back.

As the town buzzed with gossip about the impending duel, Philippe remained focused on his plan. He assured Rouget that Flore would soon be back, and they would be happy together.

When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting his control over the situation. He warned her that if she did not comply with his wishes, he had ways to eliminate her from Rouget's life.

Flore, shaken by Philippe's demeanor, realized the precariousness of her position. Rouget, still emotionally vulnerable, clung to Flore, believing she would return to him.

The next morning, the town was abuzz with speculation about the duel. Philippe and Gilet's confrontation loomed large, with many hoping for a resolution to the tensions that had arisen.

Philippe, confident in his strategy, prepared for the banquet, knowing that the outcome of the duel would determine the future of Rouget's fortune and his own ambitions.

CHAPTER XVI

Around four o’clock the next day, officers of the old army gathered in the place du Marche in Issoudun, awaiting Colonel Philippe Bridau for a banquet celebrating the coronation. Discussions revolved around Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house, with only Potel and Renard defending him. An old sub-lieutenant remarked that if Max had moved in with Rouget, he should not have been dismissed without a fight. Max joined the old soldiers, receiving a cold welcome, contrasting sharply with the warm reception for Philippe, which fueled Max’s resolve to confront his adversary.

As the dinner commenced, Philippe proposed a toast to the Grand Army, which was met with enthusiasm. Eleven soldiers, including Benjamin and Kouski, joined in the toast. Mignonnet then proposed a toast to those who tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max, rising, ironically wished everyone to meet again next year. Tension filled the room as Max’s known character led to an expectant silence.

Philippe and Max exchanged barbs, with Philippe maintaining a calm demeanor while Max struggled to keep his composure. The dinner proceeded without incident until dessert, where the atmosphere remained tense. The duel was confirmed for the next morning, with sabres as the chosen weapons, and Goddet, a former army surgeon, was to attend.

Later that night, Philippe was visited by Monsieur and Madame Hochon, who urged him to pray before the duel. Madame Hochon offered Philippe a relic for protection, which he humorously declined, but she insisted on praying for him. The next morning, Max arrived at the duel site with his seconds, where they prepared for combat. Both men displayed calmness, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance.

As the duel commenced, Max attempted an aggressive move but was quickly disarmed by Philippe, who paused to allow Max to regain his weapon. This act of chivalry only heightened the tension. When the fight resumed, both men exchanged fierce blows, resulting in Philippe sustaining a wound while delivering a fatal strike to Max. The duel ended with Max’s death, marking the end of his ambitions and the Order of Idleness, much to the town's relief.

Flore Brazier, devastated by Max’s death, fell ill, suffering from a fever and inflammation. Meanwhile, Philippe, recovering from his injuries, wrote letters detailing his plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation. He noted that Flore’s illness kept her from fleeing, and he intended to use her to his advantage.

In February 1823, Flore, encouraged by those around her, began to consider marrying Rouget, especially after Philippe suggested it would secure her future. Philippe manipulated the situation, presenting himself as a supportive figure while secretly plotting to benefit from the marriage. Flore, recovering from her illness, was persuaded to accept Philippe’s proposal, which he framed as a means to gain respectability.

The marriage contract was signed, securing Flore a substantial dower and life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, was pleased with her son’s apparent transformation and the town’s admiration for him. However, Desroches, a family lawyer, warned Agathe that Philippe’s cunning would ultimately prevent her and Joseph from inheriting Rouget’s property.

Philippe, now married to Flore, asserted control over her and the household, demanding a power of attorney from Rouget. He planned to use Flore to manage his uncle’s affairs while keeping a close watch on her. Philippe’s ambitions extended beyond Issoudun, as he sought to reestablish himself in the army and secure a future in Paris.

In a letter to the minister of war, Philippe expressed his loyalty to the new regime and requested reinstatement in the royal army. He included endorsements from local officials praising his conduct, which ultimately led to his reinstatement as lieutenant-colonel.

Lieutenant-Colonel Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he took to the Treasury to transfer his uncle's income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon immersed in the extravagant lifestyle of Parisian society, where Rouget ultimately succumbed to excess, dying after a lavish supper. Lolotte, a ballet dancer, was implicated in his demise, which was attributed to indigestion.

Madame Rouget thrived in this new environment, but Philippe ensured she was monitored by Mariette to prevent any serious missteps. In October 1823, Philippe returned to Issoudun to settle his uncle's estate, quickly amassing a fortune of sixteen hundred thousand francs, excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon. He entrusted this wealth to Mongenod and Sons for investment.

Philippe later married, securing his wife's fortune in the event of her death without children. He purchased a furnished apartment for her and secretly acquired a grand hotel in the rue de Clichy, investing heavily in renovations and furnishings. The rise of Charles X favored the Duc de Chaulieu's family, and Philippe, having provided valuable information about past conspiracies, was appointed lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse's regiment. His connections at court led to his promotion and eventual ennoblement as Comte de Brambourg.

Ambitious and insatiable, Philippe sought further honors, including a position as aide-de-camp to the Dauphin. He distanced himself from his past, refusing to associate with old friends who might jeopardize his status. Philippe aimed to marry Mademoiselle Amelie de Soulanges and sought to become a general, receiving various military honors to secure his ambitions.

Meanwhile, Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother, Madame Bridau, who managed a lottery office. Despite Joseph's talent, he faced financial difficulties and criticism, while Philippe's success overshadowed him. Agathe, unaware of her son's struggles, believed Philippe's rise justified his neglect of them.

In a moment of desperation, Agathe wrote to Philippe, asking for financial help for Joseph. Philippe's cold response devastated her, leading to her decline in health. As she faced death, she sought solace from her confessor, who revealed the painful truth about her sons' contrasting natures. Agathe's realization of her misplaced affections for Philippe deepened her sorrow, and she passed away shortly after.

Joseph, heartbroken, buried his mother alone, while Philippe, indifferent to her death, was driven away by Joseph's scathing letter. Joseph, now bereft of his mother, found solace in the company of friends who supported him through his grief.

Later, Joseph received a letter from Flore, Philippe's wife, pleading for help as she faced destitution. Joseph, moved by her plight, sought to assist her, revealing the depths of Philippe's cruelty. Bixiou and Joseph visited Flore, discovering her in a dire state, a shadow of her former self. They arranged for her to receive medical care, but her condition was grave.

As Flore's health deteriorated, Joseph and his friends plotted to expose Philippe's neglect and cruelty. They aimed to ensure Flore received justice, while Philippe continued to rise in society, oblivious to the consequences of his actions. Eventually, Philippe's fortunes crumbled during the July Revolution of 1830, leading to his downfall.

In contrast, Joseph inherited Philippe's estate and found success in his art, becoming Comte de Brambourg. Despite his achievements, he remained humble, often reflecting on the tragic fate of his family and the lessons learned from their tumultuous lives.
