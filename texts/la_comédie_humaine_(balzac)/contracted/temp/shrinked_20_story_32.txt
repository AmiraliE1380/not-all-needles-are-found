DEDICATION

To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

Dear Nodier, this book exposes actions hidden behind domestic doors where, too often, divine intervention replaces human justice. Its morals concern family and motherhood and the harms that follow when paternal authority—the traditional tribunal for domestic wrongs—is weakened. A loving mother cannot substitute for patriarchal power. The story defends indissoluble marriage and warns against unchecked selfishness in a society that exalts wealth and success while the law proves impotent. I hope such a society will return to Catholic moral guidance through different education.

In “Scenes from Military Life” I will portray noble self-sacrifice and also show war’s corrupting influence on those at home who live as if on a battlefield. You have observed our times keenly; I entrust this tale to your name as a shield from criticism.

Your sincere admirer,  
De Balzac  
Paris, November 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the people of Issoudun disliked Dr. Rouget; gossip blamed him for his beautiful wife’s unhappiness, though the household’s true nature remained secret. Madame Rouget, née Descoings, had been frail from youth. They had a son, Jean-Jacques, and, ten years later, a daughter, Agathe.

Rouget’s insistence on masculine firmness caused much female suffering. The Descoings family—once wealthy wool merchants who invested in forfeited estates—were rich and miserly. Descoings, Rouget’s brother-in-law, left Issoudun for Paris, failed as a grocer, and later sought a wealthy widow. Expecting his wife’s death, Rouget sent Agathe to Paris to secure Descoings’ favor and a share of any inheritance.

Agathe, admired in town, provoked a quarrel between Rouget and their friend Lousteau, who then left. Madame Rouget, learning her daughter had gone, predicted she would never see Agathe again—a prophecy fulfilled as her health declined, worsened by her son’s neglect; Jean-Jacques offered little comfort.

Agathe reached Paris as Descoings was imprisoned for imprudent political remarks during the famine and was later executed alongside the poet André Chénier—an event that silenced local grocers and left a sharper mark on the community than posterity would note.

Agathe married Bridau, a ministry secretary, with Rouget’s approval; Rouget hoped to profit from any Descoings inheritance. Bridau, captivated by Agathe, supported Napoleon, earned promotion in 1804, and worked relentlessly. In 1808 he died of overwork just as another promotion—intended by the Emperor—arrived too late.

After Bridau’s death Agathe received no letters from Issoudun. Her mother died shortly before the birth of her second son; her father died around the Emperor’s coronation, an event that distracted Bridau. Jean-Jacques remained absent. Madame Hochon, Agathe’s godmother, warned she would inherit nothing unless she granted power of attorney to her brother; Agathe hesitated. Bridau’s savings provided only a modest income; Agathe and Madame Descoings lived on twelve thousand francs.

By 1809 Madame Descoings, sixty-five but fond of theater and fine food, was secretly gambling and hiding mounting lottery debts while concealing family scandals about her son Bixiou. Devoted and refusing to remarry, Agathe took on maternal and household duties. Her limited judgment and blind trust in Madame Descoings proved disastrous when the debts emerged; Agathe sold possessions and liquidated investments to settle them—a decisive turning point in their lives.

One of the bleakest stretches of Paris was rue Mazarin between rue Guenegard and rue de Seine, shadowed by college walls and Cardinal Mazarin’s library. On the third floor of a damp, cold house there lived the poor widow Madame Bridau (Agathe). From her windows she watched the Institute opposite and the students’ uproar at examinations, and to brighten the room she defied police rules by setting boxes of earth on the sill for little gardens.

The shallow house, reached by a winding staircase, held three rooms—a dining room, a small salon, and a bedroom—plus a kitchen and two single rooms. Agathe chose it because it was cheap (four hundred francs a year), near her sons’ school and in a familiar neighborhood. The interior matched the building’s dreariness: yellowed wallpaper, unglazed tiles, and only essential furniture. The salon held an Aubusson carpet given to her and a mahogany sofa bearing a pastel portrait of her late husband, Bridau, which captured his noble essence; a Napoleon hung above a card table.

After Bridau’s death Agathe’s life narrowed. She filled the room with two large birdcages—canaries and Java sparrows—and millet, chickweed and cat hair littered the floor and furniture, outward signs of grief and neglected personal care. She preserved everything belonging to her husband, even wrapping his last pen with a note. Grief seemed to erode her sense of womanhood; she devoted herself entirely to her children and to managing the precarious household.

Agathe lived for her sons and worried ceaselessly over their finances. The Emperor provided three hundred francs a year for each child, with scholarships supplementing this. Her aunt, Madame Descoings, on the second floor, assigned three thousand francs from her annuity to help Agathe; the Descoings themselves now lived modestly on twelve hundred francs a year and shared dinners with the two widows. Madame Descoings doted on Philippe and Joseph, indulging them with treats and small sums. Distrustful of the future, Agathe scrimped and saved—she set aside three thousand francs from her pension—hoping to secure some inheritance for her boys. Thus both widows had passed from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty, each for different reasons.

The house, the students’ uproar, and her husband’s portrait formed the setting that would shape her youngest son, Joseph Bridau, into a notable painter of the modern French school. Philippe, the elder, resembled his mother in looks but had a bold demeanor often mistaken for courage; at fifteen his charm reinforced Agathe’s hopes. Joseph, three years younger, took after his father in less favorable ways: untidy, often scolded, and plainly less favored by his mother.

Joseph’s artistic bent was sparked by a visit to the Tuileries, where a caricature on an Institute wall captivated him and made him want to draw. He began sneaking into the Institute courtyard, enduring teasing from older students until his persistence won their respect. He later visited the sculptor Chaudet’s atelier; Chaudet recognized and encouraged his talent. Agathe feared art meant hardship and tried to dissuade him, but Chaudet defended the vocation as a divine calling, insisting true talent must be followed.

Meanwhile Philippe, enchanted by military life, secretly petitioned the Emperor to enlist. He was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr and rose rapidly, becoming a captain by nineteen. After his scholarship was withdrawn, Joseph tried to earn a living by art with Madame Descoings’s encouragement. In 1816 Joseph persuaded his mother to let him convert the adjoining garret into an atelier—partly financed by Madame Descoings—installing a skylight and continuing studies under Gros and Schinner. Unlike the two widows, who treated painting as a trade, Joseph embraced it passionately.

Political unrest simmered in France. Philippe was implicated in conspiracies, briefly arrested, then released for lack of evidence, though his military pay was cut and Agathe feared for him. Friends urged the widows to send him abroad for safety; though she resisted the idea of him serving a foreign power, Philippe joined General Lallemand in the United States to help found the Champ d’Asile—an enterprise that failed. Agathe had earlier equipped him with ten thousand francs; by late 1817 the household had adjusted to reduced means and Joseph lived frugally to support the family while his talent developed.

In 1818 the notary Roguin absconded with funds intended for Madame Descoings. Soon after, Philippe sent a bill for a thousand francs, having lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. Agathe, distraught, blamed herself for urging his departure. Despite her own difficulties, Madame Descoings continued to support them. Desperate, Joseph appealed to Gérard, who arranged for him to paint copies of a portrait of Louis XVIII; Joseph worked tirelessly, selling panels to cover Philippe’s debt.

When Philippe returned, Agathe welcomed him warmly but failed to notice how America had hardened him. He became self-centered—drinking, gambling and swaggering—and lived off others, disappearing for days in cafés and gaming halls. He refused to serve the Bourbons, neglected family duties, and sought to impress Mariette; a proposal to turn his experiences into journalism and a cashier’s post at a newspaper, secured by Agathe and Madame Descoings, brought temporary relief but little reform. Joseph felt increasingly overshadowed and unappreciated. Philippe’s return and altered behavior strained relationships and finances, exposing the consequences of choices, the limits of maternal devotion, and the fragile balance between ambition and family duty that governed the Bridau household.

Three months on, the colonel—who “ate for four”—still complained about the food while the widows, reluctant to press him, kept paying though he gave nothing. His mother and Madame Descoings avoided reminding him of his promise; months passed without the “tigers with five claws,” as Léon Gozlan joked, migrating from Philippe’s pocket into the household purse. The colonel eased his conscience by seldom dining at home. “Well, he is happy,” his mother said, “he has a place.”

Prompted by a feuilleton edited by Vernou, Mariette debuted at the Porte-Saint-Martin and triumphed with Begrand. A wealthy general, enchanted and connected to Philippe and Giroudeau, became her impresario and arranged the debut—an instance of solidarity among admirers. Bixiou soon told his grandmother and Agathe that Philippe, the cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. The widows were appalled; Agathe deemed stage women morally suspect and both feared extravagant spending. “Do you think my brother will spend on Mariette? Such women ruin rich men,” Joseph protested. Bixiou suggested Mariette might be stolen by higher-ranking admirers; he’d even heard an ambassador was infatuated.

Partly for financial reasons, Philippe proposed; Mariette declined, perhaps suspecting his intent or valuing freedom, hoping instead for a Grand Opera engagement. Over the next year Philippe visited his mother only twice a month, spending days at the office, the theatre, or with Mariette, enjoying pleasures and mixing with Tullia and other entertainers. The carnival lasted eighteen months but grew stormy. After her Opera debut in January 1821 Mariette attracted a distinguished duke. Philippe, driven by passion, began dipping into the funds he managed—taking eleven hundred francs by May. Mariette left for London to seek patrons while the temporary opera house was prepared. Despite her infidelities and using him as a stepping stone, Philippe remained deeply in love.

Forced to relinquish Mariette, Philippe returned to his mother’s apartment, unable to imagine life without recent pleasures. Suicide crossed his mind—not just over the deficit but because he could not bear life without Mariette. He entered Joseph’s studio, where Joseph was copying a picture for a dealer and discussing earnings. Pale and subdued, Philippe asked how long his portrait would take, saying he wanted to leave his mother a likeness. When Joseph asked if he was leaving again, Philippe said he was going never to return and, pressed, confessed he had taken money and could not live with the dishonor.

Joseph tried to reassure him that repayment would save his honor, but Philippe, despairing, fled downstairs, leaving Joseph anxious. Joseph told Madame Descoings, who was horrified. Agathe was devastated, crying at the thought of Philippe’s dishonor. That day Philippe calmly submitted his accounts, claiming he’d retained the money for safekeeping. He then went to a gambling house—winning at first, then losing everything. Returning home, he found his family’s concern and affection, which briefly comforted him. “Promise not to kill yourself, and all shall be forgotten,” his mother pleaded. Philippe, sensing the plea had worked, feigned gratitude and affection; they shared dinner and he felt momentarily relieved.

The next day Agathe seemed older and more anxious, aware the family’s finances were precarious. Madame Descoings suggested consulting Desroches to manage the affair and preserve their honor. Desroches, diplomatically, advised Agathe to sign a transfer of funds and petition for Philippe’s reinstatement in the army, assuring that he would regain his rank and easing some fear. Yet Agathe’s faith was shaken and she lived in constant dread of his downfall.

Philippe returned to a life of leisure—gambling, drinking, looking disheveled and indifferent to the family’s plight. His love for Mariette had hardened him; he treated women with contempt. Joseph, focusing on painting, gained recognition and Madame Descoings’s support; she pinned her hopes on his future. Noticing discrepancies, Joseph began to suspect Philippe and confided in Madame Descoings. When Joseph discovered Philippe had indeed taken money from him, he felt betrayed; despite anger he still struggled to confront his brother. Madame Descoings urged sympathy for Philippe’s struggles, but Joseph remained wary of his influence on the family’s future.

Despite Philippe’s careful nightly gambling, he was sometimes “cleaned out.” Needing his usual ten francs, he began stealing from family—pilfering from his brother and whatever Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a dream in which Philippe robbed her, Agathe recognized his decline and begged him to ask for money instead of stealing. Within two weeks her savings were gone; she took worsted work for twenty sous a day, endured sleepless nights waiting for Philippe, and watched his increasing drunkenness and mood swings.

When Madame Descoings suggested new clothes, Philippe retorted that his mother had no money and that he had mortgaged his pension. He admitted borrowing from Giroudeau, calling it a “debt of honor.” Agathe excused him as out of his element, while Joseph criticized his neglect of appearance and duty. Philippe sank further into debauchery and gambling, losing the steadiness and good looks he once had.

One day Philippe asked Joseph for money to pay a tobacconist; Joseph refused. Philippe then guessed where Madame kept her savings and, expecting she’d be out buying Christmas treats, broke into her room. He found twenty napoleons hidden in the mattress and took the money to gamble.

At the gambling house Philippe won substantial sums over several sessions and, intoxicated by success, amassed over one hundred fifty thousand francs by night’s end. Pressed by greed, he continued and lost everything. The next morning he awoke in despair, knowing he had ruined his family.

Agathe and Madame Descoings waited for Philippe at Christmas dinner; when he failed to show they worried. Madame had pinned hopes on a lottery stake, but upon checking her savings she found the money gone and fainted. The concierge confirmed Philippe had been in the apartment. Agathe returned pale and shaken; the family confronted Philippe. He dismissed their accusations, claiming he’d taken what was owed him. A fight erupted between Joseph and Philippe, and Madame Descoings suffered a stroke amid the chaos.

Joseph offered his mother money afterward, but she refused, clinging to lottery hope. When the winning numbers were announced it was clear she’d missed her chance; she died shortly after. Philippe, recovering from illness, was told by Agathe that their finances had collapsed and urged to rejoin the army. Indifferent and feeling abandoned, he accepted the money she reluctantly offered and left to seek the company of those he called true friends. Agathe, heartbroken, prayed for her son and resigned herself to the loss of the child she once loved.

By February 1822 Madame Bridau had been moved into the attic above the kitchen of her former apartment, recently occupied by her son Philippe. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph set out to make her comfortable: he rearranged the garret, added better furnishings, and took Agathe daily to a table d’hôte that cost him nearly a hundred francs a month. Alarmed by the expense and urged by her confessor, Agathe accepted a seven-hundred-franc-a-year post at a lottery office that demanded long hours.

She adapted to the routine—preparing Joseph’s breakfasts, mending his clothes, keeping the studio tidy—and aided his work despite having no artistic training. Their bond deepened through these small acts of tenderness. They spoke often of Philippe—Agathe feared his reckless life, but Joseph dismissed her worries, convinced Philippe was prospering in New York.

One afternoon Joseph visited Philippe, who worked at a newspaper. Philippe invited Joseph to dine; Joseph declined to stay with his mother. When he described Philippe afterward, Agathe felt hopeful. She decorated Joseph’s studio and prepared an elaborate breakfast for a sitting for Philippe’s portrait; overwhelmed, she hid and watched the two sons together. After the sitting Philippe invited them to a sumptuous dinner, which Agathe eagerly accepted while Joseph felt uneasy at the ostentation. At another sitting she learned more of Philippe’s circle—journalists and actresses—and heard boastful plans, though he remained vague about money.

Later Joseph postponed a session for a commission. Philippe, mistaking a valuable copy of a Rubens for an original, took it and sold it for a fraction of its worth. Discovering the theft devastated Joseph; Agathe was heartbroken, feeling she had lost her son to vice.

Two months after, an old officer told Agathe Philippe was ill and needed money. Working at the lottery, she had little to spare and at first refused to help the son she felt she had banished from her heart; Joseph insisted Philippe go to the hospital. When she later found Philippe destitute on the street, she gave what she could and despaired.

Agathe then learned Philippe had been implicated in a conspiracy and was arrested; she fainted. Joseph consulted Desroches, who urged them to secure the family inheritance and gather evidence of undue influence; he planned to visit Issoudun at the holidays to help. Agathe’s letter to her godmother brought a discouraging reply about an unhelpful brother. Agathe and Joseph set off for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to face the consequences and Agathe to confront their deep estrangement.

Armand Peremet’s excavations in Issoudun uncovered a fifth‑century basilica rebuilt with stones from a Roman temple to Isis; the town’s name—Isis + dun—suggests Celtic origins.

Medieval Issoudun was powerful: Richard Coeur‑de‑Lion built a tower above it; later the town sheltered mercenaries Henry II sent against his son. Its prominence waned. About 8,000 vine‑growers burned tax offices and asserted autonomy in the 1830 insurrection.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun had 16–17 thousand inhabitants, prospering on wool (blankets, gloves); by Louis XIV it had some elegance. Today about 12,000 live there. The wine economy suffers from outdated practices: resistant growers produce poor wine, sapping prosperity.

This reflects broader stagnation: locals opposed a Paris–Toulouse road, fearing higher food prices and preferring tradition to progress. With nobility gone, a complacent bourgeoisie of intermarried leading families runs an ineffectual administration; the clergy offers little.

Against this backdrop youth, feeling trapped, formed the "Knights of Idleness," led by Maxence Gilet—rumored an official’s illegitimate son who rose from poverty to challenge the bourgeoisie. Their disruptive, creative pranks—staged from a tavern run by Père and La Cognette—express youthful frustration.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank: an old Spanish ex-prisoner left his empty cart at the foot of the Issoudun tower, and Maxence proposed hauling it up. In an hour they dismantled and hoisted the cart like artillery, reassembled it above, and the hungry, thirsty youths returned to Mère Cognette’s to await the Spaniard’s astonishment.

Their amusements varied—hunting, harvesting grapes, winter skating—and they treated Max as their idol. François Hochon and Baruch Borniche, Madame Hochon’s grandsons, regarded Max as family, relying on him for money and the adventures they lacked at home. After a night drinking twelve to fifteen bottles of wine, François and Baruch mentioned that Madame Bridau and her son were coming and suggested Rouget’s sister might claim the property when he died. Max, thirty-four and soldierly, concealed his unease behind confidence. When François flippantly used a contemptuous nickname for Flore Brazier, Max snapped, defending her and, for the first time, spoke openly about Flore—Rouget’s servant-mistress, scorned by society—and his attachment to her.

Max explained that people assumed he lived off Flore’s supposed money and claimed he planned to marry her when Rouget died. He denied mercenary motives, insisting he loved her and preferred her to Mademoiselle Fichet, the town heiress courted by Goddet. He asked the Knights—within the bounds of their fooleries—to help him against Parisians if needed, clarifying he would not ask them to kill or poison anyone. Moved, they pledged loyalty. They joked and toasted, completed the cart prank, and slipped away at half-past three. Max returned to Place Saint-Jean still worried; his time in Cabrera had taught him to hide unease.

Rouget’s forty thousand francs a year anchored Max’s attachment to Flore, but news of legitimate heirs unnerved him: Rouget’s savings, kept in a doctor’s name, plus some seven hundred and fifty thousand francs lodged with various notaries could not be quickly liquidated in a gossiping town. Furious that Flore had never mentioned her sister and nephews in seven years, Max concluded they needed to be eliminated to secure the property. Exhausted, he used a pass-key to enter Père Rouget’s house and went to bed, hoping clearer thoughts would come.

The narrative then traces how Flore Brazier became known as the “Rabouilleuse” and quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s household. One day in September 1799, Doctor Rouget found a captivating little girl by a brook, wet and in rags, who said she was from Vatan and had been catching crabs for her uncle Brazier. He brought her home, paid the uncle, and provided food, clothing, and instruction. Flore disliked books; after a few years formal schooling was abandoned, though the doctor continued to instruct her for five years despite town gossip. Born in 1787 amid Revolutionary upheaval, she grew up with shaken traditional values and a pragmatic self-interest. As Rouget neared death, his notary urged him to provide for her; his cynical refusal to leave her anything revealed bitterness and set the stage for the inheritance struggle now threatening Max’s position and Flore’s hopes.

Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father’s death, though Flore Brazier did; the old doctor had made his son miserable but given Flore the country comforts she valued. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore what would become of her, Jean-Jacques showed animation for the first time.

At seventeen Flore still had the beauty that had attracted the doctor, though she was growing plump like other countrywomen. Jean-Jacques hesitated to speak; his father’s death and bequests weighed on him, and Flore was puzzled by his silence.

When his father died Jean-Jacques was thirty-seven: timid, submissive, almost childlike. His torpor left him passionate but lacking confidence—awkward, unattractive, solitary. Too late the doctor had recognized his son’s weaknesses. Wanting him to marry but fearing loss of control over his property, the doctor sought a daughter-in-law and tried to instill avarice in Jean-Jacques to give him some financial instinct.

Secretly Jean-Jacques loved Flore, the only woman whose educated presence he could tolerate; he even took pride in the instruction his father had given her, thinking it would make her easier to win. Flore, unaware of his feelings, waited for him to declare himself, but he remained silent.

After three weeks of uncertainty, Flore awoke one night to find Jean-Jacques crouched outside her door and took it as proof of his love; the next day she found him less repellent. Their halting conversations—about returning to the fields and her honesty—revealed his growing affection despite his difficulty expressing it.

Ten months later Jean-Jacques brightened and tended his appearance, pleasing Flore; the townspeople noticed. Fanchette, who had disapproved of Flore’s role, eventually left; Flore had anticipated this and learned to cook and run the house, surpassing the former cook and taking pride in order.

Over nine years Flore gained complete control: familiar, commanding, she became the household’s dominant figure while Jean-Jacques, childlike and dependent, relied on her. By 1815 she was at the height of her beauty; in 1816 she fell for Maxence Gilet, a penniless officer. Pressing Jean-Jacques about his wealth, she suggested inviting Max. With Max in the home, Flore shirked duties, hired a new cook, refined her appearance, bought Max a horse, and took on a servant. Gradually Flore and Max became central and Jean-Jacques receded, increasingly dependent on them.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim her threatened inheritance, Jean-Jacques Rouget had become almost vegetative. After Max provided money, Flore improved Rouget’s care, feeding him from Vedie’s kitchen. Better tended, he nevertheless sank into utter submission; his devotion to Flore was his only motive and made him seem far older than fifty-seven.

Max’s apartment reflected that devotion: provincial comforts, elegant wallpaper and mahogany, with a rush matting that muffled footsteps so Max could return late without waking anyone — not even Rouget, who remained ignorant of Max’s nocturnal activities.

One morning Flore, in a cotton gown, found Max asleep and noted his late return. He told her Rouget’s family were coming to Issoudun, likely to evict them. Flore vowed confrontation; Max urged caution, asking for coffee and suggesting they plan later. As she began making it, Baruch interrupted: Fario was searching for his cart.

Max joined the crowd. Fario, a small peculiar man, claimed his cart had been stolen. Max led him to the tower embankment where, to everyone’s surprise, the cart was found at the top. Amid laughter Max theatrically tossed it down, humiliating Fario and turning the episode into town gossip.

The incident heightened concern. François informed Max the Bridaus would arrive that night. Max decided they should not seem hostile and proposed letting the Hochons handle driving them out. Meanwhile Flore, furious that Rouget’s sister and nephew were coming, punished Rouget by withdrawing affection, leaving him bewildered and distressed. Vedie urged him to placate Flore. Rouget denied inviting them and blamed Madame Hochon.

Max counseled politeness for appearances, advised Rouget to keep his will, and schemed to have the Hochons remove the Bridaus. Opposite Rouget’s, the modest Hochon household — Monsieur Hochon, a famously frugal retired tax collector, Madame Hochon, and their hopeful granddaughter Adolphine — prepared for visits. The town split between cautious bourgeoisie and Max’s loyal Knights of Idleness; the Fario affair showed how fragile reputations and power were, and how a few calculated acts could shape the looming struggle over inheritance and influence.

Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales in Issoudun at three. Though weary, Agathe (Madame Bridau) felt revived by familiar sights. News spread fast: Madame Hochon greeted Agathe like a daughter, clinging to that affection after years of loss.

Agathe met the reserved Monsieur Hochon and the household: the young men Baruch and François, and shy Adolphine, who noticed Joseph. Monsieur Hochon, miserly, remarked on Joseph’s recent hospital stay and hunger; the youths were struck by his looks. “This is my son, the painter; my good Joseph,” Agathe said proudly. Joseph’s blunt remark about resembling his father was misread by Madame Hochon as affection for Agathe. Baruch rushed off to spread the news, emphasizing Joseph’s striking appearance.

Townspeople quickly gossiped, likening Joseph to a brigand and airing disdain for artists; many planned to call on the Hochons that evening to satisfy their curiosity about the Parisians.

Installed in modest attic rooms, Joseph contrasted his Parisian artistic life with the drab provincial existence. Dinner revealed the Hochons’ frugality—plain bouilli, hard eggs, and scant accompaniments. Accustomed to better fare, Joseph was disappointed but tried to adapt. Agathe learned her childhood home had been sold to the Borniches, and Madame Hochon braced for an influx of visitors. Joseph noted the family’s strict ways and limited hospitality.

After dinner the women gathered in the salon. Agathe and Madame Hochon exchanged life stories—Agathe recounting hardships, Madame Hochon offering hard-won advice—continually interrupted by callers eager to meet the Parisians. Joseph remained detached, studying visitors with a critical eye that made them uneasy. Later, the two women resumed their heartfelt exchange.

Elsewhere the Knights of Idleness convened to plot mischief; Max proposed a rat prank to spoil merchant Fario’s grain to drive the Bridaus away without harming the Hochons.

Next morning Joseph glimpsed his brother Jean-Jacques Rouget with the conspicuous Flore Brazier; Joseph admired her beauty while Agathe feared her influence. A letter inviting Joseph to dine arrived, which the Hochons suspected Flore had arranged. Monsieur Hochon advised Joseph to watch closely and speak frankly about his nephews. With tensions and rival schemes in motion, a fraught family reunion was imminent.

At four o’clock Joseph crossed from the Rouget to the Hochon house, where Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph greeted his uncle Rouget, Flore, and Maxence Gilet with light banter, admired Flore and the house, and noted Max’s good looks. Baruch and Francois arrived and Flore suggested a walk before dinner. While she prepared, Joseph examined the house’s paintings, which Rouget said had come from the Descoings and been bought during the Revolution. Ecstatic, Joseph inspected more upstairs with Max. Flore urged Rouget to give the paintings to Joseph, arguing Rouget didn’t care for them.

Rouget offered the pictures to Joseph—without frames—and Flore proposed paying him four thousand francs to have copies made. Overwhelmed, Joseph accepted. News spread through Issoudun and enhanced Flore’s standing. Dinner that night included local dignitaries. Joseph, somewhat tipsy, went home at eleven; Rouget, having drunk heavily, was carried to bed. Left alone, Max and Flore agreed it was better to befriend the Bridaus than antagonize them and planned to send the pictures to Joseph the next day.

The next morning the paintings were propped against Rouget’s wall. Agathe, Joseph’s mother, visited Rouget to thank him after Hochon urged her—he feared the Bridaus’ influence and warned that Joseph had been fooled. Hochon urged Agathe to cultivate Flore to gain access to Rouget. Agathe was warmly received; Rouget lay ill from the previous night. Flore feigned servitude and Agathe hinted Rouget should marry her, but Flore insisted Rouget would remember her in his will.

In the following days the Knights of Idleness—local troublemakers—released rats and mice into Fario’s grain store, causing havoc. The Bridaus’ hold on Rouget seemed to strengthen: Flore claimed Rouget was too weak for walks and needed a carriage; they bought a shabby vehicle that drew town attention. Rouget signed a power of attorney granting Max authority over his investments, while Flore handled local transactions. Fario returned to find his store devastated and suspected Max’s involvement; he began gathering evidence against the Knights.

Agathe believed Rouget had not made a will and intended to ask him directly, while Hochon urged her to stay longer and use religious influence to counter Max and Flore—advice she reluctantly considered. Progress for the Bridaus was slow; Hochon stressed persistence.

Meanwhile Joseph, sensing the paintings’ real value, sent them to Desroches in Paris for appraisal, convinced they were worth far more than the 4,000-franc copying job. Agathe warned him to keep their worth quiet, but word leaked and searches for old artworks spread through Issoudun. Max regretted aspects of their scheme, now fearful of the Church’s influence if the paintings’ value became public.

Letters from Desroches and Schinner advised keeping Agathe in Issoudun, warned about Max, and suggested Rouget might already have a will—so the Bridaus’ prospects depended on a struggle between Flore and the Church. Disturbed, Joseph decided to return to Paris. He bade Rouget farewell, resolved to rely on his talent, and planned later to return the pictures to Rouget. The Hochon family suspected Max and Flore had known the paintings’ value and manipulated Joseph; Agathe defended her son, but Hochon warned that returning the paintings might jeopardize any chance at inheritance.

Between midnight and one the Knights of Idleness fed the town’s dogs; their foray lasted until three. After supper at Cognette’s, Max, turning from rue l’Avenier into Grande rue at half-past four, was stabbed by Fario, who had lurked in a recess. Fario fled toward Vilatte, wiped the knife on his handkerchief, washed it in the Rivière forcée, and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne, where his watchman later found him in bed.

Max fell, cried out, and alarmed Lousteau-Prangin and young Goddet, who ran up the street calling for help; the townspeople, used to false alarms, mostly stayed in bed. Max fainted. On regaining consciousness and seeing the wound was not fatal, he seized the chance to accuse someone: “I think I recognized that cursed painter!” Lousteau-Prangin hurried to his father, the judge. Max was carried home by Cognette, Goddet, and others. Goddet, suspicious of Max’s words, asked Gritte if Joseph Bridau was in bed; she replied Joseph had left at half-past four, restless all night. Shocked, they rushed to Rouget’s house and found Max in a bloody shirt.

Joseph had spent the night anxious about how the townspeople might view him, fearing they would think he had robbed his uncle. He longed to flee to Paris and confront Max; at dawn he walked to the poplar avenue toward Tivoli to steady his nerves. Max, plotting to rid the town of the Parisians, told Flore they could use the incident: pretend he was dying and have Joseph arrested. Flore feigned tears and spread that Joseph had attacked Max out of jealousy over paintings. Inflamed, the crowd demanded Joseph’s capture and grew hostile.

Monsieur Hochon, warned by Gritte, locked himself in, fearing for his safety and wealth. Madame Hochon, anxious for their daughter Agathe, hoped she would sleep through the shock; Agathe awoke, learned of the accusations, and fainted at the sight of the gendarmes. Monsieur Hochon pressed the magistrates to act; the lieutenant reported a large crowd demanding justice.

Joseph was soon spotted returning home and nearly seized by the mob. Gendarmes intervened and, to protect him, took him to the Palais. There he calmly insisted on his innocence and gave an alibi, recounting his morning movements. The magistrates were somewhat swayed by his composure but kept him in custody while verifying his statements. Joseph wrote to his mother, reassuring her he would soon be free.

Max, recovering, recalled a crucial detail: the attacker had worn list shoes, while Joseph had left in boots. He wrote to the magistrate urging Joseph’s release. As the inquiry proceeded, witnesses confirmed Joseph’s alibi and he was freed. He returned to his mother’s relief; the ordeal strengthened their bond and Joseph felt grateful.

The townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians; Max and Flore were pleased. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris; Joseph sought refuge at the Comte de Sérizy’s chateau to work. Philippe, punished for his role in the unrest, was sentenced to five years’ surveillance and exile to Autun; Desroches intervened to change his residence to Issoudun and urged him to reclaim his inheritance from Uncle Rouget. Determined to restore the family fortunes and confront Max and the Rabouilleuse, Philippe prepared to return to Issoudun and, after a brief dinner, departed.

On All-Souls’ Day, November 2, Philippe Bridau arrived at the Issoudun commissary and was told to stay on rue l’Avenier. His presence as a banished officer linked to a painter falsely accused in the affair unnerved the town. Maxence Gilet, recently recovered, had converted all of Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash, alarming many. After consulting notary Héron—who thought Rouget’s heirs should thank him for preventing Gilet’s profit—Monsieur Hochon resolved to protect the Bridaus, long resentful of Gilet and fearful of losing property.

Gilet and Flore Brazier received Philippe, confident they could manipulate Rouget to secure his funds. Philippe arrived in tattered clothes; his disordered appearance showed hardship, but his refusal to beg and blunt pride impressed Flore and Gilet. Gilet sensed a latent threat but avoided open conflict.

Philippe visited the Hochons, meeting curiosity and fear—Madame Hochon especially linked him to past tragedies. Candid about living on little, he won some household members with charm and honesty. Asking for secrecy, he requested from Monsieur Hochon the names of officers hostile to Gilet—Mignonnet and Carpentier—and then befriended them. He impressed them with knowledge of the recent conspiracy against the Bourbons and, by careful conduct, integrated into the local military circle while concealing his aims.

He dined regularly with Mignonnet and Carpentier, cultivated a respectable image, took fencing lessons and practiced shooting. His disdain for Gilet was evident, yet Gilet remained cautious.

Philippe’s inquiries led him to Fario, who revealed the schemes of the Knights of Idleness. Suspecting Gilet and Flore planned to secure Rouget’s income and flee, Philippe urged Rouget to take walks to loosen Flore’s influence and warned that Gilet sought a power of attorney to access Rouget’s funds. They agreed to watch Rouget’s movements. When Gilet proposed going to Vatan to secure the money and marry Flore—who, fearful of Philippe’s influence, reluctantly consented—tensions rose and Philippe’s resolve to protect his family and restore their honor hardened.

Walking arm in arm with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, Philippe contemplated the looming confrontation between Colonel Bridau and Captain Maxence Gilet—the town hoped it would settle the struggle over Rouget’s fortune. He bluntly told Rouget that Flore loved Maxence and wanted Rouget out of the way so she could marry him. Though Rouget still loved her, Philippe insisted he could make her obedient again—by killing Maxence—if Rouget would refuse to sign the power of attorney before December third. That document, Philippe warned, would let them sell Rouget’s assets and flee to Paris. Rouget recoiled at Philippe taking Maxence’s place; Philippe argued that fear and force were the only ways to handle women.

He instructed Mignonnet and Carpentier to arrange banquet seating so he could force a public confrontation with Gilet. They agreed but warned of Gilet’s dueling skill. At home Kouski wept: Flore had left with Vedie. Rouget’s frantic search failed. Gilet, feeling pressure and urged by Captain Potel to face Bridau, remained focused on securing Rouget’s fortune first, hoping to transfer assets to Flore.

Aware of these moves, Philippe prepared to counter them, planning to use Baruch to secure the power of attorney for Rouget while keeping Gilet occupied. Rouget drafted a letter threatening to revoke his will in Flore’s favor unless she returned; Philippe encouraged it to force her back. Town gossip swelled about the impending duel and the estate’s fate. When Flore returned, Philippe asserted control and warned her of consequences if she disobeyed. Rouget, emotionally fragile, clung to her; by morning the town speculated on the duel, and Philippe, confident in his banquet plan, believed it would decide both Rouget’s fate and his own.

Around four the next afternoon, officers of the old army gathered at the place du Marché in Issoudun for a coronation banquet, awaiting Colonel Philippe Bridau. Conversation turned to Max’s recent dismissal from old Rouget’s house; only Potel and Renard defended him, while an old sub‑lieutenant said Max should have resisted. When Max arrived he was coldly received—sharply contrasted with Philippe’s warm welcome—which stiffened Max’s resolve to confront him.

At dinner Philippe toasted the Grand Army and several soldiers echoed him. Mignonnet toasted those who had tried to restore the Emperor’s son, and Max, whose reputation lent weight, rose with an ironic wish to meet again next year. The meal ended tensely as Philippe and Max exchanged barbs: Philippe stayed composed while Max struggled to control himself. A duel was arranged for the next morning with sabres, Goddet, a former army surgeon, to attend.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon urged Philippe to pray; Madame Hochon offered a relic, which he declined, though she vowed to pray for him. The next morning both appeared composed, but Max felt intimidated by Philippe’s superior stance. Max attacked first but was quickly disarmed; Philippe paused to let him recover his weapon, increasing the strain. When they resumed they exchanged fierce blows; Philippe was wounded but delivered a fatal stroke. Max died—his ambitions and the Order of Idleness ended—which brought relief to the town.

Flore Brazier, devastated, fell ill with fever and inflammation. Philippe, recovering, wrote letters outlining plans to secure his inheritance and manage Flore’s situation, noting her illness prevented her from fleeing and that he intended to use her to his advantage.

By February 1823, urged by others and influenced by Philippe’s suggestion that marriage would secure her future, a recovering Flore was persuaded to accept his arranged match. The contract granted her a substantial dower and a life annuity. Agathe, Philippe’s mother, delighted in his apparent reform and the town’s approval, but the family lawyer Desroches warned that Philippe’s cunning would likely keep Rouget’s property from Flore and Joseph.

Once married, Philippe asserted control: demanding a power of attorney from Rouget, using Flore to manage her uncle’s affairs while keeping her under close watch, and seeking to reestablish himself in the army and in Paris. Backed by local endorsements and letters to the minister of war, he was reinstated as lieutenant‑colonel.

Lieutenant‑Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the ailing Rouget, whom he escorted to the Treasury to redirect his uncle’s income to himself. The old man and the Rabouilleuse were soon absorbed into extravagant Parisian life; Rouget overindulged after a lavish supper with the dancer Lolotte and died, said to be of indigestion.

Madame Rouget prospered in Paris while Philippe kept Mariette to watch her. In October 1823 he went to Issoudun to settle his uncle’s estate, rapidly amassing 1,600,000 francs (excluding valuable paintings held by Monsieur Hochon) and entrusting the money to Mongenod and Sons. Philippe married, legally safeguarded his wife’s fortune in case she died childless, bought her a furnished apartment, and secretly purchased a grand hôtel on rue de Clichy, lavishing funds on renovations.

The rise of Charles X aided the Duc de Chaulieu’s circle; because Philippe supplied information about past conspiracies, he was made lieutenant‑colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment and ennobled as Comte de Brambourg. Ever ambitious, he sought further honors—an aide‑de‑camp post to the Dauphin and ultimately a general’s rank—alienating old friends and plotting to marry Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges to cement his position.

Meanwhile Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist, supported by his mother Agathe, who ran a lottery office. Despite talent he endured poverty and scorn while Philippe’s success overshadowed him. Agathe, deceived by appearances and broken by Philippe’s refusal of help, died; Joseph buried her alone but found solace with friends. When Flore later begged aid, Joseph and Bixiou arranged care and conspired to expose Philippe. Philippe’s fortunes crumbled in the July Revolution of 1830; Joseph inherited the estate, achieved artistic success, became Comte de Brambourg, and remained humble, often reflecting on his family’s tragedy.
