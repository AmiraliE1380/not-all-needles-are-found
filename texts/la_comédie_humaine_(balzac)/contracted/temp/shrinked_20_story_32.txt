PREPARER’S NOTE

Two Poets is part one of a trilogy and begins the story of Lucien, his sister Eve, and his friend David in the provincial town of Angoulême. Part two, A Distinguished Provincial at Paris, centers on Lucien’s Parisian life. Part three, Eve and David, returns to Angoulême. In many references parts one and three are combined under the title Lost Illusions, while A Distinguished Provincial at Paris is titled separately. Lucien’s story continues later in Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life.

DEDICATION

To Monsieur Victor Hugo,

It was your birthright to be, like a Rafael or a Pitt, a great poet at an age when others are children; it was your fate, as with Chateaubriand and every man of genius, to struggle against jealousy lurking in newspapers and the subterranean places of journalism. For this reason I desired that your victorious name should help to win a victory for this work, which some say is an act of courage as well as a veracious history. If there had been journalists in Molière’s time, who can doubt they would have been within the province of the playwright? Why should Comedy, qui castigat ridendo mores, exempt one power when the Parisian press spares none? I am happy to subscribe myself your sincere admirer and friend,

DE BALZAC.

TWO POETS

When this story opens, the Stanhope press and the modern ink roller were not yet common in small provincial printing houses. Even in Angoulême, despite links to paper-mills and Parisian typography, the machinery was primitive: wooden presses, leather ink-balls, and a heavy movable marble “impression-stone.” Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard had a near-superstitious attachment to this obsolete gear, which figures in this chronicle.

Séchard had been a journeyman pressman, nicknamed a “bear” in compositors’ slang—so called for the pacing of the pressman from ink-table to press. “Bears” called compositors “monkeys” for their nimble fingering of type from the many compartments of the cases.

In the disastrous year 1793, Séchard, fifty and married, escaped the great Requisition that swept most workmen into the army. He was the only hand left in the printing-house; when the master died, leaving a widow but no children, the business seemed doomed because the solitary “bear” could not become a “monkey” and, as a pressman, could neither read nor write. A Representative of the People, hurried to publish Convention Decrees, bestowed a master printer’s license on Séchard and requisitioned the establishment. Jérôme-Nicolas accepted the dangerous patent, bought the business from the widow with his wife’s savings, and took over the plant at half value—but he still had to print the Republic’s decrees without mistakes.

At this crisis Séchard was fortunate to find a noble Marseillais unwilling to emigrate or risk his head, who agreed to work disguised in a provincial printer’s jacket. M. le Comte de Maucombe set up, read, and corrected the decrees that forbade citizens to harbor aristocrats; the “bear” printed and posted them, and both remained safe.

In 1795, when the Terror had passed, Séchard needed a new jack-of-all-trades to be compositor, reader, and foreman; an Abbé who declined the oath succeeded the Count after the First Consul restored public worship. The Abbé later became a Bishop at the Restoration; in time the Count and the Abbé met again on the same bench in the House of Peers.

By 1795 Jérôme-Nicolas had not learned to read or write; by 1802 he still had not; yet by allowing a large margin for “wear and tear” in his estimates, he managed to pay a foreman’s wages. Once an easy-going journeyman, he became a terror to his “bears” and “monkeys.” Where poverty ceases, avarice begins. On glimpsing the possibility of making a fortune, covetousness sharpened a practical faculty for business—greedy, suspicious, and keen-eyed. He scorned theory; he could estimate at a glance the cost of printing per page or sheet for every kind of type. He proved to unlettered customers that large type costs more to move; if small type was discussed, he argued it was more difficult to handle. Setting up type was the one part of the craft he did not know; afraid of undercharging, he always made a heavy profit. He never took his eyes off compositors paid by the hour. If a paper manufacturer was in trouble, he bought up stock cheap and warehoused it. So he became his own landlord and owned the old house that had been a printing office for ages.

He had every sort of luck. He was left a widower with one son. He sent the boy to grammar school, not so much for the child’s sake as to train a successor; and Séchard treated the lad harshly to prolong parental rule, making him work at case on holidays and telling him he must learn to earn his living to repay his father’s toil.

The Abbé left and Séchard promoted one of his four compositors to foreman, chosen on the future bishop’s recommendation as an honest, intelligent man. These measures, Séchard thought, would tide him over until his son could take over what he expected to be a flourishing business.

David Séchard’s school career was brilliant. Old Séchard, as a self-made man without education, entertained considerable contempt for book learning; yet when he sent his son to Paris to study higher typography, he urged him to save money in the “working man’s paradise” and made clear he was not to draw on the paternal purse. David learned his trade and completed his education without costing his father a farthing; Didot’s foreman became a scholar. At the end of 1819 he was summoned home to take the helm.

Until then Nicolas Séchard’s establishment had enjoyed a monopoly of all regional official printing plus the prefecture and diocese—connections that should have been profitable. But at that moment the firm of Cointet Brothers, paper manufacturers, applied for the second printer’s license in Angoulême. Séchard had kept the license dormant during war-induced commercial atrophy but neglected to buy it outright; this parsimony threatened ruin. Séchard rejoiced when he heard the coming struggle would be fought by his son and not by himself—“a young fellow from the Didots will pull through,” he thought.

The septuagenarian sighed for an easy life. Though his knowledge of higher typography was slight, he was master of “tipple-ography,” an art in which he indulged. Bound by the laws of etymology to be a dry subject, he suffered from an inextinguishable thirst. His wife had controlled the passion in her life, but old age revived it. The habit altered his appearance: a large nose, veined cheeks, and bloated purple patches gave his face a truffled look. His small gray eyes, under thick brows, glimmered with the cunning of avarice that had supplanted fatherly instinct. Those eyes retained their craftiness even in drink. Séchard resembled one of La Fontaine’s friars with a fringe of grizzled hair about a bald pate—short, corpulent, like an old-fashioned lamp burning much oil for a small wick; excess confirmed his body’s condition.

For thirty years he had worn the municipal three-cornered hat still seen on provincial towncriers. His breeches and waistcoat were greenish velveteen, with an old brown greatcoat, gray cotton stockings, and shoes with silver buckles. This costume suited the man so completely it seemed part of his nature. If the old printer had been given to blind greed, it showed plainly in his manner of abdicating.

Knowing his son had learned the business thoroughly at Didot’s, Jérôme-Nicolas had long plotted the bargain he meant to drive with David. All that the father made, the son was to lose, but in business Jérôme-Nicolas knew nothing of father or son. At first he regarded David as his only child; later he saw him as the natural purchaser of the business and therefore an antagonist whose duty it was to get the better of him. The transformation of sentiment into self-seeking, slow and veiled in more educated people, was swift and direct in the old “bear,” who showed the superiority of shrewd tipple-ography over book-learned typography.

David returned home and the old man greeted him with the cordiality that cunning folk assume for business. He fussed over him—giving his arm, cautioning where to set his foot, warming his bed, making supper. After a sumptuous dinner and much potation, Jérôme-Nicolas began, between hiccoughs, with “Now for business.” David begged to postpone; the old “bear” would not. He had dragged the chain fifty years and would not wait another hour; his son would be “gaffer” tomorrow.

A word about the premises: the printing-house had stood since Louis XIV’s reign at the angle of Rue de Beaulieu and Place du Mûrier. The ground floor was a single large room lit by a street casement and a sash window to the yard; a side passage led to the private office. In the provinces, typography excited curiosity, so customers entered through the street glass door and descended three steps because the workshop floor lay below street level. Newcomers, staring at sheets strung across the ceiling, often ran into cases or tie-bars; they watched compositors pick out type from the hundred and fifty-two compartments, read copy, and assemble lines, only to trip over a ream of damp paper or bump against a bench—delighting the boys, “bears,” and “monkeys.” No one passed through the shop without mishap. Two glass-windowed cages extended into the yard for foreman and master. The yard walls were decorated with trellised vines—tempting, given the owner’s reputation. One side held the kitchen, the other the woodshed, and a ramshackle penthouse against the hall housed paper trimming and damping; there forms—masses of set-up type—were washed. Inky streams from there mixed with kitchen waste and ran into the kennel in the street, so peasants thought the Devil was taking a wash inside the establishment.

Above the shop, the house had three rooms on the first floor and two attic rooms. The first room served as dining-room and lobby; it was the same length as the passage below, minus the old wooden staircase, and lit by a narrow casement and a bull’s-eye into the yard. Its chief trait was a cynical simplicity born of money-making greed: bare whitewashed walls, a dirty brick floor never scoured, three rickety chairs, a round table, and a sideboard between bedroom and sitting-room doors. Windows and doors were dingy. Reams of paper or printed matter cluttered the floor; remains of Séchard’s dinners, empty bottles, and plates lay among packages.

The bedroom, lit to the yard by a leaded window, was hung with old-world tapestry typical in provincial houses on Corpus Christi Day. Its furniture was the old Seigneur Rouzeau’s: a vast four-post bed with canopy, valances, and crimson serge quilt; a couple of worm-eaten armchairs, two tapestry-covered walnut chairs, an aged bureau, and a mantel clock. The room remained as Rouzeau had left it.

The sitting-room had been modernized by the late Mme. Séchard: powder-blue wainscot, sepia Oriental panels, six lyre-backed chairs with blue leather cushions. Two arched windows looked onto the Place du Mûrier without curtains; no clock, sconce, or mirror crowned the mantel because Mme. Séchard died before finishing the scheme. The “bear,” seeing no profit in further improvements, left it as it was.

Pede titubante, Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard led his son into the room and pointed to a foreman’s valuation he had prepared. “Read that, my boy,” he said, rolling a wine-dark eye from the paper to David. The sheet listed “three wooden presses, held in position by iron tie-bars, cast-iron plates—” to which the father added, proudly, “An improvement of my own.” David, surprised at the total—“sixteen hundred francs!”—let the paper fall. He scoffed: the presses were old “sabots” fit only for firewood.

“Sabots?” roared the old man. He dragged David downstairs, flung open the workshop door, and, with anxious pride, showed him the three oaken presses—oiled, cleaned, and polished by the apprentice. Throwing a frisket and tympan, he worked the lever with the agility of a young man; the press creaked like a startled bird. “Where is the English press that could go at that pace?” he demanded. At the third press he found a patch missed by the apprentice, swore, and rubbed it with his coat skirt like a dealer polishing an animal for sale. “With those three presses, David, you can make your nine thousand francs a year without a foreman. As your future partner, I’m opposed to replacing them with your cursed cast-iron machines that wear out the type. You in Paris rave about those Stanhopes—two thousand five hundred francs apiece—and they maul your type to pieces. I haven’t book-learning like you, but the life of the Stanhope is the death of the type. People in Angoulême won’t pay more.”

David read on: “Item, five thousand pounds weight of type from M. Vaflard’s foundry—taken at ten thousand francs.” He smiled; Didot type cost far less. The father, indignant, defended Vaflard’s hard metal and the lasting quality of his earlier purchases. He produced packets of unused sorts, insisting they were as bright as when cast. The gulf between them was plain: it was take it or leave it. The inventory included ropes across the ceiling and every small implement—jobbing chases, wetting-boards, paste-pots, rinsing-troughs, lye-brushes—valued with miserly exactitude. The total came to thirty thousand francs, including license and goodwill. David wondered if it was feasible.

Jerôme-Nicolas, uneasy at his son’s silence, preferred a stormy argument to wordless acceptance; to him haggling was a sign of a man who would guard his interests. He extolled old tools—hot-presses, cutting-presses, hideous vignettes and theatrical masks—arguing that provincial taste would not accept Parisian innovations for a hundred years. David, sensitive, affectionate, and disliking dispute, found himself at a disadvantage. Jérôme-Nicolas had bought the place from Rouzeau’s widow for ten thousand assignats; thirty thousand in coin seemed exorbitant.

“Father, you are cutting my throat!” cried David.

“I,” answered the old toper, pointing to the ceiling ropes, “I who gave you life? Do you know what the license is worth? The sheet of advertisements alone at fivepence a line brought in five hundred francs last month. Turn up the books and see what we make by placards, the prefecture registers, the mayor’s work, and the bishop’s. You are a do-nothing.” He accused David of haggling over the horse that would carry him to Marsac.

Attached to the valuation was a deed of partnership: the father would let house and premises to the new firm for twelve hundred francs per annum, reserving one garret room for himself. Until David paid the purchase money in full, profits were to be divided equally; once paid, David would be sole proprietor. David mentally calculated the value of license, goodwill, and paper stock, leaving plant out, and thought it possible to clear the debt, so he accepted the conditions. The old man, suspecting hidden funds or an intention not to pay, tried to extract some payment on account; David kept his purse closed.

Next day the old man had the apprentice move household stuff to the attic until a cart could take it to the country. David entered on the day he was installed into the printing-house—three bare, unfurnished rooms—without a sou to pay his men. When he asked his father, as partner, to contribute to working expenses, Jérôme-Nicolas pretended not to understand: he had found the printing-house but wasn’t bound to find money. Pressed, he said he had none left after paying Rouzeau’s widow. If he, a poor ignorant working man, had made his way, Didot’s apprentice should do still better. Had not David earned money thanks to an education paid for by the father’s sweat? Now the education should be useful.

“What have you done with your ‘polls?’” he demanded—David’s savings.

“Why, had I not to live? and books to buy besides?” David answered indignantly.

“Oh! you bought books? You’ll make a poor man of business. A man who buys books is hardly fit to print them,” retorted the “bear.”

David endured the humiliating contempt and niggardly explanations. He saw he was alone and that his father was trying to make money out of him; philosophically he tested how far the old man would go. He asked about his mother’s fortune; perhaps it might pay working expenses.

“Your mother’s fortune?” echoed old Séchard. “Why, it was her beauty and intelligence!”

David understood: any legal claim would be long, expensive, and disgraceful. He accepted his heavy engagements and resolved to work. “After all, if I have a rough time of it, so had the old man; besides, I shall be working for myself, shall I not?”

“I am leaving you a treasure,” said Séchard, uneasy at his son’s silence.

“What treasure?” asked David.

“Marion!” said his father.

Marion, a big country girl, was indispensable—damping and cutting paper, cooking, washing, marketing, unloading paper carts, collecting accounts, cleaning ink-balls. Had she known how to read, she might have set type.

Old Séchard set out on foot for the country, delighted by the sale but uneasy about payment. A vendor’s anguish of uncertain completion succeeded his pleasure. The miser, believing in honor and education, mortgaged thirty thousand francs on the expectation that his son’s training and fine feelings would make him pay. By the time he reached Marsac he was convinced David would pay. The old man had bought a little property there in 1809, added bits of land, and given up the press for vineyard labor; he had been in the trade long enough to think he knew it.

In rural retirement the thought of thirty thousand francs intoxicated him; he pictured the coin and hurried to Angoulême to see how matters went. There stood the presses; the apprentice in a paper cap cleaned the ink-balls; a press creaked over a trade circular; the type remained unchanged; he found his son and the foreman reading books, which he took for proof-sheets. He would join David at dinner and return to Marsac chewing the cud of anxious reflection.

Avarice, like love, has second sight and clings to presentiments. Far from the shop the old man felt disquiet at his son’s seeming inactivity. The name of Cointet Brothers haunted him; he feared Séchard & Son falling into second place. His presentiments proved right: disaster hovered.

Indifferent to political reaction, David tried to remain neutral. But provincial men of business had to profess politics to secure patronage; the Cointet Brothers deliberately cultivated monarchical opinion: fasting on Fridays, keeping Lent, frequenting the cathedral, and currying favor with the clergy. When devotional books came back into demand, the Cointets profited handsomely. They slandered David as a Liberal and as immoral because of his father’s reputation; they argued they needed the business for their families and urged customers away.

Their tactics worked. The monopoly of prefectorial and diocesan work gradually passed to the Cointets; David’s circulation fell by half as rivals started a second local sheet of announcements. The Cointets grew richer and offered to buy Séchard’s printing-paper and the departmental announcements.

The news sent David’s father rushing from Marsac. “Leave me to manage the Cointets,” he said; “don’t you meddle.” He saw the Cointets’ meaning and took alarm. If David gave up the paper, the connection would be lost; all depended on the paper. The Cointets had tried to ruin the Séchards by accusing them of Liberalism; the old man seized a plank to cling to—soldiers of the Liberal party should keep the Séchards. He demanded sixty thousand francs for the printing business so as not to ruin his son. David was unwilling; the Cointets, after wringing offers, finally paid twenty-two thousand francs for the Charente Chronicle. But David had to pledge thenceforward to print no newspaper under penalty of thirty thousand francs damages.

That deal was the death-blow to the Séchard establishment; yet the old vinegrower cared little. To pocket ready money he consented to abandon his share of the business but not the premises; the rent remained at the famous twelve hundred francs per annum. After the sale he came into town rarely, pleading age, though he still loved his stock-in-trade. The old foreman, who had gone to the rival establishment, knew exactly how much this fatherly generosity was worth; the old fox meant to reserve a right to interfere and had arranged to appear in the bankruptcy as a privileged creditor for arrears of rent.

The causes of David’s heedlessness illuminate his character. A few days after taking over the paternal office he met an old school friend in dire poverty. Lucien Chardon, about twenty-one, son of a surgeon-major, had been trained in scientific habits by a father who was a chemist and had run a retail druggist business. The father had died, his unfinished gout remedy and hoped-for discovery lost. He had married a beautiful Rubempré woman, saved from the guillotine in 1793 by the plea she was pregnant. The family had been pushed into straitened circumstances; their children inherited the mother’s beauty but little else.

M. Chardon’s devotion to science might have secured a good education, and the family had been used to comfort; now poor, the mother and daughter accepted humble employments. Mme. Chardon sold the druggist’s shop and, on a small capital yielding three hundred francs, earned seven francs a week as a monthly nurse, adopting the name Madame Charlotte to spare Lucien embarrassment. Lucien’s sister Eve worked for a laundress; the combined incomes—three hundred in rentes and the women’s scant wages—amounted to about eight hundred francs a year for three persons, barely adequate.

Lucien, once a brilliant pupil at Angoulême grammar school and in the third form when David left, had been trained by his father’s passion for natural science. Worn out by penury and impatient at twenty, he accepted David’s generous offer of forty francs a month to learn the printer’s reader’s work—David had no real need of a reader but saved Lucien from despair. Their school friendship renewed, their bond deepened through shared hopes and misfortune. Both possessed intelligence and literary taste; Lucien aimed at speculative natural science and literary fame; David, meditative and poetic, was drawn to natural science too. They became almost brothers in devotion. David glimpsed Eve’s fair face and loved her, and seeing the mother and sister’s blind devotion to Lucien, resolved to add his support; he believed in Lucien’s genius with exaggerated confidence.

In urgent need the two ruminated on ways to make a fortune. Lucien recalled two of his father’s ideas: a chemical method to refine sugar and an American vegetable fibre for making paper à la chinoise, promising huge savings in raw material. David seized on the paper-fibre idea, seeing a fortune and treating Lucien as benefactor.

Their ruling thoughts and inner lives unfitted them for the practical running of a printing-house. Instead of making the fifteen to twenty thousand francs that the Cointets did, Séchard & Son made barely three hundred francs per month gross; from that the foreman’s salary, Marion’s wages, rent and taxes had to be paid. David himself scarcely made twelve hundred francs a year. Active businessmen would have bought new type and machinery and sought cheap Paris orders; but master and foreman, absorbed in intellectual pursuits, were content with whatever trickled in.

The Cointets, understanding David’s habits, did not crush him outright; their policy required letting the business flicker so it would not be taken over by a stronger rival. They sent prospectuses and job-printing work to the Séchards, a practice like mail-coach owners running sham opposition to keep competitors out. Unwittingly, David owed his commercial existence to their cunning. They behaved fairly in appearance but were tactically clever.

Both inside and out, the condition of the Séchard establishment testified to the old “bear’s” sordid avarice, who never spent a sou on repairs. The old house had weathered sun and rain until it resembled a venerable tree trunk, seamed and cracked. The front, brick and stone, bent beneath a worm-eaten roof of curved pantiles. Decrepit casements, heavy shutters, iron cross bars—there was no more dilapidated house in Angoulême. The workshop, lit at either end and dark in the middle, had walls plastered with handbills and begrimed by thirty years of handling; a cobweb of cordage spanned the ceiling. Stacks of paper, old-fashioned presses, slabs for weighting damp sheets, rows of cases, and two dens in the corners where the master printer and foreman sat completed the scene—an apt setting for the drift of the two friends’ lives.

One day early in May, 1821, David and Lucien stood by the window looking into the yard. It was nearly two o’clock; four or five men were going out to dinner. David waited until the apprentice had shut the street door and fastened the bell; then, as if the smells of paper, ink, presses and old woodwork had become intolerable, he drew Lucien into the yard and they sat under the vines, keeping the office and door in view. Sunbeams played among the trellised shoots, making an aureole about their heads and sharpening the contrast between their faces and characters as if some great painter had set them in relief.

David’s frame belonged to the fighter: broad shoulders, a full body, thick black hair, a high-colored swarthy face on a thick neck that suggested rough solidity. On closer view the lines about his thick lips, a dimpled chin, square nostrils and a broad irregular cleft, and above all the eyes—steady, lit by an all-absorbing love—revealed the real character: thoughtful, strenuous melancholy, a spirit that discerned horizons on either side and could see to the end of winding ways. He was capable of clear analysis and quick to renounce joys still only ideas. One expected flashes of genius and saw the ashes of an extinguished volcano—hopes quenched by the sense of social annihilation to which low birth and want of fortune often condemn loftier minds. Near this poor printer, who loathed the handicraft allied to intellectual work, stood Lucien, graceful as some sculptured Bacchus.

Lucien’s face had the distinction of antique beauty: a Greek profile, velvet-white skin, and eyes full of love—so blue they looked dark against their pearly setting, dewy and childlike beneath long chestnut lashes and penciled brows. Silken down on his cheeks and bright curling hair gleamed golden in the sunlight. A divine graciousness transfigured the white temples; a matchless nobleness settled on the short chin, raised but not abrupt. The coral lips smiled like those of a sorrowing angel. Lucien’s hands denoted race—shapely hands that men obey at a sign and women love to kiss. Slender and of middle height, his feminine contours might suggest a girl in disguise, a trait common to keen-witted men. This did not mislead: when he analyzed society his restless mind often stood on the lower ground of diplomatists who hold that success justifies any means. One misfortune of great intellects is that they comprehend both good and evil.

Their lofty judgment of society came from being at the lower end of its scale; unrecognized power avenges lowly station by viewing the world from on high. Yet their flights into higher thought left them only more bitter and hopeless. Lucien had read much and compared; David had thought much and deeply. Despite David’s robust, country look, his mind was melancholy and somewhat morbid—he lacked confidence; but Lucien, despite an almost effeminate figure, had the Gascon temperament in high degree—rash, brave, adventurous, apt to see the bright side and to shrink from the dark. His nature would stoop at no crime if anything could be gained, and laugh at vice that served as a stepping-stone. For the moment these tendencies were checked partly by youth’s fair illusions and by the enthusiasm that led him to prefer nobler methods, as every man in love with glory does at first. Lucien struggled with himself and his desires, not with the baseness of other men; he was at strife with his own power. The brilliancy of his intellect attracted David keenly. David admired his friend while keeping him out of scrapes into which the furie française led him.

David, with his well-balanced mind and timid nature at variance with a strong constitution, had the persistence of the Northern temper; though he saw all difficulties, he vowed to conquer and never give way. In him the unswerving virtue of an apostle was softened by pity springing from inexhaustible indulgence. In their long friendship one was the worshiper—David; Lucien ruled him like a woman sure of love, and David loved to yield. He felt that his friend’s physical beauty implied real superiority, which he accepted, seeing himself as coarser human clay.

“The ox for patient labor in the fields, the free life for the bird,” he thought. “I will be the ox, and Lucien shall be the eagle.”

For three years they had shared destinies bright with such promise. Together they read the great works that had appeared since the Peace—Schiller, Goethe, Byron, Scott, Jean-Paul, Berzelius, Davy, Cuvier, Lamartine, and many more. They warmed themselves at these hearthfires and tried their powers in abortive creations, work laid aside and taken up again with renewed enthusiasm. Incessantly they worked with youthful vitality; comrades in poverty, comrades in the consuming love of art and science, they forgot the hard present, their minds bent on laying foundations of future fame.

“Lucien,” said David, “do you know what I have just received from Paris?” He drew a tiny volume from his pocket. “Listen!”

David read, as a poet can read, André de Chénier’s Idyll Néere, then Le Malade, the Elegy on a Suicide, another elegy in the classic taste, and the last two Iambes.

“So that is André de Chénier!” Lucien exclaimed repeatedly. “It fills one with despair!” he cried when David surrendered the book, unable to read further for emotion. “A poet rediscovered by a poet!” he added, reading the prefatory signature.

“After Chénier had written those poems, he thought he had written nothing worth publishing,” David added.

Then Lucien read aloud the fragment L’Aveugle and two Elegies, and on encountering the line—

If they know not bliss, is there happiness on earth?

—he pressed the book to his lips. Tears came to both eyes, for the two friends were lovers and fellow-worshipers.

The vine-stems were changing with spring; they covered the rifted walls of the old house, with fluted columns, knots and bas-reliefs and uncounted little grotesques—fairy-work on a gloomy yard. Fancy had scattered flowers and crimson gems over the place, and Chénier’s Camille became for David the Eve he worshiped, for Lucien a great lady to whom he paid homage. Poetry had shaken out her starry robe above the workshop where “monkeys” and “bears” were grotesquely busy among types and presses. Five o’clock struck, but the friends felt neither hunger nor thirst; life had turned to a golden dream, and all the treasures of the world lay at their feet. Far on the horizon lay the blue streak to which Hope points a finger in storm and stress; a siren voice sounded, calling, “Come, spread your wings; through that streak of gold or azure lies the way of escape from evil fortune!”

Just then the low glass door of the workshop opened, and out came Cérizet, an apprentice David had brought from Paris. He introduced a stranger, who saluted the friends politely and spoke to David.

“This, sir, is a monograph I am desirous of printing,” he said, drawing a huge package of manuscript. “Will you oblige me with an estimate?”

“We do not undertake work on such a scale, sir,” David answered without looking at the manuscript. “You had better see the Messieurs Cointet about it.”

“Still we have a very pretty type which might suit it,” put in Lucien, taking up the roll. “Leave your commission with us and call again to-morrow, and we will give you an estimate.”

“Have I the pleasure of addressing M. Lucien Chardon?”

“Yes, sir,” said the foreman.

“I am fortunate to meet a young poet destined to such greatness,” returned the author. “Mme. de Bargeton sent me here.”

Lucien flushed at the name and stammered something about gratitude for Mme. de Bargeton’s interest. David noticed his friend’s embarrassed color and left him in conversation with the country gentleman, the author of a monograph on silk-culture, prompted by vanity to print the effort for fellow-members of the local agricultural society.

When the author had gone, David spoke.

“Lucien, are you in love with Mme. de Bargeton?”

“Passionately.”

“But social prejudices set you as far apart as if she were in Pekin and you in Greenland.”

“The will of two lovers can rise victorious over all things,” said Lucien, lowering his eyes.

“You will forget us,” returned the alarmed lover, as Eve’s fair face rose before his mind.

“On the contrary, I have perhaps sacrificed my love to you,” cried Lucien.

“What do you mean?”

“In spite of my love, and motives that might secure a footing in her house, I have told her I will never go thither again unless another is made welcome too, a man whose gifts are greater than mine, a man destined for a brilliant future—David Séchard, my brother, my friend. I shall find an answer waiting when I go home. All the aristocrats may have been asked to hear me read this evening, but I shall not go if the answer is negative, and I will never set foot in Mme. de Bargeton’s house again.”

David brushed tears from his eyes and wrung Lucien’s hand. The clock struck six.

“Eve must be anxious; good-bye,” Lucien added abruptly.

He hurried away. David stood overcome by an emotion unusual at his age and in his station—the friends were like two young swans with wings unclipped by provincial experience.

“Heart of gold!” David exclaimed as his eyes followed Lucien across the workshop.

Lucien went down to L’Houmeau along the Promenade de Beaulieu, the Rue du Minage, and Saint-Peter’s Gate. It was the longest way round, so you may be sure Mme. de Bargeton’s house lay on the route. So delicious it was to pass under her windows, though she knew nothing of his presence, that for two months he had taken this circuit daily.

Under the trees of Beaulieu he saw how far the suburb lay from the city. Custom had raised barriers harder to surmount than the steep steps Lucien descended. Youth and ambition had thrown a flying-bridge of glory across the gulf between the city and suburb, yet Lucien was uneasy over his lady’s answer as any court favorite who fears to fall. To understand the situation it is necessary to sketch the topography and manners of Angoulême, and especially the position of Mme. de Bargeton.

The old city of Angoulême perches aloft on a crag like a sugar-loaf, overlooking the plain where the Charente winds. The crag is a spur marking three picturesque valleys. The ramparts, great gateways and ruined fortress on the summit witness to the stronghold’s importance during former wars. But its old-world strength is a source of modern weakness; Angoulême could not spread down to the Charente, and shut in between ramparts and steep crag, the old town was condemned to stagnation.

The Government tried to extend the town toward Périgord, building a Prefecture, a Naval School, and barracks, opening roads. Private enterprise had been beforehand. The suburb of L’Houmeau had sprung up at the foot of the crag and along the riverside where the road runs from Paris to Bordeaux. The great paper-mills of Angoulême, established three hundred years on the Charente and its branches, the State ordnance factory at Ruelle, carriers, wheelwrights, posthouses and inns, every industry tied to road or river, crowded Lower Angoulême to avoid the ascent. Tanners, laundries, waterside trades and warehouses for brandy and raw materials lined the quays.

Thus the Faubourg of L’Houmeau grew into a busy, prosperous city, a second Angoulême rivaling the upper town. Yet L’Houmeau, with all its business, remained an appendage of the city above: the noblesse and officialdom dwelt on the crag, trade and wealth remained below. No love was lost between these two sections; in Angoulême it was hard to say which detested the other more. Under the Empire the machinery worked fairly smoothly, but the Restoration sharpened animosities.

Nearly every house in the upper town was inhabited by noble or old burgher families who lived on incomes—a kind of autochthonous nation that suffered no outsiders. After two hundred years of unbroken residence, and perhaps an intermarriage or two, a family from another district might be tolerated, but in the eyes of the aborigines they were newcomers of yesterday. Prefects, receivers-general, and various administrations tried to tame these ancient families; the families were always willing to accept invitations to dinners and dances, but to admit strangers to their houses they were inexorable. Ready to scoff and disparage, jealous and niggardly, marrying only among themselves, they formed a serried phalanx to keep out intruders. Of modern luxury they had little notion; as for sending a boy to Paris, they thought it ruin. Such sagacity shows their old-world manners—thick-headed Royalism infected with bigotry rather than zeal—stagnant, like their town on a rock. Yet Angoulême enjoyed a regional reputation for educational advantages, and neighbors sent daughters to its boarding schools and convents.

It is easy to imagine the influence of this class sentiment, which held Angoulême aloof from L’Houmeau. The merchant classes were rich, the noblesse usually poor. Each side took revenge in scorn. The tradespeople espoused the quarrel. “He is a man of L’Houmeau!” a shopkeeper of the upper town would say, with an accent words cannot describe. When the Restoration revived hopes for the noblesse, the distance between Angoulême and L’Houmeau widened. The better families, devoted to the Government, grew more exclusive. “The man of L’Houmeau” became little better than a pariah. Hence the deep, smothered hatred that later broke out in the insurrection of 1830 and destroyed durable social elements in France. As the Court nobles’ haughtiness detached the provincial noblesse from the throne, so did these nobles alienate the bourgeoisie from the royal cause by behavior that galled their vanity.

So a man of L’Houmeau—a druggist’s son—in Mme. de Bargeton’s house was nothing less than a little revolution. Who was responsible? Lamartine and Victor Hugo, Casimir Delavigne and Canalis, Béranger and Chateaubriand; Villemain and M. Aignan, Sourmet and Tissot, Étienne and Davrigny, Benjamin Constant and Lamennais, Cousin and Michaud—all the old and young illustrious names in literature, Liberals and Royalists alike, must divide the blame. Mme. de Bargeton loved art and letters—an eccentric taste much deplored in Angoulême. In justice to her it is necessary to sketch the previous history of a woman born to shine, left by unlucky circumstances in the shade, a woman whose influence decided Lucien’s career.

################################################
M. de Bargeton was the great-grandson of an alderman of Bordeaux named Mirault, ennobled under Louis XIII for long service. His son, Mirault de Bargeton, became an officer in Louis XIV’s household troops and married so well that by Louis XV’s reign his son dropped Mirault and was called simply M. de Bargeton. This M. de Bargeton squandered the family fortune and checked its progress. Two of his brothers, great-uncles of the present Bargeton, returned to business; thus the name Mirault still appears among Bordeaux merchants. The lands of Bargeton, in Angoumois within the barony of Rochefoucauld, were entailed, as was the house in Angoulême called the Hôtel Bargeton; these hereditaments descended to the grandson of the prodigal ancestor. The year 1789 deprived him of seignorial rights but left rents of about ten thousand francs a year. Had his grandsire followed the example of illustrious forebears, Bargeton V (who may be called Bargeton the Mute) would properly have been Marquis of Bargeton, allied to great families and likely to rise to a dukedom. Instead, in 1805 he considered himself fortunate to marry Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, daughter of a noble long relegated to his manor-house, from a younger branch of one of southern France’s oldest families. A Nègrepelisse had been among St. Louis’s hostages; the elder branch later bore the d’Espard name after a Nègrepelisse married an heiress. M. de Nègrepelisse, a younger son of a younger son, lived on his wife’s small estate near Barbezieux, farming, selling his corn himself, distilling brandy, and scoffing at those who ridiculed him so long as he could amass silver crowns and occasionally add a parcel of land.

In that remote region unusual circumstances gave Mme. de Bargeton a taste for music and books. During the Revolution the Abbé Niollant, the Abbé Roze’s best pupil, found refuge at the manor of Escarbas and brought compositions. The hospitable country gentleman was repaid handsomely: the Abbé undertook his daughter’s education. Anaïs, called Naïs, would otherwise have been left to herself or a coarse maid. The Abbé, a musician and wide reader who knew Italian and German, taught her those languages and counterpoint, explained masterpieces of French, German, and Italian literature, and deciphered great composers’ music. In enforced seclusion he also taught Latin, Greek, and some natural science. A mother might have tempered the effects of such a man’s instruction on a young girl, but the Abbé’s artistic temperament and broad judgments fostered Naïs’s independence: he was an enthusiast and poet, at once estimable and prone to elevate judgment above bourgeois prejudices. In society such intellects win pardon for boldness by depth and originality; in private life their influence can mislead, suggesting wanderings from the beaten track. The Abbé’s kindness made his ideas more contagious; the high-spirited girl, confirmed by lonely country life, learned fearlessness in criticism and ready judgment—qualities admirable in a man but awkward in a woman destined for the homely life of a house-mother. Though the Abbé urged modesty, Mlle. de Nègrepelisse conceived an excellent opinion of herself and a robust contempt for ordinary humanity. Surrounded by inferiors or obsequious servants, she grew haughty like a great lady but without its charm or urbanity. Vanity was flattered by the poor Abbé’s pride in his pupil—the pride of an author who sees himself in his work—and for misfortune she met no equals to measure herself against. Isolation is a great drawback of country life: without the habit of making small sacrifices for others, form and spirit deteriorate together.

With no social intercourse to enforce self-repression, Naïs’s bold ideas passed into manner and expression. She had a cavalier air that suits adventurous women but made her ridiculous at Angoulême once her admirers ceased to worship the eccentricities that charm only in youth. M. de Nègrepelisse himself would have given all his daughter’s books to save a bullock; so miserly was he that he wouldn’t give her two farthings beyond her allowance, even for necessary educational trinkets.

The Abbé died in 1802 before his pupil’s marriage, which he likely would not have advised. Her father, now the sole guardian, feared her high spirit and felt unequal to the struggle against her independence. Like many women who leave the appointed track, Naïs had opinions about marriage and little inclination toward it. She recoiled from submitting body and soul to the weak, undignified specimens of manhood she knew. She wished to rule; marriage meant obedience. Between obedience to coarse caprices and a life without indulgence for her tastes, and flight with a lover who might please her, she would not have hesitated.

M. de Nègrepelisse, though retaining some sense of birth, dreaded a mésalliance. He resolved to marry his daughter, not chiefly for her sake but for his peace. He sought a noble or country gentleman: not too clever, incapable of meddling with trustees’ accounts, stupid and easy enough to let Naïs have her way, disinterested enough to take her without a dowry. Finding such a son-in-law was the problem. To him M. de Bargeton seemed the ideal. Aged forty, much shattered by amorous dissipations and generally thought of remarkably feeble intellect, he possessed exactly the commonsense required to manage his fortune and had sufficient breeding to avoid blunders in Angoulême. In blunt fashion M. de Nègrepelisse pointed out the groom’s negative virtues and showed Naïs how to manage him to secure her own freedom. So Naïs married the bearer of arms—two hundred years old by the reckoning of the narrative. With a chaperon she could steer her fortunes as she pleased under the guise of submission, and hope to use her wit and beauty to advance in Paris. Naïs liked the prospect of such liberty. M. de Bargeton considered himself making a brilliant match, expecting that M. de Nègrepelisse would soon leave him the estates he was rounding out; but to an unprejudiced observer it seemed as if the father-in-law’s epitaph might be written in advance.

By then Mme. de Bargeton was thirty-six and her husband fifty-eight; the disparity showed because M. de Bargeton looked seventy while his wife looked barely half her age. She still wore rose-color and let her hair hang loose. Though their income did not exceed twelve thousand francs, they ranked among Angoulême’s half-dozen largest fortunes, merchants and officials excepted; they had to remain in Angoulême until Mme. de Bargeton’s inheritance should fall in and allow a move to Paris. Meanwhile they were bound to be attentive to old M. de Nègrepelisse, who took his time so long that his son-in-law predeceased him. Naïs’s brilliant gifts and latent wealth profited her little; Time transformed them into absurdities. Absurdities often spring from an abnormally developed good quality: pride, untempered by contact with the great world, stiffens upon petty things; in a loftier moral atmosphere it would have become nobility. Enthusiasm, a saintly virtue when rightly placed, turns to exaggeration when devoted to the trifles of a narrow existence. Far from centers of light where thought quickens, knowledge stagnates, taste corrupts like stagnant water, and passion dwindles on small objects. Here lie avarice and tittle-tattle poisoning provincial life. The contagion of narrow-mindedness and meanness affects the noblest natures; thus those born to be great and women who might have been charming, if formed by greater minds, are balked of their lives.

Mme. de Bargeton, for example, smote the lyre for every trifle and published her emotions to her circle. When sensations appeal to an audience of one, it is better to keep them to ourselves. A sunset is a glorious poem; but when a woman describes it in high-sounding words for matter-of-fact people, she becomes ridiculous. Some pleasures are fully felt only when two souls meet, poet with poet. She had a habit of using grandiose phrases and exaggerated expressions—the “tartines” of French journalistic satire—squandering superlatives; the smallest things grew immense. She began to coin verbs and neologisms to express her whimsies; heat of language communicated to her brain, and the dithyrambs on her lips came from the abundance of her heart. She palpitated, swooned, and went into ecstasies over anything: the devotion of a Sister of Charity, an execution, a melodramatic novel, an escaped hero, or a friend who scared off burglars by imitating a man’s voice. Everything became heroic, extraordinary, divine. She exhausted herself with chronic admiration and wasted strength on curious dislikes. Her mind ran on picturesque figures—the Pasha of Janina, Lady Hester Stanhope—and she entertained notions of heroic martyrdom and exotic adventure: being sewn in a sack and thrown into water, dying of yellow fever in a Barcelona hospital, yet still a noble destiny. She adored Byron and Rousseau and anyone with a dramatic career. She was convinced gifted immortals lived by incense and light.

Many regarded her as a harmless lunatic, but a keener observer might have seen in these extravagances the broken fragments of a magnificent edifice crumbled before completion—the stones of a heavenly Jerusalem—love without a lover. This was indeed the truth.

The first eighteen years of Mme. de Bargeton’s married life can be summed up briefly. For a long time she lived on herself and distant hopes. When their narrow income made the Paris life impossible, she saw the emptiness of her future and shuddered at her loneliness. There was not a single man to inspire the madness women are prone to when they despair of stale lives with no future. Some lives admit no chance. When the Empire reached its noonday and Napoleon sent troops to the Peninsula, her disappointed hopes revived. Curiosity drew her to see heroes conquering Europe; cities, however avaricious or refractory, honored the Imperial Guard with ceremonies. Mme. de Bargeton went to a ridotto given by a regiment and fell in love with an officer of a good family, a sub-lieutenant whom Napoleon had shown the possibility of rising. This love, nobler and more restrained than many of the day’s ties, was consecrated coldly by death: on Wagram a shell destroyed the only record of Mme. de Bargeton’s young beauty—a portrait worn on the Marquis of Cante-Croix’s heart. For long she wept for the young soldier, for the heart hot with love and glory that placed a letter from Naïs above Imperial favor. The grief left a veil of sadness over her face that faded only when a woman discovers with dismay that her best years are gone, she has had no joy of them, and sees her roses wither. Her nobler qualities suffered many wounds as provincial cold seized her. She might have died of grief if chance had thrown her among men bent on nightly gains at cards; pride saved her from shabby intrigues. A woman too far above those around her, forced to choose between empty men and an empty life, can make only one choice; marriage and society became a cloister for Anaïs. She lived by poetry as a Carmelite lives by religion. All the famous foreign books published in France between 1815 and 1821, the great essayists like M. de Bonald and M. de Maistre—eagles of thought—and the lighter French literature of that outburst could delight her solitary life but not give flexibility of mind or body. She stood strong and erect like a lightning-blasted tree. Dignity became a stilted manner; social supremacy bred affectation and sentimental over-refinements; she queened it with foibles as those do who let courtiers adore them.

This past is necessary to understand Lucien’s relation to her. Lucien’s introduction came oddly. The previous winter a newcomer had brought interest into Mme. de Bargeton’s monotony. The controller of excise post had fallen vacant, and M. de Barante appointed a man whose adventurous life opened the doors of the sovereign lady’s house, where feminine curiosity reigned.

M. de Châtelet—born Sixte Châtelet, since 1806 using the particle—was one of those agreeable young men who escaped conscription by keeping close to the Imperial sun. He began as private secretary to an Imperial Highness, a post for which he had many superficial qualifications. Personable, well-figured, clever at billiards, a passable actor, a good dancer, he could sing a ballad and laugh at a witticism. Supple, envious, never at a loss, there was nothing he did not pretend to know—though nothing he truly knew. He could accompany a woman on the piano after much pressing, compose an impromptu quatrain, fill in a tapestry background, copy a landscape, sketch a profile, or design and color a costume. He had the little talents useful where women exercised notable influence. He professed diplomacy as a strong point; a craft suited to those ignorant yet mysterious, who can safely say nothing, take refuge in a shake of the head, and appear profound. Mediocrities abound in this art, and despite Châtelet’s services Her Imperial Highness could not procure him a Privy Council seat; the Princess preferred to keep him as secretary. He was made a Baron and went to Cassel as envoy-extraordinary—no empty title there—and Napoleon used him as a diplomatic courier in a European crisis. Just as he had been promised the ministerial post to Jérôme in Westphalia, the Empire fell; cheated of his “ambassade de famille,” he went despairing to Egypt with General de Montriveau. A strange chain of accidents separated him from his companion; for two years Sixte du Châtelet wandered among Arab tribes, who sold and resold him as a captive—his talents useless to nomads. At last, around the time Montriveau reached Tangier, Châtelet found himself in the Imam of Muscat’s country, luckily boarded an English vessel, and returned to Paris a year before his sometime companion. Back in Paris, his misfortunes, connections, and services to great persons recommended him to the President of the Council, who placed him in M. de Barante’s department until a controllership fell vacant. Thus M. du Châtelet’s name, reputation with women, and travel tales awakened Angoulême’s ladies’ curiosity.

Baron Sixte du Châtelet acclimatized to upper-town manners and took his cue. He appeared as a world-weary man, broken in health and weary, habitually raising his hand to his forehead as if perpetual pain recalled travels and made him interesting. He was on visiting terms with authorities—the general, the prefect, the receiver-general, the bishop—but in every house he was frigid, polite, and slightly supercilious, like a man out of place awaiting favors. His social talents survived in reputation. When curiosity ran high, he reconnoitred men and found them nought, studied the women in the cathedral for several Sundays, and decided Mme. de Bargeton was the one to be intimate with. Music, he judged, would open doors in a house that seldom received strangers. Surreptitiously he procured one of Miroir’s Masses, learned it on the piano; one Sunday when all Angoulême went to cathedral, he played the organ, sent the ignorant into ecstasies, and let his name be known. As he came out after mass, Mme. de Bargeton complimented him, regretting she had no opportunity for duets with such a musician; in an interview of her seeking he received the passport he could not have obtained by asking.

Thus the adroit Baron entered the queen of Angoulême’s circle and paid her marked attention. The elderly beau—he was forty-five—saw that her dormant youth was ready to revive; he saw treasures to be turned to account, possibly a rich widow to wed, or expectations to profit by. A marriage into the Nègrepelisse family meant connection with the Marquise d’Espard and a political career in Paris. Here was a fair tree to cultivate despite its mistletoe; he would hang his fortunes upon it, prune it, and wait to gather golden fruit.

High-born Angoulême shrieked at introducing a Giaour into the sanctuary: Mme. de Bargeton’s salon was a holy of holies in a society keeping itself unspotted. The only outsider intimate there was the bishop; the prefect was admitted twice or thrice a year; the receiver-general was never received at all. Mme. de Bargeton went to concerts and “at homes” at his house but never accepted dinner invitations. And now she who had declined the receiver-general welcomed a mere controller of excise! Such a novelty of precedence had not entered their minds.

Those who can grasp a kind of pettiness found at every social level will understand the awe with which Angoulême’s bourgeoisie regarded the Hôtel de Bargeton. The inhabitant of L’Houmeau beheld the grandeur of that miniature Louvre, the glory of the Angoumoisin Hôtel de Rambouillet, shining at a distance; yet within it were gathered all the direst intellectual poverty and decayed gentility from twenty leagues about.

Political opinion in Angoulême ran to inflated commonplaces; the Quotidienne was lukewarm in loyalty and Louis XVIII was called a Jacobin. The local women were for the most part awkward, silly, insipid, and ill-dressed; something was always amiss—neither toilette nor talk, flesh nor spirit, was complete. Châtelet tolerated the society only for his designs on Mme. de Bargeton. Yet the manners and spirit of the ruined nobles and their memory of good breeding covered many deficiencies. Nobility of feeling was more real there than in Paris. These country Royalists were like old-fashioned, tarnished silver—antiquated but weighty; their attachment to the Bourbons was as fixed as a faith. Their exclusiveness elevated them; each noble had a value for the townsmen.

Some women, flattered by M. du Châtelet, found in him the qualities their men lacked and were soothed. They hoped to succeed to the Imperial Highness. Purists thought the intruder might be seen in Mme. de Bargeton’s house, but not elsewhere. Du Châtelet endured much insolence yet held his ground by cultivating clergy and the queen of Angoulême, indulging her provincial foibles and bringing her the latest books and poems. He read aloud what appeared, joining her in ecstasies she felt sincerely and he suppressed. He bore the Romantics with patience hardly to be expected of a man of the Imperial school. Mme. de Bargeton, inflated by the Bourbon revival, loved M. de Chateaubriand for calling Victor Hugo “a sublime child.” She longed for Paris and for knowing genius personally. Du Châtelet believed he had performed a clever act when he told her there was in Angoulême “another sublime child,” a young poet surpassing Paris—though he knew nothing of it. The headmaster had shown the Baron admirable verses: a poor, humble lad from L’Houmeau, a second Chatterton without the Englishman’s political baseness.

Mme. de Bargeton’s little circle pretended taste for art and letters—one scraped a fiddle, another dabbed sepia, another presided over an agricultural society. To her, amid these grotesques, any genuine artist was a feast. She raved at the news and insisted on seeing the poet; within two days the sometime diplomatic courier had arranged (through the headmaster) for Lucien’s appearance at the Hôtel de Bargeton.

For humble provincials, where class distances are greater than in Paris, the headmaster’s news threw Lucien into ferment. He and David had often looked up at the Hôtel’s gables in wonder; now Lucien was to be welcomed there. Only his sister Eve knew. Thrifty and proud, she raised a few louis to buy him thin shoes and a new suit, made a frill for his best shirt, washed and pleated it, and gave countless practical cautions—don’t rest elbows on the table, don’t forget yourself in those aristocratic precincts. She walked with him to St. Peter’s Gate, watched him go, trembling at the thought that Lucien in Mme. de Bargeton’s house meant the dawn of success. She was naive to expect that where ambition begins ingenuous feeling ends.

The Rue du Minage’s Hôtel looked less like a palace than a sober country house of mellow stone. Inside, it was plain but in good repair: a provincial courtyard, a chilly yet neat reception, a wainscoted drawing-room beyond a dim salon. The carved woodwork had been painted gray; crimson damask walls bore a meagre border; furniture was modest and covered. Mme. de Bargeton sat on the sofa, lit by two wax candles in a sconce on a round table.

She did not rise but twisted gracefully, smiling on the young poet. For Lucien those first three hours were a dream he wished to prolong. She was slender, in love with love and loverless, delicate despite strength; her foibles, exaggerated by manner, appealed to his youthful taste for hyperbole. He did not notice faded cheeks or hardened patches; her spirit captivated him. She wore a coif of slashed black velvet that recalled medieval legend; red-gold curls escaped, framing an oval face with bright gray eyes. Her hands were tapering and well-kept. She pointed to a seat by her side; du Châtelet settled into an easy-chair; Lucien perceived there was no one else.

Mme. de Bargeton’s words intoxicated him. He was diffident and extremely beautiful; she took him to be poetry incarnate. He scrutinized her discreetly and found her a perfect great lady. She did not ask him for verses—she meant he should come again to read them—this was enough. M. Sixte du Châtelet resented a rival. He accompanied Lucien to the first flight of steps to practice diplomacy and then, with the complacency of a man who had engineered the introduction, gave him advice.

Châtelet bewailed the insolence Lucien must expect from the town’s bourgeoisie, hinting at the danger of another Revolution if they did not alter their behavior. He declared his devotion to Mme. de Bargeton—he had been paying court to her for want of better and now was desperately in love; she would be his soon. He spoke like one resolved to destroy a rival. The elderly butterfly of the Empire pressed down upon the poet with self-importance, embellishing tales to impress and to crush. Lucien, though awed by the coxcomb, was not afraid.

He returned again and again to the house—at first sparingly, as a man of L’Houmeau should, then more often as he grew used to condescension. When noblesse called and found him there, they treated him with the graciousness well-bred people use toward inferiors; at first he took their kindness at face value, later detecting their patronizing tone, which stung and confirmed a bitter Republicanism—a phase many aspirants to patrician life pass through.

But would Lucien endure anything for Naïs?—for so he heard her named. In Angoumois, these folk called one another by Christian names as a last distinction. Lucien loved Naïs as a young man loves the first woman who flatters him. She prophesied great things for him and soothed her own pride by exalting him. She treated him like a child to keep him near, making him her reader and secretary, caring for him more than she expected after her calamity. She tortured herself arguing it would be folly to love a man of twenty, socially inferior; her behavior mixed familiarity and capricious pride, loftiness and tenderness. At first, Lucien felt dread, hope, despair—the bliss and anguish of first love. For two months she was a benefactress who took a mother’s interest; then confidences came. She began to call him “dear Lucien,” then simply “dear.” He grew bolder and called her Naïs; she reproached him—then allowed him the less-used appellation “Louise.” Lucien felt in heaven.

One evening she showed him a portrait she had quickly put away, then Cante-Croix’s picture, and told the piteous story of a stainless love cruelly cut short. Lucien, too young to analyze whether she was practicing infidelity or preparing a rival, gave himself to despair when she raised scruples about duty or appearances. He cried, “I shall not die for you, I will live for you,” and meant to have no more of M. de Cante-Croix. Startled by the progress of their love, Louise demanded some verses for her album. When she read his stanzas she was moved and changed. The passage she liked—lines that spoke of a mistress’ pencil and hidden gladness, of memories bringing visions fair as cloudless skies—brought her to tears. Her first kiss on his forehead calmed him. She intended to form him, teach him languages, perfect his manners—pretexts for having him constantly near.

She resumed music for his sake, played Beethoven until he swooned with delight, and asked, “Is not such happiness as this enough?” He answered, “Yes.” A week earlier things had reached a point where she asked him to dine with M. de Bargeton as a third; the town knew and the outcry was terrific. Some cried “See what comes of Liberal doctrines!” Others gossiped.

Then jealous du Châtelet discovered that the monthly nurse, Madame Charlotte, was Mme. Chardon, “the mother of the Chateaubriand of L’Houmeau,” he joked. Mme. de Chandour hurried to Mme. de Bargeton.

“Naïs, dear,” she said, “do you know what everyone is saying? This little rhymster’s mother nursed my sister-in-law.”

Mme. de Bargeton replied with regal coolness. “What is extraordinary in that? She is a druggist’s widow. Suppose you and I had not a penny; what should we do?” Her presence of mind quelled the jeremiads. Great natures make a virtue of misfortune; perseverance under evil report has irresistible attraction. Innocence has the piquancy of the forbidden.

That evening Mme. de Bargeton’s rooms were crowded with friends who came to remonstrate. She used her caustic wit, saying that since noble families could not produce a Molière or Rousseau, people must accept that great men had upholsterers and clockmakers for fathers; genius was always noble. She railed at boorish squires for ignorance, and with such talk she bewildered her audience and conjured away the storm.

When Lucien obeyed her request and appeared in the faded drawing-room where whist-tables were set, she brought him forward like a queen who means to be obeyed. She addressed the controller of excise as “M. Châtelet,” leaving him thunderstruck at the discovery she knew about his hollow particle. Lucien was thrust upon her circle and received as a poisonous element everyone vowed to expel with insolence.

Naïs had won a victory but lost supremacy. Rumor of insurrection spread. Amélie, Mme. de Chandour, following Châtelet’s counsel, resolved to erect a rival salon on Wednesdays. Mme. de Bargeton’s salon had been open every evening; those faithful to routine were devoted to its staircase and mistress. Alexandre de Brébian made a gibe: before the Revolution the greatest nobles admitted men like Dulcos and Grimm—nobodies like this poet—but they never received tax-collectors, he added; after all, Châtelet is only a tax-collector. Du Châtelet suffered for Chardon: everyone turned cold toward him; feeling attacked, he swore he would possess her. He then sided with the mistress, supported the young poet, and declared himself Lucien’s friend. The overlooked diplomatist fêted Lucien with a lavish dinner, inviting the prefect, receiver-general, colonel of the garrison, the head of the Naval School, the president of the Court, and other authorities. The poet was praised so highly that anyone but a young man of twenty-two might have suspected a hoax. After dinner Châtelet induced him to recite “The Dying Sardanapalus”; the headmaster applauded wildly and compared it to Jean-Baptiste Rousseau’s finest. Sixte, Baron du Châtelet, thought the rhymster would wither in this hothouse of adulation and perhaps ruin himself; yet he continued to court Mme. de Bargeton and watched, waiting for the chance to ruin Lucien.

From then on vague rumors reported a great man in Angoumois. Mme. de Bargeton was praised for her interest. No sooner was her conduct approved than she sought general sanction. She announced a soirée with ices, tea, and cakes—an innovation where tea was sold only by druggists as medicine. The flower of Angoumois was summoned to hear Lucien read. Louise spoke to him of the social cabal against him and warned of perils and obstacles insurmountable to weaklings. She lauded sacrifice and glory, dressing her speech with pompous epithets, and in doing so enlarged her own sense of grandeur. She counseled him to renounce his patronymic and take the noble name of Rubempré; she would obtain the favor, being related to the Marquise d’Espard. The words “King,” “Marquise,” and “the Court” dazzled Lucien; baptism into a noble name seemed necessary.

“Dear child,” said Louise, mock-tenderly, “the sooner it is done, the sooner it will be sanctioned.”

She spanned social strata and showed him that this step would raise him many rungs. Seizing the moment she persuaded him to forswear the chimerical notions of ’89 about equality; she roused a thirst for social distinction, softening David’s commonsense. The rabid Liberal became a clandestine Monarchist. Lucien set his teeth on the apple of desire for rank, luxury, and fame. He swore to win a crown to lay at his lady’s feet, even if blood were shed. To prove his courage, he told her of his present life—hardships, shackles of poverty borne with pride, days of work for David, nights of study. His ardor recalled the colonel of six-and-twenty; Mme. de Bargeton’s eyes grew soft; Lucien seized a hand she had let fall and kissed it with the frenzy of a lover and a poet. She even allowed him to kiss her forehead.

“Oh, child! child! if anyone should see us, I should look very ridiculous,” she said, shaking off ecstatic torpor.

That evening her wit assailed Lucien’s prejudices. Men of genius, she argued, had no time for family obligations; their tasks demanded sacrifices. Families might suffer initially but would later share the spoils of victory. Genius is accountable to no one and must judge means to an end that others cannot see. She quoted Bernard Palissy, Louis XI, Fox, Napoleon, Columbus—gamblers who began life hampered by debt or poverty, reviled as bad sons or brothers, yet became the pride of family and country.

Her arguments fell on fertile soil: when desires burned, all means seemed admissible to Lucien. But the other side was visible—failure leads to social exile and disgrace. Unconsciously he stood with genius’s palm in one hand and the shameful ending of a convict in the other.

Louise loosened provincial bands so well that Lucien resolved on an experiment. He wanted to be certain this proud conquest was truly his without risking a rebuff; the forthcoming soirée gave the opportunity. Ambition blended with love: he loved and meant to rise. Youth’s idealism was corrupted by the mercenary lure of society; Lucien persuaded himself his scheming was for David’s sake.

He wrote a long letter to Louise—copying it three times—telling of his father’s genius and wasted hopes and of his grinding poverty. He described his sister as an angel and David as another Cuvier, a great man of the future, father, friend, and brother. He begged Louise to do for David what she had done for him; he would feel unworthy of her love if he did not ask. It was a wild letter, full of boyish casuistry and naive eloquence, a tissue of words embroidered by unconscious revelations women love.

Lucien left the letter with the housemaid and went to the office, spending the day reading proofs, supervising orders, and attending to the printing-house. He said nothing to David; youth bears a child’s heart and sometimes silent resolve. Perhaps he feared David’s clear-sighted eyes that read to the depths. But when he later read Chénier’s poems with David, the secret rose in him at the sting of reproach, like the probing of a wound.

And now try to understand the thoughts that troubled Lucien as he went down from Angoulême. Was the great lady angry with him? Would she receive David? Had he, in his ambition, flung himself back into the depths of L’Houmeau? Before he set that kiss on Louise’s forehead, he had measured the distance between a queen and her favorite; so far had he come in five months, and he did not reckon that David could cross the same ground in a moment. Yet he did not know how completely the lower orders were excluded from that upper world; he did not suspect that a second experiment of this kind meant ruin for Mme. de Bargeton. Once accused of liking canaille, Louise would be driven from her circle as men shunned a leper. Naïs might have broken moral law and been defended, but admitting all sorts of people to her house was an unforgivable sin. The sins of those in power are overlooked; once they abdicate, they pay the penalty. And what was it but abdication to receive David?

Lucien’s aristocratic instinct found other difficulties. Fine manners are not always signs of noble feeling; manners can be acquired or bestowed by fortune—graceful figure, distinguished features, a certain ring in the voice. David lacked those outward trifles Nature had given Lucien. Lucien, of gentle blood on his mother's side, could hear the jokes at David’s expense; David had his father the pressman’s physique and a flat Gael’s foot. Lucien pictured Mme. de Bargeton’s repressed smile and, without being ashamed of his friend, resolved to disregard his first impulse and think twice before repeating such risks.

So, after the hour of poetry and self-sacrifice, after the reading that had opened literature like a sun, the hour of worldly wisdom and scheming struck for Lucien.

Back in L’Houmeau he wished he had not written that letter; he wished he could take it back; down the vista of the future he saw the inexorable laws of the world. He guessed that nothing succeeds like success, and it cost him something to step down from the first rung of the ladder by which he meant to storm the heights. Pictures of his simple life rose before him—David, generous and self-sacrificing; his mother proud in her lowly lot; his sister submissive and gracious; his own unspoiled conscience and budding hopes—and the past broke into flowers.

Then he told himself it was finer to hew his own way through hostile mobs of aristocrats and philistines by repeated strokes than to reach the goal through a woman’s favor. Sooner or later his genius would shine; others had tamed society. Women would love him when that day came. Napoleon’s meteoric rise encouraged such ambitions. He flung his schemes to the winds and blamed himself. Lucien was made to shift between extremes easily.

Lucien had none of the scholar’s love for retreat; for a month he had felt shame at the shop front reading POSTEL (LATE CHARDON), PHARMACEUTICAL CHEMIST in gaudy letters. It offended him that his father’s name should be posted where every carriage passed. Every evening, closing the ugly iron gate and going up to Beaulieu to give his arm to Mme. de Bargeton among the dandies, he chafed at the disparity between his lodging and his fortune.

“I love Mme. de Bargeton; perhaps soon she will be mine, yet here I live in this rat-hole!” he thought, descending into the little yard behind the shop. Bundles of boiled herbs lay along the wall, the apprentice scoured a caldron, and M. Postel, girded in a laboratory apron, inspected some product while watching for customers.

A strong scent of camomile and peppermint pervaded the yard and the poor little dwelling reached by a short ladder. Lucien’s room was an attic under the roof.

“Good-day, sonny,” said M. Postel. “Are you pretty middling? I have just been experimenting on treacle, but it would take a man like your father to find what I am looking for. Ah! he was a famous chemist, he was! If I had only known his gout specific, you and I should be rolling along in our carriage this day.”

Postel’s officious talk of Chardon senior’s unlucky secrecy always stabbed Lucien. He began to find Postel vulgar, though he blessed the man for his kindness; honest Postel had helped the widow and children more than once.

“Is there a letter for me?”

“Yes, a letter that smells like balm! it is lying on the corner near my desk.”

Mme. de Bargeton’s letter, lying among physic bottles—Lucien sprang in to rescue it.

“Be quick, Lucien! your dinner has been waiting an hour!” a sweet voice called through a half-opened window; but Lucien did not hear.

“That brother of yours has gone crazy, mademoiselle,” said Postel, lifting his face. At the sight of Eve his expression became ceremonious and amiable; he often hinted that he would make advances toward his predecessor’s daughter.

Eve was tall, dark-haired, blue-eyed; though showing strength, she was gentle, tender-hearted, and devoted. Her frank innocence, quiet acceptance of a hard life, and spotless character won David Séchard’s heart. Their love had grown quietly, German fashion—without fervid protestations, in secret as if something barred them; they hid feelings so as not to wrong Lucien. David had no confidence and could not believe Eve could care for him; Eve, penniless, was shy. She was proud, not vain, and would not advance toward the son of a father said to be rich. Some said the vineyard at Marsac was worth more than eighty thousand francs; old Séchard had savings and bought bits of land. David, however, lived simply and knew little of this; to him Marsac was a hovel bought years ago, seen once a year at vintage time when his father boasted.

David was a student leading a solitary life; his love, nurtured in solitude, was timid and sought encouragement. He stood in awe of Eve and was awkward and eager to hurry away. He repressed his passion and was silent. Often he would approach L’Houmeau, but the green railings made his heart fail and he turned back. Eve read clearly what little he showed; she was proud of his deep reverence, but it was his enthusiastic belief in Lucien that drew her most. The mute delights of their love were like wildflowers: glances and half-words, melancholy tender as moss. Eve had seen the strength beneath David’s weakness and made allowance; a single word might bind them closer.

Eve opened the door, and Lucien sat at the little X-shaped trestle table. There was no tablecloth; the household had but three silver spoons and forks, and Eve used them all for her dearly loved brother.

“What have you there?” she asked when she had set a dish and put the extinguisher on the portable stove.

Lucien did not answer. Eve set a little plate garnished with vine-leaves and a jug of cream.

“There, Lucien, I have had strawberries for you.”

But Lucien was absorbed in his letter. Eve sat beside him without murmur; in a sister’s love there is pleasure to be treated without ceremony.

“Oh! what is it?” she cried as she saw tears in his eyes.

“Nothing, nothing, Eve,” he said, and, putting his arm about her, drew her close and kissed her with surprising warmth.

“You are keeping something from me.”

“Well, then—she loves me.”

“I knew very well that you kissed me for somebody else,” Eve pouted, flushing.

“We shall all be happy,” cried Lucien, swallowing great spoonfuls of soup.

“We?” echoed Eve, with the same presentiment that had crossed David’s mind. “You will not care so much about us now.”

“How can you think that, if you know me?”

Eve clasped his hand, carried off the empty plate and brought the dish she had made. Lucien read his letter again instead of eating; Eve, discreet, respected his silence. The letter said, in effect, that Mme. de Bargeton, although sympathetic to merit, knew the prejudices of her circle and could not compel them to accept a man like David. She offered to help, but attached a condition: she must see the friend herself and decide whether his character and manners would suit her salon—a kind of anxious mother's duty toward the poet she favored.

Lucien had no suspicion of the art with which polite society says “Yes” on the way to “No,” and a “No” that leads to a “Yes.” He took the note for a victory. David should go to Mme. de Bargeton’s house! David would shine in the majesty of his genius! He raised his head proudly; his face radiated hope, and Eve told him he looked handsome.

“If that woman has any sense, she must love you! And if so, to-night she will be vexed, for all the ladies will try all sorts of coquetries on you. How handsome you will look when you read your Saint John in Patmos! If only I were a mouse, and could just slip in and see it! Come, I have put your clothes out in mother’s room.”

The mother’s room testified to self-respecting poverty: white curtains, a strip of cheap green carpet, a chest of drawers, a looking-glass, a few walnut chairs. A clock on the chimney-piece told of vanished prosperity. Eve’s adjoining closet had room for a narrow bed, a low chair, and a work-table; the door always stood open for air. Though these things spoke of poverty, the atmosphere was sedate and studious.

Lucien was tying his cravat when David’s step sounded in the little yard. He appeared in a hurry.

“Well, David!” cried Lucien, “we have gained the day! She loves me! You shall come too.”

“No,” David said with confusion. “I came to thank you, but I have been thinking seriously. My life is cut out for me, Lucien. I am David Séchard, printer to His Majesty in Angoulême, my name on bills posted on every wall. For people of that class, I am an artisan, a craftsman who lives over a shop in the Rue de Beaulieu. I have not the wealth of a Keller nor the name of a Desplein—powers the nobles ignore. How can I prove my claim to this sudden elevation? I should only make myself a laughing-stock for nobles and bourgeoisie alike. As for you, your position is different. A foreman is not committed. You are gaining knowledge indispensable for later; you can explain your present work by your future. You can leave your place tomorrow and begin something else; nobody has pigeon-holed you. Take advantage of your social maiden fame to walk alone and grasp honors. Enjoy all pleasures. I wish you luck; I shall enjoy your success; you will be like a second self. Yes, in my mind I shall live your life. You shall have the holiday life; I will lead the tradesman’s life of sober toil, patient scientific research.

“You shall be our aristocracy,” he continued, looking at Eve. “If you totter, I will steady you. If you suffer treachery, find refuge in our hearts; love there will never change. Influence and favor might fail us if we were two; we should stand in each other’s way; go forward, you can tow me after you if it comes to that. I shall dedicate my life to yours. The thing you did—risked losing your benefactress, your love perhaps, rather than forsake me—that little thing was so great—ah, well, Lucien, that alone would bind me to you forever if we were not brothers already. Have no remorse; be not concerned about seeming to take the larger share. This one-sided bargain is exactly to my taste. Suppose you give me a pang now and again, who knows that I shall not be your debtor all my life?”

He glanced timidly at Eve; her eyes were full of tears; she saw all beneath the surface.

“In fact,” he added, “you are well made, you have a graceful figure, you wear your clothes with an air, you look like a gentleman in that blue coat with yellow buttons and plain nankeen trousers; I should look like a workingman among those people, awkward and out of my element, or say foolish things—or say nothing. But you can take your mother’s name; call yourself Lucien de Rubempré; I am and shall be David Séchard. Everything tells for you there and against me. Women will worship that angel face of yours; won’t they, Eve?”

Lucien sprang up and flung his arms about David. David’s humility had dispelled many doubts. Was it possible not to be tender towards a friend who, in friendship, thought the very thoughts he had reached by ambition? The aspirant for love and honors felt the way smoothed; the young comrade felt his whole heart go out.

It was one of those rare moments when forces in us are sweetly strung and every chord gives full resonance.

Yet this noble goodness increased Lucien’s inclination to take himself as the center. Don’t we all say, “L’État, c’est moi!”? Lucien’s mother and sister concentrated tenderness on him, David was devoted; he had all the faults of a spoiled eldest son. Their unselfishness fostered egoism in him; Mme. de Bargeton was doing her best to develop the same fault by inciting him to forget his sister, mother, and David. He was far from doing so yet; but there was ground to fear that as his sphere widened his thought would be how to maintain himself in it.

When emotion subsided, David suggested Lucien’s Saint John in Patmos might be too biblical for such an audience. For his first appearance before the exacting Charente public, Lucien seemed nervous. David advised him to take André de Chénier and substitute certain pleasure for dubious delight. Lucien was a perfect reader; the listeners would enjoy him, and his modesty would serve him. Like most young people, the pair endowed the world with their own intelligence and virtues; youth not yet gone astray is pitiless for others’ sins and magnanimous in faith. Only experience teaches Raphael’s truth—“To comprehend is to equal.”

The power of appreciating poetry is rare in France; esprit dries up the source of sacred ecstasy; nobody cares to decipher the sublime. Lucien was about to have his first experience of worldlings’ ignorance and indifference. He went by the printing office for David’s volume.

The two lovers were left alone, and David had never felt more embarrassed. Countless terrors seized him; he half wished, half feared Eve would praise him; he longed to run away, for modesty is not exempt from coquetry. He was afraid to utter a word that might seem to beg for thanks; everything he could think of put him in a false position, so he held his tongue and looked guilty. Eve guessed his agony and enjoyed the pause; when David wrung his hat as if to go, she smiled.

“Monsieur David,” she said, “if you are not going to Mme. de Bargeton’s, we can spend the time together. It is fine; shall we take a walk along the Charente? We will have a talk about Lucien.”

David longed to fling himself at her feet. Eve’s tone solved the difficulties: her suggestion was better than praise; it was the first grace given by love.

“But give me time to dress!” she said as David made to go.

David went out; he, who had never known one tune from another, hummed. Honest Postel heard him and suspected Eve’s feelings for the printer.

Small things that evening made a great impression on Lucien; his character was peculiarly susceptible to first impressions. Like all inexperienced lovers, he arrived so early that Louise was not in the drawing-room; M. de Bargeton was there alone. Lucien had begun to practice the small deceits lovers pay for married happiness—deceits through which a woman learns her power—but he had not met the lady’s husband.

M. de Bargeton’s intellect was limited, poised between harmless vacancy and excessive stupidity. He was impressed with doing his duty in society; to be agreeable he adopted an opera-dancer’s smile as his sole expression. Satisfied, he smiled; dissatisfied, he smiled again. He smiled at good and evil alike; when obliged to express approval a complacent laugh reinforced the smile; otherwise he hardly spoke. A tête-à-tête embarrassed him, and he rescued himself by returning to the artless ways of childhood—thinking aloud about the smallest personal sensations. He never talked weather or commonplaces; he plunged you into intimate trivialities: “I took veal this morning to please Mme. de Bargeton, and my stomach has been uneasy since. How do you explain it?” or “I am just about to ring for a glass of eau sucrée; will you have some?”

These remarks permitted no discussion; a “Yes” or “No” and conversation fell dead. Then M. de Bargeton implored help with a gaze that said, “You were saying?” He loved bores who talked about themselves and listened with delicate interest, so twaddlers credited him with more understanding than he showed. When others could find nobody to listen they went to him, sure of his eulogistic smile. Mme. de Bargeton’s rooms were always crowded, and generally her husband felt at ease. He watched arrivals, helped his wife bring guests, lay in wait for departures to take them to the door with that eternal smile. When conversation grew lively, he stood happy and mute, planted like a swan, listening to political talk or watching card-players without notion of the game; or he walked about taking snuff to promote digestion. Anaïs was the bright side of his life; she made it unspeakably pleasant. Stretched in his armchair he watched admiringly while she played hostess. He tried to see the point in her remarks, and often succeeded only after delay; his smiles appeared like delayed explosions. His respect for his wife almost amounted to adoration. She had drilled him into military subordination; he yielded passive obedience. “Go and call on Monsieur So-and-So,” she would say, and he went forthwith, like a soldier at command. He stood at attention in her presence and waited motionless for orders.

There was talk of nominating the mute gentleman for a deputy. Lucien had barely begun to understand this unfathomable man; he had scarcely frequented the house long enough. M. de Bargeton, stretched full-length in his great chair, seemed to see and understand everything; his silence added to his dignity, and his figure inspired Lucien with profound awe. Imaginative natures magnify everything; Lucien took this gentleman for a formidable sphinx rather than a mere sentinel, and thought it necessary to conciliate him.

“I am the first comer,” he said, bowing with more respect than people usually showed the worthy man.

“That is natural enough,” said M. de Bargeton.

Lucien took the remark for an epigram; he imagined the lady’s husband jealous and reddened, trying to compose himself.

“You live in L’Houmeau,” said M. de Bargeton, “and people who live a long way off always come earlier than those who live near by.”

“What is the reason of that?” asked Lucien politely.

“I don’t know,” M. de Bargeton answered, relapsing into immobility.

“You have not cared to find out,” Lucien continued; “any one who could observe could discover the cause.”

“Ah!” said M. de Bargeton, “final causes! Eh! eh!...”

The conversation died; Lucien racked his brains to revive it.

“Mme. de Bargeton is dressing, no doubt,” he began, shuddering at the silliness of the question.

“Yes, she is dressing,” her husband naturally answered.

Lucien looked at the ceiling, saw the little chandelier stripped of gauze and filled with wax candles, covers removed from furniture revealing faded damask—preparations that meant something. He glanced at his boots, misgave about his costume, and fixed on a Japanese jar, trying to find a hobby of M. de Bargeton’s to humor him.

“You seldom leave the city, monsieur?” he began.

“Very seldom.”

Silence again. M. de Bargeton watched Lucien’s slightest movements like a suspicious cat; the young man’s presence disturbed him. Each was afraid of the other.

“Can he feel suspicious of my attentions?” thought Lucien; “he seems anything but friendly.”

His unease eased when the old man-servant announced “M. du Châtelet.” The Baron entered at ease, greeted Bargeton, and favored Lucien with the trendy nod the poet called purse-proud impertinence.

Sixte du Châtelet wore dazzling white trousers, pumps, thread stockings, an eyeglass with a black ribbon over a white waistcoat and a fashionable black coat. He was the faded beau his antecedents suggested: dye hiding grizzled hair and whiskers, a travel tan, and the retained air of an Imperial Highness’ charming private secretary. He inspected Lucien’s clothes from head to foot, then coolly returned his eyeglass to his pocket with a look that said, “I am satisfied.” Lucien, eclipsed by the inland revenue department’s elegance, thought his turn would come when he turned a poetic face upon the assembly; but the sharp pang of M. de Bargeton’s imagined hostility remained. The Baron seemed to bring the weight of his fortune to humiliate his poverty.

M. de Bargeton, having little to say, kept a question in reserve for emergencies.

“Well, monsieur,” he said to Châtelet, “is there anything fresh? anything people are talking about?”

“The latest thing is M. Chardon,” Châtelet said maliciously. “Ask him. Have you brought some charming poet for us?” he added, adjusting his side curl.

“I should have asked you whether I had succeeded,” Lucien answered; “you have been before me in the field of verse.”

“Pshaw!” said the Baron, “a few vaudevilles, lines for music, and my long Epistle to a Sister of Bonaparte will not hand down my name to posterity.”

At that moment Mme. de Bargeton appeared in full toilette: a Jewess’ turban with an Eastern clasp, cameos gleaming through gauze about her shoulders, short sleeves displaying bracelets. Lucien was charmed by the theatrical dress. M. du Châtelet lavished fulsome compliments that pleased her; she was glad to be praised in Lucien’s hearing but scarcely glanced at him, meeting Châtelet with a civility that kept him at a distance.

Guests began to arrive. First came the Bishop and his Vicar-General, dignified and reverend though unlike—one tall and attenuated, the other short and fat. Both were impassive; their silence and reserve were supposed to hide great intellects.

Close after them came Mme. de Chandour and her husband, Stanislas, a ci-devant young man slim at forty-five, with a sieve-like countenance. His cravat presented two menacing points, his coat-tails were awry, a cut-away waistcoat showed a swelling starched shirt fastened with massive gold studs. Exaggerated dress and talk made him almost a living caricature.

Stanislas admired himself, verified waistcoat buttons, admired his tight trousers and pointed shoes, then checked his hair in a mirror and surveyed the women with happy, flinging airs—a king of the poultry-yard—pleasant to the aristocratic circle. His eighteenth-century grossness procured success with women; his coxcomb superciliousness intrigued them and challenged M. du Châtelet.

Amélie de Chandour, short, plump, fair and dark-haired, was a poor actress; her loud voice matched her restless head, feathered or flowered. She had a flow of conversation, though asthma wheezed it away.

M. de Saintot, Astolphe, President of the Agricultural Society, tall, stout, high-colored, usually appeared with his wife Élisa, called Lili—a lady with a withered-fern face, solemn, pious, and trying at cards. Astolphe was supposed to be a scientific man; in truth he was ignorant but had compiled articles on Sugar and Brandy for a Dictionary of Agriculture by pillage of others’ work. It was believed he was writing on modern husbandry, but though he locked himself in his study mornings, he had not written much in years. If callers arrived, he contrived to be found rummaging papers, yet spent time on puerilities: reading newspapers, cutting corks, drawing on blotting-paper. He’d turn to Cicero to find anything applicable to current events and drag a quotation into conversation, prompting the remark, “Astolphe is a well of learning.” The belief in his abilities sustained itself by rumor.

After this pair came M. de Bartas, Adrien, who boomed his song in a bass voice and made prodigious musical claims. His self-conceit rested on solfeggi; he admired his singing, then talked music to the point of monomania. Animated only by that subject, he was miserable until begged to sing. After bellowing an air he strutted and accepted praise, moving from group to group for more; when compliments ceased, he returned to the song’s difficulties or extolled the composer.

M. Alexandre de Brébian produced heroic sepia sketches, disfiguring friends’ walls and spoiling albums. He and M. de Bartas arrived together, each with the other’s wife on his arm in a cross-cornered arrangement that gossip said was carried to extremes. Mesdames Charlotte de Brébian and Joséphine de Bartas—Lolotte and Fifine—both cared equally for a scarf or color reconciliation, aiming to look like Parisiennes while neglecting their homes. Their husbands availed an artist’s privilege to dress as they pleased; provincial dowdiness on them contrasted with their wives’ doll-like finery, making them like stage supernumeraries at third-rate theatre weddings.

One of the queerest figures was M. le Comte de Senonches, Jacques, a mighty hunter, lean and sunburned, haughty as a wild boar, suspicious as a Venetian, jealous as a Moor, yet intimate with M. du Hautoy—Francis, the friend of the house.

Madame de Senonches, Zéphirine, was tall and fine-looking though her complexion showed pimples of liver complaint, making her exacting. Slender and delicate, she affected languid manners but revealed passion and the consciousness that every caprice would be gratified.

Francis had given up a consulship in Valence and sacrificed diplomatic prospects to live near Zéphirine in Angoulême. He managed the household, educations, languages, and fortunes of M. and Mme. de Senonches with complete devotion. Noble, administrative, and bourgeois Angoulême had long looked askance at this perfect union of three persons, but the conjugal trinity became so pleasing that had M. du Hautoy suggested marriage, he would have seemed monstrously immoral. Mme. de Senonches had a lady companion and goddaughter, Mlle. de la Haye; her excessive attachment to Françoise de la Haye raised surmises of disquieting mysteries, and despite discrepancies in dates, people thought Françoise bore a striking likeness to Francis du Hautoy.

When “Jacques” was shooting nearby, people asked after Francis, and Jacques would discourse on his steward’s ailments and speak of his wife in the second place. Such blindness in a jealous man amused friends who drew him out on the topic. M. du Hautoy was a finical dandy whose minute self-care degenerated into mincing affectation: he fussed about coughs, appetite, digestion, and sleep. Zéphirine made a valetudinarian of him; she doctored and coddled him, fed him delicacies, embroidered his waistcoats and handkerchiefs until he became a kind of Japanese idol. Their understanding was perfect: Zizine consulted Francis with a look; he took his ideas from her eyes. They frowned and smiled together, seeming to take counsel even before making the simplest remark.

The largest landowner was the Marquis de Pimentel; between him and his wife they had forty thousand livres and spent winters in Paris. This evening they arrived in their calèche with neighbors, the Baron and Baroness de Rastignac, their party, the Baroness’ aunt and two charming, penniless daughters carefully brought up and simply dressed to show natural loveliness.

These first families met a reception of chilling silence; respect paid them was tinged with jealousy, especially since Mme. de Bargeton paid marked attention to the guests. The two families were aloof from provincial gossip, belonged to no clique, lived quietly, and maintained dignified reserve. M. de Pimentel and M. de Rastignac were addressed by full names, and no length of acquaintance had brought their wives and daughters into Angoulême’s select coterie; both families were too near the Court to risk provincial follies.

The Prefect and the General in command arrived last, with a country gentleman who had brought a treatise on silkworms to David that morning. Evidently a mayor or canton gentleman, his fine estate sufficed for gentility; but he was unused to polite society, uneasy in his clothes, at a loss with his hands, shifting from foot to foot, half rising and sitting as people spoke to him. He seemed ready to perform menial service: obsequious, nervous, grave by turns, laughing eagerly at every joke, listening with servility, then assuming a knowing air when imagining others laughed at him. His treatise weighed on his mind; he tried repeatedly to talk silkworms but met M. de Bartas, who talked music, and M. de Saintot, who quoted Cicero, until late in the evening Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard showed sympathy.

Mme. and Mlle. du Brossard were interesting yet pitiable: as poor as they were noble, their dress betrayed carefully hidden penury. The daughter, a big, heavy seven-and-twenty, was lauded as a good pianist by her mother in the clumsiest way. Mme. du Brossard, anxious to marry off Camille, declared her child liked everything desirable—roving life, then country farmhouses—adapting to any suitor’s taste. Mother and daughter bore a pinched dignity learned from experience; they had been objects of benevolent egoism and had sounded the void beneath consoling formulas the world offers the unfortunate.

M. de Séverac, fifty-nine and a childless widower, spoke of his silkworm nurseries; Mme. du Brossard enthused, “My daughter has always been fond of animals... she will grasp anything in a moment. Did she not understand one day the inverse ratio of the squares of distances!” This remark brought the conversation between Mme. du Brossard and M. de Séverac to a glorious close after Lucien’s reading that night.

A few habitués slipped in, as did one or two eldest sons: shy, mute young men tricked out in jewelry, highly honored by an invitation to the literary solemnity; the boldest chatted with Mlle. de la Haye. The women arranged themselves in a circle, the men stood behind them. It was a quaint assemblage of wrinkled faces and heterogeneous costumes but very alarming to Lucien; his heart beat fast under everyone’s look. Mme. de Bargeton welcomed illustrious Angoulême personages with ostentatious courtesy, but the uncomfortable feeling was aggravated by a trifling matter that would shock a young man unused to the world. Lucien noticed that no one except Louise, M. de Bargeton, the Bishop, and some few who wished to please the hostess called him M. de Rubempré; to the formidable audience he was M. Chardon. Lucien’s courage sank under inquisitive lips; he read his plebeian name in their movements and heard anticipatory criticisms in blunt provincial fashion. He had not expected such pin-pricks; it put him more out of humor with himself. Impatient to begin the reading, he hoped to assume an attitude to end his mental torment; but Jacques told Mme. de Pimentel of his sport, Adrien expounded Rossini to Mlle. Laure de Rastignac, and Astolphe, with a newspaper paragraph on a patent plow, lectured the Baron. Lucien, luckless poet, did not know there was scarce a soul in the room besides Mme. de Bargeton who could understand poetry. The matter-of-fact assembly was there by misapprehension; for the most part they did not know why they had come. Certain words—“beauty,” “glory,” “poetry”—bewitch even the coarsest intellect.

When everyone had arrived and the buzz faded after M. de Bargeton, obedient to his wife, made the circuit like a beadle tapping the pavement with his wand, securing silence at last, Lucien went to the round table near Mme. de Bargeton. A fierce thrill ran through him. He announced in an uncertain voice that, to prevent disappointment, he would read the masterpieces of a great poet discovered only recently (although André de Chénier’s poems had appeared in 1819, no one in Angoulême had heard of him). Everybody took the announcement one way—it was a shift of Mme. de Bargeton’s to save the poet’s self-love and put the audience at ease.

Lucien began with Le Malade and received a murmur of applause; but when he followed with L’Aveugle the strain proved too much for the average mind. Only artists or those with an artistic temperament can sympathize with such an ordeal. For poetry rendered aloud the listener must give devout attention; an intimate alliance between reader and audience is required, or swift, subtle communication of the poet’s thought and feeling is impossible. Here that sympathy was lacking, and Lucien was like an angel trying to sing of heaven amid the chucklings of hell. An intelligent man in the sphere most stimulating to his faculties can perceive everything around him; a musician or poet knows at once whether his audience admires or fails to follow him and feels it as a plant revives or droops. The men who had come with their wives discussed their own affairs; by acoustic law every murmur rang in Lucien’s ear; he noticed gaps caused by spasmodic jaw-work and teeth that seemed to grin defiance.

Searching, like the dove in the deluge, for any spot to rest on, he saw nothing but rows of impatient faces. Their owners were clearly waiting for him to finish; they'd come to discuss practical questions. Except for Laure de Rastignac, the Bishop, and two or three young men, they all looked bored. Those who understand poetry strive to develop its germs quickened by the poet; but this glacial audience, far from attaining the spirit, did not even heed the letter.

Lucien felt discouraged, damp with chill perspiration; a glowing glance from Louise gave him courage to persevere, but his poet’s heart bled.

“Do you find this very amusing, Fifine?” inquired the wizened Lili.

“Don’t ask me; I cannot keep my eyes open when anyone reads aloud.”

“I hope that Naïs will not give us poetry often in the evenings,” said Francis. “If I am obliged to attend while somebody reads aloud after dinner, it upsets my digestion.”

“Poor dearie,” whispered Zéphirine, “take a glass of eau sucrée.”

“It was very well declaimed,” said Alexandre, “but I like whist better.”

After this dictum, taken as a joke on “whist,” several card-players thought the reader’s voice needed a rest, and one or two couples slipped away into the card-room. But Louise, the Bishop, and Laure besought Lucien to continue, and he caught his audience’s attention with Chénier’s spirited reactionary iambes. Several, carried away by his impassioned delivery, applauded without understanding; such people are impressed by vociferation, as a coarse palate is tickled by strong spirits.

During the interval, as they had ices, Zéphirine sent Francis to examine the volume and told Amélie the poetry was in print.

Amélie brightened visibly. “Why, that is easily explained,” she said. “M. de Rubempré works for a printer. It's like a pretty woman making her own dresses,” she added, looking at Lolotte.

The women murmured, “He printed his poetry himself!”

“Then why does he call himself M. de Rubempré?” inquired Jacques. “If a noble takes a handicraft, he ought to lay his name aside.”

“So he did,” said Zizine, “but his name was plebeian; he took his mother’s name, which is noble.”

“Then we can read them ourselves,” said Astolphe.

This stupidity complicated matters until Sixte du Châtelet informed them that the prefatory announcement was factual, adding the poems had been written by a Royalist brother of Marie-Joseph Chénier, the Revolutionary leader. All Angoulême, except Mme. de Rastignac, her daughters, and the Bishop—who had felt the poetry's grandeur—were mystified and offended by the hoax. A smothered murmur rose, but Lucien did not heed it. Intoxicated by the poetry, he was far from the hateful world, striving to render in speech the music filling his soul, seeing faces through a cloudy haze. He read the sombre Elegy on the Suicide, lines in the taste of a bygone day, pervaded by sublime melancholy; then he turned to the page with the line, “Thy songs are sweet, I love to say them over,” and ended with the delicate idyll Néere.

Mme. de Bargeton sat with a hand buried in her curls, heedless of the havoc she wrought, gazing with unseeing eyes, lost in delicious dreaming; for the first time she had been transported to the sphere that was hers by right of nature. Judge how unpleasantly she was disturbed by Amélie, who expressed the general wish.

“Naïs,” the voice broke in, “we came to hear M. Chardon’s poetry, and you are giving us poetry from a book. The extracts are nice, but the ladies prefer the native wine; they'd rather have that.”

“The French language does not lend itself very readily to poetry, does it?” Astolphe remarked to Châtelet. “Cicero’s prose is a thousand times more poetical to my mind.”

“The true poetry of France is song, lyric verse,” Châtelet answered.

“I'd very much like to hear the poetry that cost Naïs her reputation,” said Zéphirine; “but after Amélie’s request it's unlikely she'll give us a specimen.”

“She ought to have them recited in justice to herself,” said Francis. “The little fellow’s genius is his sole justification.”

“You have been in the diplomatic service,” Amélie said to M. du Châtelet, “go and manage it.”

“Nothing easier,” said the Baron.

The Princess’ private secretary, used to such manoeuvres, went to the Bishop and brought him forward. At the Bishop’s entreaty Naïs had no choice but to ask Lucien to recite his own verses, and the Baron received a languishing smile from Amélie as reward.

“Decidedly, the Baron is a very clever man,” she observed to Lolotte.

But Amélie’s earlier remark about women making their own dresses rankled Lolotte.

“Since when have you begun to recognize the Emperor’s barons?” she asked, smiling.

Lucien had attempted to exalt his beloved in an ode titled “TO HER!” and, confident under Mme. de Bargeton’s protection, delivered it with schoolboy pride. Mme. de Bargeton, who usually regarded the company from an intellectual height, betrayed anxiety as Lucien recited stanza after stanza.

The poem pictured an angel visiting earth to bring solace, Love blinded by too much light, and a luminous pathway in heaven indicating the angel’s flight.

Amélie coquetted; the Baron, arbiter of taste, sneered that the verse was a Christianized Ossian—full of “immense, infinite, solitude, intelligence” and strained rhymes. Zéphirine found it obscure though the dedication was clear; Francis compared the archangel’s armor to thin gauze. The women, eager for poets to flatter them as angels, professed polite enchantment while remaining inwardly cold.

Lolotte ordered Adrien not to congratulate the poet; Adrien complied. Zéphirine told Francis that love was something lived rather than written; Stanislas agreed, flattering himself. Amélie, aiming to humble Naïs, attacked Lucien’s lowly origins—mocking his family trades—and the company joined in to humiliate him. Francis, seeking a public blow, engineered a subtler thrust through the Bishop.

He told the Bishop that Lucien’s mother, a modest woman of uncommon powers, supplied subjects for her son’s verses and that Lucien idolized her. Having planted the idea, he left the Bishop to make the intended allusion in conversation.

When Francis and the Bishop returned, the circle watched with renewed interest. Lucien, shy and unused to their manners, was embarrassed by their questions and by being called alternately M. Chardon and M. de Rubempré. He mistakenly addressed M. de Senonches as M. Lili, provoking rude laughter and Mme. de Bargeton’s crimsoned shame. Senonches muttered displeasure. Zéphirine compared Lucien to M. de Cante-Croix. Mme. de Bargeton, addressing the Marquise de Pimentel, philosophized on glory’s attraction and on women drawn to greatness. The Marquise laughed and sided with Naïs.

The Marquise and Marquis treated Lucien lightly; Lolotte asked if he worked quickly, as one would ask a joiner about a box. Stung, Lucien found courage in Mme. de Bargeton’s reply: poetry did not grow in his head like grass in their courtyards. The Bishop, sanctifying poetry, laid a hand on Lucien’s head and declared him a poet marked by Fate.

Glad for the championing, Lucien thanked the Bishop with a grateful look, unaware that the prelate’s next comment would wound him. Mme. de Bargeton’s penetrating glances increased her rivals’ fury.

Lucien spoke of the poet’s suffering, the difficulty of making ideas clear, the need for experience and deep feeling to create true characters—he named great literary creations as their fruit. When Châtelet asked what Lucien would make, Lucien said to proclaim it would be vanity; such works required long experience. He hinted bitterly that he had only begun. The Bishop’s joke—“Your excellent mother might assist you”—elicited delighted smiles of caste and M. de Bargeton’s familiar laugh.

Mme. de Bargeton retorted that a poet who looked to the Bible for inspiration had a mother in the Church, and challenged Lucien to recite Saint John in Patmos or Belshazzar’s Feast, mockingly invoking Rome’s role. The women smiled at the Latin.

Lucien faltered under the blows, sank, then rose resolved to conquer this world. He prepared to declaim Saint John in Patmos; but card-tables called the company back to amusement, and they returned to games, leaving Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton alone. The social world of Angoulême, conscious of its ignorance of poetry, clustered about the Pimentels and Rastignacs to hear their verdict; the Marquis and Marquise’s approval—“not bad for provincial poetry”—spread and grew malicious.

Music followed: Châtelet mangled du Bartas and Figaro; Châtelet sang a Chateaubriand ballad; duets and schoolroom songs filled the evening. Hurt by the company’s contempt for her poet, Mme. de Bargeton withdrew to her boudoir, followed by the Bishop and Mlle. de Rastignac, who was entranced by the poetry.

There, with no one to watch, Mme. de Bargeton murmured to Lucien that they had not understood him; she loved to repeat his songs. Lucien took comfort; she urged him to endure his troubles, asserting that suffering is the price of immortality and that struggle gives the eagle room to spread its wings. She begged him to remember, when he reached greatness, the minds disinherited by fate and to sing of lives choked and unblossomed—allegories of the spirit that remembers Heaven. The Bishop compared such a theme to the Song of Songs; Laure de Rastignac suggested it as a subject. The Vicar-General declared the sacred poem of France unwritten and prophesied success for the man who should write for religion. Mme. de Bargeton claimed the task for Lucien, seeing a dawn of vision in his eyes.

During this quieter talk, Amélie, Fifine, Adrien, and Francis reentered with Mme. de Rastignac to draw Naïs back for music. They gleefully announced that M. de Rubempré was to recite Saint John in Patmos; the word “biblical” became mockery, and Lucien excused himself for a lapse of memory. When he reappeared, no one noticed him; the poet’s halo had been plucked away. The landowners and pretentious set had no use for him; the women, jealous of Mme. de Bargeton, looked on him with icy scorn.

“So this is society!” Lucien thought as he took the long way home to L’Houmeau, walking to ease his anger and muse on answers that had come too late. Far from disheartened, his ambition burned: like many who reach a higher sphere before they can belong there, he vowed any sacrifice to remain. He imagined Eve and David by the river and hurried to meet them.

Eve had dressed simply but becomingly; David, shy at her beauty, had resolved to speak of himself and their future. They walked across the Bridge of Saint Anne along the Charente in silence until Eve broke it with small talk about the evening. David, thinking of Lucien, feared Mme. de Bargeton was spoiling him for hard work and would either make him her plaything or, if indifferent, break him. Lucien loved pleasure and would not endure the privations required for great literary work; society would stimulate desires beyond his means.

Eve, undismayed, promised to work with her mother to support Lucien until success came. Then David proposed: to marry Eve, give up his present life, and devote himself and any future fortune to Lucien’s support. He argued Lucien should not be foreman nor remain at L’Houmeau; together they could maintain Lucien until he made his way to Paris. He pledged that love and the desire to help Lucien would give him the perseverance to pursue a profitable invention he had been researching—something in paper-making—if Eve would marry him and keep his confidence while he worked.

Moved, Eve confessed she had never thought of any other man. David admitted his faults and modest means, and disclosed a plan based on his knowledge of chemistry and the paper trade. He explained the economic problem: since the Empire’s fall, calico had grown common and linen rags—used for paper—were scarce and dear. Paper makers mixed linen and cotton rags; cotton paper is soft, easily creased, and dissolves in water, whereas linen makes durable paper. If cheap, durable paper could be made from a common vegetable fiber—like Chinese paper from bamboo—the cost and weight of books could be drastically reduced, advancing literature and printing. David had studied earlier attempts and recent discoveries. He described how the Chinese used bamboo, how European experiments had been gradual, and how the shift from rag-based to machine-made paper was slow. The press industry longed for a substitute for linen rags since cotton predominated in England. If they could replace linen with local vegetable fiber processed by machinery, books would be cheaper and lighter—Voltaire’s set would weigh far less—allowing a wider diffusion of knowledge.

David sketched the paper process: rag-pickers gather rags; merchants sort and stock them; mills wash and reduce rags to pulp; the pulp is strained through a mould that sets sheet size, then pressed and dried to make paper. He recounted work with the Didots and discussions about Chinese paper, and how research pointed to bamboo as the material. The insight that the Chinese used a native vegetable fiber led David to consider French reeds and other local plants. He acknowledged the difficulty of mechanically imitating Chinese handwork—Chinese labor was cheap and their handwork gave surface quality—but argued an industrial replacement could revolutionize European paper-making.

As David spoke, Lucien arrived, distraught. He related his humiliation: the company’s sneers, the Bishop’s epigram, the ridicule of his origins, and his fear that Mme. de Bargeton would be both his consolation and his ruin. He believed she loved him as he loved her, that she comforted him and would never give him up. David and Eve, absorbed in their joyous news—their engagement and David’s plans to support Lucien—did not at first notice Lucien’s shock that Mme. de Bargeton’s attachment might make his sister’s marriage to David impossible in his imagined future. Lucien’s ambition had pictured Mme. de Bargeton as his path to elevation; the prospect of her becoming Mme. de Rubempré jarred him. But David’s warm description of the planned household—first floor for his wife, second for Lucien, rooms above the shed for Mme. Chardon—reassured Lucien. The brother’s taste for the burgher life returned; the glow of the aristocratic world faded. They walked back together, reconciled, Lucien joining in the lovers’ joy.

They returned late; Postel, uneasy, saw light in Eve’s room and asked Lucien if anything was wrong. Lucien told him that his mother had consented to Eve’s engagement, and Postel slammed his window in despair that he had not earlier asked for Mlle. Chardon.

David did not return to Angoulême to sleep; he walked through the night to his father’s house at Marsac. At sunrise he found his father among the vines. After a brief domestic exchange, David declared his purpose: he was to marry Eve Chardon, and sought his father’s consent and permission to build the second floor and rooms above the shed to house their projected household.

The father, a practical vinegrower, was at once interested in prospects of money but skeptical. He complained about taxes, boasted of making more puncheons per acre, and scoffed at book-learning. He consented to the marriage but had no money to give. He assumed Eve must be rich and hoped for funds to pay his rent, but when David revealed Eve had as much as his mother—i.e., nothing—his father suggested other, wealthier matches and railed at David’s education, which had led him away from practical advantage. He offered to find a miller’s widow with land or arrange other profitable matches, but refused to expend anything on building. Though David urged his father to build, the old man refused, saying he had no cash and that such expenditure would be wasted. He would, however, consent to the marriage and not press the rent, leaving David to fund any improvements himself.

David left Marsac disappointed; he realized he could not count on his father for help and that his plans must be achieved by his own means.

In Angoulême that day everyone spoke only of the Bishop’s epigram and Mme. de Bargeton’s reply. By evening the story swelled; the poet became the town’s hero. A few of the bourgeoisie caught the rumor; young men looked after Lucien in Beaulieu and whispered flattering things that fed his vanity.

“Lucky young fellow!” said Petit-Claud, an attorney’s clerk who had been at school with him.

“Yes,” answered one who had been at the reading. “He’s good-looking, has some talent, and Mme. de Bargeton is wild about him.”

Lucien hurried to find Louise. He had news of his sister’s marriage and sought the mistress of his destiny. After the previous soirée, Louise was more tender than usual; she welcomed him with vehement feeling, seeming to reward his ordeal with ardent kisses. She used the familiar tu, wiped his brow, praised his reading, and pledged to make an oasis for his poet’s life — sometimes busy, sometimes languid — but reminded him he owed his laurels to her and must let that thought be her guerdon for the sufferings she would endure. Tears flowed; Lucien kissed her hand, and every friend and relative indulged his vanity. Flattery and the fury of enemies together lodged him more securely in an unreal world; youth readily accepts such flattering estimates.

“Do you mean to be my Beatrice?” he asked.

“If you prove worthy—some day!” she said with an angelic smile. “Are you not happy? To be the sole possessor of a heart, and certain of being understood, is not that happiness?”

Lucien, trembling, told her that David and his sister Eve loved each other and were to be married. Louise laughed away his fear of blame and, toying with his hair, declared his family mattered little if he were her exception. “Be great, find fame—that is our affair!” she urged, consoling him and making him supremely happy.

M. de Bargeton arrived; Lucien seemed taken aback, but Louise signaled him to stay for dinner and read André de Chénier aloud until the card players came. Cajoled by host and hostess and waited on like a favored guest, Lucien stayed at the Hôtel de Bargeton and began to enjoy the luxuries he took as the rightful accessories of Lucien de Rubempré. He felt secure in Louise’s love and her husband’s weakness, assumed a lordly air in the full rooms, and tasted the despotic sway acquired by a woman of fashion. Some marked these airs and concluded, with the old saying, that he had reached the last term with the lady. Amélie, who had come with M. du Châtelet, was sure of the deplorable fact; the jealous and envious whispered in the drawing-room.

Châtelet defended Mme. de Bargeton, arguing Lucien mistook courtesy for passion, while Amélie, perfidious, suggested that Naïs might be pleased—youth’s company makes a woman young. Adrien hummed that love knows no degree. By the next day every household debated the intimacy between M. Chardon (alias de Rubempré) and Mme. de Bargeton; though their guilt amounted only to a few kisses, the world assumed the worst. Society judged unevenly: what some did with impunity others were punished for. A glance between Mme. de Bargeton and Lucien outweighed a dozen years of other connections; a simple squeeze of the hand brought Angoulême down upon the pair.

David had saved a little from Paris for his marriage and for adding a second story to his father’s house. He built a timbered upper floor for Lucien, making rooms for Eve bold enough for her future life. The friends indulged a time of bright, unmixed happiness. Lucien accepted provincial frugality without complaint, intoxicated by radiant hope: he saw a star, dreamed of greatness, and founded his future on M. de Bargeton’s indulgence. By September Lucien was no longer a printer’s foreman; he was M. de Rubempré, housed sumptuously compared to his tumbledown attic, dined often with Mme. de Bargeton, had befriended the Bishop, and went to the palace. He comforted himself about earning only thirty francs a month by thinking his forthcoming historical romance and volume of verse would repay his family. Great in his own eyes and hearing echoes of his future name, he accepted present sacrifice with noble assurance.

Eve and David postponed their wedding to furnish a home for Lucien; they painted rooms and bought furniture, putting Lucien’s affairs first. No one who knew Lucien doubted their devotion. He was engaging and persuasive; his impatience and desires were gracious, so he always won causes before he spoke. That gift of fortune often ruins as many as it saves. Lucien basked in favor instead of using it profitably. Yet David, sober and observant, worried that triumph might change Lucien. He once tested him, seeing Lucien prefer the great world; later, Lucien consistently chose home pleasures, and David reassured himself that the friends would not be spoiled. They still arranged simple picnics and inexpensive outings; when they dined at a restaurant they divided small bills between David and the Chardons. David credited Lucien for forsaking Mme. de Bargeton and grand dinners for country days, and the party made much of Angoulême’s great man.

The new home neared completion and David went to Marsac to persuade his father to attend the wedding, hoping he might contribute. Then an event occurred that changed everything in the town. Lucien and Louise had a spy in Châtelet, who watched them with hatred and greed for scandal. Sixte (Châtelet) resolved to compromise Mme. de Bargeton; he posed as a humble confidant, admired Lucien in public, and disparaged him elsewhere. Naïs had gradually yielded small special favors to him because she no longer mistrusted her admirer; but Châtelet had presumed too much—love remained platonic, to the despair of both Louise and Lucien.

Love that starts with tactics often founders; in the provinces, lovers skirmish intellectually, and society’s surveillance turns innocent intercourse suspicious. Provincial life keeps desire unsatisfied: houses are transparent, servants comings-and-goings become evidence, and every small departure from custom is read as confession. Mme. de Bargeton, who had no country retreat and could not be private, confronted these constraints daily. If she refused visitors when Lucien was present, it would have been an admission; if she had shut herself up with him, it would have been more dangerous. Visitors flocked in from curiosity, servants went in and out, and Angoulême hung in doubt.

Châtelet did not credit their innocence. He lay in wait, followed Lucien into the house, and shortly after, accompanied by M. de Chandour—the most indiscreet in the clique—hoped to find something compromising. To play his part he feigned neutrality and attached himself to Amélie to allay suspicion in the lovers. He provoked a quarrel with Chandour about the nature of Madame’s conduct to ensure their positions were visible and to enlist partisans through the argument. These polemics kept Mme. de Bargeton and Lucien under watch.

At last Châtelet remarked that whenever he and Chandour came and found Lucien present there was nothing suspicious: doors stood open, servants moved freely, no mystery marked the scene. Stanislas vowed he would cross the room on tiptoe the next day; perfidious Amélie held him to his bargain.

For Lucien the next day was to be decisive. He had matured from bashful reader to urgent lover; six months had brought him to equality with Louise, and he intended to be extravagant in protest and devotion. He planned torrid eloquence and open petition. Madame, too, read purpose in his face and resolved to baffle him, partly from pride: she saw herself as a sovereign Beatrice who must be won by prowess. She valued love as a stimulus to artistic labor and meant to reward prolonged service; like many provincials with lofty romantic notions, she prized testing and delayed recompense.

Lucien began with vehement petulance, to which Louise answered in a lofty oration. “Was that your promise to me, Lucien?” she demanded. “Do not sow regrets in the present, so sweet as it is, to poison my after life. Is not my whole heart yours? What more must you have? If I am not something more than a woman for you, I am less than a woman.”

“That is just what you might say if you cared nothing at all for a man,” cried Lucien; passion and pride tangled. “If you cannot feel the sincere love underlying my words, you will never be worthy of me.”

“You are throwing doubts on my love to avoid responding to it,” she answered, and he flung himself weeping at her feet. “You have never loved me!” he sobbed. “You do not believe what you say,” she replied, flattered. “Then give me proof that you are mine,” he begged.

Just then Stanislas arrived unheard and beheld Lucien on the floor with his head on Louise’s knee—an attitude easily misread. He turned on Châtelet, who stood at the salon door. Mme. de Bargeton sprang up, but the spies retreated, and she was not quick enough to prevent observation.

“If they saw you just now, I am lost,” she told Lucien.

“So much the better!” exclaimed the poet, and she smiled at his selfish cry.

In the provinces, such a story multiplies as it’s told. M. de Chandour first boasted at the club, then from house to house; Châtelet, pretending to have seen nothing, stirred Stanislas to add detail; Stanislas exaggerated for wit. Exaggerated versions circulated among the Angoulême nobility and the ladies gathered at Amélie’s. The women who clutched their hands the most loudly were Mesdames Amélie, Zéphirine, Fifine, and Lolotte, each offering heavy indictments.

“Well, well,” said one, “poor Naïs! I do not believe it; she is blameless. Still, if true, I pity her.”

“She makes herself ridiculous,” another said. “She’s old enough to be his mother.”

“That M. de Rubempré’s position proves innocence,” Châtelet suggested, “A man does not kneel to ask for what he already has.”

“Tell us how it was,” they begged Stanislas, who dramatized the scene with pantomime, making matters worse. Gossip thrived: “At midday? Naïs the last person I should suspect! What will she do now?” Châtelet’s clumsy defense only stirred talk.

Lili, distraught at the fall of Angoulême’s fairest angel, blurted the news to the palace. Châtelet, assured the town was agog, went to Mme. de Bargeton where one whist game was in progress and asked discreetly for a talk in the boudoir. He began, underhandedly offering himself as her champion and advising her to stop Stanislas’s mouth at once. He urged that she call on him if questioned and hinted M. de Bargeton might demand Stanley’s explanation; if necessary, he declared, he would serve her honor. Naïs listened, weary of provincial life; part of her mind drifted to Paris. Châtelet, unable to read her high heart, left, his adoration unchanged.

Later, when Lucien and the elderly whist players had gone, Mme. de Bargeton called her husband into the boudoir.

“Perhaps I was wrong to show warm interest in M. de Rubempré,” she said with solemnity. “This morning Lucien threw himself at my feet with a declaration; Stanislas came in just as I told the boy to rise. Stanislas now says he found us in an equivocal position. A woman has claims which courtesy prescribes to a gentleman, but Stanislas has been saying he entered unexpectedly and found us so. If you think he should be answered, you must demand a full, public retraction in the presence of creditable witnesses. Go at once to Stanislas and ask him to give you satisfaction for his insulting language; do not accept any short explanation. I will send Gentil to Escarbas; my father will be your second. Choose pistols; you are an admirable shot.”

“I am going,” said M. de Bargeton, taking his hat and cane.

She kissed his forehead, feeling something like maternal affection for this great child who would give his life for her. When the carriage closed behind him she even had tears, thinking, “How he loves me! He clings to life, poor dear man, and yet would give it for me.”

On the way to M. de Chandour’s, M. de Bargeton fretted over what to say; his limited intellect made him overprepare remarks he feared might be foolish. Yet in great crises such men sometimes speak with unexpected propriety. So M. de Bargeton comported himself with uncommon sense and spirit. He reached Stanislas’ house at nine, bowed to Amélie amid the assembled, greeted others with a simple smile that seemed ironical under the circumstances. A great silence followed, like the pause before a storm. Châtelet had returned and looked meaningfully among the group; Stanislas, who had been accused, was accosted politely.

Châtelet knew a visit at that hour meant only one thing: old M. de Bargeton was in bed. It was evidently Naïs who had set the feeble arm in motion. Châtelet, intimate in the house, took M. de Bargeton aside.

“Do you wish to speak to Stanislas?”

“Yes,” replied the old man, pleased to find a go‑between.

“Very well; go into Amélie’s bedroom,” said the controller of excise, pleased at the prospect of a duel that might make Mme. de Bargeton a widow and put a barrier between her and Lucien. Châtelet then went to M. de Chandour.

“Stanislas,” he said, “here comes Bargeton to call you to account, no doubt, for the things you have been saying about Naïs. Go into your wife’s room, and behave, both of you, like gentlemen. Keep it quiet and make a great show of politeness—behave with phlegmatic British dignity.”

In another minute Stanislas and Châtelet went to Bargeton.

“Sir,” said the injured husband, “do you say that you discovered Mme. de Bargeton and M. de Rubempré in an equivocal position?”

“M. Chardon,” corrected Stanislas with ironic emphasis; he did not take Bargeton seriously.

“So be it,” answered the other. “If you do not withdraw your assertions at once before the company now in your house, I must ask you to look for a second. My father‑in‑law, M. de Nègrepelisse, will wait upon you at four o’clock tomorrow morning. Both of us may as well make our final arrangements, for the only way out is the one I have indicated. I choose pistols, as the insulted party.”

The speech, long rehearsed, came out without excitement. Stanislas turned pale. “After all, what did I see?” he wondered.

Put between the shame of recanting and the mute terror this man inspired, Stanislas chose the more remote peril.

“All right. Tomorrow morning,” he said, thinking the matter might be arranged somehow.

They rejoined the room. Everybody scanned their faces; Châtelet smiled, M. de Bargeton looked in his element, but Stanislas looked ghastly. Some women guessed the conference’s nature, and the whisper, “They are going to fight!” circulated. Half the room thought Stanislas in the wrong—his white face and demeanor convicted him of lying; the other half admired Bargeton. Châtelet was solemn and mysterious. Bargeton stayed a few minutes, scrutinized faces, and retired.

“Have you pistols?” Châtelet asked Stanislas in a whisper; Stanislas shook from head to foot.

Amélie knew what it meant. She felt ill, and the women took her into her bedroom. There was a sensation; everybody talked at once. The men stopped in the drawing‑room, declaring with one voice that M. de Bargeton was within his right.

“Would you have thought the old fogy capable of acting like this?” asked M. de Saintot.

“But he was a crack shot when young,” said the pitiless Jacques. “My father often told me of Bargeton’s exploits.”

“Pooh! Put them at twenty paces, and they will miss each other if you give them cavalry pistols,” said Francis, addressing Châtelet.

Châtelet stayed after the rest had gone to reassure Stanislas and his wife, and to explain that all would go off well. In a duel between a man of sixty and a man of thirty‑five, all advantage lay with the latter.

Early next morning, as Lucien sat at breakfast with David, who had come back alone from Marsac, in came Mme. Chardon with a scared face.

“Well, Lucien,” she said, “have you heard the news? Everyone is talking of it, even the market. M. de Bargeton all but killed M. de Chandour this morning in M. Tulloy’s meadow; people are making puns on the name. It seems M. de Chandour said he found you with Mme. de Bargeton yesterday.”

“It is a lie! Mme. de Bargeton is innocent,” cried Lucien.

“I heard about the duel from a countryman who saw it from his cart. M. de Nègrepelisse came at three in the morning to be Bargeton’s second; he told Chandour that if anything happened to his son‑in‑law he would avenge him. A cavalry officer lent the pistols. Nègrepelisse tried them repeatedly. Du Châtelet tried to prevent practice, but they referred to the officer; he said that, unless they meant to behave like children, they ought to have working pistols. The seconds put them at twenty‑five paces. Bargeton looked as if out for a walk. He fired first; the ball lodged in Chandour’s neck, and he dropped before he could return the shot. The house‑surgeon says Chandour will have a wry neck for the rest of his days. I came to tell you how it ended, lest you go to Mme. de Bargeton’s or show yourself in Angoulême, for some of Chandour’s friends might call you out.”

As she spoke, the apprentice brought in Gentil, Bargeton’s footman. He had a note for Lucien from Louise.

“You have doubtless heard the news,” she wrote, “of the duel between Chandour and my husband. We shall not be at home today. Be careful; do not show yourself. I ask this in the name of the affection you bear me. Would it not be best to spend this melancholy day listening to your Beatrice, whose whole life has been changed by this event, who has a thousand things to say to you?”

“Luckily, my marriage is fixed for the day after tomorrow,” said David, “and you will have an excuse for not going to Mme. de Bargeton’s so often.”

“Dear David,” returned Lucien, “she asks me to go today; I ought to do as she wishes; she knows better than we do how I should act now.”

“Then is everything ready here?” asked Mme. Chardon.

“Come and see,” cried David, delighted to exhibit the transformation of the first floor. Everything was new and fresh, pervaded by the sweet influences of early married days, still crowned by the wreath of orange blossoms and the bridal veil; days when love’s springtide finds expression in material things, and everything is white and spotless.

“Eve’s home will be fit for a princess,” said the mother, “but you have spent too much, you have been reckless.”

David smiled, but Mme. Chardon had touched a sore spot. The cost far exceeded his estimates; he could not afford to build above the shed. His mother‑in‑law must wait awhile for the home he meant. Nothing is more painful to a generous nature than failing to keep such promises; it mortified the little vanities of affection. David hid his embarrassment to spare Lucien; he feared Lucien might be overwhelmed by the sacrifices made for his sake.

“Eve and her girl friends have been working hard,” said Mme. Chardon. “The wedding clothes and house linen are ready. The girls, without letting her know, covered the mattresses with white twill and rose‑colored piping. So pretty! It makes one wish one were going to be married.”

Mother and daughter had spent all their savings to furnish David’s home with things a young bachelor never thinks of. They knew he was furnishing splendidly; something had been said about ordering a Limoges dinner‑service, and the two women strove to make Eve’s contributions worthy of David. This emulation in love and generosity would bring husband and wife into difficulties at the outset of married life, yet everything looked homely and comfortable, positive luxury in a town so behind the times as Angoulême then was.

As soon as Lucien saw his mother and David enter the bedroom with blue‑and‑white draperies and neat furniture, he slipped away to Mme. de Bargeton. He found Naïs at table with her husband; Bargeton’s early morning walk had sharpened his appetite, and he breakfasted quite unconcernedly after all that had passed. Lucien saw the dignified face of M. de Nègrepelisse, the old provincial noble, a relic of the old French noblesse, sitting beside Naïs.

When Gentil announced M. de Rubempré, the white‑headed old man gave him a keen, curious glance; the father was anxious to form his opinion of the man his daughter singled out. Lucien’s beauty impressed him; he could not repress an approving glance, but seemed to regard the affair as a flirtation, a mere passing fancy. Breakfast over, Louise left her father and Bargeton together; she beckoned Lucien to follow.

“Dear,” she said, half glad, half melancholy, “I am going to Paris, and my father is taking Bargeton back with him to the Escarbas, where he will stay during my absence. Mme. d’Espard (a Blamont‑Chauvry) has great influence and influential relations. The d’Espards are connections of ours; if she acknowledges the relationship, I shall cultivate her; she may procure a place for Bargeton. At my solicitation, it might be desired at Court that he represent the Charente, a step toward his election here. If he were a deputy, it would further other steps I wish to take in Paris. You, my darling, have brought about this change in my life. After this morning’s duel, I must shut up my house for some time; people will side with the Chandours against us. In our position, in a small town, absence is the only way to soften bad feeling. But I shall either succeed and never see Angoulême again, or I shall not succeed and then wait in Paris until I can spend summers at the Escarbas and winters in Paris. It is the only life for a woman of quality; I have waited too long. One day will suffice for our preparations; tomorrow night I set out. You are coming with me, are you not? You shall start first. I will overtake you between Mansle and Ruffec, and we shall soon be in Paris. There is the life for a man with anything in him. We are only at our ease among equals; we are uncomfortable in other society. Paris, besides, is the capital of the intellectual world, the stage on which you will succeed; overleap the gulf that separates us quickly. You must not let your ideas grow rancid in the provinces; put yourself into communication at once with the great men of the nineteenth century. Try to stand well with the Court and those in power. No honor, no distinction, comes to talent that perishes for lack of light in a little town; tell me any great work executed in the provinces! See how Jean‑Jacques, sublime in his poverty, felt the irresistible attraction of that sun of the intellectual world which produces ever‑new glories and stimulates intellect—Paris, where men rub against one another. What is it but your duty to take your place in the succession of pléiades that rise generation after generation? You have no idea how much being brought into a high social light helps a clever young man. I will introduce you to Mme. d’Espard; it’s not easy to get into her set; but you meet all the greatest people at her house—cabinet ministers and ambassadors, great orators, peers and men of influence, wealthy or famous people. A young man with looks and more than sufficient genius could fail to excite interest only by very bad management.

“There is no pettiness about those who are truly great; they will support you; and when you have a high position, your work will rise in public opinion. The great problem for the artist is putting himself in evidence. There will be hundreds of chances of making your way, sinecures, a pension from the civil list. The Bourbons are fond of encouraging letters and the arts; you must be a religious poet and a Royalist poet at the same time. Not only is it the right course, but it is the way to get on. Do the Liberals give places and rewards and make the fortunes of men of letters? Take the right road and reach the goal of genius. You have my secret; do not breathe it, and prepare to follow me. Would you rather not go?” she added, surprised that he made no answer.

To Lucien, listening to these alluring words and bewildered by the rapid bird’s‑eye view of Paris, it seemed as if he had been using only half his brain and suddenly found the other half; his ideas widened. He saw himself stagnating in Angoulême like a frog under a stone. Paris and her splendors rose before him: the Eldorado of provincial imaginings, with golden robes and the royal diadem, arms outstretched to talent. Great men would greet him there. Everything smiled upon genius. There were no jealous booby‑squires to invent gibes and humiliate a man of letters; no stupid indifference to poetry. Paris was the fountain‑head of poetry; there the poet was brought into the light and paid. Publishers should no sooner read the opening pages of An Archer of Charles IX than open their cash‑boxes with “How much do you want?” And besides all this, he understood that this journey with Mme. de Bargeton would virtually give her to him; that they should live together.

At “Would you rather not go?” tears came to his eyes; he flung his arms about Louise, held her tightly, and kissed her passionately. Suddenly he checked himself, as if memory had struck.

“Great heavens!” he cried, “my sister is to be married the day after tomorrow!”

That exclamation was the last expiring cry of noble and single‑hearted boyhood. The strong ties that bind young hearts to home and early friendship were to be severed.

“Well,” cried the haughty Nègrepelisse, “and what has your sister’s marriage to do with the progress of our love? Have you set your mind so much on being best man at a wedding of tradespeople and workingmen that you cannot give up these exalted joys for my sake? A great sacrifice, indeed!” she went on, scornfully. “This morning I sent my husband out to fight in your quarrel. There, sir, go; I am mistaken in you.”

She sank fainting upon the sofa. Lucien went to her, entreating pardon, cursing his family, his sister, and David.

“I had such faith in you!” she said. “M. de Cante‑Croix had an adored mother; but to win a letter from me, and the words, ‘I am satisfied,’ he fell in the thick of the fight. And now, when I ask you to take a journey with me, you cannot think of giving up a wedding dinner for my sake.”

Lucien was ready to kill himself; his desperation was so unfeigned that Louise forgave him, though she made him feel he must redeem his mistake.

“Come, come,” she said, “be discreet, and to‑morrow at midnight be upon the road, a hundred paces out of Mansle.”

Lucien felt the globe shrink under his feet; he went back to David’s house, hopes pursuing him like Furies, for he had glimmerings of endless difficulties, summed up in the appalling words, “Where is the money to come from?”

He stood in such terror of David’s perspicacity that he locked himself into his pretty new study until he could recover; his head swam in this new position. So he must leave the rooms just furnished at such cost, and all the sacrifices had been made in vain. Then it occurred to him his mother might take the rooms and save David the heavy expense of building at the yard’s end; his departure would be a convenience. He found many urgent reasons for sudden flight; there is no such Jesuit as the desire of your heart. He hurried to tell his sister in L’Houmeau and take counsel. As he reached Postel’s shop, he bethought himself that if all else failed he could borrow enough to live on for a year from his father’s successor.

“Three francs per day will be abundance if I live with Louise,” he thought; “it is only a thousand francs for a year. And in six months’ time I shall have plenty of money.”

Under seal and promise of secrecy, Eve and her mother heard Lucien’s confidences. Both women began to cry as they heard his ambitious plans; when he asked why they were troubled, they said every penny had been spent on table‑linen, house‑linen, Eve’s wedding clothes, and many things David overlooked. They had been glad to do it, for David had made a marriage‑settlement of ten thousand francs on Eve. Lucien then spoke of a loan; Mme. Chardon undertook to ask M. Postel to lend a thousand francs for a year.

“But, Lucien,” said Eve, a thought clutching her heart, “you will not be here at my wedding! Oh! come back; I will put it off a few days. Surely she will give you leave to come back in a fortnight if you go now? Surely she would spare you a week when we brought you up for her? We shall have no luck if you are not at the wedding.... But will a thousand francs be enough?” she asked, interrupting. “Your coat suits you divinely, but you have only that one! Only two fine shirts; the other six are coarse; three of your white ties are common muslin, only two lawn cravats, your handkerchiefs not good. Where will you find a sister in Paris to get up your linen in one day? You will want much more. You have just one pair of new nankeen trousers; last year’s are tight; you will be obliged to have clothes made in Paris, and Paris prices are not Angoulême’s. You have only two presentable white waistcoats; I have mended the others already. Come, I advise you to take two thousand francs.”

David came in as she spoke and apparently heard the last two words; he looked at the brother and sister and said nothing.

“Do not keep anything from me,” he said at last.

“Well,” exclaimed Eve, “he is going away with her.”

Mme. Chardon came in again, and, not seeing David, began at once:

“Postel is willing to lend you the thousand francs, Lucien,” she said, “but only for six months; and he wants a bill endorsed by your brother‑in‑law, for he says you give him no security.”

She turned and saw David; there was a deep silence. The Chardons felt ashamed for having abused David’s goodness. Tears stood in the young printer’s eyes.

“Then you will not be here at our wedding,” he began. “You are not going to live with us! And here have I been squandering all that I had! Oh, Lucien, as I came along, bringing Eve her little bits of wedding jewelry, I did not think I should be sorry I spent the money on them.” He brushed his hand over his eyes as he drew the tiny morocco‑covered boxes from his pocket and set them on the table.

“Oh! why do you think so much for me?” protested Eve, giving him a divinely sweet smile that belied her words.

“Mamma, dear,” said David, “just tell M. Postel I will put my name to the bill, for I can tell from your face, Lucien, that you have quite made up your mind.”

Lucien’s head sank dejectedly; there was a pause, then he said, “Do not think hardly of me, my dear, good angels.”

He put his arms about Eve and David, drew them close, and held them tightly as he added, “Wait and see what comes of it, and you shall know how much I love you. What is the good of our high thinking, David, if it does not enable us to disregard petty ceremonial in which the law entangles our affections? Shall I not be with you in spirit despite distance? Shall we not be united in thought? Have I not a destiny to fulfil? Will publishers come here to seek my Archer of Charles IX and the Marguerites? A little sooner or later I shall be obliged to do as I am doing to‑day, should I not? And shall I ever find a better opportunity? Does not my success depend upon my entrance into life in Paris through the Marquise d’Espard’s salon?”

“He is right,” said Eve; “you yourself said he ought to go to Paris at once.”

David took Eve’s hand and led her into the narrow little room where she had slept for seven years.

“Love, you were saying just now that he would want two thousand francs?” he murmured in her ear. “Postel is only lending one thousand.”

Eve looked anguish at him; he read her meaning.

“Listen, my adored Eve, we are making a bad start. Yes, my expenses have taken all my capital; I have just two thousand francs left, and half will be needed to carry on the business. If we give your brother the thousand francs, it will mean giving away our bread; we shall live in anxiety. If I were alone, I know what I should do; but we are two. Decide for us.”

Eve, distraught, sprang to his arms and kissed him tenderly through tears: “Do as you would if you were alone; I will work to earn the money.”

Despite the most impassioned kiss ever given and taken by betrothed lovers, David left Eve overcome, and went out to Lucien.

“Do not worry,” he said; “you shall have your two thousand francs.”

“Go see Postel,” said Mme. Chardon, “for you must both sign the bill.”

When Lucien and David returned unexpectedly, they found Eve and her mother on their knees in prayer. The women felt sure Lucien’s return would bring realization of many hopes; at the moment they only felt how much they lost in his parting, and the coming days would be filled with fears for Lucien.

“If you could ever forget this sight,” David said in Lucien’s ear, “you would be the basest of men.”

David, no doubt, thought these brave words needed; Mme. de Bargeton’s influence seemed less to be feared than Lucien’s unlucky instability—he was so easily led. Eve soon packed Lucien’s clothes; the Fernando Cortez of literature carried but little baggage. He wore his best overcoat, his best waistcoat, and one of the two fine shirts. The whole of his linen, the celebrated coat, and his manuscript made a small package. To hide it from Mme. de Bargeton, David proposed to send it by coach to a paper merchant he dealt with and wrote asking him to keep the parcel until Lucien sent for it.

Despite Mme. de Bargeton’s precautions, Châtelet found she was leaving Angoulême; to discover whether she traveled alone or with Lucien, he sent his man to Ruffec to watch every carriage changing horses there.

“If she is taking her poet with her,” he thought, “I have her now.”

Lucien set out before daybreak the next morning. David went with him. David had hired a cabriolet, pretending he was going to Marsac on business, a deception that seemed probable. The two friends went to Marsac and spent part of the day with the old “bear.” As evening came they set out again, and at dawn waited on the road beyond Mansle for Mme. de Bargeton. When the seventy‑year‑old traveling carriage he had often seen in the coach‑house appeared, Lucien felt more deeply moved than ever; he sprang into David’s arms.

“God grant that this may be for your good!” said David; he climbed into the shabby cabriolet and drove away with dread clutching his heart; he had terrible presentiments of Lucien’s fate in Paris.
