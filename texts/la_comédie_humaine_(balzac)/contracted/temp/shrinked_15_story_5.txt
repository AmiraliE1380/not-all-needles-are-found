Many tales, whether dramatic or simple, can be told without losing charm; but some human experiences demand a delicate touch. Certain details—almost anatomical—require nuanced thought. Portraits must carry a soul, and some things are expressed only through secret harmonies shaped by time, circumstance, or an inner moral inclination.

Such revelations are necessary to tell this simple story, intended for the grave and reflective who find sustenance in tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, feels reverence for the subject, should not the reader share that feeling? Many know the vague sadness around us, a gentle suffering that can be oddly pleasing. If the reader has mourned a dear one, is alone, or the day is waning, they may continue; otherwise they should set this book aside. Those who have never buried a beloved will not grasp these pages, which may seem fragrant to some and colorless to others. The reader must have known the luxury of tears and the silent pangs of memory, evoking both regret for loss and smiles for past joys.

The writer assures that this is a true story, worthy of the reader’s feeling, if they possess it.

In contemporary France, language reflects as many idioms and idiosyncrasies as there are individuals; it is amusing to hear differing interpretations of the same person from various Parisians. Madame Firmiani, for instance, is pictured in many partial but revealing ways:

- The Practical man measures her by means: a fine house in the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of one hundred thousand francs, and a husband once receiver-general—solid people, he concludes, with nothing to criticize.

- The Lounger knows her socially, attends her parties, and values her house as an exclusive, respectable salon noted for excellent tea.

- The Egotist dismisses such gatherings as dull, believing a mind should avoid trivial salons.

- The Student calls her enchanting and wishes to introduce you.

- The Fop, noting faded beauty, still grants her lingering charm and remnants of allure.

- The Connoisseur focuses on a fine collection of paintings.

- The Spiteful woman warns other women away, labeling her a dangerous siren who provokes jealousy.

- The Attache recalls her beauty from a decade past.

- The Distinguishing observer notes connections to the elite despite modest household circumstances.

- The Duke professes not to know her, recognizing only those presented at court.

- The Ninny mistakenly claims she used to sing at the Opera, while two old ladies discuss her lineage, revealing ties to the noble Cadignan family.

- The Original assures you her salon is safe—any scoundrels there are of high quality.

- The Observer depicts her as a beautiful woman who rarely rises to greet guests, a mystery married to a mythical husband; he notes her charm and conversation, hints at many admirers but insists none are favored.

- The Contradictor spreads rumor she was once Murat’s mistress.

- The Envious reduces her wealth to coquetry that ruined a young man’s prospects, an example of persistent spite.

Each portrait is partial: the Practical man gauges property and rank; the Lounger hospitality; the Egotist his disdain; the Student romance; the Fop faded attractiveness; the Connoisseur art; the Spiteful rivalry; the Attache memory; the Distinguishing social ties; the Duke courtly recognition; the Ninny ignorance; the old ladies lineage; the Original quality of companions; the Observer enigmatic grace; the Contradictor and Envious rumor and resentment. Each view reveals as much about the observer’s habits and biases as it does about Madame Firmiani.

In sum, Madame Firmiani appears complex, refracted through many lenses. The narrative invites readers to consider these interpretations while reflecting on the deeper emotional truths—reverence, loss, memory, and the quiet harmonies—that connect them all.

In January 1824, opinions about Madame Firmiani were so varied that no clear portrait emerged: widow or wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain. This multiplicity of views showed how society distorts reputation, like lithographic blocks producing different images from small variations.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly Touraine landowner, became intrigued. Practical and observant from managing an estate and peasants, he came to Paris to learn more, but gossip only deepened his confusion. A man of honor, he prized his nephew Octave de Camps and was annoyed when Octave sold an estate without consulting him. Worse, a relative reported that Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and was reduced to teaching mathematics—a claim that alarmed Monsieur de Bourbonne.

Refusing to assume the worst about Octave, he resolved to investigate. Using connections in the faubourg Saint-Germain and adopting the name de Rouxellay, he sought the truth of Madame Firmiani’s relation to his nephew. Choosing a moment when Octave would be occupied, he soon confirmed that Octave’s financial troubles were real.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was no simple provincial uncle. A former member of the King’s guard, he understood society’s manners and politics. Entering Madame Firmiani’s salon with the Marquise de Listomere, he voiced pity for Octave, wondering how a woman of such luxury could be involved with a man in such straits. Yet on seeing Madame Firmiani, his anger softened. In the salon’s atmosphere her beauty and elegance captivated him and prompted him to reassess his initial judgments.

Madame Firmiani, thought to be twenty-five but nearer twenty-eight, had married the elusive Firmiani at sixteen. At her age she combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit—qualities that made her the height of desirability and esteemed in high society. Admired even by rivals, her popularity also invited malicious gossip: envy and suspicion turned success and propriety into fodder for unfounded rumors.

This brief portrait hints at her true nature: a complex blend of qualities that inspire admiration and intrigue. Her beauty, grace, and innate charm made her a figure of fascination capable of stirring deep emotions. In her one could see elements ranging from the noble to the tragic, reflecting the multifaceted nature of womanhood and the wider human experience.

The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had gone, and Madame Firmiani found him seated before her, apparently determined to stay. The clock struck two.

“Madame,” he began as she rose, indicating she should ask him to go, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat down again, visibly affected. Monsieur de Bourbonne, despite his experience, could not tell whether her pallor was shame or pleasure; provincials often misread such things.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked, fixing him with an enigmatic look.

“Well, madame,” he replied, “people in my province say my nephew has ruined himself for you, living in a garret while you enjoy wealth. Forgive my rustic frankness; you should know these rumors.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interrupted, raising her hand. “I know of those rumors. You are too polite to press me if I ask you not to, and too gallant not to admit you have no right to question me. It would be absurd to defend myself. Believe me when I say I scorn money; if I married into wealth it was not for its sake. Octave’s fortune or poverty matters not: I welcome him as a friend, and I cannot accept anyone I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip.”

Her tone rose but ended with composed teasing, like Celimene to the Misanthrope.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Bourbonne said, trembling slightly, “I am almost a father to Octave. I implore your pardon for the question I will ask. I swear as a gentleman your answer will stay here.” He placed his hand on his heart. “Do you love Octave?”

“To another man I would answer with a look,” she said. “To you, I ask what you would think of a woman who answered such a question. To confess love is an effort, a reward for him. To say it to another—” She broke off, rose, bowed, and withdrew into her private rooms, leaving the uncle bewildered.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought. “What a woman! Sly or angelic.” He left in his hired coach.

The next morning, at eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed to Octave’s garret. The young professor was astonished to see him so early, after staying up all night.

“You rascal!” the uncle began, taking a seat. “Since when is it fashionable to mock uncles with twenty-six thousand francs a year? Have I not been good to you? I’m making you an estate in Touraine. And what do you do? Sell your property and live like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I’m not talking about uncles; about nephews. Come, confess: have you been gambling? Lost at the Bourse? Say ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie, I’ll sell my property and go back to my youth.”

“Uncle—”

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” he continued. “She is charming. You have my approval if that helps. But have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! I’d have wagered it. In my day women ruined men cleverly; this one is a piece of the last century.”

“Uncle,” Octave said tenderly, “you’re mistaken. Madame Firmiani deserves esteem and adoration.”

“Youth!” cried the old man. “Go on; tell me the same old story.”

“My dear uncle, here is a letter that explains nearly everything,” Octave said, taking it from the elegant portfolio she had given him. “Read it and I’ll tell you the rest. You’ll know a Madame Firmiani unknown to the world.”

“I don’t have my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began: “’My beloved—’”

“Then you are still intimate with her?” the uncle interrupted.

“Of course.”

“You haven’t parted?”

“We are married.”

“Heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Then why the garret?”

“Let me continue.”

Octave read, faltering with emotion: “‘My beloved Husband, you ask why I am sad. I cannot deceive you; a wife should be gay and caressing. I long to love you forever; a woman’s glory is in the man she loves. But a tale you told tarnished my joy. I feel humiliated by you—whom I thought most honorable—because of your father’s having unjustly deprived others of their property. I must have deep confidence to avow this: how can you knowingly keep such property? I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. If you had committed a crime I would hide you from justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love, for a woman, is boundless confidence plus reverence. I have never conceived of it otherwise. Come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Consult your conscience. An act of justice should not be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife, not your mistress. If I am mistaken, if you can rightly claim the property, reflect and decide from your own conscience. I know I have preached; scold me if you will.’”

“What!” the uncle interjected. “Surely you didn’t tell that woman about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity,” Octave answered, and resumed the letter: “‘I cannot respect the man who knowingly is smirched for a sum of money—five francs stolen or five times a hundred thousand gained by a legal trick are equally dishonoring. I cannot respect such conduct. I ask only for conscience; act solely from yourself. A man who loves a woman sincerely respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself.’”

“Well, uncle?” Octave asked, eyes wet.

“There’s more; finish it,” the old man said.

“Oh, the rest is for lovers’ eyes,” Octave smiled.

“I don’t understand why you teach mathematics,” the uncle said gently after a moment.

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that like dipping into my father’s capital? After reading this letter a revolution happened in me. I paid my entire debt of remorse in one day. I was ashamed at everything—riding someone else’s horse, eating food not honestly mine. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. That day I felt pleasures of the heart far beyond millions. Together we calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself to pay interest. But all I had did not cover the amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer her savings, and I accepted.”

“What! Besides her other virtues, does that adorable woman save money?” his uncle exclaimed.

“Don’t laugh; her position forced her to be careful. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died three years later. Legal proof of his death and his will—in which he left everything to her—were lost or stolen amid the Greek troubles; records there are not like France’s. Unsure of her title, she lived prudently. I want to buy property of my own to provide for her if she loses hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me? I love you enough to pay such debts. I’ll play the traditional uncle and help!”

“Ah! uncle, I want to be rich by my own industry. If you help, let it be an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year until I find a small enterprise needing capital. I teach to live honestly. If you’d seen my happiness in making restitution! The Bourgneufs lived miserably: an old father working as a lottery agent, daughters tending house by lamplight, a sick mother. My visit was like bringing Providence. To lift them from a poor fire to a comfortable life—words fail me. My conscience felt cleansed; my father, if there is a Paradise, is happy there. And Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she inspires a delicacy of feeling I lacked. I call her my dear conscience. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich by my own industry. I have an industrial scheme and, if it succeeds, shall earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, eyes filling with memories.

At that moment, despite the garret’s distance from the street, a carriage halted.

“There she is!” he exclaimed. “I know her horses by the way they pull up.”

Madame Firmiani entered.

“Ah!” she said, surprised to see Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has sent me a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet kept safe for me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Overcome, she laid her head on his breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are the best in humanity; you are hardly guilty of fault, for they come through us.”
