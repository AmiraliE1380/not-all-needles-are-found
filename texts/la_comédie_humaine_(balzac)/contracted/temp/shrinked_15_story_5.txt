In January 1824 opinions about Madame Firmiani varied so widely that no clear picture emerged: she might be a widow or a wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain. This multiplicity showed how society can distort a reputation, like lithographic blocks producing different images from slight variations.

Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly provincial magnate from Touraine, became intrigued that winter. Practical and observant from managing an estate and dealing with peasants, he went to Paris to learn more, but gossip only deepened his confusion. A man of honor, he cherished his nephew Octave de Camps and had high hopes for him; he was annoyed when Octave sold an estate without consulting him. A relative then claimed Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and been reduced to teaching mathematics—a report that alarmed Monsieur de Bourbonne.

Refusing to assume the worst, he went to Paris determined to investigate. Using connections in the faubourg Saint-Germain, he gathered information and resolved to meet Madame Firmiani under the name de Rouxellay to learn the truth of her relation to Octave. Choosing a moment when Octave would be occupied, he soon confirmed his nephew’s financial troubles.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was no typical provincial uncle; a former member of the King’s guard, he knew society’s manners and politics. Entering Madame Firmiani’s salon with the Marquise de Listomere, he expressed pity for Octave, wondering how a woman of such apparent luxury could be involved with someone in such straits. Yet upon seeing Madame Firmiani his anger softened. In the salon’s atmosphere her beauty and elegance captivated him and made him rethink his initial judgments.

Madame Firmiani, thought to be twenty-five but closer to twenty-eight, had married the invisible Firmiani at sixteen. At this age she combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit. Esteemed in high society, she was admired by rivals and peers alike. But her popularity made her a target for malicious gossip: many envied her happiness and propriety, and unfounded rumors persisted, revealing the dangers of societal suspicion.

This brief portrait hints at a complex nature that could inspire admiration and intrigue. Her beauty, grace, and natural charm made her a figure of fascination, capable of stirring deep emotions and embodying elements from the noble to the tragic—reflecting the multifaceted nature of womanhood and the human experience.

The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had left; the clock struck two. “Madame,” he began as she rose, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat down again, visibly affected. The old planter could not tell whether her pallor was shame or pleasure; women hide their joys, and the more delicate they are, the more they conceal them. Monsieur de Bourbonne misread her agitation.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked, giving him a look that left men baffled.

“Well, madame,” he replied, “someone in my province told me my nephew has ruined himself for you, living in a garret while you enjoy wealth. Forgive my rustic frankness; I thought you should know.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interrupted, raising her hand. “I know of all that. You are too polite to continue if I ask you to drop the subject, and too gallant not to admit you have no right to question me. It would be absurd to defend myself. Believe me when I say I despise money in principle, though I married a wealthy man. Your nephew’s wealth or poverty is irrelevant; I welcome him as a friend. All my friends respect one another; I cannot include those I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip or dishonesty.”

Her voice rose slightly, then she finished with the composed teasing of Celimene before retreating to her rooms.

“Ah! the mischief!” Monsieur de Bourbonne thought. “What a woman! She is either sly or an angel.” He left in his hired coach.

The next morning, around eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed to Octave’s rooms. The young professor was astonished. The uncle, who had stayed up all night, sat down and scolded him.

“You rascal! Since when is it fashionable to mock uncles with twenty-six thousand francs a year? What fault do you find with me? Have I not been a good uncle? I’m making you a fine estate in Touraine, though I won’t give it until the last moment. And what do you do? Sell your property and live like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I’m not talking about uncles; I’m talking about nephews. Have you been gambling? Lost money at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll forgive you. But if you lie, I’ll go back to my youthful extravagance. I saw Madame Firmiani yesterday—she is charming. Have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“In my day women were cleverer at ruining men, but this one—she’s from the old school.”

“Uncle,” Octave said, “you are mistaken. Madame Firmiani deserves your esteem.”

“Youth, youth! Always the same!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Go on, tell me the same old story. But remember, I’ve had my share of gallantry.”

“My dear uncle, here is a letter that will explain nearly everything,” Octave said, taking an elegant portfolio. “When you’ve read it, I’ll tell you the rest.”

“I don’t have my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began: “‘My beloved—’”

“Then you are still intimate with her?” his uncle interrupted.

“Of course.”

“You haven’t parted from her?”

“Parted! We are married.”

“Heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Then why do you live in a garret?”

“Let me continue.”

Octave read on, emotion making him falter: “‘My beloved Husband, you ask why I am sad. I cannot deceive you; this may be a misfortune, for a wife should be gay and caressing. I cannot deceive you, even if my happiness depended on it. I long to love you forever; a woman’s glory is the man she loves. Esteem, consideration, honor—must they not belong to him who receives our all? Well, my angel has fallen. The tale you told me has tarnished my past joys. I feel humiliated by you—whom I thought the most honorable of men. I must have deep confidence in your heart to make this avowal: how can you, knowing your father unjustly deprived others of their property, keep it? You told me of this in the room where we loved; you are a gentleman, yet you think yourself noble and remain silent. I try to excuse you for youth and thoughtlessness, but your laughs wounded me. Reflect on that ruined family always in distress; poor young girls who might curse you nightly; an old father saying each night, “We might not be starving if that man’s father had been honest.”’”

“Good heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Surely you didn’t tell that woman about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity. But let me read on: ‘Octave, no power on earth can change the principles of honor. Look into your conscience and ask by what name you call the action of keeping your property.’”

The nephew looked at his uncle, who lowered his head.

“‘I will not tell you all the thoughts that assail me; they reduce to one: I cannot respect the man who knowingly keeps ill-gotten gains, whatever the amount. Five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand won by a legal trick are equally dishonorable. I feel degraded by the very love which has hitherto been my joy. There rises in my soul a voice my tenderness cannot stifle. I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. Were you to commit a crime, I would hide you from justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love for a woman means boundless confidence and reverence for the being to whom she belongs. I have always conceived love as a fire that purifies noble feelings. I have one thing to say: come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Should I see you no more, I shall know what it means. But I do not urge restitution as a sacrifice to love. Consult your conscience. A man who loves sincerely respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself. I blame myself for preaching; scold me if you will—but not much. Between us, the power is yours—you alone should perceive your faults.’”

“Well, uncle?” Octave asked, tears in his eyes.

“There’s more; finish it.”

“Oh, the rest is for a lover’s eyes only,” Octave smiled.

“Yes, right, my boy,” the old man said gently. “I have had many affairs, but I too have loved. But I don’t understand why you teach mathematics.”

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that as good as saying I dipped into my father’s capital? After reading this letter, a revolution occurred within me. I paid my entire debt of remorse in one day. I cannot describe my state. As I drove in the Bois, a voice said, ‘That horse is not yours’; when I ate, it said, ‘You have stolen this food.’ I was ashamed. The fresher my honesty, the deeper it was. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. That day I experienced pleasures of the heart far beyond millions. Together, we calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself to pay three percent interest. But all I had did not cover the full amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer her savings, and I accepted.”

“What! Besides other virtues, does that adorable woman save money?” his uncle exclaimed.

“Don’t laugh at her, uncle; her position forced prudence. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died three years later. It has been impossible to obtain legal proof of his death or the will he made leaving everything to her; those papers were lost or stolen during the troubles in Greece. Uncertain whether she might lose her fortune, she lived carefully. I wished to acquire property of my own to provide for her if she lost hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me? I love you enough to pay your debts. I’ll play the generous uncle now and take revenge!”

“Ah! uncle, I want to get rich by my industry. If you want to help, give me an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year until I find a venture needing capital. Right now, I’m so happy all I desire is the means to live. I give lessons so I do not live at anyone’s expense. If you only knew the joy of making that restitution! I found the Bourgneufs miserable. The old father was a lottery agent; the daughters kept his books and cared for the house; the mother was always ill. The daughters are charming but have learned that beauty without money is undervalued. My visit was a drama: to be Providence itself, to fulfill that futile wish, ‘If heaven sent us twenty thousand francs a year’—to bring wealth to a family sitting by one lamp over a poor fire! Words cannot describe it. My extreme justice seemed unjust to them. If there is a Paradise, my father is happy there now. As for me, I am loved as no man has been. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she inspires a delicacy of feeling I lacked. I call her my dear conscience. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich by myself. I have an industrial scheme; if it succeeds, I’ll earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, eyes filling with memories of his sister.

At that moment a carriage stopped.

“There she is!” he exclaimed. “I know her horses by the way they pull up.”

Moments later Madame Firmiani entered.

“Ah!” she said, annoyed to see Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has sent me a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet kept safe for me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here,” she added, laying her hand on his heart, “to which none but God can add.” Unable to contain her joy, she laid her head on her husband’s breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults, for they come through us.”
