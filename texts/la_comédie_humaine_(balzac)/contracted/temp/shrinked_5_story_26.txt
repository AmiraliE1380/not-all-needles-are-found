Bianchon, trained in surgery under the surgeon Desplein, became a theoretical physiologist. Desplein’s surgical skill left no school; his avowed atheism—hardened by dissecting corpses without perceiving a soul—combined with practical brilliance to produce a skeptical paradox. Critics noted contradictions: eccentric dress, moods of kindness and harshness, and contempt for humanity’s follies. Among his students, Horace Bianchon, who endured poverty but showed integrity and cheerfulness, won Desplein’s mentorship and began Paris practice. When Bianchon mentioned a destitute water‑carrier with a debilitating illness, Desplein hurried to help, showing compassion for Auvergnats. Bianchon later saw him enter a church—surprising for an atheist—and learned he had attended mass for twenty years and founded it. These episodes complicate Desplein’s beliefs, portraying a man of skill, skepticism, compassion, and paradox.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Some time passed before Dr. Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opening to ask Desplein about a pivotal episode in his life. Their meetings rarely allowed private talk, but seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and knelt beside him; they attended mass together.

“Can you explain your sudden piety?” Bianchon asked as they left. “You, who do not believe in God, at mass? You owe me an explanation.”

Desplein parried that many are outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical, then criticized politicians, but Bianchon pressed: why had he founded this mass? Desplein answered simply: he was nearing the end of his life and could trace its beginnings. Standing in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, he pointed to the house where he had lived two desperate years and recalled hunger, poverty, isolation, and an irritable temper that made friendship difficult. He had no money for books or medical training and struggled alone to rise from destitution.

He spoke of meager meals and tireless labor without sympathy or support. Those born to wealth could not imagine his plight and asked as if debt were a choice. Early hardships shaped his view of humanity: he had witnessed selfishness and envy and expected mediocrity to try to pull down those who strove for excellence.

A turning point came when, at the depth of his poverty, he contemplated enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when the porter demanded too much to release it, despair set in. Then his neighbor Bourgeat—a humble water-carrier—appeared and changed his fate.

Bourgeat, though poor himself, paid Desplein’s debts and secured new lodgings. He devoted his savings to Desplein’s future, understood his mission, and supported him selflessly. Desplein described Bourgeat as nurturing, almost maternal, who took pride in his protégé yet sought no recognition. When Desplein became a house surgeon, leaving Bourgeat pained him; Bourgeat was proud nonetheless.

Later, Desplein repaid him by buying him a horse and a barrel, fulfilling a long-held dream. Bourgeat was angry that Desplein had spent his money on him, yet delighted; he never claimed credit for Desplein’s rise, though Desplein felt he owed him everything.

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein tended him devotedly; Bourgeat died in his arms, leaving him all he had. He had strong faith, and Desplein honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death. Desplein explained that the mass he founded was a tribute to Bourgeat’s faith; he hoped, if there is a paradise, that Bourgeat had found peace there. Desplein admitted he would give anything for the faith Bourgeat possessed.

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein’s last days, could not say definitively whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He wondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
