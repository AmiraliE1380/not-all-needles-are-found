Bianchon, a physician known for his theoretical physiology, first trained in surgery under the celebrated French surgeon Desplein. Desplein's genius lay in his diagnostic and operative skill, but it remained personal rather than a legacy shaping future generations. His atheism, reinforced by dissecting bodies without perceiving any soul, formed a central part of his outlook.

Despite his brilliance, Desplein's life was full of contradictions critics used to diminish him. His eccentricities—alternating between lavish and indifferent dress—and a temperament that swung from kindness to harshness produced mixed judgments. He understood human nature deeply but often displayed contempt for humanity, having observed its follies closely.

Among his students, Horace Bianchon distinguished himself by enduring poverty while studying medicine. Bianchon was noted for integrity, loyalty, and a cheerful disposition despite financial hardship. Desplein recognized his potential, mentored him, and introduced him to medical practice and Parisian social dynamics.

Once Bianchon informed Desplein about a destitute water-carrier with a debilitating illness. Moved, Desplein rushed to help, revealing a compassionate side beneath his stern exterior. This sympathy for the less fortunate, especially fellow Auvergnats, recurred in his actions.

Bianchon later saw Desplein enter a church, an odd sight given the surgeon's outspoken atheism. Curious, he followed and observed Desplein attending mass—behavior at odds with his declared disbelief. At a later dinner Desplein dismissed the mass as a farce, yet Bianchon remained puzzled.

Months afterward another physician questioned Desplein about the church visit; Desplein gave a matter-of-fact explanation, but Bianchon stayed skeptical and resolved to investigate. He returned to the same church a year later and again found Desplein at mass, confirming a pattern. The sacristan disclosed that Desplein had attended that mass regularly for twenty years and had even founded it.

This discovery deepened the mystery of Desplein's beliefs and actions, revealing a complexity that resisted simple labels. Bianchon confronted a man who professed atheism yet engaged in religious ritual—an apparent contradiction highlighting the intricate relationship between belief, practice, and human behavior. The episodes suggested Desplein's public declarations and private practices both sprang from a multifaceted character shaped by skill, skepticism, compassion, and paradox.

Some time passed before Doctor Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opportunity to discuss a significant incident in Desplein's life. Their meetings, whether professional or social, rarely allowed private conversation. Finally, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint-Sulpice and knelt beside him without surprise. They attended a mass together.

"Can you explain your sudden piety?" Bianchon asked as they left the church. "You, who do not believe in God, attending mass? You owe me an explanation."

"I am like many devout people—outwardly religious but inwardly skeptical," Desplein replied, then launched into a critique of certain political figures.

"That doesn't answer my question," Bianchon insisted. "Why did you found this mass?"

"Well, my dear boy," Desplein said, "I am nearing the end of my life; I can tell you its beginnings."

They stood in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, a grim Paris street. Desplein pointed to a building where he had lived for two years. "I lived up there," he said, recalling the hardships of his early life. "I endured hunger, poverty, and isolation. I had no money for books or medical training, and my irritable nature made it hard to connect with others. I was alone, struggling to rise from the depths of poverty."

He recounted meager meals and the sacrifices he made for study. "I worked tirelessly, often without sympathy or support. I had no friends to share my burdens, and I felt the weight of my circumstances."

He expressed frustration at those born into wealth who could not grasp his struggles. "They ask why I got into debt, as if it were a choice. They have no idea what it means to be truly destitute."

Desplein explained how early experiences shaped his view of humanity. "If I seem hard or bitter, it's because I've witnessed the selfishness and envy around me. In Paris, mediocrity often seeks to undermine those who strive for excellence."

He recalled a pivotal moment when he faced utter poverty and considered enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when it arrived the porter demanded too much to release it. Despairing, he met his neighbor, Bourgeat, a water-carrier who offered help.

Bourgeat, despite his own hardships, paid Desplein's debts and helped him find new lodgings. "He devoted his savings to my future," Desplein said, visibly moved. "He understood my mission and supported me selflessly."

Desplein described Bourgeat as a nurturing figure who cared for him like a mother. "He took pride in my success and never sought recognition for his sacrifices. When I became a house surgeon, I suffered leaving him, but he was proud of me."

Desplein later repaid Bourgeat by buying him a horse and barrel, fulfilling a long-held dream. "He was furious I had spent my money on him, yet he was delighted. He never claimed credit for my success, though I owed him everything."

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein dedicated himself to his care, but Bourgeat died in his arms. "He left me everything he had. He had a strong faith, and I honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death."

Desplein concluded, "I can only offer this as a tribute to Bourgeat's faith. I hope that if there is a paradise, he finds peace there. I would give anything for the faith he had."

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein's last days, could not definitively say whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He pondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
