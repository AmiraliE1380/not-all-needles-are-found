Bianchon, a Parisian physician, trained under the troubled Desplein, an atheist who denied the soul, grappled with his legacy, felt isolated and eccentric.

Having been poor as a student, Bianchon was mentored by Desplein, who saw his promise. Puzzled to see Desplein enter a church despite his atheism, Bianchon heard him at dinner dismiss mass and criticize its effects.

Later Bianchon found Desplein had founded the service, deepening the mystery. After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon pressed for an explanation; Desplein told of extreme poverty and the struggle to finance his studies. Determined to succeed, he felt misunderstood by those who'd never faced true want. His neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water‑carrier, quietly supported him and their bond grew. Desplein tended Bourgeat until his death and honored him by arranging masses for his soul in lasting gratitude.
