In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a hub of Besançon society, the Archbishop often visited due to her devout nature. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, which deepened Madame de Watteville's piety. Their home, filled with fine furnishings, showcased their social status and attracted notable clergy.

During a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a legal victory by young lawyer Monsieur Savaron, piquing the interest of the guests, especially Madame de Watteville. The Vicar-General noted Savaron lived nearby, but his origins were unclear, raising curiosity about his character.

Historically, France and England have influenced each other culturally, particularly in language and fashion. Recently, England introduced terms like "dandy" and "lion," the latter describing a flamboyant man, influencing towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas emerged as a local "lion." Amedee, of Spanish descent, sought to elevate his status in a conservative town.

Living modestly, Amedee maintained a lavish lifestyle with the help of his stylish servant, Babylas. Despite his extravagant appearance, he struggled financially, relying on a limited income. His social life revolved around impressing the local aristocracy, particularly Rosalie de Watteville, a sheltered young woman from a wealthy family.

Amedee aspired to marry Rosalie for her dowry, but her mother scrutinized his intentions. Despite Amedee's charm, he faced challenges in gaining their approval, as Rosalie remained indifferent. His pursuit of Rosalie reflected broader themes of aspiration and societal expectations.

Monsieur de Soulas shared the story of Albert Savaron, who moved from Paris to Besançon, rented an apartment, and registered as a barrister. Mademoiselle de Watteville recognized Savaron's noble lineage, but Amedee clarified he was a commoner who had added a bar-sinister to his name.

Initially ignored by the local legal community, Savaron worked hard and kept to himself, leading to his obscurity. His reputation shifted when he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery, impressing the court and attracting clients. The Vicar-General noted Savaron's striking appearance and eloquence.

Rosalie, intrigued by Savaron, found him more appealing than her superficial suitors. She planned to meet him at church and felt a connection during mass. As her fascination grew, she encouraged her father to build a Belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron's private life. Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, promoting Besançon's interests, which Rosalie's father subscribed to, deepening her infatuation.

In July 1823, friends Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland. Rodolphe became captivated by Fanny Lovelace in Gersau and decided to stay while Leopold continued. Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was passionate and sensitive, while Leopold was set to become a notary.

Rodolphe found lodging with the Stopfer couple and learned Fanny lived modestly as a translator and writer. Eager to meet her, he gained access to her gardens, where he suspected her mute servant, Gina, had a hidden background. 

One evening, while listening to Fanny sing, Rodolphe overheard her speaking Italian with Gina, suspecting they were refugees. After a misunderstanding led to Gina attacking him, Fanny and Gina helped him recover, deepening Rodolphe's attraction.

As Rodolphe healed, he learned of his mother’s death, which plunged him into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where he found solace in Fanny's company. Their bond deepened as they shared sorrows and dreams. Fanny revealed she was married to an older man, Emilio, but expressed hope for a future together.

Rodolphe's feelings for Fanny intensified, admiring her strength and independence. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential. Their conversations revealed a shared passion for freedom and liberal ideals. When Fanny received news that her family was safe and would return to Italy, she felt joy, but Rodolphe struggled with jealousy regarding her past. Despite these challenges, Rodolphe remained devoted to Fanny, vowing to wait for her. Their love blossomed against the stunning Swiss landscape, symbolizing their hopes amid political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days, Rodolphe was enchanted by Francesca, who represented youthful recklessness and the intricacies of womanhood. He observed her affectionate yet playful relationship with her husband, Emilio, which intrigued him. One evening, Francesca expressed her desire for luxury, likening it to her love for art, and declined to join Rodolphe in Geneva to protect Emilio’s reputation. While professing her love for Rodolphe, she stressed the importance of discretion.

After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe went to Geneva, settling in Eaux-Vives and seeking information about Italian refugees, particularly a bookseller named Lamporani. At Villa Diodati, he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her ties to the aristocratic Colonna family. Rodolphe felt inadequate loving a princess disguised as a commoner and sought an audience with her husband, Prince Gandolphini, who welcomed him. Despite wanting to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, preoccupied with thoughts of their future.

When they finally met at a gathering, their connection deepened, and Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions while remaining youthful. Her background as a well-educated heiress, initially destined for a cloistered life, contrasted with her revolutionary spirit, aligning with Rodolphe’s aspirations.

As spring approached, Rodolphe struggled with longing and anxiety, questioning Francesca’s feelings. Tension arose when he compared her to a French noblewoman, inadvertently hurting her. She instructed him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, marking a pivotal moment in their relationship.

Back in Paris, Rodolphe discovered a portrait of Princess Gandolphini inspired by Francesca. Driven by ambition and love, he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell, he sought solace at Villa Gandolphini in Naples. Just as he was poised for success, the July 1830 revolution struck, but Rodolphe persevered, supported by love.

Meanwhile, Mademoiselle de Watteville was consumed by jealousy over Francesca, believing she loved Albert. This ignited her desire to win his affection, leading her to confront Mariette about her relationship with Jerome. Rosalie learned of Albert's noble lineage and ambitions, intensifying her infatuation. As Albert's career flourished, Rosalie's feelings morphed into obsession, compelling her to read another letter detailing his hopes for the Duchesse.

Despite her turmoil, Rosalie resolved to step back, believing she should sacrifice her desires for Albert and the Duchesse's happiness, grappling with unrequited love and questioning when she would find someone who truly loved her. Ultimately, her internal conflict highlighted the complexities of love, jealousy, and the sacrifices made in the name of affection.

In the spring of 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie aspired to overcome the Duchesse d’Argaiolo, driven by her solitude. Meanwhile, Albert de Savarus gained political traction in Besançon, securing support for an aqueduct restoration from influential figures, including a wealthy contractor.

Savarus, who had represented local priests in lawsuits, masked his ambitions with indifference. In October 1834, he financed a merchant's election bid. The Abbe de Grancey recognized Savarus's potential but noted his reluctance to align with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Rosalie visited her family estate, les Rouxey, where she learned of her father's legal troubles and suggested hiring Savarus as their lawyer to protect her inheritance. While trying to keep him in Besançon, she attempted to undermine his political ambitions by informing the Prefect of his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated, and his eloquence won the townsfolk's support.

As the election approached, the Prefect and his allies sought to weaken Savarus's backing. In despair over his unrequited love for Francesca, Savarus received encouragement from the Abbe to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie could provide crucial support. This led Savarus to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie, balancing love and ambition.

At a gathering hosted by Madame la Baronne de Watteville, concern grew over Savarus's absence. While Madame de Chavoncourt dismissed it, Rosalie suspected otherwise. They learned he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions.

The next morning, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned of Savarus's sudden disappearance after a visit from an old gentleman, leading to the election of Monsieur de Chavoncourt. As time passed, most forgot Albert, except for Rosalie, who struggled with the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo's death and refused her mother’s suggestion to marry Monsieur de Soulas.

Tension escalated at les Rouxey, culminating in tragedy when the Baron drowned. Grief-stricken, Rosalie contemplated confessing her role in Albert's fate. The Vicar-General urged her to seek redemption, revealing that Albert had entered a monastery. Years later, after her mother married Monsieur de Soulas, Rosalie confronted the Duchess about Albert's letters, leaving her shaken. Now living alone at les Rouxey, Rosalie, after a severe steamboat accident, leads a life devoted to religious practices.
