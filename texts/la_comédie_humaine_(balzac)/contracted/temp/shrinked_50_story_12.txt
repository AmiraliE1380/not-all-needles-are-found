In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a hub of Besançon society, the Archbishop often visited due to her devoutness. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married wealthy heiress Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, enhancing Madame de Watteville's piety and attracting notable clergy to their home.

During a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbe de Grancey, who announced a legal victory by young lawyer Monsieur Savaron, intriguing Madame de Watteville. The Vicar-General noted Savaron's obscure origins, raising questions about his character.

Culturally, France and England have influenced each other, with English terms like "dandy" entering French. Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a local "lion," sought to elevate his status while living modestly and relying on his stylish servant, Babylas. He aspired to marry Rosalie de Watteville for her dowry, but her mother scrutinized his intentions, and Rosalie remained indifferent.

Monsieur de Soulas shared the story of Albert Savaron, a commoner who gained recognition after successfully defending a mentally challenged man. Rosalie found Savaron more appealing than her superficial suitors and planned to meet him at church. Meanwhile, Savaron launched a fortnightly publication, the _Eastern Review_, deepening Rosalie's infatuation.

In July 1823, Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland, where Rodolphe fell for Fanny Lovelace, learning of her modest life and her servant Gina's hidden background. Their bond deepened as they shared sorrows, with Fanny revealing her marriage to an older man, Emilio. Despite challenges, Rodolphe vowed to wait for her, symbolizing their hopes amid turmoil.

For twenty days, Rodolphe was captivated by Francesca, who represented youthful imprudence and the complexities of womanhood. He was intrigued by her affectionate bond with her husband, Emilio. One evening, Francesca expressed her desire for luxury and declined to join Rodolphe in Geneva to protect Emilio’s reputation, emphasizing the need for discretion despite her professed love.

After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe went to Geneva, seeking information about Italian refugees, particularly bookseller Lamporani. At Villa Diodati, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage, revealing her aristocratic ties. Feeling inadequate loving a disguised princess, he sought an audience with her husband, Prince Gandolphini. Their connection deepened at a gathering, where Francesca encouraged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions.

As spring approached, Rodolphe's longing and anxiety grew, leading to tension when he compared Francesca to a French noblewoman. After Francesca asked him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, he returned to Paris, discovering her portrait. Driven by ambition, he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. Meanwhile, Mademoiselle de Watteville's jealousy over Francesca and her obsession with Albert highlighted the complexities of love and sacrifice.

In spring 1835, nineteen-year-old Rosalie aimed to thwart the Duchesse d’Argaiolo while Albert de Savarus gained political traction in Besançon for an aqueduct project. Savarus, masking his ambitions, arranged financing for a merchant's election bid but hesitated to ally with the Wattevilles due to neutrality concerns.

Visiting her family estate, les Rouxey, Rosalie learned of her father's legal issues and suggested Savarus as their lawyer to safeguard her inheritance. She attempted to sabotage his political aspirations by informing the Prefect of his Royalist views, yet Savarus was nominated and gained local support. As the election approached, the Prefect and allies worked to undermine him. The Abbe de Grancey urged Savarus to connect with the Wattevilles, particularly Madame de Chavoncourt, considering a marriage alliance.

At a gathering, guests were shocked to learn Savarus had left unexpectedly. The Abbe realized Rosalie's feelings for him and discovered he had withdrawn from the election, leaving her desperate to hide this from Sidonie. The next day, news of Savarus's disappearance led to Monsieur de Chavoncourt's election. As time passed, most forgot Savarus, except Rosalie, who struggled with her emotions and her mother's marriage plans. After her father's tragic drowning, Rosalie contemplated confessing her role in Savarus's fate, later learning he had entered a monastery. Years later, after her mother remarried, she confronted the Duchess about the letters, seeking closure while living alone at les Rouxey, devoted to religious practices following a steamboat accident.
