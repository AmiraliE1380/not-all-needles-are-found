**The Deserted Woman**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage*  

**Dedication**  
To Her Grace the Duchesse d’Abrantes, from her devoted servant, Honoré de Balzac. Paris, August 1835.

---

In spring 1822, M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil, a young Parisian recovering from illness, was sent to Lower Normandy for convalescence. He settled in Bayeux, where he was welcomed by his cousin, Mme. de Sainte-Severe, and quickly became acquainted with the local aristocracy, characterized by their antiquated customs and rigid social structures.

The local nobility included a proud family with dubious claims to nobility, a wealthy but less distinguished couple, and various lesser gentry. They all shared a disdain for modernity and clung to outdated traditions. Gaston, initially amused by their quirks, soon grew weary of their monotonous lives, feeling stifled in their provincial existence.

One evening, while playing cards, Gaston overheard a conversation about Mme. de Beauseant, a woman of noble birth who had fallen from grace due to a scandal involving M. d’Ajuda-Pinto. Intrigued, he learned that she lived in seclusion, shunned by society. The mention of her name stirred something within him, igniting a desire to meet her.

Despite the barriers of her social standing and the stigma of her situation, Gaston felt compelled to seek her out. He began to fantasize about her, viewing her as a symbol of passion and romance, contrasting sharply with the dullness of his current surroundings. His infatuation grew, leading him to devise a plan to gain access to her.

Gaston enlisted the help of M. de Champignelles, who agreed to inquire if Mme. de Beauseant would receive him. After some hesitation, she consented, piquing her curiosity about this young man. When Gaston finally met her, he was struck by her beauty and grace, feeling both admiration and a sense of inadequacy.

Their initial conversation was fraught with tension, as Gaston struggled to express his feelings. Mme. de Beauseant, aware of her precarious position, maintained a facade of indifference, yet she was intrigued by Gaston’s youthful ardor. Their exchanges were charged with unspoken emotions, revealing the complexities of their respective situations.

As their relationship developed, Gaston found himself torn between his growing love for Mme. de Beauseant and the expectations of society. He was drawn to her, yet he understood the implications of their connection. Mme. de Beauseant, aware of the societal constraints surrounding them, cautioned him against pursuing her, fearing the consequences of their liaison.

Despite her warnings, Gaston’s feelings deepened, leading him to write her a passionate letter declaring his love. However, he was met with a cold response, as she urged him to consider the realities of their situation. She expressed her unwillingness to be the cause of his downfall, emphasizing the importance of his future.

Gaston, undeterred, continued to pursue her, believing that their love could transcend societal norms. Yet, as time passed, the weight of their circumstances began to take its toll. Mme. de Beauseant struggled with her past and the stigma attached to her name, while Gaston faced pressure from his family to conform to societal expectations.

Eventually, Gaston’s mother introduced him to Mlle. de la Rodiere, a wealthy heiress, presenting a tempting alternative to his tumultuous relationship with Mme. de Beauseant. Caught between duty and desire, Gaston found himself at a crossroads, grappling with the implications of his choices.

In a moment of desperation, Mme. de Beauseant decided to end their relationship, believing it was for the best. She wrote to Gaston, expressing her sorrow and urging him to move on. The letter shattered Gaston, who realized the depth of his feelings for her and the impossibility of their situation.

In a tragic turn of events, Gaston chose to marry Mlle. de la Rodiere, believing it was the only way to secure his future. However, the weight of his decision haunted him, and he struggled to reconcile his love for Mme. de Beauseant with his new life.

The story culminates in a heartbreaking conclusion, as Gaston, unable to bear the pain of his lost love, takes his own life. Mme. de Beauseant, left to mourn the loss of their love, remains in solitude, forever marked by the choices they made.

In the end, Balzac’s tale serves as a poignant exploration of love, societal constraints, and the tragic consequences of choices made in the name of duty. The characters’ struggles reflect the complexities of human emotion and the often-painful realities of life, leaving readers to ponder the true cost of love and the sacrifices it demands.
