Honoré De Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, emphasizing the story's melancholy and the need to confront sorrows for loved ones.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut admires Pierrette Lorrain's home but is urged to flee when she hears a noise. Pierrette reflects on her sorrowful life, marked by her father's death and her mother's financial struggles. Living with the self-interested Rogrons, she becomes increasingly dependent on them.

The narrative explores social class, familial duty, and financial struggles. The Rogrons refuse the Lorrains' offer to care for Pierrette, fearing for their inheritance. Sylvie invites Pierrette to live with them to regain social standing, complicating her situation. 

Brigaut's return disrupts the Rogron family, as he seeks to improve himself for Pierrette. Sylvie's jealousy grows, fearing a connection between Pierrette and Colonel Gouraud. Tensions escalate, leading to Pierrette's injury and eventual decline in health. Despite community efforts, Pierrette tragically passes away, prompting Brigaut to seek justice amid societal indifference.
