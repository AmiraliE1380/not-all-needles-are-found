Monsieur de Manerville, a respected Norman gentleman, became a Gascon after marrying a wealthy heiress and selling his estate. He served as major of the Gate Guards until his death in 1813, having avoided the Revolution by going to Martinique. Following his wife’s death in 1810, he grew miserly, depriving his son, Paul, of resources.

Paul de Manerville returned from college in 1810, living under his father's strict control for three years, which stunted his emotional growth and made him timid. He felt trapped in his father's mansion, unable to socialize due to financial constraints. His father took him to royalist gatherings, where Paul felt out of place, coping through physical activities while his intellectual pursuits were limited to novels.

After his father's death, Paul inherited a fortune but was disillusioned with Bordeaux. He traveled Europe for six years, exhausting his capital and relying on his estate's revenues. Realizing he needed to settle down, Paul returned to Bordeaux, believing his noble title would attract a suitable partner.

Now a count, Paul recognized the societal value of marriage and confided in his friend de Marsay about his desire to marry and live as a country gentleman. De Marsay warned him against marriage, arguing it would lead to a loss of freedom and happiness, suggesting he enjoy bachelorhood instead. Paul defended his desire for a family, longing for companionship and stability, contrasting sharply with de Marsay’s cynical view.

In winter 1821, Comte de Manerville returned to Bordeaux, eager to restore his family mansion and uphold his reputation. His charm quickly won him favor among the local elite, but he realized that marriage was crucial for a fulfilling life, particularly with Mademoiselle Evangelista, the celebrated beauty of Bordeaux.

Mademoiselle Evangelista, daughter of a wealthy Spanish merchant, was raised in luxury after her father's death. By the time Paul returned, she was a stunning young woman, but rumors about her extravagant upbringing made suitors wary. When Paul and Natalie met, they were instantly attracted, and Paul dismissed concerns about their financial situation, confident in his ability to manage it.

As their relationship deepened, Paul idealized Natalie, overlooking potential flaws. The societal pressures surrounding their union added to the allure, though Natalie's upbringing and her mother’s ambitions posed challenges. Paul remained unaware of Madame Evangelista’s desire for power through her daughter's marriage.

In winter 1822, Paul sought to marry Natalie with the help of his great-aunt, Baronne de Maulincour. After consulting Madame Evangelista, the baroness warned Paul about the dowry, noting that Natalie's fortune was tied to her father's estate. She advised him to consult their notary, Maitre Mathias, to protect his interests, leaving him anxious about potential deceit.

During the notaries' meeting, Solonet proposed terms favoring Madame Evangelista, while Mathias insisted on protecting Paul’s interests. Their discussions revealed stark differences: Mathias represented traditional values, while Solonet embodied opportunistic strategies. Despite tender moments between Paul and Natalie, the tension regarding the marriage contract loomed. Ultimately, a compromise was reached, securing Natalie’s inheritance while ensuring Madame Evangelista’s financial stability, though underlying tensions hinted at future challenges.

The following day, jeweler Elie Magus appraised Madame Evangelista's diamonds for the marriage contract of Comte Paul and Mademoiselle Natalie, valuing the jewelry at 250,000 francs and the “Discreto” diamond at 100,000 francs. However, he offered only 75,000 for the diamond and 160,000 for the jewelry due to depreciation. This appraisal fueled rumors about the marriage's stability, suggesting Madame Evangelista was selling her house and that the wedding might be canceled. Despite notaries denying these claims, public skepticism persisted, driven by jealousy of the Evangelista family's wealth.

To counter the gossip, Madame Evangelista organized an extravagant "fete of the camellias" to display her wealth. Preparations took over a month, during which the marriage contract was finalized, generating community buzz. On signing day, she realized the contract's implications and feared for her daughter's fortune, prompting her to seek clarity on the terms.

The courtyard was transformed into a lavish tent filled with camellias, creating an opulent atmosphere. Guests arrived with excitement, but the contract signing loomed ominously. As the entailment clause was explained, Madame Evangelista recognized the potential financial ruin for Natalie if Paul died first, realizing her earlier manipulations had jeopardized her daughter's future.

As the contract was finalized, Madame Evangelista confronted the notaries, expressing her frustration. Solonet suggested delaying the signing, but she felt that would worsen their reputation. Ultimately, she decided to proceed with the marriage, vowing to protect her daughter's interests and planning to invest wisely for Natalie's security.

The signing marked a turning point, with Madame Evangelista determined to navigate her new reality. As festivities began, she felt a mix of triumph and resentment, knowing her daughter's happiness was intertwined with her ambitions and fears. The night unfolded with celebration, but underlying tensions hinted at future conflicts.

Meanwhile, Paul was troubled by doubts, particularly regarding Madame Evangelista. While he reassured himself about their financial arrangements, he remained cautious, mindful of Mathias's warnings. Unbeknownst to him, Madame Evangelista contemplated withdrawing her interests from their lives, struggling with her complex Creole identity.

The next day, Madame Evangelista and Natalie reminisced about their harmonious life. She expressed sorrow over Natalie’s marriage, warning that love could fade if not managed carefully. She advised Natalie to establish her authority as a wife while maintaining allure through social engagements.

As the wedding approached, Madame Evangelista proposed selling her jewelry to support Natalie, which Paul accepted, unaware of the underlying tensions. Determined to step back, she believed her absence would allow Natalie and Paul to bond. Paul felt relieved, sensing newfound freedom.

As the wedding neared, Natalie felt anxious about her new life without her mother but was determined to be a good wife. The ceremony took place at midnight, often associated with ominous omens. Afterward, Madame Evangelista feigned despair to extract promises from Paul, ensuring his commitment to Natalie. As they departed for Paris, Natalie signaled to her mother that Paul had won the initial battle, but she was beginning to assert her influence over him.

Five years later, Comte Paul de Manerville visited his old friend, Monsieur Mathias, in Bordeaux. Mathias noted Paul's changed demeanor and expressed sorrow over his financial troubles. Paul revealed he had left Natalie without her knowledge, planning to sail to Calcutta to rebuild his fortune under the name Camille. He explained his massive debts, exceeding a million and a half francs, incurred from lavish spending.

Mathias suggested Paul could live off Natalie's income while recovering, but Paul rejected this, wanting to earn his fortune independently. The next day, Mathias received a bill of exchange for 150,000 francs from de Marsay, a friend of Paul’s. Despite concerns, Mathias accepted Paul's determination to leave for India. At the quay, they faced gossip about Paul's downfall. As Paul prepared to board, Mathias signaled him with important letters, but Paul pocketed them unread.

At sea, Paul reflected on his choices and, overcome by seasickness, spent days in his cabin. Eventually, he opened the letters. Natalie's letter expressed her anguish over his departure and her desire to support him, revealing she might be pregnant. In contrast, de Marsay's letter detailed Paul's financial ruin and warned of a conspiracy against him, urging him to return to Paris. As Paul absorbed these harsh realities, he felt anger and despair but resolved to confront his challenges and restore his honor.
