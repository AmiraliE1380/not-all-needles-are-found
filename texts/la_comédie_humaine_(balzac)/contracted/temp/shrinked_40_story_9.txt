The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, mixing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade that also supports his political ambitions. While some women reserve judgment until he becomes a peer, her conduct remains strictly principled.

The marquis is notable only for his lack of qualities: a silent deputy who votes properly but lacks charisma, nicknamed “dull weather.” His mediocrity makes him a safe match for a virtuous wife, since he cannot lead either to folly or scandal.

Though reserved, the marquise provokes both respect and scorn from would‑be flirts, whom she answers with contempt. She speaks pleasantly with agreeable men without stirring gossip and does not affect coyness—her probity is sincere.

The narrator admires her presence: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she has a captivating air and animated eyes that suggest depth; she charms without trying. This emerges in a recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, lively young man. At a ball they converse from opera to women’s duties, an exchange hinting at mutual interest before he abruptly departs. The next morning Rastignac, distracted while writing letters, grows absorbed in feelings nearing romance until his friend Horace interrupts talk of an expedition to Morea.

Dressing, the marquise receives Rastignac’s affectionate letter. Her impulse is to discard it, then to decipher his intent; the note leaves her fatigued as she ponders its impertinence and implications. The episode reveals tension between her outward virtue and inward stirrings, foreshadowing complex relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to treat him with more than disdain; his recent insolence surpassed previous offenses. She had thought to keep a letter he sent but burned it. "Madame received a fine love‑letter," Caroline told the housemaid, who was surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come, despite intending to confront him with coldness. Stendhal called this mental process "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet Joseph for misplacing letters. Joseph, distracted, remembered one was for Madame de Listomere and the other for Rastignac's lawyer. When Eugène learned the letter had not been for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed at his own wandering heart. His errors began when, planning to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the mix‑up, he then stayed away for several days, letting her resentment harden.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out. The Marquis, unaware of tensions, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noted social faux pas. On seeing him, Madame de Listomere blushed; he thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere remained absorbed in his newspaper, oblivious.

Madame de Listomere quickly composed herself. When Eugène asked after her health she assured him she was well. He suggested his visit might be unwelcome and blamed the marquis's introduction; she hinted that her husband did not know all her secrets. Eugène tried to lighten the mood, proposing silence as his best excuse. She promised to forget, maintaining dignity. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement; he insisted he meant no harm.

Monsieur de Listomere interrupted to announce a mutual acquaintance's death. The conversation revealed the marquise's irritation. When Eugène named Madame de Nucingen her demeanor shifted: she rang for wood, signaling the visit should end, and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Discomfited, Eugène stammered and departed.

Days later Madame de Listomere confirmed Eugène's claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, her physician a nervous condition. The episode highlights the complexities of social interaction, misunderstanding, and romantic pride.
