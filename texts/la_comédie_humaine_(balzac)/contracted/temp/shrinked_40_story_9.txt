The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration ideal: sincere religious devotion combined with worldly elegance. Seven years into a marriage with a bland, politically useful marquis, she maintains a virtuous façade to support his ambitions; acquaintances withhold judgment until he attains the peerage. Her reserve—respectable, sometimes scornful—rebuffs flirtations, sustains guarded conversation, and signals genuine principle rather than coquetry.

The narrator admires her: not classically beautiful but possessing a captivating presence—animated eyes and effortless charm. This charm emerges in her encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever, modestly vivacious young man. At a ball they converse about opera and women’s duties; he abruptly departs. The next morning Rastignac, distracted and composing letters, imagines pleasures until his friend Horace arrives to plan an expedition. Meanwhile the Marquise reads one of Rastignac’s affectionate letters; initially intrigued, she grows offended by its impertinence and implications, a clash between her outward virtue and a stirred feeling.

Offended, she forbids her porter to admit Rastignac and burns the letter. Caroline, the housemaid, remarks on the fine love‑letter. That evening she attends the Marquis de Beauseant’s party expecting Rastignac; by two a.m. she realizes he will not come—Stendhal calls this hardening of resentment “crystallization.”

Four days later, when Rastignac scolds his valet Joseph for misplacing letters, he learns one was for the Marquise and another for a lawyer. Conscious that his affections have wandered, he laughs; his trouble began when he tried to amuse the Marquise with talk of the blunder and worsened by failing to visit, letting her displeasure fester.

When he finally calls, the marquis, unaware, admits him. The meeting is awkward: the Marquise blushes, gives guarded answers, and promises to forget while remaining dignified. Mention of the letter provokes feigned offense and hidden amusement; the name of Madame de Nucingen hardens her, and she rings for wood to signal his departure. Embarrassed, he leaves. Days later she confirms the letter’s true recipient and withdraws from society; the marquis blames a stomach ailment, her physician calls it nervousness. The episode exposes social misunderstandings, intense but constrained feeling, and rigid romantic roles.
