DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, shifts enterprise toward intellectual influence: linking local power to markets, equalizing goods, and spreading Parisian ideas. Self-taught, he reads people, judges solvency, and sees vice and virtue. Persuasive and adaptable, he often succeeds where ambassadors cannot, negotiating deals in taverns and importing innovations.

Gaudissart exemplifies this type: an acrobat of commerce who extracts provincial wealth with a torrent of phrases. In a striped shirt he is disarming and charming. After making his fortune selling hats—winning vendors and social favor—he remains festive but businesslike, tailoring pitches to each audience. After 1830 turned ideas into commodities, writers and speculators prospered; Gaudissart adapted, selling life insurance and the lucrative Children's Journal.

CHAPTER II

In April, after a week of Saint-Simonism, he set out despite offers to return to hats, joking with Jenny the florist about furnishing her room from his profits and boasting of sales as he stopped in Blois.

CHAPTER III

In Vouvray the townspeople’s wit and hostility thwarted him. At the Soleil d’Or he met Monsieur Vernier, who mocked commercial travellers and sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, the madman obsessed with wine and newspapers. Margaritis’s absurd replies and the amused crowd undercut every pitch.

CHAPTERS IV–V

Provoked, Gaudissart demanded satisfaction; Vernier’s refusal led toward a duel until the innkeeper brokered a breakfast mediation. They patched up the quarrel and Gaudissart left, resolved to return to Paris—reminded that eloquence cannot always overcome discernment.
