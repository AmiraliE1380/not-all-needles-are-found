In late October 1800 a foreigner with a woman and a little girl stood a long while before the Tuileries, near the ruins where Catherine de’ Medici’s chateau once joined the Valois Louvre.

The man stood with folded arms and bowed head, glancing between the consular palace and his wife, who sat on a stone. The woman, occupied with the nine- or ten-year-old child whose long black hair she toyed with, watched every look her companion gave. Their bond was more than love; misery—perhaps the strongest tie—stole their anxious attention.

The stranger had a broad, serious head, thick hair streaked with white, noble features marred by hardness. Despite an erect, strong figure he looked over sixty. His worn clothes marked him as foreign. His wife’s faded face showed deep sadness, though she forced smiles when he looked. The little girl, sun-tanned and plainly Italian, had large black eyes and native grace. Passersby felt moved by this plain despair; when the stranger noticed attention he looked so savage that bold loungers hurried off.

After a time the tall stranger wiped his face, drew a dagger from his breast, and gave it to his companion, saying in Italian, “I will see if the Bonapartes remember us.” He walked slowly to the palace entrance where a consular guard stopped him and refused to permit discussion. When the sentinel presented his bayonet, fortune favored the stranger: the guard was changing, and the corporal pointed to the post commander.

“Let Bonaparte know that Bartolomeo di Piombo wishes to speak with him,” the Italian said to the captain.

The officer insisted one could not see the First Consul without a written request. Bartolomeo persisted that the soldier must go to Bonaparte. The officer refused, citing regulations. Bartolomeo frowned, casting a terrible look as if charging him with the consequences of refusal. Then he remained under the portico. At that moment a carriage drew up and Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, emerged.

“Ah, Loucian, it is lucky for me I have met you!” cried the stranger in Corsican patois. Lucien recognized him and, after a word, took the Corsican away.

Murat, Lannes, and Rapp were in the First Consul’s cabinet. As Lucien entered with Piombo, conversation ceased. Lucien took Napoleon by the arm and led him to a window recess. Napoleon signaled Murat and Lannes to retire; Rapp pretended not to see and lingered. Bonaparte opened the door suddenly and found his aide-de-camp close to the wall.

“Do you choose not to understand me?” said the First Consul. “I wish to be alone with my compatriot.”

“A Corsican!” replied the aide-de-camp. “I distrust those fellows too much to—”

Bonaparte smiled and pushed him aside. “Well, what has brought you here, my poor Bartolomeo?” he asked.

“To ask asylum and protection from you, if you are a true Corsican,” replied Bartolomeo roughly.

“What ill fortune drove you from the island? You were the richest—”

“I have killed all the Portas,” said the Corsican in a deep voice, frowning.

The First Consul stepped back in surprise.

“Do you mean to betray me?” cried Bartolomeo, with a dark look at Bonaparte. “Do you know that there are still four Piombos in Corsica?”

Lucien seized his compatriot’s arm and shook it. “Did you come here to threaten the savior of France?” he said.

Bonaparte signaled Lucien to be silent, then looked at Piombo. “Why did you kill the Portas?”

“We had been friends,” the man replied. “The Barbantis reconciled us. The day after we drank to end our quarrels I left for Bastia. The Portas stayed in my house and set fire to my vineyard at Longone. They killed my son Gregorio. My daughter Ginevra and my wife had taken the sacrament that morning and escaped; the Virgin protected them. When I returned my house was ashes. I found Gregorio’s body by accident at night and knew the Portas had done it. I went to the woods, called together all the men who had ever served me—do you hear me, Bonaparte?—and we marched to the Portas’ vineyard. We arrived at five; at seven they were all before God. Giacomo says Eliza Vanni saved a child, Luigi. But I myself bound Luigi to his bed before setting fire to the house. I left the island with my wife and child without learning whether Luigi is alive.”

Bonaparte listened with curiosity but not surprise. Lucien asked how many there had been.

“Seven,” replied Piombo. “All of them persecuted you in olden times.”

The brothers showed no hatred. “Ha! you are no longer Corsicans!” cried Piombo in despair. “Farewell. In other days I protected you,” he added reproachfully. “Without me your mother would never have reached Marseille,” he said to Bonaparte, who stood silent and thoughtful, his elbow on a mantel-shelf.

“As a matter of duty, Piombo,” said Napoleon at last, “I cannot take you under my wing. I have become the leader of a great nation; I command the Republic; I am bound to execute the laws.”

“Ha! ha!” said Bartolomeo scornfully.

“But I can shut my eyes,” Bonaparte continued. “The tradition of the vendetta will long prevent law's reign in Corsica,” he added to himself. “But it must be destroyed, at any cost.”

After a pause he spoke more personally. “Live here, in Paris,” he said to Bartolomeo. “We will know nothing of this affair. I will cause your property in Corsica to be bought to give you enough to live on for the present. Later, we will think of you. But remember, no more vendetta! There are no woods here to fly to. If you play with daggers you must expect no mercy. Here, the law protects all citizens; no one is allowed to do justice for himself.”

“He has made himself the head of a singular nation,” said Bartolomeo, taking Lucien’s hand and pressing it. “But you have both recognized me in misfortune, and I am yours henceforth, for life or death. You may dispose as you will of the Piombos.”

At these words his Corsican brow unbent and he looked pleasantly about him. “You are not badly off here,” he said, smiling, “You are all in red, like a cardinal.”

“Your success depends on yourself; you can have a palace too,” Bonaparte replied, watching him keenly. “It will often happen that I shall need some faithful friend in whom I can confide.”

A sigh of joy heaved the Corsican’s chest. He held out his hand to the First Consul. “The Corsican is in you still.”

Bonaparte smiled and looked at the man who seemed to bring a breath from the island that had saved him—the land he would never see again. He signaled to his brother, who then took Piombo away. Lucien asked about the family’s finances. Piombo showed his wife and Ginevra sitting on a heap of stones.

“We came from Fontainebleau on foot; we have not a single penny,” he said.

Lucien gave him his purse and told him to come the next day so arrangements could be made for their comfort. The value of Piombo’s Corsican property, if sold, would scarcely maintain him honorably in Paris.

Fifteen years elapsed between Piombo’s arrival in Paris with his family and the following event, which would be scarcely intelligible without this account of the preceding circumstances.

Servin, a respected painter, was the first in Paris to open a studio specifically for young ladies who wanted to learn painting. About forty, of strict morals and devoted to his art, he had married a general’s dowerless daughter and guarded the respectability of his classes carefully. He accepted only young women from wealthy, well-placed families, excluding those who wished to be professional artists because they required different, more technical instruction. Mothers, reassured by his prudence and the genteel company he guaranteed, entrusted their daughters to him; sending a girl to Servin’s was a recommendation. In Parisian society his studio became a kind of specialty—like Herbault for bonnets or Leroy for gowns—where a young woman trained by Servin could be expected to judge a museum work, produce a striking portrait, copy an old master, or paint a genre picture. Although he taught the aristocracy, Servin remained independent and patriotic, and his pupils preserved the easy, brilliant, half-ironical painterly tone.

Servin’s studio occupied the whole attic above his rooms, reached by a small spiral stair enclosed within his apartments; the usual attic entrance had been walled up. The gallery was lit from above by enormous panes shaded with green linen, and its dark-gray walls displayed caricatures, rapid heads, dusty plaster models—Niobe, a smiling Venus, yellowed écorchés—frames and canvases in various states; the room had that particular artists’ mixture of jumble and bareness: poverty and richness, care and neglect. Ancient garments, armor, fragments of machinery lay about; amid these remnants stood a mysteriously grand presence—genius and death, Diana and Apollo beside a skull—colors glowing in shadow. It was an artist’s world.

On a bright July morning, a dozen easels rose beneath two greenish rays tracing gold lines in the dust. Several young girls animated the scene with varied expressions, attitudes, and dress. The green serge curtains at each easel cast strong shadows and piquant light-and-shade effects; the group formed the prettiest picture in the room. Among them a fair young girl, simply dressed, sat apart, working intently and seeming in dread of mishap; she was the prettiest, most modest, and apparently the least rich. The class showed two distinct cliques: daughters of bankers, notaries, and merchants on one side—rich and sharp, conscious of slights from the aristocratic set—and a patrician group led by Mademoiselle Amélie Thirion, daughter of one of the King’s ushers: a little creature silly, vain, pretentious, always late, heavily dressed, and demanding low voices.

The aristocratic group included several girls with exquisite figures and distinguished features but little simplicity or candor; polished manners had dulled deeper feeling and developed self-regard. Yet when they all assembled, their childlike heads and pure, virgin faces could show through—a virgin smile like a flash—and the studio could resemble a group of angels on a cloud.

Servin himself had been absent, engaged in another studio finishing a picture for the Exposition. During his absence Amélie Thirion made a decisive, petty but telling move. She rose, took an easel near Ginevra Piombo, carried it away from the patrician group, and placed it close to a partition that separated the studio from the attic’s extreme end—where broken casts, defaced canvases, and winter wood were kept. In moving the stool, color-box, and even a Prudhon copy that Ginevra had been working on, Amélie staged a coup d’état, putting a deliberate slight upon Ginevra by driving her from the place she had always occupied. The Right clique, led by Amélie, worked in silence; the Left—the banking and commercial girls—discussed and protested.

The act was more than personal spite. It occurred in late July 1815, in the immediate aftermath of the second Bourbon Restoration—a time that had shaken friendships and inflamed hostilities. Political divisions ran through families and households; many Parisian families were split, and children reflected their elders’ monarchical fervor or Bonapartist allegiance. Ginevra Piombo adored Napoleon; her father, Baron di Piombo, was among those who had helped the return from Elba. The baron had remained in Paris despite the evident danger; Ginevra’s grief at the restoration was no secret; she had even been moved to tears by news of Napoleon’s captivity and the arrest of Labédoyère. The aristocratic girls in Servin’s studio came from devoted royalist families; their hatred of Bonapartists was feverish and exaggerated. Thus Amélie’s act, while petty now, was then a natural expression of that hatred.

But political prejudice was not the only motive. Ginevra was the ablest and most admired of Servin’s pupils; the master esteemed her talent and character, and she inspired attachment akin to Bonaparte’s hold over soldiers. She also provoked jealousy. The aristocracy had long resolved on her fall; none had openly challenged her until Amélie’s decisive move forced public complicity. When Ginevra arrived and was met by profound silence, the scene carried the weight of both political enmity and petty envy. Of all the pupils she was the handsomest, tallest, best-made: noble, graceful, and dignified, she commanded respect. Her face radiated intelligence and Corsican passion, tempered by calm; long hair and dark eyes expressed ardor, while a high forehead suggested a savage pride, a remnant of Corsican moral instincts. Everything about her—simplicity and Lombard ease—was seductive. She inspired strong attachment; her father accompanied her to and from the studio. Her only acknowledged defect was a fully developed beauty that made her seem a woman already grown; she had refused marriage out of devotion to her parents. Painting had replaced the usual passions of her sex.

Ginevra received the silence with a calm that puzzled both friends and foes. She greeted the young Laure—who painted apart—tenderly, praised her drawing, and then walked slowly to her easel. She paid little attention to the patricians’ profound silence and passed before them as if oblivious. The banking-group glanced toward her; the Thirion camp watched with sidelong looks. Finally, Ginevra noticed and measured the distance between her and the aristocrats, but said nothing. Matilde Roguin, of the banking group, attributed to Ginevra’s silence a grandeur of soul, yet others foresaw vengeance or remembered that Ginevra could be vindictive when injured. The banking girls, after some musing, turned their sarcasm upon the Thirion party, humbling them.

Ginevra’s indifference had a deeper reason that day. The attic’s partition had a slight opening, and through it Ginevra had, on a previous evening, glimpsed a glimpse that stirred her imagination: the Imperial eagle and through a shaft of light, the sleeping form of an officer of the Guard. When she climbed to look, she had seen enough to conclude that Servin was hiding a proscribed man. Her new position beside the partition enabled her to listen and watch, and she resolved to keep that place to protect the concealed stranger from accidental discovery—the sort of precaution a conscientious person takes in dangerous times. Thus her apparent calm and indifference to the insult were partly because she valued the new position for its proximity to this secret.

Her curiosity had been piqued and her concern genuine: she feared that some companion, casual or spiteful, might reveal the man’s presence by loose talk or curious inquiry. She thought it better to remain and guard him than to leave him at the mercy of neglect or betrayal. This explained her unusual attention to sounds and an expression of preoccupation that Amélie noticed and later used to interpret malice.

Madame Servin’s arrival interrupted the budding drama. Her glance turned at once to the door where Ginevra sat; Amélie noted it and would later remember the look as wild and frightened, though at the moment it passed unnoticed by most. Madame Servin told the class that Servin would not come that day and circulated compliments and gentle words among the young women, lingering, with obvious unease, near Ginevra. She spoke to Ginevra of the latter’s copy, complimenting it; Ginevra, perceiving the wife’s deliberate blindness to what lay beyond the partition—wittingly, perhaps—responded with a gentle, incredulous smile and began to hum a Corsican canzonetta, both to cover the prisoner’s breathing and to reassure herself. The song was unusual and drew all heads, later being used as a pretext for charitable suppositions about jealousy.

After Madame Servin left and the class ended, Ginevra appeared eager to be left alone; she betrayed impatience with slow pupils. Amélie, cruel toward one who excelled her, suspected her rival had some secret purpose in lingering by the partition. Stealthily returning after pretending to have left, Amélie found Ginevra again on her precarious scaffold, absorbed. She coughed lightly; Ginevra started, blushed, and quickly altered the shade to justify her position. She descended and left the studio, her mind occupied with the image of a man’s head as beautiful as a Girodet Endymion—a head that had lingered with her.

For two days Ginevra pondered this man. On the third day, despite haste to arrive first, she found Amélie already there, in a carriage. Both watched each other with impenetrable faces. Amélie had glimpsed the handsome head through the crevice but had not seen the Imperial eagles and uniform that made the significance of the sight obvious. When Servin arrived earlier than usual and asked Ginevra to move because the light was poor, she refused and instead began to sketch in sepia the hidden man’s head. Works done under strong emotion carry a special stamp, and her sketch showed an intuition approaching genius. She drew amid awkward trembling, furtively glancing to hide the sheet.

Amélie, spying, turned her eyeglass on a sheet from a large portfolio and recognized the sketch of the mysterious man—so she showed herself, darting forward. The prisoner, perhaps awakened by the noise, stirred. Ginevra knocked over her stool, made incoherent noises, and began to laugh; she had thrown the sketch into her portfolio before Amélie could seize it. In the sudden press around Ginevra’s easel, Servin praised the beauty of her copy, and the entire class, except Amélie, was duped. Amélie slipped behind the crowd and tried to open the portfolio where she had seen the sketch thrown; Ginevra, taking up the portfolio, placed it before her, and the two girls looked at each other in silence.

Servin resumed teaching, chiding the girls about fashions and frivolities. Later he sat beside Ginevra and, in low voices, told her the truth about the man: he was an intimate friend of Labédoyère, who had helped unite regiments during the brief uprising; he had been a major in the Imperial Guard and had fought at Waterloo. He had been brought to Servin by the painter’s father-in-law, who had returned to the army during the campaign and cleverly rescued the man from those who had captured Labédoyère. The major was hidden in this attic corner because the father-in-law was too closely watched to shelter him at home. Servin had hoped to keep him out of sight in the only safe spot in the house; he had brought him by night about a week earlier.

Ginevra, shocked and indignant, wondered why Servin had not burned the uniform or disguised the man with civilian clothes. Servin replied they would remove the uniform that night; he planned to get him away. Ginevra, fearing for the man’s life, insisted he remain longer at the studio, asserting that Paris still offered places to hide a man. Servin explained he had done his best; the risk remained. Ginevra offered to help—knowing the Maréchal de Fétre—and Servin accepted the sympathy without assuring specifics. Their whispered exchange lasted long enough that the other pupils took notice; Servin’s lingering extended the session late into the hour when pupils usually left.

Amélie, thwarted and more curious than ever, had pretended to go down for her bag, then returned and slunk outside the studio door, placing herself where she could overhear or observe. Servin’s apparent secrecy—his earlier iron smile and the serious energy with which he praised Ginevra’s sketch—confirmed to Amélie that the matter was important and secret. The scene ended with the studio filled with a new tension: Ginevra now the central figure, admired and watched by friends and enemies alike; Servin the cautious protector of an Imperial officer hidden in his attic; and Amélie, resolved to pry further, stationed just outside the studio door. The political passions of the time, combined with personal jealousies and the artist’s private patriotism, had turned a simple lesson into a theater of intrigue and emotion.

When the painter and Ginevra believed themselves alone, Servin softly rapped on the garret door and admitted a tall, well-made young man in an Imperial uniform, one arm in a sling, his pallor showing suffering. He recoiled at the sight of an unknown woman. Amelie, frightened and unable to see into the room with the door closed, slipped away silently when she heard the garret open.

“Fear nothing,” Servin reassured the officer. “Mademoiselle is the daughter of a faithful friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo.” At once the soldier’s doubt vanished.

“You are wounded,” Ginevra observed.

“Oh, it is nothing,” he replied; “the wound is healing.”

From the street came the vendors’ cry: “Condemned to death!” They all shuddered, and the soldier turned ashen at a name.

“Labedoyere!” he cried, collapsing onto a stool.

Drops stood on his livid forehead; he seized his hair in despair and rested his elbow on Ginevra’s easel. “After all,” he said suddenly, “Labedoyere and I knew what we were doing. We were certain of the fate awaiting us. He dies for the Cause, and here am I hiding!” He rushed to the door; Ginevra reached it first and barred him.

“Can you restore the Emperor?” she asked. “Do you expect to raise that giant who could not maintain himself?”

“But what can I do?” the young man asked. “I have no relations. Labedoyere was my protector and friend; without him I am alone. Tomorrow I may be condemned myself; my only fortune was my pay. I spent my last penny to come here and try to snatch Labedoyere from his fate. Death is a necessity; when a man decides on death he ought to know how to sell his life to the executioner. I thought just now that the life of an honest man is worth that of two traitors, and a dagger well placed may grant immortality.”

Servin and Ginevra were alarmed. She admired the stranger’s handsome face and sweet voice, which kept its fervor even in anger. She soothed him.

“Monsieur,” she said, “as for your pecuniary distress, allow me to offer my savings. My father is rich; I am his only child; he loves me and will not blame me. We owe our property to the Emperor; it is only money. As for friends—those you shall have. The head that falls tomorrow before a dozen muskets will save yours. Wait till the storm is over; you can escape and take service abroad or in the French army.”

There is a maternal delicacy in such consolation; when hope comes from a beautiful benefactress, a young man yields. A rosy tinge colored his cheeks; his eyes brightened. “You are an angel of goodness—but Labedoyere!” he exclaimed.

They looked at one another in silence; a bond formed. “Dear friend,” said Servin, “can you save him?”

“I can avenge him.” The stranger’s cry of vengeance—the proscribed soul devoted to Napoleon—moved Ginevra. Though young and handsome, what touched her more was his nobility and suffering. She admired him not only for looks but for soul; curiosity led to pity, pity to deep interest. For the first time a man produced a keen sensation in her. She put his soul on a par with his beauty. Uneasy at her feeling, she gave him a gentle parting smile.

“Tomorrow,” he said sadly; “but tomorrow, Labedoyere—”

Ginevra silenced him with a finger to her lips. In his own language he cried, “Ah! Dio! che non vorrei vivere dopo averla veduta?”—Who would wish to live after seeing her? The accent made Ginevra quiver.

“Are you Corsican?” she cried.

“I was born in Corsica,” he replied; “brought to Genoa young, and when of age I enlisted.”

His beauty, attachment to the Emperor, wound, and misfortunes all blended into a new feeling in Ginevra. He was a child of Corsica, speaking her language; he seemed like a living portrait conjuring childhood memories. At Servin’s invitation he sat on a divan while the painter undid his sling and dressed the long sabre gash across his forearm. The wounded man’s pale face, black hair, and red-and-blue uniform, touched by a soft evening ray, made an artist’s contrast; Ginevra moaned at the gash, but the stranger smiled at her. For a moment she was motionless, dreamy, then blushed, exchanged a soft glance with him, and fled with his image before her eyes.

The next day, though not a class day, she returned to the studio and found the prisoner beside her easel. Servin finished a sketch while the two talked chiefly in Corsican. The soldier related the sufferings of the retreat from Moscow; at nineteen he had crossed the Berezina, and was almost the last of his regiment. He described Waterloo with fiery words. Brought up as a Corsican, Ginevra was true and unfeigned; she surrendered to impression without Parisian coquetry. She often sat with brushes in hand without touching paint, eyes fixed on the officer, listening as if painting an unseen stroke. He seemed to her as a celestial messenger whose speech conjured her native land. They agreed that on class days, when they could not meet, she would signal important political events by singing certain Corsican melodies.

Soon gossip began. Mademoiselle Thirion, speaking under pledge of secrecy, told the class that Ginevra had a lover who hid in the garret. The girls listened to her songs and watched her glances; when she thought herself unseen, they peered through cracks. By the end of a week, Laure was the only pupil who had resisted spying; she still defended Ginevra. Mademoiselle Roguin tried to make Laure wait so she could surprise them on the staircase, but Laure refused to condescend to such espionage and became an object of reprobation.

Thirion declared it improper to attend classes with a painter tainted by patriotism and Bonapartism; she stopped coming. Though she forgot Ginevra, the gossip had sown its seeds. One by one the girls told their mothers of the strange goings-on. Matilde Roguin and others ceased to come until only Ginevra and Laure remained in the deserted studio.

Ginevra, absorbed in methods of communicating with Luigi, did not notice the falling-off. Though she admired genuine characters who kept their political faiths, she urged Luigi to accept royal authority to be released and remain in France; he refused. Their friendship deepened rapidly—adversity revealing character—and in a month it surpassed many ordinary friendships. Ginevra, older than Luigi, found charm in being courted by a youth both tried by fate and graceful; Luigi adored being under the protection of a woman of twenty-five. Their love grew quietly, without need of proclamation.

One evening Ginevra heard their signal: a pin scratch on the woodwork. She glanced around, saw Laure, and opened the door; Luigi, seeing Laure, retired. Surprised, Ginevra went to her easel.

“You are staying late,” she said to Laure. “That head seems finished.”

Laure, trembling, asked Ginevra to add a few touches so she could carry away something to remember her by. Then, in distress, she revealed she was leaving the studio forever. “The young ladies say you have some intrigue,” she said. “My mother insists I must go. Madame Roguin told her falsehoods at a ball. Mamma scolded me for not telling her everything. Oh, my dear Ginevra! I cannot be your companion.”

Ginevra was struck. Laure added that Madame Roguin and her mother were coming to reproach Monsieur Servin the next day; “hadn’t you better warn him?”

Servin entered then, announcing his picture was finished and being varnished. Ginevra confessed that the young ladies had left because of gossip that Luigi was shut up in the attic and that Servin aided their love. The painter laughed.

“My reputation? Why, my picture will make it at the Exposition,” he said. “Those mothers are foolish; had they come to me I would have explained. But I don’t care a straw! Life is short.” Luigi, who had heard part of the conversation, came in. “You have lost all your scholars,” he cried. “I have ruined you!”

Servin took Luigi’s hand and Ginevra’s and, with fatherly mock solemnity, “Marry one another, my children,” he said. Their silence was the first avowal of love. “You will be happy,” he went on; “there is nothing like the happiness of two beings bound by love.” Luigi pressed his protector’s hand. Servin joked that he blessed and wed them, and laughter dissolved the strain.

Then Servin warned them that all would not be so easy—Madame Servin was a little straitlaced. Ginevra, proud and practical, suggested a remedy: Luigi would be sent into royal service, and she would petition a powerful man at the War ministry—the daughter of the Baron di Piombo would be refused nothing. They would obtain a tacit pardon for Captain Luigi; perhaps not his rank as major, but sufficient to justify Servin to the mothers. Servin cried, “You are an angel!”

Meanwhile Ginevra’s parents grew anxious at her delay. “It is six o’clock, and Ginevra not yet home!” cried Bartolomeo; his wife shared his fret. Bartolomeo, over seventy-seven but vigorous, paced the salon. Despite age he retained erect carriage and a stern, Napoleonic demeanor. His face, seamed with deep wrinkles, had a noble expression; his eyebrows and manners retained the severity of an old Republican faithful to the Empire. In his political life he had been feared; he possessed no great fortune—an income of about thirty thousand francs—and owed his barony to the Emperor’s need. He had resigned after the first Bourbon restoration and ceased wearing the Legion of Honor; with the second fall of Napoleon his views hardened. Even in retirement, living frugally in the Portenduere mansion he had purchased for a moderate price, Bartolomeo and his wife treasured their sole child above all.

The old baron and his wife lived simply, their grand apartments austere, the furniture antiquated. Under the Empire Bartolomeo had maintained a large establishment; after his resignation he lived quietly. For them, Ginevra’s presence was everything. They often spent evenings listening to her sing; small domestic intimacies gave them delight. Ginevra, like them, shared political passions; their undivided mutual love was both their strength and their weakness.

Ginevra’s character echoed her father’s: unyielding, vindictive, and passionate. Bartolomeo had encouraged these feelings, teaching her a kind of savage ardor; as a child she forgave him nothing, and she acquired a habit of ruling her parents. Yet in later years she had grown wiser; her faithfulness, devotion, and good sense tempered her temper. Still, being treated as an equal by her parents had left a certain dangerous independence.

Both parents had allowed her to study freely; she flitted among subjects until painting became her dominant passion. Had her mother guided and harmonized her gifts she might have been nobler; lacking that, she retained defects from her training. For five years, however, she had avoided scenes; her devotion to her parents was absolute.

Bartolomeo, restless, rang the bell and ordered a servant to go meet Ginevra. “I always regret our carriage on her account,” the baroness murmured; Ginevra had declined it. The baron buttoned his blue coat, seized his hat and cane, and departed. The porte-cochere opened and shut; then Ginevra’s steps sounded in the courtyard. Bartolomeo reappeared almost instantly carrying his daughter, who struggled in his arms.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!” Ginevra was set down respectfully and nodded to her mother, whose pale face brightened at the sound. Piombo rubbed his hands in satisfaction; his stern features softened into kindness. The two old people seemed revived.

“Now, to dinner!” cried the baron, offering his hand to his daughter. Piombo teased her about lingering at the studio, implying painting ranked above family. Ginevra, after a pause, admitted she was “very much occupied.” When her mother fretted at her paleness, Ginevra answered with resolution: “No! it shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie.” Then, lowering her lids as if to veil her fire, she disclosed, “I love a young man.”

Piombo sneered, “Is he a prince?” She answered gently that he had no fortune, that he was unfortunate—“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed, without refuge; Servin concealed him.” The mother praised Servin; Piombo scolded his daughter for loving any man but him.

Ginevra insisted love is not a choice. She had borne his absence on missions with courage and never reproached his Napoleon fanaticism, but she would not sacrifice seeking a husband who would protect and cherish her. When Piombo accused her of ingratitude and egotism, she replied tenderly: “Heaven is my witness that no girl has fulfilled her duty to her parents better than I. I have never felt anything but love and happiness where others see obligation.” Still, she asked, “Is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me hereafter?”

Piombo swung between menace and pleading: no man could love her as a father did; if this man truly loved her, he would kill him—if not, Piombo would. Ginevra stood firm: “No, you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. But love her a little for her own sake. If you knew how he loves me! Ah! He would never make me unhappy!” Piombo, choking with fury, declared, “You shall not marry him.” She answered simply, “I shall marry him.”

The baroness begged for calm; the baron pleaded with her to spare him the sight of her marriage. Ginevra, unshaken, said refusing an unjust parental command is not disobedience and that forbidding her would make her hate them. When he charged that she did not love them, she denied it, but he still demanded she choose between the family’s ancient vendetta and the man she loved. “My choice is made,” she said.

For a week Ginevra shifted her mood—extra affectionate, playing nocturnes, flattering and jesting—to win his consent while quietly pressing Luigi’s cause. The mother finally signaled success: she had persuaded her husband to receive Luigi.

That day Luigi came out of hiding and returned on Ginevra’s arm. The Duc de Feltre had restored Luigi’s name to the roll of officers; though brave in battle, the young man trembled at meeting Piombo. Ginevra took his tremor as proof of love.

Bartolomeo sat in his arm-chair and would not rise. Ginevra introduced Luigi as a soldier who had fought at Mont-Saint-Jean. The baron glanced at him and sneered, “Monsieur is not decorated.” Luigi replied he no longer wore the Legion of Honor. Madame Piombo remarked a resemblance to someone they had known in Corsica—“Nina Porta”—and Luigi answered simply, “Nina was my sister.” At that name Bartolomeo rose, tottered, beckoned his wife, and the two old people, supporting each other, left the room, abandoning their daughter.

Left alone, bewildered by their abrupt departure, Ginevra learned the family vendetta might doom the union. Luigi trembled but reassured her with calm resolve; she took comfort in his composure and counted their love blessing enough. They left only when she had seen him safely to the room Servin had found.

Returning home she found her mother weeping. Dinner passed in silence; Piombo sat brooding. He said bluntly, “Ginevra, you love the enemy of your family.” She answered, “That is true.” He demanded she choose: one who does not share his vengeance is not of his family. She said, “My choice is made.” He urged that Luigi’s father had killed her brother; she answered that Luigi was six at the time and not guilty, that she had never been taught to hate all Portas, and that no resemblance of lineage could make Luigi a monster. Piombo raged that a Porta would never be his son; “I am Bartolomeo di Piombo,” he cried, brandishing a dagger and adding darkly, “Corsicans explain themselves to God.” Ginevra calmly answered, “And I am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta. You are a tyrant, my father.”

After this she affected coolness—playing the piano, singing nocturnes—trying to triumph by normality over his bitterness. But Piombo hardened and kept his distance. He closed the gates and forbade her leaving; for five days a chambermaid—illiterate—served as go-between, carrying letters between the lovers. Both father and daughter nursed rancor; mutual pride and Corsican blood made compromise impossible.

On Ginevra’s birthday the baroness, despairing, tried to reconcile them. They sat in Bartolomeo’s study when a servant announced two notaries with witnesses. Roguin, the senior notary, explained the “respectful summons” by which an adult daughter could seek marriage despite parental non-consent, urging paternal conciliation to avoid scandal. Piombo’s face darkened into a tigerish expression. Roguin’s legal pedantry—“it is usual…paternal resistance being null…after a final remonstrance to leave her at liberty”—inflamed him.

When asked whether French laws destroyed paternal authority and tore a daughter from her father, the notary answered in cool legal forms. Piombo’s suppressed rage surged. He sprang for the dagger above the mantel and rushed toward his daughter. The younger notary and a witness flung themselves before her and were knocked down; Piombo’s face was aflame and his eyes shot lightning.

Ginevra knelt slowly. “No, no! I cannot!” he cried and flung the weapon away; it buried itself in the wainscot. Ginevra implored him on her knees: “Well, then! have mercy! have pity! You hesitate to be my death and refuse me life! Oh! father, never have I loved you as I do at this moment; give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees: a daughter can humble herself before her father. Luigi, give me my Luigi, or I die!” Shaken, she lay between life and death.

Bartolomeo, still thunderous, shoved her roughly away. “Go,” he said. “The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. I have not the strength to curse you, but I cast you off; you have no father. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he added, pressing his heart. Then, after a pause, he took her arm and silently put her out of the house.

Ginevra rushed to Luigi’s lodging. “Luigi!” she cried, “we have no other fortune than our love.” He answered, “Then am I richer than the kings of the earth!” She told him they had been cast off. He vowed to replace their love: “I will love you in place of them.” She replied with bitter, determined gaiety: “Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after Ginevra was driven from her father’s house she sought refuge with Madame Servin until the legal period before her marriage to Luigi.

Madame Servin received her coldly, annoyed by the scandal Ginevra’s affair had caused her husband, and warned she could not be relied upon afterward. Too proud to insist yet stunned by such selfishness, Ginevra took a room in the lodging-house nearest Luigi’s. The son of the Portas spent his days at his future wife’s feet; his youthful love and pure words dispelled the banished daughter’s gloom. The future he painted was so beautiful she finally smiled again, though she never forgot her father’s severity.

One morning the lodging-house servant brought trunks and packages of linens, clothes, and articles necessary for a young wife’s home. In this welcome provision Ginevra recognized her mother’s foresight, and among the gifts found a purse of money the baroness had gathered and a letter imploring her to forego the fatal marriage if possible. It had cost untold difficulty to send these few things; the mother begged her not to think her hard if she must now abandon her to want, feared she could not assist again, yet blessed and prayed for her happiness if she persisted. Tears had effaced some words.

“Oh, mother!” cried Ginevra, deeply moved.

She felt the impulse to return home, to fling herself at her father’s feet, to see her mother. She was springing forward when Luigi entered. At his sight her filial emotion vanished; her tears stopped, and she no longer had the strength to abandon that loving and unfortunate youth. To be the sole hope of one so noble, to love him and then desert him—such a sacrifice young hearts cannot commit. Ginevra had the generosity to bury her grief silently.

The marriage day arrived. Ginevra had no friend with her. While she dressed, Luigi fetched the witnesses needed for the certificate: a cavalry sergeant, indebted to Luigi from the battlefield, and a master-mason who owned the house the young couple had rented. Each brought a friend, and the four, with Luigi, escorted the bride. Little used to social functions and seeing only business, they were plainly dressed; nothing denoted the usual joy of a marriage procession.

Ginevra herself was simply dressed; yet her beauty was so noble that the witnesses’ customary greetings died on their lips. They bowed with respect; she returned the bow; they looked at her with admiration. This reserve cast a chill over the entire party. Joy bursts forth freely among equals; thus chance made all dull and grave around the bridal pair. Nothing outwardly reflected the happiness within their hearts.

The church and the mayor’s office being near, Luigi and Ginevra, followed by the four witnesses, walked the short distance with a simplicity that stripped pomp from the greatest social event. At the mayor’s court-yard they found a crowd of carriages and two other wedding-parties impatiently awaiting their turn.

Ginevra sat beside Luigi at the end of a long bench; their witnesses remained standing for want of seats. Two brides, elaborately dressed in white with ribbons, laces, and pearls, crowned with orange-blossoms, were surrounded by joyous families; all faces reflected happiness. Fathers, witnesses, brothers, and sisters moved like a happy swarm. Each seemed impressed with the importance of the moment when the heart sits between two hopes—the wishes of the past, the promises of the future.

As she watched them, Ginevra’s heart swelled; she pressed Luigi’s arm and gave him a look. A tear rolled from the young Corsican’s eyes; never did he so plainly understand the joys his Ginevra was sacrificing to him. That tear caused her to forget all else but him—even the abandonment in which she sat. Love poured down its light upon their hearts; they saw nothing but themselves amid the joyous tumult; they were there alone in that crowd, as likely they would be in life. Their witnesses, indifferent, conversed quietly on their own affairs.

Then they walked round the hall.

“How one loses time here,” said the mason, replacing a thick silver watch in his fob.

Luigi and Ginevra, sitting pressed to one another, seemed like one person. A poet would have admired their two heads inspired by the same sentiment, colored in the same tones, silent and saddened amid that humming happiness sparkling in diamonds and gay with flowers—a gaiety with something fleeting. The loud, splendid groups showed their joy; that of Ginevra and Luigi was buried in their bosom. On one side tumult, on the other the delicate silence of happy souls.

Ginevra, superstitious by nature, saw in this contrast an omen and felt a fear as invincible as her love.

Suddenly the office servant opened a folding-door. Silence reigned, and his voice called Monsieur Luigi da Porta and Mademoiselle Ginevra di Piombo. The celebrity of the bride’s name attracted attention, and spectators seemed to wonder that the wedding was not more sumptuous. A murmur increased; a general whisper recalled the absence of her parents; her father’s wrath seemed present.

“Call in the families,” said the mayor to the clerk who read the certificates.

“The father and mother protest,” replied the clerk, phlegmatically.

“On both sides?” inquired the mayor.

“The groom is an orphan.”

“Where are the witnesses?”

“Here,” said the clerk, pointing to the four men, who stood with arms folded like statues.

“But if the parents protest—” began the mayor.

“The respectful summons has been duly served,” replied the clerk, and he laid before the mayor the papers annexed to the certificate.

This bureaucratic decision had something blighting; in a few words it contained the whole story. The hatreds of the Portas and the Piombos and their terrible passions were inscribed on this page of civil law as annals of a people are engraved on a tombstone. Ginevra trembled. Like a dove on the waters with no resting place but the ark, she could take refuge only in Luigi’s eyes amid the cold waste around her.

The mayor assumed a stern air, and his clerk looked on with malicious curiosity. No marriage was ever so little festal. Deprived of accessories, it became a simple legal act, great only in thought.

After a few questions, to which the bride and bridegroom responded, and a few words mumbled by the mayor, and after signing the registers with their witnesses, Luigi and Ginevra were made one. Then the wedded pair walked back through two lines of joyous relations who did not belong to them, whose only interest in their marriage was the delay it caused to their own weddings. When Ginevra at last found herself in the mayor’s court-yard, under the open sky, a sigh escaped her.

“Can a lifetime of devotion and love suffice to prove my gratitude for your courage and tenderness, my Ginevra?” said Luigi.

At these words, spoken with tears of joy, the bride forgot her sufferings; she had endured to appear before the public for a happiness her parents refused to sanction.

“Why should others come between us?” she said, with the artless feeling that delighted Luigi.

A sense of accomplished happiness lightened their step; they hurried to the church. In a dark little chapel in one corner of the building they stood before a plain, undecorated altar, and an old priest married them. There, as in the mayor’s office, other marriages were taking place. The church, filled with friends and relations, echoed with carriages and the hum of beadles and priests. Altars glowed with sacramental luxury; wreaths of orange-flowers crowned the Virgin; flowers, incense, gleaming tapers, and embroidered cushions were everywhere. When it came to place over Luigi and Ginevra the symbol of eternal union—the white satin yoke—the priest looked in vain for the acolytes whose place it was to perform that joyous function; two of the witnesses fulfilled it. The priest gave a hasty homily on life’s perils and duties, with an indirect reproach to Ginevra for her parents’ absence; then, after uniting them before God as the mayor had before the law, he left the couple.

“God bless them!” said Vergniaud, the sergeant, to the mason at the church porch. “No two creatures were ever more fitted for one another. The parents are foolish. I don’t know a braver soldier than Colonel Luigi. If the whole army had behaved like him, ‘l’autre’ would be here still.”

This blessing from the old soldier, the only one bestowed on their marriage-day, shed a balm on Ginevra’s heart.

They parted with hearty handshakes; Luigi thanked his landlord.

“Adieu, ‘mon brave,’” he said to the sergeant.

“I am now and ever at your service, colonel—soul, body, horses, and carriages; all that is mine is yours.”

“How he loves you!” said Ginevra.

Luigi hurried his bride to the house they were to occupy. Their modest apartment was soon reached; and there, when the door closed upon them, Luigi took his wife in his arms, exclaiming,—

“Oh, my Ginevra! for now you are mine, here is our true wedding. Here,” he added, “all things will smile upon us.”

Together they toured the three rooms of their lodging. The first served as salon and dining-room; on the right was a bedchamber; on the left a large study Luigi had arranged for his wife; in it she found easels, colors, lay-figures, casts, pictures, portfolios—in short, an artist’s paraphernalia.

“So here I am to work!” she said with childlike happiness.

She looked long at the hangings and furniture, turning again and again to thank Luigi, for there was something approaching magnificence in the little retreat. A bookcase held her favorite books; a piano filled an angle. She sat on a divan, drew Luigi to her side, and said, caressing his hand,—

“You have good taste.”

“Those words make me happy,” he replied.

“But let me see all,” said Ginevra, to whom Luigi had made a mystery of the adornment.

They entered the nuptial chamber, fresh and white as a virgin.

“Oh! come away,” said Luigi, smiling.

“But I wish to see all.”

The imperious Ginevra inspected every piece of furniture with the minute care of one admiring wedding treasures; she touched the silken hangings and surveyed each article with the artless satisfaction of a bride.

“We begin by ruining ourselves,” she said half-joyfully, half-anxiously.

“True! for all my back pay is there,” replied Luigi. “I have mortgaged it to a worthy fellow named Gigonnet.”

“Why did you do so?” she asked reproachfully, though inwardly pleased. “Do you believe I should be less happy in a garret? But,” she added, “it is all charming—and it is ours!”

Luigi looked at her with such enthusiasm that she lowered her eyes.

“Now let us see the rest,” she cried.

Above these three rooms, under the roof, were Luigi’s study, a kitchen, and a servant’s room. Ginevra was pleased with her little domain, though the view from the windows was limited by a high neighboring wall, and the court-yard, from which their light came, was gloomy. But the two lovers were so happy that hope adorned their future, and they chose to see nothing but charm in their hidden nest. In that vast house, lost in the immensity of Paris, they were like two pearls in their shell; to others it might seem a prison; to them it was paradise.

The first days of their union were given to love. The effort to work at once was too difficult; they could not resist their passion. Luigi lay for hours at his wife’s feet, admiring the color of her hair, the moulding of her forehead, the purity and whiteness of the arches beneath which she turned her eyes—expressions of the happiness of satisfied love. Ginevra caressed Luigi’s hair, never weary of gazing at his blazing beauty and the delicacy of his features. She was constantly charmed by the nobility of his manners; he in turn was enraptured by her grace.

They played together like children with small things that kept returning them to love—then fell into reveries. An air sung by Ginevra reproduced to their souls the lights and shadows of their passion. Together, uniting their steps as they did their souls, they roamed the country, finding their love everywhere—in flowers, in the sky, in the glowing tints of sunset; they read it in the capricious vapors that met and struggled in the ether. Each day differed from the last; their love increased because it was true. They tested each other and instinctively recognized that their souls were akin, with inexhaustible riches promising unceasing joys.

Their love was artless and entire, with interminable conversations, unfinished speeches, long silences, reposes and ardor. Luigi and Ginevra comprehended love. Love is like the ocean: superficial souls find monotony; privileged beings can pass their lives admiring it and finding ceaselessly varying phenomena that enchant them.

Soon, however, prudence and foresight drew the young couple from their Eden; they had to work to live. Ginevra, talented in imitating old paintings, began copying and soon found customers among picture-dealers. Luigi, whose talents had lain in strategy, sought long and actively for work in Paris, but it was hard for a young officer.

At last, weary of vain efforts and seeing the whole burden fall upon Ginevra, he made use of his excellent handwriting. With persistency he went among lawyers and notaries asking for papers to copy. His frankness and situation interested many; he soon obtained enough work to need assistants, and this employment gradually became a regular business. The profits of his office and the sale of Ginevra’s pictures gave the couple a competence they were proud of, the fruit of their industry.

This, to the busy pair, was the happiest period of their lives. Days flowed rapidly, filled with occupation and the joys of love. At night, after working all day, they met with delight in Ginevra’s studio. Music refreshed their weariness. No expression of regret or melancholy clouded the young wife’s face; she never complained. She appeared to Luigi with a smile and beaming eyes. Each cherished a ruling thought that would have made them take pleasure in still severer labor: Ginevra worked for Luigi, and Luigi for Ginevra.

Sometimes, in his absence, Ginevra imagined the perfect happiness they might have had if this life of love could have been lived with her parents. The thought made her melancholy; mournful pictures passed like shadows across her imagination—her old father alone, her mother weeping in secret lest the inexorable Piombo perceive her tears. The two white, solemn heads rose before her, and she felt that never again should she see them except in memory. This thought pursued her like a presentiment.

She celebrated the anniversary of her marriage by giving her husband a portrait he had long desired—the portrait of his Ginevra, painted by herself. Never had the young artist done so remarkable a work. Aside from the resemblance, the glow of her beauty, the purity of her feelings, the happiness of love were there depicted by a sort of magic. This masterpiece of her art and joy was a votive offering to their wedded felicity.

Another year of ease and comfort went by. The history of their life may be given in three words: They were happy. No event happened to them of sufficient importance to be recorded.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Early in 1819 the picture-dealers wanted more than copies; competition had made sales difficult. Madame Porta, who admitted neglecting “genre” painting that might have made Ginevra known, tried portrait work but found as many starving artists as herself. Luigi and Ginevra had a little savings, so urgent distress had not yet come.

Toward the end of winter Luigi worked without interruption. Pay for writing had fallen; he could no longer hire help and had to labor longer for the same income. Ginevra had completed several noteworthy paintings, yet dealers bought mostly for names; her canvases had little chance. Offers were scarce.

Love overwhelmed them even as poverty rose like a skeleton within their joy. They hid anxieties: when Ginevra wanted to weep at Luigi’s worn face she redoubled her tenderness; Luigi, concealing his fears, showed yet greater affection. To compensate for fear they intensified words, caresses, and hopes, each deceiving the other by clinging to whatever hope remained.

One night Ginevra woke and missed Luigi. A faint light on the opposite courtyard wall showed him writing in his study; he now waited till she slept to go to his garret and work. Four o’clock struck. Pretending to sleep, she watched him return exhausted; toil and care had already etched his face. She wept and resolved to help. Using a letter of recommendation from Elie Magus she secured an order for coloring lithographs. She painted by day and colored engravings at night, sharing Luigi’s clandestine labor. They entered their bed only to deceive each other; both feigned sleep and rose as soon as the other seemed asleep.

One night Luigi, feverish from fatigue, opened his garret window for air and saw the reflection of Ginevra’s lamp on the opposite wall. He went down softly and surprised her coloring engravings.

“Oh, Ginevra!” he cried.

She rose convulsively and blushed. “Could I sleep while you wear yourself out?” she said.

“But to me alone belongs the right to work in this way,” he answered.

“Could I be idle?” she asked with tears. “When every mouthful we eat costs a drop of your blood? I should die if I could not add my efforts to yours. All should be in common between us: pains and pleasures.”

“She is cold!” Luigi cried in despair and wrapped her shawl closer. They stood at the window, she leaning on his breast, he holding her, as the sky brightened toward dawn.

“See! It is an omen. We shall be happy,” Ginevra said.

“Yes, in heaven,” Luigi replied bitterly. “Oh, Ginevra! you who deserved all the treasures on earth—”

“I have your heart,” she answered.

“Ah! I complain no more!” he said, drawing her close and kissing the face growing thin from hardship. They spoke of the night’s majesty and the hush that had awakened Ginevra. When dawn came he urged sleep.

Their courage seemed briefly rewarded when Ginevra bore a son, “as beautiful as the day.” Motherhood doubled her strength. Luigi borrowed to meet confinement expenses. At first the new burden felt like joy; they delighted in their child. It was their last happiness.

Like swimmers joining to breast a current, they struggled; sometimes apathy resembling death’s sleep overcame them. Soon they sold jewels. Poverty appeared—plain, insistent; it broke their comforts and dried the springs of pride. Want, indifferent to feelings, trampled human sentiments.

Seven or eight months after little Bartolomeo’s birth, it was hard to recognize in the mother nursing her sickly babe the original of the earlier beautiful portrait—the sole remaining ornament. Without fire through a hard winter, Ginevra’s outline was wasted; her cheeks grew white as porcelain, her eyes dulled as if life’s springs were drying. Watching her shrunken, discolored child, her only suffering was his misery; Luigi had no courage to smile.

“I have wandered over Paris,” he said one day. “I know no one; can I ask strangers? Vergniaud, my old sergeant, is imprisoned in a conspiracy and has lent all he could. As for our landlord, he has not asked rent in over a year.”

“But we are not in want,” replied Ginevra gently, forcing calm.

“Every hour brings new difficulty,” Luigi answered, terror in his voice.

Another day Luigi sold Ginevra’s pictures, her portrait, and household items for a miserable sum, prolonging their agony. In this wretchedness Ginevra displayed the sublimity of her nature and resignation.

Stoically she bore misery’s blows; her strong spirit upheld her. She worked beside her dying son with unfaltering hand, performed household tasks with amazing activity, and sufficed for all. She was gladdened to see Luigi’s surprised smile at the cleanliness she produced in their one poor room.

“Dear, I kept this bit of bread for you,” she said one evening when he returned, worn out.

“And you?”

“I? I have dined, dear Luigi; I want nothing more.”

The beseeching tenderness in her face urged him more than words to take the food she denied herself. Luigi kissed her with one of those despairing kisses like friends mounting a scaffold. In such moments two beings see each other heart to heart. Realizing suddenly that his wife was starving, Luigi was seized by the same fever consuming her. He shuddered and went out, pretending business called, for he would rather have drunk poison than save life by eating the last morsel of bread in their home.

He wandered wildly about Paris amid flaunting luxury, hurrying past glittering windows. At last he resolved to sell himself as a substitute for military service, hoping the sacrifice would save Ginevra and that her father would take her home during his absence.

He went to an agent and found an old officer of the Imperial guard. “It’s two days since I have eaten,” he said slowly. “My wife is dying of hunger and has never complained; she will die smiling, I think. For God’s sake, comrade,” he added bitterly, “buy me in advance; I am robust; I am no longer in the service, and I—”

The officer gave Luigi a sum on account. The wretched man laughed convulsively as he grasped the gold and ran breathless home, crying, “Ginevra! Oh, my Ginevra!”

It was almost night when he reached their room. He entered softly, fearing to shock his wife, whom he had left so weak. The last rays of sun faded from Ginevra’s face as she sat sleeping in her chair, holding her child on her breast.

“Wake, my dear one,” he said, not seeing the infant, who at that moment shone with a strange pallor.

Hearing his voice, the poor mother opened her eyes, met Luigi’s look, and smiled; but Luigi cried out in horror; he scarcely recognized his wife, half mad. With a savage gesture he showed her the gold. Ginevra laughed mechanically, then cried in a dreadful voice, “The child, Luigi, he is cold!”

She looked at her son and swooned. The little Bartolomeo was dead. Luigi took his wife in his arms; after laying her on the bed he went out to seek help.

“Oh! my God!” he cried as he met his landlord on the stairs. “I have gold, and my child has died of hunger, and his mother is dying, too! Help me!”

Neighbors came to gather what things the humbled couple had hidden. Luigi had cast his gold upon the floor and knelt by the bed. Ginevra, delirious, murmured, “Father! take care of my son, who bears your name.”

“Oh, my angel! be calm,” Luigi said, kissing her; “our good days are coming back.”

“My Luigi,” she said, looking at him with extraordinary attention, “listen. I feel that I am dying. My death is natural; I suffered too much; besides, such happiness as mine must be paid for. Yes, Luigi, be comforted. I have been so happy that if I lived again I would accept our fate. I am a bad mother; I regret you more than my child—My child!” she added hollowly.

Two tears escaped her dying eyes, and she pressed the little body she could not warm.

“Give my hair to my father, in memory of his Ginevra,” she said. “Tell him I have never blamed him.”

Her head fell on her husband’s arm.

“No, you cannot die!” cried Luigi. “The doctor is coming. We have food. Your father will take you home. Prosperity is here. Stay with us, angel!”

Her faithful heart, full of love, grew cold. Ginevra turned her eyes to him she loved, though she was conscious of nothing else. Confused images passed before her; memory of earth faded. She knew Luigi was there and clasped his hand tightly as if to hold herself from some precipice.

“Dear,” she said at last, “you are cold; I will warm you.”

She tried to put his hand upon her heart, and died.

Two doctors, a priest, and neighbors came into the room, bringing what might save the poor couple and calm despair. They made some noise entering; afterwards an awful silence filled the room.

While that scene took place, Bartolomeo Piombo and his wife sat in their antique chairs at the corners of a vast fireplace in their salon. A friend had just left; the chair he had occupied remained between the two Corsicans. The clock told midnight. The old couple had lost the ability to sleep; they sat silent, like persons in their dotage, gazing at things they did not see. Their deserted salon, filled with memories, was feebly lit by a single expiring lamp.

Piombo cast glances at the empty chair—glances full of remorse—for it was Ginevra’s seat. Elisa Piombo watched expressions cross her husband’s pallid face; tonight they seemed threatening and melancholy. Would Bartolomeo yield at last to the memories that chair awakened? Had he been struck to see a stranger occupy it, used for the first time since his daughter left? Had the hour of his mercy struck—the hour Elisa had prayed and waited for?

These thoughts shook the mother. For an instant her husband’s countenance became so terrible she trembled at having spoken of Ginevra. The wintry night blew snow sharply against the blinds; the old woman dropped her head to hide tears. Suddenly a sigh burst from the old man; he seemed crushed. She risked speaking—for the second time in three years—of his daughter.

“Ginevra may be cold,” she said softly.

Piombo quivered.

“She may be hungry,” she continued.

The old man dropped a tear.

“Perhaps she has a child and cannot suckle it; her milk is dried up!” said the mother in despair.

“Let her come! let her come to me!” cried Piombo. “Oh! my precious child, thou hast conquered me.”

The mother rose as if to fetch her daughter. At that instant the door opened noisily, and a man, his face no longer human, stood before them.

“Dead! Our two families were doomed to exterminate each other. Here is all that remains of her,” he said, laying Ginevra’s long black hair upon the table.

The old people shook as if struck by lightning.

Luigi no longer stood before them.

“He has spared me a shot, for he is dead,” Bartolomeo slowly said, gazing at the ground.
