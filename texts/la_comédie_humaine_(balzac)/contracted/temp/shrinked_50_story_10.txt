In Paris, balls often split into two parts: a formal round of pleasantries and a later, intimate meeting where wit, genuine conversation, and camaraderie flourish in salons hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches. At one such evening, the Minister de Marsay recounted an early passionate affair with a woman six years his senior: a secret liaison disguised by outward indifference and private signals sent through the flowers she carried. When illness forced him to cancel a meeting, jealousy drove him to visit; he found her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly soothed him, but the sight ignited a storm—an end to innocence and a bleaker view of love. He wrestled with revenge but concluded true love must transcend vengeance. His story stirred the salon, prompting reflection on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

Cured of his cold and disillusioned with pure love, the narrator begins an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty contrasts with a previous “angel.” He avoids quarrels with this clever woman, suspecting that practiced deceit may rival virtue. A visit to a hair artisan in the Rue Boucher, who displays hair mementos and mentions passing fashions, confirms his suspicions when he recognizes a handkerchief the artist has worked on. Convinced of women’s inauthentic affections, he resolves to pursue pleasure skeptically.

Two months later, in the heroine’s boudoir, he tests Charlotte’s devotion. She professes exclusive love and claims he is the only man in her life, yet when he bluntly asks, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrays guilt. He suggests she accept the Duke—rich, devout, and proper. She falls to her knees swearing before God that she loves only him; he presses her, recalling seeing her attend mass with the Duke. She dismisses his jealousy, alternates supplication and defiance, and laments that if he had spoken sooner they might have been happy. He insists she has gone too far and urges her to show devotion to avoid the Duke’s suspicions. When she asks what will become of him, he admits having made advances to another woman. Charlotte accuses him of unfaithfulness; he retorts that women cannot truly be loved in society, likening serious attachment to romanticizing an actress. She denounces his betrayal; he feigns submission and questions whether she will remember their grievances once she is a duchess. Her lingering bitterness convinces him he has successfully distanced himself—he believes he has mastered the art of withdrawal.

In the salon, others sympathize for Charlotte while De Marsay smiles. Conversation shifts to the erosion of social distinctions: Lady Dudley notes marriages aren’t always seen as inconstancy; De Marsay laments the aristocracy’s decline under the Civil Code, which leveled hierarchies and created a new class of women. Vandenesse and Lord Dudley add that unsettled property hastens the fall; Joseph Bridau and Daniel d’Arthez bemoan the loss of women who once shaped society.

They debate the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, poised, and graceful but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Madame de Nucingen argues worth now lies in character and presentation; Blondet defines her as effortlessly elegant, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor. The dialogue highlights modern femininity’s complexity—women who combine charm, beauty, and intellect may compensate for vanished aristocratic splendor or simply reflect a society where merit and presentation replace inherited greatness.

“You will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet began, painting the modern lady with grave satire. Married, she keeps her husband nearly invisible: at a ball a middle-aged man with a decoration bows and goes; she orders her carriage and departs. You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose; you sleep under a golden canopy of a delightful dream that may linger.

At home she admits no one before four and keeps callers waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; luxury is for daily use and regularly renewed—no dusty shades or forgotten wrappings. The staircase is warm, flowers abound—accepted only from chosen people, for they symbolize a promise. Fashionable trifles are everywhere but not museum pieces. She sits by the fire without rising to greet you. At the ball she was a creditor; here she owes you the pleasure of her wit. She seeks a man to widen her circle, an object for her attentions. To draw you into her drawing-room she will be charming. All this shows how isolated women are and why they want a little world to shine in. Conversation there lives on generalities.

De Marsay agreed: the epigram of the age—brief wit—no longer attacks enduring traits but momentary incidents, fading quickly. Blondet continued: a lady’s intelligence, if she has any, lies in doubting everything. Compare two women: the townswoman is virtuous; the lady hesitates and contends with her virtue. This uncertainty is one of the last graces left her. She rarely goes to church but will discuss religion; if you express free thought she will try to convert you, slipping into familiar moral phrases—“For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering, and you deprive it of support. Religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children! Let us not be selfish! Individualism is the disease of the age, and religion is the only remedy”—drifting into a neo-Christian, quasi-political, muddled discourse neither Catholic nor Protestant but very moral.

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire. He told Count Adam the portrait showed that the “perfect lady” embodies intellectual and political confusion, surrounded by flashy but fleeting products of an industry that constantly replaces its output. She feigns ignorance to learn everything, probing your heart while seeming delicately to ask your secrets. There are things she never knows, even when she does; you alone feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart. The great ladies of old flaunted affairs; today’s lady arranges passions like a musical score, with pauses and accents. A weak woman fears to compromise love, husband, or children’s future, for name, position, and fortune no longer shield her. The aristocracy no longer protects. Unlike the great lady of the past, she lacks the power to crush; she must master Jesuitical compromises, moving between propriety and anonymous passion. She keeps servants and appearances in check like an Englishwoman fearing a divorce trial. So free at a ball and charming on a walk, she is a slave at home; she is never independent except in privacy or theoretically. Her task is to preserve her position as a lady.

Blondet added that a woman repudiated by her husband, reduced to a meager allowance without carriage, luxury, or box at the opera, is neither wife, maid, nor townswoman; she is adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites would not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover still want her? That is the question. Thus the perfect lady may provoke calumny but never slander.

“This sketch,” he concluded, “places today’s woman between English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness—a bastard system symptomatic of an age of transition where nothing that emerges truly resembles what has vanished. Transition leads nowhere: everything is a matter of degree; distinction becomes purely personal. I am convinced no woman, even near a throne, can, before twenty-five, acquire the encyclopedic trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the small but important arts—tones, silences, angelic mischief, innocent cunning, speech and silence, wit and obtuseness, diplomacy and ignorance—that made the perfect lady.”

Mademoiselle des Touches asked where the female author would fit. Blondet replied: when she has no genius, she is a woman “comme il n’en faut pas,” casting a look that suggested praise for Camille Maupin; the epigram, he allowed, was not his but Napoleon’s.

Canalis deflected the barb: Napoleon had his literary jealousy, but who can depict him? He was paradox itself: a concentrated force who willed everything, conquering on impulse, a genius for action who spread armed civilization without fixing it. He could do everything because he willed everything; he was a phenomenon of will, conquering illness with battle yet destined to die in bed. He wielded code and sword in his brain, foresaw everything but his fall, was capricious yet decisive, made empires, codes, and even romance. He aimed to make all Europe French and, after changing laws and customs, left things poorer in some ways. He was a man of thought and action who understood his lieutenants, both despot and dispenser of justice when necessary—a true king in times.

De Marsay and others exulted: what a pleasure to digest such conversation; wit’s charms are priceless. Joseph Bridau remarked that conversational flashes, like music, have no second performance. The Princesse de Cadignan, smiling at the women, defended contemporary female greatness. Under a regime that makes everything small, we adopt small dishes and small rooms—does that prove women have become smaller? Passions remain the same. There is beautiful devotion and sublime suffering without the old publicity. Contemporary figures—Marquise d’Espard, Madame Doublet, Madame du Deffant—have their counterparts now; Taglioni matches Camargo; Malibran the old singers. If the Grocers who govern us have stifled a distinct style, the Empire had its own stamp; sciences have not retreated. The Comte de Vandenesse agreed: our age contains many women who, like Madame Récamier, will appear to posterity as finely proportioned as any of the past. He urged that Blondet’s portrait of ephemeral fascinations masks the capacity for heroism. Where feelings are illegal, Providence often overwhelms what seems light.

A playful exchange ensued: Madame de Vandenesse and others teased the Count to preach; the Baronne noted his preaching matched his example. General Montriveau, turning serious, offered a drama of conscience and will—an involuntary cause of a terrible disaster he had witnessed in the 1812 campaign that might interest Doctor Bianchon, since it concerned both body and mind.

He recounted the Beresina retreat. Discipline had dissolved in the mixed, starving army. Wandering from the marshes of Zembin, hungry and without shelter, he found at dusk a wretched Polish farm: a single-room peasant house with a partition for forage. Inside, a few officers and a woman were eating potatoes, horseflesh, frozen beetroots; straw promised a bed. The general, an extraordinary Italian colonel, sat at the table: tall, powerfully built, with abundant black curls, a white complexion, small hands, an aquiline nose, and a temper like a thunderbolt—air that made him both beloved and feared. His force was paradoxical: tender in repose, ferocious in rage.

The colonel had once, in a violent outburst on the road where battery wheels clashed, split an opponent’s skull in seconds—proof of irresistible and terrifying fury. The captain whose skull was split had a Sicilian wife, Rosina, who attracted the colonel’s attention. In the hovel opposite the colonel sat that captain and Rosina. Rosina was dark, thin, sun-browned, still possessing grace despite rags; her husband was a Piedmontese gentleman of ironic simplicity that hinted at cruelty. Conversation at the farmhouse was muted. After eating, the colonel bade goodnight, shot a black look at Rosina and called, “Rosina?” She made an expression that mixed offended dignity and helpless submission; a minute later the colonel called again, more brutally. Rosina rose, passed behind the comrades, and went to him. The company sat in silence; Montriveau, despite himself, laughed, and the others followed.

The husband, perhaps sensing something, accused Montriveau of laughing. Montriveau professed contrition and at once all made themselves to sleep. Next morning they set off, selfishness and misery governing each man. Soon they converged again. From a knoll they saw the farmhouse barricaded and in flames; the roar and a woman’s scream rose above the smoke. The captain approached and, answering their unspoken curiosity, pointed to his chest and waved toward the fire, saying simply, “Son’io” (“It was I”). The group passed without comment. Montriveau left the scene as a terrible emblem of private revolt and the cruel smallness of human behavior in the rout.

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay observed. The image haunted some listeners; Madame de Montcornet feared she would dream of it.

Conversation swung back to lighter jest—Lord Dudley’s quip about English foils, Blondet’s retort—but then de Marsay called on Dr. Bianchon, who had seen a dying woman’s end. Bianchon described, with his usual tenderness, the death of a duchess he and the Duke had watched. Pulmonary consumption in its final stage had reduced her; she had taken the sacrament. The Duke had fallen briefly asleep; waking and seeing his wife, he sat by her side. The dying woman, with a delicate serenity and a pity that seemed boundless, took his hand and murmured, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” She died looking at him. The doctor’s story left a deep, sweet impression.

Mademoiselle des Touches rose, and the scene closed on that lingering note of gentle pathos.

PARIS, June 1839–42.
