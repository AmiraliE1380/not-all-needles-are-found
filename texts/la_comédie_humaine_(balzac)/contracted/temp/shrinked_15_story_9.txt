The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration's mix of religious devotion and worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she upholds a virtuous image to support his political ambitions. Known as "dull weather," the marquis provides a stable home life that aligns with her values.

Though reserved, the Marquise commands respect and skillfully deflects flirtatious advances. Her beauty is subtle, revealing depth when animated. Recently, she danced with Eugène de Rastignac, whose views on women's roles left an impression, though he chose not to pursue her further.

The next morning, Rastignac reflected on her while writing letters, interrupted by his friend Horace. The Marquise received a letter from Rastignac, which intrigued her despite her initial impulse to discard it. She decided to forbid her porter from admitting him and ultimately burned the letter, surprising her maid, Caroline. At a party, she anticipated Rastignac's arrival but felt disappointment when he did not come.

Days later, Rastignac learned he had mistakenly sent a love letter meant for another to Madame de Listomere. When he visited, the marquis invited him in, and Rastignac noticed the Marquise blush, flattering his vanity. Their conversation revealed tensions, with her expressing skepticism about his intentions. Soon after, she withdrew from society, claiming illness, while the marquis attributed her absence to stomach issues, masking her nervous attack and highlighting romantic misunderstandings.
