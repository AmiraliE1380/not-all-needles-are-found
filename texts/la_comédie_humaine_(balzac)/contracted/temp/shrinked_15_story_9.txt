The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade to support his political ambitions. Some women reserve judgment until he becomes a peer, but the marquise’s conduct is steady and principled.

The marquis is notable for his lack of qualities rather than merit: a silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, nicknamed “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature is seen as a safe match for a virtuous woman, incapable of folly.

Despite her reserve, the marquise commands both respect and scorn from men who attempt to flirt; she responds with contempt. She converses with agreeable men without arousing gossip, unlike those who pursue ulterior motives. She does not affect coyness; she is genuinely principled.

The narrator, who admires her, describes her presence as neither plain nor conventionally pretty but possessing a captivating air. Her animated eyes reveal a deeper soul, and she charms without seeking it. This quality appears in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who combines modesty with liveliness.

Rastignac, skilled at social navigation, speaks with the marquise at a ball on topics from opera to women’s duties. Their exchange shows mutual interest, though he abruptly leaves afterward. The next morning Rastignac finds himself preoccupied with thoughts of her. Writing in a comfortable setting, he becomes increasingly absorbed in his feelings until interrupted by his friend Horace, with whom he discusses, among other things, a possible expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac while dressing. It begins with affectionate terms, provoking curiosity; as she reads, her initial impulse to discard it shifts to a desire to understand his sentiments. The letter’s conclusion leaves her fatigued; she reflects on Rastignac’s impertinence and the implications of his words.

This episode captures the tension between her virtuous exterior and the stirrings of emotion within, setting up the complexities of her character and relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to treat him with more than disdain; his recent insolence topped previous offenses she had tolerated. She had thought of keeping a letter he sent but burned it.

"Madame received a fine love-letter," Caroline remarked to the housemaid, who was surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant, expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come, despite intending to confront him with coldness. Stendhal described this mental process as "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters he had been given. Joseph, distracted, finally remembered one was for Madame de Listomere and the other for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned the letter was not for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed, realizing his heart had wandered.

Eugene's mistakes began when he planned to amuse Madame de Listomere by mentioning the blunder. He worsened matters by staying away for several days, allowing her resentment to harden.

When he finally called, the porter said she was out. The Marquis de Listomere, unaware of tensions, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noticed social awkwardnesses.

On seeing Eugene, Madame de Listomere blushed, which he observed. He thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere, absorbed in his newspaper, missed the undercurrent.

Madame de Listomere quickly composed herself. When Eugene asked about her health she assured him she was well. He suggested his visit might be unwelcome and blamed the marquis's introduction. She hinted that her husband did not know all her secrets, revealing the complexity of their relationship.

Eugene tried to lighten the mood, suggesting silence as his best excuse. Madame de Listomere promised to forget, preserving her dignity. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement.

Eugene insisted he meant no offense and that the letter was not for her. Madame de Listomere, while pretending hurt, warned him against further mistakes.

Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted to announce a mutual acquaintance's death. The ensuing talk exposed the marquise's irritation with Eugene's earlier remarks. He naively proposed she might be seeking a secret from him, which increased her curiosity.

When Eugene named Madame de Nucingen, Madame de Listomere's demeanor shifted. She rang for wood—signaling it was time for him to leave—and coldly confronted him about the letter, asking how his pen had written her name. Discomfited, Eugene stammered before departing.

Days later Madame de Listomere confirmed the letter's true recipient and withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition she used to justify seclusion. The episode highlights the complexities of social interaction and misunderstanding in romantic contexts, revealing characters' emotional reactions and social roles.
