The Marquise de Listomere embodies the Restoration spirit, balancing religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she maintains a virtuous facade—perhaps to support his political ambitions. While some women defer judgment until he becomes a peer, the marquise’s conduct remains strictly principled.

The marquis is notable for his lack of qualities rather than any merit. A silent deputy who votes correctly but lacks charisma, he is nicknamed “dull weather.” His unremarkable nature is seen as a safe match for a virtuous woman, since he is incapable of folly.

Despite her reserve, the marquise commands both respect and scorn from men who attempt to flirt; she returns only contempt. She converses with agreeable men without arousing gossip, unlike others who pursue ulterior motives. She does not affect coyness; she is genuinely principled.

The narrator admires her presence: neither plain nor conventionally pretty, she has a captivating air. Her animated eyes reveal a deeper soul, and she charms without seeking it. This quality appears in her recent encounter with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man who combines modesty with liveliness.

Rastignac, adept at social navigation, speaks with the marquise at a ball on topics from opera to women’s duties. Their exchange shows mutual interest, though he abruptly leaves her side afterward. The next morning Rastignac, writing letters in a comfortable room, finds himself preoccupied with thoughts of her and increasingly absorbed in feelings that verge on romance, before his friend Horace interrupts a conversation that ranges as far as a possible expedition to Morea.

While dressing, the marquise receives a letter from Rastignac. It begins with affectionate terms, stirring curiosity about his intent. As she reads, an initial impulse to discard it gives way to a desire to understand his sentiments. The letter’s conclusion leaves her fatigued; she reflects on Rastignac’s impertinence and the implications of his words.

The episode captures the tension between her virtuous exterior and the stirrings of emotion within, setting up the complexities of her character and her relationships.

Madame de Listomere forbade her porter to admit Monsieur de Rastignac and resolved to treat him with more than disdain; his recent insolence exceeded previous offenses she had tolerated. She had considered keeping a letter he sent but ultimately burned it.

"Madame received a fine love-letter," Caroline remarked to the housemaid, who was surprised.

That evening Madame de Listomere went to a party at the Marquis de Beauseant expecting Rastignac; by two in the morning she realized he would not come, despite intending to confront him with coldness. Stendhal called this mental process "crystallization."

Four days later Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters. Joseph, distracted, finally remembered one was for Madame de Listomere and the other for Rastignac's lawyer. When Rastignac learned the letter had not been for Madame de Nucingen, he laughed at his own wandering heart.

Eugène’s errors began when he planned to amuse Madame de Listomere by discussing the mix-up and worsened them by staying away for several days, letting her resentment harden.

When he finally called, the porter said Madame de Listomere was out. The Marquis, unaware of tensions, invited him in. As they climbed the stairs Rastignac noticed several social faux pas. On seeing him, Madame de Listomere blushed; he thought, "What! that fortress, too?" and adjusted his cravat. Monsieur de Listomere remained absorbed in his newspaper, oblivious.

Madame de Listomere quickly composed herself. When Eugène asked after her health she assured him she was well. He suggested his visit might be unwelcome and blamed the marquis's introduction; she replied that her husband did not know all her secrets, hinting at their complicated intimacy.

Eugène tried to lighten the mood, suggesting silence as his best excuse. She promised to forget, maintaining dignity. When he mentioned the letter she feigned offense but could not hide amusement. He insisted he meant no harm and that the letter was not for her; she pretended to be hurt but warned him against further mistakes.

Monsieur de Listomere, still engrossed in his paper, interrupted to announce a mutual acquaintance's death. The ensuing talk exposed the marquise's irritation with Eugène’s earlier remarks. He naively suggested she might be trying to extract a secret from him, which only increased her curiosity.

When he named Madame de Nucingen her demeanor shifted: she rang for wood, signaling his visit should end, and coldly confronted him about how his pen could have written her name. Discomfited, Eugène stammered and departed.

Days later Madame de Listomere confirmed Eugène's claim about the letter's true recipient. She withdrew from society; the marquis explained her absence as a stomach ailment, while her physician diagnosed a nervous condition she used to justify seclusion. The episode underscores the complexities of social interaction and misunderstanding, especially in romantic contexts, and reveals characters' emotional reactions and social roles.
