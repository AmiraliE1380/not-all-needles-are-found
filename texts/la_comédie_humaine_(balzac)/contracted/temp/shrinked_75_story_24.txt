**HONORINE**

**By Honoré De Balzac**

**Translated by Clara Bell**

**DEDICATION**

To Monsieur Achille Deveria

An affectionate remembrance from the Author.

---

The French often dislike traveling, unlike the English, who are known for their love of it. This aversion stems from the belief that while other countries may offer beauty and comfort, the unique charm of France is hard to replicate elsewhere. The essence of French life—its wit, conversation, and cultural nuances—cannot be found abroad, making emigration unappealing to many Frenchmen.

In 1836, two notable Parisians found themselves in a palazzo in Genoa, where they could still feel the essence of Paris. The setting was picturesque, especially after a rain, with the Mediterranean's beauty enhancing the atmosphere. Among the guests at a dinner hosted by the French Consul-General were the celebrated Mademoiselle des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, and her companions, the landscape painter Léon de Lora and the critic Claude Vignon.

Mademoiselle des Touches had traveled to Florence for business and brought Léon along to experience Italy. Their stop in Genoa was a courtesy to the Consul-General, who wished to honor her presence. Despite the allure of the evening, Camille was aware of the scrutiny surrounding her, particularly regarding her talent and femininity.

The Consul-General, a man of striking appearance reminiscent of Lord Byron, had married Onorina Pedrotti, a wealthy heiress. Their union was marked by a deep affection, though the Consul's melancholic demeanor raised questions about his happiness. Onorina, beautiful and devoted, seemed to embody the ideal of Genoese beauty, yet there was an underlying tension in their relationship.

As the evening progressed, the conversation turned to literature and the complexities of women's morality. The Consul-General shared a personal story from his youth, revealing his journey from innocence to the complexities of adult life. He spoke of his uncle, the Abbe Loraux, who had guided him into a career as a secretary for Comte Octave, a prominent statesman.

The Consul's narrative detailed his experiences working for Comte Octave, a man of great intellect and hidden sorrow. Despite his success, the Count was burdened by a profound sadness that stemmed from unfulfilled desires and a life dedicated to public service. The Consul observed the Count's struggles, noting the contrast between his public persona and private anguish.

As the Consul grew closer to the Count, he became increasingly aware of the depths of his patron's character. The Count's life was marked by a commitment to duty, yet he grappled with personal demons that left him emotionally isolated. The Consul's admiration for the Count deepened as he recognized the complexity of his situation—a man revered in society yet tormented by unexpressed passions.

The Consul's reflections on the Count's life revealed a profound understanding of the human condition, illustrating the tension between public duty and private longing. The story culminated in a poignant exploration of love, loss, and the sacrifices made in the pursuit of happiness.

On a Monday, M. le President de Grandville and M. de Serizy met at Comte Octave’s house to discuss a political matter, with me as the secretary. The Count had secured my position as Auditor to the Council of State, and we were tasked with examining important documents. As the meeting extended late into the evening, we were interrupted by a servant informing us that two priests had been waiting for two hours.

Despite initial hesitations, we decided to dine with them. The dinner was enjoyable, filled with engaging stories from the politicians present. My uncle, a priest, contributed witty and refined commentary, while the Abbe Gaudron, a simple man of faith, added innocent curiosity to the conversation. They discussed the societal issue of adultery, highlighting the disparity between civil and religious laws. My uncle criticized the Code for treating adultery as a minor offense, suggesting that it should be addressed more seriously, akin to historical practices where guilty parties faced severe consequences.

The discussion turned to the nature of marriage and the role of women in society. Comte Octave argued that the Church's high regard for women necessitated strict penalties for adultery, but noted that societal practices had evolved, leading to a disconnect between law and morality. He lamented the dual standards of morality, where the world often punished hypocrisy more harshly than the law did.

As the conversation progressed, the three men shared their personal experiences with marriage, revealing their struggles. Comte Octave's reaction to a comment about divorce indicated deep emotional pain, hinting at his troubled marriage. After the priests left, Octave confided in me about his wife’s departure three years prior, revealing the profound impact it had on him. He described her letter announcing her flight, which he found dignified despite the betrayal. 

Octave had lived alone since then, and I sensed a complex drama behind his situation. His suffering was evident, and I felt a deep admiration for him, recognizing the weight of his grief. The mystery surrounding his wife's departure only deepened my concern for him. 

Days passed, and I observed Octave's melancholy, realizing that his pain was not merely a personal loss but a reflection of broader societal issues regarding marriage and happiness. One evening, he finally asked me to stay, signaling a readiness to share more about his struggles.

This is the story of Count Octave and his wife, Honorine. When Octave returned home from college, he found Honorine, his father's ward, a beautiful and innocent girl of sixteen, raised by his mother. She dreamed of happiness, viewing life as a perpetual celebration, untouched by the harsh realities of the world. Their relationship was pure and brotherly until Octave proposed marriage when she turned seventeen. After a brief delay imposed by Octave's mother, they married when he was twenty-six and she was nineteen.

Initially, their marriage was marked by respect for their parents' traditions, but as Octave introduced Honorine to society, he became more of a teacher than a husband. This dynamic led to a gradual erosion of their love, as Octave's authoritative approach stifled Honorine's youthful spirit. Eighteen months after Octave's father died, Honorine left him, driven by an unknown passion or misfortune, leaving behind a farewell letter that shattered Octave.

Despite the pain of her departure, Octave continued to love Honorine deeply, living in her memory and struggling with his feelings. He felt no anger towards her, viewing her as a child who had made a mistake. He recognized that his overwhelming love may have suffocated her, and he blamed himself for not understanding her needs. Octave's love transformed into a desperate longing, and he dedicated himself to finding and protecting Honorine, even as she fell into poverty after being abandoned by her lover.

Octave orchestrated a plan to care for Honorine from a distance, ensuring she had the means to survive while remaining unaware of his involvement. He arranged for her to live modestly, believing she was earning her living through her work as an artificial flower maker. For years, he watched over her, maintaining a façade of indifference while secretly longing for her return.

As time passed, Octave's obsession with Honorine grew, and he became increasingly desperate to reconnect with her. He attempted to write her a letter, but she rejected it without reading it, further deepening his despair. Octave's life became a constant struggle between his duties as a lawyer and his unyielding love for Honorine, which consumed him.

In a twist of fate, Octave enlisted the help of a young man, Maurice, to act as a liaison between him and Honorine. Maurice moved into the gardener's cottage next to Honorine's home, posing as a simple florist. This arrangement allowed Octave to gather information about Honorine's life while maintaining his distance.

As Maurice spent time with Honorine, he became enchanted by her beauty and grace. Despite his initial role as a spy, he found himself drawn to her, and their interactions blossomed into a friendship. Honorine, believing Maurice to be a harmless eccentric, welcomed him into her life, unaware of his true connection to her past.

Their conversations revealed Honorine's struggles and her determination to maintain her independence despite her hardships. She expressed a desire for freedom and a rejection of societal expectations, which resonated with Maurice. As they grew closer, Maurice grappled with his own feelings for Honorine, torn between his loyalty to Octave and his burgeoning affection for her.

The story unfolds against the backdrop of Parisian society, where love, betrayal, and the quest for happiness intertwine. Octave's relentless pursuit of Honorine's heart, coupled with Maurice's growing attachment to her, sets the stage for a complex emotional drama. The narrative explores themes of love, sacrifice, and the consequences of choices made in the name of passion.

Ultimately, Octave's unwavering devotion to Honorine and his desire to protect her from the world's cruelties drive the plot forward. As the characters navigate their intertwined fates, the story delves into the depths of human emotion, revealing the complexities of love and the lengths one will go to for those they cherish.
