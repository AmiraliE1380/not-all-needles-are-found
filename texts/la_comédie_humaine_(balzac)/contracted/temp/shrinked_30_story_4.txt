In late October 1800 a foreigner with a woman and a little girl stood long before the Tuileries, near the ruins where the wing meant to join Catherine de’ Medici’s chateau to the Valois Louvre now begins.

The man stood with folded arms and bowed head, occasionally glancing between the consular palace and his wife, who sat on a stone. Though she seemed occupied with the nine- or ten-year-old child whose long black hair she toyed with, she watched every look her companion gave. Their bond was not mere love; misery—perhaps the strongest tie—drew their anxious attention.

The stranger had a broad, serious head, thick hair like those in Carracci paintings; the jet black was streaked with white. His noble, proud features were marred by a hardness. Despite an erect, strong figure he looked over sixty. His worn clothes were foreign. His wife’s faded, once-beautiful face showed deep sadness, though she forced smiles when he looked. The little girl stood despite weariness, sun-tanned, her Italian cast marked by large black eyes beneath arched brows, native nobleness, and simple grace. Passersby felt moved by the plain despair; when the stranger noticed attention he looked so savage even bold loungers hurried off.

After some time the stranger wiped his face as if to clear his thoughts. He drew a dagger from his breast and gave it to his companion, saying in Italian, “I will see if the Bonapartes remember us.” He walked slowly to the palace entrance, where a consular guard stopped him and refused to permit discussion. The sentinel presented his bayonet; the officer insisted one could not see the First Consul without a written request. The Italian persisted and then addressed the captain: “Let Bonaparte know that Bartolomeo di Piombo wishes to speak with him.”

The officer refused on grounds of regulation. Piombo’s frown charged him with the consequences of refusal, and he stood under the portico. Fortune favored him: a carriage drew up and Lucien Bonaparte, minister of the interior, emerged. Recognizing Piombo, Lucien took him away.

Murat, Lannes, and Rapp were in the First Consul’s cabinet. As Lucien entered with Piombo, conversation ceased. Lucien took Napoleon by the arm and led him to a window recess. Napoleon signaled Murat and Lannes to retire; Rapp lingered, pretending not to see. The First Consul then ordered Rapp out; the aide-de-camp objected, suspicious of Corsicans, but Napoleon insisted on being alone with his compatriot.

“Why did you come?” Bonaparte asked.

“To ask asylum and protection from you, if you are a true Corsican,” Piombo answered roughly. Napoleon probed the misfortunes that drove him from the island. Once rich, Piombo declared, “I have killed all the Portas.” The First Consul stepped back in surprise.

“Do you mean to betray me?” cried Piombo, looking darkly at Bonaparte. “Do you know that there are still four Piombos in Corsica?” Lucien seized his arm, reproaching him: “Did you come to threaten the savior of France?” Napoleon signaled Lucien to be silent and listened.

Piombo told his story: they had been friends with the Portas; the Barbantis reconciled them. The day after they drank to end their quarrels he left for Bastia. The Portas stayed in his house and set fire to his vineyard at Longone. They killed his son Gregorio. His daughter Ginevra and his wife had taken the sacrament that morning and escaped; when he returned his house was ashes. He found Gregorio’s body by accident at night and knew the Portas had done it. He called all the men who had ever served him, and they marched to the Portas’ vineyard. They arrived at five; at seven the Portas were dead. He added that Giacomo said Eliza Vanni saved a child, Luigi, though Piombo himself had bound Luigi to his bed before setting the house on fire. He left the island with his wife and child without learning whether Luigi was alive.

Bonaparte listened with curiosity but not surprise. Lucien asked how many the Portas had been. “Seven,” Piombo replied. “All of them persecuted you in olden times.” Though the brothers had shown no hatred, Piombo reproached the Bonapartes: “Ha! you are no longer Corsicans! Farewell. In other days I protected you.” He added reproachfully that without him their mother would never have reached Marseille. Napoleon stood silent, thoughtful, his elbow on a mantel-shelf.

“At present,” Bonaparte said at last, “I cannot take you under my wing as a matter of duty. I have become the leader of a great nation; I command the Republic; I am bound to execute the laws.” Piombo laughed scornfully. Napoleon continued: “But I can shut my eyes. The tradition of the vendetta will long prevent law’s reign in Corsica. It must be destroyed, at any cost.”

He spoke more personally: “Live here, in Paris. We will know nothing of this affair. I will cause your property in Corsica to be bought to give you enough to live on for the present. Later, we will think of you. But remember, no more vendetta! There are no woods here to fly to. If you play with daggers you must expect no mercy. Here, the law protects all citizens; no one is allowed to do justice for himself.”

Piombo accepted; his Corsican brow unbent and he looked pleasantly about him. “You are not badly off here,” he said, smiling. “You are all in red, like a cardinal.” Bonaparte replied, watching him keenly: “Your success depends on yourself; you can have a palace too. It will often happen that I shall need some faithful friend in whom I can confide.” Joy heaved the Corsican’s chest; he took Napoleon’s hand. “The Corsican is in you still,” he said.

Bonaparte smiled and looked at the man who seemed to bring a breath from the island that had saved him—the land he would never see again. He signaled to his brother, who then took Piombo away. Lucien asked about the family’s finances. Piombo showed his wife and Ginevra sitting on a heap of stones. “We came from Fontainebleau on foot; we have not a single penny,” he said. Lucien gave him his purse and told him to come the next day so arrangements could be made for their comfort. The value of Piombo’s Corsican property, if sold, would scarcely maintain him honorably in Paris, Bonaparte had implied, but he promised immediate assistance and a guarded future.

Fifteen years elapsed between Piombo’s arrival in Paris with his family and the following event, which would be scarcely intelligible without this account of the preceding circumstances.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Servin, one of our distinguished artists, was the first to open a studio specifically for young women wishing to take painting lessons.

He had married, out of inclination, the dowerless daughter of a general. At first the mothers accompanied their daughters; later they sent them alone, reassured by Servin’s principles and the pains he took to deserve their confidence.

He meant to take only young ladies of wealthy, well-placed families to avoid complaints about class composition. He even refused those who wanted to become professional artists, for they required instruction beyond what he offered. Gradually his prudence and skill, the certainty each mother felt that her daughter would be among well-bred companions, and his respectable marriage made him an excellent society teacher. When a young girl wished to learn drawing, friends invariably advised, “Send her to Servin’s.”

Servin thus became a social specialty for feminine art—like Herbault for bonnets, Leroy for gowns, and Chevet for cookery. A girl trained by him could judge the Musée, make a striking portrait, copy an old master, or paint a genre picture. He sufficed for the aristocracy’s educational needs. Yet despite his familiarity with Paris’s best families, he remained independent and patriotic, retaining the easy, brilliant, half-ironical tone and freedom of judgment that mark painters.

He was careful about his studio’s arrangements. The attic above his apartments was accessed by a small spiral stair within his rooms; its entrance from outside was walled up. The studio occupied almost the whole attic under the roof and, contrary to visitors’ expectations of cramped quarters, presented vast, surprising proportions. It was lit from above through enormous panes with green linen shades that artists use to temper the light. Caricatures and heads drawn quickly in color or with a knife, on walls painted dark gray, showed that distinguished young girls, barring expression, have as much fun and folly as men.

A small stove with a long zigzagging pipe, a shelf running round the walls with plaster models mostly covered with gray dust, a Niobe head nailed up in a pose of woe, a smiling Venus, a pauper’s begging hand, a few smoked “écorchés,” pictures and frames in various states—these gave the room that studio physiognomy: jumble and bareness, poverty and riches, care and neglect. An atelier is like an opera’s backstage: ancient garments, gilded armor, fragments of stuff and machinery; yet amid this clutter there is something mysteriously grand—genius confronting death, Diana and Apollo beside a skull, beauty and ruin, colors glowing in shadow. It is the strange symbol of an artist’s head.

At the moment this story begins, a brilliant July sun filled the studio; two rays traced diaphanous gold lines where dust shimmered. A dozen easels rose like masts in a port. Several young girls animated the scene with varied expressions, attitudes, and dress. Strong shadows cast by green serge curtains arranged at each easel produced piquant light-and-shade effects. This group was the prettiest picture in the studio.

A fair young girl, simply dressed, worked at some distance, appearing modest and poor compared to the rest. No one looked at or spoke to her; she seemed the least rich among them. Two principal groups, clearly separated, formed two cliques—two minds even in this place where rank and fortune might seem forgotten.

The girls, sitting or standing amid color-boxes, playing with brushes or preparing them, handling dazzling palettes, painting, laughing, talking, and singing, showed their real selves in a spectacle unknown to men. One, proud and capricious with black hair, cast sharp glances around; another, light-hearted with chestnut hair, smiled thoughtlessly; a third was dreamy, melancholy, pale, like a drooping flower; her tall, indolent neighbor with Asiatic eyes said little and reflected, glancing at Antinous’s head. A jocose girl, witty and arch, watched the rest and made them laugh, seeming to rule the first group—daughters of bankers, notaries, and merchants—rich but conscious of the slights the aristocratic group directed at them.

The aristocratic group was led by Mademoiselle Amélie Thirion, the daughter of one of the King’s ushers: silly, vain, proud of her father’s “office at court,” using an eyeglass and dressing well, always late, and insisting that companions speak low. They included several girls with exquisite figures and distinguished features but little simplicity or candor: elegant maneuvers and polished manners that had early formed character and, through social pleasures, dulled sentiment and bred egotism.

Yet among both cliques there were childlike heads, ravishingly pure, faces with half-open lips, spotless teeth, and virgin smiles; the studio sometimes resembled not a workshop but a group of angels on a cloud.

On this particular day in mid-July 1815, Servin had not appeared. For days he had been at another studio finishing a picture for the Exposition. Suddenly, Mademoiselle Amélie Thirion began to speak low to her neighbor, and a great silence fell on the patrician group; the commercial party likewise fell silent, curious. Amélie rose, took an easel near her, carried it away from the noble group, and placed it by a board partition that separated the studio from the attic’s extreme end, where broken casts, defaced canvases, and winter wood were stored. She rolled aside the stool, the color-box, and even the Prudhon picture her absent pupil was copying. After this coup d’état, the Right resumed work in silence while the Left discoursed at length.

“What will Mademoiselle Piombo say to that?” asked a girl.

“She’s not the sort to speak,” replied Matilde Roguin, of the banking group; “but fifty years hence she’ll remember the insult as if done the night before, and revenge it cruelly. I don’t want to be at war with her.”

“The slight upon her is unkind,” another said, “because yesterday Mademoiselle Ginevra was sad. Her father, they say, has just resigned. They oughtn’t to add to her trouble; she was considerate during the Hundred Days.”

“I have a mind to get Mademoiselle Piombo’s easel and place it next to mine,” Matilde said, then sat down, fearing how Ginevra might take civility.

The steps of someone climbing the narrow stair announced a late arrival. “Here she comes!” went round the room, and an absolute silence followed.

To grasp Amélie Thirion’s ostracism, note that this scene took place toward the end of July 1815. The second return of the Bourbons had rent friendships that had survived the first Restoration. Families, nearly all divided in opinion, renewed many scenes of hatred common to civil war. Children, young girls, and old men shared monarchical fever. Discord and distrust flavored words and deeds, even among intimates.

Ginevra Piombo loved Napoleon to idolatry; how, then, could she be otherwise? The emperor was her compatriot and the benefactor of her father. Baron di Piombo had been one of Napoleon’s devoted servants during the return from Elba. Incapable of denying his political faith and anxious to confess it, the old baron remained in Paris among enemies. Ginevra openly mourned the second Restoration; the only tears she had shed had been at news of Napoleon’s captivity on the Bellerophon and Labédoyère’s arrest.

The studio’s aristocratic girls belonged to the most devoted royalist families in Paris, and the anti-Bonapartist hatred then prevailing explained their action. Ginevra, one of Servin’s earliest pupils, had occupied the place now taken from her since her first day. The aristocratic circle had grouped about her. Driving her from a place that belonged to her personally was not only an insult but an artistic wound; artists have favorite spots where they work.

Political prejudice was not the sole cause. Ginevra, the ablest of Servin’s pupils, drew intense jealousy. The master admired her talents and character; she outshone all comparisons and exercised a prestige like Bonaparte’s over his soldiers. The aristocracy had for days resolved on the fall of this queen; Mademoiselle Thirion’s act was meant to force others to join her hatred; though some of those royalists loved Ginevra sincerely, many were indoctrinated at home and preferred to show aloofness.

On Ginevra’s arrival she met profound silence. Of all the young women at Servin’s, she was the handsomest, tallest, and best made. Her carriage and demeanor had a nobility and grace commanding respect. Her face, instinct with intelligence, seemed to radiate light, inspired by the passion of Corsicans yet calm. Long hair, black eyes and lashes, and softly defined mouth suggested passion but also a kindliness from conscious strength. A marble forehead bore a savage pride and Corsican moral instinct; the rest of her Lombard simplicity was seductive. Her only defect came from mature beauty: she looked like a woman. She had refused marriage out of love for her parents and devoted herself to painting.

“You are very silent today, mesdemoiselles,” she said after advancing among them. “Good-morning, my little Laure.” She spoke gently to a young girl working apart. “That head is strong—the flesh tints a little too rosy, but the drawing is excellent.”

Laure looked tenderly at Ginevra; their mutual affection showed. Ginevra smiled faintly, thoughtful, and walked to her easel, greeting the first group without noticing the stares she provoked. She was like a queen in her court, unaffected by the profound silence of the patricians, and sat before her easel to prepare to work, apparently unaware of the reason for her removal. All heads in the banking group turned toward her. The Thirion camp’s sidelong glances, though not openly impatient, followed her.

“She hasn’t noticed it!” said Mademoiselle Roguin.

Ginevra abandoned her meditative air and glanced toward the aristocrats; she measured their distance at a glance but said nothing.

“It hasn’t occurred to her that they meant to insult her,” Matilde whispered; “she neither colored nor turned pale. How vexed they’ll be if she likes the new place as much as the old!” she added aloud.

The Italian pretended not to hear; perhaps she really did not. Abruptly she walked to the partition that gave on the adjoining closet and examined the sash through which her light came. Mounting a chair, she raised the green serge; at her eye-level was a slight opening in the partition. Her glance through it resembled a miser discovering treasure. She sprang down and, dissatisfied with the light, pushed a table close to the partition, placed a chair on it, climbed up, and looked again through the crevice. She cast but one glance into the space beyond lit by a skylight; what she saw staggered her.

“Take care, Mademoiselle Ginevra, you’ll fall!” cried Laure.

All the girls watched the imprudent climber; fear of discovery gave Ginevra courage. She steadied herself and replied, balancing, “Pooh! it is more solid than a throne!” She secured the curtain, descended, pushed the chair and table away from the partition, returned to her easel, and arranged it to suit the light. But her mind was not on painting; she had settled by the closet door to hear a sound that had excited her curiosity the previous evening. She recognized the firm, regular breathing of a man she had seen asleep. The sight through the crevice had shown the Imperial eagle; upon the pallet, faintly lighted, lay an officer of the Guard.

She guessed at once: Servin was hiding a proscribed man.

She trembled at the danger of some companion discovering him by his breathing or a deeper sound. She resolved to keep her place by that door to prevent some accidental betrayal.

This was why she had seemed indifferent to the move of her easel. The change enabled her to gratify her curiosity, and she was too preoccupied to perceive its social implications.

To young girls, it is mortifying when mischief, insult, or criticism misses its sting because the intended victim shows contempt or indifference. Hatred grows as that enemy is raised above the assailants. Ginevra’s calm puzzled both friends and foes; the few occasions where she had shown vindictiveness made people suspicious.

After many conjectures Mademoiselle Roguin declared the Italian’s silence a grandeur of soul; the banking circle, inspired, formed a plan to humiliate the aristocrats and succeeded in a volley of sarcasm. Madame Servin’s arrival put a stop to the open struggle. Amélie Thirion had noticed Ginevra’s preoccupation and guessed the secret lay beyond simple insult. The vengeance of Roguin’s group on the Thirions forced the ultras to search for the cause of Ginevra’s obstinate silence. The beautiful Italian became the center of all glances and was watched by friends and foes alike.

It is difficult to hide the slightest emotion from fifteen inquisitive young girls whose wits and mischief demand secrets and schemes. Madame Servin entered and, glancing at the door near which Ginevra sat, betrayed uneasiness. “Mesdemoiselles,” she said, “Monsieur Servin cannot come today.” She complimented the girls in turn, her eyes lingering on Ginevra in a way that later confirmed to Amélie that the servant’s look had been charged with doubt, fear, and mystery.

Madame Servin reached Ginevra with an uneasiness she could not conceal. They nodded but said nothing; one painted, the other stood looking at the painting. The soldier’s breathing in the closet could be distinctly heard, but Madame Servin feigned ignorance so obviously that Ginevra recognized willful deafness. The unknown man turned on his pallet.

Ginevra looked fixedly at Madame Servin, who, without changing her face, praised Ginevra’s copy: “Your copy is as fine as the original; if I had to choose between the two I should be puzzled.” Ginevra thought, “Monsieur Servin has not taken his wife into his confidence about this mystery,” but replied with a gentle smile and began to hum a Corsican canzonetta to cover the prisoner’s noise. It was so unusual to hear her sing that all the other girls looked up in surprise; later this act would be used to support hypotheses about jealousy.

Madame Servin went away, and the session ended without further incident. Ginevra let the others depart and seemed to intend to work later, but she showed a desire to be left alone by impatient glances at pupils slow in leaving. Thirion guessed that her rival’s industry hid some purpose; watching, she saw Ginevra listen as if to sounds no one else perceived. Amélie stayed last, went down to Madame Servin’s apartment, spoke briefly, pretended to have left her bag, and ran back. She found Ginevra mounted again on her frail scaffolding, absorbed and unaware of Amélie’s soft steps. Amélie coughed; Ginevra quivered, saw her enemy, blushed, and altered the curtain to give meaning to her position. She left the studio, carrying in memory the image of a man’s head as beauteous as Girodet’s Endymion—a masterpiece she had lately copied.

“To banish so young a man! Who can he be? For he is not Marshal Ney—” These two sentences summarized many ideas Ginevra turned over for two days. On the third day, despite her haste, she found Mademoiselle Thirion already at the studio, having come in a carriage. They observed each other long, making faces impenetrable. Amélie had seen the man’s handsome head but not the Imperial eagles and uniform through the crevice; she was lost in conjecture. Suddenly Servin arrived much earlier than usual.

“Mademoiselle Ginevra,” he said after a glance around, “why have you placed yourself there? The light is bad. Come nearer to the rest and pull down that curtain a little.”

Then he sat near Laure, who received his cordial attention. “Well, well!” he cried; “here is a head extremely well done. You’ll be another Ginevra.” He went from easel to easel, scolding, flattering, jesting—his jests more dreaded than reprimands. Ginevra had not obeyed his order but remained at her post, resolved not to quit it. She took a sheet of paper and sketched in sepia the head of the hidden man. A work done under emotion bears its own mark: passion often substitutes for genius; intuition supplied a momentary, almost supernatural talent. The head was dashed off amid a trembling she mistook for fear, really the fire of inspiration.

From time to time she glanced furtively at companions to hide the sketch if anyone came near. But Amélie, with her eyeglass turned, saw the drawing in a great portfolio and suddenly showed herself. Ginevra hastily covered the sheet.

“Why do you stay there in spite of my advice, mademoiselle?” asked the professor gravely.

Ginevra turned her easel so only the master could see the sketch, placed it upon it, and in an agitated voice said, “Do you not think, as I do, that the light is very good? Had I not better remain here?”

Servin turned pale. Malice had penetrated the scene; Mademoiselle Thirion had become, as it were, a sharer in the sudden emotion of master and pupil.

“You are right,” said Servin, “but really,” he added with forced laughter, “you will soon know more than I do.” He examined the officer’s head with growing excitement. “It is a masterpiece! worthy of Salvator Rosa!” he exclaimed with the energy of an artist.

All the pupils rose, and Mademoiselle Thirion darted forward with the velocity of a tiger. At that instant the prisoner, perhaps awakened by the noise, stirred. Ginevra knocked over her stool, uttered incoherent words, and began to laugh; she had thrown the portrait into her portfolio before Amélie could reach it. The easel was surrounded; Servin extolled his favorite’s copy, and the whole class was deceived by the stratagem—except Amélie, who slipped behind companions and tried to open the portfolio where she had seen the sketch. Ginevra took it up without comment and placed it before her; the two girls looked at each other fixedly and silently.

“Come, mesdemoiselles, take your places,” said Servin. “If you wish to do as well as Mademoiselle di Piombo, you mustn’t be always talking fashions and balls, trifling your time away.”

When they were reseated, Servin sat beside Ginevra. “Was it not better that I should be the one to discover the mystery rather than the others?” she asked in a low voice.

“Yes,” replied the painter, “you are one of us, a patriot; but even if you were not, I should have confided the matter to you.”

Master and pupil understood one another, and Ginevra no longer feared to ask: “Who is he?”

“An intimate friend of Labédoyère, who did more than any other, except the unfortunate colonel, to unite the 7th regiment with the grenadiers of Elba. He was a major in the Imperial Guard and was at Waterloo.”

“Why not have burned his uniform and shako and given him citizen’s clothes?” asked Ginevra impatiently.

“He will have them tonight.”

“You ought to have closed the studio for some days.”

“He is going away.”

“Then they’ll kill him,” said the girl. “Let him stay here with you till the storm is over. Paris is still where a man can be hidden safely. Is he a friend of yours?”

“No; he has no claim on me but ill luck. He came into my hands by my father-in-law, who returned to the army during the campaign and cleverly rescued him from those who captured Labédoyère. He came here to defend the general—a foolish fellow!”

“Do you call him that!” cried Ginevra, astonished at the painter’s coolness.

“My father-in-law is too closely watched to keep him at home,” Servin continued. “So he brought him to me by night about a week ago. I hoped to keep him out of sight in this corner, the only place where he could be safe.”

“If I can be useful, employ me,” said Ginevra. “I know the Maréchal de Fétre.”

“Well, we’ll see,” Servin answered.

Their conversation had lasted long enough that other girls noticed. Servin left Ginevra, went round giving long lessons, and was still there at the hour pupils usually left. “You are forgetting your bag, Mademoiselle Thirion,” he called to the girl now compelled to spy.

The baffled Amélie returned for her bag, feigning surprise at her carelessness; Servin’s act only confirmed a mystery’s existence and importance to her. She noisily ran down the staircase and slammed the door into the Servins’ apartment to give the impression she had gone; then she softly returned and stationed herself outside the studio door, resolved to learn more.

When the painter Servin and Ginevra believed themselves alone, the garret door opened on rusty hinges to reveal a tall, well-made young man in an Imperial uniform, one arm in a sling and his face pale with suffering. He recoiled at seeing a woman. Amelie, unable to stay with the door shut and frightened, slipped away after hearing it open.

“Fear nothing,” said the painter to the officer. “Mademoiselle is the daughter of a faithful friend of the Emperor, the Baron di Piombo.”

At once the soldier’s doubts about Ginevra’s patriotism vanished. Ginevra noticed his wound and he replied it was healing. From the street came the vendors’ cry: “Condemned to death!” The soldier heard a name and turned pale.

“Labedoyere!” he cried, and sank on a stool. Sweat gathered on his forehead; he seized his hair in despair and rested his elbow on Ginevra’s easel. After a moment’s silence he rose abruptly.

“Labedoyere and I knew what we were doing. We were sure of our fate, whether triumph or defeat. He dies for the Cause, and here I am hiding!” He rushed to the door; Ginevra reached it first and barred him.

“Can you restore the Emperor?” she asked. “Do you expect to raise the giant who could not keep himself?”

“What can I do?” the young man retorted. “I have no relations. Labedoyere was my protector; without him I am alone. Tomorrow they may condemn me; my only fortune was my pay. I spent my last penny to try to snatch Labedoyere from fate; death is for me a necessity. When a man decides to die he should know how to sell his life to the executioner. I thought the life of an honest man is worth that of two traitors; a well-placed dagger could give immortality.”

This outburst alarmed Servin and even Ginevra, who perceived the young man’s temperament. She admired his handsome face and soft voice, still fervent even in anger, and tried to soothe him.

“Monsieur,” she said, “for your money troubles, accept my savings. My father is rich; I am his only child. He loves me and will not blame me. Our property is from the Emperor; we own nothing not due to his munificence. Is it not gratitude to help his faithful soldiers? Take what you need—money is nothing.” Then she added, proudly, “As for friends—those you shall have.”

She added with conviction that the head that fell before muskets would save his, that he should wait till the storm passed and then escape to foreign service if necessary. There is always a particular delicacy in a woman’s consolation: maternal, foresightful, complete. Spoken gently by a beautiful benefactress, hope softens a man. The prisoner breathed in love through all his senses; a faint color returned to his pale cheeks and his eyes lost some sadness.

“You are an angel of goodness,” he said. “But Labedoyere—” he burst out.

At that cry they all fell silent, each feeling the unity of their thought. They were no longer mere acquaintances; the thin threads of circumstance quickly wove a bond between them. Servin asked if he could save Labedoyere.

“I can avenge him,” the stranger answered. Ginevra quivered. Though the man’s looks moved her, what touched her more was pity for suffering that was not ignoble; the cry of vengeance, devotion to Napoleon, Corsican generosity—these stirred her deeply. She looked at him with respect that moved his heart. For the first time a man had produced a keen sensation in her. Curiosity and pity became powerful interest; she felt such profound sensations she grew uneasy at staying.

“Until tomorrow, then,” she said, giving him a gentle parting smile. The smile lit her face anew and the stranger forgot his torment for a moment.

“Tomorrow,” he said sadly; “but tomorrow, Labedoyere—” She put a finger to her lips, urging calm prudence. In his own tongue he cried, “Ah! Dio! che non vorrei vivere dopo averla veduta?”—who would wish to live after seeing her? The peculiar Corsican accent made Ginevra tremble.

“Are you Corsican?” she asked, her heart beating faster.

“I was born in Corsica,” he replied, “but raised in Genoa, and enlisted when old enough.”

His beauty, devotion to the Emperor, his wound and misfortunes combined into a new, delightful feeling in Ginevra. To find a persecuted man who spoke her language was magical. Servin invited the officer to sit; the painter removed the sling to dress a long, broad sabre gash across his forearm. Ginevra shuddered at the wound; the stranger smiled at her. Servin dressed the cut with delicate care; the wounded man’s pale face expressed more pleasure at the girl’s presence than pain. The contrast—white linen and bared arm against red and blue uniform—had an artist’s charm.

A soft half-light filled the studio; an evening ray lit the soldier’s blanched face and black hair. To Ginevra’s Italian imagination it seemed an omen: he seemed a celestial messenger speaking her language and unconsciously conjuring childhood memories. A fresh, pure feeling dawned in her; for a short time she was dreamy, then blushed, exchanged a soft glance with him, and fled with his image before her eyes.

The next day, though not a class day, Ginevra returned to the studio and the prisoner sat beside her easel. Servin, with a sketch to finish, played mentor as the two talked chiefly in Corsican. The soldier related the sufferings of the retreat from Moscow: at nineteen he had crossed the Beresina and was almost the last of his regiment; he described Waterloo’s disaster with fiery words. His voice was music to the Italian girl. Raised as a Corsican, Ginevra was sincere and direct; she gave herself to her impressions without Parisian coquetry. She often sat with palette and brushes in hand without touching a color, eyes fixed on the officer, listening as if painting an unheard stroke. When he sat near and watched, she painted diligently without lifting her head.

He told her his name: Luigi. They agreed that on class days, when they could not meet, she would signal important political events by singing certain Corsican melodies.

But gossip grew. Mademoiselle Thirion informed the class, under secrecy, that Ginevra di Piombo had a lover—a young man who came during lessons and hid in the garret. “You who take her part,” she said to Mademoiselle Roguin, “watch her and you’ll see how she spends her time.” The other girls listened to Ginevra’s songs and watched her glances, interpreting her moods and the way she pricked up her ears to sounds through the partition. By the week’s end Laure was the only pupil who had resisted peeking through the crevice; she defended her friend. Mademoiselle Roguin tried to make Laure wait on the staircase after class so she could catch them in the studio, but Laure refused to spy, and she became the object of reprobation.

Little by little, the seed Thirion had planted bore fruit. Though Thirion herself stopped attending on the pretext that it was improper to study with a painter whose opinions were tainted by patriotism and Bonapartism, other mothers heard the gossip. One by one the young women ceased coming: Matilde Roguin absent, another the next day, until only a few remained; eventually, Ginevra and Laure were the sole occupants of the deserted studio for several days.

Ginevra did not notice the dwindling attendance nor ask the cause. She invented means of communicating with Luigi and lived in the studio’s delightful solitude, thinking only of the officer and his dangers. Though a sincere admirer of noble characters who never betrayed their political faiths, she urged Luigi to recognize the royal authority to be released and remain in France; he refused. Adversity and shared convictions brought their friendship to maturity rapidly. Ginevra, older and proud, enjoyed being courted by a youth who combined manly experience with the graces of youth; Luigi delighted in the protection of a woman of twenty-five. Their intimacy matured in a month what would have taken ten in society. They loved each other profoundly without needing to declare it.

One evening Ginevra heard their signal: Luigi scratched with a pin on the woodwork, barely making a sound. She looked around and, not seeing Laure, opened the door; Luigi caught sight of Laure and retired abruptly. Surprised, Ginevra found Laure at her easel.

“You are staying late, my dear. That head seems finished,” Ginevra said, offering a touch. Laure spoke in a trembling voice, asking Ginevra to finish a copy she might keep as a remembrance. But Laure’s distress betrayed her: “I am going away, never to return,” she cried.

“Why? Are the young ladies ill?” Ginevra asked.

“They have left Monsieur Servin,” Laure answered. “On your account.”

Ginevra’s brow darkened. Laure, trembling, told of gossip reaching their mothers: that Luigi was shut in the attic and Servin let the young man stay to shelter a love affair. Laure had resisted believing it and had not told her mother. But Madame Roguin, at a ball, asked Laure’s mother if she still sent her to the studio; upon hearing yes, Madame Roguin repeated the calumnies. Laure’s mother scolded her and insisted she must have known and failed to confide. Heartbroken, Laure pleaded with Ginevra: “Madame Roguin and my mother are coming to see Monsieur Servin tomorrow to reproach him; hadn’t you better warn him?”

Ginevra was thunderstruck. Servin entered just then, enthusiastic about finally varnishing his picture for the Exposition, and asked where all his pupils were. Laure dried her tears, bowed, and left. Ginevra confessed she believed she had caused the loss of Servin’s reputation.

Servin smiled; his reputation would be made at the Exposition, he said. Ginevra meant not his talent but his morality: the young ladies told their mothers that Luigi was kept in the attic and that Servin had lent himself to their love. The painter admitted some truth: the mothers had been foolish and should have come to him. “But I don’t care a straw! Life is short,” he said, snapping his fingers. Luigi, having heard part of the talk, then entered.

“You have lost all your scholars,” he cried. “I have ruined you!”

Servin took Luigi’s hand and Ginevra’s and joined them. “Marry one another, my children,” he said with fatherly kindness. They dropped their eyes; their silence was the first avowal of love. Servin blessed them and jocosely wed them, laying his hands on their heads. Laughter broke the strain. Ginevra pressed Luigi’s hand simply and ardently.

“Ah ca, my dear children,” Servin resumed, “you think all will go right now, but you are mistaken. I’m the only one your romance will harm. Madame Servin is a little straitlaced; I don’t see how to settle it with her.”

Ginevra suddenly remembered to tell him that Madame Roguin and Laure’s mother were coming to reproach him tomorrow. Servin understood and, with pride, Ginevra offered a plan: Luigi would no longer object to entering royal service; she would send a petition the next morning to a powerful man at the War ministry, a man who would refuse nothing to the daughter of Baron di Piombo. They would obtain a “tacit” pardon for Captain Luigi—perhaps not a promotion to major—and Servin could confound the mothers by explaining the truth.

“You are an angel!” cried Servin.

Meanwhile Ginevra’s parents grew anxious at her delay. “It is six o’clock and Ginevra not yet home!” cried Bartolomeo; his wife agreed that she had never been so late. Too restless to wait, Bartolomeo paced the salon. At over seventy-seven he was still erect and vigorous; his sparse white hair left a broad, protuberant skull suggesting character and firmness. His face, seamed with deep wrinkles, retained a noble expression; the fire of his passions lived in his eyes and he kept a Napoleonic sternness in public. During his political life he had been feared and unsocial; he had integrity that courtiers disliked. His income after the Empire was small—about thirty thousand francs invested in the Grand Livre—yet he had been rewarded under Napoleon. He had taken a barony only because the Emperor needed to grant him a title for foreign missions. Bartolomeo professed hatred for the traitors who surrounded Napoleon; it was said he had advised the Emperor to rid himself of certain men before 1814. After the second Bourbon return he gave up wearing the Legion of Honor. He represented the old Republicans, incorruptible servants of the Empire, and was proud of his austere reputation.

He had bought the old Portenduere mansion for the modest sum Madame Mère had paid him for his Corsican estates and made few changes. Lodged usually at government expense, he did not occupy it until after Fontainebleau; he and his wife lived simply. The grand apartments, lofty, sombre, and bare, with grand mirrors in dark gilded frames and Louis XIV furniture suited them. During the Empire and the Hundred Days, while he held lucrative offices, he had maintained an establishment fitting his rank, but in his private life he and his wife were frugal; their modest fortune sufficed. To them their daughter Ginevra was more precious than all else. When Bartolomeo resigned in May, 1814, dismissed his servants and closed his stable, Ginevra saw nothing to regret. She found happiness in quiet and work; their mutual affection was profound.

Often, especially after Napoleon’s fall, Bartolomeo and his wife spent evenings with their daughter, listening to her sing and play. They took secret pleasure in every word and step of hers. Like lovers, they often sat silent together, understanding one another without speech. This shared feeling animated their life. Apart from parental affection there may have been another reason for their intensity: it was undivided love. Ginevra’s whole heart belonged to her father and his to her; if flaws bind more than virtues, she inherited his passions. The Corsican father delighted in developing these fierce sentiments in her, teaching vindictiveness and command like a lion teaching cubs to spring. But she turned the lesson against him; as a child she forgave him nothing and he yielded. Piombo saw this as childishness, but Ginevra learned to rule her parents. Amid storms, a look or a word of tenderness would pacify them; often they were near a kiss while threatening vehemently.

For five years she grew wiser and avoided such scenes; her faithfulness, devotion, and good sense overcame her temper. Still, a danger remained in their equality. Piombo and his wife, without formal education, allowed Ginevra to study as she wished. She pursued many things briefly, then abandoned them until painting became her passion. Had her mother guided her studies, her defects might have been tempered; instead, her faults came from the fatal training of an old Corsican proud of his spirited child.

After pacing and ringing, a servant announced Ginevra’s arrival. “Go and meet Mademoiselle Ginevra,” Bartolomeo ordered. The baroness lamented their lack of a carriage for her, but Piombo, impatient, shrugged that Ginevra had declined one. As the porte-cochere opened and shut, Ginevra’s steps sounded in the courtyard; Bartolomeo returned almost instantly, carrying his struggling daughter in his arms.

“Here she is, my Ginevra, Ginevrettina, Ginevrola, mia Ginevra bella!” cried the old man.

“Oh, father, you hurt me!”

Ginevra was set down with respect. She nodded to her mother, who had been alarmed by the cry, as if to say the outburst was only a trick to escape. The baroness, pale and wan, brightened; Piombo rubbed his hands violently—his token of joy, learned at court under Napoleon. When his face relaxed, every wrinkle softened into benevolence. The two old people looked refreshed.

“Now, to dinner!” cried the baron, offering his hand to his daughter, whom he nicknamed “Signora Piombellina.” Ginevra replied with a smile.

“Ah ca!” said Piombo as they left the table. “Your mother tells me you have stayed at the studio longer than usual. Painting more important than your parents—”

“Oh, father!”

“Ginevra is preparing some surprise for us,” her mother suggested.

“A picture of your own! Will you bring it?” cried the Corsican.

“Yes, I am very much occupied,” Ginevra answered slowly.

“What is the matter, Ginevra? You are turning pale!” her mother cried.

“No,” the girl said with resolution. “It shall never be said that Ginevra Piombo acted a lie.”

Piombo and his wife were astonished.

“I love a young man,” she added, her voice full of feeling, lowering her eyelids to veil the fire of her eyes.

“Is he a prince?” her father asked ironically.

“No, father. He is a young man without fortune.”

“Is he very handsome?”

“He is very unfortunate.”

“What is he?”

“Labedoyere’s comrade; he was proscribed; Servin concealed him, and—”

“Servin is a good fellow,” cried Piombo. “But you, my daughter, you do wrong to love any man except your father.”

“It does not depend on me,” Ginevra replied.

“I flattered myself,” Piombo continued, “that my Ginevra would be faithful to me until I died; I did not expect our tenderness would find a rival.”

“Did I ever reproach you for your fanaticism for Napoleon?” Ginevra demanded. “Have you never loved any one but me? You left me months at a time on missions; I bore your absence. Life imposes necessities.”

“Ginevra!” cried Piombo.

“No, you don’t love me for myself; your reproaches betray intolerable egotism.”

“You dare blame your father’s love!” he flashed.

“Father, I don’t blame you,” she said more gently than her trembling mother expected. “You have grounds for your egotism as I have for my love. Heaven is my witness that no girl has fulfilled her duty to her parents better than I. Fifteen years I have not left your wing. But is it ingratitude to desire a husband who will protect me hereafter?”

“What! do you reckon benefits with your father, Ginevra?” he said dangerously.

A dreadful pause followed. Bartolomeo at last broke it in a heart-rending tone: “Oh! stay with us! stay with your father, your old father! I cannot have you love another man. Ginevra, you will not have long to await your liberty.”

“But, father, I need not leave you; we shall be two to love you,” she pleaded. “You will learn the man to whose care I bequeath myself. You will be doubly cherished.”

“Oh! Ginevra, Ginevra!” cried the Corsican, clenching his fists. “Why did you not marry when Napoleon brought me to accept the idea? Why did you not take the counts and dukes he presented?”

“They loved me to order,” she said. “Besides, they would have made me live with them, and I did not wish to leave you alone.”

“You don’t wish to leave me alone,” he answered, “and yet you marry—that is leaving me alone. I know you, my daughter; you would cease to love us. Elisa,” he added, looking at his stupefied wife, “we have no longer a daughter; she wishes to marry.”

The old man sat down, raised his hands to heaven as if invoking divine power, then bowed over as if weighed down with sorrow. Ginevra saw his agitation and was touched; she had expected a violent crisis and had not steeled herself against paternal gentleness.

“Father,” she said tenderly, “no, you shall never be abandoned by your Ginevra. But love her a little for her own sake. If you knew how he loves me! Ah! He would never make me unhappy!”

“Comparisons already!” cried Piombo, terrible. “No, I can never endure the idea of your marriage. If he loved you as you deserve he would kill me; if he did not love you, I should put a dagger through him.”

His hands and lips trembled; his eyes flashed lightnings. Ginevra matched his look; her eyes flamed. The daughter was worthy of the sire.

“Oh! to love you! What man is worthy of such a life?” Piombo continued. “To love you as a father is paradise on earth; who is there worthy to be your husband?”

“He,” said Ginevra. “He of whom I am not worthy.”

“He?” repeated Piombo mechanically. “Who is he?”

“He whom I love.”

“How can he know you enough to love you?”

“Whether he loves me or not, if I love him—”

“You love him?” cried Piombo.

Ginevra bent her head softly.

“You love him more than you love us?” he demanded.

“The two feelings cannot be compared,” she replied.

“Is one stronger than the other?”

“I think it is,” she said.

“You shall not marry him,” cried Piombo, his voice shaking the window-panes.

“I shall marry him,” replied Ginevra, tranquilly.

“Oh, God!” cried the mother, praying for deliverance.

The baron’s sternness darkened his face; he looked fixedly at his daughter and pleaded, almost on his knees, begging her to say nothing more that night. “Do you wish to see your father on his knees, his white hairs prostrate before you? I supplicate you—”

“Ginevra Piombo does not pass her word and break it,” she replied. “I am your daughter.”

“She is right,” said the baroness. “We are sent into the world to marry.”

“Do you encourage her in disobedience?” the baron demanded of his motionless wife.

“Refusing to obey an unjust order is not disobedience,” Ginevra said.

“No order can be unjust from the lips of your father, my daughter. Why do you judge me? The repugnance I feel is counsel from on high, sent perhaps to protect you.”

“The only evil could be that he did not love me,” she answered.

“Always he!” he cried.

“Yes, always,” she persisted. “He is my life, my good, my thought. Even if I obeyed you he would be ever in my soul. To forbid me to marry him is to make me hate you.”

“You love us not!” cried Piombo.

“No!” she said, shaking her head.

“Well, then forget him; be faithful to us. After we are gone—you understand?”

“Father, do you wish me to long for your death?” she cried.

“I shall outlive you. Children who do not honor their parents die early,” he said, driven to exasperation.

“All the more reason why I should marry and be happy,” she replied.

His blood rushed to his head; his face turned purple. Ginevra shuddered, then threw herself like a bird on his knee, clasped his white hair, and cried: “Oh, yes, yes, let me die first! I could never survive you, my father, my kind father!”

“Oh! my Ginevra, my own Ginevra!” replied Piombo, whose anger melted under this caress.

“It was time you ceased,” said the baroness, trembling.

“Poor mother!”

“Ah! Ginevretta! mia bella Ginevra!”

The father played with his daughter as though she were six: releasing the waves of her hair, dandling her on his knee, kisses mingled with scoldings. She jested to obtain admission for Luigi, but he refused playfully. She sulked, coaxed, and sulked until by evening she had impressed upon him both her love and the idea of an approaching marriage.

The next day she said no more about her love; she was more caressing than ever, as if to thank him for an implied consent. That evening she sang and played long, exclaiming: “We want a man’s voice for this nocturne.” At the end of a week her mother motioned; Elisa whispered, “I have persuaded your father to receive him.”

“Oh! mother, how happy you have made me!”

That day Ginevra arrived home on Luigi’s arm. The officer had come out of hiding. Ginevra’s pleadings with the Duc de Feltre, then minister of war, had succeeded: Luigi’s name was restored upon the roll of officers awaiting orders. Warned of the difficulty with her father, the young man concealed his fear of offending the old man. Courageous in adversity and on the battlefield, he trembled at entering Piombo’s salon; Ginevra felt it—this emotion, caused by her, was to her another proof of love.

“How pale you are!” she said when they reached the house.

“Oh! Ginevra, if it concerned my life only—” he began.

Though Bartolomeo had been warned, he would not rise to meet Luigi; he remained seated, his brow stern. Ginevra introduced him as “a soldier who fought beside the Emperor at Mont-Saint-Jean.” The baron rose, glanced at Luigi, and sneered: “Monsieur is not decorated.”

“I no longer wear the Legion of Honor,” Luigi answered timidly.

Mortified by her father’s incivility, Ginevra dragged forward a chair. Madame Piombo, seeing her husband’s brows relax, began conversationally: “Monsieur’s resemblance to a person we knew in Corsica, Nina Porta, is surprising.”

“Nothing could be more natural,” Luigi replied; on Piombo’s flaming eyes now rested. “Nina was my sister.”

“Are you Luigi Porta?” asked the old man.

“Yes.”

Bartolomeo rose, tottered, leaned against a chair, and beckoned his wife. The two old people, each supporting the other, left the room, abandoning their daughter with something like horror.

Luigi, bewildered, looked at Ginevra, who had turned white and stood gazing at the closed door. The departure felt so solemn that fear entered her for the first time. She struck her hands and murmured so faintly only a lover could hear: “What misery in a word!”

“In the name of our love, what have I said?” Luigi asked.

“My father never spoke to me of our deplorable history; I was too young when we left Corsica to know anything.”

“Are we in vendetta?” Luigi asked, trembling.

“Yes.” She smiled sadly, bowed, then raised her head with pride: “Oh, Luigi, our love must be pure and sincere to give me strength for the path I tread. But it involves a happiness that will last our lives, will it not?”

Luigi smiled and pressed her hand. His calm, restrained expression promised strength. The destiny of the pair seemed decided. Ginevra foresaw a cruel struggle, but the idea of abandoning Luigi vanished. She dragged him from her father’s house with desperate energy and did not leave him until he reached the lodging Servin had found.

When she returned, her resolve was serene; no sign of uneasiness remained. She found her mother weeping; the sight shook her heart, but she hid it. No one touched the dinner; all three rose without a word.

In the gloomy salon Piombo tried to speak but his voice failed. He rang for fire, saying, “Pietro, light the fire; I am cold.” Ginevra trembled, watching anxiously. The conflict within him must have been terrible; his face distorted with emotion. Ginevra knew the peril but did not flinch. Bartolomeo cast furtive glances at her as if fearing the character he had formed.

Between such natures all was extreme. The certainty of impending change brought terror to the baroness.

“Ginevra, you love the enemy of your family,” said Piombo at last, not daring to look at her.

“That is true,” she replied.

“You must choose between us. Our vendetta is part of our being. Whoso does not share my vengeance is not a member of my family.”

“My choice is made,” said Ginevra calmly.

Bartolomeo’s momentary softness returned. “Oh! my dear child!” he cried, letting her see his eyes moistened—the first and only tears he ever shed.

“I shall be his wife,” she said abruptly.

Bartolomeo recovered his coolness: “The marriage will not take place in my lifetime; I will never consent.”

Ginevra was silent.

“Have you reflected that Luigi is the son of the man who killed your brother?” he asked.

“He was six when that crime was committed; he was therefore not guilty,” she replied.

“He is a Porta!” cried Bartolomeo.

“I have never shared that hatred,” Ginevra answered eagerly. “You did not bring me up to think a Porta must be a monster. How could I know one you thought killed my brother had survived? Is it not natural you should yield your vendetta to my feelings?”

“A Porta!” repeated Piombo. “If his father had found you in your bed you would not be living now; he would have taken your life a hundred times.”

“It may be so,” she answered; “but his son has given me life, and more than life. To see Luigi is happiness without which I cannot live. Luigi has revealed to me the world of sentiments. He will be my husband.”

“Never,” said Piombo. “I would rather see you in your coffin, Ginevra.”

The old Corsican strode up and down, dropping sentences between pauses that betrayed his agitation.

“You think you can bend my will. Undeceive yourself. A Porta shall never be my son; that is my decree. Let there be no further question between us. I am Bartolomeo di Piombo; do you hear me, Ginevra?”

“Do you attach some mysterious meaning to those words?” she asked coldly.

“They mean that I have a dagger, and that I do not fear man’s justice. Corsicans explain themselves to God.”

“And I,” said Ginevra rising, “am Ginevra Piombo, and I declare that within six months I shall be the wife of Luigi Porta. You are a tyrant, my father,” she added after a pause.

Bartolomeo clenched his fists and struck them upon the marble mantel. “Ah! We are in Paris!” he muttered. Then he was silent, crossed his arms, bowed his head, and said nothing more that evening.

After declaring her will, Ginevra affected inconceivable coolness. She opened the piano and sang nocturnes and scherzos with grace and sentiment, thus triumphing over her father, whose dark face showed no softening. The old man was cruelly hurt by this tacit insult; his training had reaped a bitter harvest. Respect, he had taught her, is a barrier that spares remorse and grief—yet now both nursed pride and rancor.

The next day Ginevra tried to leave at her usual hour and found the gates closed. She said nothing but managed to inform Luigi of her father’s severity by a chambermaid who, though illiterate, carried letters between them for five days—a youth’s inventiveness supplying secrecy.

Father and daughter spoke seldom. Each nursed hatred and suffered proudly. Neither softened. Ginevra sometimes looked at Piombo with something savage; reproach sat on that innocent brow. She gave herself to happy thoughts, yet remorse dulled her eyes. Whether they believed the other would yield or flattered themselves with hope, both clung to anger.

At last, on Ginevra’s birthday, the baroness, despairing at the estrangement, attempted reconciliation. They sat in Bartolomeo’s study. A servant announced two notaries with witnesses. Bartolomeo watched the cold formal faces of these men. The notary, Monsieur Roguin, adopted the honeyed tone of business: he spoke of “efforts at conciliation,” of the law allowing a “respectful summons” before marriage despite parental non-consent, and of custom advising “a sensible man” after a final remonstrance to leave his daughter at liberty. His pedantic delivery grated on Bartolomeo.

Piombo’s face changed; his wrinkles contracted into narrow lines of cruelty; he cast the look of a tiger. The notaries, uneasy, consulted and walked to the window.

Roguin then read the stamped paper containing the respectful summons and asked Bartolomeo his answer.

“Are there laws in France which destroy paternal authority?” demanded the Corsican.

“Monsieur—” began Roguin.

“Which tear a daughter from her father?” the baron continued.

“Monsieur—”

“Which deprive an old man of his last consolation?”

“Monsieur, your daughter only belongs to you if—”

“And kill him?”

“Monsieur, permit me—” the notary began, his cool precision in contrast to passion.

The forms around him seemed from hell to Piombo; his repressed rage knew no bounds as the measured “permit me.” He sprang to a dagger hanging above the fireplace and rushed toward his daughter. The younger notary and a witness threw themselves between Ginevra and the weapon; Piombo knocked them down, his face on fire and eyes casting flames. When Ginevra saw him approach she looked at him with triumph and slowly knelt.

“No, no! I cannot!” he cried, flinging away the dagger, which buried itself in the wainscot.

“Well, then! have mercy! have pity!” she said. “You hesitate to be my death and refuse me life! Oh! father, never have I loved you as I do at this moment; give me Luigi! I ask your consent upon my knees: a daughter can humble herself before her father. Luigi, give me my Luigi, or I die!”

The violent excitement that suffocated her stopped her words; her convulsive movements showed she lay between life and death. Bartolomeo roughly pushed her away.

“Go,” he said. “The wife of Luigi Porta cannot be a Piombo. I have no daughter. I have not the strength to curse you, but I cast you off; you have no father. My Ginevra Piombo is buried here,” he said, pressing his heart. He took her by the arm to the gate and silently put her out.

“Luigi!” cried Ginevra as she entered her lover’s humble lodging, “my Luigi, we have no other fortune than our love.”

“Then am I richer than the kings of the earth!” he cried.

“My father and my mother have cast me off,” she said.

“I will love you in place of them,” he promised.

“Then let us be happy—we WILL be happy!” she cried, with a gayety that had something terrible.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after Ginevra was driven from her father’s house she went to ask Madame Servin for asylum and protection until the legal period before her marriage to Luigi. There her trouble began. Madame Servin received her coldly, annoyed at the scandal Ginevra’s affair had brought on her husband, and politely warned her not to count on future help. Too proud to beg but astonished at such selfishness, Ginevra rented a room in the lodging-house nearest Luigi’s. Luigi, devoted and constant, spent his days at her feet; his youthful love dispersed the cloud of her exile, and the bright future he painted made her smile again, though she did not forget her father’s severity.

One morning the lodging-house servant delivered trunks and packages: linens, clothes, and household necessities. Among them was a purse with Ginevra’s money and some of her mother’s savings, and a letter pleading with her to renounce the “fatal” marriage if she could. The mother explained it had cost her much to send these things, feared she might soon be unable to help further, asked her daughter not to think her hard if she had to abandon her to want, and blessed her in case she persisted. Tears had effaced some words.

“Oh, mother!” cried Ginevra, moved. She felt an immediate impulse to return to her father’s house, to fall at his feet and see her mother. But Luigi entered, and the impulse vanished; the sight of him made her stop the tears and she lacked the strength to abandon the youth who was her only hope. To desert him when he depended on her would be a treachery that her generous heart could not commit. She buried her grief silently.

The marriage day came. Ginevra had no friends present. While she dressed, Luigi fetched the witnesses required by law: a cavalry sergeant who owed him a battlefield debt, and a master-mason who owned the house they had rented; each brought a friend. These five men, simple and unadorned, came to escort the bride. Used to plain living, they treated the ceremony as business rather than a festival, wearing ordinary clothes, and their manner lacked the usual joy of a wedding procession.

Ginevra was simply dressed, yet her nobility and beauty silenced the witnesses’ customary greetings; they bowed in respectful admiration. This reserve chilled the group. Joy often bursts forth only among equals, and the gravity around the bridal pair made the occasion seem muted. The church and the mayor’s office being near, Luigi and Ginevra walked together with their witnesses. At the mayor’s court-yard they found a crowd of carriages and, in the hall for civil marriages, two other wedding parties waiting.

Ginevra sat beside Luigi on a long bench; the witnesses stood. Two other brides in white, adorned with ribbons, lace, pearls, and orange-blossoms, were surrounded by cheerful families; all faces around them reflected happiness and blessing. Watching them, Ginevra’s heart swelled; she pressed Luigi’s arm and gave him a look. A tear rolled from Luigi’s eyes—he understood the joys she was sacrificing for him—and that tear made her forget the abandonment outside and cling only to him. Love laid its light between them; in the midst of the crowd they felt alone.

Their witnesses, indifferent, conversed quietly. The mason checked his silver watch and complained of delay. Luigi and Ginevra, sitting close and seeming one, exhibited a delicate, quiet happiness contrasted with the ostentatious joy around them. Ginevra, superstitious as an Italian, treated this contrast as an omen and felt a terror mingled with her love.

At that moment the clerk called, “Monsieur Luigi da Porta and Mademoiselle Ginevra di Piombo.” A murmur ran through the hall: the celebrity of the bride’s name drew attention, and spectators wondered why the wedding was not more sumptuous. The absence of her parents was remarked; her father’s wrath seemed present in the whisper. The mayor, asked by the clerk to call in the families, was informed wearily that “the father and mother protest.” On inquiry, the clerk added drily that the groom was an orphan. The mayor hesitated; the clerk presented the papers annexed to the certificate, showing the summons had been duly served. That bureaucratic decision, matter-of-fact and stinging, contained the whole story: the animosities of the Portas and the Piombos, their passions, were inscribed coldly in civic procedure. Ginevra trembled and found refuge only in Luigi’s eyes.

The mayor assumed a stern, disapproving air; the clerk surveyed them with malicious curiosity. Deprived of accessories, the ceremony became a simple legal act, great only in thought. After a few questions, some mumbled remarks by the mayor, and the signing of registers by the witnesses, Luigi and Ginevra were made one in law. They then walked back through the hall, passing among joyous relations who had no stake in their marriage other than that its delay affected their own weddings. In the mayor’s courtyard, under the open sky, Ginevra sighed.

“Can a lifetime of devotion and love suffice to prove my gratitude for your courage and tenderness, my Ginevra?” Luigi asked, with tears. At those words she forgot her sufferings; she had endured exile to stand publicly with him. “Why should others come between us?” she said simply, delighting Luigi. Their step lightened; neither noticed the world as they hurried to the church.

In a small, dark chapel in a corner of the building, an old priest married them before a plain altar. Two other marriages also took place nearby, still surrounded by pomp. The church buzzed with attendants; altars glowed with ceremony; wreaths of orange-flowers crowned the Virgin. When the priest sought acolytes to hold the symbol of union, none were present, so two of the witnesses fulfilled the task. The priest’s hurried homily included an indirect reproach to Ginevra for her parents’ absence; after uniting them before God as the mayor had before the law, he left the couple.

“God bless them!” said Vergniaud, the sergeant, to the mason at the church porch. “No two creatures were ever more fitted for one another. The parents are foolish. I don’t know a braver soldier than Colonel Luigi.” This soldier’s blessing—simple, heartfelt—soothed Ginevra.

They parted with hearty handshakes; Luigi thanked the sergeant. “I am now and ever at your service,” the sergeant declared. Luigi hurried his bride to the modest apartment they had taken. When the door closed, Luigi embraced her. “Oh, my Ginevra! for now you are mine, here is our true wedding. Here all things will smile upon us.”

They inspected the three rooms: a salon-dining room, a bedchamber, and a large study Luigi had arranged for Ginevra. In the study she found easels, colors, lay-figures, casts, pictures, and portfolios—an artist’s paraphernalia. “So here I am to work!” she said with childlike happiness. There was something nearing magnificence in the small retreat: a bookcase with favorite books and a piano. She praised Luigi’s taste and touched the hangings and furniture with the satisfaction of a bride inspecting wedding gifts.

They entered the nuptial chamber, fresh and white. Ginevra scrutinized each piece of furniture with amused, antiquarian thoroughness. “We begin by ruining ourselves,” she said half-jokingly. “True! for all my back pay is there,” Luigi admitted; he had mortgaged it to a man named Gigonnet. She reproached him mildly, but inwardly was pleased. “Do you believe I should be less happy in a garret?” she asked. “It is all charming—and it is ours!” Luigi looked at her with such enthusiasm that she lowered her eyes.

Above these three rooms under the roof were Luigi’s study, a kitchen, and a servant’s room. The view was limited by a high wall and a gloomy courtyard, but the lovers, happy and hopeful, treated their hidden nest as paradise rather than prison.

The first days of their union were given to love. Work could wait; passion prevailed. Luigi lay for hours admiring the color of her hair, the moulding of her forehead, the expression in her eyes; Ginevra caressed him, fascinated by his nobility. They played together like children with nothing, which always brought them back to their love. An air she sang reproduced the lights and shadows of their passion. They walked in the country, finding love in flowers, the sky, and sunsets; life’s simple pleasures deepened their affection. Each day was new, and they instinctively recognized that their souls were of a kind whose inexhaustible riches promised continuous joy.

Their love was artless and complete, with long conversations, silences, repose, and ardor. To them it was like an ocean of varied phenomena, never monotonous. But prudence soon drew them from Eden: they had to work to live. Ginevra, skilled at imitating old paintings, took up copying and gained customers among picture-dealers. Luigi, trained in strategy, struggled to find occupation in Paris. Finally, using his excellent handwriting, he sought work copying papers for lawyers and notaries. His frankness and situation interested many; he soon had enough work to need assistants, and his copying business developed into a regular trade. The profits of his office together with the sale of Ginevra’s pictures gave them a competence earned by industry.

This work-filled period was the happiest of their lives. Days passed rapidly, full of occupation and the joys of love. At night they met in Ginevra’s studio; music refreshed them after labor; neither complained. Ginevra always appeared smiling and to Luigi she seemed radiant. Each cherished the thought that gave them strength: she worked for him and he worked for her.

Sometimes, in Luigi’s absence, Ginevra pictured the perfect happiness they might have had if her parents had approved; the thought of remorse made her sad. Mournful images rose: her old father alone, her mother weeping in secret to hide her tears from the inexorable Piombo. The two solemn white heads haunted her, and she felt she might never again see them except in memory. This sense of loss pursued her like a presentiment.

She marked their anniversary by giving Luigi a portrait he had long desired—his Ginevra painted by her own hand. It was her finest work: beyond mere resemblance, it captured the glow of her beauty, the purity of her feeling, and the happiness of their love. It stood as a votive offering to their wedded felicity.

Another year passed in ease and comfort. Their life could be summed simply: they were happy. No event of sufficient importance disturbed that happiness to be recorded.

Early in 1819 the picture-dealers asked Ginevra for something beyond copies; competition made her work harder to sell. Madame Porta realized she had erred in neglecting genre painting, which might already have given Ginevra a reputation. She tried portrait-painting, but faced many artists more desperate for money. Luigi and Ginevra had saved a little, so immediate anxiety had not yet reached them.

Toward the end of winter Luigi worked without respite. He, too, battled rivals. Fees for writing had fallen so low that he could no longer employ help; he had to labour longer for the same pay. Ginevra had completed several worthy pictures, but dealers bought mostly for names; her canvases had little chance. She offered them almost nothing that was purchased.

Household danger loomed. Their souls floated on an ocean of happiness; love overwhelmed them even as poverty rose like a skeleton amid their joy. Yet they hid their anxieties. When Ginevra wanted to weep at Luigi’s worn, suffering face she redoubled her caresses; Luigi, concealing his forebodings, showed tenderer love. They exalted feeling to compensate; words, joys, and caresses grew frenzied. Each preferred to deceive the other, clinging ardently to any hope.

One night Ginevra woke and missed Luigi. A faint light on the opposite courtyard wall showed him writing in his study; lately he waited till she slept to go to his garret and write. At four she feigned sleep. When Luigi returned, exhausted, she saw his worn face where toil and care had begun to trace wrinkles. “It is for me he spends his nights,” she thought, and resolved to imitate him. That day, using a letter of recommendation from Elie Magus, one of her dealers, she obtained an order for coloring lithographs. She painted by day and colored engravings at night, sharing Luigi’s clandestine labour. They entered their nuptial bed only to deceive each other; both pretended sleep, each leaving the bed as soon as the other seemed asleep.

One night Luigi, feverishly tired, opened his garret casement for air and saw the reflection of Ginevra’s lamp on the opposite wall. He went down softly and surprised her coloring engravings.

“Oh, Ginevra!” he cried.

She rose convulsively and blushed.

“Could I sleep while you wore yourself out?” she said.

“But to me alone belongs the right to work in this way,” he answered.

“Could I be idle,” she asked with tears, “when every mouthful we eat costs a drop of your blood? I should die if I could not add my efforts to yours. All should be in common between us: pains and pleasures.”

“She is cold!” Luigi cried, and wrapped her shawl closer. They went to the window; she leaned on his breast, he held her round the waist, and together in deep silence they gazed at the sky brightening toward dawn. Clouds moved quickly; the East grew luminous.

“See!” said Ginevra. “It is an omen. We shall be happy.”

“Yes, in heaven,” Luigi replied with a bitter smile. “Oh, Ginevra! you who deserved all the treasures on earth—”

“I have your heart,” she said joyfully.

“Ah! I complain no more!” he answered, drawing her close and kissing her thin, worn face.

Their courage was briefly rewarded; but an event that often crowns household happiness proved fatal. Ginevra bore a son, “as beautiful as the day.” Motherhood doubled her strength. Luigi borrowed to meet the confinement expenses. At first the new burden seemed only joy; it was their last happiness.

Like swimmers uniting to breast a current, they struggled; sometimes apathy resembling the sleep before death overcame them. They sold jewels. Poverty appeared—not hideous, but plainly clothed; its voice neither terrifying nor raving, it made them lose comforts and dried pride’s springs. Soon want arrived in all its horror, indifferent to rags, treading human sentiments underfoot.

Seven or eight months after little Bartolomeo’s birth, it would have been hard to see in the mother who nursed her sickly babe the woman of the portrait that had been their sole ornament. With no fire through a hard winter, Ginevra’s graceful outlines were destroyed; her cheeks grew white as porcelain, her eyes dulled as if life’s springs were drying. Watching her shrunken, discoloured child, her only suffering was his misery; Luigi could not force a smile for his son.

“I have wandered over Paris,” he said one day. “I know no one; can I ask strangers? Vergniaud, my old sergeant, is concerned in a conspiracy and imprisoned; besides, he has lent all he could. As for our landlord, he has not asked rent in over a year.”

“But we are not in want,” replied Ginevra gently, affecting calm.

“Every hour brings new difficulty,” Luigi answered in terror.

Soon Luigi sold Ginevra’s pictures, her portrait, and the few household articles they could spare for a miserable sum, prolonging their agony. In these days of wretchedness Ginevra displayed the sublimity of her nature and resignation.

She stoically bore misery’s blows; her strong soul upheld her; she worked with unfaltering hand beside her dying son, performed household duties with marvelous activity, and sufficed for all. She was even happy when she saw Luigi’s surprised smile at the cleanliness she produced in their one poor room.

“Dear, I kept this bit of bread for you,” she said one evening when he returned, worn out.

“And you?”

“I? I have dined, dear Luigi; I want nothing more.”

The tender look on her beseeching face urged him more than words to take the food she had denied herself.

Luigi kissed her with one of those kisses of despair given in 1793 between friends mounting a scaffold. In such moments two beings see each other heart to heart. Comprehending suddenly that his wife was starving, Luigi was seized by the fever consuming her. He shuddered and went out, pretending business called, for he would rather have drunk poison than secure life by eating the last morsel of bread left in their home.

He wandered wildly about Paris amid flaunting luxury, hurried past windows where gold glittered, and at last resolved to sell himself as a substitute for military service, hoping the sacrifice would save Ginevra and that her father would take her home during his absence. He went to an agent and found an old officer of the Imperial guard.

“It’s two days since I have eaten,” he said in a slow, weak voice. “My wife is dying of hunger and has never complained; she will die smiling, I think. For God’s sake, comrade,” he added bitterly, “buy me in advance; I am robust; I am no longer in the service, and I—”

The officer gave Luigi a sum on account. The wretched man laughed convulsively as he grasped the gold and ran breathless home, crying, “Ginevra! Oh, my Ginevra!”

It was almost night when he reached their room. He entered softly, fearing to shock his wife, whom he had left so weak. The last rays of the sun faded from Ginevra’s face as she sat sleeping in her chair, holding her child on her breast.

“Wake, my dear one,” he said, not seeing the infant, who at that moment shone with a strange pallor.

Hearing his voice, the poor mother opened her eyes, met Luigi’s look, and smiled; but Luigi cried out in horror; he scarcely recognized his wife, half mad. With a savage gesture he showed her the gold. Ginevra laughed mechanically, then cried in a dreadful voice, “The child, Luigi, he is cold!”

She looked at her son and swooned. The little Bartolomeo was dead. Luigi took his wife in his arms, the child still clasped to her; after laying her on the bed he went out to seek help.

“Oh! my God!” he cried as he met his landlord on the stairs. “I have gold, gold, and my child has died of hunger, and his mother is dying, too! Help me!”

He returned like one distraught to his wife, leaving the mason and neighbours to gather a few things for needs they had been too proud to reveal. Luigi cast his gold upon the floor and knelt by the bed. Ginevra, delirious, murmured, “Father! take care of my son, who bears your name.”

“Oh, my angel! be calm,” Luigi said, kissing her; “our good days are coming back.”

“My Luigi,” she said, looking at him with extraordinary attention, “listen. I feel that I am dying. My death is natural; I suffered too much; besides, such happiness as mine must be paid for. Yes, Luigi, be comforted. I have been so happy that if I lived again I would accept our fate. I am a bad mother; I regret you more than my child—My child!” she added hollowly.

Two tears escaped her dying eyes, and suddenly she pressed the little body she could not warm.

“Give my hair to my father, in memory of his Ginevra,” she said. “Tell him I have never blamed him.”

Her head fell on her husband’s arm.

“No, you cannot die!” cried Luigi. “The doctor is coming. We have food. Your father will take you home. Prosperity is here. Stay with us, angel!”

Her faithful heart, so full of love, grew cold. Ginevra turned her eyes to him she loved, though she was conscious of nothing else. Confused images passed before her; memory of earth faded. She knew Luigi was there and clasped his hand tightly as if to hold herself from some precipice.

“Dear,” she said at last, “you are cold; I will warm you.”

She tried to put his hand upon her heart, but died.

Two doctors, a priest, and neighbours came into the room, bringing what might save the poor couple and calm despair. They made some noise entering; afterwards an awful silence filled the room.

While that scene took place, Bartolomeo Piombo and his wife sat in their antique chairs at the corners of a vast fireplace in their salon. A friend had just left; the chair he had occupied remained between the two Corsicans. The clock told midnight. The old couple had lost the ability to sleep; they sat silent, like persons in their dotage, gazing about at things they did not see. Their deserted salon, full of memories, was feebly lit by a single expiring lamp. Without the hearth’s spark they might soon be in total darkness.

Piombo cast glances at the empty chair—glances full of thought and remorse—for the seat had been Ginevra’s. Elisa Piombo watched the expressions cross her husband’s pallid face; though long used to divining his feelings, tonight they seemed threatening and melancholy, making his soul incomprehensible even to her.

Would Bartolomeo yield at last to the memories that chair awakened? Had he been shocked to see a stranger occupy it, used for the first time since his daughter left? Had the hour of his mercy struck—the hour Elisa had vainly prayed and waited for?

These thoughts shook the mother. For an instant her husband’s countenance became so terrible she trembled at having named Ginevra. The wintry night blew snow sharply against the blinds; the old woman dropped her head to hide tears. Suddenly a sigh burst from the old man; he seemed crushed. She risked speaking—for the second time in three years—of his daughter.

“Ginevra may be cold,” she said softly.

Piombo quivered.

“She may be hungry,” she continued.

The old man dropped a tear.

“Perhaps she has a child and cannot suckle it; her milk is dried up!” said the mother in despair.

“Let her come! let her come to me!” cried Piombo. “Oh! my precious child, thou hast conquered me.”

The mother rose as if to fetch her daughter. At that instant the door opened noisily, and a man, his face no longer human, stood before them.

“Dead! Our two families were doomed to exterminate each other. Here is all that remains of her,” he said, laying Ginevra’s long black hair upon the table.

The old people shook as if struck by lightning.

Luigi no longer stood before them.

“He has spared me a shot, for he is dead,” Bartolomeo slowly said, gazing at the ground.
