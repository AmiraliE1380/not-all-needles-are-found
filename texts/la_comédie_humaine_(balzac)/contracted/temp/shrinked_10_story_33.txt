DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, signals the move from mere material enterprise to intellectual influence: he connects local power to universal markets, equalizes products, and brings metropolitan ideas to the provinces. Self-taught in the world's surface, he reads people quickly, judges habits and solvency at a glance, and knows the haunts of vice and virtue across France. He persuades with polished speech, seizes opportunities, and often shows more practical diplomacy than ambassadors, negotiating deals in taverns rather than salons. By sheer persuasiveness he imports Parisian innovations into provincial life.

Gaudissart exemplifies this type: an acrobat of commerce who extracts wealth from the provinces with a torrent of phrases. Notable in an olive-green overcoat and striped shirt, he disarms and courts attention. Having made his fortune selling hats—charming vendors and winning social favor—he is jovial and festive yet businesslike when required, tailoring his approach to each audience.

After the Revolution of 1830 turned ideas into commodities, writers began selling thought, speculators followed, and Gaudissart adapted. He took a commission selling life-insurance policies, learned quickly, earned praise, and even sold newspaper subscriptions. Confident in his talents, he agreed to market both insurance and periodicals.

CHAPTER II

After a week immersed in Saint-Simonism and finance, Gaudissart prepared to travel in mid-April. Tempting hat offers tried to lure him back, but he set off, joking with Jenny, a florist, about refurnishing her room from his travels. She doubted his boasts, prompting playful banter about his prospects. He assured her he would earn handsomely from insurance and the profitable "Children’s Journal," measuring himself against successful politicians and merchants. The next morning he left for his assigned towns, stopping in Blois and writing to Jenny, boasting of his sales.

CHAPTER III

Arriving in Vouvray, Gaudissart expected his persuasive gifts to work but met provincial resistance. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, skeptical of commercial travellers and aware of Gaudissart’s reputation. Vernier resolved to humiliate him and sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a local reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with wine and newspapers, began a conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the madman’s eccentricities produced nonsensical replies. Gaudissart tried to maintain composure and sell his product, but the man’s erratic behavior and the watching townspeople thwarted him.

CHAPTERS IV–V

Seated at home with wine, Margaritis spoke delusions while two women listened amused and Vernier watched, intent on spectacle. The talk devolved into misunderstandings and incoherence. When the innkeeper revealed Vernier had engineered the encounter, Gaudissart, enraged at the insult, demanded satisfaction. The townspeople enjoyed the scene; Vernier’s refusal escalated toward a duel until the innkeeper proposed mediation over breakfast. They patched up the quarrel—acknowledging its absurdity—and Gaudissart left Vouvray, resolved to return to Paris and his usual work, reminded that eloquence alone cannot overcome discernment about when persuasion is futile.
