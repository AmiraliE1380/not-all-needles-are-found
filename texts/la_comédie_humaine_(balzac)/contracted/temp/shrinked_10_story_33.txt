DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller represents a modern shift from selling goods to spreading ideas: he links provincial isolation to metropolitan thought, equalizes products, and imports Parisian innovations by persuasion. Self-educated, a mimic and quick judge of character and solvency, he moves between village and capital with a surface knowledge of the world. One such man is Gaudissart—an acrobat of commerce in an olive-green overcoat and striped shirt—who built a fortune selling hats by charm and rapid talk. Jovial and adaptable, he read audiences and opportunities, sometimes outdoing diplomats.

After 1830, when ideas became commodities and Saint-Simonianism and speculation spread, Gaudissart switched from hats to selling life insurance and periodicals. Quick to seize commissions, newspapers hired him to sell subscriptions; his employers praised his skill at turning chatter into profit.

CHAPTER II

Preparing to travel in mid‑April after a week steeped in Saint‑Simonism and finance, Gaudissart resisted tempting hat commissions and set off, joking with Jenny the florist about furnishing her room with his gains. Bragging of expected earnings from insurance—especially the lucrative "Children’s Journal"—he stopped in Blois, then continued to his assigned towns.

CHAPTER III

In Vouvray he met provincial resistance. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint‑Simonian ideas. At the Soleil d’Or inn, Monsieur Vernier, aware of Gaudissart’s fame, resolved to humble him and sent him to Margaritis, reputed lunatic.

CHAPTER IV

Margaritis, obsessed with wine and newspapers, rambled incoherently. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the talk devolved into absurdity. Two women listened amused while Vernier watched, intent on exposing Gaudissart’s vanity; mockery and unpredictability made any sale impossible.

CHAPTER V

When the innkeeper revealed the setup, Gaudissart, insulted, demanded satisfaction. A duel loomed, then—after townsfolk and the innkeeper intervened at breakfast—the quarrel was patched. Gaudissart left Vouvray mortified but wiser: commerce needs not only eloquence but the discernment to recognize when persuasion is futile, and he resolved to return to Paris and his usual work.
