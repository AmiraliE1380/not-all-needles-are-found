To Monsieur Charles Nodier, member of the French Academy.

My dear Nodier, I offer you a book of domestic deeds hidden from courts, where the finger of God, often called chance, supplants human justice, and morals—though sometimes mocked—remain striking and instructive.

These stories teach much about Family and Maternity. Perhaps too late, we may learn the consequences of the weakening of paternal authority. That authority once endured until a father’s death and served as the domestic tribunal for private crimes; kings sometimes enforced it. However tender a mother, she cannot fulfill the patriarchal function any more than a woman can occupy a king’s throne. In this work I show plainly the necessity of the indissoluble marriage bond, the fatal effects of feminine weakness, and the danger of unrestrained selfish interests. A society that idolizes wealth should shudder at the impotence of law when success is deified and every means of attaining it forgiven. May it return to the Catholic faith for moral purification and an education broader than that of a lay university.

In the “Scenes from Military Life” many noble self-devotions will appear; let me point out how the necessities of war can corrupt minds, which then behave at home as if still on campaign.

Your sagacious view of our age and your bitter reflections have perceived the havoc wrought by four distinct political regimes. I dedicate this history under your protection; your name may shield it from the criticisms that will follow—for where is the patient who stays silent when the surgeon lifts the dressing from his wound?

With pleasure in dedicating this Scene to you and pride in your friendship, I remain

Your sincere admirer,  
De Balzac  
Paris, November, 1842.

THE TWO BROTHERS

CHAPTER I

In 1792 the town of Issoudun had Doctor Rouget, a physician regarded as malign in temper. People said he made his wife miserable, though she was the neighborhood beauty. Whether the wife was foolish or merely weak in health—rumored to be why the doctor married her—friends and enemies never wholly exposed the household. Rouget was of the “not pleasant to deal with” sort; townsmen treated him civilly. His wife, a demoiselle Descoings, bore a son and, ten years later, a surprise daughter, Agathe.

These facts matter: without them Rouget might read as a monster, yet he only exemplified the evil sheltered under the axiom that “men should have strength of character,” a maxim that has caused much female misery.

The elder Descoings, Agathe’s maternal grandparents, were wool commission merchants who grew rich and stingy. Their son Descoings disliked Issoudun, moved to Paris, established as a grocer in the rue Saint-Honoré, and, with love, patience, and some support from his parents, married his predecessor’s widow, Madame Bixiou.

In 1792 Descoings seemed prosperous. When the elder Descoings retired he bought forfeited estates. Doctor Rouget, perhaps foreseeing his wife’s death, sent Agathe to Paris to his brother-in-law to let her see the capital and to win the affection of childless relatives. Agathe, the handsomest maiden of Issoudun, did not resemble her parents. Her birth had caused a breach between Rouget and his friend Monsieur Lousteau, and Madame Rouget spent much time with Madame Hochon, Lousteau’s sister and Agathe’s godmother, to whom she confided her sorrows; Madame Hochon later told most of Issoudun what it knew of Madame Rouget.

When Rouget told his wife he would send Agathe to Paris, the first words from the mother were, “I shall never see my daughter again.” Madame Hochon thought she was right. The poor mother grew yellow with grief amid the foolish neglect of her son Jean-Jacques, who added to her misery. Jean-Jacques resembled his father in the worst ways.

Agathe’s arrival did not bring happiness to her uncle. About the same time Descoings was imprisoned after an accusation by citoyenne Duplay, Robespierre’s housekeeper, who took offense at his remarks about famine and denounced him for “accaparation.” His wife’s ill-advised pleas and appeals to officials, though earnest, only worsened matters. Bridau, secretary to Roland, intervened on the family’s behalf and was struck by Agathe’s calm, innocent beauty; he married her with her father’s consent. Doctor Rouget, eager to secure his purposes, hurried to Paris to draw the marriage contract to his advantage. Bridau’s ardent and disinterested love gave carte blanche to the perfidious doctor, who exploited his son-in-law’s blindness—a practice that this story will expose.

Madame Rouget, and then the elder Descoings, died; Rouget acquired vineyards, farms, and ironworks. Jean-Jacques proved incapable and was allowed by his father to become an idle proprietor. After his wife’s death in 1799, Rouget led a debauched life within his house and died in 1805. Jean-Jacques remained a bachelor, for reasons partly his father’s fault.

Agathe, whom her father scarcely recognized as his child, bore a likeness to Rouget’s mother—an odd skipping of resemblance that vexes heredity. In turn, Agathe’s eldest son, who resembled her physically, inherited the moral qualities of his grandfather.

Agathe’s beauty was pure and placid—an oval face, clear complexion, golden hair, a composed mouth, long lashes, and tender dark-blue eyes. Bridau’s portrait of her hangs in his atelier. She was the ideal provincial housekeeper, pious though not sanctimonious, educated by the Church. She was an accomplished wife but ignorant of life, which made her vulnerable: the Roman epitaph “She did needlework and kept the house” fitted her.

Under the Consulate Bridau adored Napoleon. In 1804 he became head of a department at the ministry of the interior with a salary of twelve thousand francs and handsome emoluments. From 1804 to 1808 the Bridaus lived in a handsome suite on the Quai Voltaire near the ministry and the Tuileries, keeping only a cook and footman. Agathe rose early to market with her cook, prepared breakfast, saw Bridau off, cleared the table, cared for the children, and sat knitting by his writing table when he worked late. They attended the theatre occasionally and used a ministerial box; Bridau received friends on Thursdays and gave an annual Shrove-Tuesday ball.

Their conjugal life contained three notable events: the birth of two sons three years apart and Bridau’s death in 1808, killed by overwork just as Napoleon planned to appoint him director-general, count, and councillor of state. An inflammatory fever from exertion took him before the Emperor could reward him. Napoleon, moved by Bridau’s devotion, inquired into his widow’s circumstances: both sons received scholarships at the Imperial Lyceum from the Emperor’s privy purse, and Madame Bridau was given a pension of four thousand francs.

From marriage to her husband’s death Agathe had no communication with Issoudun. Her mother died as she was about to bear her youngest son; when her father died, the coronation occupied Bridau and Agathe chose not to leave him. Her brother Jean-Jacques did not write. Once a year she received a letter from her godmother Madame Hochon and replied with commonplaces, paying little heed to her pious advice.

Some time before Doctor Rouget’s death Madame Hochon warned Agathe she would get nothing from her father’s estate unless she gave a power of attorney to Monsieur Hochon. Agathe, reluctant to harass her brother, let matters lie. Whether because Bridau thought the transfers legal or shared his wife’s magnanimity, he refused Roguin, his notary’s, advice to use his position to contest the deeds by which her father had deprived her. Thus husband and wife tacitly sanctioned the transfer. Roguin, however, forced Bridau to reflect that if he died first Agathe would be left without property. Reviewing their finances, Bridau found that between 1793 and 1805 they had used nearly thirty thousand of the fifty thousand francs that old Rouget had given Agathe at her marriage. He invested the remaining twenty thousand in public funds, yielding about two thousand francs a year. As a widow, Agathe could live suitably on an income of six thousand francs; with prudence she planned to move, dismiss the footman, and keep only a cook. But her intimate friend Madame Descoings—insisting on being considered an aunt—sold her establishment and came to live with Agathe, taking Bridau’s study as her bedroom.

The two widows pooled their revenues, jointly possessing about twelve thousand francs a year. Such seemingly “natural” measures deserve attention, for fools excuse wrongs as natural. Here, the serpent lies amid flowers.

In 1809 Madame Descoings, never revealing her age, was sixty-five. Once a beauty, she had kept her looks by constitution—medium height, plump, fresh, with fine shoulders and a rosy complexion; her blond hair showed no gray despite her husband’s fate. She loved good cheer, the theatre, and secretly gambled in lottery shares. She dressed well, was the pleasantest of companions, and never opposed anyone. If she did not marry again, it was likely due to the times: during the Empire men found young women too easily.

Madame Descoings cheered Madame Bridau with theatre and drives, prepared dinners, and tried to marry her to her own son by her first husband, Bixiou. To this end she revealed a family secret: her son Bixiou, thirty-five, a widowed major who later perished at Lützen, had a son—a grandson she secretly saw and claimed as the child of her first wife. That boy, educated with Agathe’s sons at the Imperial Lyceum on a half-scholarship, proved clever, witty, and gifted as a draughtsman and designer.

Agathe, devoted to her children, declined to remarry out of fidelity to her husband and common sense. But being a good mother is harder than being a good wife: a widow must both love and command her children, and few women are firm enough for both. Thus Agathe, despite many virtues, became the innocent cause of grave unhappiness. Through lack of worldly intelligence and blind confidence, she fell victim to Madame Descoings, who gambled with the household money, nursing a hope for a winning combination that would enrich her grandson, Agathe, and the Bridaus.

When debts reached ten thousand francs Madame Descoings increased her stakes; failing to win, the debt rolled up. At twenty thousand francs she lost her head but still hoped. She attempted to mortgage property, but Roguin showed that Doctor Rouget had secured her only a life-interest, disinheriting Madame Descoings in favor of his son Jean-Jacques; no lender would advance twenty thousand francs to a sixty-six-year-old on an annuity of about four thousand. One morning Madame Descoings fell at Agathe’s feet and confessed the state of things. Agathe reproached her not; she dismissed the footman and cook, sold all but necessities, converted most of the government funds to pay the debts, and bade farewell to her appartement.

One of the worst corners in Paris was the stretch of the rue Mazarin between the rue Guenegard and the rue de Seine, behind the Institute: high gray walls, chill shadows, little sun and a constant north wind. The poor widow Madame Bridau took a third-floor lodging in a shallow house on that damp, dark corner, opposite the Institute’s studios where art pupils—rapins—lived in melancholy, barred cages. From her windows she saw the dome of the Institute and a street traversed only by hackney-coaches. She had chosen the lodging because it cost only four hundred francs a year on a nine-year lease, because it was near her sons’ school, and because she knew the quarter.

The interior matched the exterior. The dining-room, hung with yellow paper patterned with small green flowers and floored with unglazed tiles, contained only a table, two sideboards, and six chairs brought from the other apartment. The salon had an Aubusson carpet given when the ministry was refurnished and a common mahogany sofa covered in green silk. Over it hung an amateur pastel of Bridau—his noble brow and frank smile suggesting a man of duty, the Emperor’s “Justum et tenacem.” Opposite was a bright Vernet of the Emperor on horseback. Agathe, after her husband’s death, had indulged herself in two large birdcages for canaries and Java sparrows; the lodging soon looked littered—cats on the sofa, canaries loose at times, millet and chickweed scattered, tidbits in broken saucers, garments everywhere. Everything of Bridau’s was scrupulously preserved: his desk implements wrapped and sealed, his last pen carefully kept, the cup from which he had drunk his last draught left on the mantel.

Having lost the only man she had known and loved, Agathe renounced coquetry and ordinary self-care and lived only for her children. A melancholy took her face after the move. She placed small hopes in the Emperor, who already gave three hundred francs a year to each child from his privy purse, besides scholarships. The brilliant Madame Descoings lived on the second floor; she had advanced Madame Bridau three thousand francs out of her annuity through Roguin the notary, a loan that would take years to restore. Reduced to an income of twelve hundred francs, the Descoingses lived modestly with her niece, keeping a woman-for-mornings only; Madame Descoings liked to cook. In the evenings a few old friends—ministry men who owed their places to Bridau—came for cards with the two widows. Madame Descoings, still hoping one stroke of luck would repay her loan, was dear to the Bridaus partly from a sense of past obligation and partly because her niece never reproached her under privation.

Philippe and Joseph were cosseted. The old gambler of the Imperial Lottery cooked them good dinners and later could be persuaded to spare a few francs for pencils, shoes, or marbles. Madame Descoings’s passion for gambling forced her to be content with fifty francs a month for household expenses so she could risk the rest; Madame Bridau, by maternal love, kept her expenses to the same sum. As a penance for former overconfidence she cut off little enjoyments and glorified thrift as virtue. She mistrusted notary, aunt, and government solemn promises: Roguin’s assurance that Descoings’s assignment would restore the sold sum in seven years did not reassure her. She trusted only herself and practiced privation. She fancied that by laying aside three thousand francs every year from her pension she would have thirty thousand in ten years, yielding fifteen hundred a year for her children—if she lived long enough to see it. Thus two widows descended from hollow opulence to voluntary poverty—one through vice, the other through the purest virtue.

These petty domestic details matter: the view into student dens below, the tumult of the rapins, the need to look up at the sky to escape the damp street, the portrait full of soul, the sight of rich old colors in the calm home, the mother’s preference for her elder child and opposition to the younger’s tastes—these facts prefaced the story and help explain Joseph Bridau, who would become one of the greatest painters of the modern French school.

Philippe, the elder, strikingly resembled his mother: blond, blue-eyed, and with a daring look mistaken for intrepidity. Old Claparon, a ministry colleague and whist partner of Bridau, tapped Philippe’s cheek and prophesied he’d “stand to his guns.” Stimulated, the boy adopted a resolute manner, excelled at bodily exercises, and fought at the Lyceum, acquiring endurance and contempt for pain—the groundwork of military valor. He developed a distaste for study; public education failed to form both mind and body.

Agathe believed physical resemblance implied moral likeness and expected delicacy of feeling combined with manly vigor. Philippe’s winning ways confirmed her hopes. Joseph, three years younger, resembled his father only in defective ways: his thick black hair was always disorderly; he could not keep his clothes clean; new clothes looked old on him. Philippe’s vanity made him tend to his belongings; unconsciously Agathe scolded Joseph and held up his brother as example. Fetching the boys from school she worried especially about Joseph. Such trifles bred maternal preference.

Among the ordinary circle—Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, even the Abbé Loraux—no one noticed Joseph’s faculty for observation. Absorbed in his tastes, the future colorist paid little attention to himself; the torpor alarmed his father. His face, later to gain originality, wore a sullen expression, pinched and contracted by close attention to surroundings. Philippe flattered his mother with clever sayings; Joseph was taciturn, a dreamer. Agathe hoped great things of Philippe and expected nothing of Joseph.

Joseph’s leaning toward art began simply. During Easter 1812, returning from a walk in the Tuileries with Philippe and Madame Descoings, he saw a pupil drawing a caricature of a professor on the Institute wall and stopped in admiration. The next day he watched pupils enter by the rue Mazarin door, slipped into the long courtyard with statues, busts, half-finished marbles, plasters, and baked clays, and gazed feverishly—his instinct awakened. He entered a ground-floor room where young men drew from a statue; they mocked his ugliness and scattered crumbs at him. For a quarter hour Joseph bore the jeers in Chaudet’s atelier, but his persistency and expression struck them. When asked what he wanted, Joseph said he wished to learn to draw. They encouraged him; when he mentioned he was Madame Bridau’s son, they exclaimed that he would be a great man and tested him. One rough trial required him to hold out his arms motionless; at thirteen he stood five minutes and then ten, sweating but immovable. Then Chaudet, bald, pale, and sickly, entered. He lowered Joseph’s arms, patted him, asked his purpose, and, recalling Bridau’s service, welcomed the boy. He gave him board, paper, chalks, a pupil fetched cakes and sugar-plums, and Chaudet petted the child. The scene—rough, warm-hearted, and real—impressed Joseph. Chaudet, whose own prospects had once been opened by the Emperor and then cut short, appeared like a vision.

Joseph told his mother nothing but spent two hours every Sunday and Thursday in Chaudet’s atelier. Madame Descoings humored his fancies, supplying pencils, red chalks, prints, and paper. At school he sketched masters, drew comrades, and showed surprising assiduity in drawing class. Lemire, his drawing-master, reported Joseph’s faculty to Madame Bridau. Alarmed, she wept.

“What is to become of me!” she cried to Madame Descoings. “Joseph, whom I meant to make a government clerk—protected by his father’s memory at the ministry—wants to be a painter, a vagabond! I always knew that child would give me nothing but trouble.”

Madame Descoings confessed she had encouraged him, taking him to the Salon where his interest in pictures was extraordinary.

“If he understands painting at thirteen,” she said, “your Joseph will be a man of genius.”

“Yes; and see what genius did for his father—killed him with overwork at forty!” Agathe answered.

At fourteen, Agathe, despite Madame Descoings’s entreaties, went to Chaudet and demanded he cease to “debauch” her son. She found the sculptor in a blue smock, modeling with spasmodic jerks born of passion. Initially rough, Chaudet was moved by Bridau’s memory and, furious at the plea to turn the child from his vocation, declared that a real calling must be obeyed: an artist was a king in his realm, happier and independent; to force Joseph into a clerk’s life would be murder. He dismissed his work for the day and refused to give up the boy.

Agathe, shocked by the sight of a nude in the studio, left horrified. Chaudet forbade his pupils to admit the little Bridau; they jeered as she shut the door. When students learned she opposed the Institute, they plotted to get Joseph among them. He often slipped into Regnauld the painter’s studio and was encouraged to paint. When the widow complained, Chaudet’s pupils insisted they were not Chaudet and composed a long mocking song about her. That evening Agathe refused cards and sat drowned in tears. “She thinks her boy will have to beg his bread because he has the bump of painting,” Madame Descoings explained. The men offered usual advice: let the boy follow his bent; if not a genius, he could later be placed in an office. Their unanimity only increased Agathe’s anxieties. She feared the atelier’s naked women; Madame Descoings joked about “good fires.”

Soon after came the disasters of the retreat from Moscow and Napoleon’s return to organize troops. Philippe, tired of school, wanted to serve; he saw a review at the Tuileries—the last Napoleon ever held—and was swept by soldierly splendor. Without his mother’s knowledge he petitioned the Emperor and, within twenty-four hours, was sent to the Imperial Lyceum at Saint-Cyr. Six months later, in November 1813, he was appointed sub-lieutenant in a cavalry regiment. He spent the winter in cantonments, learned to ride, and was dispatched to the front. During the campaign in France he was made lieutenant after an outpost affair where his bravery saved his colonel’s life. The Emperor named him captain at La Fère-Champenoise and took him on his staff. Philippe won the cross at Montereau, witnessed Napoleon’s farewell at Fontainebleau, refused to serve the Bourbons, and returned in July 1814 to find his mother ruined.

Joseph’s scholarship was withdrawn after the holidays; Madame Bridau vainly entreated that it be recorded on the ministry rolls. Joseph, more devoted than ever, begged to go to Regnauld and promised to earn his living, saying he was an advanced second-class student who could do without rhetoric. Philippe, now captain at nineteen and decorated, flattered the mother immensely. Coarse and blustering, without merit beyond cavalry bravery, he was to her a man of genius; Joseph, puny, sickly, unkempt, and dreaming an artist’s glory, would only worry her.

The winter of 1814–1815 favored Joseph. Secretly encouraged by Madame Descoings and Bixiou, a pupil of Gros, he worked in Gros’s celebrated atelier, forming intimacy with Schinner. The return from Elba came; Captain Bridau joined Napoleon at Lyons, accompanied him to the Tuileries, and commanded a squadron in the Guard’s dragoons. After Waterloo—where he was slightly wounded and received the officer’s Legion of Honor—he was near Marshal Davoust at Saint-Denis and, through Davoust’s intercession, retained his cross and rank though placed on half-pay.

Joseph, anxious about his future, studied with ardor through turbulent times, sometimes to the point of illness. “It is the smell of the paints,” Agathe told Madame Descoings. “He ought to give up a business so injurious to his health.” Agathe’s anxieties remained chiefly for her lieutenant-colonel son. When he returned in 1816 reduced from a nine-thousand-franc commander’s salary to half-pay, she fitted attic rooms for him and spent her savings. Philippe belonged to the faithful Bonapartes of café Lemblin; he acquired and exaggerated the airs of a half-pay officer, spurned the Bourbons, and refused infantry opportunities. To his mother he seemed noble. “The father himself could have done no more,” she said.

Philippe’s half-pay sufficed; he cost little at home, whereas Joseph’s expenses were paid by the widows. From then Agathe’s preference for Philippe was openly shown. Up to that time it had been secret; the colonel’s persecution for the Emperor and his wound drew forth maternal tenderness. “He is unfortunate” explained everything to her. Joseph, simple in the artist’s way and brought up to admire his big brother, was not hurt by maternal preference and shared his worship of the hero who had carried Napoleon’s orders. How could he doubt the superiority of the brother he had seen in green and gold, commanding on the Champ de Mars?

Agathe, notwithstanding preference, was an excellent mother. She loved Joseph, though she could not understand him. Joseph adored her; Philippe let her adore him. Toward her the dragoon softened his military roughness yet never concealed contempt for Joseph—expressed in a patronizing, almost friendly way. He nicknamed the weak, shrunken seventeen-year-old “Cub.” Philippe’s patronizing manners would have wounded anyone less indifferent than Joseph, who believed soldiers hid goodness beneath brutal exteriors. Joseph did not yet know that soldiers of genius can be gentle; all genius is alike.

“Poor boy!” Philippe said to his mother. “We mustn’t plague him; let him do as he likes.” To Agathe, the colonel’s contempt seemed fraternal affection. “Philippe will always love and protect his brother,” she thought.

In 1816 Joseph obtained his mother’s permission to convert the garret next to his attic into an atelier; Madame Descoings contributed some money. The two widows treated painting as a trade, but Joseph arranged the humble studio himself: Madame Descoings persuaded the owner to add a skylight; Joseph painted the hall chocolate and hung sketches. Agathe reluctantly installed an iron stove so her son could work at home without abandoning Gros’s or Schinner’s studios.

The constitutional party, backed by half-pay officers and Bonapartists, stirred emeutes around the Chamber of Deputies; conspiracies were rumored. Philippe, flirting with these affairs, was arrested and released for lack of proof; the minister of war restored him to the active list, ending his half-pay. France seemed unsafe; Agathe feared provocateurs. Philippe spent time in disaffected cafés, drinking and acquiring bad habits, while Agathe lived in mortal dread. The Grecian sages still visited Madame Bridau nightly; Claparon, retained at the ministry though cautious, whispered Moniteur news. Desroches, retired after old Du Bruel, still fought for his pension. Their friends urged Agathe to send Philippe abroad.

“They talk of conspiracies, and your son will be certain to fall a victim,” they warned.

“Philippe is cut from the wood the Emperor made into marshals,” Du Bruel replied; “he mustn’t give up his profession. Let him serve in the East, in India—”

“Think of his health,” Agathe answered.

“Why doesn’t he get some place, some business?” Desroches asked. “There are private offices to be had.” Agathe insisted Philippe was a soldier and wouldn’t want anything else. Du Bruel suggested royal favor could make him lieutenant-colonel, though Madame Descoings doubted his prospects without noble birth.

To please his mother, Philippe took the notion of joining General Lallemand in the United States and helping found the Champ d’Asile, one of the most disastrous swindles carried off under the name of a national subscription. Agathe gave ten thousand francs and saw him off at Havre. By the end of 1817 she lived on the six hundred francs left from her funds and, by prudent investment, secured seven percent on some savings. Joseph emulated her frugality: dressed plainly, denied himself comforts, and lived on bread, milk, and Brie; only Madame Descoings and Bixiou showed sympathy.

Joseph later recalled how the coming summer saved him the cost of charcoal. Now a colorist as capable as Gros, he consulted his master only occasionally. He was already planning a revolt against classical traditions, aiming to free art to represent Nature — a struggle that began with his first Salon exhibition and never ceased.

1818 was terrible for the family. Roguin, Madame Descoings’s notary, absconded with seven years’ withheld annuity funds. Three days later a bill of exchange for a thousand francs arrived from New York: Philippe had lost everything in the Champ d’Asile. The letter told of debts contracted and comrades who had indorsed for him. Agathe, blaming herself, wept. The two women faced selling silver, linen, and furniture. Joseph, alarmed, told Gérard, who secured an order for two royal copies of Louis XVIII’s portrait at five hundred francs each and supplied Joseph with materials. Joseph painted four panels in ten days, sold them, and brought his mother the thousand francs to meet Philippe’s draft. Then came a letter announcing Philippe’s imminent return on a packet whose captain had trusted him for passage; he would need a thousand francs at Havre.

Joseph promised his mother the money after finishing the copies. Agathe, ecstatically grateful, kissed him and called him “his father all over.” Madame Bridau rushed to Havre and watched beyond the round tower daily for the packet. The sight of home softened Philippe, but his misfortunes in Texas and New York had developed his worst traits: he had grown brutal, selfish, rude, drank and smoked excessively, and imagined perpetual persecution. Such men either live by honest virtues or by treating every want as a necessity; Philippe had drifted into the latter, becoming cunning and self-centered while keeping an appearance of frank soldierly simplicity. His stature had grown somewhat rotund, his face bronzed, his speech terse and decisive; exposed to hardship, he had become the kind of man who makes himself feared. To his mother he remained a hero; in truth he had become, bluntly, a blackguard.

At Havre Agathe bought him clothes and could not, at first, restrain his indulgence. The scheme of conquering Texas with imperial remnants had failed less for lack of idea than for treachery and poor management; the Liberals had used the enterprise selfishly, abandoning the devoted heroes. Agathe, a household manager with shrewd common sense, saw through the political chicane and recognized the subscription’s failure; she could not make her son see he had been duped rather than persecuted. During the costly week at Havre she could not persuade him to make terms with the royal government. She brought him back to Paris before her funds gave out.

On arrival, those awaiting them noted how much Agathe had aged. Philippe greeted Madame Descoings coolly. Joseph, now twenty-one and confident, was hurt by his brother’s contempt. At a family dinner the friends — Du Bruel, Claparon, Desroches, and the Abbé Loraux — observed the exile’s roughened manners and coarse voice. While Joseph prepared the card tables, the intimate friends asked Agathe what she intended for Philippe. She could not get him to serve the Bourbons; Du Bruel suggested other roles like writing plays, as his son had once done. Philippe, Bixiou, and young Desroches played écarté. Madame Descoings, eager to celebrate the prodigal’s return, provided funds for play; Joseph secretly slipped twenty francs to Philippe.

The evening grew lively. Philippe, a poor player, ended owing fifty francs. He showed bad temper and quarreled with young Desroches and then with Desroches senior, who forbade further play though his son was right. Madame Descoings silenced her grandson’s barbed witticisms for fear of provoking the colonel. Philippe's temper and gambling continued into the night.

The next morning the two widows, counting resources, resolved to economize: Madame Descoings proposed selling furniture and giving up apartments to save seven hundred francs a year and pay Philippe fifty francs monthly until he found something. They pooled incomes — about five thousand three hundred francs, four thousand from Madame Descoings as a life annuity — and had consumed their savings. Philippe promised to find a situation and asked only for board and lodging. Agathe kissed him; Madame Descoings slipped him a hundred francs to cover his losses.

Within ten days the furniture was sold and the household rearranged. Philippe’s habits hardened: he left after breakfast, returned at dinner, and spent evenings in cafés drinking brandy, reading newspapers, playing billiards at the Café Minerve, smoking many cigars, and gambling at night. He boasted of a regular life and, while often winning, returned home talking of smashing the ultras and the Bourbon bodyguard.

He dined often with army comrades who spoke freely of hopes based on plots and inventions like a submarine to restore the Emperor. He particularly liked an old dragoons’ captain, Giroudeau, whose company led Philippe into further dissipation and into seeing women as resources. Giroudeau introduced Philippe to the theatre and to a young dancer, Florentine, and to another, Marie Godeschal — Mariette — who was beautiful, cold, and ambitious. Mariette, orphaned and poor, had a devoted brother who clerked in Derville’s law office; she resolved to use her beauty and talent to help him. Poorly dressed yet striking, she provoked Philippe’s passion. To Mariette, Philippe was a Guard staff-officer, and a liaison with him would elevate her above rivals.

Giroudeau promised to speak to his nephew Finot at a theatrical journal and to get Philippe a place on a newspaper. Philippe revealed his poverty, but Giroudeau reassured him: his nephew could put him on a paper or help him get patronage. Mariette, thinking Philippe would write for a newspaper and help her career, accepted him.

At Finot’s attic, the nephew — cool and sardonic — eyed Philippe. Finot mocked the Texan affair, then gave a plan: exploit his position as a victim of the Liberals and demand an accounting of the Champ d’Asile subscription. A new Opposition journal was starting; Finot could make Philippe its cashier with a salary of three thousand francs if Philippe found someone to go security for twenty thousand francs. “Threaten loudly,” Finot advised, “and we’ll silence you with a place.” Philippe was grateful and impressed by Finot’s worldly cleverness.

That night at cafés Philippe fulminated against the Liberals, declaring he would demand an accounting of the moneys collected. He went to Mariette to tell her of the expected post on a newspaper with ten thousand subscribers and how he would advance her claims.

Amid the political alarm following the assassination of the Duc de Berry, Agathe and Madame Descoings waited anxiously. Philippe returned with good news: he had been promised the cashiership at three thousand francs on condition they could go security for twenty thousand francs by depositing their shares in the Funds as guarantee while drawing the interest. The friends urged Agathe to go security; she and Madame Descoings, overjoyed at the prospect of providing for Philippe, transferred her shares — representing thirteen hundred francs in interest — to secure him. Philippe promised to pay one hundred francs monthly for board and lodging and was acclaimed the best of sons. Those who had doubted him congratulated Agathe and admitted they had been unjust. Joseph, not to be outdone, resolved to pay for his own support and succeeded.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Three months later the colonel, who ate and drank enough for four, began to find fault with the household provisions and, appealing to his pay, insisted the poor widows spend more on their table though he had not yet paid a penny. His mother and Madame Descoings, out of delicacy, would not press him for the promise. The year passed without a single one of Leon Gozlan’s so-called “tigers with five claws” ever finding its way from Philippe’s pocket to the family purse. Philippe quieted his conscience by seldom dining at home.

“Well, he is happy,” said his mother; “he is easy in mind; he has a place.”

Through Vernou’s feuilleton, who was intimate with Bixiou, Finot, and Giroudeau, Mariette was announced not at the Panorama-Dramatique but at the Porte-Saint-Martin, where she triumphed alongside the famous Begrand. Among the theatre’s directors was a rich general who had become an impresario for an actress he adored; he knew Philippe and Giroudeau. Finot’s journal and Philippe himself heralded Mariette’s first appearance, arranged promptly by the three men—a little conspiracy of vanity and folly.

Bixiou, always mischievous, revealed to his grandmother and to the devout Agathe that Philippe, the heroic cashier, was in love with Mariette, the celebrated ballet-dancer. The news struck the widows like a clap of thunder. Agathe, whose religious scruples saw actresses as “brands in the burning,” and Madame Descoings, imagining women on the stage living off gold and pearls, were aghast.

“Do you suppose my brother is such a fool as to spend his money on Mariette?” Joseph asked his mother. Bixiou soothed them, predicting Mariette’s swift move to the Opera and hinting an ambassador was infatuated with her—thus implying Philippe’s danger might be brief. He also related the miserly funeral of old Claparon, arranged cheaply by his banker son, and wove comic moralities from it.

Philippe, driven partly by mercenary motives, proposed marriage to Mariette; she refused, expecting engagement at the Grand Opera and preferring to keep her independence. For the rest of the year Philippe was rarely home—either at his office, the theatre, or with Mariette. His household knew nothing; his friends Giroudeau, Finot, Bixiou, Vernou, and Lousteau saw him living for pleasure. He shared the amusements of singers and dancers, and after four o’clock until midnight he was typically at some dinner, card party, or supper. Philippe relished this carnival life.

When Mariette appeared at the Opera in January 1821, she captured one of the highest dukes in Louis XVIII’s court. Philippe, who tried to oppose the noble, was driven by passion and ran into gambling losses. By April he had begun dipping into the funds entrusted to him as cashier, and by May he had taken eleven hundred francs. In that fatal May Mariette went to London to seek further patronage while the new opera house was being prepared. Philippe, consumed by love though despised by Mariette as a dull grasping soldier, found himself left behind; she had never meant to remain faithful to him, using men as rungs on a ladder. Yet she bore a peculiar gratitude to the first man who had eased her way.

Unable to follow Mariette, Philippe returned to his attic room in his mother’s apartment and brooded. The pleasures that had become a habit—dinners, suppers, enlivening social evenings, the fever of wits and journalists—were now necessities to him. The idea of life without Mariette’s glamorous atmosphere preyed on him; he even contemplated suicide, not because of the deficit but because he could not live without continual enjoyments.

Full of gloom he went to his brother’s painting-room, where Joseph was copying a picture for a dealer and spoke of his modest earnings as an artist. Philippe asked Joseph how long it would take him to paint his portrait; Joseph said three or four days—too long for Philippe, who claimed he had but one day and declared, with forced gaiety, that he was going never to return. Alarmed, Joseph pressed him and learned, to his and their mother’s horror, that Philippe had taken eleven hundred francs from the funds he administered and must render his accounts the next day. He had lost half the family security and said he could not live under dishonor. Joseph swore secrecy but pleaded that the money could be repaid; Philippe, declaring he would shoot himself rather than live dishonored, fled.

Joseph told their mother and Madame Descoings. The revelation stunned them: the son of Bridau to steal the money entrusted to him! The old women alternated between terror and consoling rationalizations. Madame Descoings, who saw similar lapses every day and judged them not entirely fatal, tried to comfort Agathe, who could only picture her son dead. They sought to lessen the sin; Agathe could not accept her son living under dishonor and begged him to return. The day was spent in cruel alternations of hope and dread, every sound at the window prompting fresh conjectures. Meanwhile Philippe, quietly audacious, prepared his defense at the office.

He presented accounts claiming he had withdrawn eleven hundred francs for safekeeping at home. Then, at five, he took five hundred francs more from the desk and went—against all propriety for an officer and cashier—to the gambling-house he had avoided since he took the cashier post. At first he won, gaining as much as six thousand francs. Thinking to erase his deficit in one stroke, he risked everything; fortune seemed to favour him when the wheel turned in his favour, but eventually intermittent “divining” senses deserted him and he lost all. Fatigued by the strain, he returned home oblivious to suicide, dishonour, or his family’s anguish. Seeing him pale and woe-begone, his mother knelt, kissed his hands, and entreated him not to kill himself. His heart touched by their tears, Philippe embraced them, said their pity gave him life, and took the indulgent rescue as a reprieve.

Madame Descoings and Agathe arranged an excellent dinner and indulgences such as cigars, making sacrifices to soothe him. The household forgave him; Agathe did not yet know all the consequences. At daybreak Agathe, having weathered a sleepless night, confronted the stark financial reality: she was reduced to six hundred francs a year. Madame Descoings, whose heaviness suggested a short future and with four thousand francs at stake in her life, was the other pillar of family security. Agathe resolved she would nurse rather than be supported by her children. Yet the two widows were practical: Madame Descoings proposed that Desroches, an old friend and shrewd clerk, spin a story to the press and authorities that Philippe had been victimized and was unfit to continue as cashier—thus persuading them to accept his resignation and avoid scandal. This lie, the two hoped, would preserve honor.

Agathe embraced the plan, and Desroches carried it out with the cool efficiency of a clerk loyal to Bridau’s family. He dined with them, urged Agathe to go the next day to the Treasury to transfer funds and secure her pension, and obliged Philippe to sign a petition to the minister of war asking reinstatement in active service. Desroches promised to follow up and to exploit the duke’s triumph over Philippe for influence. He predicted Philippe would be made lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment within three months. The press ceased its scrutiny in two months, as Finot had foreseen. Publicly, Philippe’s crime had no consequences; but Agathe’s faith in her son was mortally wounded and she lived thereafter in fear mingled with occasional satisfactions as the worst had not been realized.

When men like Philippe—brave in body but cowardly in honor—observe that life goes on after near catastrophe and that family kindness buys impunity, they grow bold. They learn how to bend the social rules to their benefit. A fortnight after the crisis, Philippe resumed the café life: drams, billiards with punch, nightly gambling where he risked modest stakes, which he won enough to cover his dissipations. To deceive his mother and Madame Descoings, he cultivated an appearance of thrift: a greasy, rubbed hat, patched boots, a shabby overcoat with a discoloured ribbon, worn gloves, and a ragged neckcloth. Mariette remained the sole object of his affection; her treachery had hardened his heart. When flush, he followed slum women with contempt; otherwise he breakfasted and dined at home and returned about one o’clock—three months of this life restored Agathe’s confidence.

Joseph, meanwhile, worked in his atelier on a promising picture he hoped would make his reputation. Madame Descoings, believing Bixiou’s prediction of Joseph’s future fame, tended him like a mother—bringing breakfast, running errands, blacking his boots. Joseph was absent until dinner and spent evenings at the Cenacle among friends, giving himself the deep self-education of a man of talent. Agathe, seeing little of him and feeling no anxiety, concentrated on Philippe, whose alternations of fear and calm consumed her maternal attention. Desroches, who visited weekly, brought hopes that the Duc de Maufrigneuse had asked for Philippe, the minister of war had ordered an inquiry, and reinstatement would come early in the year.

To this end Desroches used all his influence. At the prefecture of police he learned of Philippe’s gambling and advised Madame Descoings, discreetly, to watch him; one indiscretion might imperil the entire plan. Agathe sat in the chimney-corner reading prayers; Madame Descoings, less pious, consulted the cards, interpreted dreams, and applied cabalistic rules to her lottery obsession. She pursued a “trey” of three numbers she believed long overdue—one of its numbers having, she fancied, been absent from wheels for twenty-one years. Convinced it would finally pay, she hoarded savings sewn into her mattress to pile on a last, enormous stake. She saw the lottery as a social opium for the poor, a five-sous chance at five days of happiness that allowed hope where nothing else could. Joseph was indulgent toward this folly; he listened to her dreams without scolding and soothed her.

Madame Descoings’s passion for the trey and Joseph’s absorption in art became a domestic pattern: each member loved something—Philippe tobacco and drink, Agathe God, Desroches the law, Joseph the “beau idéal.” Joseph, who had once used Madame Descoings as a model, was painting a large canvas—an old woman leading a young courtesan to a Doge of Venice—which promised great success when completed. This masterpiece, later mistaken by Gros for a Titian, would secure recognition for Joseph at the Salon of 1823. But for the present he had to work for dealers, copying masters and learning their methods; the discipline improved his brush. Practical and frugal about his earnings, Joseph concealed the small savings he had begun to accumulate, knowing both his mother and Madame Descoings had their own roads to ruin—one through Philippe’s dissipations, the other through the lottery.

To hide his savings, Joseph bought an old chest with carved bas-reliefs and used a skull in a compartment as a receptacle for his monthly reserve. Since Philippe’s return, he noticed a steady discrepancy between his recorded spending and the small sums lying in the skull. At first he blamed carelessness; the discrepancy persisted, then grew. He confided his suspicions to Madame Descoings, whom he trusted with a tenderness he had never received from his own mother. Together they watched. The next day they discovered the thief: Philippe had been entering the studio when Joseph was absent and taking the little sums he needed. The poor painter, torn between brotherly affection and outrage, resolved to catch him.

Madame Descoings wrapped the matter in maternal pity—“Poor boy! he wants tobacco; he’s accustomed to it”—but Joseph, more outraged, took a sterner view. He had long considered Philippe a dead weight, a man who lived the life of a Sardanapalus while his and their mother’s fortunes were imperilled; he predicted Philippe would end by bringing the family to beggary and suggested emigration to divert him. Bixiou’s censure—“He is a downright rogue, that brother of yours”—echoed Joseph’s private scorn. For Joseph the peril was not only financial but moral: Philippe’s conduct threatened the family’s honor repeatedly and without learning. Yet Joseph’s passion for art, his deep study, and his determination to succeed sustained him. He observed his companions and noted how each human passion found its outlet: his brother’s stupidity and vice, Madame Descoings’s gambling, his mother’s piety,—all were human foibles he could turn into motives and material for his genius.

Thus the household lived in uneasy equilibrium: Agathe’s maternal devotion and shrinking income; Madame Descoings’s lottery hopes and domestic benevolence; Joseph’s quiet artistry and growing suspicion; Philippe’s reckless pleasure-seeking and occasional remorse. Desroches, for his part, continued to smooth ways within administration and society to shield the family name, while the press and acquaintances arranged the narrative that made Philippe a victim rather than a criminal. The result was temporary peace and a reconstitution of the family’s external respectability—the very mercy that encouraged Philippe’s relapse into vice, and the domestic arrangements that allowed him to pilfer from the only brother who worked, even as Joseph prepared the picture destined to bring him fame and, perhaps, to save them all from ruin.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Despite Philippe’s outward coolness at his nightly gambling, he was often “cleaned out.” To get his ten-franc stake each evening he robbed the household—his brother’s pocket, whatever coins Madame Descoings or Agathe left about. After a dreadful dream in which he stole every coin from her gown, Madame Descoings could no longer deny the truth. Agathe, pretending to sleep, wept through the night. Madame Descoings’s old remark, “One wrong act is not a vice,” repeated enough times, had ceased to comfort: Agathe saw that her son lacked delicacy and honor.

The morning after the vision Agathe entreated Philippe to ask her for whatever he needed. His requests became so frequent that in two weeks Agathe’s savings were gone. Penniless and shrunken by worry, she and her aunt discussed taking low-paid needlework. Madame Descoings watched Agathe decline; the young woman’s face lost its freshness, her eyes grew dim, sleep fled her. A consuming inward fire ate her; she listened each night for Philippe’s step, learning the nuances of his walk, voice, even the sound of his cane—the clues that told her whether he had won or lost. When drunk and victorious his walk dragged and his voice gruff; when he had lost, his step was sharp and his manner angry. Debauchery coarsened him: his features grew hard, eyelashes fell out, his breath and boots smelled of taverns.

At the end of December Madame Descoings suggested Philippe get new clothes; he answered bitterly that his mother had no money and he lived on a mortgaged pension. Joseph defended the need for decency; Agathe begged Joseph to be indulgent. Philippe dismissed political loyalty as no excuse for slovenliness and spoke of a “debt of honor” to Giroudeau who had lent him money by way of Florentine. He excused shabby boots by invoking Napoleon selling his plate at Saint Helena—an absurd romanticism to cover shame.

One day Philippe learned, or guessed, that Madame Descoings had hidden twenty napoleons in her mattress as payment for a heavy lottery stake—the famed Paris “trey.” Obsessed with the sixteen-page dream of easy wealth that the trey promised, Madame Descoings kept the money tucked away. Philippe watched and schemed. When the household went out at midday and left the door locked, he feigned forgetting something and had a locksmith let him in. He searched the bed, ripped open the ticking, took twenty napoleons, and remade the bed so no one would suspect.

Intent on multiplying this theft, Philippe adopted the gambler’s regimen of short, measured sessions. But gambling’s science—its bank’s apparent impassibility, the quick, mechanical operation of the tables, the cunning of croupiers—preys on the best self-control. Philippe, however, had the cold nerve of a veteran commander; he could stare into the abyss with no vertigo. With four hundred francs he went to the Palais-Royal gambling-house, and in half an hour walked out with seven thousand. He paid those he owed and returned. By six he had twenty-five thousand; by ten he had seventy-five thousand. Drunk with success, he broke his rule at midnight and played longer; by three in the morning the one hundred and fifty thousand he'd briefly amassed had vanished back to the bank. Exhausted and drunk, he was carried away by a waiter to a lodging-house and left there to sleep off a nervous fever. Thus, in a single night, he had brought mourning, poverty, and ruin into his home.

The night before, as dinner waited, Agathe and Madame Descoings expected Philippe. Alice—Agathe—always went to bed early for midnight mass; Madame Descoings and Joseph lingered with dreams of the trey and plans for doubles and singles. When Joseph innocently asked where the four hundred francs to “pay up” the stake were kept, the old woman led him and Agathe to her bed-chamber and opened the mattress to show him the savings. She found only the torn ticking. Overcome, she fainted; Joseph and Agathe revived her with ordinary remedies. She had felt the coins when she made the bed that morning and knew they were gone. “HE has taken them, the monster!” she cried.

Agathe, distraught, questioned the concierge and learned of Philippe’s return and the locksmith. The old woman, white and moving like a spectre, repeated her conviction. She begged Agathe to sell her silverware if need be; Agathe produced the box of plate only to find a pawnbroker’s ticket where the spoons had been. The falsehood—that she had pawned them secretly to replenish the mattress—collapsed; the box was empty. Silence and mutual accusation filled the room until Joseph, moved, ran to his studio and returned with three hundred francs—the fruit of his frugal life—placing it in Madame Descoings’s hands. She refused, torn between hope in the trey and abhorrence of taking such help. Still, Joseph had acted: affection triumphing over vice in an understated domestic heroism. At midnight Joseph dashed out to find a lottery office open to pay the stakes; he failed, and the tickets under the Palais-Royal portico were already sold paid-up.

Agathe and her aunt went to mass; Joseph went to bed dejected. At dawn they rose late: had Madame Descoings paid, she could have checked the results by half-past nine. But she had not. Old Desroches brought the printed winning numbers and announced that her trey had come up—three millions. The news struck the old woman like a thunderbolt. She collapsed; a doctor was sent for. The thin hope that had been her life—twenty years of sacrifice and waiting—was realized only to be thwarted by the robbery. The shock threw her into apoplexy; she lay unconscious, murmuring “Three millions!” when she returned to consciousness late that afternoon. As she lay there, the house was silent and exhausted.

Philippe returned that evening, disordered and feverish. Agathe and Joseph were waiting, their grief raw. Philippe arrived staggering, flushed and raving. When confronted, he defied them: if his mother had taken twenty thousand francs from him, he felt he had a right to take twenty napoleons from her mattress; he called his nightly theft repayment. He ridiculed their melodrama and claimed he was “the glory of the family,” while they called him “the disgrace.” In fury Joseph struck him down; a struggle ensued. Bixiou and Doctor Haudry entered; Joseph had overpowered his brother. The doctor, seeing Philippe’s state—chilled, teeth chattering, with legs swollen—ordered him undressed and put to bed. When Agathe cut off his boots, ten pieces of gold rolled out onto the floor—coins Philippe had forgotten he had hid in his footwear. He muttered that had he remembered them earlier he might have won the trey; then fell into a raging delirium.

Philippe’s fever was violent. They removed him to his room, and the doctor, fearing self-harm, borrowed a strait-waistcoat from the hospital. At nine o’clock his madness quieted somewhat. Abbé Loraux and Desroches tried to comfort Agathe, who would not be consoled. Only Joseph and Madame Descoings knew the depth of her final inward wound.

On her deathbed, Madame Descoings obtained from Joseph a solemn promise: he would not let Philippe ruin Agathe and would convert her property into a life annuity for her niece. She died on December 31, five days after the seizure that began when she discovered the loss. The five hundred francs left in the house barely sufficed for the funeral; some small silver and furniture were sold. Reduced to an eight-hundred-franc annuity and stripped of the capital, Agathe sold what she could, gave up the third-floor apartment, and prepared to work.

Philippe’s convalescence was slow. When he seemed better, Agathe told him kindly that the costs of his illness had consumed their ready money and that she would have to work; she urged him, gently, to re-enter the army and support himself. He sneered, said she and Joseph did not love him, and declared he preferred life alone. When Agathe implored him to make himself worthy of their affection, he interrupted with scorn, pulled on his shabby hat, and left. She gave him a hundred francs wrapped in paper; he took it, embraced her without warmth, and said he would visit Florentine, Giroudeau’s mistress, because “they are real friends.” Agathe, trembling and bereft, fell to her knees and prayed that God would take her unnatural child into His keeping—her maternal love broken and abdicated.

Thus ended the household’s quiet tragedy: Philippe’s gambling and theft shattered the hopes and health of those who loved him, Madame Descoings’s long-awaited fortune slipped through their fingers, and a mother’s last charity replaced where judgment could not. The family was left impoverished and morally exhausted, the wounded ones carrying the scars of betrayal and sacrifice.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By February 1822 Madame Bridau had taken the attic room over the kitchen of her old apartment; Joseph’s studio and bedroom faced it across the stair. Seeing his mother reduced, Joseph refurnished the garret with an artist’s neatness: a rug, a simple bed, glazed cotton on the walls, a double door with a portière, and a blind to soften the light. Though Agathe’s life in Paris was now penniless and plain, Joseph tried to make it decent.

To spare her frugal housekeeping, Joseph took Agathe daily to dine at a table d’hôte on the rue de Beaune frequented by well-bred women, deputies, and the titled; dinners cost ninety francs a month. Agathe paid only for breakfast and enjoyed the remnant habits of her married life. Alarmed when she learned the dinner’s cost and ignorant of how her son earned money (“painting naked women” never occurred to her), she obtained through her confessor, the Abbé Loraux, an office in the Comtesse de Bauvan’s lottery—an appointment worth seven hundred francs a year. Lottery offices often sufficed to support a manager’s family. The Comtesse promised the manager’s place when it vacated; meanwhile Agathe took an assistant’s post at six hundred francs, working from ten until midnight. For two years Joseph fetched her every night; friends saw him come straight from the opera or a salon to be punctual at the rue Vivienne office.

Agathe settled into the monotonous regularity that civil misfortune brings. Mornings she tidied her room—no cats or birds now—prepared breakfast for Joseph, arranged his bedroom, tended the fire, and sat sewing by the iron stove, retiring when a comrade or model entered. She understood nothing of art and made no pretence; amazed at the fuss about color and composition, she later found no reason for painters’ disputes. She made Joseph’s shirts, mended stockings, cleaned his palette, supplied rags, and kept the studio in order. Joseph noticed her care over trivial things and lavished tenderness on her; their relationship was affectionate rather than intellectual.

One morning, while Joseph sketched a large picture, Agathe asked about Philippe as if casually: Had he suffered? Had poverty changed him? Joseph denied it, saying Philippe kept carnival in New York as in Paris, and that if he were suffering he’d gladly give him money but did not want to see him—“he killed our poor Descoings.” Agathe hoped Joseph would paint Philippe’s portrait; Joseph consented for her sake, calling Philippe “your passion.”

That afternoon Joseph found Philippe in the rue du Sentier. Philippe had taken Giroudeau’s place as cashier at a weekly journal run by Finot’s nephew; his salary was cut and now amounted to six hundred francs plus five hundred from his cross—enough for a warm office day and theatre passes at night. He lived with journalists and actresses, oddly well behaved at the office. He agreed to sit for a portrait in his uniform, saying two Sundays would suffice.

When Joseph told Agathe, she trembled with joy and decorated the studio with flowers. On the first Sunday Philippe sent his uniform and breakfast was prepared by Agathe, who wept silently. He posed for several hours, then dressed ordinary and proposed dining at the Palais-Royal, jingling gold. Joseph, uneasy at seeing him flaunt money, hesitated, but Agathe insisted. Philippe gave a splendid dinner costing a hundred francs and later took them to the theatre; Agathe feigned illness and refused to go, returning to rue Mazarin in gloom.

At the next sitting Agathe was visibly present, interrogating Philippe about his lodgings—he lodged with journalists and actresses, ate kirsch before posing, boasted of future greatness but was reticent about means. Joseph, saying the Salon was near and he needed money for frames and to finish a copy of a Rubens ordered by dealer Elie Magus, postponed the sitting till the next Sunday. That evening Joseph prepared a stratagem: he varnished his finished copy of the Rubens, placed it where the original had been, and set the original on his easel. Pierre Grassou, a fellow painter, was taken in and delighted. Magus did not come as agreed; Joseph left the key with the concierge and dined with Grassou.

An hour later Philippe arrived, obtained the key, went upstairs, took the copy thinking it the original, returned the key claiming he’d forgotten something, and rushed off to sell the Rubens for three thousand francs. He had sent Magus a note asking him not to call till the next day. When Joseph returned that night with his mother, the concierge told him Philippe’s trick. Joseph feared ruin and ran to the studio. Relieved to find the original still there, he cursed Philippe as a “vile scoundrel.” Agathe, bewildered, wept when told. Joseph insisted they warn the concierge and keep the keys themselves; he vowed to paint his brother’s blackguard face from memory. Agathe, heart-broken, asked him to leave the portrait as it was—she could not bear to look at it.

Philippe knew the money from the Rubens would have been used to prevent his brother’s ruin, yet he still took the copy. After this crime Agathe never mentioned him; her face grew cold and bitter. One thought possessed her: “Some day we shall hear of a Bridau in the police courts.”

Two months later Giroudeau, an old dragoon and supposed friend of Philippe’s, appeared on urgent business. He was a debauched figure—fishy gray eyes, greasy hair, a faded coat with a Legion of Honor ribbon, gold rings in his ears. He told them Philippe could no longer work at the office; lodged with Mademoiselle Florentine in a miserable attic, he was dying and needed a doctor and medicines or he would be taken to the hospital. He had pledged his pension and had nothing. Giroudeau and Florentine would pay half the cost if Agathe paid the rest—three hundred francs for a nurse and two months’ care. Agathe thanked him but said the son was banished from her heart and she had no money—she worked in the lottery office to avoid burdening Joseph.

Giroudeau pressed Joseph; Joseph declared Giroudeau a swindler and refused. Giroudeau left mortified. Three months later, in July, as Agathe crossed the Pont Neuf she saw a man along the Quai de l’Ecole who looked like Philippe in the last stages of poverty: ragged neckcloth, scurfy hat, patched boots, threadbare coat, dirty collar, black hands, a waistcoat in place of a shirt. His nearly bald head and sunken face showed he had just left the Hôpital du Midi. His blue overcoat still bore the ribbon of his cross; passersby noticed the rosette with curiosity and pity.

Agathe trembled. Forgetting past crimes—stealing, gambling, debauchery—she saw only a man recovering from illness and likely to die of starvation. Impulsively she thrust her purse into Philippe’s hand and fled as if guilty. For two days she ate nothing, haunted by the image of her son dying in Paris; at forty-seven she came to look seventy. Her eyes were dimmed with tears and prayers. But worse followed: a conspiracy among officers was discovered and the Moniteur printed lists of arrests. Agathe heard Philippe’s name in her lottery office and fainted; the manager, understanding, gave her two weeks’ leave.

Joseph consulted Desroches about Philippe. While Joseph spoke to the younger Desroches—now reputed a clever lawyer—Giroudeau called on the widow Desroches and persuaded Agathe that twelve thousand francs would secure Philippe’s release by buying the silence of two witnesses. Desperate, Agathe promised to get the money without knowing how. She wrote to her godmother, Madame Hochon, begging her to ask Jean-Jacques Rouget to send or lend the sum and promising to repay in two years. Madame Hochon’s reply, returned by courier the next day, refused. She explained that her brother Hochon, with an income of at least forty thousand francs and savings estimated at over six hundred thousand, would give nothing to nephews he had never seen; she could not act without her husband’s signature and Rouget would refuse. She hinted Agathe’s presence in Issoudun might neutralize Rouget’s concubine’s influence and suggested Agathe come stay with her; she promised to try to make her husband consent and hinted at using a will to coerce him if necessary. She urged Agathe to employ a good lawyer and to come to Issoudun at once.

The letter impressed Agathe, who showed it to Joseph after telling him of Giroudeau’s demand. Joseph advised telling everything to Desroches. The next morning at six they found Desroches in the rue de Bussy. The lawyer—cold, stern, with a cracked voice, rough skin, implacable eyes and a foxlike visage—bounded like a tiger on hearing Giroudeau’s proposal and scornfully warned Agathe not to be duped. He urged her not to give a farthing; he’d answer for Philippe and would rather see him brought before the Court of Peers if that would save his future. He advised securing the property at Issoudun and getting evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring a woman; he offered to go to Issoudun himself in the holidays if needed. His briskness alarmed Joseph; Desroches winked and told Joseph bluntly that Philippe was a “great scoundrel” who might have caused the conspiracy’s discovery—either a fool or a traitor—and would be under police surveillance.

“Go to Issoudun with your mother,” Desroches urged. “Try to save the property.” Joseph, having sold two pictures to fund the trip, told his mother they would start for Berry. After writing to Madame Hochon, Agathe and Joseph left that evening for Issoudun, leaving Philippe to his fate. On the road, Agathe, reflecting on the Luxembourg prison to which Philippe had been transferred, said, “If it were not for the Allies he would never be there!” Joseph gallantly embraced his mother and praised her maternal devotion: “You are a mother just as Raphael was a painter. And you will always be a fool of a mother!”

Distracted by the journey, Agathe re-read Madame Hochon’s letter, seized by the words “concubine” and “slut” used by a pious septuagenarian, and by the suggestion that her presence might save the inheritance. Joseph, ignorant of the Code, admitted his helplessness; he recalled Desroches’s counsel to gather evidence of undue influence if Jean-Jacques had made a will favoring the woman. “Don’t trouble your heads uselessly,” Agathe said; “when we get to Issoudun my godmother will tell us what to do.” Their naivety in the task Desroches set them was plain as they entered the Sologne.

Returning to Issoudun after thirty years’ absence, Agathe was about to find changes in its manners that require a portrait of the town. Without it the reader would scarcely understand Madame Hochon’s heroism in helping her goddaughter, or the strange position of Jean-Jacques Rouget. That a brother should for thirty years give no sign of life to a sister was extraordinary and suggested peculiar circumstances; any other sister and nephew would long since have inquired. There is, moreover, a connection between Issoudun’s condition and the Bridau family’s interests, which will appear as the story goes on.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Issoudun, without disparaging Paris, ranks among France’s most ancient towns. Tradition makes Probus the Noah of the Gauls; Caesar praised the Champ-Fort wine (“de Campo Forti”), still one of Issoudun’s best. Rigord noted the medieval population and trade, yet Armand Peremet’s excavations revealed older strata: beneath the famed tower lay a fifth‑century basilica—probably unique in France—whose stones came from a Roman temple once on the site, preserving traces of an earlier civilization.

Peremet contends that place‑names ending in “‑dun” point to autochthonous, Druidic, or Celtic origins. Beneath the Gaulish dun likely stood a Roman temple to Isis, hence Chaumon’s name Issous‑Dun—“Is” for Isis. Richard Coeur‑de‑Lion probably built the tower—where he coined money—over the fifth‑century basilica and enclosed the church within feudal fortifications. Issoudun later briefly sheltered the routiers and cottereaux Henry II sent against his son Richard during the Poitou rebellion.

Evidence of Roman ingenuity survives in the Tournemine’s conversion into a canal—the “Rivière forcée”—a stream raised above the Theols that surrounds the town, doubtless Roman work. A northern suburb crossed by a rue de Rome is inhabited by people who call themselves Romans: vine‑growers with distinct features and customs, perhaps descendants of those who exterminated Cottereaux and Routiers on the plain of Charost in the twelfth century.

After the 1830 insurrection, national turmoil hid a violent local uprising of vine‑growers. The bourgeois resisted military entry, invoking ancient municipal rights. Backed by seven or eight thousand vine‑growers, rioters burned archives and excise offices and dragged a customs officer through the streets, crying at each lantern, “Let us hang him here!” The National Guard saved him by carrying him to prison on a trumped‑up indictment. The general entered only after compromise; yet a faubourg man once swung a volant (a huge pruning‑hook) round his neck, shouting “No more clerks, or there’s an end to compromise!” A leader promised suppression of excisemen and ultimately intervened to save the general.

In the fourteenth century Issoudun numbered sixteen or seventeen thousand—remnants of a once larger medieval population. Charles VII owned a mansion, known into the eighteenth century as the Maison du Roi. Once a center of the woollen trade, the town supplied much of Europe with wool for blankets, hats, and Chevreautin gloves. Under Louis XIV., Issoudun, birthplace of Baron and Bourdaloue, was famed for elegance and correct speech; the curate Poupard praised its subtlety and wit. Today that wit and splendor have faded. The town now counts barely twelve thousand inhabitants, including vine‑dressers of four large suburbs—Saint‑Paterne, Vilatte, Rome, and Alouette—that are almost separate villages. The bourgeoisie are scattered through the streets. Issoudun still holds Berry’s fleeces market, a commerce endangered by gradual improvements in stock‑breeding spreading through the region.

The vineyards produce wine drunk across two departments and, if tended differently, could rival Burgundy or Gascony. Yet conservative habits persist: growers leave the marc in the fermenting juice, making the wine detestable though it might be a source of wealth. They claim the marc’s bitterness lessens with age, so a vintage will keep a century—an excuse as old as Guillaume le Breton.

Issoudun’s decline stems from torpor and shortsighted parochialism. When a Paris–Toulouse highroad was planned, the natural line from Vierzon to Châteauroux would have passed through Issoudun, shorter than the present route via Vatan. Leading citizens and the town council insisted the road detour by Vatan, arguing a highway through Issoudun would raise prices and force them to pay thirty sous for a chicken—a narrow, protective folly like that once forcing a Sardinian highway to detour eight miles.

Falling wine prices please bourgeois consumers seeking cheap provisions but threaten vine‑growers as costs and taxes rise—just as the woollen trade suffers from poor sheep‑breeding. Country folk often prefer poverty to the social stigma of adopting new habits; many bourgeois express this with inward pride.

Social stagnation has set in. Families live apart; society lacks the class distinctions that give manners character. Nobility has vanished—cottereaux, routiers, the Jacquerie, religious wars, and the Revolution swept them away, a loss Issoudun applauds. The town repeatedly refused a garrison, claiming cheap provisions, thereby losing intercourse with the age and the benefits troops bring; before 1756 it had been a delightful garrison town. A judicial quarrel between a lieutenant‑general and the Marquis de Chapt deprived Issoudun of its garrison, and the forty‑fourth demi‑brigade’s temporary sojourn during the civil war did not reconcile inhabitants to soldiers.

Bourges, likewise declining, shows the same malady. Vitality drains from such communities. The government is partly to blame: administration should find and heal wounds by sending energetic men with power to change things, but instead it approves fatal tranquillity. Who would accept being buried in arrondissements where useful work lacks glory? Ambitious outsiders soon sink into local inertia; Issoudun would have benumbed Napoleon.

In 1822 the arrondissement’s administrators were nearly all locals, turning administration into nullity or farce. The procureur du roi, Monsieur Mouilleron, was cousin to much of the community; his substitute likewise from a town family. The court judge, notorious for a provincial saying, ended a summing‑up by telling the accused, “My poor Pierre! the thing is as plain as day; your head will be cut off. Let this be a lesson to you.” The commissary of police, in office since the Restoration, had relations throughout the arrondissement. Religion’s influence was weak; even the curate was held in little esteem. This radical, ignorant bourgeoisie—fond of annoying others—spread tales and gossiped, though Sunday‑schools continued, taxes were paid (the one thing Paris extracts from the provinces), and the mayor passed routine resolutions. But lax government reflected the moral and intellectual state of the governed. Such provincial conditions risk spreading if bourgeois rule continues to dominate policy.

Topography: Issoudun stretches north–south along a hillside curving toward the Châteauroux road. At the hill’s foot the canal called the “Rivière forcée,” fed from the Theols, was built in the town’s flourishing days—to supply manufactories or flood rampart moats. This artificial arm of the Tournemine joins other streams beyond the Rome suburb. Threads of water irrigate wide meadows, enclosed by pale terraces dotted with dark specks—the vineyards’ appearance for many months. Vine‑growers annually cut plants to ugly stumps, leaving them unsupported and sheltered by barrels. A traveller from Vierzon, Vatan, or Châteauroux, weary of monotony, is pleasantly surprised by Issoudun’s verdant meadows—an oasis supplying vegetables within thirty miles. Below the Rome suburb lie vast kitchen‑gardens, upper and lower Baltan. A long poplar avenue leads from town across the meadows to the ancient convent of Frapesle, whose English gardens, grandly named Tivoli, draw Sunday lovers.

Traces of ancient grandeur appear in town divisions. The château, once almost a town with walls and moats, remains a distinct quarter entered by ancient gateways via three bridges across two river arms, resembling an old city. Ramparts show formidable foundations on which houses have sprung. Above the château stands the famous tower, once the citadel. To conquer the city, which lay around these two fortified points, still left the tower and castle to be taken.

The Saint‑Paterne suburb, a palette‑shaped extension beyond the tower encroaching on meadows, was likely part of the ancient city. In 1822 this seemed certain because the charming Romanesque church of Saint‑Paterne still stood—recently pulled down by the heir of its buyer from the Nation. That splendid portal perished without a single drawing; the lone voice raised to save it found no echo in town or department. Though the château quarter resembles an old town of narrow streets and mansions, the main city, repeatedly captured and burned—especially during the Fronde—has a modern air. Spacious streets and well‑built houses contrast with the citadel’s look, earning Issoudun the epithet “pretty” in some geographies.

In a town lacking industry, art, or learned occupations, many young men after the Restoration (1816) found no career until marriage or inheritance. Bored and craving distraction, they “shed their cuticle” by sowing wild oats at the town’s expense. To avoid daytime recognition and police courts, they took their pranks by night. Thus, amid traces of lost civilizations, a last flare of drollery burst forth.

These young men, imitating courtly and military camaraderie, banded together as the “Knights of Idleness.” By day they played saints, sleeping late after nights of mischief. Beginning with commonplace pranks—changing signs, ringing bells, tipping casks into cellars—they escalated after 1817 when Maxence Gilet, called Max, became Grand Master and until 1823 led a campaign of mischief that spread a sort of terror in Issoudun and kept artisans and bourgeois perpetually uneasy.

Maxence’s background, energy, and bearing fitted him for the role. Town rumor claimed he was the natural son of the sub‑delegate Lousteau; others said Doctor Rouget. In truth his father was a dragoon officer; his mother, wife of a poor sabot‑maker in the Rome suburb, was strikingly beautiful. Madame Hochon paid for the lad’s schooling; Doctor Rouget subsidized his seminary board until 1805, leaving paternity ambiguous. In 1806, at seventeen, Max committed an involuntary homicide—stealing fruit, he startled a pregnant woman whose death was blamed on him. Threatened, he fled to Bourges and enlisted in a regiment bound for Egypt.

In three campaigns Max distinguished himself and rose to captain. In Portugal (1809) he was left for dead in an English battery, taken prisoner and sent to the Spanish hulks at Cabrera from 1810 to 1814—an appalling prison. To resist corruption there he killed seven bullies in duels, establishing order on his hulk by strength and skill. Returning in 1814, innocent of worse guilt, he found his parents dead and destitute. He sought rank and decoration at Paris; a marshal put him in the Guard as captain and promised the Emperor’s recognition. At Fleurus he earned mention; Napoleon later put him on the decoration list.

After Waterloo Max retreated. Marshal Feltre refused to acknowledge his grade or cross; Max resigned in disgust, losing half‑pay. The town roused to help: the mayor found him a six‑hundred‑franc post, which Max soon left. He became Grand Master of the Knights of Idleness and lived in ways that lost bourgeois favour—though none openly contradicted him, for he was feared. Issoudun harbored more Bonapartists than most towns; these men became nearly all Liberals.

Around Max a dozen officers rallied—many veterans who had refused Bourbon service. They admired him except Captain Carpentier and Major Mignonnet. Carpentier, a cavalry officer risen from the ranks who had married into the best local family, and Mignonnet, an École Polytechnique man, judged Max dishonourable. The Imperial army had divided types: many soldiers despised civilians, while others—artillerymen, engineers—kept republican habits. Thus Potel and Renard befriended Max; Mignonnet and Carpentier sided with the bourgeoisie.

Max showed no ostentatious complaint at exclusion from polite society. Handsome, well dressed, keeping a horse—a rarity—he had pride and expected revenge. Though the bourgeois disapproved, the common people admired his courage and daring; many degradations were unknown to them. He became a local champion of Bonapartism and Opposition, counted on in great occasions.

One episode displayed his heroism. In 1819 a battalion of royalist officers, fresh from Maison Rouge, lingered at the café Militaire—the Bonapartist rendezvous—and insulted the patrons by demanding royalist papers. Max, with Major Potel and Captain Renard and thirty young men, entered; a captain tore and spat on a paper. Max slapped an officer and fought; in a street fight at Frapesle he killed his man, Potel wounded another who later died, and the battalion left that night. The affair spread through Berry and made Max a local hero.

The Knights, nearly all under twenty‑five, trained like pupils of Amoros—bold, agile, clever and strong. They practised roof‑climbing, scaling houses, noiseless walking, mixing mortar, walling doors, and amassed ropes, ladders, tools, and disguises. They perfected pranks with a Panurge‑like genius that provoked laughter while covering victims with ridicule and silencing complaints. Being sons of good families, they held household secrets that facilitated outrages.

They lurked in doorways at night, calling “What’s the matter?” in tones of terror to rouse citizens into the streets in shirts and caps. They dressed as devils and invaded a poor bookbinder’s basement, forcing him into his cutting‑press and leaving him shrieking about Lucifer. They rebuilt a tax collector’s chimney so the flue sent smoke into the house for two months until he rebuilt it. They stuffed hay and oiled paper into a pious woman’s chimney; when she lit her fire the next morning it seemed a volcano—fire‑engines came, the Knights among the firemen deluged her house, frightening her into illness. They wrote anonymous letters threatening robbery, then prowled softly under windows, whistling. One prank sent letters to eighty heirs of a miserly woman announcing her death; they arrived in deep mourning only to find a scene of confusion between servants and notaries.

The sub‑prefect finally awoke to the intolerable state, made worse by his own pretensions. He boasted of his egg‑boiling skill—breakfasting on two fresh eggs precisely timed—and for a month each night his eggs were replaced by hard‑boiled ones, ruining his reputation as the “sous‑préfet à l’oeuf.” Max greased his stoves with fetid oil nightly; his wife once found her shawl glued inside and had to go without it. The sub‑prefect requested transfer. His cowardly submissiveness helped establish the Knights’ comic authority.

Beyond rue des Minimes and place Misère lay a quarter enclosed by an arm of the Rivière forcée and ramparts, from place d’Armes to the pottery market. This square of narrow streets and hovels, long a haunt of the poor and disreputables, housed low trades and a kind of “cour des Miracles.” One thoroughfare is still called “the street of the Executioner,” and a red door marks where the executioner, or his assistant from Châteauroux, lives; vine‑dressers keep intercourse with him, for he inherits the craft of curing wounds. Second‑hand sellers and old‑clothes dealers made the air fetid—typical of such quarters where two or three Jews sometimes gain ascendancy.

At a corner in the livelier half of this quarter stood, from 1815 to 1823, a public house kept by Mère Cognette. The house, of white stone, one storey with an attic, bore a huge pine branch over the door. A blue poster above the words “Good Beer of Mars” showed a soldier pouring foam toward a very décolletée woman. The ground floor was an immense hall; a winding staircase led to the upper storey. A low, long room lit from a narrow, sunken court served as the Knights’ secret assembly chamber—hidden, like a provincial chimney flue.

Père Cognet, formerly a groom, and La Cognette, formerly a cook, had bought the house by uniting savings. La Cognette, about forty, lively and shrewd, was chosen by Max as the Order’s Leonarde for her character and cookery; Père Cognet, thick‑set and under his wife’s rule, kept absolute silence about nightly gatherings. From 1816 to 1823 neither revealed what went on. Their devotion was absolute, secured by self‑interest: when the Knights knocked a certain way Père Cognet would light fires and candles, open the door, fetch a particular wine laid in for the Order, and La Cognette prepared excellent suppers for the expeditions planned that night or the next.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While Joseph and Madame Bridau were traveling from Orléans to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played one of their best jokes. Early one morning an old Spaniard, a former prisoner of war who sold grain near Issoudun, left his empty cart at the foot of the town tower. Maxence, who had come for a Knights’ rendezvous, whispered, “Here’s Père Fario’s cart. I nearly cracked my shins over it. Let us get it up on the embankment of the tower first, and we’ll decide afterwards.” The Issoudun tower, raised by Richard Cœur-de-Lion on ancient foundations, sat atop a steep cone of monuments; toward the town its pedestal rose eighty feet. In an hour the Knights took the cart off its wheels and hoisted it piece by piece to the top of the embankment at the tower’s base—a feat like soldiers carrying artillery over a mountain pass. They remounted the cart and, hungry and thirsty, returned to Mère Cognette’s to laugh at how Père Fario would fume when he came for his barrow in the morning.

The Knights did not prank nightly; three clever fellows could not invent mischief every day. When not meeting at night they hunted, harvested, and skated. Among the twenty youths some were closer to Maxence and made him their idol. Two grandsons of Madame Hochon—François Hochon and Baruch Borniche—were his henchmen. Accepting family gossip about Lousteau’s left-handed parentage, they regarded Max as kin. Max lent them money, took them hunting, and exercised more influence than their grandfather; both were orphans under Monsieur Hochon’s guardianship.

That night François and Baruch sat one on each side of Max at the middle of a table lit by four tallow candles. A dozen or fifteen bottles had been drained; only eleven Knights were present. Baruch, whose name reflected Issoudun’s Calvinist past, said as the wine loosened tongues, “You are threatened in your stronghold.” Max asked what he meant. Baruch explained that Madame Hochon had had a letter from Madame Bridau—her goddaughter—saying she and her son were coming to Issoudun; his grandmother was readying two rooms. The suggestion alarmed the others: Madame Bridau was sister to Rouget, and if she took steps when Rouget died she might secure the property that Max counted on.

Max listened with an officer’s gravity. He was thirty-four, with a soldierly face, ardent eyes, black hair bristling in five “points,” about five feet six, well proportioned and broad-shouldered—attractive, commanding, and used to concealment after hard experience. When François and Goddet voiced the town’s fears that Flore Brazier—the “Rabouilleuse,” as respectable folk mockingly called her—might lose influence to legitimate relatives, Max cut in. He resented the contemptuous nickname for a woman he was known to favor. In the conversation he admitted, more plainly than before, his attachment to Mademoiselle Flore Brazier: people thought she provided his money and that he intended to marry her when Père Rouget died. He acknowledged that Flore aimed at the old man’s property, and faced the Knights’ crude gossip about her.

Max declared he had already silenced those who meddled with his affairs, and asked the Knights if they would help get rid of the Bridaus “within the limits we have marked out for our fooleries.” He denied being an idiot—he would not poison anyone—and said he would be satisfied if Flore, reduced to her own standing, still sufficed for him; he loved her enough to prefer her to Mademoiselle Fichet, the richest heiress in Issoudun. The Knights cheered him: “We’ll stand by you!” Eleven acclamations, toasts, and relaxed morals reflected their mood. Max’s leadership among these youths—coming from the best families and giving them favors—had created a following useful in his designs to recover position. He waved his glass and made arrangements: “Be in the market-place early, all of you, and let me know when the old fellow goes for his cart.” Goddet added, “Don’t forget Fario’s cart!”—a joke some wanted to keep.

At half-past three in the morning the Knights slipped away. Max returned slowly to the quarter of the rich bourgeoisie, having concealed his fears though the news had struck home. His time on the hulks at Cabrera had taught him dissimulation. Above all, the forty thousand francs a year from Rouget’s landed property underpinned Max’s interest in Flore; her influence over Rouget had given him hope of a secure financial future. Yet the arrival of legitimate heirs disturbed that faith. Rouget’s savings—seven hundred and fifty thousand francs placed with different notaries at Bourges, Vierzon, and Châteauroux—could not be quietly transferred into Flore’s name without discovery. “The important thing is to get rid of these relations; once they’re driven away we must hurry to secure the property,” thought Max. He entered Rouget’s house with a pass-key and went to bed, promising himself clearer thoughts the next day.

The narrative then explains why Flore became known as the “Rabouilleuse” and how she came to be quasi-mistress of Jean-Jacques Rouget’s home. As old Doctor Rouget aged he perceived his son’s nonentity and treated him harshly; coming one day from his rounds he encountered a bewitching little girl at the edge of some fields along the avenue de Tivoli. The child sprang up from the brook, half naked and barefoot, wearing a forlorn petticoat of coarse strips and a rough paper hat; despite rags she had a charming head and figure. She and her uncle were catching crabs. “Rabouiller” is a Berrichon word meaning to trouble the water to bring crabs to the surface; Flore Brazier held her rabouilloir with childlike grace. The doctor learned she was from Vatan, that her father was in an asylum, and that she earned five sous a day catching crabs and gleaning in harvest, spinning in winter. She was about twelve.

Rouget proposed she come to his house: “You shall be well fed and well dressed, and have some pretty shoes.” Uncle Brazier, a sun-rotted peasant, agreed—more readily when Rouget offered wages and proposed to pay in advance. The doctor pronounced her “worthy of the name” and ordered a room prepared. That evening all Issoudun talked of Rouget’s sudden adoption of the little rabouilleuse; in that satirical region the nickname stuck to Flore before, during, and after her good fortune.

Rouget’s house, inherited from the Descoings family, impressed the peasant child: a panelled hall, a marble chimney-piece with a gilded mirror, old masters and sacred pictures, Boulle clock, silver candlesticks, carved armchairs—luxuries little valued in the town but unmistakable signs of wealth. Rouget treated her to good living, Fanchette the cook showing both Rouget’s despotism and domestic bustle. The doctor’s ostensible motives varied in town gossip: some thought he desired a youthful companion; others declared him past such things and merely calculating. From 1800 to 1805 Rouget provided Flore a life of comfort. He dressed her, gave jewels and a gold watch, and provided a master to teach her to read, write, and cipher; but Flore disliked the bit of learning and Rouget stopped at the point she would not take to studies.

Provincial gossip multiplied explanations: was the old man courting her, planning a marriage, or merely indulging a fancy? The town’s minds insisted on sensational motives, and few sympathized with Rouget’s restraint or the complexity of his feelings. The doctor’s intentions—whether paternal, selfish, or lecherous—were debated and colored by small-town malice. Some suggested he might marry Flore and father an active boy like Max; others laughed at the improbability of old men’s virility. Brazier, when drunk, was said to boast of having cheated Rouget; rumor thrived.

Flore grew up between the saturnalias of 1793 and 1798 and the slow restoration of worship under the Concordat; revolutionary impulses had loosened moral restraints, and marriage often became a civil contract. By 1802, religious practice was still weak in many parishes; moral checks were slack, and Flore—born in 1787—had been formed in this environment. She was envied by peasant girls and deemed “wide awake,” a local phrase implying a shrewdness born of practical life. The doctor treated her kindly but remained, at heart, calculating; in his last years he displayed less warmth, and town gossip read that as confirming his indifference.

As Rouget aged and his end approached, the notary urged him to make provision for the young woman. His answer revealed his nature: “So I do,” he said cynically; “my death sets her at liberty.” That phrase—bitter, calculating, and clothed in a certain witty cruelty—registered in the notary’s mind as the concentrated hatred of a man whose desires had been frustrated by Nature. When urged again to provide for Flore, the doctor replied, “Her beauty will make her rich enough!”—a remark confirming that he intended to leave her nothing, relying on the world’s appetite for a pretty woman to secure her future. Thus the town’s suspicions about the doctor’s motives and the precariousness of Flore’s position were confirmed by his whitely cruel remarks, and by the prevailing gossip that shaped how she was viewed thereafter.

Throughout, Max’s personal stakes are clear: Flore’s connection to Rouget and the prospect of Rouget’s fortune shaped his fortune-seeking plans. He both loved and depended upon her influence. The arrival of Madame Bridau and her son threatened that arrangement, and Max—practical, deceitful, and socially adept—turned to his followers among the Knights of Idleness to execute petty and perhaps decisive measures, confident in the small-town setting where rumor, pranks, and private schemes could alter fortunes as surely as law or inheritance.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Jean-Jacques Rouget did not mourn his father, though Flore Brazier did. The old doctor had made his son unhappy since Jean-Jacques came of age in 1791, but he had given the little peasant girl the comforts country folk prize. After the funeral, when Fanchette asked Flore, “Well, what is to become of you, now that monsieur is dead?” Jean-Jacques’s dull face suddenly showed feeling.

Seventeen and still delicate, with the embonpoint of a life without toil, Flore retained the distinction that had attracted the doctor. When Jean-Jacques, trembling, asked if she felt at home and whether she was honest, his timidity made him clumsy; he could only take her hand and, unable to say more, suggest she stay and be mistress of the house. Flore, touched and practical, agreed; the explanation of his character would come slowly.

At his father’s death Jean-Jacques—then thirty-seven—remained as submissive and timid as a child. His timidity sprang from dulled faculties and nervous weakness; he had passion without intellect, enough sense for ordinary affairs but no resource when desire froze his speech. Small, awkward, with a vulgar flattened face and pale skin, he was socially awkward and sought solitude. The doctor, who wanted his son married but feared surrendering control of the estate, bred avarice and mechanical habits into him, keeping the lands in order while sparing management burdens. This failed to conceal Jean-Jacques’s passionate love for Flore. She was the light of his home; he watched her secretly and rejoiced that his father had trained her into easy obedience. Passion steadied his mind as much as intellect could have.

For three weeks after the funeral the strange situation persisted: Flore treated him indulgently, and Jean-Jacques hovered between confession and silence. One night she found him crouched like a dog on the landing and thought, “He loves me.” Eventually, during another dessert, Flore told him the truth about his father’s temper; Rouget, taking her hand, asked if she would stay with him. She answered she would if he wished it. Ten months later, after long hesitation and need of certainty, Jean-Jacques at last relaxed: his face cleared, he breathed happiness, and Flore—as mistress—insisted he take better care of himself. The town noticed the transformation and gossiped: some thought Flore would make him marry, others praised her devotion. Thus she obtained management of the Rouget household—from father to son, as young Goddet had said.

Fanchette, the cook, alone thought it wrong that Flore should preside. She protested injured virtue but was humiliated by a child mistress. Owning three hundred francs a year and an annuity from the doctor, she could live without working and left the house nine months after the funeral, on April 15, 1806—a date the narrator hints may mark when Flore ceased to be an “honest girl.”

Foreseeing Fanchette’s departure, Flore resolved to manage without a servant. For six months she studied the cook’s arts. The doctor, trained in Paris under Rouelle, had applied chemistry to cooking and improved provincial technique—subtleties of omelettes, the use of a cagnard, how to avoid browned-butter tartness. Flore, a born fryer and roaster, soon surpassed Fanchette and became a cordon-bleu, caring finely for Jean-Jacques’s comfort. She rubbed furniture, kept everything clean, managed the wash and mended linen with thrift. She learned the family business, consulted the notary Monsieur Heron, and advised Jean-Jacques. Having lost both her own parents—the uncle Brazier, who left a wine-shop, dying shortly before the doctor—she served Rouget with an orphan’s gratitude.

This period was paradise for poor Jean-Jacques. He was trained into monastic regularity: Flore woke him with breakfast, he took a morning walk, read papers at three, lingered at dinner, and went to bed early to save fire and candles—provincial economies that lull the mind. For nine years their life was regular. Jean-Jacques lent at five percent on first mortgages, releasing the wife’s rights if married, never more than a third of a property’s value, and charged notes for an extra two and a half percent—his father’s rules. Rouget’s seven and a half percent levy, while a burden on peasant ambition, seemed reasonable and allowed him choice; notaries gained commissions.

During these nine years Flore obtained absolute control. At first she treated him familiarly; then, surpassing him in intelligence and force, she became in fact his superior and he the servant of his servant. He accepted this by clinging to her care as a son to a mother. Flore kept the house and managed all business; Jean-Jacques left everything to her so completely that life without her seemed impossible. She indulged his fancies, understood him thoroughly, and her brightness was his chief pleasure. Secure in her power, she did not think to make him marry her.

By the end of 1815 Flore, now twenty-seven, had reached full beauty: plump, fresh, white-skinned, the ideal buxom housewife, likened to a handsome barmaid of a higher type. In 1816 she saw Maxence Gilet and fell in love at first sight. Maxence, a penniless officer, saw more than a passing fancy in Flore once he learned of her position and Jean-Jacques’s weakness. He readily took up abode at the Rougets’, recognizing how little the old bachelor would resist.

Flore’s passion changed household affairs: she grew sullen and gave Jean-Jacques flashes of temper like a wife suspecting infidelity. She reproached him bitterly: sixteen years she had spent her youth in his house; he hoarded wealth—“six hundred and fifty-nine thousand well invested, and sixty thousand francs’ income”—yet refused to offer the worthy and impoverished Captain Maxence a home on the empty second floor. “You’d rather rats and mice run riot than put a human being there,” she cried, calling him a fratricide for his inaction. Jean-Jacques, terrified at losing her, promised to invite Maxence.

He did so politely and publicly; a reconciliation took place, but Jean-Jacques noticed that Flore’s affections had wholly shifted. For weeks she complained to tradespeople about Rouget’s alleged stinginess in welcoming his natural brother. No one was deceived: they saw Flore as clever and artful. Old Rouget, however, found comfort after Max’s arrival; he gained a companion who paid him attention without servility. Maxence talked, discussed politics, walked with Rouget—he was lively company.

After Max’s arrival Flore stopped cooking, saying it spoiled her hands. Mere Cognette, grand master of idleness, produced a relative: an old maid who had lost a curate master and promised devotion—an excellent cook named Vedie, pitted by smallpox and ugly, who took over the kitchen in exchange for an annuity of three hundred francs after ten years. Flore now adopted the title Madame Brazier and upgraded her dress: corsets, fine wool and silk, neckerchiefs, embroidered caps, collars, lace ruffles and boots. She renewed her youth through richness and elegance, polishing a rough diamond into worth, and wished to honor Maxence. In 1817 she brought an English horse from Bourges for the weary cavalry captain. Max also found in Issoudun an old lancer of the Imperial Guard, a Pole named Kouski, who, now poor, quartered himself at Rouget’s as the captain’s servant and became idolized by Maxence after a duel with three royalists.

Thus, from 1817, the household numbered five people, three of whom were masters, and expenses rose to about eight thousand francs a year. Flore had come from peasant origins to absolute control of a comfortable fortune, and Jean-Jacques, passive and devoted, remained fenced in by the very domestic regularities and thriftiness that had once consoled him—now the instruments of his subordination to a woman who had learned how to rule.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun to claim, Maitre Desroches said, an inheritance at stake, Jean‑Jacques Rouget had fallen into a semi‑vegetative state. After Max’s instalment, Flore imposed strict order in the household. Rouget ate better under Vedie’s cooking but did not regain health; he grew weaker, his eyes circled with dark rings. Neighbors, thinking him mentally deficient, did not note his failing faculties. He lived only for Flore, obeying her slightest look and watching her like a dog. At fifty‑seven he seemed, in Madame Hochon’s phrase, older than Monsieur Hochon, an octogenarian.

People assumed Max’s apartment suited his charm. In six years the captain had improved and adorned it for himself and Flore—colored tiles, passable wallpapers, mahogany furniture, gilt mirrors, muslin curtains, a canopy bed—comfort that was luxury in Issoudun though common in Paris. One small oddity caused comment: rush‑matting on the stairs to muffle footsteps. Max came in at daybreak without waking anyone; Rouget, as always, suspected nothing of his guest’s nocturnal activities with the Knights of Idleness.

One morning Flore, in a striped cotton dressing‑gown, lace cap, and furred slippers, softly opened Max’s door and found him asleep. He woke instantly, like a soldier. Flore feared mischief; Max told her something serious had happened and sent her for coffee, bidding her feign ignorance and return at nine. A quarter‑hour later Baruch burst in, exclaiming that Fario was hunting for his barrow.

Max dressed and strolled to the tower embankment, where a crowd had gathered. Fario was a withered little Spaniard—called “goodman Fario”—with piercing eyes and an air of old‑world gentility concealing a peasant stubbornness. He swore his cart had been left at the foot of the tower; the horse stood harnessed thirty feet away. Max gravely examined the scene and suggested the cart might have been drawn somewhere. Fario insisted the cart was light but now sat inexplicably atop the embankment.

Pointing to the top of the embankment, Max revealed the barrow perched there. The market erupted. Goddet joked the devil made common cause with inn‑keepers; laughter rose and Fario lost his temper. Max directed Francois to clear the crowd below, then, taking advantage of Fario’s stupefaction, he and Baruch and others climbed the steep embankment. There was no trace of how the cart had been carried up.

At the top, Max, with a theatrical air, lifted the barrow by the shafts and flung it down, warning those below. The cart smashed picturesquely; the crowd, kept at a distance, cheered. Fario raged, first accusing brigands, then suspecting Max, and finally, mestizo and vindictive, threatened to demand a new cart. Max japed that he would give a magnificent cart if Fario lent him two hundred and fifty francs, cooling the Spaniard with a mocking bargain. Though furious, Fario left muttering to procure a replacement. The spectacle—one shaft standing upright like a tree—kept Issoudun talking for days; the tale spread through Berry and reached other towns. Because of Fario’s vindictive remark, Max and the Rabouilleuse were whispered about and discussed in Bourges, Vatan, Vierzon, and Chateauroux. Max enceinte knew how venomous such talk would be.

“We can’t stop their tongues,” he said. “Ah! I did a foolish thing!”

“Max!” Francois warned. “They are coming tonight.”

“Who?”

“The Bridaus. My grandmother has had a letter from her goddaughter.”

Max, always calculating, counseled caution. He thought neither he nor Flore ought to seem opposed to the Bridaus; if the heirs were to be disposed of, let the Hochons drive them out. He advised judging the Parisians at Mere Cognette’s the next day and then planning how to set old Hochon against them. Max saw social manipulation as the proper approach.

While Max plotted, Flore, despite advice to the contrary, erupted at the poor bachelor she controlled. Rouget’s small attentions and vulgar fondlings—his only joys—were suppressed. Flore cast him into disgrace: no tender epithets, no help with his toilette, no anticipation of his wants. A cold, sharp, ironic “you” cut through his heart; the “you” was a declaration of war. If he called, she answered from below the stairs with impatience. Her blows of contempt and feigned neglect wounded the miserable old man, whose existence depended on her favor.

Vedie, the servant, intervened tactfully, urging Rouget to cherish Flore, reminding him the woman was superior and that making her unhappy would be his ruin. Rouget, bewildered and humiliated, was stricken; these alternations of tenderness and severity affected him like climate changes attack the body. Emotion set off his coughing fits. Flore’s cruelty was a moral pleurisy that wore him down; she could make only him foolish. She berated him for nothing and then, when she pleased, relented with artful tenderness.

That morning she told him vaguely of his sister Agathe’s coming from Paris with her sons, much to his astonishment. Rouget protested he had never sent for them and that his father had made him promise to leave nothing to Agathe, who, he said, had no claim on him. Flore threatened to tear his will and drive them off, declaring she and Max his true family. Max intervened, arguing Rouget should receive his sister and nephews to avoid scandal and the appearance of manipulation. He counseled keeping the property arrangements unchanged while entertaining the Bridaus to preserve appearances in Issoudun. His motive was practical: rumor could ruin him.

Flore, now mollified, helped set the table. Rouget, full of admiration for Max, took him by both hands and declared his love for him as if he had been a son. Max urged Rouget to receive his kin but not to alter his will. After breakfast, Max carried Flore back to his rooms and discussed finances. He feared a secret will had been made; they must be cordial to the Bridaus while converting Rouget’s mortgages into cash. The Funds were rising; by investing Rouget’s seven hundred and fifty thousand francs in the Funds at eighty‑nine, they could secure a profit, make him borrow a guaranteed sum for two years, and keep control. Flore delighted in these schemes and praised Max’s cleverness. He planned also to maneuver the Hochons into disposing of the Parisians.

The narrative then turns to the Hochon household across from Rouget’s in the Grand and Petite Narette at the Place Saint‑Jean. The two houses, likely by the same architect, faced each other so occupants could observe goings‑on when curtains and doors were open. Monsieur Hochon, formerly tax‑collector at Selles and born at Issoudun, had returned to marry Lousteau’s sister and had exchanged offices. Retiring before 1787, he escaped the Revolution’s perils yet adhered to its triumphs like many “honest men.” He had a reputation for miserliness, illustrated by a story: at his daughter’s wedding, when the cook asked for string to truss a turkey, he gave a used bit and demanded it be returned.

As he aged, Hochon became petty; at eighty‑five he was the sort to pick up a pin mid‑talk and call it “the wife’s stipend.” He enforced strict, abstemious rules at home, weighing and measuring food for his household: Madame Hochon née Lousteau, his grandson Borniche and his sister Adolphine (heirs of old Borniche), and his other grandson Francois. His eldest son, swallowed by the 1813 draft, had died at Hanau; his widow left debts Hochon refused to pay, citing that “women are minors.”

The house was large but sparsely furnished. Two second‑floor rooms, barely fit for Madame Bridau and Joseph, contained old armchairs, a walnut table with a pitcher, and heaps of winter fruit on straw where rats ran—an odor of fruit and vermin. Madame Hochon, ever provident, directed cleaning, patched wallpaper, pieced curtains from old cloth, borrowed night‑tables, and hired chests of drawers to make these rooms presentable. She even risked using two candlesticks carved by her father in his turner phase: relics from 1770–80 ornamented with root circlets. These sacrifices increased Hochon’s anxiety; he had not previously believed the Bridaus’ arrival would be real.

After breakfast the day of Fario’s trick, Madame Hochon reminded her husband to receive her goddaughter, Madame Bridau, properly, and privately warned him she would make provision in her will for Agathe if he slighted her. Hochon replied he knew forms of civility at his age. She pressed him, denouncing Maxence Gilet as the man getting Rouget’s money—“you’ve warmed a viper in your bosom,” he muttered—and warning of the risk of living with the Rouget connection. He fretted about the Parisians’ influence and the possibility that, once established, they would “extricate” Rouget from the Hochons and their circle.

Madame Hochon, spare and erect with powdered hair and a taffeta petticoat, placed her snuff‑box down and insisted she loved Agathe and would see justice done. Hochon left bewildered by her firmness. She took up her prayer‑book; old age prevented daily attendance at church, but she added petitions for Agathe’s success to her services, begged God to open Rouget’s eyes, and had the curate say a mass during a novena. Concealing her plans from her grandchildren, whom she called “parpaillots,” she set Adolphine, her granddaughter, to make the novena in church by proxy.

Adolphine, eighteen and raised at her grandmother’s side in a household of monotony, sewed for seven years, and now took an interest in Joseph Bridau, hoping to sway him away from the “monstrosities” grandfather Hochon attributed to the young Parisian. The town’s older bourgeoisie approved Madame Hochon’s conduct in receiving her goddaughter; they secretly despised Maxence Gilet’s methods yet wished the Bridaus well. Thus Rouget’s sister and nephew’s impending arrival split Issoudun into two parties: the respectable, older bourgeoisie who offered good wishes but little help, and the Knights of Idleness with Max’s partisans, who might resort to high‑handed measures against the Parisians.

Throughout these events the author underscores a social truth: Flore Brazier’s coarse behavior is the same species of feminine dominance as a duchess’s pouting or a wealthy bourgeoise’s ironies—only education and circumstance differ. The same tactics of insult, contempt, and feigned tenderness operate at all social levels when women move from obedience to power. Max, meanwhile, is the strategist, using public scandal, private cajolery, and financial manipulation to secure Rouget’s fortune and his own advantage. The Bridaus’ arrival threatens these plans, and the town’s factions ready to back either the newcomers or the entrenched clique prepare the stage for the next maneuvers.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Agathe and Joseph arrived at the Messageries-Royales office in the Place Misère at three in the afternoon. Madame Bridau, though fatigued, felt revived by the sight of her native town; each step stirred memories of girlhood. News of the Parisians spread at once through Issoudun; within minutes everyone knew they had come. Madame Hochon greeted her godchild at the door with affectionate, almost maternal warmth; after seventy-two barren years and the graves of three unhappy children, she welcomed this tie to the past and immediately took an interest in Agathe. Agathe was led into the salon where Monsieur Hochon sat, dour and sardonic.

Introductions were made: Baruch Borniche, François Hochon, and little Adolphine, a shy, blushing girl who avoided looking at Joseph Bridau. The miserly Monsieur Hochon sniffed at flattery; the two young men and the old man studied Joseph’s gaunt, brigand-like face and wild expression with curiosity and guarded mistrust. Agathe presented Joseph warmly as “my good Joseph,” betraying a mother’s pride and recollections of her own son in the Luxembourg prison. Joseph admitted bluntly he was “like my father, and very ugly at that.” Madame Hochon squeezed Agathe’s hand as if to say she now understood her preferences.

The household bustled: trunks were carried to attic rooms, town gossip flared, and Baruch rushed off to announce Joseph’s arrival and to describe his haggard appearance. By evening Joseph was the talk of Issoudun; gossip made him seem monstrous, potentially dangerous, and the very thing everyone speculated about. Some joked of having their portraits painted while he was in town.

Inspecting the Hochon house underscored the fall from Paris to provincial life: bare, damp rooms, shabby stairs and furniture. At dinner, Hochon’s miserliness governed everything. Joseph found the meal meager, each course served with ceremonious stinginess—watered soup, thin bouilli reduced to shoe-thin slices, vegetables parceled out as if separate parts of the meal, custard flavored ungenerously, and a ritualistic handing out of bread from a great loaf. There was a country vintage wine, Adolphine’s table-flowers, and the household’s slow, habit-bound economy. Joseph felt they might better have stayed at an inn. When he asked for more bread, Hochon unlocked a cupboard and broke off a section with the resigned sanctity of someone performing a ritual; requesting more was evidently forbidden.

Yet the surroundings revealed small mercies. When Gritte, the aged servant, brought dessert—Touraine-Berry cheese with its mold, nuts and old-fashioned biscuits—and claimed there was no fruit, Joseph laughed; parsimony had become second nature. Hochon, prodded, produced peaches, pears, plums and grapes. The young men’s healthy appearances suggested they managed to eat well despite the household’s prudence; Baruch smiled, hinting that family visits to Mére Cognette supplied their regular feasts.

Madame Hochon’s kindness showed in a private flask of ratafia she brewed from a convent recipe. She counseled patience: life in Issoudun was always a kind of Lent, nourishing enough to keep existence going and no more. She told Agathe that Hochon would soon go to his club to read the papers, leaving them some privacy. In the salon—frozen in provincial stasis as if Monarchy, Revolution, Empire and Restoration had left no mark—she and Agathe spoke of life’s trials and resignation. Adolphine, destined for the cloister, listened for tales of local characters: Maxence Gilet, the Rabouilleuse, and Jean-Jacques Rouget. Madame Hochon promised numerous curious visitors that night and recounted local intrigues—Maxence’s influence over Rouget; the Rabouilleuse’s exploits—hinting the house and its furniture had been recently restored after being abandoned by a usurper.

Joseph, meanwhile, observed less of these social dynamics than the faces in the salon: from five till half-past nine he sat and studied the guests, taking in their features and expressions like a painter’s practice. His sarcastic looks and sinister visage left the company uneasy. After the household retired, Madame Hochon kept Agathe speaking with her until midnight; Agathe glimpsed the waste of a noble unrecognized soul in Joseph and acknowledged that despite Parisian small joys, she was not the most unhappy. The old woman framed hardship as preparation for a final reckoning.

At midnight the town’s Knights of Idleness gathered under the boulevard Baron trees. François thought Maxence meant to give supper; Baruch fretted about feeding two extra mouths. Maxence arrived and called for a raid on Mére Cognette’s—an all-out cry to the town. At her modest tavern supper, some twenty-two members of his Order ate well. After the feast Maxence rose and spoke: his honor had been attacked that morning by a peddler—“a Spaniard, Cabrera”—and he intended a revenge that would remain within their rules. His scheme was perversely ingenious: each member was to procure clandestinely twenty rats—male or female—within three days, starve them until ravenous, and release the four hundred beasts in the old Capuchin church where Fario stored grain. The rats would eat a “not insignificant quantity,” causing Fario to suffer, and thus the Spaniard’s store would be diminished.

Maxence framed the plan as philanthropic, protecting “persecuted” animals like Brahmins; he credited the idea to others while comparing it to Samson’s foxes, though he claimed higher motives. Mademoiselle Flore Brazier set mousetraps; Kouski sought field-mice; Goddet offered to find large animals; Beaussier would commandeer pigeons; the committee even discussed a monkey or squirrels to attract more rats. Max appointed commissariat and emphasized secrecy: turn shoe-soles backward, make the watchman drunk. He offered prizes—his shotgun or horse—to anyone devising a trick causing the Parisians trouble with the Hochons or driving them away without harming the ancestors of his two friends. Thus a townful of small plots set to work, collectively scheming against Agathe and Joseph.

The next morning, before the Hochons’ second breakfast at ten, Agathe and Joseph saw Jean-Jacques Rouget across the street in a state of dishabille and debility that shocked Agathe. He was gaunt and altered, forced into odd postures by the influence of local characters—chief among them Flore Brazier. Flore stood behind him, made up and fashionable, one arm around him in a staged scene: snowy shoulders, a dazzling décolletage, a gauze neckerchief trimmed with lace, a grenadine dress with leg-of-mutton sleeves and a glittering gold chain. The tableau was theatrical, and Joseph, unrestrained as a man of the atelier, burst into praise: Flore’s beauty and form were like a Titian’s Venus, a “magnificent caryatid,” a model fit for masterful art. Adolphine and Madame Hochon took his talk for studio jargon; Agathe signed behind his back that she was used to such talk. But Agathe feared this woman’s power over her brother, noting that if Jean-Jacques were not thereby deprived of reason and prospects, he would have married and had children, likely dazzling consequences for Agathe’s claims to the family property.

Mid-breakfast, Kouski presented a letter from Jean-Jacques to his sister. Monsieur Hochon read it aloud: Rouget, constrained by ill health, regretted he could not pay the first visit but invited Joseph to dine the next day, asking Baruch Borniche and François Hochon to accompany him. Hochon’s slyness surfaced: he muttered that Jean-Jacques could as well have written the note as he could have given away twenty-five louis, implying a hint of shared manipulation and a soldierly hand in his correspondence. Madame Hochon, fearing that Agathe might be left with a legacy that could fall into Hochon’s hands, was eager to settle the matter quickly: an enormous fortune was at stake, and the situation roused Monsieur Hochon’s dormant political acumen.

While Gritte returned with the town’s gossip: the Rabouilleuse had had the salon and Rouget’s portion of the house in a pitiable state but had staged a grand turnaround; Kouski had fetched provisions; everything suggested a dinner “fit for the archbishop of Bourges.” Flore Brazier had dressed to dazzle—diamonds in her ears, costly lace, a silk dress, black satin slippers—so conspicuous that the town whispered of her ornaments and the expense of her attire. Joseph, amused and pleased, quipped they would have to dress up for the occasion.

As they drafted Agathe’s reply to Rouget’s letter, the Hochons arranged political strategy. Agathe’s reply thanked her brother for the invitation, explained that her long neglect of correspondence owed to the trials of Paris—her husband’s disgrace and their son Philippe’s imprisonment for devotion to the Emperor—and justified her coming to seek consolation among her birthfolk. She explained Joseph’s profession required long study and sacrifice; while it might bring honor, it promised little immediate fortune. She accepted Jean-Jacques’s invitation on Joseph’s behalf, asserting a sister’s right to come to her brother’s house and promising Joseph would attend the dinner. The letter emphasized family ties, the hardships she had borne, and the distinct fates of her two nephews—one in prison, the other pursuing art—hoping to touch Jean-Jacques’s sense of family duty.

Monsieur Hochon, who had been ruminating over the possibilities, finally spoke his counsel: Agathe must see Jean-Jacques and press him plainly about his nephews. He distrusted Maxence and Flore as practised scoundrels and feared Joseph lacked the force to struggle with them; he warned Joseph that with his romantic notions he would risk folly. Still, Hochon urged Joseph to go, to observe and to try to get a private moment with his uncle: if he succeeded, he must “pull the wool from his eyes” about the situation Jean-Jacques had let himself into and plead for his mother’s cause; if he failed, that failure would shed light on the strength of Maxence and Flore’s hold. Joseph, who admitted he lacked domestic statesmanship, was told to report everything he saw and heard that night—Hochon’s interest had been unexpectedly aroused by the prospect of a great fortune and by the path through which it might be secured.

Thus the Parisians, newly arrived and already under the town’s curious microscope, prepared to meet Rouget. Around them, provincial manners—stingy dinners, ritualized hospitality, theatrical female influence—and secretive malice—Maxence’s rat conspiracy—set the scene for the coming contest over property, reputation and family ties. The Hochons positioned themselves as Agathe’s allies, shrewd and fearful of losing any advantage; Maxence and his Order schemed to harry the newcomers; Flore Brazier’s influence over Jean-Jacques loomed as a dangerous obstacle. Joseph, an unrecognized artist with a dramatic, unsettling face, would be both a subject of gossip and the crucial interlocutor in the effort to restore Agathe’s claims.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At four o’clock Joseph crossed the avenue of weak lindens from the Rouget house to the Hochon house. Kouski announced him. The table was set in the large hall; Joseph, who at once recognized his uncle, went up, kissed him, and bowed to Flore and Max.

“We have not seen each other since I came into the world, my dear uncle,” the painter gayly announced.

“You are very welcome, my friend,” said the old man, looking at his nephew in a dull way.

“Madame,” Joseph said to Flore with an artist’s vivacity, “this morning I was envying my uncle the pleasure of admiring you every day.”

“Isn’t she beautiful?” the old man murmured; his dim eyes brightened.

“Beautiful enough to be the model of a great painter.”

“Nephew,” said Rouget, nudged by Flore, “this is Monsieur Maxence Gilet; he served the Emperor, like your brother, in the Imperial Guard.”

Joseph rose and bowed.

“Your brother was in the dragoons, I believe,” Maxence said. “I was only a dust-trotter.”

“On foot or on horseback,” said Flore, “you both risked your skins.”

Joseph and Max inspected one another. Max, a dandy with an eager face and his father’s gray eyes, presented to the artist’s eye a show of handsome compensation in Flore and the promise of tedious company; he called it a triangular household.

At that instant Baruch and François entered.

“Have you seen the tower of Issoudun?” Flore asked Joseph. “No? Then, if you’d like a walk before dinner in an hour, we’ll show you the great curiosity of the town.”

“Gladly,” said the artist, incapable of seeing any impropriety.

While Flore went to put on her bonnet, gloves, and shawl, Joseph jumped up to look at the pictures.

“Ah! you have pictures, indeed, uncle!” he cried at one that struck him.

“They came to us from the Descoings, who bought them during the Revolution, when the convents and churches in Berry were dismantled,” the old man answered.

Joseph was lost in admiration.

“Magnificent! Oh, what painting! That fellow didn’t spare his canvas. Better and better, as at Nicolet’s—”

“There are seven or eight very large ones up in the garret, kept on account of the frames,” said Gilet.

“Let me see them!” cried the artist; Max took him upstairs.

Joseph returned wildly enthusiastic. Max whispered to the Rabouilleuse, who drew the old man to a window embrasure while Joseph overheard her say, “Your nephew is a painter; you don’t care for those pictures; be kind, and give them to him.”

“It seems,” said Jean-Jacques, leaning on Flore’s arm to reach Joseph by an Albano, “—it seems that you are a painter—”

“Only a ‘rapin,’” said Joseph.

“What may that be?” asked Flore.

“A beginner,” replied Joseph.

“Well,” continued Jean-Jacques, “if these pictures can be of any use to you in your business, I give them to you,—but without the frames. Oh! the frames are gilt, and besides, they are very funny; I will put—”

“Well done, uncle!” cried Joseph, enchanted; “I’ll make you copies of the same dimensions, which you can put into the frames.”

“But that will take your time, and you will want canvas and colors,” said Flore. “You will have to spend money. Come, Père Rouget, offer your nephew a hundred francs for each copy; here are twenty-seven pictures, and I think there are eleven very big ones in the garret which ought to cost double,—call the whole four thousand francs. Oh, yes,” she added, turning to Joseph, “your uncle can well afford to pay you four thousand francs for the copies, since he keeps the frames—but bless me! you’ll want frames; and they say frames cost more than pictures; there’s more gold on them. Answer, monsieur.” She shook the old man’s arm. “Hein? It isn’t dear; your nephew will take four thousand francs for new pictures in place of the old ones.”

“Well, nephew, I will pay you four thousand francs for the copies—”

“No, no!” said the honest Joseph; “four thousand francs and the pictures, that’s too much; the pictures, don’t you see, are valuable—”

“Accept, simpleton!” said Flore; “he is your uncle, you know.”

“Very good, I accept,” said Joseph, bewildered; for he had recognized a Perugino.

The artist beamed as he went out with the Rabouilleuse on his arm, which suited Maxence’s plans. Neither Flore, nor Rouget, nor Max, nor anyone in Issoudun knew the true value of the pictures, and Max reckoned he had secured Flore’s triumph cheaply as she paraded the town, leaning on the master’s nephew. People flocked to their doors to see the crab-girl’s triumph. This sensation helped Max; when they returned at five, everyone talked of the cordial understanding between Max, Flore, and the nephew. The incident of the pictures and the four thousand francs circulated. The dinner, with Lousteau, one of the court judges, and the Mayor, was splendid and lasted five hours. By nine o’clock, at dessert, Joseph, seated opposite his uncle and between Flore and Max, had fraternized with the soldier and thought him a good fellow. Joseph returned home at eleven somewhat tipsy. Rouget was carried to bed dead-drunk; he had soaked up the wine like sand.

“Well,” Max said to Flore when alone, “isn’t this better than making faces at them? The Bridaus are well received, given presents, smothered with attentions; they will sing our praises and leave us in peace. Tomorrow you and I and Kouski will take down the pictures and send them over to the painter; we’ll put the frames in the garret and cover the walls with one of those varnished papers representing scenes from Telemachus.”

“Oh, that will be much prettier!” said Flore.

Next morning Joseph did not wake till midday. From bed he saw the pictures, brought in while he slept, leaning against the opposite wall. While he examined them anew, recognizing each masterpiece and searching for signatures, his mother had gone to see and thank her brother, urged by old Hochon, who, having heard of the painter’s follies the night before, despaired of the Bridau cause.

“Your adversaries have the cunning of foxes,” he said to Agathe. “In all my days I never saw a man carry things with such a high hand as that soldier; they say war educates young men! Joseph has let himself be fooled. They shut his mouth with wine, those miserable pictures, and four thousand francs! Your artist hasn’t cost Maxence much!”

The long-headed old man instructed Madame Bridau about a line of conduct: enter into Maxence’s ideas, cajole Flore, set up intimacy, and thus obtain a few moments’ interview with Jean-Jacques alone. Madame Bridau was warmly received by her brother, to whom Flore had taught her lesson. The old man was ill from his excesses. Agathe, under the circumstances, could scarcely at once speak of family matters; Max thought it proper and magnanimous to leave brother and sister alone. The calculation worked: poor Agathe found her brother so ill she would not deprive him of Madame Brazier’s care.

“Besides,” she said to the old bachelor, “I wish to know a person to whom I am grateful for the happiness of my brother.”

These words pleased the old man, who rang for Madame Flore. Flore, not far off, showed servile attentions and tenderness to her master, fussing over him like a new bride, and the poor creature received it with feeling.

“We owe you much gratitude, mademoiselle,” said Agathe, “for the attachment you have long shown my brother, and for the way you watch over his happiness.”

“That is true, my dear Agathe,” said the old man; “she has taught me what happiness is; she is a woman of excellent qualities.”

“And therefore, my dear brother, you ought to have recompensed Mademoiselle by making her your wife. Yes! I am too sincere in my religion not to wish to see you obey the precepts of the church. You would each be more tranquil in mind if you were not at variance with morality and the laws. I have come here, dear brother, to ask for help in my affliction; but do not suppose that we wish to make any remonstrance as to the manner in which you may dispose of your property—”

“Madame,” said Flore, “we know how unjust your father was to you. Monsieur here can tell you,” she went on, looking at Jean-Jacques, “that the only quarrels we have ever had were about you. I have always told him that he owes you part of the fortune he received from his father, and your father, my benefactor,—for he was my benefactor.” She added tears: “I shall ever remember him! But your brother, madame, has listened to reason.”

“Yes,” said the old man, “when I make my will you shall not be forgotten.”

“Don’t talk of these things, my dear brother; you do not yet know my nature.”

After such a beginning, it is easy to imagine how the visit went on. Rouget invited his sister to dinner on the next day but one.

During these three days the Knights of Idleness captured an immense quantity of rats and mice, kept them half-famished, then let four hundred and thirty-six into a grain storage one night, including some breeding mothers. They also put a dozen pigeons into the old church roof. These animals revelled while the watchman, enticed away by drink, took no care of his master’s property.

Madame Bridau believed, contrary to old Hochon, that her brother had as yet made no will; she intended to ask about Mademoiselle Brazier as soon as she could take a walk with him alone—a hope Flore and Maxence constantly dangled and disappointed.

Meantime the Knights searched for a way to put the Parisians to flight, finding none practicable.

At the end of a week—half the time the Parisians were to stay—the Bridaus had made no progress. “Your lawyer does not understand the provinces,” old Hochon told Madame Bridau. “What you have come to do can’t be done in two weeks, nor in two years; you ought never to leave your brother, but live here and try to give him some ideas of religion. You cannot countermine the fortifications of Flore and Maxence without getting a priest to sap them. That is my advice, and it is high time to set about it.”

“You certainly have very singular ideas about the clergy,” said Madame Hochon to her husband.

“Bah!” the old man exclaimed, “that’s just like you pious women.”

“God would never bless an enterprise undertaken in a sacrilegious spirit,” said Madame Bridau. “Use religion for such a purpose! Why, we should be more criminal than Flore.”

This conversation took place at breakfast with François and Baruch listening.

“Sacrilege!” exclaimed old Hochon. “If some good abbé, keen as I have known many, knew what a dilemma you are in, he would not think it sacrilege to bring your brother’s lost soul back to God, force him to send away the woman who causes the scandal (with a proper provision, of course), and show him how to set his conscience at rest by giving a few thousand francs a year to the seminary and leaving his property to the rightful heirs.”

The passive obedience the old miser exacted from his children and grandchildren prevented Baruch and François from showing surprise, but they exchanged glances about how dangerous such a scheme would be to Max’s interest.

“The fact is, madame,” said Baruch, “that if you want to secure your brother’s property, the only sure way will be to stay in Issoudun for the necessary length of time—”

“Mother,” said Joseph hastily, “you had better write to Desroches about all this. As for me, I ask nothing more than what my uncle has already given me.”

After recognizing the great value of his thirty-nine pictures, Joseph had carefully unnailed the canvases, covered them with paper, gummed the edges, and laid them one above another in an enormous wooden box, which he sent to Desroches by the carrier’s wagon, proposing to write him a letter by post. The precious freight had been sent off the night before.

“You are satisfied with a pretty poor bargain,” said Monsieur Hochon.

“I can easily get a hundred and fifty thousand francs for those pictures,” replied Joseph.

“Painter’s nonsense!” exclaimed old Hochon.

“Mother,” said Joseph, “I am going to write to Desroches and explain the state of things here. If he advises you to remain, you had better do so. As for your situation, we can always find another like it.”

“My dear Joseph,” said Madame Hochon, following him from the table, “I don’t know anything about your uncle’s pictures, but they ought to be good, judging by the places from which they came. If they are worth only forty thousand francs—a thousand apiece—tell no one. Though my grandsons are discreet, they might, without intending harm, speak of this windfall; it would be known all over Issoudun; and it is very important that our adversaries should not suspect it. You behave like a child!”

In fact, before evening many persons in Issoudun, including Max, had heard this estimate, which caused a search for old paintings and the appearance of many execrable daubs. Max repented having driven Rouget into giving away the pictures, and his rage against the heirs, after hearing old Hochon’s ecclesiastical scheme, increased. The influence of religion on such a feeble creature as Rouget was the one fear. The news decided Maxence to turn Rouget’s investments into money and to borrow on his landed property to buy into the Funds as soon as possible; but he considered it even more important to get rid of the Parisians at once.

Flore, by Max’s advice, pretended Monsieur was too feeble to take walks and ought to have a carriage. This pretext avoided inquiry when they went to Bourges, Vierzon, Châteauroux, Vatan, and other places where withdrawing investments obliged Max and Flore to be with Rouget. At the close of the week, all Issoudun was amazed that the old man had gone to Bourges to buy a carriage—a step the Knights regarded as favorable to the Rabouilleuse. Flore and Max selected a hideous “berlingot,” twenty-two years old, a sort of demi-fortune carriage. It only cost four hundred and fifty francs; Max bought a stout mare and repainted the carriage dark brown, buying a tolerable harness at a bargain. The town awaited Pere Rouget’s equipage; at its first appearance every household was on its doorstep.

The second time the old bachelor went out he drove to Bourges, went before a notary, and signed a power of attorney in favor of Maxence Gilet to make all the transfers enumerated. Flore reserved to herself the business of making Monsieur sell investments in Issoudun and its neighborhood. The principal notary in Bourges was requested by Rouget to get him a loan of one hundred and forty thousand francs on his landed estate. Nothing was known at Issoudun of these secret proceedings. Maxence, a good rider, went with his horse to Bourges and back between five in the morning and five in the afternoon. Flore never left the old bachelor. Rouget consented without objection to the actions Flore dictated; but he insisted that the investment in the Funds producing fifty thousand francs a year should stand in Flore’s name as holding a life-interest only, and in his as owner of the principal. His tenacity in this domestic dispute worried Max; he thought it the result of reflections inspired by seeing the natural heirs.

Amid these movements, which Max concealed, he forgot the Spaniard and his granary. Fario returned to Issoudun to deliver his corn after various trips to raise the price of cereals. The morning after his arrival he noticed the church roof black with pigeons. He hurried to the storehouse, where he found half his grain devoured. Thousands of mice and rat marks showed further ruin. The church was a Noah’s ark. But anger turned the Spaniard white when he found the grain at the bottom sprouting from water introduced by tin tubes into the pile. The pigeons and rats could be instinct, but the wetting showed human hand.

Fario sat on the chapel steps with his head between his hands. After half an hour he spied the squirrel Goddet had sent him playing on a cross-beam. He rose, turned to his watchman with a calm face, made no complaint, but hired laborers to gather sound grain and spread the moist to dry, saving as much as possible; after estimating his losses at about three fifths, he attended to filling his orders. But his previous manipulations had raised the price of cereals, and he lost on the three fifths he had to buy to fill orders; his losses were really over half. The Spaniard, with no enemies, at once attributed this revenge to Gilet. He was convinced Maxence and others were the authors of the nocturnal mischief and intended to ruin him. It was a matter of over three thousand francs—nearly his whole capital since the peace. Driven by the desire for vengeance, the man displayed the persistence of a detective. Hiding at night in different parts of Issoudun, he soon acquired proof of the Knights’ proceedings: he saw them, counted them, watched their rendezvous, and knew of their suppers at Mère Cognette’s; he lay in wait, witnessed their deeds, and thus became well informed of their nocturnal habits.

In spite of Max’s journeys and preoccupations, he did not neglect his nightly employments—first, because he did not wish his comrades to suspect his operations with Père Rouget’s property; second, to keep the Knights in hand. They were convened for a prank to be talked of for years: poisoned meat was to be thrown to every watchdog in town and environs. Fario overheard them congratulating each other after a supper on the probable success of this massacre and laughing over the general mourning it would cause.

“It will make people forget Fario’s cart,” said Goddet.

Fario needed no further proof; his mind was made up.

After three weeks in Issoudun, Agathe and Madame Hochon were convinced of the old miser’s observation that it would take years to destroy Max and Flore’s influence. She had made no progress in Jean-Jacques’s confidence and was never left alone with him. On the other hand, Mademoiselle Brazier triumphed openly by taking Agathe to drive in the caleche, sitting beside her on the back seat while Monsieur Rouget and his nephew occupied the front. Mother and son impatiently awaited an answer to the confidential letter they had written to Desroches. The day before the night on which the dogs were to be poisoned, Joseph, nearly bored to death in Issoudun, received two letters: one from the great painter Schinner and the second from Desroches.

Schinner’s letter, from Beaumont-sur-Oise, said Joseph was recommended for the chateau de Presles for the Comte de Serizy; Schinner was off to Italy; Joseph should take his place at the Salon if need be. Desroches’s letter gave practical advice:

My dear Joseph,—Your Monsieur Hochon strikes me as an old man full of common-sense, and you give me a high idea of his methods; he is perfectly right. My advice, since you ask it, is that your mother should remain at Issoudun with Madame Hochon, paying a small board—say four hundred francs a year—to reimburse her hosts. Madame Bridau ought, in my opinion, to follow Monsieur Hochon’s advice in everything; for your excellent mother will have many scruples in dealing with persons who have no scruple at all, and whose behavior to her is a masterstroke of policy. That Maxence, you are right enough, is dangerous. He is another Philippe, but of a different calibre. The scoundrel makes his vices serve his fortunes, and gets his amusement gratis; whereas your brother’s follies are never useful to him. All that you say alarms me, but I could do no good by going to Issoudun. Monsieur Hochon, acting behind your mother, will be more useful to you than I. As for you, you had better come back here; you are good for nothing in a matter which requires continual attention, careful observation, servile civilities, discretion in speech, and a dissimulation of manner and gesture wholly against the grain of artists.

If they have told you no will has been made, you may be sure they have one. But wills can be revoked, and as long as your uncle lives he is susceptible of being worked upon by remorse and religion. Your inheritance will be the result of a combat between the Church and the Rabouilleuse. There will come a time when that woman will lose her grip and religion will be all-powerful. So long as your uncle makes no gift of the property during his lifetime, and does not change his estate, all may come right when religion gets the upper hand. For this reason you must beg Monsieur Hochon to keep an eye on the condition of your uncle’s property. It is necessary to know if the real estate is mortgaged, and if so, where and in whose name the proceeds are invested. It is so easy to terrify an old man with fears about his life, in case you find him despoiling his own property, that almost any heir with a little adroitness could stop the spoliation at its outset. But how should your mother, with ignorance of the world and religious ideas, manage such an affair? I cannot throw more light. All you have done probably gave the alarm, and your adversaries may already have secured themselves—

“That is what I call an opinion in good shape,” exclaimed Monsieur Hochon, proud of being appreciated by a Parisian lawyer.

“Oh! Desroches is a famous fellow,” answered Joseph.

“It would be well to read that letter to the two women,” said the old man.

“There it is,” said Joseph, giving it to him; “as to me, I want to be off to-morrow; and I am now going to say good-by to my uncle.”

“Ah!” said Monsieur Hochon, “I see that Monsieur Desroches tells you in a postscript to burn the letter.”

“You can burn it after showing it to my mother,” said the painter.

Joseph dressed, crossed the little square, and called on his uncle, who was finishing breakfast. Max and Flore were at table.

“Don’t disturb yourself, my dear uncle; I have only come to say good-by.”

“You are going?” said Max, exchanging glances with Flore.

“Yes; I have work at the chateau of Monsieur de Serizy, and I am all the more glad because his arm is long enough to do a service to my poor brother in the Chamber of Peers.”

“Well, well, go and work,” said old Rouget, with a silly air. Joseph thought him extraordinarily changed within a few days. “Men must work—I am sorry you are going.”

“Oh! my mother will be here some time longer,” remarked Joseph.

Max made a movement with his lips which Flore observed, meaning: “They are going to try the plan Baruch warned me of.”

“I am very glad I came,” said Joseph, “for I have had the pleasure of making your acquaintance and you have enriched my studio—”

“Yes,” said Flore, “instead of enlightening your uncle on the value of his pictures, which is now estimated at over one hundred thousand francs, you have packed them off in a hurry to Paris. Poor dear man! he is no better than a baby! We have just been told of a little treasure at Bourges,—what did they call it? a Poussin,—which was in the choir of the cathedral before the Revolution and is now worth, all by itself, thirty thousand francs.”

“That was not right of you, my nephew,” said Jean-Jacques, at a sign from Max, which Joseph could not see.

“Come now, frankly,” said the soldier, laughing, “on your honor, what should you say those pictures were worth? You’ve made an easy haul out of your uncle! and right enough, too—uncles are made to be pillaged. Nature deprived me of uncles, but damn it, if I’d had any I should have shown them no mercy.”

“Did you know, monsieur,” said Flore to Rouget, “what your pictures were worth? How much did you say, Monsieur Joseph?”

“Well,” answered the painter, red as a beetroot,—“the pictures are certainly worth something.”

“They say you estimated them to Monsieur Hochon at one hundred and fifty thousand francs,” said Flore; “is that true?”

“Yes,” said the painter, with childlike honesty.

“And did you intend,” said Flore to the old man, “to give a hundred and fifty thousand francs to your nephew?”

“Never, never!” cried Jean-Jacques, on whom Flore had fixed her eye.

“There is one way to settle all this,” said the painter, “and that is to return them to you, uncle.”

“No, no, keep them,” said the old man.

“I shall send them back to you,” said Joseph, wounded by Max and Flore’s offensive silence. “There is something in my brushes which will make my fortune, without owing anything to any one, even an uncle. My respects to you, mademoiselle; good-day, monsieur—”

Joseph crossed the square in a state of irritation artists can imagine. The entire Hochon family were in the salon. Seeing Joseph gesticulate and talking to himself, they asked him what was the matter. The painter, as open as the day, related before Baruch and François the scene; two hours later it was the town’s talk, embroidered with various circumstances. Some persons insisted the painter was maltreated by Max; others that he had misbehaved to Flore and been turned out.

“What a child your son is!” said Hochon to Madame Bridau; “the booby is the dupe of a scene they have been keeping for the last day of his visit. Max and the Rabouilleuse have known the value of those pictures for the last two weeks—ever since he foolishly told it before my grandsons, who blurted it out to all the world. Your artist had better have taken himself off without taking leave.”

“My son has done right to return the pictures if they are really so valuable,” said Agathe.

“If they are worth, as he says, two hundred thousand francs,” said old Hochon, “it was folly to put himself in a way to be obliged to return them. You might have had that, at least, out of the property; whereas, as things are going now, you won’t get anything. And this scene with Joseph is almost a reason why your brother should refuse to see you again.”

Between midnight and the small hours the local idlers made their rounds feeding the town’s dogs; their errand over, they supped and slipped home before dawn. As Max turned into the Grande rue, Fario, lurking in a recess, stabbed him, wiped the blade on his handkerchief, and fled toward Vilatte. The Spaniard washed the handkerchief in the Riviere forcée and returned to his lodgings at Saint-Paterne; later a watchman found him asleep.

Max cried out as he fell. Lousteau-Prangin, the judge’s son and a relation of the sub-delegate, and young Goddet ran up, shouting that Max was being killed; the town, used to false alarms, mostly dozed on. They fetched Monsieur Goddet, the surgeon, and carried Max home, while the Rabouilleuse, Jean-Jacques Rouget, Kouski, and the Vedie clustered about in alarm. Max, reviving and finding the wound not mortal, seized the moment to inflame suspicion: he whispered that he believed he had recognized “that cursed painter.”

The servant at Monsieur Hochon’s, seen sweeping the steps, reported Joseph Bridau had risen at half-past four and had walked his room all night. This confirmed what artists guessed: Joseph had been restless with worry over the scandal at Issoudun and the charge that he had cheated his uncle. He had risen at dawn and gone to the poplar avenue to steady himself. Seeing him pacing, neighbors made him the obvious suspect.

Monsieur Goddet probed Max’s wound and, like many country surgeons who relish the gravity of their work, declared the blow serious and left a dramatic verdict—“he might die”—to excite the town. By dawn nearly two hundred people crowded around Place Saint-Jean and the two Narettes. Max, playing his part, whispered to Flore that he would not be long in bed and urged them to exploit the assault: pretend he might die; accuse Joseph Bridau to have him jailed briefly so his mother would hurry back to Paris and the Parisians would quit the town—thus ridding Issoudun of the hated city folk and any priestly meddling they feared.

Flore descended in tears and, sobbing, accused “the painter” — she pointed to the very face that townsfolk distrusted — of having been angry with Max over pictures he had “wormed out” of Pere Rouget. The Rabouilleuse and the Knights of Idleness took the cue: they interpreted the assault as proof the Parisian painter had schemed to secure his uncle’s fortune by removing Max. Rumors spread that the accused had left that morning, and fury mounted.

A Knight of Idleness ran to Monsieur Mouilleron, the local commissary, and the crowd swelled, raw with provincial prejudice against Parisians. Lousteau-Prangin, Monsieur Mouilleron, the lieutenant of gendarmes and two men pushed through the throng, though the shouting already prejudiced them against Joseph. After conferring with Max, Mouilleron sent officers to inspect the scene. The Hochon house was promptly guarded by gendarmes. Gritte, the Hochon servant, rushed to wake her master with the cry that the town was in revolt and that Joseph was accused of murdering Max.

Hochon, alarmed for his gold, bolted his house. Madame Hochon, pale and weeping, accepted the rumor of Joseph’s guilt only to worry for Agathe’s fragile nerves; Agathe, roused and half-dressed, fainted at the sight of the magistrates and gendarmes arriving. Lousteau-Prangin noted the crime was committed near Hochon’s, and, when pressed, Gritte confirmed she had seen Joseph leave that morning. Lousteau ordered warrants issued; the magistrates fretted that the exasperated crowd—said to be reinforced by twelve hundred more from the faubourg de Rome—might take the law into their own hands.

While officials debated, a clamor from another quarter announced Joseph’s capture. He had been seen at the place Misere and, returning quietly past the mill at Landrole to breakfast, had been seized by townspeople ready to execute justice. Two gendarmes snatched him from the hands of those who had already pinioned him and threatened him with death. The men escorting him declared they meant to protect him; whether guilty or innocent, they argued, the crowd adored Max Gilet and would not be restrained.

Joseph, pale but composed, was dragged amid jeers, insults and stone-throwing from Place Misere to Place Saint-Jean. The gendarmes formed a ring, one was wounded, and Joseph bore blows on his legs and shoulders. The lieutenant told the magistrates the only way to prevent a massacre was to take Joseph to the Palais under armed escort; a show of legal custody might disperse the mob’s thirst for summary vengeance. Mouilleron agreed, and Hochon, trembling for his household, consented. Joseph himself, calm and dignified, agreed to accompany them if they would allow him to write to his mother.

At the Palais, in Lousteau-Prangin’s private office, Joseph gave a composed account of his movements: he had been out past five, stood at half-past five before Saint-Cyr’s facade, talked with the sexton, passed the vegetable market where women had assembled, gone to the mill of Landrole and watched the ducks for several minutes — miller’s men would have seen him — and then walked along the avenue to Tivoli where he talked with the gardener. He asked the magistrates to verify these facts, requested not to be imprisoned and offered to remain in their office until cleared. His clear, sensible statements impressed them; Mouilleron promised to summon witnesses but warned an arrest might be inevitable to pacify the crowd. Joseph requested to write to his mother and did so; the letter calmed Madame Bridau, who had been in a nervous collapse. When Monsieur Goddet later said Max’s wound was not mortal, people began to breathe easier.

Earlier that night someone had slipped Max a typed note advising him that the assailant promised not to strike again if Max had Joseph Bridau released. Max had burned the note but sent a message later to Mouilleron asserting he had recognized the attacker and that a small pocketbook had deflected the blade, making the wound less fatal; Goddet added the attacker wore list shoes whereas Joseph left in boots. When Max wrote to Mouilleron reporting the list-shoe detail and asked for Joseph’s release, Lousteau-Prangin had already verified Joseph’s alibi by questioning the bell-ringer, market-women, washerwomen and the miller’s men. Max’s note clinched matters: Joseph was innocent. Mouilleron himself escorted him back to the Hochons’. His mother embraced him with a tenderness she had not shown before; the wronged son found in misfortune a human warmth.

Mouilleron, self-satisfied, declared he had read Joseph’s innocence in his face, but that the only way to protect him from the mob had been the ostensible arrest. Everyone urged Joseph to leave the town: the procureur du roi counseled taking the postmaster’s carriage that night and returning to Paris by the diligence from Bourges; Hochon, burning to see them go, agreed. Agathe, distraught and affectionate, kissed Madame Hochon’s hand and pleaded to be gone. The old woman, pious and melodramatic, answered that perhaps they would never meet again below — she had suffered enough.

Monsieur Rouget arrived with a farewell visit; he offered his caleche for the drive to Bourges and accepted responsibility for the pictures only half convinced of their value. Baruch, at his grandfather’s hint, saw the postmaster. At eleven that night the two Parisians, Joseph and Agathe, left Issoudun in a wicker cabriolet drawn by one horse and postilion. Adolphine and Madame Hochon wept as they parted; they alone of Hochon’s household regretted the Bridaus’ departure.

Back at Max’s bedroom Francois Hochon and the Rabouilleuse checked Max, who, tired and feverish, confessed the plan had succeeded. He had told Mouilleron that his assassin might try to strike again and had begged the magistrates to conduct an ostensible active search but in truth to let the attacker go, unless they preferred to stand accused of his death. Flore rejoiced that at least for a while they were rid of the Parisians. The next day Issoudun celebrated the Parisians’ departure as a provincial victory over the City; townspeople mocked the Bridaus as schemers who came to fleece the countryside and were outwitted by their betters. A few voices, the Hochons’ and the sober inhabitants’, disapproved of the triumphalism, but the prevailing mood was triumphal and xenophobic: “That’s Paris for you!” they cried. The Rabouilleuse, the Rabouilleuse’s friends and Max’s partisans were exultant.

Joseph and Agathe, on the road, found some consolation: Joseph recovered spirits lost by the insult and imprisonment; his mother, however, remained in low spirits, anxious about the Court of Peers’ trial of Philippe and the military conspirators, and the cloud of suspicion surrounding her son. To get away from the trial’s news and protect his nerves, Joseph, after briefing Desroches fully on Issoudun, took a commission with the Comte de Serizy to decorate the chateau at Presles.

Philippe’s own fate was briefly noted by contemporary history: whether his conduct in the trial was treacherous or merely part of political maneuvering, the Court of Peers sentenced him to five years’ police surveillance and ordered him to leave Paris that day for Autun. The punishment was essentially a parole, a town as prison. Hearing that the Comte de Serizy, one of the peers on the court-martial, had engaged Joseph for frescoes, Desroches sought out the count and persuaded him of the brothers’ need. He urged the minister of war to have the director-general of police move Philippe from Autun to Issoudun and to grant the former lieutenant-colonel a small monthly allowance of sixty francs. The count promised to obtain what Desroches asked.

Three days later Desroches, armed with this authority, fetched Philippe from the Court of Peers’ prison to his house in the rue de Bethizy and delivered a stinging, practical lecture. He rebuked Philippe for careless dissipation, for nearly wrecking his mother by his folly, and for the consequences of his past conduct. Then, bluntly, the lawyer explained the case in terms a client could understand: the brothers’ financial ruin could be repaired only by prudence; revenge was irrelevant. Desroches insisted Philippe could recover his uncle’s inheritance if he behaved with policy and prudence. He had already, without telling Joseph, sent the disputed pictures to Monsieur Hochon with instructions that they should be surrendered only to Philippe. Desroches praised Maxence Gilet’s courage, reminding Philippe that he would need a brave and constant rival in the local struggle.

Philippe, hardened and shrunken by imprisonment, brightened at the news that his residence would be Issoudun rather than Autun. He was told he would receive a quarterly stipend to clothe him decently, and Desroches promised to put him on the diligence for the evening. Philippe, who owned little beyond the threadbare blue overcoat on his back, asked for his sabre, sword and pistols; Desroches, aghast at the man’s appearance, said more than weapons would be needed. Godeschal, Desroches’s head-clerk and the brother of Mariette, appeared; Mariette — the actress Philippe cared for — was preparing for the opening of a new theatre. They dined frugally; then the two lawyers put the political convict on the diligence and wished him luck. Thus Philippe, thinned by disgrace yet scheming, was sent toward Issoudun to pursue, with instruction and a modicum of means, the recovery of an inheritance complicated by Max’s machinations and the local animosities fanned by the wound in the night.

The episode closed with the town’s triumphal exultation at having chased away the Parisians, the temporary victory of provincial prejudice, and the movement of the central characters: Joseph and Agathe removed and soothed for the moment, Max convalescent and triumphant that his stratagem had paled the city folk, and Philippe dispatched back toward the scene with a new plan and the legal backing of Desroches.

On November second, All-Souls’ day, Philippe Bridau registered at the Issoudun police and, on the official’s advice, took rooms in rue l’Avenier. Word spread that an officer, banished after a recent military conspiracy, had arrived — more excitement because he was the brother of the painter who had been wrongly accused. Maxence Gilet, recovered from his wound, had turned all Père Rouget’s mortgages into cash and placed the proceeds in the “grand-livre.” The loan of 140,000 francs on Rouget’s real estate caused scandal; in the provinces such news leaks swiftly. Monsieur Hochon, anxious for the Bridaus and still worried by the earlier terror over his house, asked old Monsieur Heron, the Bourges notary, about the matter.

Heron explained that, but for him, Rouget would have kept the 50,000-franc income in Maxence’s name; he had advised Mademoiselle Brazier to rely on the will and had urged Maxence and his mistress to let the sudden change pass unnoticed. Hochon, uneasy, pressed for protection of the Bridaus. Maxence and Flore, feeling secure, welcomed Philippe’s arrival; they believed they could get Rouget to sign a power of attorney transferring funds to Max or Flore if danger threatened. Even if the will naming Flore principal were revoked, the 50,000-franc income and the newly obtained 140,000-franc mortgage would be consolation.

The day after arriving, Philippe called on his uncle about ten, dressed shabbily. Flore recoiled at his repulsive appearance; Gilet felt the chill of a hidden enemy. Philippe’s ruined blue greatcoat was buttoned to the throat; his trousers and boots were ragged, his hat greasy, his malacca cane dull. His face had the exhausted look of a gambler — sunken cheeks, red-rimmed lids, a menacing brow, a fringe of hair, and cold steel-blue eyes. He introduced himself hoarsely as Philippe Bridau, a lieutenant-colonel ill-treated by the Bourbons, complaining that by police order he must live on a pay of sixty francs a month, and he made a point of disdain for relying on his uncle.

He offered his hand and declared he would sooner cut off his hand than beg from his uncle after the treatment of his mother and brother in Issoudun. Flore invited him to breakfast; he declined, asserting he had eaten. He hinted at safeguarding the Bridaus’ honor, spoke curtly about pictures at Monsieur Hochon’s for identification, and left, slamming the door so violently that it shocked Flore and Maxence more than his appearance had.

They watched him cross to the Hochons’. Though furious, Gilet kept his temper; he knew political prudence was needed. At the Hochons’, curiosity and unease were intense — Madame Hochon shuddered at the man she associated with several misfortunes. Philippe, however, was polite and composed. He explained he had come to introduce himself and to manage living on sixty francs, admitted past faults, promised reformation, and praised Lousteau the journalist, which gained Madame Hochon’s favor. She advised him gently; he said prison had taught him much and he intended another life.

Privately to Monsieur Hochon, Philippe warned that town gossip could ruin them and asked that advice be given only discreetly. Desroches had told Philippe to use Hochon’s counsel quietly; they agreed to “meet accidentally” in open places when needed. Philippe asked for names of veterans in Issoudun not favorable to Maxence. Hochon named Mignonnet, an artillery captain of the Guard, and Captain Carpentier, a cavalry officer who had replaced Gilet as mayoral clerk and befriended Mignonnet. Hochon suggested a job at a mutual insurance agency paying about fifty francs a month.

Within a week Philippe had bought on credit a decent suit, boots, gloves, and a hat. Giroudeau sent linen, weapons, and a letter to Carpentier, who welcomed Philippe and introduced him to Mignonnet, recommending him as a man of merit. Philippe narrated parts of the conspiracy to them in a way that won admiration: he portrayed the recent plot as the continuation of older army efforts against the Bourbons — a northern operation with a Paris diversion — betrayed and reduced to a smaller barrack affair. His ruined costume, he implied, had helped minimize the conspiracy’s apparent size to the authorities. He presented himself as a man who had played both sides, preserving favor with influential figures while waiting to join whichever party served his interests.

Carpentier and Mignonnet, impressed, brought him into their circle and recommended him for the insurance office Hochon had mentioned; the job required only three hours a day. Philippe frequented the officers’ club; his manners and their praise earned him respect. Determined to appear sober and respectable and to lull Gilet’s suspicions, he affected simplicity while quietly observing his uncle’s property and plotting. Hochon had given him details of Rouget’s wealth, which inflamed his covetousness. He and Hochon agreed not to alarm Maxence: if Rouget were spirited off to Bourges, the game would be lost.

Philippe dined regularly with the officers and Hochon and soon had engagements most nights. He never spoke of his uncle, brother, or mother except in passing. Among Maxence’s friends he seemed a “sabreur,” an ostentatious soldier of the old army; some thought him either a dupe or a spy. Philippe fenced with Carpentier and practiced pistols with them — ostensibly sport, really to persuade Maxence that he preferred pistols for a duel. When he met Gilet he returned a bow with an officer’s condescension; Gilet showed no impatience and never spoke of Bridau at the Cognettes’.

Philippe’s contempt and Gilet’s restrained bearing became the talk of Issoudun. Philippe investigated the circumstances of his brother’s arrest and of Gilet and the Rabouilleuse’s past, and he came into contact with Fario the Spaniard. Finding Fario reliable, Philippe made him a confidant and promised indemnity for past losses; Fario, a willing henchman, put his knowledge of the Knights of Idleness and his services at Philippe’s disposal. The town, abuzz, expected a deadly clash between the two men who intensely despised one another.

Toward the end of November Philippe told Hochon he had discovered Hochon’s grandsons, Baruch and François, were intimate with Maxence and had spied in his house, reporting what was said there. He claimed to have overheard them and learned from Fario of their nocturnal visits to drinking-shops with Maxence; they owed Max over three thousand francs. Philippe suspected that Maxence and the Rabouilleuse intended to secure the 50,000-franc income and then run away to marry. They must learn what was happening under Rouget’s roof, Philippe insisted; he did not yet know how to proceed. Hochon promised to consider it.

Meanwhile Jean-Jacques Rouget suffered as never before since Philippe’s first visit. Flore, fearing harm to Maxence, planned to leave Issoudun and marry Max in Paris after getting the funds transferred. Rouget refused to transfer, insisting Flore be sole heir — he feared she would abandon him if made rich. She tried cajolery then coldness; Rouget was found weeping and, for a week, breakfasted alone.

The day after Philippe’s talk with Hochon, he returned to Rouget’s. Flore feigned tenderness; Gilet stayed away. Philippe, sensing opportunity, forced Flore to look him in the face and asked whether Rouget was free to walk with him. Flore stammered that he was; Philippe insisted Rouget accompany him on foot, implying refusal would confirm that Rouget was under Flore’s control. Seeing Rouget hesitate, Philippe pretended to go; he caught Flore making a menacing gesture at her uncle and told Rouget to meet him at the door in ten minutes or he would take other measures. He left for the Hochons’.

At nine that night Hochon, Madame Hochon, Heron the notary, and the grandsons assembled. Hochon denounced Baruch and François for their nocturnal life with Maxence and for betraying secrets for money, even facilitating past crimes. He read the guardianship accounts: the Borniche children’s net fortune was 70,000 francs (part of their mother’s dowry), and Baruch’s portion was twenty thousand. Hochon declared he would release their property but no longer tolerate the grandsons’ conduct; he discharged his guardianship and conditioned their futures on strict behavior. Baruch was to be sent to Paris to study banking, François to Poitiers to study law with severely limited means. Having settled the legal business and obtained receipts, Heron prepared to leave when Philippe arrived.

Philippe and Hochon spoke briefly: Heron had shown that the security for the 50,000-franc income could not be sold without the owner’s signature or a power of attorney; Rouget had given no such power since his arrival. If Rouget tried to give one in Issoudun they would know immediately; if he left town to sign, registration would reveal it. Therefore, if Rouget left, they would follow him and learn his movements. Philippe reported seeing his uncle cross the street and said Fario and a soldier named Benjamin Bourdet were watching; they would follow Rouget secretly. “If they let him walk with you,” Hochon mused, “Maxence must believe he has found some means to win the game.” Philippe said Maxence was afraid of him but could not evade him; Fario had already stirred public censure against Maxence and set gossip in motion.

When Philippe left, Flore hurried to Maxence with a full account of his boldness. Max resolved that before resorting to fighting him they would play a daring card. He told Flore to send at once for Père Cognette’s horse and a char-à-banc; pack their things and go to Vatan pretending to stay, taking Vedie and the twenty thousand francs in Rouget’s drawer. If Philippe brought Rouget to Vatan, Flore must refuse to return unless Rouget signed a power of attorney. As soon as they obtained it, Max planned to slip off to Paris while she came back to Issoudun; when Jean-Jacques found her gone, he would be desperate and likely follow, and Max intended to use that moment to secure what he wanted.

While that plot unfolded, Philippe walked arm in arm with his uncle along the boulevard. Adopting a Parisian air, he taunted Rouget with the truth everyone suspected: Flore adored Max and treated Rouget like a servant; she wanted Max and aimed to remove Rouget from the way. The old man answered that he loved her still. Philippe replied that he could make “your Rabouilleuse supple as my glove,” and boasted he would kill Max like a dog—on one condition. He urged Rouget not to sign the power of attorney they sought before the third of December; delay it. They only needed it to sell the fifty thousand francs a year Rouget received in the Funds to pay for a wedding out of his millions.

Rouget, bewildered and fearful of Flore’s influence, was told to promise to sign but not actually to do so, and to inform Philippe the night before he meant to sign. Philippe’s plan was stark: Max would not be Rouget’s proxy unless he killed Philippe first; if Philippe killed Max, Rouget was to accept Philippe in Max’s place and Philippe pledged to make Flore love him—or, failing that, to thrash her. Rouget protested he could never allow violence against Flore; Philippe, scolding him for weakness, insisted it was the only way to govern women and horses.

Mignonnet and Carpentier joined them. Philippe reduced the rescue of Rouget to one outcome: either Bridau must kill Gilet or Gilet must kill Bridau. He asked them to arrange the coronation banquet seating so he would sit opposite Gilet and to act as seconds in the duel. They agreed. Philippe assured Rouget that postponing the signature until after December third would secure the uncle’s fortune and Flore’s affection.

They walked nearly two hours; Philippe repeated his advice and mocked women as inferior. At half-past two Rouget reached home to Kouski’s tears—on Max’s orders—announcing, dramatically, that “Madame has gone away, and taken Vedie with her!” Rouget searched the house, fell into a chair and wept; he knew nothing of Max’s whereabouts. Gilet, aware it was politic to be seen, left Rouget alone to suffer. Flore’s departure made the old man frantic.

Maxence Gilet found himself shunned by many who had once greeted him; the Knights of Idleness scandal and Bridau’s arrest had disgraced him. Captain Potel berated him for bringing shame on the Guard and urged him to appear at the coronation banquet; Max resolved to be there if possible. Determined to secure Rouget’s money before any duel, he muttered that he would have the funds before he fought; if he died, they should not go to Philippe. He planned to put property in Baruch’s name by a power of attorney and transfer it by his order. Calm outwardly, Max displayed the cool resolve of a general diverted to petty local ambition.

He entered Rouget’s salon, feigned surprise at Flore’s flight, and questioned Kouski. The Pole reported that Flore had taken twenty thousand francs in gold from Rouget’s drawer and declared she would not return, weeping that Rouget had no affection for her and his nephew treated her like the lowest of the low. Rouget begged Max to find Flore; Max pretended reluctance to oppose Bridau but agreed to make inquiries and have dinner ready. Kouski announced that Flore had driven toward Vatan—“Madame is going back to her own people,” he said. Max suggested they ride to Vatan that night: he would go on ahead if Rouget saddled his horse.

Monsieur Hochon had already told Philippe of Flore’s departure. Philippe guessed Max’s aim: if Rouget and Max drove to Vatan, Max would promise to reconcile them and likely secure the power of attorney. Philippe sent Fario and Benjamin to keep watch and hurried to Mignonnet’s to recruit Carpentier. A lad then brought Baruch a letter from Max asking his friend to receive a power of attorney from Rouget at Vatan the next morning and promising to send Rouget to Paris. Baruch, with most to lose, counseled surrender and amendment to save their prospects. Hochon rejoiced at the apparent salvation of Rouget’s property and urged Baruch to go to Vatan and accept the power, then await instructions.

Soon Francois spied wheels—Rouget and Bridau returning together to the Grande-Narette, Benjamin and Carpentier following on horseback. Hochon went to Rouget and found the old man writing, at Philippe’s dictation, a letter to Flore threatening to revoke her inheritance if she did not return at once. Philippe had intercepted Rouget before he fell into Max’s trap. “I have made my uncle see he was running into a noose,” Philippe told Hochon. He believed the letter would bring Flore back that very night.

Carpentier and Rouget read Maxence’s letter and jeered at his infamy. Philippe insisted on keeping Flore’s interests in hand; Rouget feared she loved Maxence too much to stay. Philippe planned for Carpentier to ride to Vatan and hand Rouget’s letter to Flore, hoping it would recall her. Carpentier drove off in the old calèche with Benjamin following, while the Pole—who had ridden ahead to warn Max and Flore—was to switch places with Benjamin later. When Philippe learned Kouski had fled to rejoin Max at the hotel de la Poste, he named Benjamin to replace the Pole and told Hochon the banquet would be jovial; he would sleep in the room next to Gilet’s if Rouget consented. Hochon agreed to stay and help keep Rouget from signing anything rash.

At half-past eleven wheels returned to the Grande-Narette. Rouget, from agony to joy, saw two women emerge from the carriage. Philippe brusquely informed Flore she was no longer Rouget’s servant and must leave that night; Benjamin would replace Kouski. Seizing her hand, he led Flore to the place Saint-Jean and, with threats and assurances, told her bluntly that in three days Maxence might be dead or Rouget off guard. If Bridau died, Flore would be mistress of Rouget’s fortune; if Bridau lived, she would have to treat Rouget well. Philippe warned there were ways to get rid of a woman as well as a man, and reminded her he had younger Parisian alternatives waiting.

Flore trembled at the thought of Max’s death and asked about killing him. Philippe cut her off and sent her back to Rouget. The old man took Flore home and shut the door; Kouski departed to rejoin Max, who had taken up at the hotel de la Poste.

Next day Issoudun buzzed with speculation about what would happen at the banquet between Max and Bridau. Philippe made Vedie a brief proposal—an annuity or dismissal—which kept her neutral. Flore, fearing for Max’s life, adopted an affectionate manner toward Rouget; self-interested devotion sometimes passes for love, and it seemed to work: she came down to breakfast on Rouget’s arm, tears in her eyes when she saw Philippe sitting where Max had sat. Philippe coolly declared he had no desire to kill Gilet; he suggested Max might do better to go to America and make a fortune rather than scandalize Issoudun.

Max, routed before the town and too proud to run from Philippe, refused to flee. He planned instead to kill the colonel. Though admitting they had committed a monstrous folly and that he ought to have gone to Paris, he would not retreat: his sense of honor required him to recover what he had lost by killing Bridau. Flore counseled flight to America and promised to find a way to get rid of Bridau, but Max insisted on fighting. The point of honor prevailed over sensible policy. Flore returned home and shut herself up to cry.

Gossip swelled in Issoudun. Mignonnet told Hochon people were uneasy because Gilet knew all weapons. But Hochon praised Philippe’s skill; the colonel had managed well from the start. Some defended Max’s nocturnal amusements; others recalled his betrayal of Joseph Bridau and sided with the colonel. Above the personal duel, the town fretted over the fate of Rouget’s property—fifty thousand francs a year that Max seemed ready to seize. Every household in town prayed for the honor of Colonel Bridau.

Towards four o’clock the next day the old army officers at Issoudun gathered in the place du Marché, before Lacroix’s eating-house, to await Colonel Philippe Bridau; the coronation banquet was set for five. They discussed Max’s disgrace and dismissal from old Rouget’s house; the officers would dine at Lacroix’s, the common soldiers at a wine-shop. Only Potel and Renard defended Max. Renard asked why they must mind the affairs of an old man’s heirs; Potel blamed Max’s weakness with women. Predictions of sabres and crime circulated.

Max joined the old soldiers and was received in heavy silence, supported only by Potel and Renard. Philippe arrived in full dress; his assured manner contrasting with Max’s constrained gratitude toward his few supporters. Bridau was warmly greeted by several officers, which dissolved Max’s last scruples born of Flore’s entreaties.

“We shall fight,” Max told Renard; “to the death. Don’t talk to me—let me play my part well.” The Bonapartists returned to the officers’ group. Max bowed to Bridau; their glance was frigid. Renard toasted “the Little Corporal” and, at Philippe’s request, the adversaries sat opposite each other. Town youths lingered outside, expecting a duel.

Throughout the meal both men masked vigilance with apparent joviality. At dessert Philippe toasted “To the Grand Army!” and Mignonnet followed with “Let us drink to those who attempted to restore his son!” All but Maxence Gilet bowed to Philippe. Max rose and bowed ironically to Philippe, then delivered a cold phrase—“May we all meet again at this time next year”—that raised tension. Plans for a duel were calmly fixed: sabres at ten that night, and at eight the next morning behind the Capuchins’ church; Goddet, the former army surgeon, would be present. The seconds set the combat to last ten minutes.

That night Monsieur and Madame Hochon surprised Philippe in his rooms. Madame Hochon, tearful, implored him to say his prayers and presented a relic—a tooth of Sainte Solange on black velvet—urging him to wear it. Philippe joked, but Benjamin was paid thirty francs to sew the relic into his trousers. The old lady departed confident.

On the cold morning of December third, Max, his seconds, and the Pole arrived at the meadow by the Capuchins’ apse. Philippe and his seconds waited, Benjamin among them. Potel and Mignonnet measured twenty-four feet and attendants drew lines: no man could retreat beyond his line under pain of dishonor. Each was to stand at his line and advance only on the seconds’ word.

They stripped to shirts without trembling. As the signal was given Max spotted Fario’s sinister face peering through a hole in the church roof—eyes full of hatred that unsettled him. Philippe adopted an inside guard of a seasoned duellist; Max felt inferior. He tried a moulinet to bewilder Philippe, but the colonel’s wrist was iron and spring-like. Philippe’s parries were cool and precise; victory depended on calculation or a single false movement. At a poorly executed parry Max’s sabre was spun from his hand.

“Pick it up,” Philippe said; “I am not the man to kill a disarmed enemy.” The magnanimous words stunned onlookers. When Max resumed position he had lost composure. Determined to redeem his shame, he rushed furiously with both hands on his sabre. Philippe received a cut slashing his forehead and part of his face, but with a terrible oblique moulinet he cleft Max’s head. These two savage blows ended the combat in the ninth minute. Fario came down to gloat over Max’s convulsions; Philippe was carried back to his uncle’s house.

Maxence Bridau’s death closed the exploits of the Order of Idleness and relieved Issoudun. He had courage, self-possession, and political sagacity, but lacked the nobility of conduct needed for greatness. Because of the perfidy that brought him into disrepute, he was not much regretted. Reports praised both combatants’ bravery; “they had better both have been killed,” Monsieur Mouilleron remarked, meaning it would have been a good riddance for the Government.

Flore Brazier’s situation, which might have embarrassed Philippe, was saved by her collapse after Max’s death. A brain fever set in, with dangerous inflammation from her escapade to Vatan; she hovered between life and death for three months, attended by Goddet, who also cared for Philippe.

As soon as Philippe could write he sent letters. To Monsieur Desroches he reported he had “killed the most venomous of the two reptiles,” though his own head had been split. He implied the other “viper” remained and that he must come to terms with her because his uncle clung to her. He said illness had kept Flore in bed, a condition he hoped Providence would use to call her soul if she repented. He claimed Goddet and others had been won to his side; Goddet preached resignation to Flore and had promised to keep her in bed for three months. He had paid off the cook, who spread tales suggesting Max would have ill-treated Flore. “All goes well so far,” he wrote; “my uncle, by old Hochon’s advice, has destroyed his will.”

To Monsieur Giroudeau, care of Mademoiselle Florentine, he requested a girl—Cesarine, if available—or some ballet-girl who could appear as the respectable daughter of a brave soldier and be ready at his call; if she answered his purpose he would give her fifty thousand francs on his uncle’s death. He described the duel and hinted at lively times ahead.

Madame Bridau’s sister Agathe, informed by Madame Hochon, rushed to Issoudun and occupied Philippe’s former room. Town praise for Philippe revived her maternal tenderness. Madame Hochon excused Philippe’s conduct as youthful fling and extolled him as a future comfort if kept from Paris temptations. Philippe played the saint to his mother because he needed her. He did not want to bring in an actress unless he remained an object of horror to Mademoiselle Brazier; recognizing Flore’s hold on his uncle, he conceived the idea of marrying her to Rouget to legitimize and keep her—and the property—within reach. He asked his mother to visit Flore and treat her sisterly.

Philippe presented the plan affectingly before Monsieur and Madame Hochon: Rouget’s way of life was not becoming, but by marrying Flore he could make her respected and stop scandal. The next day Agathe and Madame Hochon repeated this to Flore at her bedside. In Issoudun the colonel was praised for supposed nobility toward Flore. For a month she heard Goddet, Madame Hochon, Agathe, and Rouget urge a marriage. The idea of becoming Madame Rouget enticed her, and it was made plain she would be unwelcome in the Rouget family if she intended to turn Philippe out.

“Besides,” the doctor whispered, “you really owe him this good fortune. Max would never have allowed you to marry old Rouget. And, if you have children, you can revenge Max, for that will disinherit the Bridaus.” Thus the notion of revenge through offspring was planted.

Two months after the duel, in February 1823, urged by those around her and entreated by Rouget, Flore consented to receive Philippe; his scars moved her to tears, and his softened manner calmed her. By his wish they were left alone. He then began to play his hand: claiming to have advised the marriage from the start, he promised to ensure Flore’s treatment in the marriage contract and urged her that wealth and respect outweighed past losses. He hinted again at revenge through children, saying her offspring would disinherit the Bridaus. His manipulative phrasing masked calculation as sentiment; self-interest did the work of persuasion.

At the beginning of April 1823 Rouget’s hall hosted a splendid dinner celebrating the signing of the marriage contract between Flore Brazier and the old bachelor. Guests included Monsieur Heron, the witnesses, Mignonnet, Carpentier, Hochon, Goddet, the mayor, the curate, Agathe Bridau, Madame Hochon and Madame Borniche. The bride, protected by Philippe and the two ladies, shone; the curate had instructed her for a fortnight and she was to take first communion the next day. Local journals hailed the marriage as a revival of religion and moral correction; the “Journal du Cher” and the “Journal de l’Indre” lauded Philippe’s supposed disinterestedness.

By the contract Rouget secured to Flore a dower of one hundred thousand francs and a life annuity of thirty thousand. After the wedding Agathe returned to Paris triumphant and told Joseph and Desroches the “good news.” Desroches predicted Philippe would still keep his paw on the inheritance; Madame Bridau defended her son as statesmanlike. Desroches replied that Philippe would manage to appear honest while pursuing his ends.

The morning after the marriage Philippe took Madame Rouget aside and laid out his plan with cold frankness. He revealed a letter from Mariette and friends describing a plot to provide an actress to entrap Rouget and secure the property for Philippe; the letter terrified Madame Rouget, showing the danger she had escaped. “I trust you,” he said, “but I expect some return. I have made you my aunt intending to marry you some day. You are worth more to me than Esther in managing my uncle. In a year from now we must be in Paris; you will amuse yourself much better there than here; I shall return to the army and become a general, and you will be a great lady. There’s our future; now work for it. But I must have a pledge to bind this agreement.”

He demanded a power of attorney from Rouget within a month, under pretense of relieving the old man of business fatigues, and a month later a special power to transfer the income in the Funds to his name. When that stood in his name, he said, he and Flore would have equal interest in marrying each other—he could marry his aunt after a year’s widowhood but not “a disgraced girl.” He left without waiting for an answer. Vedie, clearing the table, found her mistress pale and sweating. Flore felt as if she had fallen to the bottom of a precipice; fear and a sense of guilty desertion overwhelmed her, yet an inner voice told her she deserved such subjection.

Flore had once had a room in Rouget’s house; now Madame Rouget belonged to her husband and was deprived of freedom. She briefly held hope of bearing a child, but Philippe’s constant watch made that impossible. Benjamin served as Philippe’s innocent spy; Vedie trembled under his scrutiny. Flore felt deserted; without knowing how, she was certain that if he suspected pregnancy her doom would be sealed. Philippe’s politeness cloaked brutality; his gambler’s eye and the slightest movement suggested threat. The power of attorney he demanded he obtained as soon as he wanted it; Flore fell under his dominion as France had under Napoleon.

Rouget rapidly declined into decay; in his decline the nephew remained cold and impassible, like a diplomat in convulsed times, neutral to the old man’s degradation.

Meanwhile Philippe, now skeptical of Napoleon II., wrote to the minister of war via intermediaries to offer his services to the King. He declared Napoleon gone and offered to serve the monarch, professing submission and fidelity and asking for consideration and reinstatement. He enclosed requests for permission to go to Paris on urgent family business and endorsements from Issoudun’s mayor, sub-prefect, and commissary of police praising his conduct and citing newspaper articles about Rouget’s marriage.

Two weeks later Philippe received permission and a letter informing him that by order of the King he was, as a preliminary favor, reinstated as lieutenant-colonel in the royal army.

Lieutenant-Colonel Philippe Bridau returned to Paris with his aunt and the frail old Rouget; three days later he escorted them to the Treasury where Jean-Jacques Rouget’s income was assigned to Philippe. Exhausted and vulnerable, the old man was drawn by his nephew into the dissipations of the theatrical and journalistic world—actresses, artists, and questionable women among whom Philippe had wasted his youth. Induced by Giroudeau, Lolotte, one of the most beautiful ballet-girls of the Opera, befriended the old man and became his willing destroyer. Rouget died after a magnificent supper at Florentine’s; Lolotte blamed a slice of pâté de foie gras, and the Strasburg gourmand’s infamy was quietly put down to indigestion.

Madame Rouget adapted to the loose society well enough, but Philippe placed her under the care of Mariette, who prevented actual ruinous excesses. In October 1823 Philippe returned to Issoudun with a power of attorney from his aunt to liquidate his uncle’s estate. By March 1824 he was back in Paris with sixteen hundred thousand francs—the net proceeds of Rouget’s property, not including the pictures, which remained with Monsieur Hochon. On Hochon’s recommendation he deposited the whole with Mongenod and Sons at six percent, with three months’ notice for withdrawal.

Philippe invited his mother, Agathe Bridau, to witness his marriage contract; the bargain arranged by him ensured that the widow Rouget’s million-franc portion would pass to him should she die childless. He lodged his wife in a rue Saint-Georges apartment bought furnished from Lolotte, a dwelling she found charming but which he rarely visited. Secretly he bought a splendid hotel in the rue de Clichy for 250,000 francs—150,000 paid down—and lavished immense sums restoring and furnishing it, even using money meant to represent two years’ income. The restored paintings from Issoudun, estimated at three hundred thousand francs, embellished this show.

With Charles X’s accession, the Duc de Chaulieu’s family regained court favor. Philippe, who had likely revealed conspiracies of 1820 and 1822, was made lieutenant-colonel in the Duc de Maufrigneuse’s regiment; the duke felt obliged to protect the man from whom he had taken Mariette. The appointment, and an effort to add a liberal tint to the Dauphin’s circle, led to Philippe’s presentation to the Dauphin and Dauphine. He played the part of rough soldier well, and parlayed this into an aide-de-camp position to a marshal well-placed at court.

Promoted and intent on social advancement, Philippe solicited ennoblement. Colonel Bridau had bought the estate of Brambourg and asked to entail it as a county; he became Comte de Brambourg. He adopted the external trappings of a great lord—horses, carriages, liveries—and began frequenting the house of the Comte de Soulanges, a lieutenant-general of artillery. Insatiable for influence and supported by mistresses of powerful men, Philippe aimed to be one of the Dauphin’s aides-de-camp and even to marry Mademoiselle Amélie de Soulanges. He had the audacity to plead with the Dauphin that a veteran soldier might be useful “on occasion,” and employed a full range of sycophantic tones to ingratiate himself in high society. Having copied the respectability that Mignonnet had once affected, he now fit the part required by court life: fine establishment, sumptuous entertainments, complete social posing—but he excluded old friends whose past could compromise him.

He was unrelenting toward companions of former debaucheries. When Bixiou sought his influence for Giroudeau, who wished to re-enter the army after Florentine’s desertion, Philippe brusquely refused: “The man has neither manners nor morals.” Yet he kept his own ruthless ambitions in view. He secured orders and titles—the Commanderships of the Legion of Honor and of Saint-Louis—and flaunted his status. Once, as Agathe and Joseph Bridau walked home in the rain, Philippe, bedizened in uniform and decorations, rode by and splashed them with mud as he waved a condescending greeting. Agathe, always ready to rationalize, excused his neglect as the price of great station, while Joseph bitterly noted his brother’s hauteur.

To further his plans, Philippe painted himself as a victim of an unsuitable marriage. To the Duc de Maufrigneuse he declared in theatrical tones that his wife was a cook with base tastes, that she had taken to drinking, and that her imminent death would free a million-franc legacy he had been saving to invest. In truth Philippe had engineered the collapse of domestic virtue to make him appear a respectable suitor for Amélie de Soulanges: he cultivated libertine society around his wife and then withdrew support until she sank into dependence and vice. He counted on the legacy, his entail, and a prospective marshal’s baton for Soulanges to elevate him to general and peerhood.

Meanwhile Agathe’s fortunes shifted. After the 1823 Salon, one of Joseph Bridau’s admirers awarded his mother the management of a lottery-office. She later exchanged it for an office near her son’s atelier in the rue de Seine, thereby supporting herself and freeing Joseph from obligation. Yet Agathe’s attachment shifted with appearance and success: Philippe’s glittering uniforms and courtly presence impressed her more than Joseph’s struggling genius. Joseph, a painter of talent but of uneven success, was thwarted by classical critics and an ungrateful bourgeois clientele. Though supported by friends like d’Arthez and Mademoiselle des Touches, he produced too much variety and too few commissions; he owed money and did not fulfill the material expectations borne by his circle. Agathe, unable to appreciate his artistic subtleties, yearned instead for the public honors that Philippe displayed—medals, parades, and courtly favor—so she took pride in her prosperous son and neglected Joseph’s worth.

Yet practical tenderness endured: Agathe bore the household running on the lottery-office proceeds and secretly supported Joseph, paying his bills while hoping Philippe would do more. For three years she had expected Philippe’s visits; convinced he could easily relieve Joseph’s debts, she wrote him a pleading letter, reminding him that he had forgotten his mother for five years and beseeching him to help Joseph financially. The reply she received shattered her: a single savage note in which Philippe brusquely alluded to the social magnitude of the man he intended to marry and the gulf between a count’s wife and a cook. The curtness and callousness of “Your son, Philippe Bridau” on the envelope struck her down. Agathe fell half-fainting; Joseph nursed her, sent for Bianchon, and secured funds for pressing debts without revealing the truth to his mother. She lingered in a death agony for three weeks.

On her deathbed Agathe summoned Abbé Loraux, her long-time confessor, and poured out her perplexed sorrow—“What have I done to displease God?” The priest gently indicted her life’s misplacements. He declared that her punishment was God’s proof to bring her to repentance: she had misjudged her children and lavished love upon an “unnatural son” who had pillaged her while overlooking the son who loved her without equal return. The priest’s rebuke opened her eyes. She wept, repented, and turned her heart to Joseph, whom she pressed to forgive. He, though stunned by the ostracism and his brother’s cruelty, embraced her and forgave her in the fulness of that last tenderness. For two weeks each expression seemed the sum of a lifetime; Agathe, soothed by Joseph’s devotion, died reconciled. In delirium she murmured of Philippe’s conspicuous sacrifices—“Whom does Philippe take after?”—and Joseph followed her alone to the grave. Philippe, who had been invited to business at Orléans on the day of the funeral, had actually fled Paris after receiving Joseph’s reproachful note: “Monster! my poor mother has died of the shock your letter caused her. Wear mourning, but pretend illness; I will not suffer her assassin to stand at my side before her coffin.”

Joseph, too shattered to work, was kept company by devoted friends: d’Arthez, Michel Chrestien, Fulgence Ridal, Pierre Grassou, and Bianchon. Two weeks after the funeral they received a curious letter from a shabby old woman, requesting assistance for Comtesse Flore de Brambourg. Written with clutching pathos and accusing Philippe of desiring his wife’s death, the letter alleged that Flore, abandoned and penniless, faced eviction and was considering suicide. Joseph read the name: “Comtesse Flore de Brambourg” — his sister-in-law. He shuddered, suspecting deeper villainy.

The woman who delivered the letter, Madame Gruget, presented as a miserable, worn nurse. She recounted her pitiable charges: a young protégée who had loved imprudently and drowned herself, and Flore—beautiful once, now hideously wasted, penniless and ill. Bianchon, Desroches, Bixiou, and Joseph went to see Flore. Bixiou, with savage wit, supplied a summary of Philippe’s methods: paying off dissolute companions to corrupt his wife, ceasing to support her until vice and poverty did their work, and then abandoning her to languish. They found Flore in a garret cell under the roof, green and hollow-eyed, head bound, a mere skeleton of the former woman. She wept at Joseph’s compassion. Flore declared Philippe guilty—“Philippe killed Max, who advised dreadful things, and now he has killed me. God uses him as a scourge!”—and implored for priestly consolation she could not secure.

Bianchon judged her disease possibly curable with surgical aid, and Desroches arranged transfer to Dr. Dubois’s private hospital. There, with the energetic intervention of Bianchon and Desroches and the sympathy of Joseph’s friends, Flore underwent bold surgery reported later in the Hospital Gazette. Still, she was too far gone: misery and starvation and the ravages of vice had exhausted her. She died three weeks later; the operation could not overcome her dereliction.

While this drama unfolded, Bixiou took revenge in his own way. Knowing how Philippe courted the Soulanges family for marriage with their daughter, Bixiou, with characteristic malice and theatricality, disguised himself as a secular priest and recounted to the Comte de Soulanges—in a mock-hushed and portentous voice—stories of Philippe’s past: the suspicious deaths, the thefts, the moral lapses. He presented gossip, and the Comte, prudent toward his daughter’s prospects, made inquiries. The Soulanges housewoman later found reasons to hesitate about marriage with a parvenu whose youthful ledger contained such disreputable entries. Bixiou’s stratagem had its effect.

Philippe, meanwhile, remained a figure who could be at once flamboyant and cold. He gave supper to du Tillet, Nucingen, Eugène de Rastignac, Maxime de Trailles, and Henri de Marsay, boasting of manifold millions—“three millions, excluding house and estate”—and of a future fortune. The bankers du Tillet and Nucingen traded on Philippe’s disdain for “civilians” and his confidence in the aristocratic Restoration. In July 1830 they used his credulity to ensnare him in speculation: they pretended to share his conviction of a Restoration triumph, encouraged him to risk, and then played against him as revolution loomed. Philippe fought for what he believed to be the cause; when orders called him with the Duc de Maufrigneuse to Saint-Cloud on July 25th he was spared the immediate danger of presiding over a charge upon the boulevards—an order that likely saved his life that day. But within a month his immense fortune had been swept away—only his house, furniture, Brambourg estates, and the pictures from Issoudun remained. He had trusted the wrong men and misjudged political probabilities.

Broke and desperate to recover prestige, Philippe re-entered the army. In 1835 he obtained a regiment command in Algeria—three perilous years that offered a chance of attaining the general’s epaulets he craved. But his severity and brutality alienated his men, and the rivalry with Giroudeau—once a companion—became malignant. Philippe’s harshness verged on cruelty; his troops hated him despite sometimes consummate bravery. In early 1839, during a risky retreat before superior Arab forces, he rashly headed in with only a single company to break through, and there, isolated, he was surrounded. His cries—“Your colonel! to me! a colonel of the Empire!”—went unheeded. The conflict was ferocious and personal. Philippe Bridau met a savage end: hacked to pieces by scimitars, his head cut off.

Joseph Bridau’s fortunes were, ironically, reversed by these tragedies. Around this time he married through the intercession of the Comte de Serizy a farmer’s daughter endowed with means; his father-in-law’s thrift and industry provided steady financial support. As a result of the entail on Brambourg, Joseph inherited his brother’s Paris house and the estate, plus the cherished Issoudun pictures—the very collection Philippe had once used as social capital. He thus acquired an income of sixty thousand francs and continued assistance from his father-in-law. Joseph’s art flourished: he painted magnificent works and did important service to the artistic community, though he had not yet been elected to the Institute. A clause in the entail also conferred the title Comte de Brambourg upon him—a distinction that caused him to laugh among his atelier friends, for it married noble name to the life of a bohemian painter.

Throughout this narrative various figures from Balzac’s Human Comedy orbit appear: Bianchon, the faithful physician; Bixiou, the satirical society wit; Giroudeau, the military rival; Bianchon’s medical interventions; and a host of others—Madame Raoul Nathan, the Duc de Maufrigneuse, the Soulanges family, Nucingen and du Tillet the bankers, and more—each playing parts in the shifting fortunes of the Bridau family. The story traces Philippe’s cynical social climbing and cold calculations, his moral degradation in pursuit of rank and marriage, the collateral tragedies of his coldness—Agathe’s death from shock, Flore’s abandonment and death—and his final, violent fall in Algeria. Opposed to him stands Joseph, the struggling artist who, consistently generous and devoted, finally inherits what his brother had burned through: modest happiness, the pictures he loves, and an ironic title that crowns an improbable victory of virtue and art over cynical ambition.
