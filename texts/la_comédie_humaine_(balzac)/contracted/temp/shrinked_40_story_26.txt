Trained by surgeon Desplein, Horace Bianchon became a physiologist. Desplein blended surgical brilliance with staunch atheism—hardened by dissecting corpses—and was criticized for odd dress, moodiness, and contempt for human folly. Bianchon earned his mentorship and began practice. Desplein’s urgent care for a destitute water‑carrier—who had long attended and even founded a mass—revealed unexpected compassion.

Some time passed before Dr. Bianchon, despite their friendship, found an opening to ask Desplein about a pivotal episode in his life. Their meetings rarely allowed private talk, but seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, Bianchon saw Desplein entering Saint‑Sulpice and knelt beside him; they attended mass together.

“Can you explain your sudden piety?” Bianchon asked as they left. “You, who do not believe in God, at mass? You owe me an explanation.”

Desplein first deflected, noting how many are outwardly devout yet inwardly skeptical, then criticized politicians; Bianchon pressed him about why he had founded this mass. Desplein answered plainly: he was nearing the end of his life and could trace its beginnings. Standing in the Rue des Quatre‑Vents, he pointed to the house where he had lived two desperate years and recalled hunger, poverty, isolation, and an irritable temper that made friendship difficult. He had no money for books or medical training and struggled alone to rise from destitution.

He spoke of meager meals and tireless labor without sympathy or support. Those born to wealth could not imagine his plight and treated debt as a choice. Early hardships shaped his view of humanity: he had seen selfishness and envy and expected mediocrity to try to pull down those who strove for excellence.

A turning point came when, at the depth of his poverty, he contemplated enlisting. He had hoped for a box of linen from a relative, but when the porter demanded too much to release it, despair set in. Then his neighbor Bourgeat—a humble water‑carrier—appeared and changed his fate.

Bourgeat, though poor himself, paid Desplein’s debts and secured new lodgings. He devoted his savings to Desplein’s future, understood his mission, and supported him selflessly. Desplein described Bourgeat as nurturing, almost maternal, who took pride in his protégé yet sought no recognition. When Desplein became a house surgeon, leaving Bourgeat pained him; Bourgeat was proud nonetheless.

Later, Desplein repaid him by buying him a horse and a barrel, fulfilling a long‑held dream. Bourgeat was angry that Desplein had spent his money on him, yet delighted; he never claimed credit for Desplein’s rise, though Desplein felt he owed him everything.

When Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein tended him devotedly; Bourgeat died in his arms, leaving him all he had. He had strong faith, and Desplein honored his wishes by having masses said for him after his death. The mass Desplein founded was a tribute to Bourgeat’s faith; Desplein hoped, if there is a paradise, that Bourgeat had found peace there and admitted he would give anything for such faith.

Bianchon, who had witnessed Desplein’s last days, could not say definitively whether the great surgeon died an atheist. He wondered whether Bourgeat might have opened the gates of Heaven for Desplein, just as he had opened the doors to the earthly temple they had shared.

PARIS, January 1836.
