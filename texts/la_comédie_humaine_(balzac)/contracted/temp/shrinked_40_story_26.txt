Bianchon, a Parisian physician, trained under the troubled Desplein, who struggled with his legacy and embraced atheism, feeling isolated in his beliefs. Desplein, who denied the existence of a soul, faced mixed judgments about his eccentricity. 

Bianchon, who experienced poverty during his medical studies, was known for his integrity. Desplein recognized his potential and mentored him in medical practice. Bianchon was intrigued when he saw Desplein enter a church, given his atheism. At dinner, Desplein dismissed the significance of mass, criticizing its historical implications.

When questioned about his church visit, Desplein offered a vague excuse. Bianchon later learned that Desplein had founded the service he attended, adding to the mystery of his participation in rituals contrary to his beliefs.

After the 1830 Revolution, Bianchon joined Desplein at Saint-Sulpice for mass, seeking an explanation. Desplein recounted his early years of extreme poverty and the support he received from his neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier. Desplein honored Bourgeat's faith by arranging masses for his soul after his death.
