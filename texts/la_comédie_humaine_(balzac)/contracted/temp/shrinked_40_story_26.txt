Bianchon, a prominent Parisian physician, trained under the complex Desplein, who struggled with his atheism and the desire to leave a legacy. Desplein rejected the soul's existence, which he never found in dissections, leading to mixed judgments about his character; he was both generous and miserly, often disdainful of humanity.

Bianchon, who faced poverty during his medical studies, distinguished himself through integrity and loyalty. Desplein recognized his potential and mentored him in medicine and Parisian society. Bianchon was intrigued by Desplein's church visit, given his atheism. At dinner, Desplein dismissed the significance of mass, criticizing its historical context.

When questioned about his church attendance, Desplein offered vague excuses. Bianchon later confirmed Desplein had founded the service, deepening the mystery of his rituals contradicting his beliefs.

Bianchon took seven years to recount a significant incident in Desplein's life. After the 1830 Revolution, amidst chaos, Bianchon joined Desplein at Saint-Sulpice for mass, where Desplein shared his struggles with poverty while financing his education, often surviving on stale bread soaked in milk.

Despite hardships, Desplein was determined to succeed, feeling misunderstood by those unaware of true poverty. Facing eviction, his neighbor Bourgeat, a humble water-carrier, helped him financially. Their bond deepened, and when Bourgeat fell ill, Desplein cared for him until his death. Heartbroken, Desplein honored Bourgeat's faith by arranging masses for his soul, prompting Bianchon to reflect on whether Bourgeat's faith might have opened Heaven's gates for Desplein.
