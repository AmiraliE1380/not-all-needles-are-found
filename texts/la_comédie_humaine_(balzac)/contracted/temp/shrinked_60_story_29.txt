In Saumur, a melancholic house stands beside a dilapidated artisan's home, belonging to Monsieur Grandet, a wealthy man who gained his fortune during the Revolution by acquiring church properties and vineyards. By 1816, his estate was valued at nearly four million francs, earning him admiration and fear. Grandet lived frugally, focused on marrying off his daughter, Eugenie, amidst competition from the Cruchots and des Grassins. The sale of Froidfond intensified this rivalry, raising questions about love, wealth, and duty in their society.

Monsieur Grandet's cold tufa stone house serves the family and local townsfolk, with Grandet tightly controlling resources. His wife, Madame Grandet, and daughter, Eugenie, manage household tasks, while their long-serving servant, La Grande Nanon, saves her meager wages despite Grandet's disdain.

In November 1819, the family prepares for Eugenie's birthday, where Grandet gifts her a gold coin and Madame Grandet a dress. Local notables arrive, vying for Grandet's favor, when an unexpected knock reveals his nephew, Charles Grandet from Paris. Charles's charm contrasts with Grandet's stern demeanor, and he captivates Eugenie with his refinement.

Grandet receives news of his brother's bankruptcy and impending death, complicating his feelings about family duty. Charles feels out of place, while Eugenie develops feelings for him, but Grandet dismisses the idea of a match. Tragedy strikes with news of his brother's suicide, shocking the family. Eugenie, urged to keep this from Charles, prepares a special breakfast, but Grandet chastises them for the lavish meal. Charles learns of his father's death, deepening Eugenie's affection amid their shared sorrow.

At four o'clock, Madame Grandet was surprised by her husband’s joyful announcement of a profitable wine sale. However, when Eugenie suggested helping her grieving cousin Charles, Grandet's mood soured, and he scolded her, threatening to send her away if she mentioned it again. He then retreated to calculate his wealth while Charles mourned alone, refusing to eat.

During a tense family dinner, Madame Grandet suggested mourning attire, but Grandet dismissed the idea as unnecessary expense. Eugenie secretly visited Charles, offering comfort and awakening feelings of love. The next morning, Grandet planned to manipulate the situation for his financial gain while Eugenie and Charles grew closer.

Grandet hosted a dinner for the Cruchots, where unspoken emotions simmered between Eugenie and Charles. After dinner, Charles excused himself to write letters, allowing Grandet to discuss his brother's financial troubles with the Cruchots. Grandet expressed reluctance to liquidate his brother's affairs, preferring to maintain his reputation.

Later, Grandet instructed his servant Nanon to prepare for a secretive journey to sell his gold, reflecting his greed. Meanwhile, Eugenie found Charles asleep with letters revealing his father's suicide and his plan to seek fortune in the Indies. Moved, she offered him her savings, which he accepted, marking a shift in their relationship.

As their affection grew, Grandet remained indifferent. On the day of Charles's departure, sadness filled the air, but Grandet unexpectedly arranged for his belongings to be sent to Nantes. Afterward, Eugenie mourned Charles's absence, her beauty transformed by love, while Nanon expressed frustrations about the sacrifices women make.

Two months later, Eugenie and her mother, Madame Grandet, were anxious about a secret involving Charles, Eugenie's cousin. Eugenie often looked at her aunt's portrait, searching for Charles's resemblance. When Madame Grandet discovered Eugenie's exchange of gold for Charles's affection, panic set in about facing Grandet on New Year’s Day, when he would expect to see Eugenie's gold.

As the new year approached, dread filled the household. On January 1, 1820, Madame Grandet requested a fire to delay their visit to Grandet, who was in good humor due to a financial success. Oblivious to the family's turmoil, he pressed Eugenie for her gold after breakfast. When she claimed she no longer had it, he erupted in anger, accusing her of deceit.

Desperate to protect her mother, Eugenie pleaded for mercy, but Madame Grandet, weakened by the confrontation, was taken to bed, leaving Eugenie to face Grandet alone. His fury escalated as he demanded to know what she had done with her gold. Eugenie maintained her silence, asserting her independence, which only infuriated Grandet further.

Months passed, and as Madame Grandet's health declined, townspeople speculated about Eugenie's treatment. Despite this, Eugenie remained focused on her love for Charles. With spring nearing, Madame Grandet sought help from notary Cruchot, who warned Grandet that if his wife died, Eugenie would inherit her estate. This revelation shocked Grandet, prompting him to reconcile with Eugenie. However, upon entering their home, he found her and her mother admiring a dressing-case filled with gold from Charles. Driven by greed, Grandet attempted to seize the gold, leading to a confrontation where Eugenie threatened self-harm. Hesitating, Grandet backed down.

After her mother’s death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home. M. Grandet showed unexpected tenderness, which soon revealed itself as a façade. He pressured Eugenie to renounce her rights to the estate, promising her a monthly allowance, which he never fulfilled. 

In the following years, M. Grandet trained Eugenie in managing the household, instilling his miserly habits in her. After his passing, Eugenie learned from Maitre Cruchot that she inherited a vast fortune, yet her thoughts were consumed by her missing cousin, Charles. Despite her wealth, she felt no happiness, overshadowed by her mother’s suffering and her unfulfilled love for Charles, who had not contacted her in seven years.

In Saumur, Charles Grandet, now wealthy from the slave trade, distanced himself from family and returned to Bordeaux in 1827 as "Shepherd" with nearly two million francs. He caught the attention of Monsieur d’Aubrion, who wanted him to marry his daughter, overshadowing Charles's feelings for Eugenie. 

In Saumur, Eugenie hesitated to read Charles's letter about his engagement, feeling devastated and recalling her mother’s warnings about suffering. As she avoided memories of Charles, she expressed a desire to retreat from the world. When informed of his engagement, she felt betrayed and questioned the priest about remaining a virgin after marriage.

Eugenie later asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay but insisted on her freedom and settling her uncle's debts. She ultimately severed ties with Charles, wishing him happiness but acknowledging their love couldn't survive societal norms. After marrying the president, who died shortly after, she lived modestly, dedicating herself to charity and embodying resilience amidst societal constraints.
