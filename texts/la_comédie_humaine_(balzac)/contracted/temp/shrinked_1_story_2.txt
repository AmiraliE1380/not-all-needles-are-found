The Comte de Fontaine, head of an ancient Poitou family, had served the Bourbon cause with intelligence and bravery during the war in La Vendée. Having escaped the dangers that threatened royalist leaders, he joked, “I am one of the men who gave themselves to be killed on the steps of the throne,” a pleasantry from surviving the bloody battle of Les Quatre Chemins. Though ruined by confiscation, the staunch Vendeen refused lucrative posts under Napoleon. Immovable in his aristocratic faith, he obeyed its precepts in marriage, wedding Mademoiselle de Kergarouet—without fortune but from one of Brittany’s oldest families—despite a rich revolutionary parvenu’s blandishments.

When the second Restoration burst on Monsieur de Fontaine he was encumbered with a large family. Though a nobleman would not solicit favors, he yielded to his wife’s wish, left his estate—whose income barely sufficed for the children—and came to Paris. Saddened by the greed of his former comrades rushing for places under the new Constitution, he was about to return to his property when a ministerial dispatch named him maréchal de camp, under a rule allowing officers of the Catholic armies to count the twenty submerged years of Louis XVIII’s reign as service. Days later he received, without solicitation, the crosses of the Legion of Honor and of Saint-Louis.

Shaken by these favors, which he supposed due to the monarch’s remembrance, he no longer contented himself with taking his family to cry “Vive le Roi” in the Tuileries on Sundays; he craved an audience. The royal drawing-room was full of old adherents whose powdered heads suggested a carpet of snow. There the Count met old friends who received him coldly; but the princes he found “ADORABLE,” an enthusiastic expression that escaped him when the most gracious of his masters—whom the Count thought knew him only by name—came to shake hands and called him the most thorough Vendeen of them all. Despite this ovation, none inquired about the sum of his losses or the money he had poured into the Catholic regiments. He discovered, a little late, that he had made war at his own cost. Toward evening he ventured a witty allusion to his affairs. His Majesty laughed—any speech bearing the hall-mark of wit pleased him—but replied with a royal pleasantry whose sweetness was more formidable than anger. An intimate of the King politely hinted that the time had not yet come for settling accounts; bills of longer standing, the history of the Revolution, would remain. The Count prudently withdrew, and, disentangling his sword among the lean legs clustered there, crossed the Tuileries courtyard to his hackney-cab. In the cab he bewailed, loudly enough to compromise him, the change at Court: formerly nobles could ask favors and recover what they advanced; now the cross of Saint-Louis and the rank of brigadier-general would not make good the three hundred thousand livres he had spent. He resolved to speak to the King face to face in his own room.

This scene cooled Monsieur de Fontaine’s ardor still more because his requests for interviews were unanswered. He saw upstarts of the Empire obtain offices once reserved for the highest families. “All is lost!” he exclaimed one morning. “The King has certainly never been other than a revolutionary. But for Monsieur, who never derogates, and is some comfort to his faithful adherents, I do not know what hands the crown of France might not fall into if things go on like this. Their cursed constitutional system is the worst possible government, and can never suit France. Louis XVIII. and Monsieur Beugnot spoiled everything at Saint Ouen.”

Preparing to retire to his estate, abandoning claims to repayment, the Count was warned by the events of 20th March (1815) of a fresh storm threatening the monarch. Like one who does not dismiss a servant in a torrent of rain, he borrowed on his lands to follow the routed Court into exile, uncertain whether emigration would prove more propitious than past devotion. He perhaps hoped greater profit from accompanying the King abroad than from dangerous service at home. The calculation was not rash. He was among the faithful five hundred who shared the Court’s exile at Ghent, and among the fifty thousand who returned with it. During the short banishment he was employed by Louis XVIII and found opportunities to show political honesty and attachment. One evening, when the King had nothing better to do, he recalled the Count’s witticism at the Tuileries; the old Vendeen told the story with vivacity, and the king, who never forgot anything, might remember it at a convenient season. The monarch also observed notes the discreet gentleman had recast with elegant style. This little success stamped Monsieur de Fontaine on the King’s memory as one of the Crown’s loyal servants.

At the second Restoration the Count was among special envoys sent through the departments, charged with absolute jurisdiction over leaders of revolt; but he used his powers with moderation. Afterward he served in the Privy Council, became a deputy, spoke little, listened much, and changed his opinions considerably. Certain circumstances brought him into such intimacy with the Sovereign that once, as he came in, the shrewd monarch said: “My friend Fontaine, I shall take care never to appoint you to be director-general, or minister. Neither you nor I, as employees, could keep our place on account of our opinions. Representative government has this advantage; it saves Us the trouble We used to have of dismissing Our Secretaries of State. Our Council is a perfect inn-parlor, whither public opinion sometimes sends strange travelers; however, We can always find a place for Our faithful adherents.”

This ironical speech prefaced a rescript giving Monsieur de Fontaine an appointment as administrator in the office of Crown lands. Thanks to the intelligent attention with which he listened to his royal friend’s sarcasms, his name rose to the King’s lips when commissions carrying handsome salaries were to be appointed. He held his tongue about the favor, and entertained the monarch in those familiar chats Louis XVIII delighted in, as much as in a well-written note, with political anecdotes and parliamentary tittle-tattle. The King was immensely amused by every detail of his “Gouvernementabilité,” a word the facetious Majesty adopted.

Thanks to Comte de Fontaine’s wit and tact, every member of his large family, however young, ended, as he jested, by attaching himself like a silkworm to the leaves of the Pay-List. By the King’s intervention his eldest son found a high fixed position as a lawyer. The second, before the Restoration merely a captain, was appointed to command a legion on the return from Ghent; then, thanks to 1815’s confusion, he passed into the bodyguard, returned to a line regiment, and after the affair of the Trocadero became a lieutenant-general with a commission in the Guards. The youngest, appointed sous-préfet, soon became a legal official and director of a municipal board in Paris, secure from legislative changes. These bounties, bestowed without parade, fell unperceived. Though father and sons each had sinecures enough to enjoy incomes almost equal to a chief of department, their political good fortune excited no envy. In those early days of the constitutional system, few had precise ideas of the civil service domain, where astute favorites found equivalents for demolished abbeys. Monsieur le Comte de Fontaine, who had boasted he had not read the Charter and showed indignation at courtiers’ greed, had proved to his master that he understood, as the King did, the spirit and resources of representative government. Still, despite established careers and pecuniary advantages from four official appointments, Monsieur de Fontaine headed too large a family to re-establish his fortune rapidly.

His three sons were rich in prospects, favor, and talent; but he had three daughters and feared wearing out the monarch’s benevolence. He mentioned them one by one. The King, having taste, did not leave his work incomplete. The marriage of the eldest with a Receiver-General, Planat de Baudry, was arranged by a royal speech that cost nothing and was worth millions. One evening, when the Sovereign was out of spirits, he smiled on hearing of another Demoiselle de Fontaine, for whom he found a husband in a young magistrate of inferior birth but wealthy, whom he created Baron. When the next year the Vendeen spoke of Mademoiselle Emilie de Fontaine, the King replied in thin sharp tones, “Amicus Plato sed magis amica Natio.” A few days later he treated his “friend Fontaine” to a harmless quatrain—an epigram—making fun of these three daughters introduced as a trinity. Report said the monarch found the point in the Unity of the three Divine Persons.

“If your Majesty would only condescend to turn the epigram into an epithalamium?” said the Count, trying to turn the sally to good account.

“Though I see the rhyme of it, I fail to see the reason,” retorted the King, who did not relish any pleasantry, however mild, on the subject of his poetry.

From that day his intercourse with Monsieur de Fontaine showed less amenity. Kings enjoy contradicting more than people think. Like most youngest children, Emilie de Fontaine was a Benjamin spoiled by almost everybody. The King’s coolness therefore caused the Count regret, because no marriage was ever so difficult to arrange as that of this darling daughter. To understand the obstacles we must visit the fine residence where the official was housed at the nation’s expense. Emilie had spent childhood on the family estate, enjoying the abundance that suffices for youthful joys; her lightest wishes had been law to her sisters, brothers, mother, and even her father. All her relations doted on her. Having come to discretion just when her family was loaded with fortune’s favors, the enchantment continued. The luxury of Paris seemed natural to her. As in childhood she had never been thwarted, so at fourteen she was still obeyed when she rushed into the whirl of fashion.

Accustomed by degrees to money, elegant dress, gilded drawing-rooms, and fine carriages, these pleasures became necessary to her as the compliments of flattery and the vanities of court life. Like most spoiled children, she tyrannized over those who loved her and kept blandishments for the indifferent. Her faults grew with her growth, and her parents were to gather bitter fruits of this education. At nineteen Emilie had not yet chosen among many suitors brought by her father’s politics. Though young, she asserted all the freedom of a married woman. Her beauty was so remarkable that to appear in a room was to be its queen; but like sovereigns she had no friends, though she drew everywhere that adulation which a finer nature might have used better. Not a man, not even an old man, could contradict a young girl whose lightest look could rekindle love in the coldest heart.

She had been carefully educated, painted pretty well, spoke Italian and English, and played the piano brilliantly; her voice, trained by the best masters, made her singing irresistibly charming. Clever and intimate with literature, she might have made folks believe that people of quality come into the world knowing everything. She could argue on Italian or Flemish painting, on the Middle Ages or the Renaissance; pronounce on books new or old, and expose defects with cruelly graceful wit. The simplest thing she said was accepted by an admiring crowd like a fez accepted by Turks. She dazzled the shallow; to deeper minds her tact revealed itself, and for them she gave so much charm that, under its cover, she escaped scrutiny. This charming veneer covered a careless heart; the common opinion that no one else dwelt in a sphere lofty enough to understand her merits, and a pride based on birth as well as beauty, governed her. In the absence of the overwhelming sentiment that sooner or later works havoc in a woman’s heart, she spent young ardor in an immoderate love of distinction, and expressed deepest contempt for persons of inferior birth. Supremely impertinent to newly-created nobility, she strove to have her parents recognized as equals by the most illustrious families of Saint-Germain.

These sentiments did not escape Monsieur de Fontaine, who, after his two elder girls were married, smarted under Emilie’s sarcasm. Logical readers may be surprised to see the old Royalist bestow his eldest daughter on a Receiver-General, and his second on a magistrate lately made a Baron. This change in a noble on the verge of sixty—an age when men rarely renounce convictions—was due not merely to residence in modern Babylon, where country folks get their corners rubbed down; the Count’s new political conscience resulted also from the King’s advice and friendship. The philosophical prince had taken pleasure in converting the Vendeen to ideas required by the advance of the nineteenth century and the new aspect of monarchy. Louis XVIII aimed at fusing parties as Napoleon had fused things and men; the legitimate King, not less clever perhaps than his rival, acted contrariwise. The last head of the Bourbons was eager to satisfy the third estate and creations of the Empire by curbing the clergy, as Napoleon had courted old nobility or endowed the Church. In the secret of these royal projects the Privy Councillor became one of the most prudent and influential leaders of the moderate party desiring fusion in the nation’s interests. He preached the expensive doctrines of constitutional government and lent his weight to the political see-saw enabling his master to rule France amid storms. Perhaps Monsieur de Fontaine hoped that a sudden gust of legislation might carry him to the peerage. One rigid principle he embraced was to recognize no nobility in France but that of the peerage—the only families that might enjoy privileges.

“As far from Lafayette’s party as from La Bourdonnaye’s,” he engaged in general reconciliation, which promised a new era and fortunes. He sought to convince families frequenting his drawing-room how few openings would henceforth be offered by civil or military careers, urging mothers to give boys a start in independent and industrial professions, explaining military posts and high government appointments must at last pertain, in constitutional order, to younger sons of peers. According to him, the people had conquered enough of practical government by an elective assembly and appointments to law-offices and the exchequer, which, he said, would always be the natural right of distinguished men of the third estate.

These new notions and the prudent matches they led to met strong resistance at home. The Comtesse de Fontaine remained faithful to ancient beliefs no woman could disown who, through her mother, belonged to the Rohans. Though she had opposed the happiness and fortune awaiting her two eldest girls, she yielded to private considerations husband and wife confide when their heads rest on the same pillow. Monsieur de Fontaine calmly showed by exact arithmetic that residence in Paris, entertaining, the magnificence of the house replacing privations shared in La Vendée, and the expenses of their sons swallowed most of their salary income. They must therefore seize opportunities to settle their girls with wealth. Would they not one day enjoy sixty–eighty–a hundred thousand francs a year? Such matches were rare for girls without portions. It was time to economize, add to the estate of Fontaine, and re-establish territorial fortune. The Countess yielded to such cogent arguments, though perhaps with less grace; but she declared Emilie must marry to satisfy the pride she had helped foster.

Thus events that should have brought joy introduced a leaven of discord. The Receiver-General and the young lawyer were objects of ceremonious formality the Countess and Emilie contrived. This etiquette soon found broader scope; the Lieutenant-General de Fontaine married Mademoiselle Mongenod, daughter of a rich banker; the President found a wife in a young lady whose father, twice or thrice a millionaire, had traded in salt; and the third brother, faithful to plebeian doctrines, married Mademoiselle Grossetete, only daughter of the Receiver-General at Bourges. The sisters-in-law and brothers-in-law found political salons and Faubourg Saint-Germain drawing-rooms so charming and advantageous that they formed a little court around overbearing Emilie. This treaty between interest and pride was not so cemented but that the young despot sometimes caused revolts in her realm. Scenes that the highest circles would not have disowned kept up a sarcastic temper among family members; this, without seriously diminishing public regard, sometimes turned private sentiments far from charitable. Thus the Lieutenant-General’s wife, a Baronne, thought herself as noble as a Kergarouet, and imagined her good hundred thousand francs a year gave her the right to be as impertinent as Emilie, whom she sometimes wished happily married, announcing a peer’s daughter had married Monsieur So-and-So with no title. The Vicomtesse de Fontaine amused herself by eclipsing Emilie in taste, magnificence of dress, furniture, and carriages. The satirical spirit in which brothers and sisters received Emilie’s claims roused her to wrath that a hailstorm of sharp sayings could hardly mitigate. So when the head of the family felt a slight chill in the King’s tacit and precarious friendship, he trembled, all the more since, as a result of her sisters’ mockery, his favorite had never looked so high.

In the midst of these circumstances, and when this petty domestic warfare had become serious, the monarch, whose favor Monsieur de Fontaine still hoped to regain, was attacked by the malady of which he was to die. The great political chief, who steered his bark amid tempests, soon succumbed. Certain then of favors to come, the Comte de Fontaine made every effort to gather the elite of marrying men about his youngest daughter. Those who have tried to settle a haughty and capricious girl will understand the trouble taken by the unlucky father. Such an affair, carried out to Emilie’s liking, would worthily crown the decade the Count had spent in Paris. From the way his family claimed salaries under every department, it might be compared with the House of Austria, which by intermarriage threatens to pervade Europe. The old Vendeen was not discouraged in bringing forward suitors, so much had he the daughter’s happiness at heart, but nothing could be more absurd than how the impertinent young thing pronounced verdicts on her adorers. It might have been supposed that, like a princess in the Arabian Nights, Emilie was rich and beautiful enough to choose from all princes. Her objections were each more preposterous than the last: one had too thick knees and was bow-legged, another was short-sighted, this one’s name was Durand, that one limped, and almost all were too fat. Livelier, more attractive, and gayer than ever after dismissing two or three suitors, she rushed into the winter festivities and balls, where her keen eyes criticized the celebrities of the day and delighted in encouraging proposals she invariably rejected.

Nature had given her all the advantages needed for the part of Célimène. Tall and slight, Émilie de Fontaine could assume a dignified or frolicsome mien at will. Her somewhat long neck allowed her to affect graceful attitudes of scorn and impertinence. She had cultivated many turns of the head and feminine gestures that emphasize a smile. Fine black hair and strongly arched eyebrows lent her countenance an air of pride, which her coquettish instincts and her mirror had taught her to temper with terror by a stare, or gentleness by the softness of her gaze, the curve of her lips, and the coldness or sweetness of her smile. When Émilie meant to conquer a heart, her clear voice had melody; but she could make it sharp and decisive when she wished to silence an indiscreet tongue. Her colorless face and alabaster brow were like a lake that, rippled by a breeze, soon regained serenity. More than one young man, a victim to her scorn, accused her of acting a part; she justified herself by inspiring desire and then subjecting admirers to her most contemptuous caprice. Among fashionable young women, none knew better how to assume reserve when a man of talent was introduced, or to display that insulting politeness which treats an equal as an inferior. Wherever she went she seemed to accept homage rather than compliments; even in a princess her airs would have transformed the chair into an imperial throne.

Monsieur de Fontaine discovered too late how utterly his daughter’s education had been ruined by the tender devotion of the whole family. Early admiration had added to Émilie’s pride and self-confidence. Universal subservience developed the selfishness natural to spoiled children, who, like kings, make playthings of everything that comes to hand. Her graces and talents hid these faults from most, but nothing escaped a good father’s eye, and Monsieur de Fontaine often tried to explain to her the more important pages of life. Vainly. He had to lament his daughter’s capricious indocility and ironical shrewdness too often to persevere in correcting an ill-disposed nature. He contented himself with occasional gentle advice; he had the sorrow of watching his tenderest words slide from her heart as if it were marble. A father’s eyes are slow to be unsealed, and it needed experience before the old Royalist perceived that his daughter’s rare caresses were bestowed with an air of condescension. She was like a child who seems to say to her mother, “Make haste to kiss me, that I may go to play.” In short, Émilie vouchsafed to be fond of her parents. But by sudden whims she often kept aloof and scarcely appeared; she complained of having to share her parents’ hearts with too many people and was jealous even of her brothers and sisters. Then, after creating a desert about her, she accused all nature of her unreal solitude and wilful griefs. Strong in the experience of her twenty years, she blamed fate, not knowing that the mainspring of happiness is in ourselves. She would have fled the planet to escape a marriage like her sisters’, yet was horribly jealous at seeing them married, rich, and happy. Sometimes her mother suspected a touch of madness.

Such aberrations are inexplicable; nothing is commoner than this unconfessed pride in young girls of high families and great beauty. They imagine that mothers, now forty or fifty, cannot sympathize with their young souls and will dress them to eclipse them. Hence secret tears and dumb revolt against supposed tyranny. In the midst of these woes they compose schemes of life and cast their horoscope, taking dreams for reality. In long meditations they decide to give their heart and hand to none but a man with particular qualifications, painting a model to which any future lover must correspond. After some years, by observing the world, those brilliant hues fade, and one day they are astonished to find themselves happy without the nuptial poetry of their day-dreams. It was from such poetry that Mademoiselle Émilie de Fontaine had drawn a programme for suitors. Hence her disdain and sarcasm.

“Though young and of an ancient family, he must be a peer of France,” she told herself. “I could not bear not to see my coat-of-arms on the panels of my carriage, not to drive like princes down the Champs-Élysées on Longchamps days. Besides, my father says that will someday be the highest dignity in France. He must be a soldier—but I reserve the right to make him retire; and he must bear an Order, that the sentries may present arms to us.”

These rare qualifications would count for nothing if he had not amiable temper, a fine figure, intelligence, and, above all, if he were slender. To be lean, a grace which is fugitive under a representative government, was indispensable. Émilie had an ideal standard. A young man who did not fulfil the requisite conditions did not even get a second look.

“Good Heavens! see how fat he is!” was her utmost expression of contempt.

To her, people of respectable corpulence were incapable of sentiment, bad husbands, and unfit for civilized society. Fat seemed a misfortune in a woman; in a man, a crime. These paradoxes were amusing thanks to her lively rhetoric. The Count felt, nevertheless, that his daughter’s affections would become a subject of ridicule, and feared lest her eccentric notions should deviate into bad style. He trembled to think the pitiless world might already be laughing at a young woman who remained so long on the stage without concluding the drama she was playing. More than one suitor, disgusted by refusal, seemed to wait for the slightest misfortune to take revenge. The indifferent were beginning to weary; admiration is exhausting. The old Vendéen knew better than any one that while there is an art in choosing the right moment to appear on the world’s boards, it is harder to quit them in time. So during the first winter after Charles X.’s accession he redoubled efforts, seconded by his sons and sons-in-law, to assemble in his official rooms the best matches Paris and the departments could offer. The splendour of his entertainments and dinners, fragrant with truffles, rivalled the banquets by which ministers secured votes.

The Honorable Deputy was pointed at as an influential corrupter of legislative honesty. A whimsical result: his efforts to get his daughter married secured him splendid popularity. Certain mocking Liberals accused him of selling truffles twice over, but the Poitevin gentleman was so noble and honorable that he was not the subject of those epigrams hurled at the centre votes, the Ministers, the Directors-General, and the official supporters of the Villele Ministry.

After this campaign, during which the Count had brought out all his forces, he believed this time the procession of suitors would not be a dissolving view in his daughter’s eyes. He felt an inward satisfaction at having fulfilled his duty. Having left no stone unturned, he hoped among so many hearts laid at Émilie’s feet there might be one to which her caprice would give a preference. Incapable of repeating such effort, tired of his daughter’s conduct, one morning toward the end of Lent, when the Chamber’s business did not demand his vote, he determined to ask her views. While his valet was powdering his bald head, the Count told him to desire the haughty damsel to appear.

“Joseph,” he added while dressing, “air the room, draw back the curtains, put those chairs straight, lay the rug neat. Dust and open the window.”

The Count multiplied orders. The old servant, understanding his master’s intention, tidied the study, gave order to the chaos of bills, letter-boxes, books, and furniture, and set forward things to please the eye. He admired his handiwork and went.

The anxious sinecure-holder did not share Joseph’s favorable opinion. Before seating himself in his deep chair, which screened him from draughts, he looked round doubtfully, straightened his dressing-gown, wiped his nose, made the fire, pulled up his slippers, restored his little queue of hair, swept the hearth ashes, and examined the room once more, hoping nothing could give occasion to the saucy remarks with which his daughter answered his advice. He was anxious not to compromise his dignity as a father. He took a pinch of snuff, cleared his throat, and then heard his daughter’s light step; she came humming an air from Il Barbiere.

“Good-morning, papa. What do you want with me so early?” she sang, kissing the Count, but with the light carelessness of a mistress confident of pleasing.

“My dear child,” said Monsieur de Fontaine gravely, “I sent for you to talk seriously about your future. You must make such a choice of husband as may secure durable happiness—”

“My good father,” Émilie interrupted in a coaxing tone, “it strikes me the armistice on which we agreed as to my suitors is not yet expired.”

“Émilie, we must to-day forbear from jesting on so important a matter. For some time past the efforts of those who most truly love you have been concentrated on settling you suitably; you would be guilty of ingratitude in meeting with levity proofs of kindness lavished on you.”

Émilie brought forward an armchair, set it by the fireplace to sit facing him, and settled in mocking solemnity, crossing her arms over the frills of a pelerine à la neige, ruthlessly crushing its tulle. After a laughing side glance at her father’s troubled face, she broke silence.

“I never heard you say, my dear father, that the Government issued its instructions in its dressing-gown. However,” she smiled, “that does not matter; the mob are probably not particular. Now, what are your proposals for legislation, and your official introductions?”

“I shall not always be able to make them, headstrong girl!—Listen, Émilie. I intend no longer to compromise my reputation, which is part of my children’s fortune, by recruiting the regiment of dancers which, spring after spring, you put to rout. You have already caused dangerous misunderstandings with certain families. I hope to make you perceive the difficulties of your position and ours. You are twenty-two, my dear, and you should have been married nearly three years. Your brothers and two sisters are richly and happily provided for. But the expenses occasioned by these marriages and your mother’s required style of housekeeping have so reduced our income that I can hardly promise you a hundred thousand francs as a marriage portion. From this day I shall think only of providing for your mother. If I were taken from my family, Madame de Fontaine could not be left at anybody’s mercy. You see, my child, that the amount of your fortune bears no relation to your notions of grandeur. Even that would be a sacrifice I have not hitherto made for either child; but they have agreed not to expect future compensation for an over-favored sister.”

“In their position!” said Émilie, tossing her head.

“My dear, do not depreciate those who love you. Only the poor are generous as a rule; the rich have always reasons for not handing over twenty thousand francs to a relation. Come, do not pout, let us talk rationally.—Among the young marrying men have you noticed Monsieur de Manerville?”

“Oh, he minces his words—he says Zules instead of Jules; he is always looking at his feet because he thinks them small, and he gazes at himself in the glass! Besides, he is fair. I don’t like fair men.”

“Well, then, Monsieur de Beaudenord?”

“He is not noble! he is ill made and stout. He is dark, it is true.—If the two gentlemen could agree to combine their fortunes, and the first would give his name and figure to the second, who should keep his dark hair, then—perhaps—”

“What can you say against Monsieur de Rastignac?”

“Madame de Nucingen has made a banker of him,” she said meaningly.

“And our cousin, the Vicomte de Portenduère?”

“A mere boy, who dances badly; besides, he has no fortune. After all, papa, none of these people have titles. I want, at least, to be a countess like my mother.”

“Have you seen no one, then, this winter—”

“No, papa.”

“What then do you want?”

“The son of a peer of France.”

“My dear girl, you are mad!” said Monsieur de Fontaine, rising. But he suddenly lifted his eyes to heaven, and seemed to find resignation in a religious thought; then, with pity at his daughter who was moved, he took her hand and said: “God is my witness, poor mistaken child, I have conscientiously discharged my duty to you as a father—conscientiously, do I say? Most lovingly, my Émilie. This winter I have brought before you more than one good man, whose character, habits, and temper were known, and all seemed worthy of you. My task is done. From this day thou art the arbiter of thy fate, and I consider myself both happy and unhappy to be relieved of the heaviest of paternal functions. I know not whether you will long hear a voice which to you has never been stern; but remember conjugal happiness rests not so much on brilliant qualities and ample fortune as on reciprocal esteem. This happiness is modest, devoid of show. So now, my dear, my consent is given beforehand, whoever the son-in-law you introduce; but if you are unhappy, remember you will have no right to accuse your father. I will help you, but your choice must be serious and final. I will never again compromise the respect due to my white hairs.”

The affection thus expressed touched Émilie deeply, but she concealed her emotion, seated herself on her father’s knees—he had drooped into his chair again—caressed him fondly, and coaxed him until his brow cleared. As soon as she thought him over his agitation, she said gently: “I thank you for your graceful attention, dear father. You had your room set in order to receive your beloved daughter. You perhaps did not know you would find her so foolish and headstrong. But, papa, is it so difficult to get married to a peer of France? You declared they were manufactured by dozens. At least you will not refuse to advise me.”

“No, my poor child, no,—and I may have occasion more than once to cry ‘Beware!’ Remember that making peers is so recent that they have no great fortunes. Those who are rich look to becoming richer. The wealthiest of our peerage has not half the income of the least rich lord in the English Upper Chamber. Thus French peers are on the lookout for great heiresses. The necessity to marry for money will exist for at least two centuries.

“Pending such a fortunate accident as you long for—this fastidiousness may cost you the best years of your life—your attractions might work a miracle, for men often marry for love now. When experience lurks behind so sweet a face it may achieve wonders. In the first place, have you not the gift of recognizing virtue in the size and shape of a man’s body? This is no small matter! To so wise a young person as you are, I need not enlarge on the difficulties. I am sure you would never attribute good sense to a stranger because he had a handsome face or all the virtues because he had a fine figure. And I agree that sons of peers ought to have an air peculiar to themselves. Though no external sign now stamps a man of rank, those young men may have the indefinable something to reveal it. You have your heart well in hand, like a good horseman sure his steed cannot bolt. Luck be with you, my dear!”

“You are making game of me, papa. Well, I assure you I would rather die in Mlle de Condé’s convent than not be the wife of a peer of France.”

She slipped from his knees proud to be mistress of her fate, and went off singing an air from Il Matrimonio Segreto.

That day the family were keeping an anniversary fête. At dessert Madame Planat, the Receiver-General’s wife, spoke with enthusiasm of a young American of immense fortune who had passionately proposed to her sister.

“A banker, I think,” observed Émilie carelessly. “I do not like money dealers.”

“But, Émilie,” said the Baron de Villaine, husband of the Count’s second daughter, “you do not like lawyers either; so if you refuse men of wealth without titles, I do not see where you will find a husband.”

“Especially, Émilie, with your standard of slimness,” added the Lieutenant-General.

“I know what I want,” replied the young lady.

“My sister wants a fine name, a fine young man, prospects, and a hundred thousand francs a year,” said the Baronne de Fontaine. “Monsieur de Marsay, for instance.”

“I know, my dear,” retorted Émilie, “I do not mean to make such a foolish marriage as some I have seen. Moreover, to end these matrimonial discussions, I hereby declare I shall look on anyone who talks to me of marriage as an enemy to my peace.”

Her uncle, a vice-admiral of seventy whose fortune had risen by twenty thousand francs a year thanks to the Act of Indemnity, feeling privileged to say hard things to his grand-niece, exclaimed:

“Do not tease my poor little Émilie; don’t you see she is waiting till the Duc de Bordeaux comes of age!”

The pleasantry drew general laughter.

“Take care I don’t marry you, old fool!” replied Émilie, her last words drowned in the noise.

“My dear children,” said Madame de Fontaine to soften the saucy retort, “Émilie, like you, will take no advice but her mother’s.”

“Bless me! I shall take no advice but my own in a matter which concerns no one but myself,” said Mademoiselle de Fontaine distinctly.

All eyes turned to the head of the family. The venerable gentleman enjoyed much consideration; happier than many fathers, he was appreciated by his family for the solid qualities by which he had made their fortunes. Hence he was treated with the deep respect shown to living representatives of an ancient pedigree. Silence fell as guests looked from the spoilt girl’s proud pout to Monsieur and Madame de Fontaine’s severe faces.

“I have made my daughter Émilie mistress of her own fate,” the Count replied in a deep voice.

Relations and guests gazed with curiosity and pity. The words declared that fatherly affection was weary of contest with an incorrigible character. Sons-in-law muttered; brothers glanced at their wives with mocking smiles. From that moment every one ceased to take interest in the haughty girl’s prospects. Her old uncle alone ventured to stand on her tack and take broadsides without returning fire.

When fine weather came and the budget was voted, the whole family—an example of parliamentary families with footing in every department—flew away to Aulnay, Antony, and Châtenay. The Receiver-General had bought a country-house there for his wife. Though Émilie despised the commonalty, she was not so carried as to scorn the advantages of fortune acquired in a profession; so she accompanied her sister to the villa, less from affection than because fashion ordained leaving Paris in summer. The green seclusion of Sceaux answered perfectly the requirements of good style and an official position.

Since the fame of the “Bal de Sceaux” may be unknown beyond the Department of the Seine, a brief account is necessary. The environs of Sceaux have a reputation for charming scenery. Perhaps the fame owes partly to Parisians’ hunger for green; yet artists, foreigners, and many pretty women confirm it. In the midst of a garden stands a large rotunda open on all sides with a roof on elegant pillars sheltering a dancing-floor. The most stuck-up landowners sometimes make excursions there in dashing parties or elegant carriages, hoping to meet women of fashion or to be seen by them—and to gaze at peasant girls, as wily as judges. The rotunda’s ballroom is crowded with the town’s shop clerks, medical disciples, and other youths, and many bourgeois marriages have begun to the band’s sound. If that roof could speak, what love-stories it would tell!

This medley gave the Sceaux balls a spice of amusement beyond similar places near Paris; the rotunda and the beauty of its gardens were undeniable attractions. Émilie was the first to wish to play at being common folk at this rural entertainment and promised herself immense pleasure in mingling with the crowd. Everybody wondered, but is there not a keen pleasure to grand people in an incognito? Émilie amused herself by imagining the town-bred figures; she fancied leaving a bewitching glance on shopkeepers’ hearts, laughed at the damsels’ airs, and sharpened pencils for the scenes she would sketch in her satirical album. Sunday could not come soon enough.

The Villa Planat party set out on foot, so as not to betray the rank of those attending the ball. They dined early. May favored this aristocratic escapade with one of its finest evenings. Émilie was surprised to find quadrilles that seemed upper-class, with some young men who looked as though they had saved for a month to shine for a day; she perceived several couples whose hearty glee suggested nothing conjugal. She saw that pleasure in cotton was much like pleasure in satin; middle-class girls danced as well as ladies, sometimes better. Most women were simply and suitably dressed. Country folk kept politely apart. Émilie studied the mixture for satire but had not time for many remarks before she found a flower in this wide field—one figure whose splendor and coloring fascinated her.

Seated on a clumsy chair at the edge of the circular floor, she treated the living groups as a picture gallery, turning her eye-glass on people two yards away, remarking as if criticising a study. Her eye was caught by a figure placed as if to be painted, in the best light, out of proportion with the rest.

The stranger, alone and absorbed, leaned against a column; his arms were folded and his posture elegant and unaffected. He had half turned his head and bent it a little, but without pose. His gaze followed a dancer, betraying feeling. His slender, easy frame recalled noble proportions; fine black hair curled over a high forehead. Émilie observed that his linen was fine, his gloves fresh, his feet small and well shod in Irish kid boots. He had none of the trinkets of National Guard dandies. A black ribbon, to which an eye-glass was attached, hung over a fashionable waistcoat. Long, curled lashes shaded his eyes. Melancholy and passion expressed themselves in the face; the olive complexion and a mouth ready to smile added a pathetic grace. There was too much promise to say simply “What a handsome man!” One wanted to know him. Any clear-sighted observer would take him for a clever man attracted to this rural festivity by some motive.

All these observations cost Émilie only a minute, during which the gentleman became the object of her secret admiration. She did not yet think “He must be a peer!” but “Oh, if only he is noble—” Without finishing the thought, she rose and, followed by her brother the General, made her way toward the column, feigning to watch the quadrille, but by a woman’s stratagem she did not lose a gesture. The stranger politely moved to make way and leaned against another pillar. Émilie, nettled by his politeness as she might by impertinence, began talking to her brother louder than taste enjoined; she tossed her head, gesticulated, and laughed for no reason, less to amuse him than to attract the stranger. None of her arts succeeded. She followed the direction of his eyes and discovered the cause of his indifference.

In the quadrille, a pale girl was dancing whose face recalled Girodet’s divinities in his Ossian composition. Émilie fancied she recognized her as a distinguished milady living on a neighbouring estate. Her partner was a lad of fifteen, with red hands, in nankeen trousers and a blue coat. His appearance showed the dancer’s love of dancing made her easy about partners. Her movements did not betray fragility, but a faint flush tinged her cheeks. Émilie went nearer to examine the girl when she returned to her place. The stranger went up to the pretty dancer and, leaning over, said in a gentle but commanding tone:

“Clara, my child, do not dance any more.”

Clara pouted, bowed her head, and finally smiled. When the dance was over, the young man wrapped her in a cashmere shawl with lover’s care and seated her sheltered from the wind. Soon Émilie saw them rise and walk round as if preparing to leave, and she followed under pretence of admiring the garden views. Her brother lent himself with malicious good-humour to her divagations. Émilie watched the couple get into an elegant tilbury, by which stood a groom in livery. As the young man from his high seat drew the reins, she caught a glance from his eye such as a man casts aimlessly at the crowd; then she enjoyed the feeble pleasure of seeing him turn to look at her. The young lady did the same. Was it jealousy?

“I imagine you have now seen enough of the garden,” said her brother. “We may go back to the dancing.”

“I am ready,” said she. “Do you think the girl can be a relation of Lady Dudley’s?”

“Lady Dudley may have some male relation staying with her,” said the Baron; “but a young girl—No!”

Next day Émilie expressed a wish to ride. She gradually accustomed her uncle and brothers to early rides, which she declared excellent for health. She fancied the environs of the hamlet where Lady Dudley was living. Despite her cavalry manoeuvres, she did not meet the stranger as soon as she hoped. She went several times to the Bal de Sceaux without seeing the Englishman who had fallen into her dreams. Though obstacles often quicken passion, there was a time when Émilie nearly gave up the search. She might have wandered long without meeting him; Clara was not English, and the man escorting her did not dwell in Châtenay.

One evening riding with her uncle, who during fine weather had a truce from gout, she met Lady Dudley in an open carriage with Monsieur Vandenesse. Émilie recognized them, and her suppositions were dissipated. Annoyed, she touched up her horse so suddenly her uncle had difficulty following.

“I am too old, it seems, to understand youthful spirits,” said the old sailor, cantering. “What ails my niece? Now she walks at a foot-pace like a gendarme. One might fancy she wanted to outflank that worthy man who looks to me like an author with a notebook. My word, I am a great simpleton! Is not that the very young man we seek?”

At this thought the Comte de Kergarouet moderated his horse to follow without noise. He had played many pranks in earlier years when gallantry was honoured and guessed at once that by chance Émilie had met the Unknown of Sceaux. Despite age, the Count recognized signs of extreme agitation in his niece. “Ay, that’s it,” he thought. “She is following him as a pirate follows a merchantman. When she loses sight of him she will be in despair at not knowing whether he is a marquis or a shopkeeper. These young heads need an old fogy like me...”

He suddenly spurred so as to come between her and the young man, forcing the latter onto the grassy bank. Drawing up, the Count exclaimed:

“Couldn’t you get out of the way?”

“I beg your pardon, monsieur. But I did not know it lay with me to apologize because you almost rode me down.”

“There, enough of that, my good fellow!” the sailor replied harshly, touching the young man’s shoulder. “A liberal citizen is a reasoner; every reasoner should be prudent.”

The young man went up the bankside, crossed his arms, and said excitedly, “I cannot suppose, monsieur, as I look at your white hairs, that you still amuse yourself by provoking duels—”

“White hairs!” cried the sailor. “You lie in your throat. They are only gray.”

The quarrel quickly became fierce; the younger man forgot moderation. As the Comte de Kergarouet saw his niece returning in uneasiness, he told the antagonist his name and bade him keep silence before the young lady. The stranger could not help smiling as he gave a visiting card to the old man, saying he lived at a country-house at Chevreuse; then he hurried away.

“You very nearly damaged that poor young counter-jumper, my dear,” the Count said to Émilie. “Do you not know how to hold your horse? You leave me to compromise my dignity to screen your folly; whereas if you had stopped, one of your looks or pretty speeches—one of those you can make so prettily when not pert—would have set everything right.”

“But, my dear uncle, it was your horse, not mine, which caused the accident. I think you can no longer ride; you are not so good as last year. Instead of talking nonsense—”

“Nonsense, by Gad! Is it nothing to be impertinent to your uncle?”

“Ought we not to go and inquire if the young man is hurt? He is limping—look!”

“No, he is running; I rated him soundly.”

“Oh, yes, uncle; I know you there!”

“Stop,” said the Count, pulling Émilie’s horse by the bridle, “I do not see the necessity of making advances to some shopkeeper who is only too lucky to have been thrown down by a charming young lady, or the commander of La Belle-Poule.”

“Why think him common, uncle? He seems to me to have fine manners.”

“Everyone has manners nowadays, my dear.”

“No, uncle, not everyone has the air and style of drawing-rooms. I’ll bet he is of noble birth.”

“You had not long to study him.”

“No, but it is not the first time I have seen him.”

“Nor is it the first time you have looked for him,” laughed the admiral.

Émilie coloured. Her uncle amused himself with her embarrassment; then he said: “Émilie, you know I love you as my own child, precisely because you are of high birth. Who could have believed sound principles would become so rare? Well, I will be your confidant. I see he is not indifferent to you. Hush! The family would laugh if we sailed under the wrong flag. We two will keep our secret, and I promise to bring him into the drawing-room.”

“When, uncle?”

“To-morrow.”

“But, my dear uncle, I am not committed to anything?”

“Nothing whatever, and you may bombard him, set fire to him, and leave him to founder if you choose. He won’t be the first, I fancy?”

“You ARE kind, uncle!”

At home the Count put on his glasses, quietly took the card from his pocket, and read: “Maximilien Longueville, Rue de Sentier.”

“Make yourself happy, my dear niece,” he said, “you may hook him with easy conscience; he belongs to a historical family, and if he is not a peer of France, he infallibly will be.”

“How do you know so much?”

“That is my secret.”

“Then do you know his name?”

The old man bowed his gray head like a gnarled oak with a few leaves fluttering, and Émilie began to try her coquettish arts. Long familiar with cajoling her uncle, she lavished childlike caresses and tender names; she even kissed him to induce him to divulge the secret. The old man, who often paid for these scenes with presents or opera boxes, amused himself with her entreaties. As he spun out the pleasure too long, Émilie grew angry, sulked, then, urged by curiosity, recovered. The admiral extracted a solemn promise that she would be gentler, less noisy, less wilful, spend less, and tell him everything. The treaty signed by a kiss on Émilie’s brow, he led her into a corner, drew her on his knee, held the card under his thumbs to hide it, then uncovered the letters and spelled Longueville; but he firmly refused to show anything more.

This incident intensified Émilie’s secret sentiment. During most of that night she evolved brilliant pictures from dreams about the imagined wealth gilding her married life. Thanks to chance, she could now see something very unlike a chimera at the fountain-head of her imaginary fortune. Ignorant, as young girls are, of the perils of love and marriage, she was passionately captivated by their externals. This birth of feeling—sweet but cruel—was like other youthful mistakes that exert fatal influence on the lives of inexperienced girls who trust only their judgment to provide for future happiness.

Next morning, before Emilie was awake, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse. In the courtyard of an elegant little villa he recognized the young man he had brusquely insulted the day before and approached him with the pressing politeness of men of the old court.

“Why, my dear sir, who could have guessed I should have a brush, at seventy-three, with the son, or the grandson, of one of my best friends. I am a vice-admiral, monsieur; is that not as much as to say I think no more of fighting a duel than of smoking a cigar? In my time no two young men were intimate till they had seen each other’s blood! But ‘sdeath, sir, last evening, sailor-like, I had taken a drop too much grog on board, and I ran you down. Shake hands; I would rather take a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than cause his family the smallest regret.”

Though cold, the young man could not resist the admiral’s frank cordiality and presently gave him his hand.

“You were going riding,” said the Count. “Do not let me detain you. But unless you have other plans, come to dinner at the Villa Planat. My nephew, the Comte de Fontaine, is a man you should know. I propose also to introduce you to five of the prettiest women in Paris. So, young man, your brow is clearing! I am fond of young people and like to see them happy. Their happiness reminds me of the good times of my youth, when adventures were not lacking, any more than duels.”

“But, monsieur, are we not in the right? The sixteenth century gave religious liberty to Europe, and the nineteenth will give political lib----”

“Oh, we will not talk politics. I am a perfect old woman—ultra, you see. But I do not hinder young men from being revolutionary, so long as they leave the King at liberty to disperse their assemblies.”

When they had gone into the woods the admiral pointed out a slender birch sapling, stopped his horse, took out one pistol, and lodged a shot in the tree, fifteen paces away.

“You see, my dear fellow, that I am not afraid of a duel,” he said with comical gravity.

“Nor am I,” the young man replied, cocking his pistol, aiming at the admiral’s hole, and sending his bullet close to it.

“That is what I call a well-educated man,” cried the admiral enthusiastically.

During the ride the old sailor questioned him on the trifles that constituted, in his code, an accomplished gentleman.

“Have you any debts?” he asked at last.

“No, monsieur.”

“What, you pay for all you have?”

“Punctually; otherwise we should lose our credit, and every sort of respect.”

“But at least you have more than one mistress? Ah, you blush, comrade! Well, manners have changed. All these notions of lawful order, Kantism, and liberty have spoilt the young men. You have no Guimard now, no Duthe, no creditors—and you know nothing of heraldry; why, my dear young friend, you are not fully fledged. The man who does not sow his wild oats in spring sows them in winter. If I have eighty thousand francs a year at seventy, it is because I ran through the capital at thirty. Oh! with my wife—in decency and honor. However, your imperfections won’t interfere with my introducing you at the Pavillon Planat. Remember, you promised to come.”

“What an odd little old man!” thought Longueville. “He is jolly and hale; but though he wishes to seem a good fellow, I will not trust him too far.”

Next day, about four, when the house party were in the drawing-rooms and billiard-room, a servant announced, “Monsieur DE Longueville.” Hearing the admiral’s protégé, everyone rushed in, curious about Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s face and this phoenix of men who had earned honorable mention to the detriment of rivals. Simple but elegant dress, an air of ease, polite manners, and a pleasant voice that struck a responsive chord won the family’s good-will. He seemed at home in the Receiver-General’s ostentation. His conversation was worldly but showed a brilliant education and thorough knowledge. He answered an old admiral’s trivial discussion on naval architecture so well that one lady said he must have passed through the École Polytechnique.

“And I think, madame,” he replied, “I may regard it as an honor to have got in.”

Despite urgent pressing, he politely but firmly refused to be kept to dinner, ending the ladies’ persisting entreaties by saying he was the Hippocrates of his young sister, whose delicate health required care.

“Monsieur is perhaps a medical man?” asked one of Emilie’s sisters-in-law ironically.

“Monsieur has left the École Polytechnique,” Mademoiselle de Fontaine kindly put in; her face had flushed as she learned the young lady at the ball was Monsieur Longueville’s sister.

“But, my dear, he may be a doctor and yet have been to the École Polytechnique—is it not so, monsieur?”

“There is nothing to prevent it, madame,” replied the young man.

Every eye was on Emilie, uneasy and curious. She breathed more freely when he added, with a smile, “I have not the honor of belonging to the medical profession; and I even gave up going into the Engineers in order to preserve my independence.”

“And you did well,” said the Count. “But how can you regard it as an honor to be a doctor?” added the Breton nobleman. “Ah, my young friend, such a man as you—”

“Monsieur le Comte, I respect every profession that has a useful purpose.”

“Well, in that we agree. You respect those professions, I imagine, as a young man respects a dowager.”

Monsieur Longueville’s visit was neither too long nor too short; he left when he saw he had pleased everybody and curiosity roused.

“He is a cunning rascal!” said the Count after seeing him to the door.

Mademoiselle de Fontaine had dressed with care to attract the stranger, but she was disappointed to find he did not bestow on her the attention she expected. Emilie usually displayed arts for newcomers—witty prattle, eloquent eyes and attitudes. Whether the stranger’s voice and manner charmed her or she was seriously in love, her demeanor lost affectation and became simple and natural—perhaps more beautiful. Some sisters and an old family friend suspected artifice, thinking she intended to delay revealing her merits to dazzle him. During dinner every one endowed Monsieur Longueville with some new quality; Emilie sat in silence until an uncle’s sarcastic remark roused her. She said epigrammatically that such heavenly perfection must cover some defect, and she would be cautious in judging a gifted man at first sight.

“Those who please everybody, please nobody,” she added; “and the worst of all faults is to have none.”

Like all girls in love, Emilie hoped to hide her feelings by misdirecting curious eyes; but by the end of a fortnight the whole family knew the secret. When Monsieur Longueville called a third time Emilie believed it was chiefly for her sake, and the discovery gave her intoxicating pleasure, though painful to her pride. Accustomed to be the centre, she recognized a force that attracted her outside herself and could not chase from her heart the fascinating image of the young man.

Her anxiety grew because two qualities of Longueville—unexpected modesty and discretion—frustrated curiosity. He never spoke of himself, his pursuits, or family. Emilie’s hints and conversational traps to extract facts were skillfully evaded. If she talked of painting he answered as a connoisseur; if she sat down to play he proved a good pianist; one evening he delighted the party by joining his voice to hers in a Cimarosa duet. But when they tried to learn whether he was a professional singer he baffled them, affording no clue to his social sphere. Bold as the old uncle was, Longueville slipped away cleverly, preserving the charm of mystery; curiosity never overstepped good breeding.

Emilie, distraught by this reserve, hoped to learn more from his sister. Aided by her uncle, she tried to bring Mademoiselle Clara Longueville into the scene. The Villa Planat party eagerly sought to meet the amiable young lady. An informal dance was proposed to coax a shy girl into talk.

A light of joy shone in Emilie’s soul; she found life delicious when linked to another than herself. Whether happiness made her better or she was simply occupied, she grew less caustic, more gentle and indulgent. The change enchanted the family. Perhaps her selfishness was turning to love. It delighted her to await her bashful admirer. Though they had not confessed passion, she knew she was loved and artfully led the stranger to unlock his varied knowledge. She perceived that she was studied, and sought to remedy the defects of her education—her first homage to love and a bitter reproach to herself. She desired to please and was enchanting; she loved and was idolized. Her family, trusting her pride would protect her, allowed enough freedom for first-love delights. More than once she and Longueville walked tête-à-tête in the garden avenues, admired sunsets, gathered daisies and sang impassioned duets—petty scenes that strengthened their feeling.

The day of the dance came. Clara and her brother, whom servants insisted on honoring with the noble DE, were the principal guests. For the first time Emilie felt pleasure in a young girl’s triumph and lavished on Clara sincere petting and attentions women often give only to excite jealousy. Emilie had an object—she wanted secrets. But Clara displayed greater tact than her brother, maintaining conversation detached from personal matters yet charming. Emilie, intending to make Clara talk, was judged instead; she betrayed herself in replies which Clara drew from her, while Clara’s modest and candid manner forbade suspicion. There was a moment when Emilie regretted an ill-judged sally she had made against the commonalty.

“Mademoiselle,” said the sweet child, “I have heard so much of you from Maximilien that I had the keenest desire to know you, out of affection for him; but is not a wish to know you a wish to love you?”

“My dear Clara, I feared I might have displeased you by speaking thus of people not of noble birth.”

“Oh, be quite easy. That sort of discussion is pointless these days. As for me, it does not affect me.”

The answer filled Emilie with joy; she explained it in the sense that harmonized with her wishes, danced with added spirit, and watched Longueville, whose figure and grace almost surpassed her ideal. She felt satisfaction in believing him well born; her black eyes sparkled and she danced with the pleasure of dancing before the being she loved. They understood each other as never before; more than once their fingertips thrilled in the dance.

Early autumn found them softened into the sweetest sentiment, strengthening it by thousand little incidents—love, in many respects, always the same. They studied each other through it all.

“Well, well; a flirtation never turned so quickly into a love match,” said the old uncle, who watched them like a naturalist at his microscope.

The remark alarmed Monsieur and Madame Fontaine. The old Vendeen had ceased to be indifferent to his daughter’s prospects. He went to Paris to seek information, and found none. Uneasy at this mystery, and not yet knowing what might come of an inquiry about the Longueville family, he warned his daughter to behave prudently. The admonition was received with mock submission and irony.

“At least, my dear Emilie, if you love him, do not own it to him.”

“My dear father, I certainly do love him; but I will await your permission before I tell him so.”

“But remember, Emilie, you know nothing of his family or pursuits.”

“I may be ignorant, but I am content to be. You wished to see me married; you left me at liberty to make my choice; my choice is irrevocably made—what more is needed?”

“It is needful to ascertain, my dear, whether the man of your choice is the son of a peer of France,” the old man retorted sarcastically.

Emilie was silent a moment, then anxiously asked, “Are not the Longuevilles—?”

“They became extinct in the person of the old Duc de Rostein-Limbourg, who perished on the scaffold in 1793. He was the last representative of the last and younger branch.”

“But, papa, there are very good families descended from bastards. The history of France swarms with princes bearing the bar sinister.”

“Your ideas are much changed,” said the old man with a smile.

The next day was the last the Fontaines were to spend at the Pavillon Planat. Disturbed by her father’s warning, Emilie awaited with impatience the hour when Longueville usually came, hoping to wring an explanation from him. She walked across the shrubbery toward a lovers’ arbor, considering the best way to discover his origins without compromising herself. Hitherto no direct avowal had sanctioned the feelings between them. Like Maximilien, she had secretly enjoyed the sweetness of first love; both were proud and feared to confess love.

Maximilien Longueville, to whom Clara had communicated suspicions about Emilie, was led by passion and a wish to test the woman he would entrust his happiness to. He had perceived prejudices in Emilie that marred her nature; before attempting to counteract them, he wished to be sure she loved him. He persistently kept a silence belied by looks and actions.

Emilie’s self-respect, and the monstrous vanity founded on her birth and beauty, kept her from meeting a declaration halfway. Thus the lovers had instinctively understood the situation without explaining motives. Sometimes such vagueness pleased youthful minds; because each postponed speaking too long they seemed to play a cruel game of suspense. He tried to discover whether he was beloved by the effect any confession would cost; she hoped he would break his too respectful silence.

Seated on a rustic bench Emilie reflected on the enchanted three months. Her father’s suspicions struck the last chord she thought persuasive, yet she disposed of them by reflections natural to an inexperienced girl: she had seen nothing vulgar in Maximilien’s gestures or words; his manner revealed a man devoted to the nation’s highest interests. “Besides,” she thought, “an office clerk, a banker, or a merchant would not spare a whole season to pay addresses to me in woods and fields; wasting his time as freely as a nobleman with life before him.”

A slight rustling announced Maximilien had been watching her. “Do you know it is very wrong to take a young girl thus unawares?” she asked smiling.

“Especially when they are busy with their secrets,” he replied archly.

“Why should I not have my secrets? You certainly have yours.”

“Then you really were thinking of your secrets?”

“No, I was thinking of yours. My own, I know.”

“But perhaps my secrets are yours, and yours mine,” cried the young man, softly seizing Emilie’s hand and drawing it through his arm.

They walked beneath trees wrapped in the hues of the sinking sun. The young man’s eager action, and the beating of his heart against her arm, stirred her to a powerful emotion, intensified by the restraint upper-class girls live under. Never had Emilie and Maximilien allowed their eyes to say so much that they dared not speak. Carried away they forgot pride and suspicion and first expressed themselves by a pressure of hands.

After pacing a few steps, Emilie spoke, trembling. “Monsieur, I have a question to ask you,” she said. “But, remember, it is in a manner compulsory on me, from the singular position I am in with regard to my family.” Then, painfully, she added, “Are you of noble birth?”

“As soon as you answer sincerely a question I will put to you,” Longueville gravely replied, releasing her arm. “What is your object in questioning me as to my birth?”

She stood motionless and cold.

“Mademoiselle,” Maximilien went on, “let us go no further if we do not understand each other. I love you,” he said, in deep emotion. “Well, then,” he added as he heard her joyful exclamation, “why ask me if I am of noble birth?”

A voice within her answered: “Could he speak so if he were not?” She raised her head, found new life in his gaze, and held out her hand as to renew the alliance.

“You thought I cared very much for dignities?” she said archly.

“I have no titles to offer my wife,” he replied half-sportive, half-serious. “But if I choose one of high rank, and among women used to a wealthy home, I know what such a choice requires. Love gives everything,” he added lightly, “but only to lovers. Once married they need something more than the vault of heaven and the carpet of a meadow.”

“He is rich,” she reflected. “As to titles, perhaps he only wants to try me. He has been told I am mad about titles.” Then aloud, in a way to distract him, “I assure you, monsieur, I have had extravagant ideas about life; but now,” she added pointedly, “I know where true riches are to be found for a wife.”

“I must believe you speak from the depths of your heart,” he said gently. “But this winter, my dear Emilie, in less than two months perhaps, I may be proud of what I shall have to offer you if you care for the pleasures of wealth. This is the only secret I shall keep locked here,” and he laid his hand on his heart, “for on its success my happiness depends. I dare not say ours.”

“Yes, yes, ours!”

They exchanged sweet nothings and rejoined the company. Emilie had never found him more amiable or witty. They sang an Italian duet with great expression and were enthusiastically applauded. Their adieus were conventional, concealing happiness. The day bound Emilie more closely to the Stranger’s fate; his strength and dignity in that scene impressed her and gave her respect without which there is no true love.

Left alone with her father, the old man asked if she had learned anything of Longueville’s family and fortune.

“Yes, my dear father,” she replied, “and I am happier than I could have hoped. In short, Monsieur de Longueville is the only man I could ever marry.”

“Very well, Emilie,” said the Count, “then I know what remains for me to do.”

“Do you know of any impediment?” she asked, alarmed.

“My dear child, the young man is totally unknown to me; but unless he is not a man of honor, so long as you love him he is as dear to me as a son.”

“Not a man of honor!” exclaimed Emilie. “As to that I am easy. My uncle, who introduced him, will answer for him. Say, my dear uncle, has he been a filibuster, an outlaw, a pirate?”

“I knew I should find myself in this fix!” cried the admiral waking up. He looked round but his niece had vanished “like Saint-Elmo’s fires,” to use his phrase.

“Well, uncle,” Monsieur de Fontaine went on, “how could you hide from us all you knew? You must have seen how anxious we have been. Is Monsieur de Longueville a man of family?”

“I don’t know him from Adam or Eve,” said the Comte de Kergarouet. “Trusting to that crazy child’s tact, I got him here by my method. I know the boy shoots with a pistol to admiration, hunts well, plays billiards, chess, and backgammon; he handles foils, rides like the late Chevalier de Saint-Georges. He knows our vintages, is as good an arithmetician as Barème, draws, dances, and sings well. The devil’s in it! what more do you want? If that is not a perfect gentleman, find me a bourgeois who knows all this. Does he compromise his dignity by hanging about an office bowing before upstarts? He walks upright. He is a man. However, I found the card he gave me: Rue du Sentier, No. 5.”

“Palma, Werbrust & Co., wholesale dealers in muslins, calicoes, and printed cotton goods, live there. Longueville the deputy has an interest in their house. Longueville has but one son of thirty-two, not like our man, to whom he gave fifty thousand francs a year that he might marry a minister’s daughter; he wants to be made a peer. I never heard him mention this Maximilien. Has he a daughter? What is this girl Clara? Besides, any adventurer can call himself Longueville. But the house of Palma, Werbrust & Co. is half ruined by some speculation in Mexico or the Indies? I will clear this up.”

“You speak a soliloquy as if on the stage, and account me a cipher,” said the admiral. “Don’t you know if he is a gentleman, I have more than one bag in my hold that will stop any leak in his fortune?”

“As to that, if he is a son of Longueville’s, he will want nothing; but,” said Fontaine, shaking his head, “his father has not washed off the stains of his origin. Before the Revolution he was an attorney, and the DE he assumed since no more belongs to him than half his fortune.”

“Pooh! pooh! happy those whose fathers were hanged!” cried the admiral gaily.

Three or four days later, on a fine November morning, Emilie, wrapped in a new fur cape, went out with two sisters-in-law to a shop at the corner of the Rue de la Paix. The Baronne de Fontaine pointed out Maximilien Longueville seated behind the desk, paying out change to a workwoman. He held patterns denoting an honorable profession.

Emilie felt an icy shudder though no one perceived it. Thanks to good breeding she concealed her rage and answered, “I knew it,” with full intonation. She went straight to the desk. Longueville looked up, pocketed the patterns with calm, bowed to Emilie, and came forward, looking keenly.

“Mademoiselle,” he said to the shopgirl, who followed, “I will send to settle that account; my house deals in that way. But here,” he whispered as he handed her a thousand-franc note, “take this—it is between ourselves. You will forgive me, I trust, mademoiselle,” he added, turning to Emilie. “You will kindly excuse the tyranny of business matters.”

“Indeed, monsieur, it seems to me no concern of mine,” replied Emilie, with sarcastic indifference.

“Do you really mean it?” asked Maximilien in a broken voice.

Emilie turned her back with amazing insolence. After buying the cape and getting into the carriage Emilie cast one last glance at the shop where she saw Maximilien standing with folded arms, superior to the disaster. Their eyes met and flashed implacable looks; each hoped to wound the lover. In an instant they were as far apart as if in China and Greenland.

Vanity withered everything. Emilie, tortured by prejudice and narrow-mindedness, reaped anguish. Her face, freshly velvety, was streaked and blotched; her paleness sometimes looked green. Hiding despair, she laughed to point out ridiculous dresses and passersby, but her laughter was spasmodic. Deeper hurt came from their unspoken compassion than any satirical comments she might have revenged. She exhausted her wit in trying to engage them in conversation, expending fury in senseless paradoxes heaping insults on merchants.

At home she had a feverish attack which for a time was serious; by the end of a month physician and family restored her.

Everyone hoped the lesson would subdue Emilie’s nature; but she fell into old habits and plunged back into fashion, declaring there was no disgrace in making a mistake. If she, like her father, had a vote in the Chamber she would move for an edict branding merchants and calico dealers on the forehead down to the third generation. She wished none but nobles could wear the antique French costume. It was a misfortune France had no outward difference between merchant and peer. Such pleasantries poured out when any accident brought up the subject.

Yet those who loved Emilie saw through the banter a tinge of melancholy. It was clear Maximilien still reigned over her heart. Sometimes she was as gentle as during the brief summer of love; sometimes unendurable. Everyone excused her inequality of temper as rooted in secret suffering. The Comte de Kergarouet exerted influence by increased prodigality, a consolation that often affects Parisian girls.

The first ball at which Emilie appeared was at the Neapolitan ambassador’s. As she took her place she saw Maximilien a few yards away who nodded slightly to her partner.

“Is that young man a friend of yours?” she asked scornfully.

“Only my brother,” he replied.

Emilie started. “Ah!” he continued, “and he is the noblest soul living—”

“Do you know my name?” asked Emilie, interrupting eagerly.

“No, mademoiselle. It is a crime, I confess, not to remember a name on every lip—I have an excuse. I have just arrived from Germany. My ambassador, on leave, sent me this evening to take care of his amiable wife, whom you may see yonder.”

“A perfect tragic mask!” said Emilie after looking at the ambassadress.

“And yet that is her ballroom face!” the young man laughed. “I shall have to dance with her! So I thought I might have some compensation.” Emilie courtesied. “I was surprised to find my brother here. On arriving from Vienna I heard he was ill in bed; I counted on seeing him before this ball; but good policy allows family affection. The Padrona della case would not give me time to call on my poor Maximilien.”

“Then, monsieur, your brother is not, like you, in diplomatic employment.”

“No,” said the attache with a sigh, “the poor fellow sacrificed himself for me. He and my sister Clara renounced their share of my father’s fortune to make an eldest son of me. My father dreams of a peerage, like all who vote for the ministry. Indeed, it is promised him. After saving up a little capital my brother joined a banking firm, and I hear he has just effected a speculation in Brazil which may make him a millionaire. I am in the highest spirits at contributing by diplomatic connections to his success. What do you think of him?”

“Your brother’s face does not look to me like that of a man busied with money matters.”

The attache shot a scrutinizing glance at his partner’s calm face.

“What! can young ladies read thoughts of love behind the silent brow?”

“Your brother is in love, then?” she asked, curious.

“Yes; my sister Clara wrote he had fallen in love this summer with a very pretty girl; but I have had no further news. The poor boy used to get up at five to settle his business so he could be back by four in the country where the lady was. In fact, he ruined a thoroughbred I had just given him. Forgive my chatter, mademoiselle; I have just come from Germany. If I talk with lack of reserve, the fault is yours. Was it not you who pointed out my brother? When he is the theme I become inexhaustible. I should like to proclaim to the world how good and generous he is. He gave up no less than a hundred thousand francs a year, the income from the Longueville property.”

Emilie had these revelations partly due to her skill in questioning her confiding partner after discovering he was the brother of her scorned lover.

“And could you, without being grieved, see your brother selling muslin and calico?” asked Emilie at the end of the third figure.

“How do you know that?” he asked. “Thank God, though I pour out words, I have learned not to tell more than I intend, like other diplomatic apprentices.”

“You told me, I assure you.”

Monsieur de Longueville, surprised, glanced from brother to partner, guessed everything, clasped his hands, fixed his eyes on the ceiling, and began to laugh. “I am an idiot! You are the handsomest person here; my brother keeps stealing glances at you; he is dancing in spite of his illness, and you pretend not to see him. Make him happy,” he added, leading her back to her uncle. “I shall not be jealous, but I shall always shiver a little at calling you my sister—”

The lovers, however, remained inexorable to each other and to themselves. At about two in the morning refreshments were served in a long corridor arranged like a restaurant. By accident Emilie found herself next to the group where Maximilien sat. The lady speaking to him was a Neapolitan duchess with satin sheen to her skin. Longueville’s intimate terms with her stung Emilie because she had given him far more tenderness than before.

“Yes, monsieur, in my country true love can make every kind of sacrifice,” the Duchess simpered.

“You have more passion than Frenchwomen,” said Maximilien, whose burning gaze fell on Emilie. “They are all vanity.”

“Monsieur,” Emilie eagerly interposed, “is it not very wrong to calumniate your own country? Devotion is to be found in every nation.”

“Do you imagine, mademoiselle,” retorted the Italian with a sardonic smile, “that a Parisian would be capable of following her lover all over the world?”

“Oh, madame, let us understand. She would follow him to a desert and live in a tent but not to sit in a shop.”

A disdainful gesture completed her meaning. Thus, under the influence of her disastrous education, Emilie for the second time killed her budding happiness and destroyed its prospects. Maximilien’s apparent indifference and a woman’s smile had wrung from her one of those sarcasms whose treacherous zest let her astray.

“Mademoiselle,” said Longueville in a low voice as the ladies rose, “no one will ever more ardently desire your happiness than I; permit me to assure you of this, as I take leave of you. I am starting for Italy in a few days.”

“With a Duchess, no doubt?”

“No, but perhaps with a mortal blow.”

“Is not that pure fancy?” asked Emilie anxiously.

“No,” he replied. “There are wounds which never heal.”

“You are not to go,” she said imperiously, and smiled.

“I shall go,” he replied gravely.

“You will find me married on your return, I warn you,” she said coquettishly.

“I hope so.”

“Impertinent wretch!” she exclaimed. “How cruel a revenge!”

A fortnight later Maximilien set out with his sister Clara for Italy, leaving Emilie a prey to violent regret. The young Secretary took up his brother’s quarrel and avenged Emilie’s disdain by making known the lovers’ separation. He described the fair foe of the counting-house—the amazon who preached a crusade against bankers—and often made Excellencies smile. The Comte de Fontaine procured an appointment to Russia for Auguste Longueville to protect his daughter from ridicule.

Not long after, the Ministry raising a levy of peers gave Monsieur Guiraudin de Longueville a peerage, the title of Vicomte. Monsieur de Fontaine also obtained a peerage for fidelity in evil days and for his name.

About this time Emilie, now of age, reflected seriously; her tone and manners changed. She lavished affectionate attentions on her uncle: brought him his stick, rode in his carriage, read him La Quotidienne amid clouds of smoke he blew over her; she learned piquet to match him and listened without impatience to his naval narratives.

Though the admiral had said he knew his longitude and latitude too well to be captured by a young corvette, Paris drawing-rooms heard of Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s marriage to the Comte de Kergarouet. The young Countess gave splendid entertainments to drown thought, but luxury failed to disguise the void at the bottom of her whirlpool; beneath assumed gaiety her face expressed unspoken melancholy. She appeared full of care for the old husband, who at night would say, “I do not know myself. Was I to wait till seventy-two to embark as pilot on board the Belle Emilie after twenty years of matrimonial galleys?”

The Countess’s conduct was so strict the keenest critic could find no fault. Lookers-on thought the admiral had reserved the right of disposing his fortune to keep his wife more tightly in hand, an insult to both. Their conduct was delicately judicious: people could not decide whether the old Count regarded her as wife or daughter. He often said he had rescued his niece as a castaway after shipwreck and never taken mean advantage of hospitality. Though she aspired to reign in Paris and tried to keep pace with duchesses and marquises, she did not yield to the Vicomte de Portenduere, who made her his idol.

Two years after her marriage, in a Faubourg Saint-Germain drawing-room where she was admired for character worthy of the old school, Emilie heard the Vicomte de Longueville announced. Playing piquet with the Bishop of Persepolis, she turned and saw her former lover in youth’s freshness. His father’s death and that of his brother, killed in Saint Petersburg, had placed on Maximilien the hereditary plumes of the French peer’s hat. His fortune matched his learning and merits; the day before his eloquence had dazzled the Assembly. At that moment he stood before the Countess free and graced with all the advantages she had formerly required. Every mother with a daughter to marry made advances to a man so gifted; but Emilie knew better than anyone that the Vicomte de Longueville had the steadfast nature in which a wise woman sees a guarantee of happiness. She looked at the admiral, who, to use his phrase, seemed likely to hold his course for a long time yet, and cursed the follies of her youth.

At this moment Monsieur de Persepolis said with Episcopal grace: “Fair lady, you have thrown away the king of hearts—I have won. But do not regret your money. I keep it for my little seminaries.”
