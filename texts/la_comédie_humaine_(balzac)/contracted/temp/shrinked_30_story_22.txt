DEDICATION

Balzac dedicates the book to a Polish lady of contrasts—childlike in faith, aged by sorrow, inspiring in love—whose inward poetry he feels unworthy to match but attempts to echo.

CHAPTER I. THE CHALET

In October 1829 the notary Monsieur Simon Babylas Latournelle walks from Le Havre to Ingouville with his wife, son Exupère, and his head clerk Jean Butscha to visit the Mignons. Latournelle coaches Exupère in a ruse: after greeting Mademoiselle Modeste Mignon, Exupère will slip aside to tell Monsieur Dumay “the young man has come,” a signal meant to stir romantic interest. Exupère is bound for Paris to study law; his vain mother and solemn father—notable for green goggles—accompany him. Butscha, a hunchback who adores Modeste, completes the party.

Ingouville, a picturesque suburb where merchants escape the city, contains the Chalet, the Mignons’ cottage with a garden and cherished hot-house. Jean François Bernard Dumay, the family’s devoted cashier, holds the lease; the new proprietor, Monsieur Vilquin, resents this and offers money to vacate. Dumay, fiercely loyal, refuses—determined above all to protect Modeste from suitors.

CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE

The Latournelles find Gobenheim, a pale, serious banker, already visiting; his attentions are worldly rather than affectionate. The household is taut: Dumay prepares to confront any man who might compromise Modeste’s honor. Blind Madame Mignon remains the dignified center while Modeste, embroidering, becomes the focus of unspoken anxieties. Dumay’s vigilance promises an evening that will test Modeste’s feelings.

CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES

Once a soldier, Dumay became the Mignons’ devoted cashier after their misfortunes. His fidelity to the exiled Colonel Mignon never falters. Dumay’s childless wife treats Modeste as her own; their devotion shapes the household’s protective tone.

CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY

A business collapse and the Colonel’s exile to Siberia reduced the family to the Chalet. Dumay and Latournelle work to settle debts; blind Madame Mignon, weakened by grief, depends on them. Modeste’s innocence and growing beauty alarm Dumay, who enforces strict guardianship to preserve her reputation amid poverty.

CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED

Modeste’s attractiveness fuels speculation; the women watch for signs of love, fearing exploitation. Dumay vows no man will approach without his knowledge.

CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE

Shaped by books and her sister’s tragedy, Modeste lives in imagination and longs to be a poet’s muse. She writes to the poet Canalis, pouring out her heart and seeking connection.

CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL

Canalis reads Modeste’s letter with amusement and condescension, recognizing her innocence and wary of being misconstrued. His flattering but cautious reply warns against romanticizing the poet’s life. Disappointed yet determined, Modeste decides to press the correspondence, setting the stage for conflict between her hopes and social limits.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Monsieur, you are a great poet and an honest man. After your frankness to a young girl on the brink of despair, answer me truthfully: would you have replied the same had you known Mademoiselle O. d’Este possessed six million francs and sought a fool for a husband?

Answer frankly. I am wiser than my years; nothing candid can harm you. If you speak openly, I will reply to your first letter.

Having admired your talent, I must also praise your integrity. Your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

Ernest de La Brière read this and paced the boulevards, conscience and curiosity at war. Parisians might call it flirtation; a man of honor felt judged by Truth and Honor. Truth accused him of hypocrisy; Justice defended the girl’s revolt against mercenary marriage; Honor demanded sincerity. He delayed, then wrote:

Mademoiselle, you are not a D’Este. The name is a disguise. Do I owe truth to someone who lies about herself? Are you noble or of the people? Ethics are constant, but obligations differ by rank. A daughter of Este with six million may candidly confess a fondness for poetry; a girl of the people would be mocked. Which are you? Answer plainly, and I will reply to your question.

She answered with mischief:

To Monsieur de Canalis, you grow sensible, my dear poet. My father is a count; we have a cardinal among us, and I am the last of our name. We are equal to the Canalis. You will excuse my refusing to send our arms. Answer me as truthfully as I have answered you. I await your response, to know if I may sign myself, Your servant, O. d’Este M.

La Brière smiled; four years in Paris had instructed him in intrigue. He booked a seat to Havre, excused himself by duty, and obtained from the post-office director a note to ensure secrecy. He watched Francoise Cochet collect letters and followed her discreetly to Ingouville, where he glimpsed a girl at a chalet window. Struck by her beauty, he learned the estate belonged to Monsieur Vilquin, the richest merchant in Havre. The Vilquins were enormously wealthy; one daughter married M. Althor, and rumors linked other daughters to fashionable visitors and possible ducal suitors.

Having seen enough to dream, La Brière resumed the correspondence, though he regretted the reconnaissance. He wrote a letter shaped by conscience and passion; Modeste read it at her window on a bright summer day.

Mademoiselle—without hypocrisy: had I known your wealth I would have acted differently. Men often mistake means for happiness; money becomes a religion. Faced with such a prize, a young man may pursue it for social gain, flattering and flattering away esteem for you while gaining mine. Honesty compels me to say this.

Your letter forced deep reflection—on you and on myself. You stirred base thoughts, from which something generous emerged. I went to Havre, followed Francoise to Ingouville; you are as beautiful as a poet’s dream. I blush at such spying and stopped. You have altered my opinion: you are not merely a woman but a poetic Ideal. Exceptional souls may transcend the crowd’s laws. If your letter sprang from lofty reveries and the wish for friendship beyond ordinary relations, you are an exception.

Accept my thanks for forcing me to sound my heart. You corrected my false idea that marriage should be for fortune. I swore to make my own fortune and not choose a wife from cupidity. I reproach myself for my curiosity. You are not worth six million; such a fortune could not be hidden in Havre. I now fix my rule: seek a partner made by God; do not marry a dunce. If I were rich and you poor, I would lay my heart and fortune at your feet; you seem loyal and rich in soul.

Modeste felt delight at touching a noble soul, joy that worth might still meet genius, and triumph at following her instinct. She altered her precautions, having Francoise slip her letters into the post, and resolved to meet the postman herself.

La Brière received another letter five days later that revealed more than any analysis.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My friend, I call you that; you have delighted me. I would not change your image from your first letter—oh, may it not be the last! Who but a poet could so delicately excuse and understand a girl on the brink?

I admit with joy I am not the Vilquin nor the withered d’Herouville; my ancestors are respectable and my precautions stand—I will remain veiled. As to my person and belongings, be easy: I am at least as beautiful as the girl whose light you saw, and not poverty-stricken. I have seen the humiliating comedy of heiresses hunted; make no attempt to find me. I am free yet watched—by myself and by resolute people who would not hesitate to stab a man who threatened my retreat. I warn you only.

About your sermon: when I read your distrust I thought the Muses may have led you astray. You are safe with me: I love poetry but do not assume hypocritical airs. Should I ever bid you come, I promise no old maid or poor wretch will appear. I regret that you came to Havre; you lowered the charm of the secret meeting I dreamed—God alone knew the treasure I reserved for a man noble enough to come. You spoiled it, but I forgive you; Paris breeds a man in every poet.

Do you think me gullible? When your first letter reached me I did not toss it aside; my net was carefully cast by a fisherman expecting a miraculous catch. You have my approval in what you say about family. The man who pleases me will have my heart with my parents’ consent; I will neither grieve nor surprise them. I am guarded by devotion and disdain for calculating motives. I worship the ideal without being fanciful; but I know social truths.

For now we must be friends. Your person may be unknown, but your mind and heart please me; an infinity of thoughts in me needs a man of genius to share them. I do not want my poem wasted; I want it known to you as to God. A true comrade’s confidences are precious; accept these leaflets of a young girl’s thoughts. Later, if our souls match, some white-haired servant may guide you to whatever dwelling fate holds for us—but not now. Come to me when you are unhappy; I have balms for your ills. I am twenty but feel fifty; I have known through another the horrors and delights of love and have no illusions but a religion and beliefs. Whoever I marry—if I choose him—will find me faithful and constant. I will not follow my husband blindly; I will be the divinity of his hearth. But I must test and choose the man who will be life’s companion.

Your letter was precious. Yes, it held necessary truths for family life. Act as an honest man, think as a poet, love as women love—that is what I long for. Adieu, my friend. I am poor at present; that is why I hide. I have read your verses in the Revue with delight now that I know your inner loftiness. Will it grieve you that a young girl prays for you, that you are her solitary thought? Send me the counterpart of these pages; your confidences—if full and true—will make me happy.

O. d’Este M.

La Brière, surprised at his own absorption, wondered if this was love. She had painted him as a great poet; could he sustain the deception? The charm of the unseen—the obscurity that stirs fancy—became an obsession. He answered with a studied letter where passion, pride, and caution mingled.

Mademoiselle,—Is it loyal of you to enthrone yourself in a poor poet’s heart while reserving the right to abandon him if he disappoints? Your confidence leaves little of yourself but much charm. I offer humble remonstrances.

Do you believe letters—brief, momentary expressions—can replace knowledge gained by daily life? Man has two lives: the invisible life of the heart and the material life. Letters may suffice for spirit, but cannot reconcile both. A solitary, educated, chaste homage consoles literary men, and I thank you as one equal might thank another.

But what next? I am neither Byron nor a colossus. I might have vanity, discordant character, and ambition; I need a career. I have modest means and the exterior of an ordinary man. I can be neither martyr nor hero. Your project is perilous: if you make yourself the handmaid of a man of genius and worship him until death, you may live on his glory; if you attach that ideal to me now, my modest gifts may disappoint you. If we continue, I might love you wildly—but if I pleased you, the tale would likely end in the common fashion: marriage, house, children. Therefore, adieu.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My friend, your letter gives me both pain and pleasure; perhaps someday all will be pleasure. I want the soul’s answers you seek from God. Cannot the friendship of Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne be revived in us? Remember Sismondi’s household or the Marquis de Pescaire’s; can two hearts vibrate in unison across distance? Man is harp, musician, and listener. Will you find me jealous like ordinary women? I know you go into the world; I know your beauties and wits. Yet I seek a flower that blooms on Alpine peaks—men of genius—and I wish to cultivate it. I am no commonplacid creature; I would not belong to a ninny or merchant who could ruin us. My father adores my wishes and will not oppose them. If I please my poet and he pleases me, we will build love high and secure.

I am an eaglet; I want to be imprisoned by love as I am now by my father’s will. A young imagination locked in a tower longs to run in its neighbor’s park—your genius. Parents arrange marriages by appearances; a young girl is often denied choice. I want a long marriage of souls before any legal pledge. My mother followed instinct and beauty and prospered; I follow mine. I will not bind us prematurely to vulgar forms. If our correspondence reveals true sympathy, we may be spared common follies and reach graveside hand in hand. I await your orders, monseigneur, and am with all my heart,

Your handmaiden, O. d’Este M.

To Mademoiselle O. d’Este M.—You are a witch and I love you! Is that what you desire, most original girl? Perhaps you amuse provincial leisure with a folly to make a poet act rashly. If so, you have done wrong. Your letters have mischief. But I am no longer master of myself; my life depends on your answer. If an unbounded affection that disregards social conventions touches you—if you will let me seek you—say so. If your reply is favorable I change my life; I shall renounce the trivial pleasures we call happiness. For me, happiness is a fusion of feeling, a harmony of souls, an imprint of a noble ideal on daily life—a constancy of heart more precious than mere fidelity. We may call such devotion sacrifice, but is it not the dream of poets and maidens? I cannot speak more of myself—my past nor my filial affection, which you have altered. Say one word and I will love you until death. Either write no more or give me hope. Let me see you, or let me go. Must I bid you adieu? Will you permit me to sign myself,

Your Friend?

To Monsieur de Canalis,—What flattery! How quickly the grave man becomes Leander! To what should I attribute such change—my ink, your fancy, or the memory of the girl at the window? Your letter has given me elating pleasures beyond family affection. What are blood ties compared to those forged by mysterious sympathies? Bless you for the happiness. You explain social injustices: glory dazzles men; God gives love to women to soothe them. I have felt my mission and you confirm it.

Sometimes I wake in inexpressible sweetness; after your feverish letter I felt heavenly freshness, destined to die for one I love. Your poem “The Maiden’s Song” paints these moments; I think you worthy to be me. Your letter lets me read within you: I have guessed your struggles, curiosity, and plans, but do not yet know you well enough. Shall we make a compact? Write your life briefly—tell me what you do in Paris day by day, as to an old friend—and then I will see you. This is a great step.

This involves my life and my parents’—they must find a son in you. To what extent can superb spirits bend to family yokes and endure little miseries? You said Goethe and Byron were egoists; you erred. Great minds are slaves of ideas; their visible egoism often hides abnegation. A man who gives gifts to his epoch is not simply selfish. The poet’s life needs vast organization to bear pleasures and sorrows; consider Molière, whose private suffering darkens his comedy.

I place you among those whose generosity borders on the divine. Had I found self-interest where I now find noble flowers, I would have been anguished. I once met disappointment before sixteen; what would have become of me had I learned at twenty that fame is a lie? I should have surrendered to my father and become resigned to a dull life. But I have you; I believe in you. That belief straightens my thoughts and fancies. Sometimes I wish I were already in the middle of the book we are beginning; I feel persistence, strength to love, constancy tempered by reason, a heroism for duty.

If you could follow me to the exquisite retreat I fancy, if you knew my plans, you might not call me foolish. I wish to be an inexhaustible spring for the twenty years nature allows me to shine; to vary happiness, put intelligence into tenderness, and give piquancy to fidelity. I wish to be courageous for my friend, to abnegate for the nest like no bird can. Tell me, do you now think me to blame for my first letter? The mysterious wind of will drove me to you. In your letter I hold your cry: “God wills it.”

Ah, you will call me a chatterbox; the people around say I am taciturn.

O. d’Este M.

The renowned surgeon Desplein’s visit deeply moved Modeste; guests praised the Mignon family and the salon debated Desplein’s “genius.” The medical community admitted his skill, but Canalis dismissed it as mere application, while Modeste defended a broader idea of genius, citing practical innovators like Parmentier and Jacquart. The argument grew heated, revealing Canalis’s wounded vanity as he tried to dominate the discussion.

Modeste’s flirtation with Canalis provoked La Brière, who left believing he’d lost her; her bold challenge to her father surprised the adults and showed increasing independence. The next day she behaved more provocatively toward Canalis, heightening La Brière’s jealousy. Canalis replied with grand gestures, but Modeste, skeptical of his motives, asserted her independence and resisted mere social expectation.

With the grand hunt and the Duc d’Herouville’s arrival, Modeste’s feeling for La Brière deepened while her rapport with Canalis frayed. Her beauty and fortune drew attention, altering the suitors’ dynamics; the hunt exposed true natures. Ultimately Modeste adopts a more complex view of love and ambition, learning the compromises required to seek happiness within society’s constraints.
