The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbon cause during the La Vendée conflict. Despite noble lineage and past sacrifices, he suffered financial ruin after his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a noblewoman without fortune. When the second revolution occurred, he moved to Paris with his large family hoping for better prospects. Initially disillusioned, he nevertheless received unexpected honors—a military promotion and royal decorations—which rekindled hopes for favor.

He soon realized his sacrifices were largely unrecognized and felt marginalized as new aristocrats rose. Disheartened, he considered returning to his estate but was drawn back into royal service during the turmoil of 1815. His loyalty was rewarded when he became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, who valued his wit and political acumen. Consequently the Fontaines began to prosper: his sons obtained significant posts and his daughters made good marriages, albeit to men of lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest, was spoiled and the center of Parisian attention. Her beauty and charm made her a sought-after match, yet she remained indifferent, asserting superiority over suitors of lower birth. Despite her father’s efforts to arrange advantageous marriages, Emilie’s high standards and capriciousness provoked family conflict. The Count’s attempts to secure a suitable husband were repeatedly frustrated as she dismissed candidates for trivial flaws.

As the political landscape shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte intensified efforts to marry Emilie off, hoping to cement the family's status. But her unreasonable expectations and disdain complicated matters, producing a string of rejections that left her father increasingly desperate. Family dynamics grew strained: Emilie’s arrogance bred friction among her siblings and their spouses, who were themselves navigating the complexities of newfound social standing.

Through this turmoil the Comte remained hopeful that royal favor and a proper match for Emilie would validate his sacrifices and restore the family’s honor. Yet as winter’s social season brought more prospects, Emilie’s dismissive attitude continued to frustrate him, leaving the Fontaine family’s future uncertain.

Nature endowed Emilie de Fontaine with the qualities to embody Célimène. Tall and slender, she could assume dignity or playful coquetry at will. Her long neck expressed scorn and impertinence; fine black hair and arched brows gave an air of pride. Emilie had mastered feminine gestures that could convey contempt or charm. To win a heart she used a melodious voice; she could also use it sharply to silence indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion resembled a tranquil lake, stirred only by fleeting emotion. Many young men, scorned by her, accused her of merely playing a part; she answered that she inspired their desire and then subjected them to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine saw too late that adoration had spoiled his daughter. Praise had inflated her pride and self-confidence, fostering the selfishness of a pampered child. Her youthful charms masked these defects, but her father perceived them and tried to teach practical wisdom. His efforts met caprice and ironic shrewdness; his gentle advice seemed to glide off her heart like water off marble. He came to believe her affection was often condescending, like a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed fondness for her parents but frequently withdrew, jealous of the love they shared with others, including her siblings. She blamed solitude on fate, failing to see that happiness comes from within. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nonetheless felt pangs of envy at their contentment. Her unpredictable behavior led her mother to suspect she might be slightly mad.

Such pride is common among beautiful young women of wealth, who often imagine their mothers cannot understand youthful dreams and suspect maternal envy. This produces secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny. In these struggles they devise elaborate life plans, picturing a perfect partner who must meet exacting criteria. With experience many find happiness unexpectedly and learn that ideals may not align with reality.

Emilie’s terms for a suitor were strict: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and possess an Order so they could be treated with respect. He also had to be slender; she considered corpulence a flaw in men, believing only the slim could be romantic. Her disdain for those who failed her standards was clear, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that indecision would harm her prospects.

During the winter of Charles X’s reign, Monsieur de Fontaine intensified efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitors. His reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity grew, yet he remained earnest. After a season of matchmaking he felt hopeful, only to see Emilie’s whims and standards unyielding.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future. He arranged his study meticulously to present a dignified front. Emilie greeted him with a light-hearted kiss lacking warmth. He broached her marriage prospects and stressed the importance of a wise choice for her happiness.

Emilie interrupted, reminding him of their prior agreement about suitors. He urged her to take the matter seriously, noting the family’s efforts to secure a suitable match. She dismissed his concerns, mocking the idea of settling for anything less than a peer and finding fault with the candidates he proposed.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now lay with her. He warned that marital happiness depends on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or status. Emilie, moved by her father’s sincerity, feigned warmth to coax him, yet her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering she reiterated her refusal to consider marriage, insisting she would only wed a peer. Her uncle joked that she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to come of age, provoking laughter, but Emilie remained resolute and forbade further talk of marriage.

As summer neared the family retired to the country house. Emilie wished to attend the Bal de Sceaux, attracted by mingling with the crowd and relishing anonymity. The ball, known for its lively atmosphere, drew a mix of social classes.

There she was surprised to find upper-class attendees mingling with the rest. While observing the festivities she noticed a striking young man who stood apart, exuding elegance and dignity. His appearance and manner stirred her imagination and she felt instant attraction.

Determined to learn more, Emilie approached him, but he remained aloof. She tried to attract his attention with exaggerated gestures and laughter, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and found him watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara.

Intrigued, Emilie decided to follow the pair as they left the ball. The young man, attentive to Clara, nonetheless caught Emilie’s eye as they departed; she felt a mix of jealousy and curiosity, eager to discover his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie encountered Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and realized the young man she admired was not what she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to conceal her feelings but remained drawn to him.

Her uncle, noticing her agitation, speculated about her feelings for the young man. Emilie insisted he must be of noble birth; her uncle promised an introduction. This intensified Emilie’s interest and she began to fantasize about a future with him.

As days passed, Emilie’s infatuation grew, fueled by imagination and the romance of the unknown. She remained unaware of the complexities and challenges that love and marriage would bring, blinded by youthful ideals.

The next morning, before Emilie woke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse and, recognizing the young man he had insulted the day before, greeted him with the polished courtesy of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d meet the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice-admiral; dueling’s no matter to me. I had too much grog and ran you down. Shake hands; I’d sooner endure a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than bring his family regret.”

The young man, despite reserve, returned the Count’s cordiality and they rode together. The admiral joked about duels, shot a pistol into a birch to show he feared none, and praised the young man when he fired close to the mark. On the road he quizzed him on trifles that made a gentleman in his eyes—debts, pleasures, heraldry—teasing about mistresses and youthful extravagance. Longueville replied with composed manners: he paid punctually, lived independently, and showed the polish of a fine education. The Count declared he would introduce him to society at the Pavillon Planat.

At the Villa Planat, when a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” curiosity turned to attention. Longueville arrived simply but elegantly, at ease in the Receiver-General’s mansion, well-spoken and knowledgeable—even on naval architecture, a topic the admiral broached. He answered discreetly: his manners suggested training at the Ecole Polytechnique, and when pressed about his profession he said he had renounced the Engineers to preserve his independence. He declined dinner courteously, saying he was caregiver to his delicate sister.

The family was struck by him. He charmed without ostentation, and though he aroused Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s interest—Emilie had dressed to attract him—his measured attention left her both delighted and wounded. She usually displayed her charms for newcomers, but now seemed simpler, perhaps more natural; she spoke little, and when roused by her uncle she quipped that perfection often hid defects—“Those who please everybody please nobody”—a remark betraying caution and pride.

Over the next fortnight Longueville called repeatedly. Emilie, used to being the center of attention, found herself drawn out of pride as an external force attracted her. He was modest and discreet: he never spoke of his family and skillfully evaded her probes. He accepted all she offered—his knowledge of painting, his piano and song—without revealing his social standing. Once they sang a duet, and the party, intrigued, could not tell whether he was professional. His reserve tormented Emilie even as it deepened her interest.

The family wished to meet Clara Longueville, and an informal dance was proposed to welcome her. When Clara and her brother arrived, Emilie felt genuine pleasure for Clara and lavished her with attention, though tinged by envy: Clara matched ease with modesty and, while amiable, judged Emilie’s earlier disdain for commoners. Clara confessed she had come out of affection for her brother Maximilien, and her frank courtesy reassured Emilie. The exchange—Clara’s gentle rebuke of Emilie’s snobbery and Emilie’s relieved response—brought the two girls closer.

With autumn, the attachment between Emilie and Longueville quietly deepened. Their life together became a sequence of small intimacies: aimless conversations skirted deeper feeling, shared sunsets, daisy gatherings, and passionate duets where music voiced what words did not. They learned each other’s ways as lovers do, testing limits, revealing little and withholding more. Longueville’s silence remained both balm and barrier; Emilie cherished the attention and resented the mystery, yet the bond made her gentler, more indulgent, and less self-centered.

Throughout, the Count and the household watched and speculated, charmed by Longueville’s worldliness and by Emilie’s changing demeanor. The young man’s blend of discretion, talent, and reserve kept his background uncertain and his admirers hopeful. Emilie surrendered to the tide of feeling, delighting in their private intimacies and the new inward life he awakened, even as unanswered questions about his rank and history lingered at the edges of their romance.
