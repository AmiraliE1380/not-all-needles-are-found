DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my "History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century" led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval gives weight to my efforts. Your devotion to research sustained me in writing about modern civilization. Accept this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stood the Hotel d’Esgrignon, home of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. The jest of a name matched a family proud of its ancient Northman lineage and stubborn provincial dignity: marriages without dowries, refusals of titles, and local reverence despite neglect by court and state.

The Revolution threatened them. The Marquis avoided emigration by hiding; family lands were sold. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, with the steward Chesnel, saved some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel was demolished. Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a new dwelling, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married; she died in childbirth, leaving a son. Grief deepened their reduced circumstances. When a local contractor proposed to marry Mlle. d’Esgrignon—a proposal she and her brother found insulting—Chesnel was reproached for negotiating with him.

Chesnel was more than a servant: confidant, manager, and protector of the family’s interests, faithful to a declining house. Politically the era split nobles between Bonapartists and monarchists; the d’Esgrignons remained royalist. By 1804 many émigrés had returned, but fortunes did not revive. The Marquis became a local emblem of old nobility, presiding over a salon mockingly called the “Collection of Antiquities.” By 1822 the Restoration brought little relief: finances strained, life modest, yet they clung to ideals and a lingering legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most thought it impossible the d’Esgrignons could regain their former glory. Many hoped Victurnien, the handsome heir, would marry into nobility to restore prestige, but the family lacked the connections, money, and stability to reclaim status after forty years. Only Chesnel, the notary, recognized this reality and urged adaptation; the Marquis trusted fate or divine intervention.

Victurnien, adored and indulged by his father and aunt, had never faced opposition. Spoiled upbringing, the Chevalier’s romantic tales of past grandeur, and constant flattery made him selfish and reckless; he came to regard himself as above common people. In society the contrast with modern realities quickly showed. His escapades alienated townspeople and led to legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened into excess, assuming lineage would shield him. Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit these weaknesses to hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin.

Vanity drove Victurnien into gambling and frivolity, with Chesnel’s financial sacrifices sustaining him. Sent to Paris with hopes of restoring the name, he found the court indifferent. Undeterred, he adopted the extravagant Parisian lifestyle; his looks opened doors and he became infatuated with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure. As pleasures mounted, so did debts, while friends egged his spending and Chesnel kept propping him up. This tension between pretension and dire finances foreshadowed downfall, illustrating the dangers of vanity and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien 200,000 francs without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. Victurnien had also taken 2,000 crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources and wrote flattering, deceitful letters home; his father and aunt remained unaware. Only du Croisier knew and delighted not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them as the debts mounted.

Du Croisier first targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly on Rue du Bercail. He presented a bill for 227,000 francs, demanding payment or threatening exposure. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance, proposed to buy the property, and completed the purchase within two days. Stripped of lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and house, consumed by worry for Victurnien.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a charming letter from Victurnien about travels in Italy with the Duchesse and remained enchanted. Chesnel’s arrival shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked dishonor. She resolved to go to Paris—pretending it was for medical reasons—to confront the Duchesse. Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis.

Du Croisier planned to cut Victurnien off. He wrote that the Kellers would advance no more without security, timing the letter with Victurnien’s desperation. After briefly contemplating suicide, Victurnien instead plotted to flee with the Duchess and forged a bill of exchange; when she agreed and he presented a bill for 300,000 francs, the Kellers advanced the sum unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of this, realized his scheme was succeeding.

Victurnien gave the money to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who hid it and persuaded him to return to the Opera. He almost confessed to Chesnel but, dazzled by the Duchess’s look, stayed at her request. The next day, planning to flee, he found her resolved to face the consequences; torn between admiration and despair, he recognized the depth of his love. After a quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught; his plea for forgiveness met indifference, worsening his anguish and clouding his judgment.

When a warrant was issued, Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, Victurnien and his aunt discussed hiding and slipping into Chesnel’s house — a plan that became a trap. Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir (who hoped to profit from Victurnien’s misfortune), was devastated. He considered taking the blame for the forgery and set out to confront Victurnien’s creditor, du Croisier, to obtain a settlement.

Du Croisier, relishing his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family, treated the affair as a chance to assert dominance and settle aristocratic scores. At his salon the news of Victurnien’s arrest spread and guests debated the impending trial. Chesnel tried to sway the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and leaned on Mme. de Maufrigneuse—who, once she understood Victurnien’s love and the stakes, felt compelled to help.

Negotiations with du Croisier were fraught: he demanded money, political favors, and social recognition—terms Chesnel could hardly meet. Desperate, Chesnel offered a large sum, even contemplated perjury to save Victurnien, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. The legal struggle came to symbolize a broader clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, where loyalty, love, honor, and ambition would decide more than one fate.

Meanwhile Blondet, a judge more devoted to gardening than the bench, neglected career prospects amid a troubled marriage; he favored his illegitimate son Émile, who prospered in Paris, while his legitimate son Joseph was groomed for the bench. Young lawyers like Michu often outshone the elders.

A politically astute prosecutor, pressured by the Tribunal’s president and his wife, was drawn into a scheme against the d’Esgrignons. The Duchess and Chesnel funded efforts to free Victurnien; the trial ultimately exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. Though socially scrutinized, the d’Esgrignons preserved their honor; Victurnien’s future remained uncertain, highlighting shifting loyalties and ambitions in a changing France.
