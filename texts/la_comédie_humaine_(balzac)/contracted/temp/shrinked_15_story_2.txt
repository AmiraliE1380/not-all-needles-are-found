The Comte de Fontaine, a loyal royalist from Poitou, had served the Bourbons during the La Vendée conflict. Despite noble lineage and past sacrifices, he was financially ruined after his estate was confiscated. He married Mademoiselle de Kergarouet, a noblewoman without fortune. After the second revolution he moved to Paris with his large family seeking better prospects. Initially disillusioned, he received unexpected honors—a military promotion and royal decorations—that rekindled hope for favor.

He soon found his sacrifices little recognized and felt marginalized as new aristocrats rose. Though he considered returning to his estate, the turmoil of 1815 drew him back into royal service. His loyalty was rewarded when he became a trusted adviser to Louis XVIII, who valued his wit and political acumen. The Fontaines then began to prosper: his sons secured important posts and his daughters made advantageous marriages, if to men of lesser nobility.

Emilie, the youngest, was spoiled and the focus of Parisian attention. Her beauty and charm made her desirable, yet she remained indifferent, asserting superiority over suitors of lower birth. Despite her father’s efforts to arrange suitable matches, Emilie’s exacting standards and caprice provoked family conflict; she dismissed candidates for trivial flaws and repeatedly frustrated his plans.

As politics shifted and the King’s health declined, the Comte intensified his efforts to marry Emilie off, hoping a good match would cement the family’s status and justify his sacrifices. Her unreasonable expectations yielded a string of rejections that left him increasingly desperate. Family dynamics grew strained: Emilie’s arrogance created friction with siblings and their spouses, who themselves navigated the complexities of newfound standing.

Through the turmoil the Comte clung to hope that royal favor and a proper marriage for Emilie would restore the family’s honor. Yet as the winter social season brought new prospects, her dismissive attitude persisted, leaving the Fontaine family’s future uncertain.

Nature had shaped Emilie de Fontaine to be the perfect Célimène. Tall and slender, she could assume dignity or coquettishness at will. Her long neck expressed scorn and impertinence; fine black hair and arched brows lent prideful air. She had mastered feminine gestures that could convey contempt or charm; her melodious voice could win a heart or, used sharply, silence indiscretion. Her alabaster complexion resembled a tranquil lake, stirred only by fleeting emotion. Many young men whom she scorned accused her of merely acting; she answered that she inspired their desire and then subjected them to her whims.

Monsieur de Fontaine saw too late that adulation had spoiled his daughter. Praise had swelled her pride and self-confidence, nurturing the selfishness of a pampered child. Her youthful charms masked these defects, but he perceived them and tried to teach practical wisdom. His efforts met caprice and ironic shrewdness; his gentle advice seemed to glide off her heart like water from marble. He came to believe her affection was often condescending, like a child demanding attention before returning to play.

Emilie sometimes showed fondness for her parents but often withdrew, jealous of their shared love with others, including her siblings. She blamed solitude on fate, failing to see happiness comes from within. Determined to resist her sisters’ marriages, she nonetheless envied their contentment. Her unpredictability led her mother to suspect she might be slightly mad.

This pride is common among beautiful young women of wealth, who often imagine their mothers cannot understand youthful dreams and suspect maternal envy. These struggles produce secret tears and revolts against perceived tyranny, and elaborate life plans that picture a perfect partner who must meet exacting criteria. With experience many find happiness unexpectedly and learn ideals may not match reality.

Emilie’s terms for a suitor were strict: he must be a peer of France, a soldier, and possess an Order so they could be treated with respect. He also had to be slender; she considered corpulence a flaw in men, believing only the slim could be romantic. Her disdain for those who failed her standards was clear, and Monsieur de Fontaine feared her eccentricities would invite ridicule and that indecision would harm her prospects.

During the winter of Charles X’s reign Monsieur de Fontaine intensified efforts to find matches, hosting gatherings to attract suitors. His reputation as a corrupter of legislative integrity grew, yet he remained earnest. After a season of matchmaking he felt hopeful, only to see Emilie’s whims and standards unyielding.

One morning he summoned her to discuss her future. He arranged his study meticulously to present a dignified front. Emilie greeted him with a light, perfunctory kiss. He broached her marriage prospects and stressed the importance of a wise choice for her happiness.

She interrupted, reminding him of their prior agreement about suitors. He urged her to take the matter seriously, noting the family’s efforts to secure a suitable match. She dismissed his concerns, mocking the idea of settling for anything less than a peer and finding fault with the candidates he proposed.

Exasperated, Monsieur de Fontaine declared he had fulfilled his duty and that the choice now lay with her. He warned that marital happiness depends on mutual respect rather than mere wealth or status. Emilie, moved by his sincerity, feigned warmth to coax him, yet her pride remained intact.

At a family gathering she reiterated her refusal to consider marriage, insisting she would only wed a peer. Her uncle joked that she was waiting for the Duc de Bordeaux to come of age, provoking laughter, but Emilie remained resolute and forbade further talk of marriage.

As summer neared the family retired to the country house. Emilie wished to attend the Bal de Sceaux, drawn by the crowd and the anonymity it offered. The ball, known for its lively atmosphere, drew a mix of social classes.

There she was surprised to find upper-class attendees mingling with the rest. While observing the festivities she noticed a striking young man who stood apart, exuding elegance and dignity. His appearance and manner stirred her imagination and she felt instant attraction.

Determined to learn more, Emilie approached him, but he remained aloof. She tried to attract his attention with exaggerated gestures and laughter, but he seemed indifferent. Frustrated, she followed his gaze and found him watching another dancer, a pale girl named Clara.

Intrigued, Emilie decided to follow the pair as they left the ball. The young man, attentive to Clara, nonetheless caught Emilie’s eye as they departed; she felt a mix of jealousy and curiosity, eager to discover his identity.

Days later, while riding with her uncle, Emilie encountered Lady Dudley and Monsieur Vandenesse and realized the young man she admired was not what she had hoped. Disappointed, she tried to conceal her feelings but remained drawn to him.

Her uncle, noticing her agitation, speculated about her feelings for the young man. Emilie insisted he must be of noble birth; her uncle promised an introduction. This intensified Emilie’s interest and she began to fantasize about a future with him.

As days passed, Emilie’s infatuation grew, fueled by imagination and the romance of the unknown. She remained unaware of the complexities and challenges that love and marriage would bring, blinded by youthful ideals.

The next morning, before Emilie woke, her uncle hurried to Chevreuse and, recognizing the young man he'd insulted the day before, greeted him with the polished courtesy of the old court.

“Who would have guessed I’d meet the son or grandson of one of my best friends at seventy-three? I’m a vice‑admiral; dueling’s no matter to me. I had too much grog and ran you down. Shake hands; I’d sooner endure a hundred rebuffs from a Longueville than bring his family regret.”

The young man, reserved but cordial, accepted the Count’s apology and they rode together. The admiral joked about duels, fired a pistol into a birch to show his fearlessness, and praised the young man’s shooting. He quizzed him on matters that marked a gentleman—debts, pleasures, heraldry—teasing about mistresses and youthful extravagance. Longueville answered with composed manners: he paid punctually, lived independently, and showed the polish of a fine education. The Count resolved to introduce him to society at the Pavillon Planat.

At the Villa Planat, when a servant announced “Monsieur de Longueville,” curiosity became attention. Longueville arrived simply but elegantly, at ease in the Receiver‑General’s mansion, well‑spoken and knowledgeable—even about naval architecture, which the admiral broached. His answers were discreet; his manner suggested training like that of the École Polytechnique. When pressed on his profession, he said he had renounced the Engineers to preserve his independence. He declined dinner courteously, saying he cared for a delicate sister.

The family was struck. He charmed without ostentation, and though he aroused Mademoiselle de Fontaine’s interest—Emilie had dressed to attract him—his measured attention left her pleased and wounded. She usually displayed her charms for newcomers, but now seemed simpler, almost natural; she spoke little and, when roused by her uncle, quipped that perfection often hid defects—“Those who please everybody please nobody”—a remark revealing caution and pride.

Over the next fortnight Longueville called repeatedly. Emilie, accustomed to being the center of attention, found herself drawn outward by his reserve. He was modest and discreet: he never spoke of his family and skillfully evaded her probes. He accepted what she offered—conversation about painting, piano and song—without revealing his social standing. Once they sang a duet and those present could not tell whether he was professional. His silence both tormented Emilie and deepened her interest.

The family wished to meet Clara Longueville, and an informal dance was arranged to welcome her. When Clara and her brother arrived, Emilie felt genuine pleasure for Clara and lavished her with attention, though tinged by envy: Clara combined ease with modesty and, while amiable, gently reproved Emilie’s earlier disdain for commoners. Clara said she had come out of affection for her brother, and her frank courtesy reassured Emilie. The exchange—Clara’s gentle rebuke and Emilie’s relieved response—brought the two girls closer.

With autumn the attachment between Emilie and Longueville quietly deepened. Their life became a series of small intimacies: aimless conversations that skirted deeper feeling, shared sunsets, gatherings of wildflowers, and passionate duets where music expressed what words did not. They learned each other’s ways as lovers do, testing limits, revealing little and withholding more. Longueville’s silence remained both comfort and barrier; Emilie cherished his attention and resented the mystery, yet the bond made her gentler, more indulgent, and less self‑centered.

Throughout, the Count and household watched and speculated, charmed by Longueville’s worldliness and by Emilie’s changing demeanor. His discretion, talent, and reserve left his background uncertain and admirers hopeful. Emilie surrendered to the tide of feeling, delighting in their private intimacies and the new inward life he awakened, even as questions about his rank and history lingered at the edges of their romance.
