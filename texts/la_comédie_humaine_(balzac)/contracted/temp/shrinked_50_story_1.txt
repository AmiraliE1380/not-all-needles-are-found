Halfway down the Rue Saint-Denis near the Rue du Petit-Lion, a weathered house helped historians reconstruct old Paris: dilapidated walls exposed timber and cracked plaster, a warped triangular roof overhung the street, and overlapping upper planks lightened the fragile structure.

On a rainy March morning a young man, cloaked like an antiquary, stood across studying its peculiarities. He ignored narrow first-floor windows and inspected the third-floor sash windows of crude green-tinted glass, glancing often at the shop below. A large wooden beam, supported by four pillars, bore many paint layers and a whimsical painting of a cat with an oversized racket; a faded sign read “Guillaume, Successor to Master Chevrel.”

Dressed as if from a ball—stylish cloak, white silk stockings, white gloves, disheveled hair—his pale face and dazzling cravat drew shopkeepers’ eyes. Three apprentices at the dormer laughed and sprinkled him with shaving cream; he brushed it off as a girl's Raphael-like face flashed and vanished. The “Cat and Racket” shutters opened, a manservant displayed the cloth sign, and behind iron bars many brown-linen packages appeared. Monsieur Guillaume, old-fashioned in appearance, was reputed the city’s best-stocked, honest cloth merchant, though rivals grumbled about his notary’s heavy discounts.

The old merchant, Monsieur Guillaume, awakens at his Rue Saint-Denis shop and watches a stranger loitering outside. A staunch traditionalist with a shrewd, patient face, he enforces a strict routine on three apprentices who fear his keen gaze. When work begins, Guillaume notices the stranger’s anxious looks; the eldest apprentice, curious, steps out, thinks he sees Mademoiselle Augustine hurrying away, and hails a cab—thwarting a prank aimed at her.

Guillaume reprimands the youths for idleness, recalling his own faster youth, and insists on rigorous labor despite the apprentices’ comfortable homes and board payments. The spotless shop and frugal household—apprentices even barred from the family dessert—follow strict tradition: Mass together, modest wages for long service. Joseph Lebas, the eldest apprentice, earns a small wage and is seen as a possible match for Virginie, though he secretly loves Augustine. Virginie is devout and severe; Augustine, eighteen, is delicate, bookish, and yearns for passion. Guillaume worries about his daughters’ prospects in changing times.

From that day the old merchant, grieving his eldest daughter’s decline, recalled marrying Mademoiselle Chevrel under circumstances like Joseph Lebas’s and Virginie’s; marrying off his daughter would repay a debt to an orphan benefactor. Joseph Lebas, thirty-three, aware of the fifteen-year gap with Augustine and Monsieur Guillaume’s rule that the elder sister marry first, suffered in silence. Warm-hearted, tall, and broad-chested, he kept his love concealed.

Months earlier a young painter paused outside the dark Cat and Racket, transfixed by a scene any artist would envy: a cave-like shop opening to a lamp-lit dining room that evoked Dutch interiors. The family, Augustine among them with a chubby maid, formed a modest, peaceful tableau. Augustine, pensive and barely touching her food, seemed almost angelic in the lamp’s glow; the sight overwhelmed the painter, who left unable to eat or sleep.

Determined to capture it, he returned repeatedly—sometimes in disguise—studying Augustine. For eight months he lived in love and art, neglecting friends and routine. His friend Girodet, seeing two masterpieces intended for the Salon, recognized their depth and said love had inspired them, though he warned against public display. The paintings were nevertheless shown and caused a sensation: The Interior launched a new genre, and the portrait became unforgettable, winning acclaim and offers the artist declined.

Madame Guillaume mentioned the exhibition; with her cousin’s aid Augustine saw the Louvre. Trembling before the portrait she recognized herself; the artist whispered, “You see how love has inspired me,” and she was stunned. Fleeing the crowd, she wept, exchanged a brief nod with him, and felt guilt and exhilaration. She feigned a headache to avoid talk, longed for the elegance of a genius’s wife, and next day learned the painter had removed the pictures—an absence that revealed her instinctive sensitivity.

The morning after the ball, Theodore de Sommervieux—who had won Augustine’s heart—found it hard to declare his love because her household constrained them. Augustine sat in the counting-house between Mademoiselle Virginie and Madame Guillaume, closely watched by her mother, while Theodore, fearing rivals among the assistants, bribed a maid to arrange discrete meetings; they agreed to meet during Mass at Saint-Leu.

At the Cat and Racket the family busied itself with stock-taking under Monsieur Guillaume, ignoring Augustine’s longing. After the day's work they relaxed—attending Cendrillon and letting the apprentices enjoy a night out. The next morning Guillaume resumed routine and summoned his assistant Joseph Lebas, offering him partnership. Overwhelmed, Joseph blurted he loved Augustine; Guillaume at first misheard Virginie's name, then, once clear, suggested Joseph accompany Augustine to Mass.

At breakfast tension rose; Madame chastised Augustine and sent her away, prompting a family discussion about her future. Augustine declared her love for Theodore and vouched for his respectable, prosperous background. Madame Roguin arrived with news of Theodore’s honors and fortune, swaying the parents. By evening Guillaume consented, Joseph was informed, Theodore presented a painting of the Cat and Racket, and the household embraced a joyful future.

A few months after that memorable Sunday, Saint-Leu’s high altar witnessed two contrasting weddings. Augustine and Theodore shone with happiness while Virginie, simply dressed, followed her sister. Monsieur Guillaume had wanted Virginie married first, but the priests attended the more elegant bride. Neighbors praised Virginie’s sensible match and envied Augustine for marrying an artist of rank; an old fan-maker warned such extravagance would bring ruin. After a splendid ball and supper the Guillaumes returned home and the Lebas family resumed their old residence. The ecstatic artist carried Augustine to their richly adorned apartment.

Their first year was blissful: Theodore lavished affection and Augustine, lost in love, believed her devotion would always enchant him. As pregnancy approached, Theodore felt the pull of work, reconnected with friends, and frequented the Duchesse de Carigliano’s salons, pleased to be seen with a beautiful wife. Augustine liked society but felt out of place among artists; her admiration was sincere but superficial, and an intellectual disconnect grew. Studio life only exposed her discomfort; at gatherings her innocence clashed with freer manners. Gradually Theodore cooled, and three years into marriage Augustine sank into sorrow as his involvement with the Duchesse deepened.

She tried to educate herself in poetry and art to reconnect, but her efforts only highlighted her inadequacy. Seeking solace at her childhood home—where Virginie and Joseph Lebas ran a steady drapery—brought no comfort. Augustine returned to her husband’s home, burdened by uncertainty and longing, like a desperate patient testing remedies for a fractured marriage.

The elderly Guillaumes welcomed their daughter warmly; her visit was a refreshing break from four years of monotony spent in reminiscence—especially over the Lecocq bankruptcy. Monsieur Guillaume checked on his shop, the Cat and Racket, and chatted with former competitors, while Madame Guillaume, placed on the Army’s clothing consulting board through her son-in-law, filled their home with extravagant, tasteless decor. Their nostalgic stagnation struck Augustine as a stark contrast to her turbulent life, leaving her both proud and appalled.

Augustine displayed her accomplishments and aired marital grievances about her husband Theodore, a painter, whom her mother judged for late nights and eccentric habits. When she mentioned divorce, her parents were shocked; Monsieur offered practical support, but Augustine recoiled, realizing only those who had suffered like her could understand her isolation.

Determined to change course, she sought the Duchess de Carigliano—not to compel Theodore but to learn to captivate him. The Duchess counseled that one must project strength, self-control, cultivate mystery, and never reveal the whole measure of affection. She even offered Augustine a portrait Theodore had painted to rekindle him. Theodore’s jealous fury at this provoked a violent quarrel; the next morning Augustine was found in despair amid the wreckage and died young. Her tragic end stands as a poignant reminder of the struggle women face pursuing love amid the complexities of artistic genius; her grave’s inscription reflects this sorrow.
