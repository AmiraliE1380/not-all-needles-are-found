In spring 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness, likely from overwork or excess. Seeking rest, light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with welcoming relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte‑Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who embodied provincial French types. One was an ancient but impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a resolute sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife convinced their daughters’ fortunes rested on the family name. They clung to obsolete customs—nobility without wealth or influence.

Opposite them stood a wealthier, more recent family attuned to Parisian trends. The wife was a society woman, slightly behind the latest fashions, while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, managed finances shrewdly and trafficked in political gossip. Their Royalism was lukewarm; they embraced modern life while preserving ancestral ties.

The local bishop, a former Vicar‑General, mediated between families: respected for his office but often reminded of his plebeian origins. Retired officers and their families kept a veneer of aristocracy while focusing on local affairs rather than national politics. The women dressed as if for court yet lived by routine and tradition. A few wealthy townspeople had infiltrated this circle and were viewed skeptically by the older generation. Ecclesiastics lent wit and a bourgeois element to otherwise staid gatherings.

Collectively the society was marked by antiquated beliefs and resistance to change: conversations replayed familiar themes, judgments were fixed, intellectual life stagnated, and critics were dismissed as misfits.

Before his arrival Gaston’s cousin disclosed his fortune: at twenty‑three he had eighteen thousand livres from land in the Auge valley and his father was bargaining for more woodland; omitted was that his elder brother was consumptive and likely dying. At first he amused himself sketching their quirks, but weeks of monotony left him weary, trapped in a cloistered existence; ennui and growing discontent threatened to make him one more fossil among the fossils.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room where family portraits looked down on four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After a pleasant dinner he absorbed provincial habits—the old cards, threadbare cloths—and their philosophic indifference to luxury, which made Paris feel remote.

His attention fell on a young lady with red hands and a shy, awkward manner, but a casual remark about Mme. de Beauseant struck him like a fine passage in a dull opera. An elderly guest said the Vicomtesse was unwell and had declined an invitation; the talk revealed her scandalous liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and her retreat to Courcelles. Though noble, she was shunned after separating from her husband—a kind of exile that intrigued him.

He became obsessed with the idea of Mme. de Beauseant as a bright point amid provincial stagnation, yearning for access while conscious of the barriers her rank and intelligence posed. The next morning he wandered near Courcelles, romanticizing a chance meeting, then decided a direct letter might be refused and asked M. de Champignelles to arrange an introduction. The Marquis’s message at first met resistance but aroused the Vicomtesse’s curiosity; after reflection she agreed to receive the young suitor.

Excited and anxious, Gaston prepared—trusting appearance to shape her judgment—and, when announced at Courcelles, entered with a blend of ease and nervousness, ready to meet the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman in a high‑backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals, picked up a fallen glove, and motioned to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poised, fastidious carelessness contrasted with the automata he had met during two months’ exile in Normandy; in her presence he felt transported back to Paris and its sophistication.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that lent her a regal, almost fallen‑angel air; her hair, arranged in a crown of plaits, intensified that bearing. Beneath her beauty lay an underlying sadness—traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a once‑vibrant youth now emptied. M. de Nueil felt humbled and captivated by this blend of beauty, nobility, and misfortune.

When she acknowledged him he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but awareness of his duplicity made him flush. Summoning his courage he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” Rather than mercy, she rang for a servant to escort him out; her manner shifted from gracious to icy.

As he left he realized that if he went now he would never return and that the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, hoping she might relent. On impulse he turned back, saying he had forgotten something; the servant followed him into the salon unaware of the real reason.

“Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half‑sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly, eyes bright with relief. She felt a thrill at his pleasure, grateful for what she took as his comprehension of her complex emotions.

He began to plead his case, admitting his blunder and blaming youthful enthusiasm and exile’s weariness: he had idealized her and, in delirious longing for love, committed a foolish intrusion. She rebuked him—calling the visit ridiculous, protesting that she would rather be scorned than pitied, warning of impropriety, and commanding him not to return. He begged indulgence for his fault.

An impassioned voice thawed Mme. de Beauseant’s chill solitude; she was moved by M. de Nueil’s sincerity and captivated by his noble youth. Gaston, in the bloom of life, seemed free of ambition and vanity. Both concealed and reflected on their feelings: he saw in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness; she discerned in him the purity of someone resisting society’s corruptions. His approach was instinctive, hers deliberate—she tested him with opposing opinions and put him at ease with wit and grace, reviving in herself a sense of innocence and offering, in effect, absolution from past shame.

When time reminded them of their separation, Mme. de Beauseant, smiling gently, insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing that any new tie would tarnish her reputation. She spoke candidly of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her. Gaston longed to declare his feelings but feared rejection; torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he finally confessed his love and vowed to heal her old wounds. She, moved yet resolute, insisted they remain strangers. She described the pain of being unable to follow a beloved man through life’s changes and reminded him that she could not yield to blind impulse.

Gaston left unsettled, unsure whether her charm was genuine or coquetry. Diffident at twenty-three, he wrote faltering letters, then, to avoid being seen, returned to Courcelles at night and sent a heartfelt plea. He waited anxiously; doubts and fear of ridicule gnawed at him. Mme. de Beauseant’s answer arrived, sealed with the arms of Burgundy. She shut herself away and read that she refused his advances—not to wound him but to be candid. Though she cared for him, she did not share his love and would not be moved by youthful passion: she was nearly thirty, he just over twenty, and feelings might change with age. Vows made lightly could become burdens; she feared he might one day resent the sacrifices she required, and she could not bear desertion. Her consolation was in God rather than earthly ties; she could offer only the friendship of an older woman, untainted by passion. For both their sakes she urged him to remain in his country and not wreck his life for an illusion.

Gaston answered with fervor: if he could cease to love her and accept an ordinary life, he would deserve his fate; his oath of fidelity could be absolved only by death, and if she feared his remorse she might take his life. When the messenger returned, he reported having delivered Mme. la Vicomtesse’s reply as she was leaving in a carriage. Realizing she was departing, Gaston followed her to Geneva, convinced that if she truly loved him she would seek the freedom of Switzerland.

Mme. de Beauseant had rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston surprised her; she dropped her book and embraced him as he explained he had followed her. For three years they lived in the villa on Lake Geneva—alone and apparently in a dreamlike happiness: a simple home, beautiful views, quiet days cherished together. Weighty obligations soon called Gaston back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They refrained from selling the villa; instead they bought the family house, and Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to purchase an estate near Manerville adjoining Gaston’s lands. His mother took the family house at Manerville, and the lovers resumed the secluded happiness they had known.

For nine years they enjoyed an almost indescribable bliss. Yet a legal obstacle remained: Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, was still alive, so any lawful union was impossible. The situation was precarious. Gaston’s mother, the dowager comtesse, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant; the Marquise feared the dowager’s influence might one day pull Gaston away. Though tempted to sell her estate and return to Geneva, Mme. de Beauseant could not act in a way that might betray distrust.

Their fragile peace was disturbed by the arrival nearby of a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, and by Gaston’s meeting with her. Mme. de Beauseant replied with a letter that laid bare her turmoil and pride. She confessed she had not known love until she met him and that, for nine years, she had felt no jealousy and had experienced infinite happiness. But now she anguished at the thought that, at forty, she might be a burden to his future: he was thirty and might desire a marriage that would bring children and security. She recoiled at the idea that his devotion could be motivated by pity rather than desire.

She admitted sensing his hesitation and being unable to hide her tears, yet she had not reproached him. Too proud to bind his future to her own fading life, she urged him to be frank: if he still loved her, he should come after reading the letter and she would love him more than ever; if he sought freedom, she would not display her suffering, because she loved him enough to put his happiness first.

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her letter had reached Gaston she was overwhelmed and stunned into numbness. Reading her words, M. de Nueil found himself in a painful dilemma—torn among passion, duty, pride, and the prospect of a conventional future.

Gaston had nearly yielded to his mother’s urgings and to the attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil pressed a respectable match, praising the honor of being chosen by a woman who had refused distinguished suitors and noting that future land revenues might raise his income to eighty thousand livres. She argued that if Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him she should encourage his marriage. Her words left him torn.

Then a letter from Mme. de Beauseant altered everything: Gaston resolved to end their liaison and marry. “One must live a man’s life,” he told himself, aware his choice would wound her. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt genuine pity for her. He planned to prepare her gently, at first sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition and marrying later to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes. He meant to send a calming letter, though he feared a woman of such perception might be devastated by a mere note.

When Jacques delivered the letter, Mme. de Beauseant trembled, dread of loss closing in. If he did not come in person, she feared all was lost; yet a woman in love can endure much pain. As she read, an inner voice cried, “He lies!” She found the message calculated rather than sincere; in despair she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil fetched Jacques, who returned with a note saying, “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau.” M. de Nueil opened the note and found within his own letter from nine years earlier, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

If the tale ended there it might seem trivial, but its notoriety resonates with those who have known love’s profound joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, and attended mass daily.

Soon after his marriage M. de Nueil fell into emotional indifference—interpreted variously as happiness or unhappiness. His mother insisted he was content. Mme. Gaston de Nueil, like many young brides, was sweet but shallow; a month after their wedding she expected a child, fitting social expectations. Two months later, however, M. de Nueil grew increasingly pensive.

After seven months a seemingly trivial incident revealed deeper turmoil. Returning from a hunt, he crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and asked Jacques if she still enjoyed game; Jacques confirmed, and M. de Nueil paid him to set aside whatever he shot for her. For days M. de Nueil returned with an empty bag while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise.

Eventually M. de Nueil dared to write Mme. de Beauseant, but his letter was returned unopened. One evening, as his wife played the piano, he suddenly rushed to the Marquise’s room and found her in despair, emptied of hope. When she saw him she threatened to throw herself from the window if he approached. Terrified, he fled.

Back home he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later the servant returned with the unopened note. In despair M. de Nueil retired to another room and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with young France’s conventions, may have been inevitable to those who have experienced a profound soul-connection. Love requires time and nurturing; abandoning deep love for a loveless marriage brings either despair or a cold, material life. Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish might lead to such an extreme act. She may have believed herself the only sufferer, yet she rightly refused humiliation: a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to degradation, for the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
