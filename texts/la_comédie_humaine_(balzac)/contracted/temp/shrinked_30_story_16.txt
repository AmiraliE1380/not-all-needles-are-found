In spring 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recuperate from an inflammatory illness attributed to overwork or excess. Seeking rest, a light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with welcoming relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte‑Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who embodied provincial French archetypes. One was an old, impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a resolute sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order; a loud, domineering wife convinced their daughters’ prospects depended on the family name. They clung to obsolete customs—nobility without wealth or influence.

Opposite them stood a wealthier, more recent family attuned to Parisian trends. The wife was a society woman, slightly behind the latest fashions, while the husband, a member of the Departmental Council, handled finances shrewdly and trafficked in political gossip. Their Royalism was lukewarm; they engaged with modern life while preserving ancestral ties.

The local bishop, formerly Vicar‑General, mediated between factions—respected for his office but often reminded of plebeian origins. Retired officers and their families kept a veneer of aristocracy, focused on local affairs rather than national politics. Women dressed as if for court yet lived by routine. A few wealthy townspeople had infiltrated the circle and were viewed skeptically, while ecclesiastics introduced wit and a bourgeois element to otherwise staid gatherings.

Collectively the society was marked by antiquated beliefs and resistance to change: conversation recycled familiar themes, judgments were fixed, intellectual life stagnated, and critics were dismissed.

Before his arrival Gaston was appraised by family: at twenty‑three he was promising, with eighteen thousand livres from land in the Auge and a father bargaining for more woodland; omitted was that his elder brother was consumptive and likely dying. At first Gaston amused himself sketching their quirks, but weeks of monotony left him weary. Ennui and mounting discontent dimmed memories of Parisian intellectual life, threatening to make him one more fossil among the fossils.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing room where portraits presided over four card tables and sixteen whist players. After dinner he noticed provincial habits—the worn cards and threadbare cloths—and their indifference to luxury, which heightened his sense of Parisian distance.

A shy young lady with red hands caught his eye, but a casual remark about Mme. de Beauseant struck him like a fine passage in a dull opera. An elderly guest said the Vicomtesse was unwell and had declined an invitation; the talk revealed her scandalous liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and retreat to Courcelles. Though noble, she had been shunned after separating from her husband—an exile that intrigued him.

He became fascinated by Mme. de Beauseant, imagining her a hopeful contrast to provincial stagnation. Eager yet respectful of her rank, he spent the next morning near Courcelles and, fearing a direct letter would be refused, asked the Marquis de Champignelles to arrange an introduction. The Marquis’s message met resistance but piqued the Vicomtesse’s curiosity; she agreed to receive him. Both excited and anxious, Gaston prepared and, when announced at Courcelles, entered composed but nervous, ready to meet her.

A great fire burned on the hearth despite mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman in a high‑backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, with graceful movements, stirred the coals, picked up a fallen glove, and motioned to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her charm and poised, fastidious carelessness contrasted with the automata he had met during two months’ exile in Normandy; in her presence he felt transported back to Paris and its sophistication.

Mme. de Beauseant had fair hair, dark eyes, and a pale complexion that gave her a regal, almost fallen‑angel air; her hair, arranged in a crown of plaits, intensified that bearing. Beneath her beauty lay an underlying sadness—traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon she seemed cut off from the world, haunted by memories of a once‑vibrant youth now emptied. M. de Nueil felt humbled and captivated by this blend of beauty, nobility, and misfortune.

When she acknowledged him he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but the awareness of his falsehood made him flush. Summoning his courage he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” Instead of mercy she rang for a servant to escort him out; her manner shifted from gracious to icy.

As he left he realized that if he went away now he would never return and that the Vicomtesse would think him a fool. He hesitated, then turned back on impulse, saying he had forgotten something; the servant followed unaware. “Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half‑sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant’s demeanor. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly. She felt a thrill at his pleasure, grateful for what she took as his comprehension of her complex emotions.

He admitted his blunder and claimed much blame. She interrupted playfully then darkened, reflecting on how her life had changed. “I cannot do as I like; I am obliged to think,” she said, accusing him of curiosity, disrespect, and the risk of impropriety, and forbidding his return. M. de Nueil owned his fault, blaming youthful enthusiasm and exile’s weariness for idealizing her and committing a foolish intrusion for which he begged indulgence.

An impassioned voice broke Mme. de Beauseant’s solitary calm; she was struck by its sincerity and by M. de Nueil’s noble countenance. Gaston, young and ideal in demeanor—without obvious ambition or vanity—seemed to her a purity resisting society’s corruptions. Both were drawn to one another, each reflecting while concealing much. He perceived in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness; she recognized in him a youth whose troubles revealed an incorruptible heart.

Gaston’s manner was instinctive; Mme. de Beauseant’s deliberate—she tested him with contrary opinions. Her wit and grace soon put him at ease; he exclaimed, “Oh! madame, how could any man have left you?” The remark revived her sense of innocence, condemned the world that had wronged her, and offered an absolution that lifted her above old shame.

She checked the hour and, smiling, acknowledged the fleeting nature of their encounter. Gaston wished to prolong it; she insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing a new connection would tarnish her reputation. Candidly she spoke of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her; her eloquence and beauty enthralled Gaston, who longed to declare himself but feared rejection. Torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he finally confessed his love and offered to heal her old wounds. Mme. de Beauseant, moved yet resolute, reminded him they must remain strangers and spoke of the pain of being unable to follow a man through life’s changes.

Gaston left unsettled, unsure whether her charm was genuine or coquetry. Diffident at twenty-three, he wrote faltering letters, then, to avoid being seen, returned to Courcelles at night and penned a heartfelt plea laying bare his feelings. Awaiting her response, doubts gnawed at him; fearing ridicule, he sought distraction and confronted the harshness of unrequited longing.

Mme. de Beauseant’s reply, sealed with the arms of Burgundy, shut him away. She refused his advances, trying to spare him a blunt rebuff by being candid: youthful passion would not move her. Though she cared for him, she did not share his love and would not yield to blind impulse. Nearly thirty while he was just over twenty, she feared vows made lightly would become burdens and that he might one day resent the sacrifices she might require; she would rather die than endure desertion. Her consolation lay in God, not earthly ties; she could offer only the friendship of an older woman, untainted by passion. For both their sakes she urged him to remain in the country and not wreck his life for an illusion.

Gaston answered with fervor: if he could cease to love her and accept the ordinary life she suggested, he would deserve his fate; his oath of fidelity could be absolved only by death, and if she feared his remorse she might take his life. When a messenger reported delivering Mme. la Vicomtesse’s reply as she was about to depart in a carriage, Gaston realized she was leaving and followed her to Geneva, convinced that if she truly cared she would seek the freedom of Switzerland.

Mme. de Beauseant had rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston surprised her; she dropped her book and embraced him as he explained he had followed her. For three years they lived in the villa on Lake Geneva, alone and apparently in dreamlike happiness: a simple home, beautiful views, quiet days cherished together.

Weighty obligations soon called Gaston back after the deaths of his father and brother. They refrained from selling the villa and instead bought the family house; Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to buy an estate near Manerville adjoining Gaston’s lands. His mother took the family house at Manerville, and they resumed the secluded happiness they had known. For nine years they enjoyed an almost indescribable bliss.

Yet a legal obstacle remained: Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, was still alive, so any lawful union was impossible. The situation was precarious. Gaston’s mother, the dowager comtesse, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant; the Marquise feared the dowager’s influence might one day pull Gaston away. Though tempted to sell her estate and return to Geneva, Mme. de Beauseant could not bring herself to act in a way that might betray distrust.

The nearby arrival of a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, and Gaston’s meeting with her disturbed the fragile peace. Mme. de Beauseant replied with a letter revealing her inner turmoil and pride. She confessed she had not known love until she met him and that for nine years she had felt no jealousy and had experienced infinite happiness. But now she anguished at the thought that, at forty, she might be a burden to his future: he was thirty and might wish a marriage to bring children and security. She recoiled at the idea his devotion could be motivated by pity rather than desire.

She admitted sensing his hesitation and being unable to hide her tears, yet she had not reproached him. Too proud to bind his future to her own fading life, she urged him to be frank: if he still loved her, he should come after reading the letter and she would love him more than ever; if he sought freedom, she would not display her suffering, because she loved him enough to put his happiness first.

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her letter had reached Gaston, she was overwhelmed and stunned into numbness. Reading her words, M. de Nueil found himself in a painful dilemma—a crisis where passion, duty, pride, and the prospect of a conventional future collided.

Gaston was close to yielding to his mother’s urgings to marry Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a 40,000‑livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil lauded the respectability of such a match, suggesting future land incomes could raise his take to 80,000 livres, and argued that if Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him she would encourage his marriage. Torn between passion and duty, Gaston felt bound to choose what he called “a man’s life,” though he knew it would wound the Marquise.

A letter from Mme. de Beauseant precipitated his resolution: he determined to end their liaison and marry. He planned to spare the Marquise by wedding Mlle. de la Rodiere—reserving later concessions if necessary to honor Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes. He meant to send a composed note, but feared that a perceptive woman might be devastated by mere words.

When Jacques brought that note, Mme. de Beauseant trembled. If he did not come in person she felt all was lost, yet a woman in love endures much. As she read, an inner cry rose—“He lies!”—and she judged the message calculated, not sincere. In despair she tore the letter and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil fetched Jacques, who soon returned with a message beginning, “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau.” M. de Nueil opened it and instead found his own letter from nine years earlier, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity to Mme. de Beauseant; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned by the reminder of past promises and by what looked like her renunciation, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stephanie de la Rodiere.

Had the tale ended there it might seem merely domestic, but its notoriety speaks to those who know love’s joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant never left Valleroy. She lived in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, ceased to be the companion she had been, and attended mass daily; her withdrawal silently testified to the rupture.

Soon after his marriage, Gaston sank into a desolate indifference that onlookers variously read as contentment or melancholy; his mother insisted he was happy. Mme. Gaston de Nueil was amiable but shallow; society quickly suspected her of being with child. Yet two months later Gaston grew increasingly pensive, and his melancholy deepened.

After seven months a seemingly trivial incident revealed his turmoil. Returning from a hunt, he crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and, by habit, asked Jacques whether she still enjoyed game. Jacques confirmed, and Gaston paid him to set aside whatever he shot for her. For days he returned with an empty bag while Jacques discreetly delivered the game to the Marquise—tender and humiliating, showing a man unable to live without the small intimacies of his former passion.

Eventually Gaston dared to write Mme. de Beauseant, but the letter was returned unopened. One evening, while his wife played the piano, he rushed to the Marquise’s room and found her mute, emptied of hope; when she threatened to throw herself from the window rather than be approached, he fled terrified. Back home he wrote another letter, insisting it was a matter of life and death, and sent it with his servant. An hour later the servant returned with the unopened note. In despair, Gaston retired to another room and shot himself.

The story contends that love requires time and care; abandoning such love for a loveless, conventional marriage breeds either despair or a cold, material life. Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee that her friend’s anguish might lead to such an extreme act. She may have thought herself the only sufferer, yet she rightly refused humiliation: a wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to degradation, for the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
