DEDICATION
Balzac dedicates the book to a Polish lady of contrasts—childlike in faith, aged by sorrow, inspiring through love—whose inner poesy he cannot match but seeks to echo.

CHAPTER I. THE CHALET
In October 1829 notary Simon Babylas Latournelle walks from Le Havre to Ingouville with his vain wife, their son Exupère (bound for law school), and their hunchbacked head clerk Jean Butscha, who adores Modeste Mignon. Latournelle coaches Exupère in a small ruse to arouse interest. They visit the Chalet, the Mignons’ cottage with its treasured hot-house. Jean François Bernard Dumay, the family’s devoted cashier, holds the lease; the new proprietor, Monsieur Vilquin, resents him and offers money to vacate, but Dumay refuses—above all determined to protect Modeste.

CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE
Pale banker Gobenheim is already visiting; his attentions are worldly. The household is tense: Dumay readies himself to confront any man who might compromise Modeste. Blind Madame Mignon remains the dignified center while Modeste’s quiet embroidery becomes the focus of unspoken anxieties.

CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES
Once a soldier, Dumay became the Mignons’ faithful cashier after their misfortunes; his loyalty to the exiled Colonel Mignon never wavers. His childless wife treats Modeste as her own.

CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY
Business collapse and the Colonel’s exile reduced the family to the Chalet. Dumay and Latournelle work to settle debts; Modeste’s innocence amid poverty alarms Dumay, who enforces strict guardianship.

CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED
Modeste’s beauty fuels speculation; the women watch for signs of love. Dumay vows no suitor shall approach unobserved.

CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE
Shaped by books and her sister’s tragedy, Modeste dreams of being a poet’s muse and writes an ardent letter to the poet Canalis.

CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL
Canalis reads the letter with amusement and condescension, replying with flattering caution and warning against romanticizing a poet’s life. Disappointed but determined, Modeste presses the correspondence, setting a clash between her hopes and social limits.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

A proud, playful letter arrives from a veiled young woman signing herself O. d’Este M., who praises the poet’s candor and demands his truth: would he have answered the same had he known she was an heiress of six million seeking a fool for a husband? Ernest de La Brière—Monsieur de Canalis—reads it with conscience and curiosity at war. Paris might call the note coquettish, but truth, justice, and honor judge him. He delays, then replies that he will be honest only if she drops her disguise: obligations vary with rank, and a countess may speak frankly about a fondness for poetry while a girl of the people would be mocked. She answers at once, refusing to send her arms but asserting a noble name, claiming a cardinal among her family and equating her standing with the Canalises.

La Brière, amused and intrigued, arranges secrecy at the post-office, follows the postmistress Francoise Cochet to Ingouville, and peeps at a girl at a chalet window. He learns the estate belongs to Monsieur Vilquin, the richest merchant in Havre, and notes rumors of fashionable visitors and a possible match. Struck by the girl’s beauty, he spies no more, ashamed of himself, and returns to Paris to write with a mixture of conscience and passion.

He confesses that had he known her wealth he would have acted differently: men confuse happiness with means and money becomes a false religion. His heart was tested—her letter forced him to face base motives and to resolve not to marry for fortune. He admits following Francoise to Ingouville and peeking, then retracts the spying. Modeste—pleased to find a noble soul rather than a mercenary—rewards his candor. She alters her postal precautions so letters may be exchanged more directly, and her delight at touching his nobility deepens their bond.

A later letter, in which she names herself O. d’Este M., expands their intimacy and her terms. She insists on keeping her veil, affirms ancestral respectability and a guarded independence, and warns him not to attempt to find her. She has seen heiresses humiliated by suitors and is watched by protectors who would not hesitate to use force; this is meant as caution, not to incite courage. She assures him she loves poetry without affectation and that his visit to Havre spoiled the secret ideal she had imagined, though she forgives him. She stresses that the man she seeks will win her heart with her parents’ consent, that she will not be the mere possession of a husband, and that she worships the ideal without naïveté. For now, she wants friendship: confidences like those between Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne, a comradeship of thought and feeling that may, if true, lead to a real union later. She offers balms for his unhappiness and vows fidelity and constancy to any man she chooses, though she must test minds and hearts before any binding pledge.

La Brière’s reply is cautious and complex. He praises the soul she reveals but insists that letters cannot fully replace knowledge gained by daily life; a man’s spirit and his material existence must both be known. He warns that he is neither hero nor large genius, but an ordinary man with vanity, ambition, and modest means who needs a career. He fears that if she fixes her worship on him now, he may disappoint her; he might love wildly, but the likely, humdrum outcome—marriage, house, children—could shatter the ideal. With a painful candor he inclines toward refusing the perilous intimacy she proposes, and signs off as if to close the correspondence.

She answers with a passionate plea that reframes their exchange as the revival of a tradition of spiritual companionship: could there not be a modern friendship like that of Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne, or the shared life of Sismondi’s household? She compares man to a harp and to its musician and listener, asks whether two hearts can resonate across distance, and insists she seeks a genius—an Alpine flower—to cultivate, not a merchant or simpleton. She declares her father will not oppose her choice, and that she hopes for a long marriage of souls before any legal bond. She sees herself as an eaglet, caged by parental will yet yearning to be carried into the neighbor’s park by a poet’s genius. If their correspondence discloses true sympathy, she believes they could avoid common follies and live a devoted life.

La Brière answers now with ardor. He confesses his life depends on her reply: give him one sign of unbounded affection that disregards social conventions, and he will renounce trivial pleasures and change his life. For him, happiness is the fusion of feeling, a harmony of souls stamped by a noble ideal onto daily living; he calls such devotion a poet’s and a maiden’s dream and declares he will love her unto death if she permits him to seek her. He demands either hope or silence: either meet him or let him go.

She replies, amused and delighted, praising how quickly his gravity gave way to a Leander-like ardor. She sees blood ties as minor compared to affinities forged by mysterious sympathies and thanks him for the happiness he has stirred. Yet she is not frivolous: she asks for a compact. If he desires a real union, he must begin by telling her his life—what he does in Paris day by day, as to an old friend—so that her parents can see the son they might accept. She asks how great spirits can bend to family constraints and small miseries, and argues that what appears egoism in geniuses often masks abnegation; the poet’s life demands a vast organization to bear both creative gifts and private suffering. She places him among those whose generosity approaches the divine, professes belief in him where she might once have despaired, and feels strengthened by that faith. The correspondence, she says, must become the middle of the book they are beginning together.

Their last exchanges in the sequence show the mutual intoxication of the unseen and the danger of idealization. La Brière bursts into a near-confession of love: “You are a witch and I love you!” he exclaims, offering to sacrifice his former life for a chance at a lifelong, spiritual union. She asks for particulars and for the slow testing she believes will guard that ideal. His ardor and her prudent high-mindedness meet: both want a devotion that will ennoble daily life, yet both are wary of the risks of vesting a living future in the brittle medium of letters. The correspondence ends poised between promise and peril—the power of the unseen already transforming two lives, while both insist that only truth, disclosed in time and with care, can convert their epistolary dream into a lasting union.

Desplein’s visit moved Modeste; guests praised the Mignons and the salon debated his “genius.” Though the medical world admired his skill, Canalis called it mere application; Modeste defended a broader notion, citing practical innovators. The argument turned heated, exposing Canalis’s wounded vanity.

Modeste’s flirtation with Canalis provoked La Brière, who left convinced he’d lost her; her bold challenge to her father revealed growing independence. She then provoked Canalis further, intensifying La Brière’s jealousy. Canalis made grand gestures, but Modeste, skeptical of his motives, asserted her autonomy.

With the grand hunt and the Duc d’Herouville’s arrival, her feelings for La Brière deepened while ties with Canalis frayed. Her beauty and fortune reshaped the suitors’ rivalry; the hunt will expose true natures. Modeste moves toward a more complex grasp of love and ambition, learning the compromises needed to seek happiness within society.
