Sweetheart, I too am free! I write first, unless you have already written to Blois.

Do not be alarmed; my escape from the convent was simple. The conscience that tormented me yielded to gentler reasons: my aunt feared I would waste away, and my mother relented. I am in Paris thanks to you. If you had seen me when I left you, you would have been proud of your influence on my young heart. At the convent we were so intimate—our imaginations fused and made the closed world a playground—that when you left I fell into deep melancholy. I was like a turtle-dove widowed of her mate; death seemed sweet, and I nearly succumbed. To be at Blois, consumed by dread of taking vows and without you, made me ill.

My aunt could not comprehend the private life we led, nor how ill-suited the strict routine was for girls of our temperament. She had sacrificed herself for a brother; to expect the same self-denial from us was too much. For a fortnight I suppressed everything, but letter-writing became my lifeline. I began a journal imagining your own, and dreamed of Gemenos as you will of Paris.

One morning a lady companion and Philippe, my grandmother's last valet, arrived to take me away. My aunt kissed me and spoke of the pride she saw in me, which might lead to heaven or hell. I kissed her hands and promised not to return unless my heart were broken.

At Beaugency I felt stunned. No one was home—my mother at Bois de Boulogne, my father at council, my brother late dressing. Philippe led me to the suite of my beloved grandmother, the Princess de Vauremont, whose fortune had been left to me. Entering the rooms where she had died brought a wave of grief; though she died in 1817, the place seemed recently occupied. It pained me that so little had been done since her death; the panels were empty and marbles broken. Philippe told me the Princess's furniture had been willed to me, and my father was awaiting a law to restore lost property before renovating—an estimate of three hundred thousand livres had been set.

At first despair gripped me: had my father meant to send me to the convent, sparing no expense on my household? But memories of the Princess softened me; the rooms preserved her spirit. I set Philippe to polishing and rearranging, giving the old furniture a youthful air.

The bedroom and drawing-room, in yellow damask and scarlet and filled with family relics—a Savonnerie tapestry, a timepiece from Maréchal de Saxe, china from Maréchal de Richelieu, my grandmother's portrait—breathed another century and her coquetry. I recalled how she tended her appearance to the end; her drawing-room showed traces of nocturnal visitors, disarranged furniture, card-tables, snuff.

When I was sent to the convent in 1816 she bade me go without display, promising I should remain independent and marry whom I pleased. Six months later she died, leaving her will to the Prince de Talleyrand, who hinted through a friend that she disapproved of the vows. I hope to meet the Prince and learn more.

Thus, though no one in flesh met me at first, the Princess's presence comforted me, and I resolved to keep our pact: to tell each other the smallest domestic details. Send me every minute picture of your life.

October 19th.

At half-past five, Rose announced my mother's return. My mother's suite corresponds to mine, and we share a secret staircase. I descended anxious about our reunion, wondering if I should feign affection. Her tact surprised me: she was neither distant nor effusive, but greeted me like a friend and kissed my forehead.

She said she preferred my living with family to dying at the convent, and wished to be more sister than mother. I found her charming at thirty-eight—beautiful, with dark-blue eyes, a smooth forehead, a marvelous figure and hands—and I responded kindly. She took me in and said we had guests for dinner, advising I not make my first appearance in society before visiting the dressmaker.

My father arrived and greeted me affectionately, calling me his rebel daughter and praising my looks; he predicted pride in my future. My brother, the Duc de Rhetore, arrived leisurely and kissed me; I was conscious of his indifference for not having come to Blois when I was there.

I tidied my rooms and began writing to you. Thus ended the return of an eighteen-year-old after nine years at a convent, to a noble household where old ways linger. Tired, I kept convent hours and slept early.

November 25th.

The next morning the rooms were made ready; Philippe had put flowers in the vases. Breakfast was troublesome as convent habits left me hungry early. About one, my father knocked and found me writing. He gave me a purse containing twelve thousand francs as the annual allowance he intended for me, and said my grandmother left me five hundred thousand francs in government stock, yielding about forty thousand francs a year. He had planned to use this to establish my younger brother but now left the decision to me, trusting my good sense. He warned that a Chaulieu daughter must comport herself with pride; any slander might have dire consequences. His frankness pleased me and spared my pride.

Philippe spent the morning arranging my transformation: dressmaker Victorine, corset-maker, shoemaker, glove-maker—all called to fashion me out of convent attire. I was impatient to see my new self; even a glovemaker was summoned. At lunch, my mother proposed we go to the milliner; I felt dazzled by this independence—like a blind man who had recovered sight. I began to sense how far I had been from society.

My father seemed absent at lunch; he is deeply engaged in state affairs and had refused an appointment as Minister of State, preferring an embassy. At home, family life is fragmentary: my brother and father are often away, my mother devotes mornings to dressing and a busy social calendar of drives, receptions, and evenings at theatres and concerts. We meet mainly at lunch and dinner; otherwise each pursues his own life. I see now why family affection is so rare: in Paris people have no time for private intimacy.

Miss Griffith, my governess—recommended by the English ambassador—is a Scotch clergyman's daughter, thirty-six, well-bred and charged to teach me English and chaperon me. She is cautious but accommodating; I quickly perceive she will be governed by me. She advised against being dazzled by society and against being seduced by what most attracts me—her moral lectures are brief and repetitive.

I have not yet seen the streets; I must first be made presentable. The Italian opera opens soon, and my mother has a box. I read too much indiscriminately; modern literature often dwells on love—the theme that once obsessed us at the convent. Corinne and Adolphe delighted me. When I asked if I might meet Mme. de Staël, my parents laughed and informed me she was dead.

December.

My Darling—tomorrow I make my debut. I have rehearsed all my follies, tried on stays, shoes, curls, dress, ornaments—performed a private inspection. Griffith alone witnessed my little play. In short, I have left the convent and been reborn as a frivolous society girl. I may be raw—shoulder lines not yet rounded—but there is strength, health, and a vivid complexion. My features are correct, my hair pale gold with a darker center, my eyes bright and insolent, the mouth expressive. I have grace of gait and voice; my grandmother's manner and voice are in me. If I wished, I can be innocent, witty, or melancholy enough to command admiration.

My father has provided me with a trousseau—ribbons, shoes, gloves, dressing-case, scent-box, fan, sunshade, prayer-book, cashmere shawl—and promised riding lessons. I can dance; tomorrow evening I am to appear in white muslin, with a Greek garland of white roses, and I intend to play the simpleton to win the other women. My mother underestimates me and sees me as a simpleton; my brother treats me with polite contempt. I suspect Alphonse harbors secret discontent and jealousy toward our father, which none understand.

We are unlike my parents: my father absorbed in public life and pleasures, my mother devoted to her toilette and society. They both know how to wear their parts; the show of unity in the family conceals indifference. It amazes me how Paris life can consume people from nine until the small hours, draining both energy and money. I am newly curious about this spell of society that keeps people enslaved and separate. For now I have sixteen hours a day to think, while those around me barely have minutes for reflection. There is much to learn in this world I sought; I am eager and anxious at once.

Yesterday at two I drove along the Champs-Élysées and through the Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day that reminded me of the Loire. For the first time I felt I had truly seen Paris; the Place Louis XV seemed a triumph of art. Impeccably dressed, pensive, poised, I rode without a smile returned. Only my father, a duke, acknowledged me with satisfaction; my mother blew a butterfly kiss. Griffith scanned the crowd; a young woman should know where to look.

I was furious when a man—probably a carriage-maker—inspected our carriage without seeing me. I had misjudged my appeal; beauty, I found, is common in Paris. Hats were tipped to the less deserving while my mother drew admiration. Determined to understand why, I studied the men: unattractive, shabby imitations of former elegance, their hats like truncated columns, faces worn by thwarted ambition. “And these are the products of Paris!” I exclaimed to Miss Griffith; she called them “most cultivated and pleasant.” I was silent—a thirty-six-year-old spinster’s heart practices tolerance.

That evening at a ball I stayed near my mother as she received every compliment; I was merely the excuse for praise aimed at her. Men praised the heat and beauty as though I were freezing and blind; each acted as if seeing me for the first time. Married women outshone me in extravagant dress, surrounding themselves with admirers; a few were genuinely beautiful—my mother among them. A girl at a ball is only a dancing machine.

The men disappointed as much here: exhausted, bland, uniform. One striking man failed to move me—he was a commoner, a professor and author, absorbed in himself. I judged intellectuals who keep their pens when they love as treating women like objects; love must displace ambition.

I resolved not to quarrel with society and simply enjoy dancing. Overhearing trivial gossip, I felt my ignorance might be a blessing. At home, my mother interrupted my account: “My dear child, it requires as much tact to know when to be silent as when to speak.” Her words revealed lessons even a mother must conceal. Suddenly I was ashamed of my candor. Must I never again express joy? The clash between candor and society’s rules left me sorrowful; already the hedges of my life are flecked with white wool. Farewell, beloved.

How deeply your letter moved me, especially as our paths diverge: you to Parisian brightness, I to a quiet retreat.

At the Castle of Maucombe my room is as I left it, now opening on the Gemenos valley I once saw without understanding. Soon after my arrival my parents took me to dine with M. de l’Estorade, a wealthy, frugal old gentleman. He lost his only son to Bonaparte’s conscription in 1813; the youth evaded the draft, joined the Emperor’s bodyguard, and vanished after Leipzig. The Baron clung to hope and hoarded his income for him. Only later I learned the son had been slowly returning from Russia, Poland, and Germany; his fortunes turned in Berlin where the French Minister helped him home. The Baron, a small Provençal landowner with about ten thousand livres of income, remains obscure in Provence despite these adventures.

The combined sum from the late Mme. de l’Estorade’s estate and the father’s savings is roughly two hundred and fifty thousand livres—excluding property—a considerable fortune here. The Baron has just bought a poorly run estate he plans to plant with ten thousand mulberry trees. Now that his son is found, his one desire is to marry him to a girl of good family. My parents approved once they learned Renée de Maucombe would be accepted without a dowry and that her prospective inheritance would be acknowledged in the marriage contract; my brother Jean signed to recognize an advance on his inheritance. Such are the arrangements of Provençal nobles under Bonaparte’s Civil Code, which otherwise forces many girls into convents rather than marriage.

That dinner was the first meeting of your sweetheart and the exile. My father-in-law-to-be has no castle, only a simple country house at the entrance of a beautiful valley: unpretentious, four pebble walls and a red-tiled roof, with a typically Provençal garden and a touch of neglect. Still, the house did its best for the dinner, serving us on old silver. The exile—my dear—is emaciated and pale, marked by suffering. At thirty-seven he looks much older, hair streaked with white; he is a little deaf, like the Knight of the Sorrowful Countenance. Despite his ravaged appearance, he seems pleased to be marrying Renée.

I have graciously agreed to become Mme. de l’Estorade, bringing the two hundred and fifty thousand livres, on condition that I may transform the grange, La Crampade, into a park; I have asked my father for a water grant to help this. In a month I will be Mme. de l’Estorade, having made a good impression.

While you enjoy Paris’s vibrant life, I descend from our shared dreams into humble country existence. I am determined to bring joy to this young man, who knew little youth, going straight from his mother’s arms to war and Siberia. I will take pleasure in improving our little oasis—trees, a hillside park, perhaps a kiosk with glimpses of the Mediterranean, orange and lemon trees—and in tending household affairs, which M. de l’Estorade has promised to leave to me. My father-in-law and the Chevalier share my religious sentiments; provincial dangers are minimal. We will busy ourselves with silkworms and the ordinary ups and downs of Provence, and I shall be cherished by a frail man in the Gemenos valley.

You, dear Louise, will provide the romance for me. Tell me of your balls, your dresses, and the men you meet; your other self will always be with you, listening and feeling the excitement. If only I could enjoy some Parisian fun while you managed La Crampade—poor M. de l’Estorade thinks he is marrying one woman; will he discover two?

I must conclude—I am foolish only through you. One kiss on each cheek; my lips remain untouched, he has only taken my hand. P.S. I have just opened your third letter. I have about one thousand livres to spend; buy me pretty things we cannot find here or in Marseilles. Think of the recluse at La Crampade; neither family has anyone in Paris with taste to buy what we need. I will answer later. Goodbye, dear.

VI. DON FELIPE HENAREZ TO DON FERNAND PARIS, September.

The head of our house lives. The massacre in the Court of Lions forced us into wearing two faces, but that survival instinct—an “Arab cunning”—probably saved me. Ferdinand’s terror made his acting convincing enough to deceive Valdez; had I not intervened, the Admiral would have died. The Liberals still do not understand kingship. I know this Bourbon; his repeated promises of protection only confirm my mistrust. A true Spaniard need not repeat assurances; too many words usually hide deceit.

Valdez sailed away on an English ship. Seeing the cause of Spain fail in Andalusia, I set my escape plan in motion: I sent my steward and coral fishers to meet me on a shore, and while Ferdinand sought to seize me, I was already at my barony of Macumer among brigands who defy authority. The last Hispano-Moorish family of Granada has taken refuge in an African desert; even a Saracen horse grazes on an estate inherited from Saracens. Brigands who once feared me now shield me from the King’s vendetta, proud to defend the Duc de Soria, a Henarez—the first of our line to visit them since Moorish rule.

I had hoped to live off the estate’s income, but Quevedo was right: my possessions are nominal, not productive—vast prairies without houses, virgin forests without furnishings. It would take a million piastres and a resident master for fifty years to make them profitable. This must be settled.

Watching my noble country reduced to a carcass for monks to exploit moved me to tears and foreshadowed Spain’s grim future. In Marseilles I learned of Riego’s fall. It struck me that my life would be prolonged as unnoticed martyrdom. Is existence worth living when one can neither die for one’s country nor live for a woman? To love and to conquer was our law. Yet my sabre is broken, the palace ruined, the spring dried to sand.

Here is my last will and testament.

Fernand, this is why I checked your enthusiasm and urged loyalty to the rey netto. As brother and friend I implore you; as master I command: go to the King and claim my dignities, property, office, and titles. He may hesitate, but tell him you are loved by Marie Heredia and she can marry none but a Duc de Soria. The Heredia fortune has long protected me; your proposal will seem to deprive me of a final resource, and the King will willingly hand over my spoils.

Marry Marie. Her secret love for you was no secret to me; I have prepared the old Count for your ascension. Marie and I discharged our family duties; everything favors you. You are handsome and possess Marie’s heart. I am an ill-favored grandee for whom she harbors an unconfessed aversion; any hesitation about my misfortunes you will soon overcome. As Duc de Soria you will not regret my predecessor nor lose a maravedi. I will keep my mother’s diamonds for my independence; the gap they leave can be made up with Marie’s jewels. Send them with my nurse, old Urraca—the only servant I shall retain. No one makes my chocolate as she does.

During our short revolution my needs were scant and covered by salary. You will find the income of the last two years with your steward. This sum is mine, but a Duc de Soria cannot marry without expenses, so we will share it. You will not refuse this wedding gift from your brigand brother; I mean it so.

The barony of Macumer, not Spanish territory, remains mine. Thus I still have a country and a name should I ever re-enter the world.

Thank Heaven, the house of Soria is saved!

Just as I resigned myself to being merely Baron de Macumer, the French cannon announced the Duc d'Angoulême’s arrival. I must stop here.

October.

When I arrived I had not ten doubloons. A poor leader finds means to live within calamities he cannot avert: for the vanquished Moor, a horse and the desert; for the frustrated Christian, the cloister and a little gold. My resignation is weariness, not surrender. I am not ready to abandon thoughts of life. Ozalga gave letters of introduction, including one to a bookseller who tends to our countrymen. He found me eight pupils at three francs a lesson. I teach every other day, earning twelve francs—more than I need. When Urraca arrives I will hand my connection to another Spanish exile.

I lodge in Rue Hillerin-Bertin with a poor widow who takes boarders. My room faces a small garden; it is quiet, with green trees, and I spend one piastre a day. I relish the calm: from sunrise to ten I smoke and enjoy chocolate, contemplating two Spanish plants—a broom rising from jasmine—colors that thrill any scion of the Moors. At ten I give lessons until four, return for dinner, then read and smoke until bed.

I can endure this life of work, meditation, solitude, and modest society for a long time. Be happy, Fernand; my abdication brings no regrets. Five days since my will feel like five centuries. Honor, titles, wealth—as if they never existed.

Now, with social pretenses stripped away, I open my heart to you. Though outwardly grave, my heart overflows with tenderness and devotion—sentiments I never gave even to my childhood bride. This is the source of my political zeal: Spain took the place of a mistress and received my homage. Now Spain is lost. Beggared of all, I look on the ruins of my former self and ponder the mystery of existence.

Why was life given to this carcass, and when will it depart? Why did a chivalrous race place its virtues in this last offshoot only for the seed not to sprout? What crime in me blocks love? Was I destined to be a stranded hulk? In my soul I see deserts where my ancestors once roamed under a scorching sun. Proud remnant of a fallen race, vain force, love wasted—an old man in the prime of youth—I will await death’s last grace. Under this murky sky no spark will rekindle these ashes. Thus my last words may echo Christ’s: “My God, Thou hast forsaken me!”—a cry of agony and terror few mortals have plumbed.

You can now understand the joy I find in living through you and Marie. I will watch you with the pride of a creator satisfied with his work. Love one another well; any discord between you would pain me more than it would you. Our mother foresaw that events would one day fulfill her wishes; perhaps a mother’s longing is a pact with God. She often revealed a heart that rightly belonged to both her sons, yet was given to you alone by irresistible passion. Her spirit will hover above you at the altar. What I have done pleases our womenfolk, the dead, and the King; it is God’s will. Do not resist, Fernand; obey and remain silent.

P.S. Tell Urraca to call me only M. Henarez. Do not mention me to Marie. You must be the sole living soul to know the secrets of the last Christianized Moor, whose veins hold the blood of a great family that began in the desert and is now about to die out in solitary exile.

Farewell.

VII. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENÉE DE MAUCOMBE

What! Married so soon? Engaged after a month to a stranger you barely know? He might be deaf, sickly, insufferable. They want you only to carry on the l’Estorades line; you will be buried in the provinces. Were these the promises we made? If I were you, I’d prefer to sail to the Hyères islands, risk capture by Algerine corsairs, and become a Sultana to stir a harem!

You are merely leaving one convent for another. You are a coward and will submit to family life; I will guide you: come to Paris. There we shall set fashion and drive men wild. Your husband may join the Chamber in three years; I know how to work that machine. My mother calls women who manage it “women of fashion.” I will not leave you in your grange!

Monday.

For a fortnight I have lived the life of society: evenings at the Italiens, the Grand Opera, and balls. Society bewitches; the Opera enchants me. Yet no young man has stirred me; none evokes the passion I feel listening to García in Otello. What jealousies and cries! I speak in a tongue you have not heard, but you know my jealousy.

Shakespeare is wretched in some ways: Othello wins battles and leaves Desdemona meekly neglected. I prefer long chivalric trials. A lout who fetches a glove among lions was impertinent—presumably he lost a prize. But I ramble.

My father will likely represent the King in Madrid; I shall be part of the embassy. My mother wishes to remain; the Duc de Canalis might be secretary, but the King appoints secretaries. The Duchess stays; Canalis cultivates my mother’s society and limns diplomacy as a hobby. The Duc de Rhetore will remain in Paris; Alphonse is enamored of a ballet girl—how can one love legs? Two ballet girls in a family are more destructive than plague. My second brother is with his regiment. Thus I must act as the Antigone of His Majesty’s ambassador. Perhaps I will marry in Spain; perhaps my father imagines a marriage without a dowry.

He teased me at lunch, probing my impressions of young people and society. I answered flippantly about looks and figures. He seemed disappointed. Still, with family, one may speak freely. I find young men self-centered; they don’t sustain attention. Girls are artificial, revealing themselves only in dance. Society’s courtesy is a thin veneer over selfishness—a likely residue of Bonapartist manners.

Saturday.

I omitted something: love is hidden here; I have seen no trace of the deep, abiding passion we imagined—the constancy, the interchange of happiness and sorrow, the daily renewal. Love, as we pictured it, seems rare; one might live a lifetime without meeting the being who can make one happy. The thought chills me. For days I have trembled at the thought of women’s destinies and begun to see why so many wear sad faces beneath social excitement. Marriage depends on chance. What soil yields the radiant flowers of the soul? Are we or the world at fault?

I have seen hundreds of men; none has moved me. No proof of admiration or a sword drawn in my defense would touch me. Perhaps I will return to the convent; remaining in society might make me do foolish things. Music alone has filled me; it has even taken the place of our friendship. At night I walk the Champs-Élysées imagining the man who will awaken me; I meet jugglers and secret lovers instead. I am tempted to ask a happy passerby, “Tell me, what is love?” but I restrain myself and swear to die an old maid. Love is an incarnation; how many conditions must be met? Only God can solve it.

My mother is happiest, adored by Canalis. Griffith tells me happiness can blacken other women’s characters; virtue buries savage instincts, but then what becomes of the heart? That is humiliating—no admirer for me yet. I am marriageable, but family honor may deter men; if so, they are cowards.

The parts of Chimène in the Cid delight me. Farewell.

VIII. THE SAME TO THE SAME, January.

Our master is a poor refugee hiding after participating in the revolution crushed by the Duc d'Angoulême—a triumph accompanied by splendid fêtes. Though probably a Liberal and a man of the people, he interests me; I suspect he was once condemned to death. I try to make him reveal his secret, but he has the true Castilian taciturnity: proud as Gonsalvo di Cordova, yet angelic in patience and gentleness. His pride is not irritable but natural, obliging civility through impeccable manners and keeping us at respectful distance.

My father praises Señor Henarez’s nobility, teasingly calling him Don Henarez. I once used that name; he looked up, usually downcast, and gave a look that abashed me; he has the most beautiful eyes. I asked if I had offended. He answered in dignified Spanish that religion should teach respect for the unhappy; a jest would be unkind if he had lost everything after Ferdinand VII’s triumph. If he is only a poor language teacher, is jesting not mean? Neither status warrants satire. I begged forgiveness; he bowed, opened my Don Quixote, and sat.

The incident disturbed me more than a harvest of compliments. During the lesson I watched him closely; he never looks at me. He seems under thirty, with black hair, pearly teeth, velvet-fire eyes, and a plain, singular look. Spaniards are sometimes called unclean, but this man is meticulously groomed, hands whiter than his face. He stoops slightly and wears smallpox scars that give his ugliness a piquancy. His prominent forehead and shaggy brows lend a frowning, plaintive look, like a sickly child dependent on exceptional care. His natural dignity unnerves the Duke, who dislikes him.

When Henarez returned two days after his rebuke, I said—perhaps to show gratitude—that I thought he had left Spain for political reasons. If my father goes to Madrid we might help get him a pardon. He replied that it was impossible for anyone to help him. Pressed, he said both that help was impossible and that he would not accept it—an answer that angered my father and made me drop Señor Henarez.

Yet there was something admirable in his refusal: he would not accept friendship as charity. When I spoke to him in Spanish about equality of sentiment between friends, he answered that such equality was necessary and lacking here. I challenged whether he meant equality in feeling or in rank, hoping to provoke. He raised those compelling eyes, and mine fell. He seemed to ask whether my words implied love; the joy, pride, and agonized doubt in his glance moved me. I perceived that with a Spaniard, light advances could grow serious, so I retreated.

The lesson ended; he bowed, and his eyes seemed to plead: “Do not trifle with a poor wretch.” This shift from grave dignity to humble vulnerability struck me deeply. It is dreadful to admit, but I cannot help believing there are hidden treasures of affection in this man.

All is over, my dear child, and it is Mme. de l'Estorade who writes to you. Between us there is no change; it is only a girl the less.

Do not be troubled; I did not give my consent recklessly. My life is now mapped out, and the certainty suits me. A great moral power has removed chance from my life. Everyday life cannot be cast in a heroic mould, yet I can still explore the infinite within our familiar craft. My humble duties allow for passion; restoring faith in happiness to those who have suffered is a noble task that will relieve the monotony of existence. There is little room for suffering and much good to be done. My love for Louis de l'Estorade does not make my heart race, but there is nothing in him that offends me.

My instincts for greatness remain, though concealed and directed to our family’s welfare. The time for a woman to seek admiration is short; I have chosen motherhood: my children will be my gods, this spot my Eldorado. Thank you for all you have sent; please consult the enclosed list. I intend to live with refinement, keeping from provincial life only what adds charm. Solitude never vulgarizes a woman. My father-in-law is delighted and is sending workmen from Paris to renovate the house.

X. Mlle. de Chaulieu to Mme. de L’Estorade — January

Oh, Renée, you have made me miserable. Your beauty, pride, grace, and goodness will be wasted in a dull marriage you will come to despise; your children will be monsters. You seem resigned, neither hopeful nor afraid. Yet what if a glorious morning brings the man who awakens you? At least you will have a friend; you will become the valley’s guardian angel and learn to love its monotony. As you walk with husband and children I foresee letters reflecting the same; I will remain your friend, untouched by social pettiness.

Monday. My Spaniard bewilders me: calm, severe dignity, solemn and silent—how odd that a language teacher fascinates me more than nobles or officers. His coldness both amuses and irks me. Yesterday something I said to M. Henarez struck him; he did not answer, and the lesson ended as if he might not return. That would suit me; I do not want a sentimental catastrophe. I’ve been reading Rousseau and find his moralizing passion detestable; Richardson’s Clarissa better captures English women. Love is for me a subjective poem; all written about it is both false and true. Since you will be authority on married love, I feel called to remain unmarried and seek a grand passion. Tell me every detail of your first days with that strange creature called a husband; I will do the same if I ever find love. Farewell, poor martyred darling.

XI. Mme. de L’Estorade to Mlle. de Chaulieu — La Crampade

Your Spaniard and you make me shudder. Dismiss him. Everything you describe fits a dangerous adventurer who risks all. He cannot be your husband or lover. I will write more about my married life when my heart is less anxious by your letter.

XII. Mlle. de Chaulieu to Mme. de L’Estorade — February

This morning my father visited, more thoughtful than usual. After flattering me and admitting he’d misjudged my intelligence, he launched into politics: France stands precarious—its Revolution persists in law and thought; the King is head without arms, nobles lack authority over elected men, and the family has ceased to exist—individuals and State coffers have replaced family spirit. He argued a state cannot stand without paternal authority and warned of calamity if our party fails. Half-joking, he asked me to persuade him to sacrifice my inheritance for my younger brother’s estate, then reminded me that a husband would expect a dowry.

Your letter made me reflect on the abyss you warned about. I took precautions concerning Henarez, whose gaze unsettles me. I hesitated to tell my father I wished no more lessons, yet find myself drawn to him. His voice thrills me; his manners are simple and genuine. After a lesson he almost reached to kiss my hand, then stopped, terrified. I smiled, understanding the courage it takes for a plebeian to love a noblewoman; his eyes were wet and I felt a strange happiness. He has fascinated me.

My dear Louise, I waited to write until I had learned many things for your happiness. The difference between a girl and a married woman is vast. I chose to marry Louis de l’Estorade rather than return to the convent. Marriage terrified me, yet I realized friendship might be the key to a happy union. I proposed we separate marriage from certain marital rights to preserve independence; he accepted. I asked to be left free, promising to attend to his feelings; he was overjoyed, and we married soon after.

I am nearly happy now, though I must conceal my full contentment because he is sensitive. For three months we lived much as before; I studied our relationship and saw how my presence changed him. I could bring new life to my husband before giving him children. His happiness brightened our home; even my father became generous. I manage the household with economy—seeking pleasure without excess—and harbor ambitious plans for Louis, hoping to see him in politics. He embraced my designs, showing the depth of his affection. To conclude, here is a letter he wrote expressing his love: he sees me as a treasure, and I feel proud of our relationship.

XIV. The Duc de Soria to the Baron de Macumer — Madrid

My dear brother, I did not make you Duc de Soria to betray the name. I cannot tolerate your exile while we enjoy happiness. Marie and I will delay marrying until you accept the money Urraca offers. We have prayed for your happiness; I believe Heaven will send you the love you seek. Marie and I will always be grateful to you. Write through the Nuncio. Farewell; take pride in the happiness you have brought us.

XV. Louise de Chaulieu to Mme. de L’Estorade — March

Ah, my friend, marriage has made a philosopher of you! Your prudent calculations trouble me. Do you think you can convert me to matrimony with cautious arithmetic? You have made a holocaust of your youth—becoming a miser before your time. If Louis loves you, he will never realize you act from necessity. Love is the first principle of all virtues; I cannot accept your justifying marriage by calculation. You act a man's part; Louis must play the woman! Your letter plunged me into thought; I see now the convent cannot replace a mother. Please, do not turn away from my cries!

Monday. Last night I looked at the stars and pictured my Spaniard in a tree by my window. He blew a letter to me through the window, but I burned it unread—it felt improper to read such an offering. At the opera he seemed sad; I learned he had renounced a rich heiress for another man’s sake. I was wretched for burning his letter—what might he have confessed? To be loved in secret, to know a man is devoted—how can I resist? Returning home I reopened my window, hoping he might be there. He was, and I received another letter, which I read and cherished.

XVI. The same to the same — March

I am dressed in white, camellias in my hair. I feel an odd desire to delay my decision and make him wait. What a vision—driving my barouche, seeing him, the thrill. I complimented him on his horse; he said it was a gift from his steward. Henarez is no longer just a simple man; he grows more distinguished. Do my love affairs amuse you? I am a little off my head and must stop.

XVII. Mme. de L’Estorade to Louise de Chaulieu — April

My angel, you have grieved me. You do not realize the force of the word indissoluble in marriage. Each marriage has laws shaped by place and character: a wife of a leader must resign to his guidance; a wife of a fool must take control. Passion can be admirable when it springs from the heart. We are answerable to God and ourselves for our happiness. A wife’s calculation is necessary to maintain happiness; a sublime hypocrisy may be required to reach the ideal of a perfect woman. I persist in my principles, convinced virtue varies with surroundings. If I wished to make Louis unhappy, I would leave him the helm entirely. I may not have married a man of distinction, but I hope to further him toward it. Five years from now we will see if I was mistaken. Farewell, my dear friend.

My dear, as I read these words, he seemed to rise before me, pale as the night when the camellias told their story and he knew his offering was accepted. These humble words, unlike usual lover’s flourishes, washed over me; it was the breath of happiness.

Wednesday evening.

I have just seen him at the Opera; he was another man. He came to our box, introduced by the Sardinian ambassador.

Anticipating this, men devised defenses against women; without them, their theories might be overturned by love or aversion. Marriage now embodies two opposing forces the law must reconcile. Toward morning he cried “Mamma!” in a strange voice and twitched. I rose for a drink; as I handed him the cup he was motionless, repeating “Mamma!” in a voice hardly a voice. His hand was stiff. He gulped water convulsively, clung to me, eyes rolling back, limbs rigid. I screamed for Louis.

“A doctor! Quick!... he is dying,” I cried.

Louis vanished. Armand gasped “Mamma!” then lost consciousness. Veins on his forehead swelled, and convulsions began. For an hour before doctors arrived I held my lifeless baby—my pride and joy. His eyes! I cannot think of them without horror. My pretty Armand was a mere shell—black, shriveled.

Two doctors from Marseilles hovered like birds of ill omen. One blamed brain fever, the other called them ordinary convulsions. Our country doctor offered no opinion. “Teething,” said one; “fever,” said the other. They agreed on leeches to the neck and ice to the head. To see a child like that—purple, silent—was horrible. They even cut his curls for the ice. The convulsions returned every ten minutes; the child writhed, white then violet, limbs clattering like wood. This unconscious flesh had smiled and said “Mamma!”—the thought pierced my soul.

After four days and nights, the doctors recommended a dreadful ointment to create open sores. Sores on my Armand! Five days earlier he had been playing and laughing. I refused, trusting nature. Louis, who believes doctors, scolded me. But in those moments when the disease mimicked death, I felt that remedy might save him. His skin was dry and rough; the ointment would not work. I wept; the doctors were at dinner!

Alone with him, I stripped off the medical appliances, pressed him to my bosom, and laid my forehead against his, begging God to spare him. For minutes I held him, longing to die with him. Then his limbs relaxed; the writhings ceased; the ghastly colors faded. I screamed; the doctors rushed in.

“He is saved!” cried the oldest doctor.

What music those words were! Two hours later Armand returned to life. I was crushed, saved only by the joy of his recovery. What tortures bind a mother to her child! Must the nails be driven to fasten him to my heart? Was I not mother enough? I who wept at his first words and steps—why these horrors?

Now Armand plays and laughs. What causes this dreadful disease in children? I ponder it, especially as I am again with child. Is it teething? A brain disorder? A nervous susceptibility? Our country doctor blames nervous trouble from teething. I would give every tooth in my head to see Armand’s through. One white pearl peeking from swollen gums brings a cold sweat. The heroism with which my angel bore suffering proves he is his mother’s son. I could walk forever with him in my arms!

Medical science offers no clear explanation for this tetanus that appears and vanishes swiftly, unpreventable and uncured. One thing is certain: it is hell for a mother to see her child in convulsions. How passionately I hold him now! To have endured this six weeks before my confinement made it worse; I feared for the coming child. Farewell, my beloved. Do not wish for a child—this is the essence of my letter!

Poor dear, Macumer and I forgave you upon hearing of your terrible trouble. I felt pain reading of your double agony; there seem compensations in being childless.

I write to tell you Louis has been promoted; he may now wear the ribbon of an Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, Renée—likely to have a little girl, as you wished!

My brother’s marriage to Mlle. de Mortsauf was celebrated on our return. Our gracious King, extraordinarily kind, has granted my brother the reversion of the post of first gentleman of the chamber, presently held by his father-in-law, on condition the Mortsauf scutcheon be placed alongside the Lenoncourt’s. “The office ought to go with the title,” he told the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry. My parents came from Madrid for the wedding and will return after the reception I am hosting for the newlyweds.

The carnival will be lively. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria are in Paris, which unnerves me. Marie Heredia is one of Europe’s most beautiful women, and I dislike the way Felipe looks at her. Therefore I am extra sweet and affectionate. Every look and gesture says what my lips do not: “She could not love like this!” Heaven knows how lovely I am! Yesterday Mme. de Maufrigneuse said, “Dear child, who can compete with you?”

I keep Felipe entertained, making his sister-in-law seem dull by comparison. I am less sorry that a little Abencerrage is not on the way, since the Duchess will likely remain in Paris for her confinement and lose her beauty. If the baby is a boy, he will be named Felipe in honor of the exile. Fate decrees I shall be godmother again.

Goodbye, dear. I plan to go to Chantepleurs early this year: our Italian tour was shockingly expensive. I will leave around the end of March to economize in Nivernais. Besides, I am tired of Paris. Felipe and I long for the tranquility of the park, cool meadows, and the sparkling Loire. Chantepleurs will be delightful after Italy; splendor becomes tiresome, and a lover’s gaze is more beautiful than any opera or painting!

He just told me (looking over my shoulder) that his sister‑in‑law, Princess Heredia—his childhood dream—has no brains. Oh, my dear, I am worse than a ballet dancer! Don Fernand, the Duc de Soria, suits her perfectly: generous but a spoiled child. I am tempted to be mischievous and make you laugh, but I will refrain. Much love, darling.

My dear Renée,

For the first time I am truly alone and in tears. I sat beneath a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh—pretty but desolate without children’s laughter. Nearly three years of marriage and no child; your house full of little ones forces me to examine my heart. I would endure any suffering to hold a cherub of my own—an Athenais of my own! I imagine her looks and sweetness, yet you tell me nothing—always discreet as ever. I am sure you share my distress.

Each disappointment plunges me into melancholy, from which I compose mournful elegies. When will I embroider caps and sew lace for a tiny head? Will I never hear “Mamma” or have a little despot tug at my dress? My gravel will lie untouched by carriage wheels, my courtyard free of broken toys. I long for a life to watch unfold—another Felipe, dearer still; a son to become my second self. My park and château seem cold and empty. A woman without children feels unnatural; we exist to be mothers. Barrenness is a bleak fate. If I cannot be a mother, I will at least indulge in romantic sorrow.

I told my Moor of this and he wept; he cannot bear jokes on his love. Sometimes I dream of pilgrimage to present my longings at some shrine. Next winter I shall consult a physician. I am too angry with myself to write more. Farewell.

In this solitary grange, minutes become hours depending on their health. My delightful hours come only when they sleep and I am no longer needed to rock or soothe them. When they lie beside me, I think, “Nothing can go wrong now.” Yet every mother is haunted by imagined dangers: razors, fires, ponds, the demon of convulsions. We spend our days on a stage of small dramas, tender and terrible.

At night I dream of their future, comforted by the watch of their guardian angel. Sometimes Armand calls in his sleep; I kiss his forehead and his sister’s feet and watch their beauty. Once an angel roused me to Athenais’ cradle—her head was too low and Armand’s feet purple with cold. How vital it is to have one’s children at night! A nurse cannot soothe them as a mother can after a nightmare. I have learned to sleep lightly, my ear tuned to every sigh or rustle by their bed.

Morning brings their chatter. Nais strains to reach me while Armand climbs up like a little monkey; they transform my bed into a playground and I am at their mercy. The baby tugs my hair and Armand guards my breast like a jealous sentinel. Their antics replace sleep; their laughter repays all care.

Then the dressing begins: dressing gown, apron, sleeves rolled up, and Mary’s help in a series of baths and scrubbings. Bath time is perilous—so much crying results from a misjudged temperature or a dropped brush. Amusement is necessary—paper boats and glass ducks—to get them to submit to the tub. I contrive diversions: prayers, scoldings, promises; motherhood becomes a game of cleverness matched to a child’s cunning. Our triumphs are private: Mary and I rejoice when, after a struggle, the children are at last clean and content. Only God, His angels, and perhaps you can grasp the satisfaction of such small victories.

English women excel at arranging material comforts; their nurses and systems are admirable. My children will always be bare‑legged in summer and in woolen socks in winter; no swaddling or tight bandages for them. A true mother, though, is never free; the helplessness of many French infants gives nurses latitude their charge’s mothers would not allow. That is why I do not write: besides managing the estate, I am raising two children, with a third on the way.

Motherhood demands silent self‑denial and attention to minute details—a mother blows on the spoon for her child’s soup, minces the chop, endures annoyances. No servant or foreign nurse can replace a mother in those daily contests where gentleness must soothe every little grief. Louise, caring for these darlings, engages my whole soul. A crying child condemns the nurse or mother unless pain is natural. With two to tend (and soon three), they occupy all my thoughts—sometimes even you, whom I adore, recede to a memory.

Often my dressing is not finished until mid‑afternoon. I distrust mothers whose rooms are immaculate and who always look perfect. Yesterday, in a lovely April, I planned an outing with the children—an epic for a mother. I had laid out Armand’s little black velvet jacket and Nais in white and pink; my heart was in my mouth. Once outside, proud of my work, I admired Armand’s princely air as he helped Nais along the path. Then a carriage approached and in our scramble the children tumbled into a puddle, wrecking my preparations. We had to return and change: a crying baby and a soaked child left me with no thought for myself.

At dinner I often accomplish nothing. Feeding two, securing bibs, turning up sleeves, cajoling food—these recurring cares, joys, and catastrophes make me neglect my own tidiness. To write you I have let them cut pictures from novels and arrange my silks; they are absorbed and content.

Yet I have nothing to complain of. My children are robust and inventive; they amuse themselves more readily than you might think. A little freedom and a few pebbles, shells, and sand do more for them than many toys. Armand talks to flowers, flies, and chickens; he imitates and admires insects and asks “why” about everything. It is touching to see his curiosity—he even thinks of you, his godmother, as a fairy.

Let me not sadden you with all my joys. A poor man once followed us begging—beggars know a mother with a child cannot resist. Armand, innocent of hunger or money, offered the beggar the trumpet I had bought him: “Here, take this!” That moment of spontaneous charity was incomparable. The poor man, moved, kept it, saying, “I too, madame, have had children.” I wept at the sweetness.

I shudder at the thought of Armand going to school: I have only three and a half years before public institutions tear him away. The bright flowers of his sunny childhood will be cut by the scythe of education; his gracious candor may be lost. What will they do to the thinking being he is, to the curls I brushed and kissed so often? And you—are you still in love with Felipe? I have no fear of the Saracen. Goodbye; Nais has tumbled, and this letter threatens to become a volume.

My sweet, tender Renée, you will have learned from the papers of the terrible calamity that has crushed me. I could not write for twenty days; I did not leave his bedside. I received his last breath, closed his eyes, kept vigil with the priests, and said the prayers for the dead. He is gone and I am bereft. To you, who knew us both, what more need be said? I would give my share of Heaven to bring him back even for a second. Will you not come soon and soothe me with such impossible promises? You told me beforehand that he would suffer through me. Was it so? Yes; I had no right to all his love. Like a thief I took what belonged to him and in my frenzied grasp crushed the life out of my bliss. The madness is over, but I feel alone and crushed. Merciful God! what torture can surpass the misery in that single word: alone?

When they took him away I lay on the same bed hoping to die. There was but a door between us and I thought I could force it. I was too young for death; after forty days of cruel care and attention, I was slowly brought back to life. Now I sit in the country by a window, surrounded by the flowers he had loved and made flourish, looking at the view he admired. Ah, Renée, how new surroundings wound when the heart is dead. His flowers torment me; to walk in my garden is to walk upon him. When I first ventured out I trembled and could not move.

My parents are in Spain, my brothers far off, and you detained in the country. But two angels of mercy comfort me: the Duc and Duchesse de Soria hurried to their brother in his illness and have been everything I could wish. The last nights found us gathered in mute grief around this great man—rare in any age, distinguished by mind and heart. Felipe’s calm resignation was angelic. His last words to me were simple and frank about his will and his love; he blessed me on his lips and spoke of my memory more fondly than any other. His brother forgave him, knowing what love is.

I owe my life to my brother‑in‑law and his wife; they wish to take me to Spain. To no one but you can I freely pour out my grief. A sense of my own faults sits heavy: my demands, jealousy, and anxious unrest may have worn him down. My love was dangerous because we were so alike in sensitivity; my careless words pierced him to the heart. You cannot imagine how submissive he was: I only had to tell him to leave me alone, and he obeyed without a murmur. His last breath blessed me, saying that a single morning with me was worth more than a lifetime with another. My tears fall as I write.

This is my life now: rising at midday, going to bed early, lingering over meals, standing motionless before a plant, gazing into the trees, finding significance in trifles. I seek shade, silence, and night. I drag through each hour, taking a gloomy pride in surviving them. My park gives me company enough; everything there echoes my vanished joy.

If I still live it is because Heaven tempers grief to the strength of those who bear it. Only a woman can know what it is to lose a love that was genuine—one that satisfied spirit and flesh. How rare a man so gifted that worship does not feel debasing! If such fortune befalls us once, we cannot hope to find it again. Men of true greatness, veiled by poetic grace, charm with a high spiritual power—beware of love: it may ruin you and the woman you love. That is my cry as I walk the paths of my wood.

And he has left me no child! That love, rich in smiles and tenderness, left no fruit. I feel cursed. Is it that intense, single‑hearted passion bears no children? Is only a marriage of reason blessed with progeny? Such wild questions haunt me.

You are the one person whose company I could bear. Come to me; none but you should help Louise in her sorrow. The day I first donned my widow’s bonnet—when I first saw myself in black—I fell back and wept until night. I weep again at the memory. Bring your children; you can nurse your baby here without making me jealous; what is gone is gone, and I will be glad to see my godson. Felipe wished for a child like little Armand. Come, then—come and help me bear this woe.

So, after two years of silence, you wonder why I have written so little. My dear Renée, words fail to express my happiness. We bear everything with ease; we are in perfect harmony. There has been no discord in our feelings, and each day brings its joy. We do not fear monotony; rather we regret that our time together may not suffice for all the expressions of our love. We take more pleasure in each other's company than on the first day, and every hour reveals new reasons to love one another.

Every evening we speak of visiting Paris, of the new boulevards and the finished Madeleine, but in the morning we linger over breakfast and take a siesta. Gaston gazes at me as if I were a painting, and we both shed tears of joy at our love. I still play the mistress, affecting to give less than I receive; I delight in the little coquetries of our life. Passion tempered by tenderness is sweet; Gaston, my master, remains a timid suitor at my command.

You asked me to describe him, yet it is impossible to portray the man we love without emotion. Society alters men so that the public man can differ from the man of deep feeling. The charming suitor may lack what a woman values, while an unrefined man may possess a profound capacity for love. Our ideal is a man whose outward appearance aligns with his true nature—a grace that cannot be acquired. This ideal haunts every woman's imagination, and in Gaston I have found it.

I never knew love could be so intertwined with youth, talent, and beauty. Gaston is unpretentious, moving with effortless grace. When we walk together our bodies seem to fit perfectly, a single figure gliding through the woods. Our thoughts, desires, and words harmonize. Sometimes we walk in silence, listening to the rain and admiring the sunset, feeling as if in silent prayer for our happiness. A timid kiss exchanged in nature feels like an act of worship.

We return home each time more in love than before. Such passion between older married people would scandalize Paris; only in the woods can we express it freely.

Gaston is of average height, well-proportioned, and agile. He has a fair complexion, dark hair, and a striking resemblance to Louis XIII. His honorable poverty has spared him the vices that ruin many young men. His teeth are magnificent, and he has a robust constitution. His blue eyes, full of tenderness for me, can flash with intensity at inspiring thoughts. He has a remarkably even temperament; I have heard of men burdened with self-discontent, but he is free from such troubles. I have learned that marrying an older man can lead to unequal unions, and I urge you not to let Athénaïs marry one.

His smile is a treasure—always fresh, full of unspoken love and gratitude. Our shared experiences are part of our joy; even the smallest details of nature recall our time together. We seem like two editions of the same book, united in thought and pleasure.

Despite these delights, life here is laborious. I do my own room, refusing a hired hand to intrude on my privacy. I rise early, bathe in cold water before he wakes, and emerge like a newly opened flower; he appreciates this freshness without knowing the effort. My maid handles daily grooming, and I dress several times a day for Gaston’s delight. We take great interest in our flowers, tending a garden that supplies abundance and pride, and the gardeners sell surplus produce.

To keep Gaston's mind engaged, I encourage him to finish plays he began in harder times; I assist him so our winter evenings are never dull. Our servants live easily; they present us as a content traveling couple.

I have learned true jealousy—the kind born of love. The thought of him ceasing to love me sends shivers through me. Alas, I am still without a child. I long for the laughter of children in our garden; it pains me to see your lovely brood, for motherhood expands a woman’s heart and I envy your happiness.

As I approach thirty, I feel my beauty waning. Though still attractive, I fear aging while Gaston remains young, and often ask him to promise he will tell me if his love ever fades; he swears it will not.

Goodbye, dear friend. Shall we let years pass without writing again? Happiness can be monotonous, and perhaps that is why Dante seems greater in the Paradiso than in the Inferno. I am not Dante, only your friend, and I do not wish to bore you. Write to me—your children supply endless stories—while I... No more of this. A thousand loves.

My dear Louise, I have read your letter repeatedly; its tone is childish. Pure love belongs to nature and is distorted by civilization. Society built the family as the foundation of organized life at the cost of individual desires. A married man dedicates himself to his wife, who benefits from his efforts. The dream of being adored suits the young, not a mature woman. Vanity may enjoy adoration, but true fulfillment lies in the life of wife and mother. Return to Paris; fear the loss of ordinary happiness, not hardship.

Life’s simple pleasures—bread, air, silence—sustain us. Passionate love exhausts; motherhood’s affections endure and reward. I chose self‑sacrifice and would choose it again. Your life, masked by romance, is rooted in selfishness; I plead with you as I watch your happiness tested. Life seeks balance; excess brings pain or fleeting pleasure. You are ill because you cling to a passion a marriage should steady. The glory of home is calmness and shared life, which you overlook. You, more passionate than tender, would be both wife and mistress, undermining marriage’s sanctity. If you could understand a mother’s sweet self‑discipline—my proud nature softened into gentle melancholy, rewarded by motherhood’s joys—time might restore your reason. Farewell; I despair that my letter, meant to reconcile you with society, only revealed what I see as selfishness.

Renee, calamity has struck—doubt is my torment. The day before yesterday I noticed Gaston gone and his mare Fedelta sweating, implying a secret trip to Paris. Suspicion seized me. When he returned he carried me to the parlor; I fled to weep. He teased about mud on Fedelta, reassured me when I asked if I bored him, yet I remained unconvinced. He suggested keeping Fedelta; his responses made me feel inadequate. Later he left with a whip he brought back from Verdier—a small confirmation of my fear.

Gaston changed: he worked harder, his drawer was empty, he tore up letters. Jealousy consumed me; I followed him to the post office and confronted him about a letter from Nathan announcing a play’s acceptance. I suspected a rival and investigated. I followed him to Paris and found he visited a woman—his brother’s widow. The discovery shattered me: he had been secretly providing for her and her children. I returned tormented by jealousy and betrayal, still loving him and fearing the truth would destroy me. I even contemplated ending my life.

My dear Louise, your last letter alarmed me and I confided in Louis; we involved the police discreetly. Gaston is not the villain you feared. His brother Louis died in Calcutta, leaving a widow and two children; Gaston has been supporting them out of duty and kindness. The woman you mistook for a rival is a mother in need. You should love him more and welcome the widow and children—perhaps adopt the boys. This should bring you peace.

Ah, my friend, it is too late for me. My actions have sealed my fate. Tormented by love for Gaston, I cannot bear losing him. I am filled with despair and long for relief. Come, dear friend, and witness my decline.

My love,—Take the children to Provence without me; I must stay with Louise, who has only a few days left. I cannot leave her or her husband, for whom I worry.

You know the letter that sent me rushing to Ville d'Avray, gathering doctors along the way. Since then I have not left my dear friend, sitting up every night for two weeks.

When I arrived, Louise and Gaston seemed charming and happy, but it was a façade. She whispered, "He must not know; I am dying." Her hand burned and her cheeks were flushed. I kept the doctors in the woods until needed.

Alone with me, she collapsed into my arms and confessed. She had deliberately brought herself to the last stage of consumption in a few days—by inducing heavy night sweats and lying in the dew by the lake. Gaston thought it a cold. I rushed to fetch the physicians.

After consultation none of the doctors gave hope; they believe Louise will die "with the fall of the leaves." Her strong constitution had prolonged the deception, but a few days of folly had made her case desperate.

I have lived in Louise as much as in my own life; the news crushed me. Yet she received it with a strange calm. She told me to stay with her to the end; she would not have sad faces around her and wished to keep the truth from Gaston. "I know I have the strength to do it," she said. "I am full of youth and spirit, and can die standing! I have no regrets. I am dying as I wished, still young and beautiful, in the perfection of my womanhood."

She reflected that for Gaston she might have made life miserable: her jealousy had already wounded him, and indifference would have been her death. She felt her thirty intense years equaled much longer lives. For him and herself the end was, paradoxically, happy; for me it was an irreparable loss—"You alone have the right to mourn my death."

More gravely she drew a lesson: marriage cannot rest on passion alone. Addressing me she praised my noble way of giving my husband growing affection while her own eager passion diminished. She accepted death with the dignity of one who had known two noble men—one of mind, one of form—and died adored. She admitted perhaps she had thought too little of God, and hoped her spirit would be reunited with the two who made her life a heaven.

She warned that her example might tempt others to expect impossible felicities, then, with her characteristic disdain for the social fuss about women's emancipation, declared women weak and foolish to make such clamour; they should yield more to their husbands.

She spoke gently, even prettily, until Gaston entered with his sister-in-law, the children, and the English nurse from Paris. Louise greeted the nephews with playful tenderness and played hostess to Mme. Gaston, insisting the chalet be hers. I wrote to the Duc and Duchesse de Chaulieu, the Duc de Rhetore, the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry, and to Madeleine. The next day Louise, exhausted, could not go out; Madeleine, her brothers, and her mother arrived. The coolness caused by Louise's second marriage melted; her family visited daily and duchesses spent evenings at the chalet. Death unites as well as separates; it silenced petty feelings.

To the last, Louise remained charming, graceful, witty, tender—a Parisian queen to the end. "I want to look well even in my coffin," she said with that matchless smile, lying on the bed where she would linger for a fortnight. Her room lacked the usual sick-chamber air; medicines and nursing supplies were hidden. She asked the priest, "Is not my deathbed pretty?"

Gaston bore the blow bravely but deeply wounded. I would not be surprised if he followed her soon. Yesterday by the lake he said, "I must be a father to those two children," gesturing to his sister-in-law and the boys, and asked me to be a second mother to them and to persuade my husband to accept guardianship. He wore smiles for Louise, but I saw through the façade.

August 25th (her birthday). Yesterday evening Louise was delirious briefly, but even her babble was beautiful—a proof that the madness of gifted people differs from that of fools. In a faint voice she sang Italian airs from I Puritani, La Sonnambula, Moïse. We stood around her bed in silence; not one of us, not even the Duc de Rhetore, had dry eyes, as we felt her soul departing. She could no longer see us, yet the charm lingered in the melody.

During the night the death agony began. It is now seven in the morning; I had just raised her from bed. A flicker of strength returned; she wished to sit by the window and asked for Gaston's hand. Then, my love, the sweetest spirit we shall ever see on this earth departed, leaving us with an empty shell.

The last sacrament had been administered the evening before, without Gaston's knowledge—he had been sleeping during the ceremony. After she was set at the window, she asked me to read the De Profundis in French, facing the lovely scene she had created. She repeated the words after me and pressed her husband's hands.

August 26th.

My heart is broken. I have just seen her in her winding-sheet; her face is pale with purple shadows. Oh! I want my children! Bring me my children!
