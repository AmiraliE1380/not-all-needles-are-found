**LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES**

**FIRST PART**

**I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.**  
My dearest Renee, I am free! I hope you have escaped the convent too. My release came from my aunt's concern for my health after your departure. In Paris, I feel our souls are intertwined, but without you, I felt trapped. My aunt cannot understand my longing for life outside the convent.

Upon arriving, I found no one waiting for me, and the emptiness of my grandmother's home overwhelmed me. Philippe, her servant, assured me my inheritance was intact, but my father's failure to restore our family home filled me with despair.

As I settled in, I found comfort in memories and resolved to make this space my own. I long to hear about your life.

**October 19th.**  
I met my mother for the first time since my return. She treated me as an equal, expressing her desire for friendship. My father was affectionate, and I felt a sense of belonging. I am preparing for my debut into society with a generous allowance and a governess, Miss Griffith.

**II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.**  
I am starting to feel at home. My father’s allowance allows me to acquire new clothes for my social debut. Miss Griffith is a good companion, and my family seems busy, leaving me to discover my path.

**III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.**  
I am ready to enter society! After much preparation, I feel confident in my appearance. I plan to present myself as charming and innocent. Despite my excitement, I regret my past life in the convent. The transition is overwhelming, and my family remains distant. I hope to uncover the mysteries of my family and the society I am about to enter.

On December 15th, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day. Despite my carefully curated appearance, I received little attention from passersby, except for my approving parents. Griffith scanned the crowd indifferently while I grew frustrated that one man inspected my carriage without noticing me, realizing that beauty in Paris is more common than I expected.

The men I observed were generally unattractive and lacked elegance, particularly in their hats. I commented to Miss Griffith about their looks, but she defended them as cultivated.

At an evening ball, I stayed close to my mother, who attracted all the attention while I was merely a pretext for compliments. My dress, which I thought stunning, paled in comparison to the married women’s finery. The men at the ball did not impress me; they appeared worn and lacked the proud bearing of their ancestors. One man stood out for his beauty and intellect but seemed self-absorbed, suggesting that intellectuals often prioritize ambition over genuine connections.

Determined not to let society's indifference affect me, I focused on enjoying the dance. Upon returning home, I shared my experiences with my mother, who advised me to be discreet about my feelings, highlighting the complexities of feminine duplicity.

After arriving at the Castle of Maucombe, my family dined with M. de l'Estorade, whose son had returned from travels through Europe after evading conscription. My parents support a match between me and Louis de l'Estorade, especially since I would be acceptable without a dowry. I have agreed to marry him, contingent upon my ability to improve his estate.

In his letters, Don Felipe Henarez reassures his brother of his safety amid Spain's chaos, reflecting on their ancestors' massacre. He laments the decline of his estates and urges Fernand to claim his titles, using his affection for Marie Heredia as leverage. Felipe plans to keep his mother’s diamonds for independence and offers Fernand a wedding gift from his limited savings.

In a subsequent letter, Felipe reflects on his modest life in France, teaching Spanish and finding solace in nature. He expresses no regrets about his abdication, feeling detached from his former wealth. He emphasizes the importance of keeping his identity as Henarez a secret from Marie.

Louise de Chaulieu writes to Renee de Maucombe, expressing disbelief at her quick engagement and warning against marriage's constraints. She shares her disillusionment with society and men while remaining hopeful for love. In her second letter, she reveals her interest in Don Henarez, grappling with her emotions and societal expectations.

Mlle. de Chaulieu worries that her friend Renee's marriage will suppress her spirit, urging her to seek true passion over societal expectations. She is intrigued by M. Henarez, a melancholic Spanish teacher, despite their social differences. Mme. de L'Estorade warns her about him, calling him a dangerous adventurer and hinting at her own marital issues.

In February, Mlle. de Chaulieu contemplates her father's political ambitions and her conflicting desires. She acknowledges her feelings for M. Henarez, admiring his charm and intellect. Mme. de L'Estorade discusses the contrasts between single and married life, stressing the value of friendship and independence.

Mlle. de Chaulieu critiques Mme. de L'Estorade's pragmatic marriage views, insisting love should guide choices. After a romantic encounter with M. Henarez, she decides to burn a letter from him, symbolizing her emotional conflict. As she prepares for an important evening, she reflects on love's power and her connection with him.

She expresses excitement about a new carriage and her feelings for M. Henarez, feeling empowered by love. Mme. de L'Estorade counters, emphasizing marriage's seriousness and the need for responsibility. Mlle. de Chaulieu grapples with her feelings for Felipe, longing for clarity in their relationship amidst the complexities of love and respect.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In her letter, Renee expresses dissatisfaction with marriage, feeling it enforces subservience on women and reduces them to possessions. She laments her emotional sacrifices and hopes motherhood will bring fulfillment, noting that her husband, Louis, is unaware of her struggles and benefits from her sacrifices.

Louise grapples with her feelings for Felipe, wanting to test his character through a secret meeting in her garden. She wishes for Renee's company to share in the excitement. Their encounter is charged with unspoken emotions, as Louise voices concerns about their relationship while Felipe vows to prove his worth.

Louise expresses disappointment over Felipe's lack of appreciation for Racine's _Berenice_, fearing that if he does not see her as unique, she will feel diminished. She warns that any sign of reduced passion would lead her to despair. Later, she shares her engagement joy with Felipe, who has accepted her family's wishes. Despite her concerns, Renee wishes to support Louise's happiness. Through their letters, themes of love, loyalty, and societal expectations are explored, highlighting the emotional complexities of relationships.

Dear friend, it has been nearly eight months since our marriage, and your silence is inexcusable.

We traveled to the Castle of Chantepleurs, purchased by Macumer in Nivernais, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants arrived ahead, and we reached the castle the following evening. I slept during the journey, comforted by Felipe's tender presence. His affectionate gestures deeply touched me, and I awoke to find his passionate gaze unchanged. Despite his calm demeanor, he later revealed intense emotions he had kept hidden.

As we settled into our new life, I found that marriage was not as daunting as I expected. Felipe reassured me of his fidelity, and I felt a mix of joy and complexity in love. True happiness in marriage is often unspoken, overshadowed by duty. I admire your strength, Renee, and see that those who marry for love, like me, experience a different reality than those who marry for convenience, like you.

We spent seven and a half blissful months at Chantepleurs, akin to lovers escaping the world. Now, we are in Paris, enjoying a lovely home in the Rue du Bac. I feel more beautiful and content than ever.

I wish to understand your heart, as I suspect you hide your own wounds. I long for you to find joy with Louis, who adores you. Remember our youthful vows to share burdens? I now pray for your happiness as fervently as you once prayed for mine.

I have an idea: encourage Louis to pursue a political career. If he becomes a deputy, we can be together in Paris, and perhaps he will gain a title. This could be a new beginning for both of us.

In January 1826, Louise de Macumer writes to her friend Renee de l'Estorade, excited about her upcoming visit to Chantepleurs in April and her role as godmother to Renee's first child. She insists Felipe must be the godfather. Louise reflects on their different lives—Renee as a mother and herself as childless—finding joy in their roles despite her desire for motherhood.

Louise describes her vibrant life with Felipe, noting society's perception of her beauty since their marriage. Their first quarrel arises when she wishes Felipe to end her life at thirty, which he refuses.

Renee responds nearly five months later, apologizing for her delayed reply. She shares her emotional journey during childbirth and the overwhelming love for her child, contrasting it with her initial fears. Renee highlights the deep bond created through nursing and notes her feelings for Louis have shifted to profound gratitude. The letters illustrate the contrasting yet complementary experiences of two friends navigating love and motherhood.

**Summary of Correspondence Between Mme. de Macumer and Mme. de L'Estorade (March - July 1826)**

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (March 1826)**  
I apologize for my late reply, assuming your silence meant consent about the luncheon service heading to Marseilles. Life in Paris is overwhelming, but my love for Felipe is my refuge. I’ve gained a fashionable reputation, pleasing him, while my family secures our legacy through my brother’s marriage. Society's initial criticism of my marriage has turned to admiration, yet the attention can be intoxicating. I long for a true friend and look forward to seeing you in May.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Motherhood keeps me busy with my son, Armand-Louis. I look forward to your visit and hope you find my home welcoming despite the challenges of a teething baby.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (April 1826)**  
I’m pleased to inform you that your father-in-law is now Comte de l'Estorade.

**Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (July 1826)**  
I left abruptly due to jealousy over Felipe’s attention to you, which highlighted my insecurities. I’m returning to Chantepleurs, hoping to find fulfillment.

**Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer**  
Your departure surprised me; you had no reason to feel jealous. I urge you to return, as Felipe respects you deeply. Embrace your role as a supportive wife to maintain his dignity and pursue true happiness. 

The Vicomtesse de L'Estorade mentions a reply waiting for her at Chantepleurs and expresses concern over the government's impending dissolution, concluding with affectionate remarks about the Baronne's influence in Italy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

I received your distressing letter, forwarded by my steward. Renee, I won’t dwell on my hurt feelings but will share the impact of your words.

We had just returned from a lovely reception hosted by the ambassador, where I felt radiant, and Macumer was swept away in love. Upon reading your harsh response, I sobbed, risking my composure. Felipe, my dear Arab, declared you were mistaken. He took me to the balcony, professing his love in Italian, insisting that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade our moments together for a mundane existence with children.

While he whispered sweet nothings, I fainted but am well now. This morning, I knelt before Felipe, confessing my fears and asking him to guide me if I ever strayed from his desires. Your letter frightened me, for you are a true friend.

Thank you, Renee. Your words revealed the depth of Macumer's love. Rome is the city of love, where passion thrives alongside art and faith.

We will soon meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Venice. If you write, please address it to Paris, as we leave Rome shortly.

Your letter unsettled me. You and Felipe seem like children. His love for you may resemble a man’s affection for a courtesan—lavish yet knowing she is unfaithful. I draw a harsh lesson from our differing fates: do not love if you wish to be loved.

Louis received the cross of the Legion of Honor upon his election to the provincial Council nearly three years ago. My father has requested the rank of Officer of the Legion for him, and I hope you can assist in this matter.

Ah, Louise, I have endured hell. Five days of torment felt like centuries. My son Armand was unusually irritable, and I feared he was dying. Miraculously, he began to recover, but I was left utterly drained. I fear for my unborn child.

Dear Renee, Macumer and I forgave your earlier naughtiness upon hearing of your distress. Your double agony resonated with me, and I see some compensation in being childless.

I write to inform you that Louis has been promoted to Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, and perhaps you will have a daughter, as you wished.

My brother married Mlle. de Mortsauf upon our return. The King has granted him a prestigious post. My parents visited for the wedding and will return to Madrid after my reception tomorrow. 

I plan to retreat to Chantepleurs after March to economize, as our Italian tour was costly. Felipe and I long for the tranquility of the countryside. We look forward to your visit, and I assure you, I will not be jealous.

My daughter, Jeanne-Athenais, is two months old, with my mother and Louis' grand-uncle as her godparents. Your godson can now say your name, calling it _Matoumer_. I worry about him while I recover from childbirth. My father noticed Felipe looks thin, and I sense something is troubling you, as your letter lacks warmth. Please write me a long letter.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For the first time, I find myself alone and crying under a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh. Married for nearly three years without a child, I yearn for motherhood, imagining the joy of a child like your Athenais. Each disappointment deepens my melancholy, prompting me to compose elegies and dream of motherhood—embroidering caps, choosing baby clothes, and hearing "Mamma" from my own child. My home feels desolate without children, and I feel unnatural as a childless woman. I long to express my sorrow, and even my Moor was moved to tears by my plight. I sometimes feel the urge to go on a pilgrimage for solace. Next winter, I plan to consult a doctor about my situation.

A year has passed without a letter from you, which pains me. Your husband Louis visits often, but I miss your thoughts and feelings. Your seclusion in the country during a time when you could enjoy Paris and your husband's political success worries me. I hope you will come soon, as I want to know more about your life and family.

You complain of my silence, but my children keep me busy. I didn’t want to bring them to Paris while expecting another child. I plan to buy a house in Paris with money from Louis' uncle and my mother. My days revolve around caring for my children, and I find joy in their sleep, planning their futures. Motherhood requires self-denial and devotion; I cannot trust anyone else to care for them as I do.

I am overwhelmed with grief after losing Felipe. I stayed by his side until the end, and now I am left alone, haunted by memories. I regret the jealousy I placed on him and live in a haze of sorrow, questioning if our passion is destined to be barren. I invite you to visit me, as your presence would bring comfort.

Dear Renee,

By the time you read this, I will be near you. Louis must remain in Provence for the elections. I come not to comfort but to share my heart. Weep, for tears bring joy in reuniting with him. I have sold my house and properties, but I assure you, I am neither mad nor ruined. After settling everything, I have about twelve hundred thousand francs left. I invested a million in the Three per Cents at fifty, yielding an income of sixty thousand francs, which is better than the thirty thousand from my properties.

The real reason for my actions is love. I am to be married soon in a secret ceremony near Paris. My beloved, Marie Gaston, has been courting me for nearly two years. Despite our age difference and his financial struggles, I am ready to end our long restraint. I have purchased a secluded property near Ville d'Avray, designed for our solitude.

In two days, I will become Madame Gaston. I am filled with both joy and sorrow, knowing my love for Gaston is immense, yet I will miss you dearly. Please address future correspondence to me as Madame Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles, to keep our lives a secret.

Farewell, my dear friend. The pain of losing you is as profound as my love for Gaston.

Dear Daniel,

I need you and Joseph Bridau as witnesses for my wedding tomorrow. My future wife prefers seclusion, which is why we’ve seen little of each other. After the wedding, we will be apart for a while, but I trust our friendship can endure this absence. She has sacrificed her closeness with a childhood friend for me, and I must do the same.

Our love is profound and genuine, built on mutual understanding. However, I feel a burden of shame; she is wealthy, while I am not. Your support has been crucial during my struggles, and I feel humiliated by our circumstances. This shame reflects my love, as I willingly accept it.

Despite this, my dreams are fulfilled. She is noble, intelligent, and beautiful, embodying the ideal woman. At twenty-seven, she is a widow without children, possessing both energy and moments of melancholy. Our love is pure and has flourished without succumbing to physical desire, reflecting our mutual respect. We have both faced disappointments, and she acknowledges her previous marriage as a mistake.

You, my friend, are a giant among men, and I hope you can appreciate the happiness that awaits me.

---

Louise, are you truly planning a life of solitude after your first marriage? You seem to be inviting misery upon yourself. It appears you have found someone who has changed your mind about remarrying, but have you forgotten your previous criticisms of men?

I cannot blame you for seeking happiness, though I fear for you. Absolute happiness often invites disaster, and I sense your joy may be too heavy to bear. I hope you will share more about this man you love.

I have not yet told Louis about your marriage plans, as it would only invite his ridicule. You are brave to face winter alone, but I will support you as your discreet friend. Your journey into this emotional wilderness is fraught with danger, yet I wish you a safe return.

---

What has happened to you, dear? After two years of silence, I worry about Louise. Is this what love does? It seems to overshadow our friendship. I miss your letters and your presence.

Regarding our situation, I want to address your comments on our political circumstances. We have chosen to remain in the Audit Department for our children's future. Louis has risen in the new order, becoming a peer and a respected figure in politics. I refused an embassy offer to stay in Paris for our children's education.

I am a happy mother, finding joy in my children. Armand is thriving at school, and I am proud of his achievements. However, I worry about Nais's future and the uncertainties of marriage. The thought of her being handed over to someone unworthy fills me with dread. Louise, your silence has reminded me of these fears. Please write back soon; I miss you dearly.

After two years, I feel compelled to share my happiness with you. Our love has flourished, bringing us daily joy. While we consider a trip to Paris, our leisurely mornings and intimate moments keep us content in our secluded life.

Gaston, my beloved, has a well-proportioned figure and natural grace, with dark hair and a fair complexion that gives him a tragic beauty. He is free from vices, possessing a steady temperament, and his smile brings me joy.

Despite my happiness, I struggle with jealousy and the absence of children weighs heavily on me. As I approach thirty, I feel the pressure of time, while Gaston remains youthful.

I hope we won’t let years pass without communication again. While my happiness may seem monotonous, I cherish our friendship and look forward to hearing about your life and children.

With all my love,  
Your friend.

Dear Louise,

Your recent letter reveals a naive view of love that concerns me. True love has evolved into the commitment of family life, requiring sacrifices from both partners. Marriage demands devotion and energy, often at the expense of personal desires.

You seek adoration, a fleeting dream of youth, but as a mature woman, your focus should shift to the responsibilities of wifehood and motherhood. I urge you to return to Paris; it is not misfortune you should fear, but the pursuit of happiness that may lead you astray.

Life's simple pleasures sustain us. Passionate love can be intoxicating but ultimately exhausting. I chose a life of self-sacrifice, and despite its challenges, I would make the same choice again. Your romantic notions seem rooted in selfishness, and I hope you reconsider your path.

Life is about balance; excess leads to suffering. You are unwell because you cling to a passion that marriage should temper. The true glory of home lies in calmness and shared experiences, which you seem to overlook. You risk degrading the sanctity of marriage by pursuing your impulses without restraint.

If only you could experience the joys of family life, you might understand the lasting happiness it brings. I despair that my attempts to share my happiness with you have only highlighted your selfishness. Your love for Gaston seems more about your needs than his true self.

Renee, calamity has struck! My heart is heavy with doubt. I discovered Gaston’s mare, Fedelta, covered in mud from Paris, leading me to suspect he had gone there without telling me. Jealousy consumed me, and I struggled to regain my composure.

When Gaston found me distressed, he reassured me of his love, but I remained unconvinced. Our day together was tense, and I foolishly considered selling Fedelta out of jealousy. When Gaston left for Paris, I felt trapped in a hell of possibilities.

He returned late, bringing me a whip as a gift, but I sensed it was a cover for something deeper. My jealousy grew, and I resolved to remain vigilant for any signs of betrayal. Days passed, and Gaston worked diligently, but I noticed he was increasingly secretive. I followed him to the post office, where he received a letter revealing a play of his had been accepted. Yet, I couldn't shake the feeling that he was hiding something from me.

I discovered his drawer was empty, and I suspected he needed money for a woman. My heart sank as I realized I might have a rival. I confirmed my worst fears: Gaston was involved with a woman, a large Englishwoman who was the mother of his children. I felt utterly betrayed.

Despite my heartbreak, my love for Gaston remained strong. I hesitated to confront him about the other woman, feeling trapped in my emotions.

Dear Louise,

I write urgently, having shared your distressing letter with Louis. We took measures to protect you, including discreetly involving the police.

Gaston is not the man you fear. His brother, Louis, died in India, leaving behind a widow and two children. Gaston has been secretly supporting them, which explains his trips to Paris. The woman you suspect is not a rival but his sister-in-law, and he has been providing for her and the children out of love for his brother.

You should find comfort in this revelation and consider embracing this family. Plan a surprise for him, and let this new understanding bring you peace.

Oh, dear friend, it is too late for me. My own actions have led to my despair. I am tormented by my feelings and cannot bear the thought of losing Gaston. I long for relief from this pain. Come to me, for I am in need of your support.

In a letter dated August 7th, the Comtesse de L'Estorade explains her decision to stay with her dying friend, Louise, instead of traveling to Provence. Louise, suffering from advanced consumption, is aware her time is limited. Although she appears cheerful around Gaston, she confides in the Comtesse about her impending death.

Upon arriving at Ville d'Avray, the Comtesse sees Louise's health deteriorating. Louise wants to keep her illness a secret from Gaston, believing it will help her maintain dignity. She reflects on her life, acknowledging that her passionate nature has contributed to her demise and recognizing the futility of jealousy in her marriage.

Despite her illness, Louise retains her charm, even joking about wanting to look good in her coffin. The Comtesse notes how Louise's spirit captivates those around her, even as she experiences delirium.

On August 26th, the Comtesse witnesses Louise's final moments. After a brief resurgence of strength, Louise passes away peacefully, creating a profound sense of loss. The Comtesse reflects on the void left by Louise's death and the deep connections formed through love and loss.
