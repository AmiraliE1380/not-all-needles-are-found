DEDICATION

To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement for my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval lends credibility to my efforts. Your dedication to research has sustained me in my labor on modern civilization. I hope this token of respect finds you well in Dobling, and reminds you of your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stands the house known as the Hotel d’Esgrignon, residence of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. What began as a jest in naming became earnest through time. The d’Esgrignon family, descended from powerful Northmen, kept a proud lineage, never bowing to kings or commoners. They were provincial nobles, revered locally, maintaining status despite long neglect by the court. Their history featured marriages without dowries and a steadfast refusal of titles that would diminish their nobility.

During the Revolution the d’Esgrignons were imperiled. The Marquis, respected in the countryside, avoided emigration but had to hide. Family lands were sold, yet Mlle. d’Esgrignon managed to save some property through the family steward, Chesnel. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a dilapidated castle, mourning lost heritage. The old Hotel d’Esgrignon had been demolished; Chesnel used the last of the Marquis’s money to purchase a new home for the family, humorously named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 emigres began returning, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married. She died in childbirth, leaving a son to carry the family name. Grief burdened the Marquis, now confronting his diminished circumstances. Mlle. d’Esgrignon, noted for her beauty, was proposed to by a local contractor — an offer she and her brother found insulting. This incident drove a wedge between them and Chesnel, who had acted on the contractor’s behalf.

Chesnel, a respected notary, had always been more than a servant: confidant, manager, and protector of family interests. The altered dynamics troubled him; the Marquis saw Chesnel as loyal companion, while Chesnel felt the strain of fidelity to a declining house. Politically, the era shifted as some nobles aligned with Napoleon and others remained monarchist. The d’Esgrignons held fast to their principles.

By 1804 many emigres returned, yet the d’Esgrignons’ fortunes did not recover. The Marquis became a symbol of loyalty and old nobility, a leader among remaining royalists. His salon, nicknamed the “Collection of Antiquities,” became a gathering for those clinging to the past. What began as a playful sobriquet assumed a more serious tone as political tides moved.

The family faced challenges from the rising bourgeoisie, epitomized by du Croisier, who sought to undermine their standing. The rivalry intensified as du Croisier’s faction gained strength while the d’Esgrignons remained a bulwark of old-world values. Political life in the town grew polarized, du Croisier adopting an increasingly aggressive stance against the nobility.

By 1822 the d’Esgrignons’ situation remained precarious. The Marquis, now elderly, found himself at a crossroads, uncertain of his role in a changing society. The Restoration had not delivered expected relief; financial struggles continued. The family lived modestly, their income barely meeting needs, while political tensions simmered.

As society evolved, the d’Esgrignons clung to their ideals, navigating a world that seemed increasingly hostile to their way of life. The Collection of Antiquities stood as a testament to a bygone era and the enduring legacy of the d’Esgrignon family.

The Marquis was oblivious to managing his estate, and the notion that the d’Esgrignons could regain their former glory struck those around him as absurd. People pinned their hopes on Victurnien, the handsome heir, marrying into nobility to restore the family's prestige. In reality the d’Esgrignons lacked the political connections and finances to reclaim status, and their continuity had been broken by events of the past forty years.

Chesnel, the notary, alone perceived the futility of such hopes. He understood the social transformations wrought by the Revolution and the altered political landscape; despite his loyalty to the family, he could not ignore their predicament. He tried gently to persuade the Marquis that adaptation was necessary, but the old noble remained convinced that divine intervention or fate would restore their fortunes.

Victurnien, adored by his father and aunt, was a spoiled child who had never faced real opposition. His indulgent upbringing left him selfish and reckless. The Chevalier, a relic of the old order, fed his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, inflating the young man’s sense of superiority. Surrounded by flattery and exemption from ordinary constraints, Victurnien came to regard himself as above the common people, deepening his moral and practical flaws.

When he entered society, the gap between his upbringing and modern realities became apparent. His behavior alienated townspeople, and escapades produced legal troubles Chesnel had to manage discreetly. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened into a life of excess, as he assumed that lineage alone would shield him from consequences.

Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit Victurnien’s weaknesses and hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin. Meanwhile, Victurnien’s vanity and appetite for pleasure obscured danger: he drifted into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices, which he accepted as his due. Sent to Paris with high expectations, he quickly found the court far less welcoming than imagined; the nobility he encountered were largely self-interested and indifferent to reviving the d’Esgrignon name.

Undeterred, he attempted to secure a place by adopting the extravagant lifestyle of Parisian elites. His charm and looks opened doors, and he became a fixture in high society, especially captivated by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Enchanted by her beauty and the glitter of her world, he fell intensely in love, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure.

As he indulged in Parisian pleasures his debts mounted beyond his means. Friends who enjoyed his company also recognized his precarious state and egged his spending, entertained by the prospect of his fall. Victurnien’s naivety and lack of foresight led him to believe he could sustain lavish living without consequence.

Chesnel, ever loyal, continued to prop him up financially but grew increasingly alarmed. Despite warnings, Victurnien remained absorbed in fantasy, oblivious to reality. The tension between his noble pretensions and dire finances would precipitate his downfall.

Victurnien’s passage through Parisian high society exposes the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of nobility. His entanglements—most notably with the Duchesse—become enmeshed in deceit and financial ruin, underscoring the fragility of status and the consequences of living beyond one’s means. The narrative serves as a cautionary tale about pride’s perils and the need to recognize one’s limits in a rapidly changing world.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing. He had also received two thousand crowns from Chesnel to conceal his sources. Victurnien wrote deceitful letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the disaster looming over their noble house. Only du Croisier knew, and he delighted not merely in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them; he sensed his revenge approaching as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier’s first move targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly in the Rue du Bercail. One afternoon, after dinner, Chesnel’s housekeeper announced du Croisier. The old lawyer’s heart sank. Du Croisier presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s debts. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the opportunity, proposed to buy Chesnel’s property—and did so within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande about the family’s financial ruin. She had just received a charming, hypocritical letter from Victurnien describing his travels in Italy with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and remained enchanted and oblivious. Chesnel’s arrival shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked bringing dishonor on their house. Devastated, she realized they had given Victurnien too much freedom.

The Marquis, having read Victurnien’s letter, remained unaware of the crisis. Mlle. Armande and Chesnel shared a silent understanding of their concern for the young Count. They discussed intervening to save him from his reckless lifestyle, fearing he would be left destitute. Mlle. Armande resolved to go to Paris to rescue Victurnien, masking the trip as a medical consultation to avoid gossip.

Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis, and she set off, determined to confront the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Du Croisier, meanwhile, had planned his revenge carefully to ensure Victurnien would be cut off from further support. He wrote to Victurnien, informing him that the Kellers would advance no more money without security, timing the letter to coincide with Victurnien’s increasing desperation.

Victurnien, until then living in luxury, suddenly faced the reality of his debts. Despite previous gambling wins, he found himself unable to procure funds and felt the weight of impending ruin. In despair he briefly considered suicide but rejected it. Instead, he thought of fleeing with the Duchess and forging a bill of exchange to finance their escape, though he hesitated, wanting the Duchess’s agreement first.

When Victurnien confessed his financial troubles to the Duchess, she assured him money could be found. Encouraged, he proposed they flee together; she agreed, eager to break from their current lives. Buoyed by her promise, Victurnien resolved to forge the bill, seeing it as the only way to secure their future.

The next day he presented a bill for three hundred thousand francs to the Kellers, who advanced the money, unaware of the deceit. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his plan for revenge was bearing fruit.

When Victurnien received his money, he gave it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and suggested one last visit to the Opera. He considered confessing everything to Chesnel but, despite doubts, lingered at the Duchess's behest, her enchanting glance keeping him longer than intended.

The next day he returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse to plan an escape, only to find the Duchess resolved to stay. She believed it would be more remarkable to face the storm than to flee, and Victurnien, torn between admiration and despair, realized how deeply he loved her.

After their argument Diane's coldness left Victurnien distraught. He begged forgiveness, but she remained unyielding—an angelic indifference that intensified his anguish. As time passed, his despair grew; he felt lost and unable to think clearly about his next steps.

Meanwhile the Count had taken Diane's hand but found it lifeless. Victurnien’s emotional turmoil was compounded by the discovery that his financial misdeeds had put him in serious trouble. When Josephin told him a warrant for his arrest had been issued, he panicked, convinced he was ruined.

Josephin urged him to flee to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Still in shock, Victurnien clung to his distraught but determined aunt. They discussed hiding and the possibility of sneaking into Chesnel’s house unnoticed.

Executing the plan, Victurnien unwittingly walked into a trap. Chesnel, meanwhile, was selling his practice to a rival notary, M. Lepressoir, who hoped to profit from Victurnien's troubles. Learning of Victurnien's plight devastated Chesnel; fearing for the young man’s future, he contemplated drastic measures, even taking the blame for the forgery himself.

Chesnel resolved to confront du Croisier, Victurnien's creditor, and negotiate a settlement. Du Croisier, however, reveled in his power over the d’Esgrignon family, seeing the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and take revenge on the aristocracy. At a gathering at his place, news of Victurnien's arrest spread among guests who were both shocked and intrigued, the atmosphere tense as allies discussed the implications and the potential trial.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Chesnel sought to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, hoping that securing Camusot's support might mitigate damage to Victurnien's reputation. As legal proceedings unfolded, Chesnel intensified his efforts, seeking Mme. de Maufrigneuse’s assistance. Initially unaware of the forgery's full extent, she felt compelled to help once she understood Victurnien's love and the consequences he faced.

Negotiations with du Croisier proved difficult; he demanded not only financial compensation but political favors and social recognition—requests Chesnel could scarcely satisfy. In desperation, Chesnel offered a substantial sum to have the charges withdrawn, but du Croisier’s pride and ambition made him resistant to compromise. Both sides were unwilling to yield, and the conflict escalated as the trial approached.

Chesnel’s determination to save Victurnien pushed him toward morally questionable options, including lying under oath. He grasped that the d’Esgrignon family’s honor hung in the balance and that dishonor could be devastating. The legal battle thus became emblematic of broader tensions between aristocracy and bourgeoisie: the outcome would not only decide Victurnien's fate but also signal shifting power dynamics in their society.

Throughout, Chesnel’s unwavering loyalty to the d’Esgrignon family and his willingness to navigate the treacherous waters of provincial politics highlighted the complexities of love, honor, and ambition in a world where social status could dictate destiny.

Blondet, a knowledgeable judge, was overshadowed by his passion for gardening—especially pelargoniums—which consumed his time and ambition. His marriage to a much younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further obscured his career. Despite having a son, Joseph, with his wife, Blondet favored his illegitimate son, Emile, whom he eventually sent to Paris with minimal support after his wife's death.

Blondet’s modest brick house and well-kept garden reflected his horticultural dedication. He intended to leave his estate to Joseph, having disinherited Emile, whom he regarded with disdain. Emile, however, prospered in Paris, helped by connections from his mother’s family, notably Mlle. de Troisville, who promised support and aided his literary recognition—achievements of which Blondet remained unaware, absorbed in legal duties and gardening. His life was marked by strict frugality, a maid managing the household and enforcing modest living.

Joseph Blondet, groomed for the bench, spent time with the Blandureau family, while Michu, a young lawyer, navigated social circles and often outshone the older judges. The public prosecutor, a shrewd politician, became involved in a plot against the d’Esgrignons, leading to a forgery charge against Victurnien d’Esgrignon, a young nobleman.

The President of the Tribunal, seeking personal gain, manipulated events to benefit du Croisier, a rival of the d’Esgrignons. Influenced by the President’s wife, the prosecutor was persuaded to pursue the case against Victurnien despite potential political fallout.

As the trial neared, the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse intervened, bringing funds to secure Victurnien’s release. Her efforts, combined with Chesnel the family notary’s support, aimed to restore the d’Esgrignon name. The trial revealed the truth, exonerating Victurnien and exposing du Croisier’s machinations.

Afterward the d’Esgrignons faced societal scrutiny, but the family’s honor was preserved. Victurnien’s future remained uncertain as he struggled with the implications of his past and the expectations of his lineage. The story closes by reflecting on the changing nature of nobility and the enduring bonds of loyalty and service, embodied by characters like Chesnel, who represent the fading ideals of feudal devotion.

Ultimately the narrative examines ambition, familial loyalty, and the complexities of social standing in a changing France, highlighting the interplay between personal desire and societal expectation.
