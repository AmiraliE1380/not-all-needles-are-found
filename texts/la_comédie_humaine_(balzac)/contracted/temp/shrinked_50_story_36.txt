To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement for my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval lends weight to my efforts. Your devotion to research has sustained me in writing on modern civilization. Receive this token of respect from your sincere admirer and friend.

DE BALZAC.

In a small French town the Hotel d’Esgrignon houses the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. The name began as a jest but suits a family proud of its ancient Northman lineage. Proud provincial nobles, the d’Esgrignons refused to bow to kings or commoners: marriages without dowries, refusals of titles, and a stubborn local reverence persisted despite neglect by court and state.

The Revolution threatened them: the Marquis hid, lands were sold, and Mlle. d’Esgrignon, through the steward Chesnel, rescued some property. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a ruined castle; the old hotel lay demolished. Chesnel used the family’s last funds to buy a modest new residence, wryly naming it the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned. The Marquis married the Baron de Nouastre’s daughter, who died in childbirth leaving a son. Reduced circumstances and grief deepened. When a contractor proposed to Mlle., she and her brother were insulted; Chesnel, who had negotiated, was blamed. More than a servant, Chesnel was confidant, manager, and protector, torn between fidelity and the house’s decline. Politically the family stayed royalist while the bourgeoisie rose and rivals like du Croisier sought to undermine them. By 1822 the Restoration brought little relief; finances remained strained, but the “Collection of Antiquities” salon endured as a testament to their bygone ideals and legacy.

The Marquis neglected the estate, and most considered it fanciful that the d’Esgrignons could regain their former glory. Many pinned hopes on Victurnien, the handsome heir, marrying into nobility to restore prestige, but the family lacked the political connections, finances, and continuity—fractured over forty years—to do so.

Only Chesnel, the notary, saw this clearly. He understood the social changes after the Revolution and gently urged the Marquis to adapt; the old noble, however, clung to belief in fate or divine revival.

Victurnien, indulged by his father and aunt, never faced opposition; spoiled, selfish, and reckless, he was further inflamed by the Chevalier’s romantic tales of past grandeur. Surrounded by flattery and exempt from restraint, he came to regard himself above common people.

In town his behavior alienated locals; his escapades brought legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Companions encouraged his arrogance, while Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit him. Victurnien fell into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s sacrifices. Sent to Paris with high hopes, he found the court indifferent; nevertheless he adopted the elite’s extravagant lifestyle. His charm won him society’s doors and the infatuation of the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse—despite her debts—while his own liabilities mounted. Friends egged his spending; Chesnel kept propping him up. The tension between pretension and ruined finances foreshadows a downfall, exposing the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of inherited status.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had secretly advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs; he had also taken two thousand crowns from Chesnel and written flattering, deceitful letters to his father and aunt. Only du Croisier knew, and he delighted in both ruining and dishonoring the d’Esgrignons as Victurnien’s debts grew.

Du Croisier’s first move targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly in the Rue du Bercail. Calling one afternoon, he produced a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening exposure. Chesnel, able to raise money only by mortgaging his lands, was forced to sell; du Croisier bought the property within two days, leaving Chesnel with only his practice and house and consumed by fear for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande, who had just received a flattering letter from Victurnien about travels with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and remained enchanted. The news shattered her; she saw they had given him too much freedom. The Marquis, though he had read Victurnien’s letter, was unaware of the crisis. Chesnel and Mlle. Armande silently resolved to save the young count; she set off for Paris—pretending a medical visit to avoid gossip—with Chesnel’s last bag of louis to confront the Duchesse.

Du Croisier ensured Victurnien would be cut off, writing that the Kellers would advance no more without security. Cornered, Victurnien rejected suicide and plotted to flee with the Duchesse, forging a bill for three hundred thousand francs which the Kellers advanced unknowingly. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his scheme was succeeding.

When Victurnien got the money he handed it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and persuaded him to make one more visit to the Opera. He nearly confessed everything to Chesnel but, despite misgivings, stayed at the Duchess’s request, held by her enchanting look.

The next day he returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse intending to flee, only to find the Duchess resolved to face the storm rather than run. Torn between admiration and despair, Victurnien recognized the depth of his love. After their quarrel Diane’s coldness left him distraught; her angelic indifference to his plea for forgiveness intensified his anguish and clouded his judgment.

Worse followed: a warrant was issued for his arrest. Josephin urged him to go to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Reeling, he and his aunt plotted to hide and slip into Chesnel’s house, unwittingly walking into a trap.

Chesnel, preparing to sell his practice to M. Lepressoir, was devastated by Victurnien’s plight and even considered taking the blame for the forgery. He resolved to confront the creditor, du Croisier, who relished his ascendancy over the d’Esgrignon family. At du Croisier’s gathering news of the arrest spread and debate about the trial flared.

Chesnel tried to influence the magistrate, M. Camusot, and enlisted Mme de Maufrigneuse’s help. Negotiations with du Croisier proved thorny—he demanded money, political favors, and recognition. Chesnel offered a large sum and even contemplated perjury, but du Croisier’s pride resisted compromise. The looming trial thus became emblematic of a wider clash between aristocracy and bourgeoisie, with love, honor, ambition, and Chesnel’s loyalty at stake.

Blondet, a competent judge, was eclipsed by his passion for gardening—especially pelargoniums—which consumed his time and ambition. His marriage to a much younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further clouded his career. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet preferred his illegitimate son, Émile, and after his wife’s death sent Émile to Paris with little support.

Blondet planned to leave his estate to Joseph, disinheriting Émile. Émile, however, prospered in Paris with his mother’s family and the patronage of Mlle. de Troisville, gaining literary recognition unknown to his absorbed father. Joseph was being groomed for the bench and mingled with the Blandureaus; Michu, a young lawyer, often outshone older judges.

A shrewd prosecutor pursued a politically motivated forgery charge against Victurnien d’Esgrignon at the President’s urging to benefit du Croisier. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and Chesnel funded Victurnien’s defense; the trial exonerated him and exposed du Croisier’s plots. The d’Esgrignons preserved their honor, though Victurnien’s future remained uncertain amid shifting loyalties and ambitions in changing France.
