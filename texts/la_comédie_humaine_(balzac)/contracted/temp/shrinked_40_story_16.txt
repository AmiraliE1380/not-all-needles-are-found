France, and Brittany especially, still shelters towns that escaped the nineteenth century’s bustle. Cut off by poor roads and slow ties to Paris, they watch modern civilization as spectacle—astonished observers rather than participants. A moral archaeologist would find in these villages the manners of bygone reigns: Louis XV in Provence, Louis XIV in Poitou, and older types in Brittany. Tradition preserves traces better than historians: local memory clings to customs and vestiges of past splendors—former capitals of tiny feudal states reduced to names on maps.

Modern industry is eroding distinctive local arts and crafts, turning unique products into mass-made goods and replacing the patrimony of individual artisans with impersonal production. Public buildings remain as retrospective monuments, but factories are becoming the new landmarks. Yet some towns retain their ancient character; one of the most intact is Guerande, a Breton jewel commanding the tides and dunes, like the summit of a triangle whose other points are Croisic and Batz. Few provincial towns—perhaps Vitre and Avignon excepted—preserve the medieval type so thoroughly.

Guerande is still ringed by strong walls, its moats full, battlements and loopholes unovergrown; even ivy has not mantled the towers. Three gates show rings of portcullises and an iron-clamped drawbridge that could still be raised. In 1820 the mayor was reproached for planting poplars along the moat to shade the promenade—though the esplanade had long been a mall where citizens strolled beneath elms. The houses have not altered their fronts, added stories, or been stuccoed; arcades rest on wooden columns, floor planks bend but do not break. Merchant houses are low, slate-faced, with carved wooden figures—grotesque faces and fantastic beasts—relics painters prize for their patina.

The streets remain as they were centuries ago, though the population has thinned so much a traveler may walk alone through lanes where mullioned windows are boarded up against the window-tax. A postern opens onto a wooded tract—one of Brittany’s luxuriant spots—seen through casemates once occupied by halberdiers. Every step recalls long-continued habits; medieval ideas and superstitions persist. Modern objects and fashions are rare and adapt to immutable customs. The public square still swarms with Breton costumes that attract artists: the white linen of the paludiers (salt-gatherers), blue and brown peasant clothes, the sacred jewelry of women, sailors’ jerkins and varnished caps. Castes remain distinct—bourgeoisie, nobility, clergy, peasants, paludiers—so that revolutionary leveling found the masses too rugged to plane. Even after 1830 Guerande remained fervently Catholic, silent, and self-contained, where modern ideas have little access.

Geography explains much. Guerande overlooks salt-marshes producing the celebrated Guerande salt—whose quality Bretons link to their butter and sardines—and is linked to the rest of France by only two roads: one from Savenay ending at Saint-Nazaire, and another from Vannes. Land travel is limited; the more frequented route is by water from Saint-Nazaire. Between Guerande and Saint-Nazaire the mail-coach does not run; the road sees almost no passengers. These obstacles discourage travelers and encourage inhabitants to accept separation. Guerande, at the edge of the continent, leads nowhere and is glad to be left alone. Much of its salt production is managed at Croisic, which communicates with Guerande across dangerous quicksands and an arm of the sea; Croisic’s port handles commerce.

Guerande is like the Herculaneum of feudality—preserved rather than destroyed. Approaching from Croisic across salt-marshes, its vast fortifications strike the traveler; from Saint-Nazaire its position and naive grace charm. The surrounding country is ravishing: hedges full of honeysuckle, roses, box, and plants that make the landscape seem an English garden. This rich nature, framed by an African-like belt of ocean-banked dunes, creates striking contrasts—the paludiers in white scattered among melancholy swamps recall Arabs in burrows. Guerande’s sleep-like silence, likened to Venice, lulls one; public conveyances are rare, reduced to a carrier’s springless wagon. Bernus, the carrier in 1829, was the town’s factotum, rising and setting the pace of arrivals; the coming of a carriage or a sea-bathing lady was an event that drew peasants to barter and to buy the distinctive jewels and linens of Breton brides and households.

For a ten-mile circuit it is always Guerande—the town of the treaty of 1365, the key of the coast, still claiming a lost splendor. Artists and some bourgeois visitors feel, as in Venice, the desire to spend days amid its peace, walking along the mall from gate to gate toward the sea. Its image lingers like that of an enchanting woman: towered, girdled, floating in a robe of dunes, scented with wild paths.

Near Guerande’s church stands a mansion that epitomizes the town’s past—a symbol of a greatness destroyed. It belongs to the du Guaisnics, the noblest family of the province, as ancient as Brittany’s granite. In du Guesclin’s time they were among the utmost nobility; perhaps Druids in origin, they now subsist on a small income. The family possesses the manor in Guerande, the little castle of Guaisnic, and nominally holds lands once bringing substantial revenue. But their estates have long been pledged to farmers; despite the barony's lands grossing perhaps sixty thousand francs under mismanaged cultivation, the family receives only two thousand francs a year. For two centuries they have been unable to repay advances and remain titular rather than actual proprietors—analogous to the ancien régime’s crown toward its engagistes. Before 1789 the Guaisnic fiefs were worth fifty thousand francs annually, but the National Assembly’s suppression of seigneurial dues on inheritance further reduced their claims. In Paris the family is little regarded and laughed at, yet in Guerande the Baron du Guaisnic remains a great baron, a man standing beneath only the King.

At the end of a damp, gloomy lane stands the mansion’s granite archway—wide enough for a horseman, indicating antiquity. The oak gate, studded with enormous nails in geometric patterns, opens under a semicircular arch carved with the Guaisnics’ arms: Gules, a hand proper gonfaloned ermine, a sword argent in pale, with the motto FAC. The coronet above proclaims baronial rank. The hand, holding the sword with vigor, expresses chivalrous pride and a family’s readiness for action—“To DO,” a motto that still arouses feeling among those who know the Guaisnics.

The gate opens into a courtyard with stables on the right and kitchen offices on the left. The house is of freestone from cellar to garret. Its courtyard façade has a portico with double steps; carved vestiges—now worn—display the Hand bearing the sword. Granite steps are disjointed and mossy, yet solidity remains. The oak door, nail-studded and likely by a Venetian-school artisan, opens into a noble hall leading to a portico and the garden.

The hall is marvelously preserved: chestnut wainscot three feet high, gilded and faded Spanish leather hangings, a painted wooden ceiling with traces of gold and floral designs, and an enormous stone chimney-piece with giant wrought-iron andirons capable of holding a cart-load of wood. Furniture is all oak, each piece bearing the family arms. Hunting arms—English guns, sabres, game-bags—hang among fishermen’s utensils. A dining-room opens to the kitchen via a door cut through a corner tower; at the opposite corner a spiral staircase rises to the upper stories.

The dining-room is hung with fourteenth-century tapestries; their inscribed banderols, in the naive language of the fabliaux, attest their age. These tapestries, spared much light and framed in ebony-toned oak, set the room’s medieval tone. On shelves, as in 1200, sit four old goblets, an embossed soup-tureen, two silver salt-cellars, and pewter plates and pitchers marked with the du Guaisnics’ arms and covered with hinged pewter lids. The fireplace here has been modernized in Louis XV taste—an anachronism that would distress a poet but to the family is indifferent. On the mantel stands a tortoise-shell clock inlaid with brass flanked by silver candelabra. A small round table by a garden window holds an odd lamp—an ostrich-egg-sized globe attached to a candlestick by a glass tube, its wick drawing nut-oil like a coiled tape-worm.

Windows to the garden and courtyard are mullioned with hexagonal panes and draped with heavy brocatelle curtains of ancient crimson silk with gold reflections. The two upper stories contain only two rooms each—the master’s bedroom and the children’s—while guests lodged under the roof and servants above kitchens and stables. The pointed roof, with noble windows nearly to the ridge, bears slender casings whose carvings have suffered from salty air; above each arch a vane grinds.

A spiral-stair tower at the corner descends to a little arched door opening on a gravel yard between house and stables. A companion, five-sided tower ending in a cupola with a bell-turret, repeats the form on the garden side. These towers connect on the first floor by a stone gallery supported by brackets ending in grotesque human heads; the gallery’s delicate balustrade and a stone dais hanging from the gable recall statues at church portals. One might imagine a woman walking this balcony to gaze over Guerande at sunlight gilding sands and ocean.

The garden, remarkable for so old a place, covers half an acre: espaliered walls divide vegetable squares bordered by cordons of fruit trees tended by Gasselin, the man-of-all-work. At the far end is a grotto with a seat, a central sun-dial, and gravelled paths. The garden façade lacks the courtyard towers but shows a slender spiral column from ground to roof that once bore the family banner—its iron socket now supporting only some weak plants. Certain sculptural details, trefoils with four leaves rather than three, suggest Venetian influence in the architecture—Venetian art, corrupted by eastern contacts, sometimes altering Christian symbolism into quartet forms.

This ancient dwelling’s charm prompts the question why such houses are no longer built. The reason is social change: mansions are sold, demolished, or parceled for streets; fathers fear the next generation will not retain the paternal home. Formerly, building was an act done for perpetuity; that faith—both in self and God—produced prodigies: durable architecture, carefully ordered interiors, and continuity of manners that now fade. The du Guaisnics still live by those traditions. Their rooms, courtyard, and external accessories preserve the spirit, grace, and candor of old Brittany. To understand the family’s surprising figures, one must first see the town and the house; things shape beings. There are public buildings whose presence molds nearby character; under the shadow of a cathedral, irreligion is harder to maintain. So too the Guaisnic mansion sustains a way of life. Were the Baron not still ready to take sword in hand, all this faithful portraiture would be untrue.

Early in May 1836 the household of Guenic comprised Monsieur and Madame du Guenic, Mademoiselle du Guenic (the baron’s elder sister), and an only son of twenty-one, Gaudebert-Calyste-Louis—called Calyste by family habit. The father bore the old family names Gaudebert-Calyste-Charles; saintly names watched over the Guenics as much by habit as piety.

The Baron du Guenic had abandoned Guerande when La Vendée and Brittany rose, fighting with Charette, Cathelineau, La Rochejaquelein, d’Elbée, Bonchamps, and the Prince de Loudon. Prudently he had sold his estate to his sister Zéphirine before leaving, to shield the family fortune from confiscation. After the leaders’ deaths he refused to submit to Napoleon and continued resistance until 1802. Defeated and nearly captured, he went to Croisic and crossed to Ireland, true to the Breton habit of mistrust toward England.

For twenty years Guerande pretended ignorance of his whereabouts; silence and discretion protected him. Mademoiselle collected rents and sent them by fishermen. The baron returned quietly in 1813 as if from a season in Nantes. During his Irish exile, well past fifty, he fell in love with an Irishwoman, Fanny O’Brien, daughter of an impoverished but noble family. She was twenty-one. He procured French marriage papers, returned to Ireland, and early in 1814 brought his wife to Guerande, where she gave him Calyste on the very day Louis XVIII landed at Calais—hence the boy’s final name, Louis.

The baron was now seventy-three, aged prematurely by guerrilla warfare, exile, and hazardous sea crossings. He matched the worn grandeur of their mansion, built when a court still reigned at Guerande. Tall, straight, wiry, and lean, his oval, wrinkled face had the hollowed look of Rembrandt’s subjects; the sun and patriotic vigilance had carved him, yet enough remained to move the soul. His firm outline, solemn lines, and a rigid, slightly wounded nose declared intrepidity without calculation, faith without reserve, obedience without question, fidelity without compromise, and love without constancy. In him, Breton granite had taken human form.

The baron had no teeth; his lips were violet and retracted, giving an expression both threatening and proud. His chin seemed to seek his hooked nose—signs of energy and Breton stubbornness. Abundant white hair fell in curls on his shoulders. Black eyes still glittered with the last flames of a generous spirit. Eyebrows and lashes were gone; shaving proved difficult, so he let his beard grow fan-shaped. His hands—large, broad, hairy—were those of a soldier and guerrilla, shaped by sword and oar and by fights among thorns and marshes; close inspection might show fresh marks from renewed Vendée service. They illustrated the Guenic motto: Fac!

His high, narrow brow, broadened by hair loss, lent majesty to that noble ruin. His countenance reflected a certain stolid calm and even savagery—a Huguenot-like impassibility where animal nature dominates and thought is rare. Thinking seemed effort; its seat appeared the heart rather than the head. He had inborn faiths and sentiments that made deep speculation unnecessary; institutions and religion did his thinking. He conserved his mind for action, drawing thought from his heart as he drew his sword; sincerity shone on his scutcheon. This temperament explained the sale to his sister before the war—prudent provision for exile, death, or confiscation.

The beauty of character displayed by him and his sister—who lived only by and for her brother—cannot be judged by the selfish standards of a later age. When the rector suggested the baron go to Paris in 1814 to seek recompense from the Bourbons, Mademoiselle cried, “Oh, fy! does my brother need to hold out his hand like a beggar?” “It would be thought I served a king from interest,” said the baron. “Besides, it is for him to remember. Poor king! he must be weary indeed of those who harass him.”

That devotion did not go unrewarded by courtly forms: the baron received the rank of colonel, the cross of Saint-Louis, and a pension of two thousand francs a year—news he accepted as the king’s remembrance, though in truth the Duc de Feltre had arranged it. In gratitude the baron held a siege at Guerande against General Travot; refusing to surrender the fortress, he and a band of Chouans evacuated into the woods and stayed armed until the second Bourbon restoration. Guerande still recalls that siege.

The Baron du Guenic was as illiterate as a peasant in many respects; he could read, write a little, cipher, and knew military art and heraldry, but beyond his prayer-book he had read hardly three volumes. He dressed always the same: stout shoes, ribbed stockings, greenish velveteen breeches, a cloth waistcoat, and a loose coat with a collar that displayed the cross of Saint-Louis. A noble serenity sat on his face; for a year or so sleep—prelude to death—seemed to be gathering him for eternal rest. These pauses alarmed neither his blind sister nor his wife—medical knowledge was small; to them these pauses were natural: the baron had done his duty.

In that ancient mansion the absorbing interests were the fortunes of the dispossessed elder branch, the future of the exiled Bourbons, the Catholic religion, and Brittany politics. The sole personal interest was the devotion of all to the only son, Calyste, heir and hope of the du Guenic name.

Some years earlier the old Vendean had a return of youth and began training his son in manly exercises for a gentleman always ready to take arms. At sixteen Calyste was taken to marshes and forest, taught by his father through the chase and small warfare—firm in saddle, steady at the shot, indifferent to fatigue, intrepid before obstacles. So, when the Duchesse de Berry landed to attempt a restoration for her son, the father judged it right to take his boy and join her, practicing the family motto. The baron left at night without informing his wife, taking his son under fire as if to a fête, accompanied by Gasselin, his sole vassal. They were absent three months without sending news to the baroness, who read the Quotidienne with trembling, nor to the blind sister, whose nerve did not falter. Considering the enterprise useless, the baron left the region before the affair of La Pénissière; otherwise the house might have been ruined.

On a stormy night after parting from Madame, the father, son, and servant returned, surprising the baroness and Mademoiselle du Guenic—though the blind sister recognized their steps in the lane. The baron looked around the anxious circle seated by the antique lamp and said, in a tremulous voice while Gasselin replaced guns and sabres, in words of feudal simplicity: “The barons did not all do their duty.” He then kissed his wife and sister, sat in his old arm-chair, and ordered supper for his son, Gasselin, and himself. Gasselin had thrown himself before Calyste to protect him and received a sabre cut on his shoulder; the women scarcely thanked him. The baron and guests uttered neither curses nor complaints about conquerors—silence being a Breton trait. In forty years no one ever heard the baron rail against his adversaries; it was for them to do their duty as he had done his. This silence marked an unalterable will.

That last effort, the flash of waning energy, had produced the present weakness and somnolence of the old man. The fresh defeat and exile of the Bourbons, driven out as miraculously as restored, weighed bitterly on him.

About six on the evening when this tale begins, the baron, accustomed to dining at four, fell asleep while his wife read the Quotidienne. His head leaned back against the armchair beside the fireplace on the garden side.

Near a gnarled tree and before the fire sat the baroness—the type of those admirable women often found in England, Scotland, or Ireland—milk-white, golden-haired, her curls wound as if by angels. Fanny O’Brien was one of those sylphs—strong in tenderness, invincible under misfortune, soft in voice, and pure in her blue eyes; her skin silken, her beauty exquisite. Still beautiful at forty-two, she might have graced any suitor’s dream. Half reclining with feet out to warm, she wore a black velvet gown against the chill; the corsage rose to the throat. Her hair was in English ringlets, the rest twisted to the crown and pinned; burnished gold gleamed. A short nape-lock, braided and hidden, was a coquettish racial sign that pleased the baron. When a woman displays such coquetry at home, it bespeaks noble devotion: she loves without calculation, does good in secret, and worships. In Fanny the Virgin’s halo seemed to preserve her beauty. Her serene face and pure blue eyes expressed boundless sympathy and angelic tenderness.

At the other hearth sat the octogenarian sister, like her brother in all but dress, knitting stockings—the work needing no sight. Both eyes had cataracts; she refused an operation despite entreaties. Secretly she said it was want of courage; truly she would not let her brother spend twenty-five louis on her. That sum saved would be for the household’s good.

These two old people set off the baroness’ beauty. Mademoiselle Zéphirine, deprived of sight, was unaware of eighty years’ effect on her face. Her pale hollow countenance and white sightless eyes produced a deathlike aspect; three or four projecting teeth showed when she smiled. She wore a brown printed cotton hood quilted like a petticoat, a cotillon over a quilted petticoat that hid double louis-d’or, and pockets sewn to a belt she fastened each morning and night. She clung to her casaquin and pleated collar, changing the collar only once a week—a point of domestic dispute between her and her sister-in-law. From large wadded sleeves came withered yet vigorous arms, brownish-red hands moving like stocking-machines; now and then she thrust a knitting-needle between hood and hair to poke her white locks, then pushed it back without fear. Straight as a steeple, erect and imposing, her carriage suggested pride—an old-age coquetry proving pride a necessary passion of life. Her smile was gay. She, too, had done her duty.

When the baroness saw her husband asleep, she ceased reading. A ray of sunshine burnished black furniture, ceiling carvings, time-worn chests, and the old oak table; Fanny’s voice and the light made music in the blind sister’s heart. The ray sank into twilight; the baroness slipped into deep meditation, a silence noticed by Mademoiselle for two weeks. The elder continued to knit; the silence deepened until the clicking of needles was plainly heard.

“You have dropped the paper, sister, but you are not asleep,” Mademoiselle remarked slyly.

Mariotte then entered to light the lamp, which she placed on a small table by the fire, fetched her distaff and ball of thread, and sat on a stool in the window recess to spin. Gasselin busied himself about the offices, tended the horses, readied the stable, and fed the two hunting-dogs; their joyful barking was the last noise to awaken echoes in the dark walls. The two dogs and two horses were the household’s only remaining vestiges of chivalry. An imaginative man on the portico might have shivered at the hounds’ baying and the horses’ neighing.

Gasselin was a short, thick Breton with black hair and sun-browned face, silent, slow, and obstinate as a mule but steady. Forty-two, he had served twenty-five years, hired by Mademoiselle at fifteen on hearing of the baron’s marriage and probable return. He considered himself family, had played with Calyste, loved the horses and dogs, petted them, and wore a blue linen jacket with flapping pockets, matching waistcoat and trousers, blue stockings, and hob-nailed shoes; in cold weather he donned a goatskin.

Mariotte, also over forty, was to woman what Gasselin was to man—same complexion, figure, lively black eyes. Curiously they had never married; perhaps it seemed immoral because they resembled brother and sister. Mariotte’s wages were ninety francs a year; Gasselin’s, three hundred. Yet thousands would not have tempted either to leave the Guenic household. Both were under Mademoiselle’s orders, who, from war to the baron’s return, had ruled the house. When she learned the baron would bring home a mistress, she feared losing her household sceptre, yet she was agreeably surprised to find in Fanny a woman of high birth who disliked petty cares and preferred plain fare to costly dishes. Fanny could perform all duties, endure privations when necessary, but had no taste for commonplace work. When the baron begged his sister, in his wife’s name, to keep charge, the old maid kissed the baroness like a sister, made a daughter of her, adored her, and rejoiced to retain control. She ran the house with an almost inconceivable economy, relaxing only for great occasions—lie-in, Calyste’s needs, and similar events.

Though masters and servants observed a stern regime, they required few orders: masters’ interests were their own. Mademoiselle insisted on overseeing all; she knew without looking how many nuts were in the barn or oats in the bin, carrying a boatswain’s whistle to summon Mariotte with one note and Gasselin with two.

Gasselin’s greatest joy was tending the garden, producing fine fruits and vegetables; with so little to do he would have been lost without it. After grooming horses and cleaning the ground-floor rooms, he weeded and watched for field-mice or cockchafer grubs, joyfully presenting his catch. He liked going to Croisic on fast-days to buy fish cheaper than at Guerande.

Thus no household was more united, more bound together than this noble family devoted to duty. Masters and servants seemed made for one another. For twenty-five years there had been neither trouble nor discord. The only griefs were the boy’s petty ailments, the sole terrors the events of 1814 and 1830. If the same tasks were performed at the same hours and food obeyed seasons and times, this monotony—like nature’s—varied only by cloud and sun, and was sustained by affection that sprang from natural causes and made life fruitful.

When night had fallen, Gasselin entered the hall and asked respectfully if he was still needed.

“You can go out, or go to bed, after prayers,” replied the baron, waking, “unless Madame or my sister—”

The two ladies signaled assent. Gasselin knelt as his masters rose to kneel on their chairs; Mariotte also knelt before her stool. Mademoiselle du Guenic then said the prayer aloud. When it ended, someone rapped at the lane-door and Gasselin opened it.

“I dare say it is Monsieur le curé; he usually comes first,” said Mariotte.

Monsieur Grimont’s foot sounded on the portico. The rector, with the unctuous civility of a priest, bowed to the three occupants and answered the mistress’s absent-minded greeting with an ecclesiastical question.

“Are you anxious or ill, Madame la baronne?” he asked.

“Thank you, no,” she replied.

Grimont, about fifty and of middle height, seemed swallowed by his cassock, from which two stout shoes protruded. His plump, pale, slightly yellow-spotted face, dimpled hands, and placid, abbatial look suggested a Dutch burgomaster; his straight black hair and lively brown eyes recalled a Breton peasant. He had the joking gaiety of a man at peace with his conscience and the untroubled confidence of a rector in a town where his authority was undisputed. Yet in the du Guenics’ salon he lowered his spiritual superiority and acted as a chaplain in his seigneur’s house; in church his blessing favored their chapel, where their armorial device adorned the keystone.

“I thought Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had already arrived,” Grimont said, taking the baroness’s hand to kiss it. “She is getting unpunctual. Can it be that dissipation is contagious? I see Monsieur le chevalier is again at Les Touches this evening.”

“Don’t say anything about those visits before Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel,” cried the old maid eagerly.

“Ah! mademoiselle,” Mariotte said, “you can’t prevent the town from gossiping.”

“What do they say?” asked the baroness.

“The young girls and the old women all say he is in love with Mademoiselle des Touches.”

“A lad of Calyste’s make is playing his proper part in making the women love him,” said the baron.

“Here comes Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel,” said Mariotte.

Gravel crackled in the courtyard and a page with a lantern ushered the lady in. Mariotte moved her stool to the great hall to talk by the lantern’s gleam, economizing their single oribus candle.

Jacqueline de Pen-Hoel was an elderly demoiselle: thin, dried, yellow as parchment, wrinkled, with gray eyes, prominent teeth, and the hard hands of a man. Short and slightly crooked, she stirred a mass of petticoats to reach a pocket; keys and coins jingled among her garments. Dangling from one side were the keys of a good housekeeper; from the other a silver snuff-box, thimble, knitting-needles and other resonant implements. She wore a green bonnet, yellowing with age, and gowns made by her nieces to an immutable pattern renewed every five years at Nantes. Using an old cane with a short hook, she bore the ermine of ancient Breton dukes. In her and her sister the illustrious house of the Pen-Hoels ended; the younger sister had married a Kergarouet who, against disapproval, added Pen-Hoel to his name and styled himself Vicomte de Kergarouet-Pen-Hoel.

“Heaven has punished him,” said the old lady; “he has nothing but daughters, and the Kergarouet-Pen-Hoel name will be wiped out.”

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had about seven thousand francs a year from rents. She had come into her property at thirty-six and managed it herself, inspecting it on horseback and showing the firmness often seen in deformed persons. Her celebrated avarice was local legend: she kept one servant and the page, and her expenses, excluding taxes, rarely exceeded a thousand francs a year. Thus she attracted the attentions of the Kergarouet-Pen-Hoels, who spent winters in Nantes and summers at their Loire estate. Supposedly ready to leave her fortune to whatever niece pleased her best, she received one of the four demoiselles de Kergarouet-Pen-Hoel every three months.

A friend of Zephirine du Guenic, Jacqueline had planned since Calyste’s birth to transmit her property to the chevalier by marrying him to whichever niece the Vicomtesse favored. She dreamt of buying back Guenic land from tenant engagistes; when avarice has an object it ceases to be vice and becomes a means to an end. Perhaps Zephirine knew Jacqueline’s plan; perhaps the baroness, devoted to her son and fond of his father, suspected it as well, seeing how persistently the old maid brought her favorite niece, Charlotte, now sixteen. The abbe Grimont was certainly in her confidence; he helped Jacqueline invest her savings.

Yet however wealthy Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel might have been, the du Guenics never showed behavior that could be read as seeking her fortune. True Breton pride kept Jacqueline glad of the esteem accorded to Zephirine and the du Guenics; she seemed honored by her relations with them. She even concealed the personal discomfort of burning the singular gingerbread-colored oribus candle that night in the Guenic hall.

At the moment of this narrative, the abbe Grimont revealed that on the evening when the old baron, the young chevalier, and Gasselin secretly departed to join Madame (to the baroness’s terror and all Bretons’ joy), Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had given the baron ten thousand francs in gold—an immense sacrifice—to which the abbe added another ten thousand from a tithe he collected—charging the old hero to offer the whole, in the name of the Pen-Hoels and of the parish of Guerande, to the mother of Henri V. This generosity showed how Jacqueline treated Calyste: as if her intentions gave her rights over him; her plans seemed to authorize supervision.

Not strict in gallantry—indulgent like women of the old school—she held modern ways in horror. Calyste might earn her esteem by a few Breton adventures but would lose it if seen entangled in what she called innovations. She might disinter a little gold to pay the result of a love-affair, but if Calyste drove a tilbury or spoke of Paris she would deem him dissipated and spendthrift. To her, novel ideas meant the overthrow of crop succession, ruin under the name of improvements, and mortgaged lands as the inevitable consequence of experiments. Prudence, filling granaries and waiting for a rise, was the true method of making fortune. By chance she often made advantageous sales that confirmed her principles. Thought maliciously clever but not quick-witted, she was methodical as a Dutchman, prudent as a cat, and persistent as a priest—qualities in routine-bound Brittany nearly equivalent to intellect.

“Will Monsieur du Halga join us this evening?” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel asked, taking off her knitted mittens after the usual greetings.

“Yes, mademoiselle; I met him walking his dog on the mall,” replied the rector.

“Ha! then our mouche will be lively to-night. Last evening we were only four.”

At the word mouche the abbe rose, fetched a small round osier basket, a pile of ivory counters yellowed with use, and a greasy pack of cards. He arranged counters before each player and set the basket beside the lamp with infantine eagerness and the practiced air of a man accustomed to this service.

A firm knock at the outer gate brought in the long, lean, methodically clothed Chevalier du Halga, former flag-captain to Admiral de Kergarouet, outlined in black on the portico.

“Welcome, chevalier!” cried Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel.

“The altar is raised,” said the abbe.

The chevalier, a man in poor health, wore flannel for rheumatism, a black-silk skull-cap to protect his head from fog, and a spencer to guard his chest from sudden gusts. He always carried a gold-headed cane and a little bitch named Thisbe. Fussy as a fine lady and speaking low to spare his voice, he had once been among the most intrepid officers of the old navy, winning de Suffren’s confidence and the Comte de Portenduere’s friendship; his splendid conduct as flag-captain to Admiral Kergarouet was written on his scarred face. Now no one would imagine the voice that had ruled storms and the eye that compassed the sea.

The chevalier never smoked or swore; he was gentle and tranquil as a girl and as concerned about Thisbe’s caprices as an elderly dowager. He moved as an invalid, walking as if stepping on eggs, complaining of wind, sun, or mist, yet showed the whitest teeth in the reddest gums—a reassuring sign that his ailments were mostly expensive comforts involving four ample meals a day. Like the baron, his frame was bony and indestructibly strong, covered with parchment-like skin bearing a tawny color from India, though he brought back no facts or ideas from there. He had emigrated with his friends, lost his property, and served in the Russian navy until Emperor Alexander ordered him employed against France; he resigned, lived at Odessa near the Duc de Richelieu, and returned to France with the duke’s help, who secured for him the pension that enabled his present life. On Louis XVIII’s death he returned to Guerande and eventually became mayor.

For the last fifteen years the rector, the chevalier, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel had regularly passed their evenings at the hôtel de Guenic, where other noble neighbors also came. The du Guenics led the faubourg Saint-Germain of the old Breton province, into which none of the new administration’s appointees were ever permitted. For six years their prayers had included Domine, salvum fac regem with a cough at the crucial words, and politics in Guerande remained faithful to that point.

Mouche was played with five cards each and one card turned up to show trumps. On a turn a player might play or abstain; abstaining cost only his stake unless forfeits already filled the basket. If he played and won at least one trick he was paid pro rata; if he won none he was “mouche,” paying the whole stake and enlarging the next basket. Those who declined threw down their hands and their play was void. Players could exchange cards with the remainder of the pack in turn, so the first exchangers sometimes took most of it. The turned-up trump belonged to the dealer, who played last and could exchange it for any card in hand. The knave of clubs, a powerful card, was nicknamed Mistigris.

The game fostered cupidity, diplomacy, and poker faces. At the Hôtel du Guénic each player put up twenty counters representing five sous, so the stake amounted to five farthings—small but meaningful to these guests; a run of luck could yield fifty sous, more than most in Guérande spent in a day. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël played with the zeal of a big-game hunter; Mademoiselle Zéphirine, who shared funds with the baroness, treated each farthing as a serious investment.

An 1825 convention forbade continuing when any player losing ten sous wished to retire; politeness required stopping. The chevalier and the baron, however, skirted this: when most wanted to quit, Du Halga, the rich free-spending sailor, would bribe Zéphirine or Jacqueline with ten counters to keep playing, promising to recompense them if they won. The baron made similar offers, ostensibly to preserve the evening. The misers accepted after a show of reluctance—the old-maid custom—but only after the baron and chevalier had won, or the offer would have been an insult.

Mouche grew more spirited when a Demoiselle de Kergarouet arrived with her aunt. The Kergarouets insisted on the single name; the aunt treated the niece at Du Guénic and told her to look amiable—easy near Calyste, whom the Kergarouet girls adored. Brought up in modern ways, they cared little for five sous; stakes rose. The aunt’s presence animated the blind elder sister, who signaled stakes to the baroness by pressing her foot a number of times. Deciding whether to play when the basket was full became a private conflict—cupidity against fear—and Charlotte de Kergarouet’s luck invited chiding lectures on reserve from her aunt.

These nightly games, carried on for twenty years, were sometimes interrupted by gossip or politics; players often sat half an hour gabbing with cards fanned on their stomachs. If a counter was missing from the basket, everyone swore they had paid; usually the chevalier made up the deficiency and was accused of being absent-minded about his health. When he paid, Zéphirine and Jacqueline fussed that they themselves might have forgotten. Such small suspicions amused the company. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël would almost always accuse the rector of cheating when he won, to which he dryly replied, “It is singular that I never cheat except when I win the trick.”

Talk sometimes drifted to royal misfortunes, and evenings could end without gain but with emotion and complaints that mouche was dull. Once, after the Kergarouets arrived, the baroness urged the group to try whist and boston; they failed to understand the new games, voted them head-splitters, and returned to the tradition of mouche—Brittany triumphing over novelty.

While the rector dealt, the baroness asked the Chevalier du Halga the same health questions as the night before. The chevalier made a comic point of inventing ailments; he never complained of real wounds but fancied pains—his stock of comic maladies resisting medical cure. Light banter from Zéphirine and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël displayed the chevalier’s old wit and suggested a life rich in memories.

Conversation turned to the recent death of the Duc de Lenoncourt; the baron remarked he would be next and his wife gently rebuked him. The company noted the baron’s melancholy and afterward would say, “Monsieur du Guénic was sad to-night.” The phrase “The Baron du Guénic fails” became a conversational opener.

When asked about Thisbé, the chevalier mimicked the dog to expose his own hand—an attempt to make neighbors reveal whether they held Mistigris or the trump. The baroness observed the rector’s nose grow pale—a sure sign he had Mistigris—because that card pleased him.

“You had company today,” the chevalier said. The baroness replied it had been a cousin of her brother-in-law and told of the marriage of the Comtesse de Kergarouet, a Demoiselle de Fontaine, whom the chevalier recognized through the admiral. Then Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël astonished them with tales about Mademoiselle des Touches—tales so strange that, if true, Calyste would not be constantly with her. The baron pricked up his ears.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël suddenly had Mistigris and could not continue. “Your play, monsieur le baron,” she said. Zéphirine returned to knitting; the rector, informed about Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches, refrained. The baron inquired again: “What is so extraordinary?” “She smokes,” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël said, and added that the woman was squandering her estate.

The baroness then announced a long run of trumps and Mistigris; the victory horrified Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël, who for a moment forgot Calyste and Mademoiselle des Touches. By nine only the baroness and rector remained; the others had retired. The chevalier accompanied Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël home as usual, remarking on Zéphirine’s delight in devouring her gains, and noting the baroness’s abstraction.

At Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël's door she confided the cause: “Calyste is lost unless we marry him promptly. He loves Mademoiselle des Touches, an actress!” “Then send for Charlotte,” the chevalier advised. “I have; my sister will receive my letter tomorrow,” Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël replied.

That declaration set the household abuzz; any stranger’s arrival or stay in Guérande provoked talk. When silence fell in the baron’s and his sister’s rooms, the baroness looked at the rector, who toyed pensively with counters. “I see that you begin to share my anxiety about Calyste,” she said. “Did you notice Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël’s displeasure to-night?” he asked. She loved Calyste as a son; his conduct in the Vendée and Madame’s praise of his devotion had increased her affection. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël intended to execute a deed of gift leaving her property to whichever niece Calyste married. The rector acknowledged this hope for a richer Irish match but counseled prudence: Pen-Hoël’s savings and well-managed land were not to be scorned.

He then described Mademoiselle des Touches’ life: a stage-woman and writer who frequented theatres and artists, squandered her fortune on pamphleteers, painters, and musicians under the name Camille Maupin, and turned Les Touches into an improper house of dissipation where more wine was drunk in weeks than in all Guérande in a year. Last year the Demoiselles Bougniol had let lodgings to men suspected of being Blues who sang wicked songs and spent time at Les Touches. This was the woman Calyste adored; he would gallop to Nantes for an infamous book but not for the Church, and if called to fight for the royalist cause he might not answer if she wished him to stay.

“Oh, no!” cried the baroness. The rector feared to test Calyste outright—the mother would suffer too much—and promised to see Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël the next day and find out the truth. Jacqueline trusted him. “Calyste is our child and pupil—he will not let the devil inveigle him,” he said; “one fault is not vice.”

“You are only telling me details,” the baroness answered. “Was I not the first to notice the change in my Calyste? A mother feels keenly being second in her son’s heart. This is a trial of motherhood. I had hoped he would love someone noble and beautiful—not a stage-player, a masquerader, an author who feigns sentiments, a creature who will deceive and make him unhappy! She has had adventures—”

“With several men,” added the rector. “Yet this impious creature was born in Brittany! She dishonors her land. I shall preach about her next Sunday.”

“Don’t do that!” the baroness begged. “The peasants and paludiers might rush to Les Touches. Calyste is Breton; something dreadful might happen if he defended her as he would the Blessed Virgin.”

“It is now ten o’clock; I must bid you good-night,” said the abbé, lighting his lantern. “Who could have told me that a young man raised by you, trained by me in Christian ideas, fervent and spotless, would plunge into such mire?”

“But is it certain?” the baroness asked. “How could any woman help loving Calyste?”

“What proof other than her staying so long at Les Touches? In twenty-four years since coming of age she has never stayed so long,” the rector replied.

“A woman over forty!” exclaimed the baroness. “In Ireland they say such a woman is the most dangerous mistress for a young man.”

“As to that I cannot say,” the rector answered.

“I wish I had loved deeply, so I might counsel and comfort Calyste,” the baroness sighed. The rector did not cross the little courtyard alone; she accompanied him to the gate, hoping to hear Calyste’s step. They heard his tread fade and the parsonage door close behind the abbé.

The poor mother returned to the salon in deep distress: all the town now knew what she had thought hers alone. She trimmed the lamp-wick, took up her worsted-work, and waited for Calyste. The baroness hoped to make her son come home earlier and spend less time at Les Touches, but his visits had grown more frequent and later; the night before he arrived at midnight.

Anxious, she stitched rapidly. Piqued by a curiosity common to pure women, she wondered what charms the daughters of Baal could possess to lure men from family and duty. Sometimes she longed to meet Mademoiselle des Touches to judge her soberly. Her mind measured the dangers that the era’s innovations—so menacing to young souls, the rector warned—might work on her only child, who until now had been guileless and beautiful with fresh innocence.

Calyste, of Breton and Irish blood, had been raised with the utmost care. After infancy and maternal guidance, she entrusted him to the rector of Guerande for strict Christian instruction: Latin, Greek, cautious history, geography, and a limited range of French writers. He knew little of modern literature or current science. The Church continued the maternal work: Calyste remained a fervent Catholic, neither over-pious nor ridiculous.

For this noble son the baroness desired a quiet, provident life. She expected to inherit a few thousand pounds from an aunt; combined with the Guenics’ fortune she hoped to find a wife with a suitable income. A wealthy heiress like Charlotte de Kergarouet would have suited her; she regarded marriage pragmatically, ignorant of passionate love. Thus the thought that Mademoiselle des Touches threatened their prudential plans filled Fanny with foreboding.

Hearing midnight strike with tears, the baroness feared her son might not return; but his step soon sounded in the lane and she opened the gate joyfully.

“Oh!” cried Calyste, grieved, “my darling mother, why did you sit up for me? I have a pass-key and the tinder-box.”

“You know very well, my child, that I cannot sleep when you are out,” she said, kissing him.

In the salon she studied his face for signs of the evening. Calyste stirred maternal pride: except for the dark, energetic eyes from his father, he resembled his mother—golden hair, lovable mouth, curving fingers, soft white skin; though slightly girlish in look, his strength was Herculean. His fatigue looked like painful thought; his mother blamed passing excitement. She feared that “those beautiful pure cheeks” would be given to another woman and that passion would tarnish her son’s innocence.

It may seem odd that in a family of limited means the son had a velvet coat and the mother a velvet gown, but Fanny had wealthy London relations who sent gifts. Several sisters, married into wealth, took an interest in Calyste and hoped to find him an heiress.

“You stayed at Les Touches longer than last night, my dear one,” said the mother.

“Yes, dear mother,” he answered, offering no explanation. The curt reply clouded her brow. Mothers who love as Fanny did tremble before their sons; they perceive what love will take and yet feel joy in their children’s happiness.

“Go to bed, dear child,” she said, repressing her tears.

The next day Calyste slept until mid-day; his mother would not wake him. Mariotte served breakfast in his bed. At one o’clock the family sat in the salon to read and prepare for the three-o’clock dinner. The baroness read the Quotidienne; as she finished a paragraph she heard Calyste’s steps above and dropped the paper.

“Calyste must be going to dine again at Les Touches; he has dressed himself.”

“He amuses himself, the dear boy,” said the old sister.

Mariotte appeared.

“What is it?” she said; “anything wanted?”

“The chevalier dines at Les Touches; don’t cook the fish.”

“But we are not sure yet,” said the baroness.

“You seem annoyed, sister; I know it by your voice.” Fanny informed them that Monsieur Grimont had heard grave charges against Mademoiselle des Touches, who had so changed Calyste. The baron, amused, tried to dismiss it.

“Suppose Monsieur le chevalier does love Mademoiselle des Touches, where’s the harm?” said Mariotte. “She has thirty thousand francs a year and is very handsome.”

“A Guenic marry a des Touches!” exclaimed the old baron. “A des Touches could hardly be an actress,” added the old man with ancestral pride. The baroness answered that Camille Maupin wrote plays and books; the baron, surprised, shrugged. Mariotte, not inquisitive, left when Calyste entered.

“Are you going again to Les Touches, my Calyste?” the baroness asked.

“Les Touches is not respectable. Its mistress leads an irregular life; she will corrupt our Calyste. Already Camille Maupin has made him read many books; you knew all that and never said one word to your best friends!”

“The chevalier is discreet,” said his father.

“Too discreet,” said the jealous mother, noting a flush on her son’s brow.

“My dear mother,” said Calyste, kneeling beside her, “I didn’t think it necessary to publish my defeat. Mademoiselle des Touches, or Camille Maupin, rejected my love more than eighteen months ago during her last stay at Les Touches. She laughed at me gently, saying she might very well be my mother and that a woman of forty loving a minor were a crime against nature; she tried to comfort me and offered her friendship nobly. She has more heart than talent; she is generous. I am now her child. On her return lately, hearing she loves another, I have resigned myself. Do not repeat calumnies against her. Camille is an artist; she has genius; she leads an exceptional life not to be judged like ordinary lives.”

“My child,” said Fanny, “nothing can excuse a woman for not conducting herself as the Church requires. She fails in her duty to God and society by abjuring the gentle tenets of her sex. A woman sins even in going to a theatre; but to write the impieties that actors repeat, to roam the world, first with an enemy to the Pope, and then with a musician—Calyste, you can never persuade me such acts are deeds of faith, hope, or charity. Her fortune was given by God to do good; what good does she do with hers?”

Calyste sprang up. “Mother,” he said, “Camille is my friend; I cannot hear her spoken of this way; I would give my very life for her.”

“Your life!” said the baroness. “Your life is our life, the life of all of us.” The old sister, knitting rapidly, declared that education had caused the change: “Educated people did the evil—be educated if you choose!”

“Calyste,” she added later in a voice from her heart, “your father has never opened books; he speaks Breton, he fought for God and king.” The family’s conflicting views left the baroness agitated. Calyste whispered that it was impossible to explain now but promised a night’s talk after which she would bless Mademoiselle des Touches. He took his leave. The baron offered to visit Les Touches himself and report; the remark hinted at future confrontation.

Guerande had seen Calyste’s frequent visits to Les Touches and concluded Mademoiselle Félicité des Touches was passionately in love with the youth and worked sorceries. Rumor swelled; the rector and local notables, alarmed, related the strange life of the author who hid behind the masculine name Camille Maupin. To Abbé Grimont she was monstrous—a cross between a siren and an atheist—violating the social laws that bound womanhood.

Camille Maupin was the mask of Félicité des Touches, a Breton whose literary virility long led the world to suppose her a man. Her two volumes of plays published in 1822 provoked literary revolution in the clash of classicism and romanticism; she later wrote more plays and a novel that sustained her success. To understand why Félicité chose a man’s name, kept her freedom, and differed from Madame de Staël, one must recall her life.

Félicité des Touches became an orphan in 1793. Her property escaped confiscation though her father and brother were killed for the king; her mother died soon after, leaving her to a sister, a nun of Chelles. Madame de Faucombe took the child to Faucombe, near Nantes. The populace of Nantes seized the nuns and Félicité on a false charge; Thermidor released them. Her aunt died of fear; another sister left France; the third entrusted Félicité to her great-uncle, Monsieur de Faucombe, an old archaeologist in Nantes.

Monsieur de Faucombe, fond of amusement, left Félicité’s education to chance. She kept him company in his library and read widely. Thus she acquired theoretical knowledge of life without the innocence of ordinary upbringing. Her intellect was crammed with undigested learning; imagination ran riot while her personal life remained chaste. By eighteen she was as well-informed as a young man entering literary circles. This prodigious reading formed an unusual intellect without corrupting chastity—a surprising outcome to those who knew only convent routine.

She helped Faucombe by writing three of his books; the strain affected her health, and doctors prescribed horseback and society. She became an admirable horsewoman and recovered. At eighteen she entered society and produced sensation, called “the beautiful Mademoiselle des Touches.” Seeking the pleasures of a woman’s triumph, she discovered coldness toward tribute and contempt for trivial talk; resentment at family ridicule led her to cultivate coquetry and music. She studied with Steibelt and later in Paris perfected harmony, composing music for two operas whose true authorship is concealed.

Provincial mediocrity soon bored her; she returned to books, piano, Beethoven, and Faucombe. In 1812, at twenty-one, Faucombe gave her the guardianship accounts and she took control of fifteen thousand francs from Les Touches, twelve thousand from Faucombe, and a saved capital. She learned finance and local administration, invested wisely at the right moments, and by young adulthood had fifty thousand francs a year—force of will equal to a man of thirty. Habits of criticism enabled sober judgments about art and public affairs. Faucombe’s later illness kept her until his death as Europe convulsed under Napoleon.

A Royalist, she hastened to Paris with the Bourbons’ return. Political commotions preserved her mind from personal entanglements. In 1815 she bought a fine house in rue Mont Blanc and settled as an independent woman. Accustomed to controlling her life, she adopted modes of thought and action considered masculine. By 1816 she knew nothing of marriage as a life-scheme; her superior mind refused the abdication married life requires. She valued independence and felt disgust for maternity’s duties. These facts explain Camille Maupin’s anomalies: having known no normal parental education and raised by an archaeologist among books, she had not played with dolls.

Toward the close of 1817 Félicité felt bodily lassitude and feared aging. Between marriage and love she wanted freedom; she was still susceptible to admiration. At forty she retained twenty-five’s vivacity; Nature favored her, and meditation preserved youth.

A Breton type with darker features, Félicité’s face was longer than oval, like an Isis in bas-relief; olive in daylight, luminous by candlelight, it changed little with emotion. Heavy black hair, a broad forehead, and incisive eyes—pupils ringed with an orange circle—gave severity and depth to her glance. In passion her eyes could be sublime; in repose they were often calm. Her nose and mouth could express disdain and tenderness in turn; the chin was firm, the bust ample, waist slim; arms and hands were strong and delicate. The head’s carriage was majestic; lips and nostrils offered expressive mobility. Yet overall the face seemed tinged with sadness from constant meditation; she listened more than she spoke, startling by silence and a deep, attentive look; no educated spectator could see her without thinking of Cleopatra.

This anatomy of character suggested ambiguity: did she judge and not feel, or feel and judge at once? Her great mind made her often unpossessable, feared as a living symbol rather than an object of ordinary affection. Adventures of her life, partly rumored and not disavowed by Camille, reinforced such questions.

Her beauty, fortune, and position aided her fame. In 1817 she opened her house to artists, authors, scientists, and publicists; her salon resembled Baron Gérard’s: men of rank mingled with distinguished talent and Parisian women. Ambitious mothers hoped to marry in; diplomats and men of distinction came for intellectual amusements. Mademoiselle des Touches studied comedies of passion and ambition on display. She despised accepted flatteries and laughed at things others took seriously.

Ordinarily a woman feels, enjoys, and judges in succession; in Félicité this order reversed. Youth had been wrapped in knowledge and reflection. This transposition explained her life’s strangeness and talent and led inevitably to a first love late in life. A first secret affair had shown her the folly of valuing physical beauty as soul-beauty; she recovered with the help of a friend—a singular man, poet, critic, traveler—who took her to Italy to teach her Art and gave her frankness and literary grace. He roused her taste for German and English literature; in Rome in 1820 he deserted her for an Italian. That betrayal hardened her contempt for men and helped shape Camille Maupin’s public persona.

Back in Paris, she collaborated with Conti, the great musician, for whom she wrote librettos. Her two volumes of plays made Camille Maupin famous; she then published a short novel, one of the period’s masterpieces and a dangerous work akin to “Adolphe.” Her pseudonym’s motives remain obscure; perhaps a woman’s wish to escape fame while using a man’s name for criticism played a part.

Her celebrity grew through salon influence, wit, judgment, and learning. She became an authority; her sayings were quoted and she could not abandon the social role Paris bestowed. She was an exception: women admired her mind, men her beauty. Her friendships seemed platonic; far from Madame de Staël’s politics, she joked about Camille Maupin, admiring rivals with composure.

From 1817 to 1834 she visited Les Touches occasionally. Her first return followed the 1818 disillusion; she took lodgings in the village to contemplate herself, then had furniture sent from Paris to Les Touches. The public’s curiosity about her luxury filled the porter’s purse; visitors came. Later, after returning from Italy with Conti, appearances excited less interest. By the time of this story, news of her double existence had spread in Guerande; letters addressed to Camille Maupin at Les Touches arrived. In a region so Catholic and prejudiced, her singular life provoked rumors that alarmed Abbé Grimont; such a life seemed improper to him.

During her present stay at Les Touches she was not alone: Claude Vignon, a scornful, powerful writer, had been her guest for years. Knowing his laziness and penury, she still appeared inclined to marry him—ambition, dread of solitary old age, and a desire to secure a superior man with her fortune may have influenced this. Yet she was misleading both Calyste and Claude; her heart was undergoing the most violent convulsion possible for a soul as strong as hers. Too late she saw life lighted by youthful love, shining again in a heart she thought past such fires.

A few hundred yards from Guérande the Breton soil gives way to salt‑marshes and dunes. We descend into a sea‑left desert along a rugged ravine where carriages have never passed: irregular ponds, muddy banks where salt is made, and a narrow arm of the sea separating the mainland from Croisic. Geographically a peninsula, Croisic clings to Brittany only by quicksands joining it to Batz and seems more an island.

Where the road to Croisic branches from the main road stands a country house with a large garden and a grove of trimmed, wind‑twisted pines—some umbrellalike, some denuded, their trunks reddened by peeled bark—trees that prepare the eye for the marshes and dunes beyond, looking like a stiffened ocean. The house, of slaty stone with granite courses, is plain: a wall pierced by evenly spaced windows—small leaded panes below, larger ones above—attics under an immense pointed roof with two gables and dormers; circular windows under each gable, one facing the sea, the other toward Guérande. One façade faces the Guérande road, the other toward the desert ending at Croisic, and a brook escapes through the park wall, crossing the road to be lost in the sands.

The house’s gray tones harmonize with the bleak landscape. The park is an oasis; at its entrance a mud hut shelters custom‑house officials. Though not large in land, most of the estate lying in Guérande, Les Touches yields over ten thousand francs a year from marshes and farms. Once a fief reduced by the Revolution, it is still called “the château” by the paludiers and would still harbor “seigneur” if the fief had not passed in the female line. When Félicité set about restoring Les Touches she, an artist in taste, kept the desolate exterior that gives it a prison‑like air, altering only by placing an entrance gate enlivened by two brick columns supporting an arch and planting trees in the court.

The ground floor follows the plan of many eighteenth‑century country houses, built on ruins of an older castle: a large paneled entrance hall—turned by Félicité into a billiard room—opens into an immense salon with six windows and the dining room; the kitchen communicates with the dining room through an office. Camille has shown noble simplicity in arranging this floor, avoiding ostentation. The salon, painted gray, is furnished in old mahogany with green silk coverings; the dining room contains great mahogany buffets, horsehair chairs, and fine Audran engravings in mahogany frames. A heavy wooden staircase, covered with a green carpet, leads up.

Above are two suites separated by the staircase. Mademoiselle des Touches has taken the one facing sea and marshes, arranging it as a little salon, a large chamber, and two cabinets—one for dressing, the other a study and writing room. The other suite she made into guest apartments; servants’ rooms occupy the attic. The guest rooms are simply furnished; a certain fantastic luxury is reserved for her own apartment. In that somber, melancholy habitation looking over the austere landscape she gathered the most imaginative art she could find. The little salon is hung with Gobelin tapestry set in carved oak. Heavy silken hangings—brocades shot with crimson and gold against green and yellow—are gathered into mighty pleats and trimmed with fringes, cords, and tassels worthy of a church. The salon contains a valuable chest or cabinet, a carved ebony table, a Venetian secretary inlaid with ivory arabesques, and other noble Gothic furniture. Her bedroom is strictly Louis XV: a carved white bedstead with Cupids, a canopy, blue silk hangings, a Pompadour dressing table, a duchesse, a chaise‑longue, a small sofa, silk window curtains lined with pink satin, and a Savonnerie carpet—the elegant dainties of eighteenth‑century women.

Her study, entirely contemporary and resembling a boudoir, contrasts with the Louis‑Quinze gallantries and contains a charming collection of mahogany furniture. The bookshelves are full, but the room is cluttered with the trivialities of a woman’s existence mingled with masculine articles: pistols, a narghile, a riding‑whip, a hammock, a rifle, a man’s blouse, tobacco, pipes, a knapsack—an odd combination that paints Félicité in bold strokes.

Beyond the park stretches the desolate landscape—the last vegetation of the continent—whose peculiar beauty any great soul entering that room would notice. Melancholy squares of water, divided by white salt crust paths where paludiers, dressed in white, rake and gather salt into mulons; saline exhalations that keep birds away and stifle botanical growth; sands brightened only by one hardy plant bearing rosy flowers and the Chartreux pansy; a salt lake, sandy dunes, the little town of Croisic afloat like a Venice, and the mighty ocean flinging foam upon granite rocks—this sight uplifts and saddens. The sublime here produces a longing for unknown things perceived by the soul; these wild harmonies belong to great spirits and great sorrows.

This desert scene, where the sun’s rays reflected on water and sand shine on Batz and ripple on Croisic’s roofs, occupied Camille’s thoughts for days; she seldom cared for the cool groves about Guérande. Her soul struggled with a deep inward anguish.

When Calyste first saw the house’s gables beyond the roadside furze and distorted pines, the air seemed lighter to him. Guérande had been a prison; his life was at Les Touches. A young man full of ardor but inexperienced, Calyste found in Félicité more than a woman—he found a grand presence before knowing her merely as a woman. His adoration, impulsive and needy, survived Félicité’s repeated, inexplicable refusals; it was more the impulse to love than love itself. Camille had analyzed her refusal as generosity rather than cruelty—a foresight Calyste did not perceive.

At Les Touches the young countryman encountered riches of a new world: a different language, modern music and painters, contemporary imaginative literature; in short, the great Nineteenth Century in all its critical spirit and desire for renovation. Félicité initiated him into these things. For him she was the mother of his intellect: kind, elevated, inspiring. A woman is always adorable to a man in whom she stirs love, even when she seems not to return it.

At that time Félicité was giving him music lessons. Her grand apartments and coquettish private rooms were vivified by a strange, undefinable spirit. The modern world’s poesy sharply contrasted with Guérande’s dullness: on one side the thousand developments of art, on the other the sameness of uncivilized Brittany. No wonder the poor lad, bored by provincial pleasures like his mother, quivered as he approached the house, rang, and crossed the court—reactions not felt by a mature man.

One morning, hearing the piano, Calyste supposed Camille was in the salon, but the music ceased in the billiard hall. He ran up the thick‑carpeted stairs, slowed as he neared the top, and perceived something unusual in the music. Félicité was playing for herself, communing with her being. Instead of entering, he sat on a Gothic seat beneath a carved window. Camille’s improvisation was mysteriously melancholy—like a cry from the depths, the prayer of a hidden plaint. She had varied and extended the introduction to the cavatina “Mercy for thee, mercy for me!” from Robert le Diable, then suddenly sang the words heart‑rendingly and broke off. Calyste entered and saw why: Camille turned to him with a face wet with tears, wiped them with a handkerchief and said simply, “Good‑morning.” She was beautiful in her morning gown: a red chenille net letting coils of black hair escape; a short upper garment like a Greek peplum revealing cambric trousers with embroidered frills and Turkish slippers red and gold.

“What is the matter?” cried Calyste.

“He has not returned,” she answered, looking out at the sands, sea, and marshes.

That answer explained all: Camille was awaiting Claude Vignon. Calyste wanted to find him.

“Where are you going?” she asked.

“To find him,” he replied.

“Dear child!” she said, taking his hand and drawing him toward her with one of those moist glances that reward a youthful soul. “You are distracted! Where could you find him on that wide shore?”

“I will find him.”

“Your mother would be in mortal terror. Stay. Besides, I choose it.” She seated him. “Don’t pity me. The tears you see are the tears a woman likes to shed. We have a faculty not in man—that of abandoning ourselves to our nervous nature and driving our feelings to an extreme. By imagining certain situations we end in tears, and sometimes illness. Women’s fancies are of the heart more than the mind. You have come just in time; solitude is bad for me. I am not the dupe of his professed desire to go to Croisic and see the rocks without me. He meant to leave us alone together; he is jealous, or pretends to be—he is young and handsome.”

“Why not have told me this before? What must I do? Must I stay away?” Calyste asked, his own tears betraying him.

“You are an angel!” she cried, then gaily sang the “Stay! stay!” of Matilde in Guillaume Tell, turning a grave answer into a dainty jest. “He only wants to make me think he loves me more than he does. He knows how much I desire his happiness. Perhaps he feels humiliated to be inferior to me; perhaps he suspects you. But even if his only crime is to take his pleasure without me and not share the ideas this new place gives him, is that not enough? Ah! I am no more loved by that great brain than I was by the musician, the poet, the soldier! Sterne is right; names signify much; mine is a bitter sarcasm. I shall die without finding in any man the love that fills my heart, the poesy that is in my soul—”

She stopped, arms pendent, head back, eyes fixed on a carpet pattern. The pain of great minds is imposing; it reveals a vast soul whose affections belong to a people and thus affect the world.

“Why did you reject my—” Calyste began, but Camille’s beautiful hand laid on his stopped him.

“Nature changed her laws in granting me youth beyond my due,” she said. “I rejected your love from egoism. Sooner or later the difference in our ages would have parted us. I am thirteen years older than he, and even that is too much.”

“You will be beautiful at sixty,” cried Calyste.

“God grant it,” she smiled. “Besides, dear child, I want to love. In spite of his cold heart, lack of imagination, cowardly indifference, and consuming envy, I believe greatness hides there; I hope to galvanize him, to save him from himself, to attach him to me. Alas! I have a clear mind, but a blind heart.”

She analyzed herself with terrible honesty, suffering and dissecting her feelings as a scientist might explain disease. “I have brought him here to judge him, and he is already bored,” she went on. “He pines for Paris; the nostalgia of criticism is on him; he has no author to pluck, no system to undermine. He dares not commit some debauch in this house that might lift his ennui. Alas! my love is not intoxicating enough to soothe his brain. Make him drunk at dinner tonight and I’ll know if I am right. I will say I am ill and stay in my room.”

Calyste flushed from neck to brow; even his ears burned.

“Oh! forgive me,” she cried. “How can I heedlessly deprave your girlish innocence! Forgive me, Calyste—” She paused. “There are some noble natures who say: ‘If I had my life to live again, I would do the same.’ I, who do not think myself weak, say, ‘I would be a woman like your mother, Calyste.’ To have a Calyste—oh! what happiness! I could be humble and submissive—and yet I have harmed only myself. But a woman cannot stand alone in society except in a primitive state. Affections not in harmony with social or natural laws, not obligatory, escape us. Suffering for suffering, we might as well be useful where we can. What care I for those children of my cousin Faucombe? I have not seen them these twenty years and they are married to merchants. You are my son who never cost me the miseries of motherhood; I shall leave you my fortune and make you happy—so far as money can, dear treasure of beauty and grace.”

“You would not take my love,” said Calyste, “and I shall return your fortune to your heirs.”

“Child!” Camille cried in a guttural voice, letting tears fall. “Will nothing save me from myself?” she added after a moment.

“You said you had a history to tell me, and a letter to—” the generous youth began, wishing to divert her; she would not let him finish.

“You are right to remind me. I will be honest before all else. I will sacrifice no one—Yes, it was too late yesterday, but today we have time,” she said cheerfully. “I will keep my promise; while I tell you that history I will sit by the window and watch the road to the marshes.”

Calyste drew a great Gothic chair near the window and opened a sash. Camille, sharing an oriental taste with her illustrious sister, took a magnificent Persian narghile given by an ambassador. She filled the nipple with patchouli, cleaned the bochettino, perfumed the goose‑quill attached to the mouthpiece, lit the yellow leaves, set the blue‑and‑gold enameled vase at some distance, and rang for tea.

“Will you have cigarettes?—Ah! I am always forgetting you do not smoke. Purity such as yours is rare! The hand of Eve herself, fresh from the hand of her Maker, alone is innocent enough to stroke your cheek.”

Calyste colored; sitting on a stool at Camille’s feet, he did not perceive the deep emotion that for a moment seemed to overwhelm her.

“I promised you this tale of the past, and here it is,” Camille began.

She told Calyste the letter came from the Marquise de Rochefide. The Rochefide family, less ancient than the Casterans, sought a prestigious match for their son; the Comtesse de Montcornet recommended Mademoiselle Béatrix-Maximilienne-Rose de Casteran, the youngest daughter of a father who aimed to marry his two daughters without dowries to preserve the estate for his son. Béatrix, raised at the château de Casteran, had married in 1828 at twenty. Camille called her what provincials call original—independent, enthusiastic, absorbed by art—a temperament dangerous for a woman, since only men have the support to keep balance at those edges.

The dowager de Casteran approved the match. Rochefide appeared superior: the marriage linked his family to other nobles and he had nearly obtained a peerage under Charles X, lost after the July revolution. Vain and foolish, he mistook Béatrix’s refinement and reserve for frigidity and soon returned to bachelor habits. He assumed a proud, artistic wife would content herself with salon pleasures; but he combined vanity with petty jealousy and sudden cruelty. Camille analyzed how this temperament and Béatrix’s admiration bred rivalry: Béatrix wanted her own salon and tried to draw Camille’s circle away, attracting only superficial acquaintances. Camille thought Béatrix thirsted for celebrity yet conceded the marquise had grandeur of soul, regal pride, clear ideas, and an uncommon facility in art, theology, and music—though sometimes affected by arcane learning.

Camille described Béatrix’s appearance in spare strokes: an ethereal blonde, slender and erect, with a long, pointed face and pale sea-green eyes; a bold forehead, a disdainful mouth, pale cheeks, fine shoulders, and an exquisite waist though not a faultlessly beautiful figure. In certain dress — cherry velvet and lace — her carriage and nobility could seem divine. Camille admitted Béatrix left an ineffaceable impression.

Félicité showed Calyste a painting by Mieris; Camille joked about the charms of blondes and warned Calyste not to fall for Béatrix from her portrait. Béatrix, she said, was severe and elegant, sometimes revealing Southern passion; an angelic flash would appear and vanish. For three years after her marriage she judged the world from her height; the July revolution and social changes unsettled her. She embraced some new intellectual currents while wishing to protect the nobility; finding limited place for individual superiority, she chose personal happiness over fading social power.

At Camille’s salon she met Gennaro Conti, a celebrated Neapolitan composer whose voice people adored as a soul rather than mere sound. Béatrix fell madly in love and took him from Camille. The act, painful for Camille, was fair play: Béatrix confided to Camille and made her arbiter of her choice. Camille admired Béatrix’s conduct—she preserved her dignity and displayed a grandeur in womanhood that men often cannot appreciate. Yet Camille confessed she had been fascinated by Conti: she had tested him, knowing him charming but vain and hypocritical in love.

Camille portrayed Conti as a consummate charlatan of feeling—insincere, theatrical, insatiable for applause; he posed as devoted to art but was often self-centered. His warmth and chivalry deceived many women; Camille had probed and seen his false sentiment, and Conti now hated her for seeing through him. She had told Béatrix nothing.

Camille had received Conti and Béatrix together in her salon to keep the affair discreet, forcing Conti to dissemble admirably. Béatrix soon told Camille she was leaving for Italy with Conti. Camille was not surprised: Béatrix felt united to him and, weary of social constraints, chose to escape into love. Conti was elated; Camille warned that he loved himself more than any woman. After a scene of fury he borrowed money from Camille for the journey. Béatrix left Rochefide a letter and went to Italy for two years; she wrote charming letters to Camille, the only woman she trusted. Conti, short of funds, accepted an offer to write a French opera, and Béatrix wrote from Genoa that they might soon return to Paris. Camille handed Calyste Béatrix’s letter.

Claude Vignon then entered unexpectedly, casting an ominous presence. He was thirty-seven, prematurely bald, his once-handsome face grown haggard; he resembled a youthful Raphael but now his features had thickened and his expressions were worn by solitude. His pale blue eyes were clouded; his face suggested deep thought but little action. Camille described him as a critic whose intellect penetrated everything but whose energy failed in outward life: he demolishes but does not create. He was simple yet wily, indulgent in excesses, prone to paralysis—an arch-critic who contemplated and did not act. Camille liked the idea of making Claude more active; he fancied himself a politician and writer but habitually let opportunity slip away.

Small talk followed, with veiled jests and loverly badges about entrances “by the door” or window-scaling. Claude’s sardonic manner disturbed Calyste, who felt uneasy. Claude interrupted Calyste’s earnestness about the letter with indifferent, mocking comments about love, angering the young man. Calyste, rash and sincere, was reproached by Camille and Félicité, who tried to shield him. He left in a storm of emotion, kissed Camille’s hand, and wept; Claude smirked that the youth would love much. Félicité announced that Madame de Rochefide might arrive, perhaps with Conti, which interested Claude, who had seen a vessel offshore near Croisic that could bring them.

Calyste, returning home, brooded on Béatrix’s letter and Camille’s portrait. At home, his mother Fanny, Aunt Zephirine, and servants created a cozy domestic scene. Calyste unfolded the letter again and imagined Béatrix as Camille had described. He told his mother how Béatrix had opened his mind, teaching him about Paris, music, literature, Italian, and social nuances; she had become, in effect, the mother of his intellect. Fanny feared the change but, moved by her son’s devotion, softened. Aunt Zephirine urged a prudent, bourgeois path: marriage to a good Breton girl, a tranquil life to secure the estate and heirs; practical counsel that contrasted with Calyste’s romantic ardor.

Calyste flared against such domestic plans, insisting he could not be confined to a married monk’s life; his imagination had been inflamed by Parisian civilization, books, and women; he longed for passion, beauty, freedom. His mother, seeing his earnestness, embraced him and blessed his pursuit of happiness while pleading prudence.

After dinner Calyste returned to Les Touches under pretext of returning the letter. Claude Vignon and Félicité remained at table; Félicité indulged Claude’s gourmandise and the dinner displayed Camille’s Parisian taste, a contrast to the Guénic family’s simpler comforts. Calyste presented the letter; Claude read only the first page and observed that Béatrix’s love for Conti seemed bound more by pride than passion: “She is held to Conti now by pride only; she is condemned to love him quand même.” Camille protested that Béatrix’s nature was too grand to change, and reminded them Conti would accompany her.

Claude’s cynical remarks pierced Calyste. When Claude casually suggested Béatrix was the right match for Calyste, the youth was wounded. Félicité admonished Claude not to plant dangerous ideas in the impressionable young man. Claude, enjoying wordplay and satire, pursued discussions about youth’s infatuations with older women—arguing that young men often adore older, semi-dowager women because they possess experience, savoir-faire, dignity, and absolute devotion that captivates youth: their love, he said, is vast and unrelieved by vanity.

The evening continued with wit and anecdotes; Claude and Félicité described Parisian life to Calyste, who was charmed—intellect had immense seduction for someone “all heart.” Claude predicted the Marquise and Conti might land at Croisic the next day, having possibly come on the Danish vessel he’d seen. Camille colored at the suggestion.

Calyste arrived late that night to his anxious mother, insisting he’d never had so delightful an evening: genius, he rhapsodized, could create life and choice among women. His mother tenderly kissed him and reminded him that love might suffice. Calyste pledged he would love in her stead for those who might have adored him. Fanny counseled him to choose noble women if he must pursue love, and bade him be prudent though she could not bind his heart.

Thus the scene closed with Calyste torn between domestic duty and the passionate horizons opened by Béatrix’s letter and Camille’s salon—a youth intoxicated by ideas, love, and the promise of Paris, watched over by a family that loved him and feared for his future.

What young man, brimming with restrained feeling, would not slip off to Croisic to see Madame de Rochefide and watch her incognito? Calyste surprised his parents by leaving at dawn, moving with nervous agility and hugging the walls of Les Touches to avoid being seen. Ashamed of his ardor and afraid of ridicule—Félicité and Vignon were so observant—he felt his very forehead transparent.

At the shore, by a stone embankment near the ferry shelter, two boats arrived loaded with trunks that announced distinguished travellers. One held only a maid in a straw bonnet with a green veil and a footman. When a boatman called him by name, Calyste, annoyed, denied expecting anyone. Yet a chest stamped MME. LA MARQUISE DE ROCHEFIDE caught his eye like a talisman; in the desert of his newly awakened desire he fastened on the first woman presented. Béatrix thus inherited the love Camille had rejected.

As the luggage landed Calyste searched for another boat that might reveal Béatrix; he stood absorbed, arms folded. Often we submit to our own inventions; chance has less to do with fate than we suppose. The maid, fretting about the marquise’s dressing if no one came to Les Touches, told him Mademoiselle des Touches had fetched her that morning. Calyste, fearing he’d blundered, rushed toward Guérande, doubling to avoid servants of Les Touches. At the mall he walked and reflected, convinced the marquise had no inkling of his agitation.

His fancy fastened on the marquise. He had not known such terror and joy with Camille; these vague emotions rise like poems in an untutored soul. Fired by imagination, youth can reach the heights of love in silence before ever meeting its object.

On the mall he saw the Chevalier du Halga with Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël, who mentioned Calyste. The chevalier advised that if Charlotte de Kergarouët came she should be kept near Calyste for months so the girl might fall in love by daily contact and they could marry in winter. A girl of sixteen, he claimed, could carry off the prize from a woman of forty. Calyste, overhearing, felt repugnance at the notion of being imposed a girl; his thoughts were fixed on the marquise, not on fortune or a match. He returned home at five, where his mother gave him a letter from Camille:

My dear Calyste,—The beautiful marquise has come; we count on you to help us celebrate her arrival. Claude says you will play Bice and she Dante. It is for our honor as Bretons, and yours as a du Guenic, to welcome a Casteran. Come soon. Your friend, Camille Maupin. Come as you are, without ceremony.

Curious about the marquise’s family, Fanny asked her husband who the Casterans were; the baron, dabbling in heraldry, identified them as an old Norman house. He admitted ignorance about the Rochefides, which partly reassured Fanny but also made her fear her son’s exposure to seduction.

Calyste went to Les Touches with violent but inward movements: tight throat, swelling heart, feverish haste that he tried to slow but could not. He found the company assembled in Camille’s little salon at about six, twilight spreading through the room. Camille, lifting the tapestry, announced him as “the future deputy of Brittany,” punctual as a king. He bowed to the marquise, who inclined her head; he did not at first look at her but took Claude Vignon’s proffered hand.

Camille presented Gennaro Conti, a medium-height, thin man with chestnut hair and a freckled complexion whose head resembled Lord Byron’s; Conti was proud of the likeness. The marquise murmured to Félicité that Calyste was “handsome as an angel.” Sitting between the sofa and the two ladies, Calyste observed the marquise in the half-light: reclining like a white serpentine shape, more beautiful than Camille’s portrait had suggested. Félicité had done her friend a kindness; Béatrix was superior to Camille’s sketch. Calyste took in her delicate, diaphanous features—diaphanous forehead, long neck, waist seemingly graspable, bare shoulders like camellia petals, a transparent fichu revealing her outline. Her white muslin gown with blue flowers, silver filigree earrings, and curls starred with bluebells completed an image that he carved into his soul. Between Béatrix’s fair, aristocratic force and Félicité’s darker gentleness, Calyste was seized at first sight by a love that crowned his hopes and fears. He felt sudden power and rivalry, especially toward Conti.

Dinner was announced. Camille gave Calyste the marquise’s arm; descending the old staircase felt like going into battle. He trembled, sweat forming as his arm shook; the marquise asked if anything was the matter. He stammered that he had never seen any woman so beautiful except his mother. Félicité, predicting his forgetfulness, prodded him to sit beside the marquise on her right, giving Claude the left. Camille retained Conti by her side to keep an eye on “coquetries.” The marquise, removing her gloves, remarked on the good opportunity with a poet.

By candlelight Béatrix seemed even more lovely: satiny forehead, spangled eyes, curls like gold, a revealed nape and graceful neck—unlike Camille’s, the mark of another character. Calyste could barely pretend to eat; nervousness drained his appetite. The ardor of his heart, restrained by moral reserve, left him in inward conflict — long hesitations and tender debate typical of the pure-hearted. Félicité’s eyes, noticing the youth’s adoration, issued repressive glances like avalanches of snow; Calyste tried to control his tears and, when Félicité asked why he ate nothing, forced himself to join the conversation.

But Madame de Rochefide paid him little attention. Camille, narrating her Italian journey, amused the men and women, while Claude Vignon’s sarcastic remarks about love—its root in desire and artifice—turned the table pensive. Calyste felt joy in looking at Béatrix mingled with pain at her apparent indifference. Conti searched the marquise’s eyes to guess her thoughts; his presence deepened Calyste’s unease. After dinner Camille drew Calyste aside.

“My dear Calyste,” she said, “you are acting in a manner that embarrasses the marquise; she may be delighted with your admiration, but she cannot accept it. Pray control yourself.”

“He was hard to me; she will never care for me,” Calyste replied. “If she does not I shall die.”

“Die! you! My dear Calyste, you are a child. Would you have died for me?”

“You have made yourself my friend,” he answered.

After coffee, Vignon asked Conti to sing. Camille sat at the piano. Together they sang the duet Dunque il mio bene tu mia sarai from Zingarelli’s Romeo e Giulietta, a passage of tragic modern poignancy whose phrase “Di tanti palpiti” expresses love’s grandeur. Calyste, sitting in the armchair where Félicité had related the marquise’s story, listened rapt. Béatrix and Vignon were on either side of the piano; Conti’s sublime voice, mingling with Félicité's, turned the music into a poem of divine melancholy—the farewell of two swans to life. The room fell under feelings beyond vulgar applause.

“Ah! music is the first of arts!” exclaimed the marquise.

“Camille thinks youth and beauty the first of poesies,” retorted Claude, whose sarcasm had already struck Camille. Béatrix, searching the room’s faces for the music’s effect, saw Calyste’s white, tear-wet face. Moved, and for Conti’s sake as much as the scene, she looked quickly toward the musician. Music had pierced Calyste’s heart, and Conti’s genius overwhelmed him; despite Camille’s warnings, he now believed in the soul’s beauty expressed by such an artist. How could he compete with a voice that entered the soul like another soul?

Calyste was left in despair and reverence. His ingenuous admission of his own nothingness showed on his face mingled with admiration. Camille, touched, cried that Conti would never obtain applause equal to that child's homage, and urged them to sing a trio. Béatrix joined. Overwhelmed, Calyste rose softly, retreated to an open-door bedroom, flung himself on a sofa, and sat with his head in his hands, plunged in meditation.

X. DRAMA

Claude Vignon enters Calyste’s room and tells him what he believes Calyste most needs to hear: Camille loves him. Calyste, who thinks himself pledged to the marquise Beatrix, is stunned. Claude, weary of life and unable yet to leave it, confesses he has suspected this for days. He explains he did not loiter in the marshes for pleasure and that when he returned to find Calyste with Camille his bitterness came from wounded pride. He plans to leave Les Touches—perhaps with Conti—preferring to witness from afar the passions he finds so rare in France, but advises Calyste to be cautious: youth entering such dangerous contests can be ruined.

Calyste, bewildered and unable to reconcile Camille’s declared love with Beatrix’s hold on him, listens while Claude confronts Felicite Maupin. Claude accuses her of coming to Paris last year desperately in love with Calyste and using him as a screen to conceal her adoration. He condemns her scheme as grand and ignoble, saying she should have chosen a common man or a distracted one to conceal her designs. He tells her she has never been loved and will not be, for her strength repels those who might love her. Felicite asks why love flees her; he answers she is not lovable because she does not yield.

When Calyste appears, Camille, deeply moved and more beautiful than before, betrays fear. Claude tells Calyste plainly that Felicite loves him—but not tenderly, rather in the peculiar way of a woman who delights in the suffering of longing, “in some sense a man,” he adds sardonically. He counsels the young man to use Beatrix’s attraction as a shield for Camille, arguing that Calyste’s passion now endangers the older woman. Humiliated and weeping, Camille cannot meet their eyes. Calyste, struck, falls at her feet and kisses her hands; she implores Claude not to abandon her.

Claude suggests there is no danger because Calyste is already enamored of the marquise, which will be a better barrier. Camille, with a dominating look, answers that she will accept loving without hope: “Do not love me, Calyste; but I will love you as no woman will!” Claude kneels and kisses her hand.

Calyste returns to Guerande dazed. At home the household notices his agitation; he tells his mother with unexpected candor, “Camille loves me, but I love her no longer.” The next day he hastens to the port but arrives as Camille and Beatrix wave to a departing steamer. Beatrix gives him an imperious look; Camille’s smile makes the marquise conscious of the vulgarity of such public behavior. After a drive where Calyste escorts the ladies and bids Camille and Beatrix goodbye, he joins a dinner at the du Guenic’s, where others assume he is free and nearly engaged to Charlotte de Pen-Hoel. Calyste is inwardly silent, torn between provincial ties and the allure of Parisian wit.

XI. FEMALE DIPLOMACY

Calyste reaches Les Touches as Camille and Beatrix leave the salon. He offers his arm to Felicite, who playfully chides him for abandoning the viscountess. Conversation reveals social links and sends subtle signals: Beatrix suggests the Kergarouet girl would suit him, which Camille quickly denies. Mortified by Beatrix’s coolness toward Charlotte, Calyste is silent; Camille accuses Beatrix of coldness and of driving him toward the marquise.

Taking him into her chamber, Camille says plainly what must be done: if Calyste wishes to win Beatrix, he must appear to still love Camille. He knows he is too candid to feign, so she will take the deceit upon herself. He must come every day, spend mornings studying with her four hours, and be with her in the evenings—obey her blindly. If he speaks privately with the marquise, he will lose her forever. Trusting Camille, Calyste submits, convinced that within a week Beatrix will love him. He goes home restored to gaiety; the card players praise him, and he returns to Les Touches to act the ruse.

After he leaves Camille, exhausted, confesses to Beatrix: “I am forty and I love him!” She laments lost youth and the shame of being loved from pity, and asks Beatrix—who loves Conti—to be noble and help keep Calyste. Flattered, the marquise resolves to behave generously; she will give him a cool shoulder while secretly enjoying the power of inspiring a man’s passion.

Camille orchestrates a series of staged scenes to draw Beatrix’s attention: small signals, arranged glances, and invitations offered and declined in ways that display regret and restraint. These feminine maneuvers become a duel of snares and feints. Camille plays the jealous, self-effacing woman who praises Calyste’s purity and nobility, making Beatrix perceive both the worth and the danger of the young man. Beatrix, vacillating between pride and curiosity, affects gentle indifference but is inwardly stirred; her etiquette and hauteur mask a private satisfaction in being coveted.

For six days Camille concentrates every art toward her end. Calyste, obeying but increasingly tortured, feels the plan’s deception nearly undoing him; he craves to speak with Beatrix and read her eyes. One morning he slips into the garden and surprises the marquise. Their talk is awkward: she tells him he has neither pleased nor displeased her, and when he says he no longer loves Camille she asks why he visits her every morning. Camille appears, furious, and drags him away, reproaching him for flouting their agreement. The marquise, stunned by Camille’s dramatic self-abnegation and the extremity of her proposed generosity, cannot quite grasp the part she is asked to play—yet the abnegation is typical of Camille’s grandeur.

At breakfast Beatrix resolves to suppress any rising love. She remains gently indifferent—tormenting Calyste without harshness. Camille proposes an excursion, but Beatrix refuses, fearing complications: Conti is returning to fetch her. The prospect of Conti’s return turns Calyste pale. Despite Camille’s urging and his entreaties, Beatrix remains inflexible. Calyste leaves Les Touches plunged into despair, brooding on how to force an explanation with the marquise and caught between arranged appearances and the tumult of real feeling.

When Calyste returned home he shut himself in his room until dinner and afterwards retreated there again. At ten his mother, uneasy at his absence, found him among blotted, half-torn sheets: he was composing a letter to Béatrix, tormented by jealousy about Camille after the marquise’s manner in the garden. As with all first love-letters, it poured from the heart and repeated itself; still, the baroness, hearing her son read it aloud, felt their old mansion glow with his passion.

In this letter Calyste declares that seeing Béatrix has made his love stronger than any dream; he needs not praise her beauty, for he has studied and “penetrated” her delicacy and believes he understands every trait of hers. He asks whether she has ever been loved, understood, and adored as she deserves, and suggests that his comprehension makes him worthy of her notice. He insists his passion concerns her more than himself: he has aged by a week of suffering caused innocently by her; he is no common lover and is drawn to her like a child to light. Absolute devotion, unbounded faith—these merit nothing unless returned; her cold, stern eye turns him to ice, and yet her disdain cannot outweigh his love.

Calyste confesses he once loved Félicité but forgot her at sight of Béatrix; he calls Béatrix his truth. He speaks of Camille dismissing stagnant love as wretched, of her mingling tears with his and rejecting a hopeless passion, and so he claims he is free to love Béatrix on earth and in heaven. If Béatrix loved him she would not make Camille’s arguments; they are young and could fly together. He pledges submission, patience, mute adoration, only begging not to be needlessly wounded. He believes Béatrix cannot love him without losing self-esteem, so he asks no return; still he cannot bear sustained coldness, which would make him fear she detests him. He imagines her passing through his life like Dante’s Beatrice—distant, white, cold—and asks for the music of pity as his crown. He cites his past behavior when Camille loved Claude Vignon as proof of his restraint, promising to bury his feelings unless driven to despair. A word of praise would make him bear martyrdom; a continued silence would make him think she feared him.

The letter continues in this repetitive, impassioned strain: he calls her his Light and Sun, asks to be allowed to see her, suggests an excursion to Croisic and Batz as a test—absence of an invitation would be an answer. Though unlikely to sway the marquise, the letter moved his mother to tears. “My child, you are not happy,” she said, terrified and gentle. Calyste, perplexed how to send the letter, followed his mother into the salon with it burning in his pocket. The Chevalier du Halga was present; Charlotte de Kergarouet tried to use Calyste’s distraction for her own aims. Calyste wandered like a distracted butterfly. When a card game ended he asked the chevalier in a whisper how best to deliver a letter secretly to a mistress. “Slip it into the maid’s hand with a louis or two,” the chevalier suggested—though he admitted it was better to give it oneself. Calyste snatched his hat and ran to Les Touches.

Appearances there were electric: Camille and Béatrix sat together in the little salon. Calyste flung himself impulsively beside the marquise, took her hand, and so rapidly slipped the letter into it that Félicité did not perceive it. His heart tingled as Béatrix pressed his hand and, without interrupting her words, slipped the letter into her glove. “You fling yourself on a woman’s dress without mercy,” she laughed. “Calyste is wanting in common sense,” said Félicité, openly rebuking him. He kissed Camille’s hand and, after a brief, embarrassed conversation, left Les Touches at eleven, having met Camille’s piercing glance.

That wakeful night Calyste searched Guérande’s main street many times for an answer. At last the marquise’s waiting-woman brought a note, which he read in the garden grotto. Béatrix’s reply is careful, proud, and composed: she calls him a noble child but still a child and reminds him that he is bound to Camille, who adores him. She insists he would not find in her the perfections that distinguish Camille, nor the happiness Camille can give. She speaks plainly of her situation: she loves and is beloved, and the circumstances of her life forbid accepting homage. For a woman in her position, a man declaring love is not a flattering thing but an insult; yielding would lower her and shame Camille, whose love must not be robbed a second time. The thought of being betrayed, humiliated, or abandoned provokes in Béatrix a horror she does not conceal; she admits she might find courage to kill a man who, seeing her thus, spoke to her of love.

Béatrix explains that, after receiving his letter and making a frank reply, she could at ease be what he asks in Camille’s house; but the bitter ridicule that would follow if she softened is dreadful. A second theft from Camille would prove her want of power and no woman could forgive such treachery. There are motives delicate women keep to themselves which men cannot understand. She praises Calyste’s mother for purity and nobility and insists she will not mock Calyste by sending him back to little Charlotte; rather she commends him to a divine young girl worthy of his love. Béatrix avows that if she were his, his life would be blighted: she is jealous, trifles would make her wretched, and inexorable thoughts of hers would poison their existence. She cannot promise gentleness; she fears becoming a tyrant of temper and resentment. She believes “true love” as Calyste imagines it is a dream; his passion, she thinks, may be mere joy of desire whose end he does not yet see.

Béatrix praises Camille as inexhaustibly tender, likely to marry Calyste to a younger woman even at her own cost; she mentions practical help—selling an acre in Paris to free the Brittany estate—and says Camille will smooth difficulties and make him her heir by adoption. For Béatrix herself there is only a friendly hand: she wishes to be a sister to him and to end a correspondence between Les Touches and Guérande that she finds absurd. Her letter is a model of prudent sacrifice: generous to Calyste but immovable, refusing to reward his passion at the price of violating Camille and her own honor.

Moved by her son’s strange exhibitions, the baroness could not sit quiet. She heard Béatrix’s reply and, with moist eyes, declared the marquise “a noble, grand woman,” worthy of pardon. “I will pray for her,” she said. Calyste cried, “Have I not every reason to adore her?” His mother, practical and anxious, warned of where such love might lead and urged a safer course: marry Charlotte de Kergarouet and release two-thirds of the estate. By selling a few farms, Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoël could secure a marriage contract and help manage the property so their children would inherit a great name and estate. “Forget Béatrix!” she begged; Calyste muffled the entreaty and left to write an answer.

The baroness, unable to bear inaction, went out to seek counsel and found the Chevalier du Halga on the mall. Certain to meet him, she recited Calyste’s letter and Béatrix’s reply without naming names, asking his opinion. The chevalier, vain and gallant, treated the affair like an adventurous leap for a thoroughbred horse and rambled into old exploits, escorting her to the alley with the courtesy of his sort.

Back home, curiosity and a mother’s fear betrayed the baroness: she found an unsealed letter addressed to the marquise on Calyste’s table and, compelled, read it. Where Béatrix’s reply had been composed and dignified, Calyste’s second letter revealed a deeper plunge into madness: a clear-eyed resolution mingled with instinctive feeling that made it seem as if some worldly chevalier had dictated it. In frantic, arrogant strokes he renounced family names, fortunes, and obligations. He wrote that the Guénic name was nothing to him now; Béatrix’s happiness was his reality, her heart his only fortune. Estates mortgaged for two centuries might remain so; he considered his love his property, object, and religion.

He rejected the thought of marrying any other woman: he would marry no one but Béatrix, and would wait twenty years if need be. He insisted his mother is a saint and has never loved; Béatrix had taught him what his mother had lost and what sacrifices she had made. Camille, he declared, had become a sister the moment he saw Béatrix—“the mother of my intellect, nothing more.” He recounted how he had taken Camille for a woman until Béatrix revealed what womanhood truly was, with all the grace and attractions he desired. From the first moment he felt Béatrix was his, he wrote, and it now seemed unnatural they should be apart.

Calyste proposed elopement rather than patient prudence: let them fly to a country where Béatrix knows no one, where only God and he could reach her heart; his mother might follow, Ireland had castles, his mother’s family would lend one, and in a boat and with a few sailors they could disappear before anyone knew. He told her she had never been loved as he could love her, and that sacred love could wipe out the past; indeed, he said, he could wish her doubly shamed if such disgrace would make him prove his love by making her a saint. He begged for a single day to walk with her by the sea and called any passing alms of tenderness eternal riches.

The baroness let the letter fall before finishing and, kneeling, prayed to God to save her son’s reason, to remove his madness and error. When Calyste entered she responded simply and tearfully that she had been praying and had committed the sin of reading his letter. “My Calyste is mad!” she said. He kissed her and called it “a sweet madness.” He even suggested: “Tomorrow let us take a boat and cross to Croisic. If you are on the jetty you can see her.”

What especially terrified the baroness was that Calyste’s second letter showed both instinctive feeling and a clear-eyed resolution that read as if a worldly adviser might have dictated it; the danger was no longer only youthful folly but a persistent, possibly ruinous, determination. She saw in her son the surrender of prudence to passion—an ardor that would not be soothed by Béatrix’s dignity nor by appeals to honor. The chapter closes with Calyste sealing his letter and departing for Les Touches, resolved and unyielding, while his mother, half-humbled and wholly anxious, looked toward God to deliver him from his craze.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

Perhaps one of the basest delights for small minds is ensnaring a great soul. Beatrix knew herself inferior to Camille Maupin—not only in talent but in the deeper matter of passion.

When Calyste rushed to Les Touches with the ardor of first love, the marquise reveled in being its object. She would not share the feeling; she fancied herself heroic in repressing a caprice and persuaded herself she matched Camille’s devotion, imagining she sacrificed for a friend. Her vanities—her coquetry—were flattered by Calyste’s ardor. Assailed by such seduction, she resisted outwardly while inwardly exulting in her own virtue.

Camille, who had set in motion the situation she now regretted, guarded herself against the clever, friendly rival she had admitted. To preserve secrets and remain aloof she contemplated nature, even turning for consolation to the idea of God; in the glow of those thoughts, Calyste appeared to her like a celestial image, a guardian angel lifting her above temporal suffering. Yet Beatrix carried an air of triumph that disturbed Camille. Between them a silent struggle played out—each pretending to magnanimity while hiding private wounds.

Calyste arrived with a letter tucked between glove and hand, intending to slip it to Beatrix. Camille watched the instant when a woman’s glance betrays what she would conceal; women, she thought, have a genius for nuance and read rivals in a look. She perceived in Beatrix and Calyste a mutual understanding and felt discomposed.

Beatrix no longer feigned indifference; she regarded Calyste as her own. Calyste, too, was transparent—blushing like the guilty and the happy. He said he had come to arrange a trip for the next day.

“Then you really intend to go, my dear?” Camille asked.

“Yes,” Beatrix answered.

Camille proposed details of the excursion and sent Calyste away to make arrangements, then, privately, chided him for some unspecified folly. When he left to hire boatmen, anxieties assailed him; Camille’s grave words had forecast danger. Four hours later he returned, tired and expecting dinner, but found a maid telling him neither her mistress nor the marquise could receive him that evening. Shut out, Calyste went home, bewildered and distressed. At his house his family noticed he was altered: his gaiety extinguished by despair, the fall from elation feeling dreadful.

While Calyste brooded at home, a scene between Camille and Beatrix unfolded that would shape the next day. Calyste’s letter had stirred emotions in Beatrix she had seldom experienced: the ardor and innocence of his affection made her suddenly desire control. Yet rereading his words bred a cruel suspicion. What had Calyste and Camille been doing together while Claude Vignon was away? If Calyste did not love Camille, and Camille knew it, how had they spent their mornings? Memory cast certain of Camille’s speeches in a new light; Beatrix pictured Camille’s intimacy with the young man and felt belittled, imagining herself a mere toy in a woman’s indulgence of a child she loved extraordinarily.

The thought was a thunderclap. Beatrix reviewed the past week, scrutinizing the roles each had played; fury and jealous anger convinced her that Camille’s conduct might be vengeance for long-nursed resentments. Uncertain yet resolved to act, she went downstairs for dinner without dressing, her taciturn manner chilling Camille, who realized a crisis had come. Camille withdrew to fight alone; she feared that Beatrix would collapse under assault and wanted to be the sole adversary.

Dinner was gloomy. Camille remained gentle but felt superior; Beatrix was hard and cutting, as though managed like a child. The battle began in glances and half-spoken sentences. When they rose, Camille offered her arm maliciously; Beatrix pretended not to see and stormed upstairs alone. After coffee came a flinty exchange—Camille’s dry wit meeting Beatrix’s venom—until Mademoiselle des Touches dismissed the footman, their signal for a private quarrel.

“You make novels more dangerous than those you write,” Beatrix said.

“They are unpublished, my angel,” Camille replied with a cigarette.

Beatrix launched into a tirade: women know each other’s pride and virtue, she said, but Camille had something of the man about her—unrestrained, contemptuous of women, cruel. She accused Camille of trickery toward Calyste and of descending to depths unbecoming of her. Camille smiled, amused by the sphinx-like barbs, and with ironic composure suggested Beatrix was capable neither of foolish love nor of profound passion—too cool-headed, too calculating.

Beatrix, stung, struck back about age and advantage; Camille, composed, retorted that she loved Calyste—“far too much for my peace of mind”—and that if he had a passing fancy for Beatrix, so be it: Beatrix was fair and young, while Camille was dark and dignified. Camille claimed she had done her best to prevent their situation but would not be humiliated by a rival unless she chose to allow it. She urged Beatrix to be frank with Calyste if he courted her, promising to aid her or to offer a refuge near Paris if the affair continued. “The purest love lies twenty times a day,” she said with a sting—suggesting love survives, even thrives, on its deceptions.

Beatrix, unsettled by Camille’s disdain and her apparent willingness to accept the situation, produced Calyste’s last letter as a weapon. Camille read it and wept—true, violent tears. The letter revealed how deeply Calyste loved Beatrix; Camille’s grief was the genuine suffering of a woman mortally wounded by being loved but not fully returned.

“Do you love him?” Camille demanded, searching Beatrix’s face. “Have you that infinite worship which triumphs over pain and survives betrayal?”

Beatrix, with naïveté and honesty, confessed she did love him but in a way that made her ashamed of herself. She felt purified by his love and refused to corrupt his destiny. “If I cannot be his wife, I shall not be his mistress,” she declared. She would not be “an eternal regret” or a millstone round his neck. To love, for her, had become a moral test; she would not spoil his life.

Camille, horrified, cried that this was not love. “To love and calculate!” she exclaimed. The closeness between them ruptured. Camille accused Beatrix of having words that would separate them forever: “We are no longer friends. Here begins a terrible conflict between us. You will either succumb or fly.” She left the room like a maddened lioness, then paused at the portière to ask coldly whether Beatrix would go to Croisic the next day. Beatrix, proud, replied she would not flee or yield. Camille answered she would write to Conti, a threat that made Beatrix pale.

That night the violence subsided, both women arguing with themselves and returning to the art of temporizing—an approach often effective between men and women but dangerous among rivals. In the background, Mademoiselle des Touches heard the great Voice whose counsels can subdue the strongest will; Beatrix heard only worldly prudence and feared social contempt.

Thus Félicité’s last deception had, for the moment, succeeded: Calyste’s mistake was repaired—he had escaped from immediate involvement—but a fresh indiscretion might yet prove fatal. The two women, bound in rivalry and wounded by different truths, had staked fortunes of feeling and honor on a hazardous game. Each returned to her defenses: Camille with the bitter certitude of a love that might cost her everything, Beatrix with a new-found rigid moralism that would not let her be the cause of Calyste’s ruin. The next day’s excursion and the letter left in tension the fates of all three, the outcome poised on pride, sacrifice, and the dangerous art of feminine strategy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
It was late August; the sky was clear, the sea near the horizon gleamed like molten silver, and the shore rippled with little waves. Sunlight made the sands almost tropical; salt shone white on the marsh. Paludiers in white with long rakes had been at their posts since morning; some leaned on mud-walls watching the harvest, others stood with family. Custom-house officers smoked calmly. The scene was exotic enough that a Parisian might not guess it was France.

The baron and baroness, who had pretended interest in the salt harvest, stood on the jetty admiring the quiet landscape where only the sea sounded and the cultivated green belt and boats lent charm. A party came along a narrow path from Les Touches: the marquise walked first alone; Calyste and Camille followed arm-in-arm, Gasselin bringing up the rear. The marquise saw Madame du Guénic and felt a repulsion for Béatrix, though Béatrix was attractively dressed in a Leghorn hat with bluebells, crimped hair, a gray gown, and a blue sash that made her seem a princess disguised as a milkmaid. Calyste introduced Camille to his parents; the paludier noted that Mademoiselle was the largest landowner and a good lady. They exchanged bows and the party boarded a waiting boat in silence.

The marquise remained cold and dignified, having lectured Calyste for his disobedience; Calyste, in despair, cast tormented glances at Béatrix, where anger and love struggled. The passage from Guérande to Croisic was wordless. At Croisic, salt was discharged and peasant-women carried it in earthen jars on their heads; barefoot carriers watched a little Danish vessel unload. To avoid remarks and stay near Calyste, Camille sprang toward the rocks, leaving him to follow with Béatrix. Gasselin kept a distance.

The Croisic peninsula is flanked by granite carved by the sea into arabesques and vaults. Under a vaulted roof is a hollow like a marble bathtub, floored with white sand and tepid water safe for bathing. Cool covered hollows, roughly carved but majestic, recall palaces copying nature. One rare tree stood on a granite promontory—a great box-bush whose roots suggested three hundred years’ life—above a cliff whose base formed a semicircle of rocks. It took nerve to climb this little Gibraltar; from its summit one sees clock towers, arid fields of Croisic, and dunes stretching to Batz.

Calyste led Béatrix to this vantage point where the view was magnificent and the granite imposing. Camille, like a wounded wild animal craving solitude, had dressed in male attire—trousers edged with frills, a short blouse, a peaked cap—and courted danger among fissures and caves. She reached the tree’s shade and sat lost in thought.

Alone, Camille considered old age and what a woman like her should do after having drunk too eagerly of fame. She ate strawberry lozenges and mused that strawberries no longer existed but lived in their qualities; perhaps people survive in their spiritual qualities too. Facing the ocean’s vast suggestion, she felt that only conviction of future holiness could give immortality meaning. A noble inward change came: the worldly woman in her died, and a more spiritual being rose. She resolved to withdraw from society, to transform the loves she once pursued into a divine impulse and renounce social ambition forever.

After walking in silence, Calyste compared the sea’s depth and purity to his love. Béatrix remarked on the grandeur, saying it differed from the Mediterranean, and laughed that “it is met by a rock.” He answered with a look that he could summon the patience of the angels when he saw her; yet alone he suffered so much his mother wept for him. The marquise used that barren plain dried by salt winds as an image of her inner desolation. She told Calyste she loved him but would never be his; she would remain bound, a sandy plain without fruit. If abandoned, she would ask pardon of the man she had offended; she would never risk taking a happiness she believed would quickly end.

Calyste replied passionately, offering to sacrifice everything—family, name, future—but she recoiled from the word “sacrifice,” seeing it as an insult. He cried that love cannot argue, it simply is; he would do the impossible. Moved but firm, she said she had done wrong and asked him not to tempt her further. They walked on in tense silence, his inward fury visible only to those who love without hope.

At the base of the rock by the box-bush, Calyste helped Béatrix ascend the steep slope; for him it was a precious privilege. He clasped her round the waist. When he asked whether she would never be his, she replied she could only be Béatrix—a dream—and that that sweet, tender dream could be without bitterness or repentance. When he, in frenzy, cried “You shall never belong to any man!” and pushed her, she slipped eight or ten feet into the cavity where the box-bush grew; her gown caught on a rock point and tore slowly, lowering her onto the bush.

Mademoiselle des Touches, horrified, signaled to Gasselin. Calyste leaned forward, savage; he saw Béatrix’s precarious position and shuddered. Her lips moved as if praying; the bush began to give. With youthful agility, Calyste slid down to the ledge below, grasped the marquise and held her though both risked sliding into the sea. She had fainted; in that aerial spot he fancied her wholly his, and his first emotion was pleasure. “Open your eyes,” he begged; she murmured “Die?” and when she opened them he kissed her, feeling her tremble convulsively with passionate joy.

Gasselin’s hob-nailed shoes sounded above them; Camille followed. They sought a way to save the lovers. Gasselin declared he must slide down so they could climb on his shoulders and be pulled up; Camille was to fetch a ladder from Croisic. Béatrix asked to be laid down; Calyste placed her on a narrow ledge where, faint and then weakly revived, she seemed ready to faint again. Camille insisted the incident must be seen as an accident whether Béatrix lived or died. Calyste feared she would hate him; Camille reassured him she would adore him. Gasselin fetched a ladder, and with a contrivance involving Camille’s red shawl, Béatrix reached the top; the Breton took her in his arms and carried her to the shore like an infant.

She murmured to Félicité that she had not wished for death, but for suffering. Camille had her taken to a farmhouse from which they had borrowed the ladder and a doctor was summoned from Croisic. Calyste, Gasselin, and Camille made a litter from spare clothes on the ladder and carried Béatrix; farmers gave her a bed while Gasselin rode for a doctor and sent a boatman for aid.

Calyste sat on a stool answering in monosyllables; Camille grew uneasy at his unnatural state. The physician bled Béatrix; she improved and consented to embark, and by five o’clock they reached the jetty at Guérande whence she was carried to Les Touches. News of the accident had spread.

Calyste spent the night at Les Touches beside Béatrix’s bed with Camille. The doctor had assured them only slight stiffness would remain. Through despair there came a gleam of joy: he could watch her sleep or wake, study her pallid face and expressions. Camille recognized in him a passion one feels but once—a consuming fire that blinds a man to everything else. She saw he would never know the real woman in Béatrix; he adored instead an image fashioned by love.

Calyste was guileless; when he saw Béatrix’s green eyes turned to him with love, confusion, and mischief, he blushed and looked away. Béatrix jested sweetly about men promising happiness and ending by flinging women down precipices. He knelt, took her moist hand, and kissed it humbly, telling her she had the right to reject his love. Félicité soothed him, asserting that unless a woman were a monster she must be flattered by such devotion.

The incident marked Calyste. That night he murmured Béatrix’s name as he slept. The hôtel du Guénic was calm; Mademoiselle des Touches had written to the baroness about the accident. Calyste returned to breakfast at Les Touches and found Béatrix up and dressed though pale and feeble. The harshness and coldness that had guarded her were gone. Camille played music, leaving Calyste free to take Béatrix’s hands; no storms erupted; Félicité effaced herself.

Cold, fragile, hard women like Madame de Rochefide—women with pale souls—need a thunderbolt to melt; to Béatrix, Calyste’s fury of love and his mad action were that thunderbolt. She felt humbled inwardly; a pure love bathed her heart. She breathed a genial air of feelings hitherto unknown and felt raised to the ideal of Woman that Bretons honor. The respectful adoration of youth cost her little; a gesture, a look, a word satisfied him. The value he placed on trifles touched and magnified her; to hold her gloved hand meant more to him than possession to another man.

Few women resist such constant deification. Béatrix felt sure of being obeyed and could command Calyste to risk his life for a caprice. This gave her a noble and imposing air; she sought to remain the loftiest of women in his eyes. Her coquetries grew persistent because of inward weakness; she played the invalid for a week with charming hypocrisy, leaning on Calyste’s arm in languid dependence on walks between house and garden.

Before the Croisic excursion the two women had laughed at men’s declarations of love and agreed that clever men often go straight to the point. Madame de Rochefide could restrain Calyste by reminding him of his violence on the rocks; a look from her filled his eyes with tears and stilled his prayers and arguments. By infernal coquetry she brought him to seek Camille’s advice. Béatrix, armed with Calyste’s own letter and his axiom that to love is the first happiness and to be loved comes later, restrained his passion within respectful idolatry. She liked the hymns of praise and adoration youth offers; she gave no definite answer. He asked only permission to love, which suited his search after love’s spiritual side.

Driven by despair, Calyste complained to Camille. She told him she had intended to cure him by making him quickly understand Béatrix, but he had spoiled it; once he was her master, now her slave. She advised he provoke a quarrel—let Béatrix ill-treat him, then stay away until she recalled him. In extreme illness one accepts cruel remedies, and Calyste followed her counsel, staying away two days. On the third he scratched at Béatrix’s door to say he and Camille awaited her for breakfast. Camille lamented he had lost another chance.

During his absence Béatrix often looked through the window toward the road to Guérande. Camille found her watching the gorse’s golden blooms along the roadway and spoke of them, trying to distract her. The marquise kept them waiting long for breakfast; when she appeared her dress showed intention to fascinate and prevent another absence. After breakfast she walked with Calyste in the garden and expressed a wish to return to the rock where she had nearly perished.

“Will you go with me alone?” Calyste asked.

“If I refused, I should give you reason to think I found you dangerous. Alas! as I have told you, I belong to another, and I must be his only. I chose him knowing nothing of love. The fault was great, and bitter is my punishment,” she said with moist eyes. Calyste was filled with compassion that tempered his ardor; he adored her as if she were a Madonna. Béatrix was torn between herself and Calyste—between reentering the world and the young happiness he offered. She began to listen to his blind passion and allowed his pity to soothe her. He spoke of understanding her past, of making her feel his comprehension day by day; she admitted silently she was indeed an angel misunderstood. “I am too humiliated,” she said. “My past has robbed the future of all security.”

It was a glorious morning for Calyste when he saw Béatrix at a window watching for him, wearing the same straw hat from their memorable first excursion. Small things of passion magnified the world for him. They left Les Touches through the garden gate onto the dunes. Béatrix loved the sands and gathered hardy plants with rose-colored flowers to mix with Chartreux pansies—a little relic for them. “We’ll add a bit of box,” she said, smiling.

They sat on the jetty awaiting the boat; Calyste told her of a youthful escapade on the day of her arrival. “I knew of your little escapade,” she said, and that had been the cause of her sternness the first night. During their walk her tone was lightly jesting with tenderness; Calyste had reason to think himself beloved. At a creek strewn with brilliant pebbles, when Calyste, at the summit of happiness, asked her plainly to fly with him to Ireland, she resumed her distant air, asked for his arm, and continued in silence toward her Tarpeian rock.

“My friend,” she said as she mounted the granite pedestal, “I have not the courage to conceal what you are to me. For ten years I have had no happiness comparable to searching for shells with you and exchanging pebbles for the necklace I will treasure more than diamonds. I have been a little girl again... The love I have inspired in you has raised me in my own eyes. Understand these words.”

They reached the rock’s summit, seeing the vast ocean on one side and Brittany on the other. “But,” she continued, “I do not belong to myself; I am more bound by my own will than by law. You must be punished for my misdeed, but be satisfied to know we suffer together. Dante never saw his Beatrice again; Petrarch never had his Laura. Such disasters fall only on noble souls. But if I should be abandoned, if I fall lower into shame and ignominy and your Béatrix is cruelly misjudged, then, my adored child,” she said taking his hand, “to you she will still be first of all; you will see her rise to heaven as she leans on you; but then, if you wish to cast her down do not fail of your blow; after your love, death!”

Calyste clasped her round the waist and pressed her to his heart. As if to confirm her words, Béatrix laid a tender, timid kiss on his brow. Walking back like those who perfectly understand each other—she thinking she had gained a truce, he not doubting his happiness—both were deceived. At a turn, Béatrix was seized with a horrible trembling—the contagion of seeing a serpent—which Calyste felt before he saw the cause: on a bench beneath a weeping ash sat Conti, talking with Camille Maupin.

The marquise’s convulsive trembling made the tragic situation plain to everyone.

“You did not expect me so soon, I fancy,” Conti said, offering his arm to Beatrix.

Unable to refuse, the marquise let Calyste’s arm fall and took Conti’s. Humiliated, Calyste flung himself on a bench beside Camille after exchanging the coldest salutations with his rival. He thought at first to attack Conti, but Beatrix’s violent trembling showed she suffered and paid for her faults; the sight struck him dumb, as if immobilized by fate.

Madame de Rochefide and Conti passed before Calyste’s seat; Beatrix shot Camille a look that said everything and avoided Calyste’s eyes, turning instead to Conti, who appeared to jest.

“What will they say to each other?” Calyste asked Camille.

“Dear child,” Camille replied, “you don’t yet know the terrible rights an extinguished love gives a man over a woman. Beatrix could not refuse Conti’s arm. He’s surely teasing her about her new love; he may have guessed it from your manner and his observation.”

“Teasing!” cried Calyste.

“Be calm,” Camille counseled. “If he wounds her pride, she will crush him. But Conti is cunning; he’ll act more cleverly. He’ll pretend not to suspect proud Madame de Rochefide of betrayal; he’ll paint you as an ambitious child wanting a marquise in love with him, setting himself as arbiter. He’ll launch insinuations; Beatrix will be forced to deny, and he’ll become her master again.”

“He does not love her. I would leave her free. True love must be chosen anew each moment. Why did he not stay away longer? What brought him back?”

“The jest of a journalist,” Camille said. “His opera failed. Some critic—likely Claude Vignon—said: ‘It is hard to lose fame and mistress at the same moment,’ and it stung him. He seemed weary, disgusted; he regrets his public alliance with the marquise, and spoke to me of happier past days in a melancholy way. Perhaps he hoped to learn your secret amid my flatteries.”

Calyste watched Beatrix and Conti approach but hardly listened further. Camille, careful when talking to Conti, revealed neither Calyste’s secret nor Beatrix’s. She warned Calyste that Conti was capable of treachery.

“My dear friend,” she said, “this is the most critical moment for you. You need caution and cleverness you do not possess; I fear you will be tricked by the wiliest man I have known, and I can do nothing to help you.”

The dinner bell rang. Conti offered his arm to Camille; Calyste gave his to Beatrix. Camille stepped back to let the marquise pass, and Beatrix found a moment to put her finger to her lips at Calyste, urging discretion.

At dinner Conti was extremely gay—perhaps to probe Madame de Rochefide, who played her part poorly. Had her conduct been mere coquetry she might have deceived Conti, but her new love was real and betrayed her embarrassment. The wily musician pretended not to notice. At dessert he steered talk to women and praised their constancy—women who abandon men in prosperity would sacrifice all in misfortune; they cling to their first lover as honor requires. His praises, meant to wound, fell like incense on the altar of one bleeding heart. Camille and Beatrix alone saw the bitter sting beneath the eulogy; they flushed but controlled themselves. After dinner they took each other’s arms and, by mutual consent, turned aside into the great salon to be alone in darkness.

“It is dreadful to let Conti ride over me; yet I can’t defend myself,” Beatrix said in a low voice. “The galley-slave is always a slave to his mate. I am lost; I must go back to my galleys! And it is you, Camille, who have cast me there! Ah! you brought him back a day too soon, or a day too late. I recognize your infernal talent as author. Well, your revenge is complete, the finale perfect!”

“I may have said I would write to Conti, but to do it was another matter,” cried Camille. “I am incapable of such baseness. But you are unhappy, and I forgive the suspicion.”

“What will become of Calyste?” asked the marquise, with naive self-conceit.

“Then Conti carries you off, does he?” Camille asked.

“Ah! you think you triumph!” cried Beatrix, anger distorting her face. Camille tried to hide a satisfaction beneath false sympathy; Beatrix, versed in such deceptions, saw through it. Sitting under a strong light on the divan where the first three weeks had hosted comedies and where many secret tragedies began, they examined each other for the last time and felt forever parted by undying hatred.

“Calyste remains to you,” said Beatrix; “but I am fixed in his heart, and no woman can ever drive me out of it.”

Camille replied ironically with the famous line: “You reign, you love, and you depart!”

Neither noticed Conti and Calyste’s absence. Conti had remained at table with his rival, begging him to keep him company finishing a bottle of champagne.

“We have something to say,” Conti added, to prevent refusal. Placed as he was, the young Breton could not refuse.

“My dear friend,” the composer said in his most caressing voice as Calyste listened after a couple of glasses, “we are both good fellows and can speak frankly. I have not come suspiciously. Beatrix loves me,”—with a gesture of self-conceit—“but the truth is, I have ceased to love her. I am not here to carry her off, but to break off our relations and leave her the honors of the rupture. You are young; you don’t yet know how useful it is to appear the victim when you are the executioner. Young men spit fire and leave a woman in fury, but wise men do as I do; they get themselves dismissed, feigning mortification, which leaves regret in the woman’s heart and a sense of her superiority. You don’t know how hampered men are by rash promises women accept when gallantry makes us swear eternal faithfulness; let time cure what we promise. I have seen that you love Beatrix. I leave her in a position where she loses nothing of her majesty; she will certainly coquet with you, if only to tease that angel Camille Maupin. Well, take her, love her—you’ll do me a service; I want her to turn against me. I fear her pride and virtue. Perhaps it will take time to effect this chase. On such occasions the wisest plan is to take no step at all. I did just now, walking on the lawn, attempt to let her see I knew all and was ready to congratulate her on her new happiness. Well, she was furious! At this moment I am desperately in love with a prima donna, Mademoiselle Falcon of the Grand Opera. I think of marrying her; yes, I have got as far as that. When you come to Paris you will see I have changed a marquise for a queen.”

Calyste’s candid face revealed the satisfaction and love Conti wanted to discover. No man, however blasé, does not flare when his love is threatened by a rival. When lovers reach this extremity, both strive to act first. Questioned by the composer, Calyste related all that had happened during the last three weeks at Les Touches; Conti, concealing fury under charm, received it all.

“Come, let us go upstairs,” Conti said. “Women are so distrustful; those two will wonder how we can sit here together without tearing each other’s hair out; they might come down to listen. I’ll serve you faithfully, my dear boy. You’ll see me rough and jealous with the marquise; I shall seem to suspect her; there’s no better way to drive a woman to betray you. You will be happy, and I shall be free. Seem to pity that angel for belonging to a man without delicacy; show her a tear—for you can weep, you are young. I, alas! can weep no more; and that’s a great disadvantage lost.”

They went to Camille’s salon. At Calyste’s request Conti sang the famous “Pria che spunti l’aurora,” which had often been his triumph. Never was his singing more extraordinary than that night, when so many feelings contended in his breast. Calyste was in ecstasy. As Conti sang the first words of the cavatina, he looked intently at Beatrix, giving the words a cruel signification fully understood. Camille, who accompanied him, guessed the order thus conveyed, which bowed Beatrix’s head. She looked at Calyste and felt sure he had fallen into some trap despite her advice. This conviction became certainty when the evidently happy Breton came up to bid Beatrix good-night, kissing her hand with a little air of confident triumph.

By dawn, as the song foretold, Conti was carrying Beatrix away with Camille’s horses to the first relay. The morning twilight allowed Madame de Rochefide to see Guerande, its towers whitened by the dawn against the dark sky. Melancholy thoughts possessed her; she was leaving one of the sweetest flowers of her life—a pure love such as a young girl dreams of; the only true love she had known or likely to know. The woman of the world obeyed the laws of the world; she sacrificed love to their demands just as many women sacrifice it to duty. Sometimes pride can appear as high as virtue. Read thus, this history is that of many women.

The next day Calyste went to Les Touches about midday. From the window where, the day before, he had seen Beatrix watching, he saw Camille, who ran down and told the cruel truth in one word: “Gone!”

“Beatrix?” asked Calyste, thunderstruck.

“You have been duped by Conti; you told me nothing, and I could do nothing for you.”

She led him to her little salon, where he flung himself on the divan and burst into tears. Felicite smoked her hookah and said nothing, knowing no words could arrest such anguish. Calyste, unable even to think, sat all day in torpor. Just before dinner, Camille tried to speak after begging him to listen.

“Friend,” she said, “you caused me the bitterest suffering, and I had not, like you, a beautiful young life in which to heal myself. For me, life has no spring, nor my soul a love. To console myself I sought above. Here, in this room, the day before Beatrix came, I drew her portrait; I did not do her injustice, or you might have thought me jealous. I wanted you to know her as she is, for that would have kept you safe. Listen now to the full truth. Madame de Rochefide is unworthy of you. The scandal of her fall was unnecessary; she did it deliberately to play a part in society. She is one of those women who prefer the celebrity of scandal to tranquil happiness; they fly in the face of society to obtain the fatal alms of rebuke; they desire to be talked about at any cost. Beatrix was eaten up with vanity. Her fortune and wit had not given her the feminine royalty she craved; they had not enabled her to reign supreme over a salon. She then bethought herself of seeking the celebrity of the Duchesse de Langeais and the Vicomtesse de Beauseant. But the world gives its interest to real feelings only. Beatrix playing comedy was judged second-rate. There was no reason for her flight; the sword of Damocles was not suspended over her. She is neither sincere, loving, nor tender; if she were, would she have gone away with Conti this morning?”

Camille spoke long and eloquently to open Calyste’s eyes, but her effort was useless; he displayed absolute belief in Beatrix. She forced him to come down into the dining-room and sit while she dined; he could not swallow. Such bodily contractions and nervous reactions belong to extreme youth; later organs harden. A man often resists violent grief because his organs are stronger.

Mademoiselle des Touches was alarmed at Calyste’s calm resignation after his burst of tears. Before he left, he asked permission to go into Beatrix’s bedroom, where he had seen her on the night of her illness; there he laid his head on the pillow where hers had lain.

“I am committing follies,” he said, grasping Camille’s hand, and bidding her good-night in deep dejection.

He returned home to the usual company at mouche and spent the evening beside his mother. The rector, the Chevalier du Halga, and Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel all knew of Madame de Rochefide’s departure and rejoiced. Calyste would now return to them; they watched him cautiously. No one imagined the result of a first love in a heart so simple and true.

For several days Calyste went regularly to Les Touches. He paced the lawn where he had walked with Beatrix. He often went to Croisic to stand on that fateful rock or lie for hours in the box-bush; by finding footholds he had means of climbing. These solitary trips, his silence, his gravity, made his mother anxious. After about two weeks of this caged-animal conduct, the poor lover ceased to cross the bay; he had scarcely strength to drag himself along the road from Guerande to the spot where he had seen Beatrix watching. The family, delighted at the departure of “those Parisians,” saw nothing fatal or diseased in the lad. The two old maids and the rector, pursuing their scheme, kept Charlotte de Kergarouet, who nightly played coquetries on Calyste, obtaining in return nothing better than advice in playing mouche. During long evenings Calyste sat between his mother and the little Breton girl, observed by the rector and Charlotte’s aunt, who discussed his mood as they walked home. Their simple minds mistook lethargic indifference for submission to their plans.

One evening when Calyste, wearied, went off to bed, the players dropped their cards and looked at each other; they had heard his steps with anxiety.

“Something is the matter with Calyste,” said the baroness, wiping her eyes.

“Nothing is the matter,” replied Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel; “but you should marry him at once.”

“Do you think marriage would divert his mind?” asked the Chevalier.

Charlotte, urged by the baron, arrived with unusual care. After a discourse on matrimony to which he could not answer, Calyste requested a few days’ grace. The old baron rubbed his hands with joy and whispered the “good news” to the baroness.

Breakfast was gay; Charlotte sparkled. After the meal Calyste took Charlotte to the grotto. Their parents watched from the window.

“They are watching us,” Charlotte said.

“They cannot hear us,” he replied.

“True; but they see us.”

“Let us sit down, Charlotte,” he said, taking her hand.

“Is it true your banner used to float from that twisted column?” she asked, already thinking of owning the house. “You will make changes, won’t you, Calyste?”

“I shall not have time,” he said, taking her hands and kissing them. “I love someone you have seen and who loves me, and I cannot make any other woman happy; though from childhood you and I have been destined. But I shall wait.”

“And I, too,” Charlotte said, tears in her eyes. “You cannot long love a married woman who, they say, has gone off with a singer—”

“Marry, Charlotte,” Calyste interrupted. “With the fortune your aunt intends to give you, you could choose some better man than I. I have brought you here not to tell you what you know, but to entreat you, as a childhood friend, to take this rupture upon yourself and say you have rejected me. Say you do not wish to marry a man whose heart is not free; thus I shall be spared the sense that I have done you public wrong. You do not know how heavy life is now; I cannot bear the slightest struggle; I am like a man whose vital spark is gone. If it were not for the grief I should cause my mother, I would have flung myself into the sea. Do not speak of this to anyone. Good-bye, Charlotte.”

He kissed her hair and left by the postern-gate to Les Touches, remaining near Camille till past midnight. Returning home at one, he found his mother waiting with her worsted-work. Softly he clasped her hand and asked, “Is Charlotte gone?”

“She goes tomorrow, with her aunt, in despair,” answered the baroness. “Come to Ireland with me, my Calyste.”

“Many a time I have thought of flying there—”

“Ah!” cried the baroness.

“With Beatrix,” he added.

Some days after Charlotte’s departure, Calyste joined the Chevalier du Halga for his daily promenade. They sat on a bench with a view from Les Touches to the rocks of Croisic, against which waves dashed. Calyste was thin and pale; his strength diminished; he felt shudders denoting fever. His eyes, surrounded by dark circles, had the fixed-brightness of obsession. The chevalier was the only companion with whom he could exchange a few ideas; in him Calyste divined an apostle of his own religion and recognized vestiges of an eternal love.

“Have you loved many women?” he asked.

“Only one,” the chevalier replied.

“Was she free?”

“No,” exclaimed Du Halga. “Ah! how I suffered! She was the wife of my best friend—my protector—yet we loved each other so!”

“Did she love you?” Calyste asked.

“Passionately,” the chevalier replied fervently. “Until her death; she died at forty-nine during the emigration at St. Petersburg. I have thought of fetching her to Brittany and laying her near me! But she lies in my heart.”

The chevalier brushed away his tears. Calyste took his hand and pressed it.

“I care for this little dog more than for life,” the old man said, pointing to Thisbe. “The little darling is precisely like the one she held on her knees. I never look at Thisbe but I see Madame l’Amirale’s hands.”

“Did you see Madame de Rochefide?” Calyste asked.

“No,” replied the chevalier. “It is sixty-eight years since I have looked at any woman with attention—except your mother, who has some of Madame l’Amirale’s complexion.”

Three days later the chevalier said, “My child, I have a hundred and forty louis laid by. When you know where Madame de Rochefide is, come and get them and follow her.”

Calyste thanked him, envying his existence. But day by day he grew morose; he seemed to love no one; all things hurt him; he was gentle only to his mother. The baroness watched his decline with growing anxiety; by prayer and entreaty she made him swallow food. Toward the end of October the lad ceased to go to the mall; the chevalier came in vain to tempt him out.

“We can talk of Madame de Rochefide,” the old man would say. “I’ll tell you my first adventure.”

“Your son is ill,” he confided to the baroness when convinced efforts were useless.

Calyste answered that he was well; but like all youths in melancholy, he took pleasure in thoughts of death. He no longer left the house but sat in the garden on a bench, warming himself in a pale sunshine, alone with his thought.

Soon after Calyste ceased even to go to Les Touches, Felicite asked the rector to come. The assiduity with which Abbé Grimont called at Les Touches became the great topic of the town. The masters and servants were afflicted by Calyste’s increasing weakness though none imagined youth might die of love. Even the chevalier had no example of such a death among his memories. They attributed Calyste’s thinness to want of food. His mother implored him to eat. Calyste tried to conquer repugnance to comfort her; nourishment taken against his will only increased the slow fever consuming him.

During the last days of October the cherished child could no longer mount the stairs; his bed was placed in the lower hall, surrounded by family. They finally sent for the Guerande physician, who broke the fever with quinine and reduced it in a few days, ordering Calyste to take exercise and find amusement. The baron, roused from apathy, recovered some strength; with Calyste, Gasselin, and two dogs he started for the forest, and for days they hunted. Calyste obeyed, visiting neighbors’ chateaus. But he had no spirit; nothing brought a smile. His livid, contracted features betrayed utter passivity. The baron, worn by exertion, returned home exhausted with Calyste almost as ill. For several days both were dangerously ill; the family sent, at the Guerande physician’s request, for two of the best doctors in Nantes.

The baron had received a fatal shock on realizing Calyste’s change. With the lucidity of the dying he trembled that his line might perish. He prayed, and his face turned constantly toward his son’s bed. At the least motion Calyste made, a commotion stirred within him. The baroness no longer left the room where Zephirine sat knitting in horrible unease. Demands were made for wood and provisions; Zephirine, not nimble enough, gave her keys to Mariotte but insisted on knowing everything; she questioned Mariotte and her sister-in-law. One night, when father and son dozed, Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel told Zephirine she must resign herself to the baron’s death. The old woman dropped her knitting, groped, and drew from her pocket an old black chaplet, on which she prayed with a fervor that gave her withered face a new splendor; the other old woman imitated her, and all present, on a sign from the rector, joined in the spiritual uplift.

“Alas! I prayed to God,” said the baroness, remembering her prayer after reading Calyste’s fatal letter, “and he did not hear me.”

“Perhaps it would be well,” said the rector, “to beg Mademoiselle des Touches to come see Calyste.”

“She!” cried Zephirine, “the author of all our misery! She turned him from his family, taught him impious books, an heretical language! Let her be accursed! She has destroyed the du Guenics!”

“She may perhaps restore them,” the rector said gently. “Mademoiselle des Touches is a saintly woman; I am her surety. She has only good intentions toward Calyste.”

“Let me know the day she sets foot in this house, that I may get out of it,” cried the old woman passionately. “She has killed both father and son. Do you not hear death in Calyste’s voice? He is so feeble he barely whispers.”

At that moment the three doctors arrived. They examined Calyste and the baron; they were surprised the baron yet lived. The Guerande doctor calmly told the baroness that for Calyste it would probably be best to take him to Paris to consult the most experienced physicians; it would cost over a hundred louis.

“People die of something, but not of love,” said Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel.

“Alas! whatever be the cause, Calyste is dying,” said the baroness. “I see all the symptoms of consumption, that most horrible disease of my country, about him.”

“Calyste dying!” said the baron, opening his eyes; two large tears rolled slowly down his wrinkled cheeks—the only tears he had probably ever shed. Suddenly he rose, walked to his son’s bedside, took his hand, and looked earnestly at him.

“What is it you want, father?” said Calyste.

“That you should live!” cried the baron.

“I cannot live without Beatrix,” replied Calyste.

The old man dropped into a chair.

“Oh! where could we get a hundred louis to bring doctors from Paris? There is still time,” cried the baroness.

“A hundred louis!” cried Zephirine; “will that save him?”

Without waiting, the old maid ran her hands into her placket-holes, unfastened her petticoat, and let it fall, producing a heavy sound as coins tumbled. She knew where she had sewn her savings and ripped them out with the rapidity of magic. The gold pieces rang as they fell, one by one, into her lap. Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel gazed in amazement.

“But they’ll see you!” she whispered.

“Thirty-seven,” answered Zephirine, counting.

“Every one will know how much you have.”

“Forty-two.”

“Double louis! all new! How did you get them, you who can’t see clearly?”

“I felt them. Here’s one hundred and four louis,” cried Zephirine. “Is that enough?”

“What is all this?” asked the Chevalier du Halga, coming in, puzzled by the old blind friend holding a petticoat full of gold.

Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel explained.

“I knew it,” said the chevalier. “And I have come to bring a hundred and forty louis I have been holding at Calyste’s disposal, as he knows.”

The chevalier produced rouleaux from his pocket. Mariotte, seeing such wealth, sent Gasselin to lock the doors.

“Gold will not give him health,” said the baroness, weeping.

“But it can take him to Paris, where he can find her. Come, Calyste.”

“Yes,” cried Calyste, springing up, “I will go.”

“He will live,” said the baron, in a shaking voice; “and I can die—send for the rector!”

The words cast terror on all present. Calyste, seeing the mortal paleness on his father’s face, came to his side. The rector, after hearing the doctors, had gone to Mademoiselle des Touches intending to bring her back; in proportion as he had formerly detested her, he now admired and protected her as a shepherd protects the most precious of his flock.

When news of the baron’s approaching end spread in Guerande, a crowd gathered; peasants, paludiers, and servants knelt in the courtyard while the rector administered last sacraments to the old Breton warrior. The whole town was agitated by the probable extinction of this old Breton race; it was felt as a public calamity.

The solemn ceremony affected Calyste deeply. His filial sorrow silenced the anguish of his love for a moment. During the baron’s last hour he knelt beside him, watching death come. The old man died in his chair in presence of the assembled family.

“I die faithful to God and his religion,” he said. “My God! as the reward of my efforts grant that Calyste may live!”

“I shall live, father, and I will obey you,” said the young man.

“If you wish to make my death as happy as Fanny has made my life, swear to me to marry.”

“I promise it, father.”

It was touching to see Calyste, or rather his shadow, leaning on the chevalier’s arm—a spectre leading a shade—follow the baron’s coffin as chief mourner. The church and little square were crowded with country people from thirty miles round.

But the baroness and Zephirine soon saw that despite his promise Calyste fell back into fatal stupor. On the day the family donned mourning the baroness took her son to a garden bench and questioned him closely. Calyste answered gently and submissively, but his replies proved only the despair of his soul.

“Mother,” he said, “there is no life in me. What I eat does not feed me; the air does not refresh me; the sun feels cold; it seems to you to light that front of the house, but to me it is a blur. If Beatrix were here it would be dazzling. There is but one thing left that keeps its shape to my eyes—this flower,” and he drew from his breast the withered bunch the marquise had given him at Croisic.

The baroness dared not say more. His answer seemed more indicative of madness than his silence. She saw no hope or light in the darkness.

The baron’s last hours had prevented the rector from bringing Mademoiselle des Touches to Calyste. But that day, while mother and son sat on the bench, Calyste quivered at seeing Felicite through the opposite windows. She reminded him of Beatrix, and his life revived. It was to Camille the poor mother owed the first motion of joy that lightened her mourning.

“Well, Calyste,” said Mademoiselle des Touches, “I want you to go to Paris with me. We will find Beatrix,” she added in a low voice.

The pale, thin face flushed red and a smile brightened him.

“Let us go,” he said.

“We shall save him,” said Mademoiselle des Touches to the mother, who pressed her hands and wept for joy.

A week after the baron’s funeral, Mademoiselle des Touches, the Baronne du Guenic, and Calyste started for Paris, leaving the household in charge of old Zephirine.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Félicité carefully planned to secure Calyste’s future by allying him with the ducal Grandlieu family, whose line would end in five daughters. She had informed the Duchesse de Grandlieu of Calyste’s past and her intentions. After selling her rue du Mont‑Blanc house for two million five hundred thousand francs, her agent bought a rue de Bourbon house for seven hundred thousand; she intended to use one million to recover the du Guenic estates and leave the remainder for Sabine de Grandlieu. Félicité knew the family’s arrangements: the youngest would marry the Vicomte de Grandlieu (the heir); Clotilde‑Frédérique would remain unmarried for Lucien de Rubempré’s memory; two sisters were already married; and Sabine, nearly twenty, was the eligible match. Félicité resolved that Sabine should cure Calyste of his passion for Béatrix.

On the journey to Paris Mademoiselle des Touches revealed these plans to the baroness. The new rue de Bourbon house was being furnished as Sabine and Calyste’s future home should the scheme succeed. At the hôtel de Grandlieu the baroness received the honors due her rank. Mademoiselle des Touches encouraged Calyste to see Paris, investigated Béatrix’s whereabouts (she had gone abroad), and occupied him with diversions. As the ball season began, Calyste noticed a resemblance between Sabine and Madame de Rochefide, Paris’s most bewitching woman; this fancied likeness made him attentive to Sabine’s coquetry. Sabine liked Calyste, and the winter of 1837 restored his spirits. He listened without repugnance when his mother reminded him of his promise to his dying father and urged marriage with Sabine. Outwardly he agreed though inwardly indifferent—an indifference the baroness alone perceived and trusted time and a happy home would conquer.

On the day the families gathered in the Grandlieu salon to sign the marriage contract, when the notary Léopold Hannequin began the preliminaries, Calyste, disquieted, suddenly and curtly refused Mademoiselle des Touches’s benefactions. Embarrassed, the company watched as Sabine entered and handed him a letter from Mademoiselle des Touches intended for this moment. Calyste read Camille Maupin’s letter.

Camille’s letter was a renunciation and a last appeal. She wrote that before entering her convent cell she was allowed one last look at the world she abandoned, and that thought was of Calyste, who had been her whole world. Now betrothed to God, she implored him not to contest her chosen service. She acknowledged that if love is suffering she had indeed loved him; yet she wanted him to accept the service she could render: not a gift but a trust. She had thought more of his children and the old Breton house than of him when planning to devote her Parisian profits. She begged him to accept part of her fortune to restore the du Guenic name and sustain his lineage. Camille promised to pray for him and his family, describing her new innocence and serene devotion; her conversion was attributed to the Abbé Grimont, who soon became vicar‑general of the diocese.

Reading the letter moved Calyste; he returned to the company and said, “Let us sign the contract.” The following week, after an early ceremony at Saint‑Thomas d’Aquin, Calyste and Sabine left amid tears and congratulations. The Duchesse de Grandlieu and Clotilde wept, fearing Sabine had been launched upon life with a husband who had not wholly married of his own free will. The baroness, in a preceding carriage, had been asked by the duchess to take maternal care of Sabine. A chasseur rode as courier; servants, postilions, and ritual accompaniments completed the convoy. The duchess hinted to Calyste that she understood him as they bade farewell.

The narrative reflects on marriage’s nature: it is not mere pleasure but a test of temperamental compatibility, physical sympathies, and character harmonies. Women, especially mothers and marriageable daughters, know its risks; they weep at weddings because they grasp what is at stake, while men smile, thinking they risk little.

Much of a honeymoon belongs to the bride. Sabine, trained in the faubourg Saint‑Germain’s manners and possessing virginal sincerity, proved as interesting as Mademoiselle de Chaulieu. Her letters to her mother during the honeymoon reveal her mind and temperament. In Guérande she wrote that Calyste had married with great sorrow; their difficulties were greater than her mother had anticipated. She confessed that she loved Calyste as if he were not her husband—so entirely and devotedly that she feared losing dignity but embraced devotion nevertheless. Alone in the carriage their initial awkwardness led to polite words; she later pressed him to recount the events that had led to his despair and the marriage he felt obliged to make. His confession about Madame de Rochefide produced an argument, but he ended with protestations of absolute forgetfulness and gestures of affection—kissing and clasping her hand—gestures that made her more devoted, proud at having cured a wound he had thought incurable. She acknowledged that her devotion risked reducing her dignity but believed that loving him absolutely might be worth that danger.

After a few days of intimacy they abandoned formalities. Her mother‑in‑law, trying to take her mother’s place, was charming and attentive; she sensed Sabine’s heroism and hid her anxiety in precaution. At the sight of Guérande’s towers Sabine tested Calyste, asking if he had truly forgotten Béatrix; his delight at such confidence led Sabine to devote herself entirely to excising Madame de Rochefide from his memory. She admitted her devotion had made her his slave, though she resolved not to surrender all self‑respect.

By May 15 Sabine declared marriage delightful. She described playing the châtelaine—riding through forests, visiting farms, dining on oaken tables with centenary linen, attending local festivities, and receiving affection from tenants and neighbors. Calyste had assumed the seigneurial role with ease; peasants greeted him as a “beautiful seigneur” and likened him to former Guenics. Félicité’s repurchase of the du Guenic estates had drawn the tenants’ admiration and proposals for fair leases. Calyste planned to quarter his arms with those of Des Touches and adopt a motto; the couple visited the convent with Abbé Grimont, who affirmed that Félicité was indeed saintly. Mademoiselle des Touches, thinner and paler, would not receive Calyste but welcomed Sabine, speaking candidly.

She explained she had entered the order of Saint‑François de Sales to discipline the intellect rather than mortify the body; her “guilty” head had deceived her heart. She urged Sabine to preserve her happiness by exercising the “science of a woman’s life”: be capricious, coquettish—not by odious scheming but by an artful balance between usury and prodigality—so as to bind Calyste to her, bear him children, and secure his respect for their mother. Above all, Mademoiselle des Touches counseled that Sabine should try to prevent Calyste from ever meeting Béatrix again.

The name Béatrix stunned them both. When Sabine asked whether she should return to Guérande, Mademoiselle des Touches answered yes but warned, “Never go to Les Touches. I did wrong to give him that property.” She called Les Touches “Bluebeard’s chamber,” a place that could awaken a sleeping passion and thus be dangerously compromising. Sabine summarized their conversation and confessed she could not fully follow the advice to be capricious; her nature made her give herself over to love. She feared losing the happiness she had so willingly seized, yet clung to her devotion like a mother to an infant, trembling at any threat.

Note: When Béatrix was first published in 1839, the volume ended with a paragraph stating that Calyste, rich and married to the most beautiful woman in Paris, retained a sadness nothing dispelled—not even the birth of a son at Guérande—and that Béatrix still lived deep in his heart, leaving uncertain what disasters might follow should he meet Madame de Rochefide again. In 1842 that concluding paragraph was suppressed and the story continued as above.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Guerande, July, 1838.

Sabine writes to her mother, the Duchesse de Grandlieu, in an outpouring of anguish and bewildered jealousy. Married only three months, she has learned the meaning of jealousy: a mingling of the deepest hatred and the deepest love. She feels betrayed, unloved, and relieved to have a mother who will listen. The source of her torment is Les Touches, a place her husband Calyste’s past is attached to and which everyone—mother-in-law, aunt Zéphirine, Mademoiselle des Touches, and the Chevalier du Halga—treats as a forbidding name. Their hints and warnings ignite Sabine’s curiosity and test her security: when forbidden, Les Touches becomes irresistible. She teases and questions, and like many women who tempt fate, she is drawn into the very peril she fears. Calyste’s reticence about Les Touches pricks her pride; he is silent or evasive when she mentions Béatrix de Rochefide, the woman connected with that place. When finally he agrees to go, Sabine allows herself to be led there, intent on discovering whether his old passion is truly dead.

At Les Touches Sabine enjoys the morbid fascination of a place she had been told to avoid. She watches Calyste closely, seeking signs that his old emotion has faded. At first he seems pensive and she thinks she has conquered the past. Yet reminders of Béatrix—the bush where he once flung her into the sea, anecdotes of her improprieties—stir uneasy glimpses of jealousy in Sabine. A remark of Calyste’s—“cease to galvanize that passion”—both alarms and reassures her. The more Sabine seeks tests, the more she reveals her dependence on his affection; the more she clings to his smiles, the more afraid she becomes that her love is not returned as fully as she gives it.

The visit convinces her that Les Touches has reawakened something in Calyste. She sees in him a revival of the stormy sentiments she feared, and she withdraws from Les Touches, swearing never to return. Though she hates Madame de Rochefide and even longs for her death, Sabine recognizes that her hatred proves the depth of her love for Calyste. She’s tormented by dreams of Béatrix and plans to investigate whether Madame de Rochefide is in Paris; if so, Sabine contemplates staying away, afraid that another meeting would tip the balance of her marriage. Her letters to her mother reveal a young woman slowly realizing that her ideal marriage—she had imagined a union of love—has become something different: Calyste, while kind and charming, behaves sometimes like a man who took her for his convenience or who remains emotionally attached elsewhere.

By October 1838 the Guénic family return to Paris. The hôtel du Guénic is being restored, Grindot the architect supervising, Clotilde and the Grandlieu duchesse overseeing. Sabine settles in the rue de Bourbon to see how her marriage is judged by family and society, more anxious to be approved than to preside over a great household. Calyste, easy and somewhat indifferent, allows others to manage his social life; he has attained his place by name and marriage. Parisian pleasures, the duchess’s and Clotilde’s direction, and the distractions of high society renew some lightness in the household. Sabine’s anxiety and envy give way to pride in being admired; pregnancy and the arrival of a son in October 1839 seem to secure the marriage. Like many mothers, Sabine makes the natural but imprudent choice to nurse her child herself, believing herself inseparable from the baby.

By August 1840 Sabine has nearly finished nursing; two years in Paris have changed Calyste. Association with the Duc Georges de Maufrigneuse, card-playing pleasures, clubs, and other habits typical of idle young men have altered him. He learns to enjoy the entertainments of the town and the company of men who find in leisure the attraction of liberty from domestic duty. A young wife’s maternal care can accommodate a husband’s dissipations; Sabine, who loves and believes in him, tolerates much. One October evening, to escape their infant’s cries, Calyste is persuaded by Sabine to go to the Variétés; he sits in a stall, agreeing as a courtesy. During the interlude his eye is struck by Madame de Rochefide in a proscenium box. Seeing Béatrix again after three years convulses him. Her presence—light, motion, seductive art—revives in him the powerful impressions of the past. Sabine, with her duty and steadiness, contrasts painfully with Béatrix’s vivacity; past passion and present comfort stand opposed.

Calyste first intends to leave but, drawn, follows the half-open proscenium door and finds Béatrix between Canalis and Raoul Nathan. She has changed in three years—she has learned the arts of dress, coquetry, and cultivated a more poetic, studied allure. Béatrix whispers, teases, and in a tone that mixes complaint and triumph calls Sabine “a little fool.” Although married, she affects an air of wounded dignity and a desire to retain remnant attachments; abandoned by Conti she has refined seduction into an art. She tells Calyste she suffers, is exhausted, and claims to need a friend. He is wounded and charmed. She places him at once in a role both consoling and compromising. Canalis leaves them together; Béatrix’s perfume and presence disarm Calyste, who feels the old passion return. Her mixture of feminine resource and cultivated deprivation—an effect of deliberate artifice—transforms her poverty into a stage of dignity: a modest house in the rue de Chartres opposite the Parc Monceau, cheap staff, and a garden that retains traces of aristocratic taste. Calyste, intoxicated by memory and the possibilities of renewed intimacy, accepts her invitation to visit.

At Béatrix’s house the air is charged: silk, velvet, antiques, a dagger used as a paper-cutter, watercolors of bedrooms—decor that suggests both charm and impertinence. She greets him in a toilette calculated to awaken desire. The fragrance she once wore at Les Touches returns; the effect is immediate. She confesses two principles: she will not deny herself foolishly to a man she truly loves, yet she will not belong to any man. She pretends to respect Calyste’s duties—his young wife, his baby—while simultaneously insinuating possibilities. She ridicules Sabine—brown, dry, stiff, the product of Grandlieu’s line—and suggests that Sabine is unworthy of a man like Calyste. Her talk is calculated to wound the sensibilities of love and pride. The conversation goes on for hours; Béatrix’s mockery of Sabine and her claim to an enduring attachment inflame Calyste.

Béatrix manipulates Calyste with a mixture of cruelty and seduction. She taunts him about his marriage, urging him to dine with her and then accusing him of cowardice when he hesitates to deceive Sabine. When he suggests writing a note to excuse himself, she flaunts her power by demanding that he actually lie. Under her goading he composes a curt line—an unelaborated “I dine out; do not expect me”—which she sends. Calyste returns home in the small hours, exhausted and guilty. The note is laconic and on feminine scented paper; Sabine, naturally trusting yet anxious, explains it away at first but is unsettled. She is delighted in the morning to see her child well and to present him to Calyste. His distracted cordiality and his charm hide a deeper turmoil; Sabine does not yet understand the dimensions of his untruth.

Calyste, inexperienced in deception, scrambles to manage witnesses. He begs the Vicomte de Portenduere to corroborate a tale, then hurries back to Béatrix to ensure further intimacy. He stays until late, returning with his horse drenched and terrified Sabine by his lateness and the scent that sticks to his clothes. On seeing him she is at once relieved and alarmed; the perfume and the note’s paper linger in her memory and body. Her reaction is violent: dread, faintness, and a sense of having been betrayed. The next step is a desperate, horribly vivid scene of inner collapse and outright hysteria.

Sabine falls into convulsed prostration. She sends messages, rings for physician Dommanget, and, overwhelmed by a mix of indignation and despair, confides in her friend the Vicomtesse de Portenduere, Ursula, asking her to find out where Savinien and Calyste went. The fatal paper with a coronet on it—evidence of some titled woman—becomes in Sabine’s mind a mark of scandal. She imagines Calyste’s fidelity lost only three years into their marriage. Ursula, moved by the young wife’s danger, pledges secrecy and decides to intercede. She goes to the duchess, who, while shocked, recognizes the need for immediate deception to preserve Sabine’s life. The duchess counsels a practical lie: to prevent Sabine’s hysteria, they will invent explanations that will shield her temporarily from the reality. They are prepared to lie for the sake of a daughter’s health, admitting that such mendacity will require penance later.

The duchess goes to Sabine and, with Dommanget and Ursula, organizes a credible fiction. She tells Sabine that Béatrix is not in Paris and that the coronet-paper is fashionable—it does not necessarily indicate a marquise. She also fabricates a convenient cause for Calyste’s lateness: gambling losses at the hands of the Duc de Trailles and the jeune noble Georges de Maufrigneuse. Dommanget, playing along, announces that Calyste lost about thirty thousand francs at cards, which will explain his odd mood and pecuniary anxieties. The duchess’s ploy is to take Calyste’s apparent guilt and recast it as a cause of domestic worry which will be more palatable to Sabine than the truth of a liaison. Her hope is to avert Sabine’s crisis by falsifying a narrative that Calyste might conceivably have—deplorably—fallen into without infidelity: the vice of cardrooms and masculine folly.

Calyste, ashamed and cowardly, is trapped between his own conscience and the lie. He sits by Sabine’s bed, trying to put tenderness into his gaze. Dommanget, the duchess, and Ursula maintain the deception. Sabine takes the explanation with the terrible ambivalence of a woman caught between gratitude for her husband and the lingering sense of betrayal. She declares, with a flash of brave pride, that her husband’s losses mean nothing to her; she will not judge. Calyste kisses her, whispers that she is an angel, and two days later she is thought out of danger. The crisis has been smoothed by lies designed to save a life rather than to conserve innocence: the duchess’s moral reckoning is that to save Sabine she herself has told a falsehood, which will afterward need atonement.

Meanwhile Calyste returns to Béatrix and tells her, almost boastfully, that he has sacrificed his wife to be with her; that Sabine nearly died from the shock, that her milk “went to her head,” and that the supposed erysipelas might leave marks on her. Béatrix listens with a face cold as ice. Far from being melted by remorse or flattered by the sacrifice, she reacts by savoring Sabine’s misfortune. Her icy sarcasms are savage: the younger, virtuous wife becomes to her a useful foil, the contrast serving Béatrix’s sense of triumph. Calyste’s attempt to arouse pity for his wife in Béatrix rebounds perversely: she uses the misfortune as an argument in her favor, insinuating that Sabine’s suffering will only make her more desirable to Calyste—a macabre and cruel way to claim victory.

Béatrix’s behavior is explained by her nature and experience: abandoned by Conti and reduced to the role of an artful survivor, she has learned to weaponize vanity, melancholy, and performance. She is not merely a courtesan but an aristocratic woman who can still hide a courtesan’s mechanisms beneath the mask of a grande dame. She understands masculine weakness and the psychology of conquest: contempt, disdain, and the simulation of indifference become tools for securing the next triumph. To win Calyste back, or at least to keep him in her orbit, she begins a calculated campaign of disdain, mockery, and organized contempt for Sabine and for the faubourg Saint-Germain’s comforts. Béatrix’s deliberate uglification of Calyste—telling him he is ugly, coarse, and humiliating his physical sense—works to stimulate his desire by provoking the pride that wants to be conquered. That reverse psychology, the performance of disgust, is for her the surest way to rekindle passion.

She also wages a subtler assault on the milieu that supports Sabine. She contrasts her own cultivated, if reduced, bohemian elegance and poetic homelessness with the hôtel du Guénic’s bourgeois comfort, implying that Sabine’s household is vulgar complacency and that Calyste’s tastes deserve a wilder, more aristocratic companion. Béatrix’s strategy is twofold: to make Sabine appear small and to render Calyste eager to reclaim the illusion of romantic struggle that first made him love Béatrix. She counts on the fact that deserted wives often abandon their former art; she plays off the natural jealousy that admiration for the courtesan produces among parlor-bred wives.

The reconciliation between Calyste and Béatrix culminates in a scene staged as theatrical romance: a bower abundant with flowers and rare plants, in which Béatrix applies the “science of nothings,” her arts of triviality made seductive. Here she exacts conditions: she requires Calyste to swear hatred of his wife, to renounce the domestic idyll, and to dedicate himself to Béatrix in the fashion of their Guérande days. She has decided that the spectacle of Sabine’s fall is her pedestal—that public notoriety, achieved by the ruin of a beautiful young Grandlieu, will be the proof of her own continued power. Calyste, caught between shame and the intoxication of a renewed passion, submits to these demands. He has learned neither the subtle lies required to juggle a wife and a mistress nor the social arts of adultery; he is inexperienced in deception and too raw and eager in his affections. His bluntness—confessing Sabine’s existence to Béatrix and boasting of her collapse—makes him more vulnerable and less able to contain the consequences of his desires.

Throughout these scenes Sabine is portrayed as the victim of her own innocence and of forces she neither comprehends nor can oppose: the cunning of a practiced coquette, the social permissiveness of Paris, the corrupting lure of masculine idleness, and the protective but manipulative devotion of her mother and friends. The duchess’s protective falsehoods are well-intended but complicate the moral landscape: they postpone the confrontation between husband and wife and substitute an expedient fiction for the truth that might heal or destroy. Calyste’s nature—enamored of the tumult of first passion, susceptible to flattery, and ill-equipped to deceive with finesse—dooms him to repeat his mistakes. Béatrix, educated in the arts of humiliation and seduction, knows how to convert every advantage into a weapon. Her refusal to pity or spare Sabine, her relish in the young wife’s humiliation, and her demand that Calyste renounce his domestic loyalty mark the cruelty of her ambition.

The narrative closes with this uneasy victory: Béatrix triumphs by making Calyste her votary and by transforming Sabine’s domestic virtue into an instrument of notoriety. Calyste’s surrender—his oath to hate his wife and to embrace the romantic drama of the past—cements the new disorder. Sabine, meanwhile, remains at the center of the wrecked ideals: pure, loving, and heroic in her consternation, she is betrayed both by the husband she adored and by the brittle society that enables the machinations of a woman like Béatrix. The domestic idyll that once promised continuity and moral certainty has given way to a Paris where artful passion, vanity, and social theater can sunder marriage and produce suffering that the most prudent maternal intervention can only temporarily palliate.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After nursing her first child, Sabine returned to ordinary life looking enchanted and refreshed. Maternity had renewed her; she tried to recapture the charm of the honeymoon, only to find that her husband was no longer the Calyste she had adored. Instead of resting in happiness, she watched him with growing unease until she smelled the fatal perfume of another woman. This time she sought certainty rather than confiding in friends or the mother who had so kindly deceived her—certainty came quickly, as inexorable as the sun.

About two weeks after the first crisis, Sabine received a letter:

Guerande.
To Madame la Baronne du Guenic:
My dear Daughter,—Your aunt Zephirine and I are lost in conjectures about the dressing-table of which you tell us in your letter. I have written to Calyste about it, and I beg you to excuse our ignorance. You can never doubt our hearts, I am sure. We are piling up riches for you here. Thanks to the advice of Mademoiselle de Pen-Hoel on the management of your property, you will find yourself within a few years in possession of a considerable capital without losing any of your income.
Your letter, dear child as dearly loved as if I had borne you in my bosom and fed you with my milk, surprised me by its brevity, and above all by your silence about my dearest little Calyste. You told me nothing of the great Calyste either, but then, I know that he is happy, etc.

Sabine wrote across it, “Noble Brittany does not always lie,” and laid the paper on Calyste’s desk. He found and read it, recognized her handwriting, then flung it into the fire, resolved to pretend it had never reached him. Sabine spent a week in an agony known to only the most solitary souls: Calyste’s silence terrified her. She reproached herself—had her virtue become hateful? Had she humiliated her idol? Those thoughts ploughed furrows in her heart.

Further proof arrived. Beatrix, bolder now, wrote to Calyste at home; Madame du Guenic, recognizing the paper and perfume, handed the letter unopened to her husband, saying, “My friend, that letter is from the Jockey Club; I recognize both the paper and the perfume.” Calyste colored and pocketed it. Sabine, left alone with little Calyste, tried to hide her torment in maternal duties, but despair overwhelmed her. When servants told her Calyste would not dine at home, she sprang up, ordered a carriage, and went to the Opera, dressing brilliantly to seem smiling and triumphant. But when Otello’s music struck her, she fled; music was sometimes stronger than resolve.

From then on luxury turned to torment. Riches that had once delighted her became nettles: jewelry and perfumes burned like vipers. Sabine entered the aristocratic mode of suffering where every indulgence reminded her of her pain. She watched her husband’s movements, learning to divine whether he was going to the rue de Chartres to Madame de Rochefide. She studied his forehead, hair, eyes, every little thing with the desperate attention of a hunted woman. These fatal investigations rotted the tender roots of trust.

If he stayed at home, Sabine tried to be gay and humble. Once he complained that the rooms were cold and bare; she thought, “Madame de Rochefide likes flowers,” and filled the hotel du Guenic with blossoms none in Paris could surpass. When Calyste complained of draughts, she bought a glass screen to match what she imagined his mistress used; when he pecked at dinner and left plates untouched, she schemed with servants to learn Madame de Rochefide’s recipes and serve better dishes, yet he still found fault. Sabine bought English cruets and spiced sauces to imitate the condiments she believed his rival provided; but the rival’s inventions were endless.

This struggle consumed months. It was agony but preferable, Sabine thought, to the deadness of indifference. Yet one evening, after dressing herself in a toilette intended to eclipse a rival, Calyste laughed, “In spite of all you can do, Sabine, you’ll never be anything but a handsome Andalusian.” She collapsed: “I may never make myself a blonde, but if this continues I shall soon be thirty-five years old.” She stayed home, tore flowers from her hair, trampled her gown, and went to bed, broken.

Her suffering thinned her; grief consumed her, though she persisted in the role she had imposed: gentle, humble, suppressing the bitter words that pain suggested. Her health became apparent to her family. The duchess, an excellent mother, perceived a moral cause to her daughter’s weakness and tried to draw Sabine away from her martyrdom. Sabine at first kept silent, fearing interference between herself and Calyste, even declaring she was happy. Eventually, after months of caressing by her mother and sister Clotilde, she confessed her despair, cursed life, and spoke of death with delirious joy. She begged Clotilde, who vowed to remain unmarried, to be a mother to little Calyste should she die.

One evening, with Athenais and Clotilde, Sabine gave voice to her bitterness and humiliation, warning her sister before marriage: falsely to adorn oneself is often necessary; virtue may wreck happiness. “I perish through my best qualities,” she cried. “All I knew—my virtues—were rocks on which my happiness is wrecked. I have ceased to please because I am not thirty-six years old. I laugh frankly, and that is wrong; to captivate I ought to play off hypocrisy. I am straightforward; but duplicity is more pleasing. I am loyally passionate, but I ought to be manoeuvring and hypocritical.” She imagined that, had she been worldly like her rival, she would be consoled, pitied, even adored. Instead, she had to learn “the wisdom of indifference” if she hoped to win her husband back.

Clotilde whispered to be vengeful; Sabine answered she wished to die “irreproachable and without the mere semblance of doing wrong. A woman’s vengeance should be worthy of her love.” The duchess, calmer, assured her that love is a means to preserve the family. She had already discussed the delicate crisis with Sabine’s father, the Duc de Chaulieu, and d’Ajuda; she promised to find means to bring Calyste back. Clotilde smiled, noting that the Marquise de Rochefide never kept her adorers long, and vowed to help rescue Calyste if their mother’s conscience forbade drastic measures.

Sabine then revealed another anguish: she believed she was pregnant again. This compounded the horror—if Madame de Rochefide bore a child, Calyste might favor that child over Sabine’s. “If Calyste loved the child of that woman more than mine—Oh! that’s the end of my patience and all my resignation,” she said, falling into a chair. Lifting the secret made her feel both exposed and relieved—the hidden sorrow had been a support that, once confessed, freed her to seek help.

The duchess, anxious to act, summoned the Abbe Brossette, a cautious, austere vicar of about fifty, known for his gravity and faith. When he arrived, the duchess, with measured gravity, asked if she might undertake a “rather wicked intrigue” for a great good and whether it would hinder her salvation. Explaining that Calyste had left Sabine for Madame de Rochefide, she confessed she had thought of launching a small man—self-willed and odious—upon Madame de Rochefide to induce her to dismiss Calyste. The abbe, smiling, advised that while the duty of a Christian woman is to withdraw a sinner from evil rather than push her along it, for a woman so far advanced as Madame de Rochefide, only a thunderbolt might save her; from the world’s point of view the method would be decisive.

The duchess proposed other plans—perhaps provoking a quarrel so that a duel would force the matter—but the abbe steered her toward a less odious scheme. Rather than driving away one nail with another, she might persuade Monsieur de Rochefide to take back his wife, thereby restoring Calyste to Sabine and perhaps saving a soul. The duchess had even resolved to spend money and had vowed a charitable donation if she succeeded. At first Monsieur de Grandlieu had laughed at her; the abbe’s prudence and spiritual nuance steadied the conversation. He suggested, more morally, giving Madame de Rochefide a husband instead of adding a fresh scandal to her life. That correction pleased the duchess; she thanked him fervently.

The abbe warned of a risk: what if, after reconciliation, Madame de Rochefide kept Calyste? The duchess admitted that when one does not commonly intrigue, one may do it badly; she would employ professional intriguers. The abbe, seeing the aim—saving a daughter—reluctantly approved that she “save her child,” and promised to advise if conscience troubled her. Delighted, the duchess accompanied him to the salon door, her resolve firm to use whatever means necessary to restore her daughter’s home.

Thus the scene ends with the duchess poised to embark on an intrigue—tempered by the abbe’s cautious endorsement—to recover Calyste, free Sabine from humiliation, and preserve the family, even if the means required moral compromises and the aid of those accustomed to such artifices.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A storm was gathering over Monsieur de Rochefide, a Parisian who enjoyed what many desired: being to Madame Schontz almost as much a husband as he had been to Béatrix. The contrast between Rochefide’s contentment and the ruin that often befalls deserted women illustrates how laws and morals favor men; what destroys a woman can bring a man comfort. This disparity might encourage some women, like Sabine du Guenic, to remain and practice chosen virtues rather than flee.

After Béatrix left him, Arthur de Rochefide, now sole heir, inherited the hôtel de Rochefide and two hundred thousand francs a year from his father; combined with his marriage fortune, this amounted to roughly a thousand francs a day. For the sort of man Mademoiselle des Touches described, such wealth was nearly sufficient happiness. While his wife performed maternal duties, Rochefide enjoyed his fortune without extravagance or intellectual ambition. Vanity, handsome looks, performed wit on stage and in journals, and a jocular military air from service in the Royal Guard made him appear amusing to uncritical women. He was essentially an imitator—adopting fashions, being accused only of long toilettes and wearing a corset—pleasing by reflecting others’ ideas and never aging in public esteem.

As a husband he was pitied; people blamed Béatrix for abandoning such a good fellow and mocked her. A member of every club and subscriber to every patriotic or partisan absurdity, this loyal, brave, very silly nobleman resembled many rich men. Seeking distinction, he cultivated a fashionable mania: he fancied himself sultan of a four-footed harem managed by an old English groom at great cost, his specialty being horse racing. He supported a hippic magazine while knowing little of horses, relying entirely on his groom. Thus this semi-bachelor had nothing truly his own—no original mind, taste, position, or eccentricity; his fortune itself came from his fathers. After marital displeasure he was so pleased to be again a bachelor that he joked he had been born with a caul.

Living without the obligations marriage demanded of husbands, Rochefide scarcely inhabited his house, rarely dined there, and seldom slept: the reason was his new liaison. Bored with fashionable women and after various adventures, Rochefide had, in effect, taken as his mistress a certain Madame Schontz—celebrated in the circulating circles of Fanny Beaupré, Susanne du Val-Noble, Florine, Mariette, and Jenny Cadine. To portray that world faithfully, one must note the varied endings of such women—poverty, early or self-inflicted death, lucky marriages, or opulence.

Madame Schontz, first called La Petite-Aurélie, belongs to a high class of women whose social utility is undeniable: without such Aspasias on Montmartre and similar quarters, far fewer houses would be built in Paris. These women pick speculative spots on architectural steppes where risk and reward hinge on exact geography. When Rochefide first saw Madame Schontz, she lived on the third floor of the last house in rue de Berlin—on the border between misery and comfort. Her real name was not Schontz or Aurélie; she concealed her father’s name, as many girls of such origin do. Madame Schontz had the Saint-Denis education—well-formed but, once the school’s protector (the Emperor) vanished, offering little real opening.

Aurélie (Josephine Schiltz) was the daughter of Colonel Schiltz, an Alsatian who nearly saved the Emperor in 1814 and died ruined. Napoleon placed little Josephine at Saint-Denis; left without resources after her parents’ deaths, she stayed on as an under-mistress until 1827. Her patience failed; beauty and the example of other resourceful comrades tempted her toward a courtesan’s adventurous life. She adopted Aurélie as a name and St. Aurélie as patron saint.

Lively, witty, and well-educated, she committed more faults than her duller companions; she knew various writers (clever but insolvent), tried rich but foolish men, and sacrificed solid prospects to one true love. One day of extreme misery—dancing at Valentino’s in borrowed garments—she attracted Rochefide, who had come to see the galop. Her cleverness captivated a man uncommitted to passion. Two years after Béatrix’s desertion, Rochefide, unblamed for consorting “in the thirteenth arrondissement,” effectively remarried.

Their happiness unfolded in four phases, a typical pattern for such liaisons: initial caution and miserly provision, followed by display and social elevation, then deeper integration into household and finances, and finally habit and consolidation of power by the woman.

At first Rochefide feared being “carotted” (duped) and behaved niggardly. He allowed Madame Schontz five hundred francs a month, furnished shabby rooms in rue Coquenard costing 1,200 francs a year, and studied Aurélie’s character while she, perceiving his intent, gave him a character to study. He was pleased to find in her a woman of noble bearing: she spoke English, German, and Italian, knew foreign literatures, held her own at the piano, had dabbled in painting to surprising effect, and had absorbed scientific amusements at Saint-Denis. For a while she allowed Arthur to take credit for traits she had cultivated.

By year’s end she began to show practical eagerness—arriving in draggled gowns at times when Rochefide waited—and by ten months the second phase began. She persuaded him to give her a fine apartment in rue Neuve-Saint-Georges, splendid furniture, a complete service of plate, 1,200 francs a month, a hired carriage, and the trappings of respectability. Unsentimental, she knew Rochefide’s calculations and used them. Sick of restaurant life, he offered Aurélie forty francs a day for dinners; she accepted. Having exhausted moral credit, she asked for 500 francs more monthly for dress—to avoid shaming her gros papa whose circle was Jockey Club men—and framed it as a way to increase Arthur’s social standing among his peers.

She assumed the role of competent housekeeper and manager: balancing two thousand five hundred francs a month without debt, serving dinners superior to Nucingen’s, with wines at twelve francs a bottle. Rochefide, amazed, declared her “a treasure.” He bought a third of an Opera box and began to consult her, taking credit for her sayings and thus enhancing his reputation. She refused a Russian prince offering five thousand francs a month, professing to love Arthur as a wife loves an honest husband—an avowal that, when repeated to club acquaintances, increased public complacency toward Rochefide and public neglect of the hôtel de Rochefide. Shifting his whole existence to Madame Schontz, Arthur prospered; after three years he had four hundred thousand francs to invest.

The third phase deepened their bond: Aurélie became loving to Arthur’s son, fetched him from school, and dowered him with presents and pocket money. She guided Arthur’s investments—urging purchases in the Funds when low and thus securing a large gain for him—yet claimed no reward. Rochefide invested six hundred thousand in Bank of France stock, placing half in Josephine Schiltz’s name; he hired Grindot to decorate a little house on rue de La Bruyère. Rochefide then ceased to manage his affairs; Madame Schontz collected revenues and paid bills. Acting as both wife and businesswoman, she gratified Arthur’s tastes as Madame de Pompadour gratified Louis XV.

She patronized a few young artists and men of letters—bright, hungry types eager for reputations. Her conduct, tactical and superior, soon made her the most agreeable woman on that social borderland. A dozen young men supplied witty sayings and social currency without questioning the mistress’s constancy; they proclaimed her intelligent and thereby advertised her attraction. Rivals spread petty calumnies, but Aurélie out-blagued them. She also directed Arthur away from racing, arguing that buying horses reduced the turf to gambling and suggesting his money be given to poets instead; persuaded, he economized sixty thousand francs and cut the expense. She taunted would-be suitors—Finot and others—declaring Rochefide had made her “half a wife and a lady,” something an upstart could not do; this reinforced the bonds.

The fourth phase—habit—completed her victory: she could say of a man, “I hold him!” Arthur, having bought a small hôtel in Mademoiselle Josephine Schiltz’s name (eighty thousand francs), merged his tastes, defects, and pleasures into Madame Schontz and became incapable or unwilling to change. In five years she consolidated such power that introductions to her required proposal; she refused dull or smirched rich people, admitting only select aristocratic names. Ostensibly she managed the three hundred thousand francs Rochefide had given her, invested by a broker, Gobenheim—the only broker admitted into her house. Secretly she controlled another hidden fortune of two hundred thousand francs, built from savings and the constant rotation of the known sum; she augmented this hoard by wearing and then selling jewels and diamonds. When called rich, she replied that three hundred thousand at the Funds produced only twelve thousand a year and that she had spent heavily in hard times—an answer that made her appear prudent and modest while concealing real independence.

Thus Rochefide, once a dilettante imitator, ended up wholly dependent on a clever, tactful woman who combined the roles of lover, housekeeper, financier, and social director. Madame Schontz’s ascent typified the strategic, stagewise conquest common to women of her class: turn cautious advantage into display, display into management, and management into intractable habit. She secured social prestige, financial autonomy (both open and secret), a patronage network, and the unshakeable devotion—if not passion—of a man who had transferred his ego and vanity into her keeping.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Such conduct implied a plan, and Madame Schontz had one. Jealous for two years of Madame du Bruel, she was consumed with the ambition to be married by church and mayor. Every social position has its forbidden fruit—some small thing magnified into the weightiest concern. Her scheme required a second Arthur; Rochefide’s secret rival remained undiscovered. Bixiou fancied Leon de Lora the favorite; the painter suspected Bixiou, who, past forty, ought to be making a fate. Suspicions also fell on Victor de Vernisset, a Canalis-school poet desperately in love; he accused Stidmann, a young sculptor, of rivalry. Claude Vignon, the young Comte de la Palferine, Gobenheim, Vermanton—all frequented the salon and were suspected, then proved innocent. No one fathomed Madame Schontz, certainly not Rochefide, who thought she preferred the young, witty La Palferine; she was virtuous from self-interest and wholly bent on making a good marriage.

Only one man of equivocal reputation was ever seen in Madame Schontz’s salon: Couture, a stock-gambler who had once scandalized his brothers. Couture had been one of Madame Schontz’s earliest friends, and she alone remained faithful to him. The false alarm of 1840 swept away the last vestige of this speculator’s credit; Aurélie, seeing his ill-luck, made Rochefide play in another direction. Glad to find a place at Aurélie’s table, Couture—who sometimes received a thousand-franc note from Finot, the cleverest or luckiest of parvenus—was shrewd enough to offer his hand and name to Madame Schontz. She studied whether this bold speculator had political power and sufficient gratitude not to desert his wife. Couture, about forty-three, half worn-out, did not redeem the unpleasant sonority of his name by birth; he said little of the authors of his days.

Madame Schontz was bemoaning the scarcity of eligible men when Couture presented a provincial, supplied with the two handles by which women take hold of such pitchers when they wish to keep them. To sketch this person is to paint a portion of the youth of the day.

In 1838, Fabien du Ronceret, son of a chief-justice of the Royal court at Caen (recently deceased), left Alençon, resigning his judgeship—an office his father had compelled him to hold—and came to Paris to make a noise. A Norman ambition, difficult to realize on an income scarcely scraping eight thousand francs a year; his mother still held a life-interest in a valuable estate in Alençon. In earlier visits he had tried his rope like an acrobat and had perceived the great vice of the social replastering of 1830. He meant to turn it to profit, following the example of the longest heads of the bourgeoisie. This requires a glance at one effect of the new order.

Modern equality, unduly pushed, has produced in private life—parallel to politics—three great divisions of the social I: pride, conceit, and vanity. Fools wish to pass for wits; wits want to be thought men of talent; men of talent want to be treated as genius. By pulling down recognized superiorities, a nation opens the sluice through which a torrent of secondary ambitions rushes, each meaner than the last. Democrats say there was an evil in the aristocracy, but they substitute a dozen armed, contending aristocracies—the worst of all. Proclaiming equality, they promulgate a declaration of the rights of Envy. We inherit the Revolution’s saturnalia transferred to mind, industry, and politics; reputations won by toil, service, and talent are treated like privileges taken from the masses. Distinction is sought at any price—by ridicule, affectation, and petty public causes—creating fictitious dignities and a profusion of presidents, vice-presidents, and secretaries for societies whose numbers exceed the questions they pretend to solve. Society on a grand scale has been demolished to make a million little ones in the image of the defunct. These parasitic organizations reveal decomposition: are they not maggots in a dead body? All these societies are daughters of one mother, Vanity. Catholic charity or true beneficence proceed differently; they study and cure wounds rather than perorate in public meetings for the pleasure of perorating.

Fabien du Ronceret, without being superior, had divined—by the greedy common-sense peculiar to a Norman—the gain he could derive from this public vice. Every epoch has a character clever men exploit. Fabien’s mind, though not clever, was wholly bent on making himself talked about.

“My dear fellow, a man must make himself talked about, if he wants to be anything,” he said on parting from the king of Alençon, a certain du Bousquier, a friend of his father. “In six months I shall be better known than you are!”

So Fabien obeyed the spirit of his age. He made his debut in Bohemia, where he was known as “The Heir” because of premeditated prodigalities. Du Ronceret profited by Couture’s follies for the pretty Madame Cadine: during an ephemeral opulence he had arranged a delightful ground-floor apartment with garden in the rue Blanche. The Norman, who wanted luxury ready-made, bought Couture’s furniture and the improvements he was forced to leave—kiosk, rustic gallery, India mattings, potteries—though he called the result his den and never acknowledged the architect Grindot, the carvings of Stidmann, or the paintings of Leon de Lora—Fabien’s crowning vanity lied for the sake of magnifying himself.

He crowned the magnificence with a greenhouse built along a southern wall—not through any love of flowers but to attack public notice through horticulture. He had nearly reached his end. Elected vice-president of a floral society presided over by the Duc de Vissembourg, he wore the Legion of Honor ribbon at an exhibition, delivered an opening speech bought from Lousteau for five hundred francs and boldly pronounced it his own, and made a stir by presenting a flower given him by old Blondet of Alençon as the product of his greenhouse.

But this was nothing. The Heir, who wanted to be accepted as a wit, planned to consort with clever celebrities and reflect their fame—hard to do on eight thousand francs a year. He repeatedly asked Bixiou, Stidmann, and Leon de Lora to present him to Madame Schontz and allow him into her menagerie of lions. Failing these, he applied to Couture, for whose dinners he had so often paid that the late speculator felt obliged to recommend him to Madame Schontz, if only as an elegant unpaid footman whom mistresses employ to run errands when servants are lacking.

In three evenings Madame Schontz read Fabien like a book and said to herself, “If Couture does not suit me, I am certain of saddling that one. My future can go on two legs now.” This queer fellow whom everybody laughed at was really the chosen one—chosen, however, with an intention that made such preference insulting. The improbability of the choice kept suspicion away. Madame Schontz intoxicated Fabien with secret smiles and little scenes on the threshold when she bade him good-night, if Rochefide stayed. She often made Fabien a third with Arthur in her opera-box, excusing it by saying he had done her some service. Men have a natural conceit common as to women—the belief of being loved exclusively. Of all flattering passions none is more prized than that of a Madame Schontz: the man she makes the object of a love she calls “from the heart,” as distinct from another sort. A woman like her, who plays the great lady and whose intrinsic value is real, would be an object of pride to Fabien, who fell in love so that he never presented himself before her eyes except in full dress, varnished boots, lemon-kid gloves, embroidered shirt and frill, variegated waistcoat—in short, with all the external symptoms of profound worship.

A month before the duchess’s conference with her confessor, Madame Schontz confided the secret of her birth and real name to Fabien, who did not at all understand the motive. A fortnight later she was surprised at his want of intelligence and exclaimed to herself, “Heavens! how stupid I am! he expects me to love him for himself.” Accordingly she took the Heir in her calèche to the Bois—she now had two little carriages—to discuss her future and declared she wished to marry.

“I have seven hundred thousand francs,” she said, “and if I could find a man full of ambition who understands my character, I would change my position; for do you know the dream of my life? To become a true bourgeoise, enter an honorable family, and make my husband and children truly happy.”

The Norman wished to be “distinguished” by Madame Schontz, but marrying her seemed a folly to a bachelor of thirty-eight whom the revolution of July had made a judge. Seeing his hesitation, Madame Schontz made him the butt of her wit and disdain and turned to Couture. Within a week Couture, whom she put upon the scent of her fortune, had offered his hand, heart, and future—three things of about the same value.

The manoeuvres of Madame Schontz had reached this stage when the Duchesse de Grandlieu began inquiries into the life of the Béatrix of the Place Saint-Georges.

Following the Abbe Brossette’s advice, the duchess asked the Marquis d’Ajuda to bring the celebrated Comte Maxime de Trailles—the archduke of Bohemia, political cut-throat and youngest of young men though fully fifty. D’Ajuda dined with Maxime at the club in the rue de Beuane and proposed afterward to go play dummy whist with the Duc de Grandlieu, who was feigning gout.

Maxime did not deceive himself about the invitation’s meaning; he felt certain the duke or duchess needed him. Club life—men playing cards with others they do not receive at home—is a telling sign of the age.

After several games the duke went to bed, leaving his wife tête-à-tête with Maxime and d’Ajuda. The duchess, seconded by the marquis, revealed her project and asked Maxime’s assistance while ostensibly seeking only his advice. Maxime listened without committing, waiting until she asked point-blank.

“Madame, I fully understand you,” he said, casting that shrewd, penetrating look by which great scamps compromise their interlocutors. “D’Ajuda will tell you that if any one in Paris can conduct that difficult negotiation it is I—of course without mixing you up in it; without it being even known that I have come here this evening. Only, before anything is done, we must settle preliminaries. How much are you willing to sacrifice?”

“All that is necessary.”

“Very well, then, Madame la duchesse. As the price of my efforts you must do me the honor to receive in your house and seriously protect Madame la Comtesse de Trailles.”

“What! are you married?” cried d’Ajuda.

“I shall be married within a fortnight to the heiress of a rich but extremely bourgeois family—a sacrifice to opinion! I imbibe the very spirit of my government and start upon a new career. Consequently, Madame la duchesse will understand how important it is to have my wife adopted by her and her family. I am certain of being made deputy by my father-in-law’s resignation, and I am promised a diplomatic post in keeping with my new fortune. I do not see why my wife should not be as well received as Madame de Portenduere in that society of young women. My wife is pretty, and I will undertake to uncotton-night-cap her. Will this suit you, Madame la duchesse? You are religious, and if you say yes, your promise, which I know to be sacred, will greatly aid my change of life. It will be one more good action to your account. Alas! I have long been the king of mauvais sujets, and I want to make an end of it. After all, we bear, azure, a wyvern or, darting fire... and we have been counts since Catherine de’ Medici.”

“I will receive and protect your wife,” said the duchess solemnly, “and my family will not turn its back upon her; I give you my word.”

“Ah! Madame la duchesse,” cried Maxime, visibly touched, “if Monsieur le duc would also deign to treat me with some kindness, I promise you to make your plan succeed without its costing you very much. But,” he paused, “you must take upon yourself to follow my instructions. This is the last intrigue of my bachelor life; it must be all the better managed because it concerns a good action.”

“Follow your instructions!” the duchess replied. “Then I must appear in all this.”

“Ah! madame, I will not compromise you,” Maxime protested. “I esteem you too much to demand guarantees. I merely mean that you must follow my advice. For example, it will be necessary that du Guenic be taken away by his wife for at least two years; she must show him Switzerland, Italy, Germany—in short, all possible countries.”

“You confirm a fear of my director,” said the duchess naively, recalling the Abbe Brossette’s prudence.

Maxime and d’Ajuda smiled at the idea of an agreement between heaven and hell.

“To prevent Madame de Rochefide from ever seeing Calyste again,” the duchess continued, “we will all travel—Juste and his wife, Calyste, Sabine, and I. I will leave Clotilde with her father—”

“It is too soon to sing victory, madame,” said Maxime. “I foresee enormous difficulties; though I shall no doubt vanquish them. Your esteem and protection are rewards which would make me commit the vilest actions, but these will be—”

“The vilest actions!” cried the duchess, whose piety disclosed selfishness.

“And you would share them, madame, inasmuch as I am only your agent. But are you ignorant of the degree of blindness to which Madame de Rochefide has brought your son-in-law? I know it from Canalis and Nathan, between whom she was hesitating when Calyste threw himself into the lioness’s jaws. Béatrix has contrived to persuade that serious Breton that she has never loved any one but him; that she is virtuous; that Conti was merely a sentimental head-love—in short, a musical love. As for Rochefide, that was duty. So, you understand, she is virgin!—a fact she proves by forgetting her son, whom for more than a year she has not tried to see. The little count will soon be twelve, and he finds in Madame Schontz a mother who is all the more a mother because maternity is, as you know, a passion with women of that sort. Du Guenic would let himself be cut in pieces and would chop up his wife for Béatrix; and you think it easy to drag a man from such credulity! Ah! madame, Shakespeare’s Iago would lose all his handkerchiefs. People mistake theatrical passion for true love. Love, Madame la duchesse, is not loving a noble woman; it is to say to oneself: ‘She whom I love is infamous; she deceives me; she is abandoned; she is hellish,’ yet we rush to her and find there the blue of heaven. That is how Molière loved, and how we, scamps that we are, love. As for me, I weep at the great scene of Arnolphe. Now, that is how your son-in-law loves Béatrix. I shall have trouble separating Rochefide from Madame Schontz; but Madame Schontz will no doubt lend herself to the plot; I shall study her interior. But as for Calyste and Béatrix, they will need blows of an axe, far deeper treachery, and so base an infamy that your virtuous imagination could never descend to it—unless indeed your director gave you a hand. You have asked the impossible; you shall be obeyed. But in spite of my settled intention to war with fire and sword, I cannot absolutely promise you success. I have known lovers who did not recoil before the most awful disillusions. You are too virtuous to know the full power of women who are not virtuous.”

“Do not enter upon those infamous actions until I consult the Abbe Brossette to know how far I may be your accomplice,” cried the duchess, with a naiveté that revealed the selfishness in her piety.

“You shall be ignorant of everything, my dear mother,” interposed d’Ajuda.

On the portico, as the marquis’s carriage drew up, d’Ajuda said to Maxime: “You frightened that good duchess.”

“But she has no idea of the difficulty of what she asks. Let us go to the Jockey Club; Rochefide must invite me to dine with Madame Schontz tomorrow, for to-night my plan will be made, and I shall have chosen the pawns on my chessboard to carry it out. In the days of her splendor Béatrix refused to receive me; I intend to pay off that score, and I will avenge your sister-in-law so cruelly that perhaps she will find herself too well revenged.”

The next day Rochefide told Madame Schontz that Maxime de Trailles was coming to dinner. That meant notifying her to display all her luxury and prepare the choicest food for this connoisseur emeritus, whom all women of Madame Schontz’s type revered. Madame Schontz considered her toilette and her house with the same care she would give to presenting to such a personage.

In Paris there are as many royalties as varieties of art, mental and moral specialties, sciences, and professions; the strongest and most capable of their practitioners has a majesty all his own and is respected by peers who know their art’s difficulties. Maxime was, in the eyes of rats and courtesans, an extremely powerful and capable man who had known how to make himself excessively loved. He was also admired by men who appreciate the difficulty of living in Paris on good terms with creditors; in short, he had no rival in elegance, deportment, and wit except the illustrious de Marsay, who often employed him on political missions. All this explains his interview with the duchess, his prestige with Madame Schontz, and the authority of his words in a conference he planned to hold on the boulevard des Italiens with a young man already known, though newly arrived, in the Bohemia of Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The next day Maxime de Trailles summoned Finot and arranged a Café Anglais breakfast with Couture and Lousteau to learn about Madame Schontz. Finot, deferential to Maxime, complied; at breakfast Couture, prompted by Finot and unwittingly by Lousteau, revealed what Maxime wished to know.

Around one o’clock Maxime loitered on Tortoni’s portico with du Tillet, ostensibly waiting for the Comte de la Palferine. When the prince of Bohemia arrived, Maxime drew him aside and asked if La Palferine had debts—fifty or a hundred thousand? The count admitted only six thousand; Maxime shrugged that small sums can hamper as much as great ones, praised the young man’s spirit, and offered help: twenty thousand francs and a scheme to make a woman love him within ten days. La Palferine, flattered but feeling inferior, accepted. Maxime instructed him to meet that evening at Madame Schontz’s and to report nightly at one a.m. on the boulevard de la Madeleine.

Maxime intended to exploit Madame d’Espard’s dislike of Beatrix; he called at the Hotel de Grandlieu and found the duchess, Madame du Guenic, and Clotilde upset: Calyste had not returned, and Beatrix was distraught. Maxime urged secrecy and proposed a plot requiring twenty thousand francs and perhaps offices—hinting at a receiver-generalship as inducement.

Madame Schontz had invited a circle of talented or ambitious men—Claude Vignon, Couture, Fabien du Ronceret (the Heir), Bixiou, Leon de Lora, La Palferine, Nathan—nine guests whose aim was to be noticed. Madame Schontz, anxious to seem young and beautiful, arranged her dress and manner to charm. Maxime arrived, praised Rochefide’s good fortune through her, and bantered about art versus lourettes; Aurelie insisted she saw herself as an artist and a progenitrix of honest men.

Conversation ranged over literary opinions and social gossip. Privately, Maxime handed La Palferine the twenty thousand-franc notes, told him not to be stingy, and directed Nathan to present him to Madame la Marquise de Rochefide two nights hence. Maxime urged a display of wit and a slightly dissolute air; La Palferine pledged to report nightly.

Fabien du Ronceret, the Heir, attended in affectation; Maxime scorned him but learned of his ties with d’Esgrignon. The dinner was lavish. After the meal Maxime confided to Couture a scheme: if Couture wanted steadiness, Madame de Rochefide could secure a receiver-generalship for him—Aurelie’s fortune would make it possible—but Maxime wanted Couture’s vote in return. Couture promised allegiance. Maxime then drove La Palferine home.

That night Madame Schontz and Fabien flirted; when Arthur Rochefide accidentally surprised them, Aurelie feigned wounded virtue and threatened marriage to Fabien, humiliating Arthur. Maxime advised Aurelie how to force the issue—rouse jealousy, make Arthur feel unloved, demand a dramatic parting—promising Fabien a chief-justiceship and the Legion of Honor soon. Arthur woke to Aurelie’s icy sarcasm; she accused him of schemes to reconcile with Madame de Rochefide. He protested. She threatened to leave him and refused his offers, preferring to cultivate Fabien.

Rochefide, in despair, sought Maxime’s counsel at the club. Maxime suggested Arthur show less obvious devotion and perhaps turn toward another woman, Madame Antonia, to recover self-respect and win his wife back. Though Arthur wished to remain faithful, Maxime urged practicality. Rochefide hurried home but found Aurelie had contrived a compromising scene: Fabien’s hat had been left, and the maid’s clumsy removal suggested intimacy. Arthur, devastated, submitted.

Within a week La Palferine transformed: he paid debts, adopted a carriage, joined clubs, and published a successful novelette. Nathan, who promoted him to Madame de Rochefide, told Beatrix that La Palferine adored her. Ten days after Maxime’s arrangement, the count made his debut at Beatrix’s house while Calyste was at a party. Nathan primed her curiosity with artful reticence and lauded La Palferine’s prowess, implying his skill as a lover, which heightened Beatrix’s interest. The contrary attraction Maxime exploited—where courtesans sometimes yearn for virtue and virtuous women secretly desire libertinism—worked on Beatrix; she began to doubt whether her upbringing had made her a dupe.

The next day Calyste seemed to Beatrix a loyal but unimaginative gentleman; after the dazzling soirée his plainness failed to satisfy her appetite for brilliance. She feared a meeting between Calyste and La Palferine; Maxime’s maneuver soon forced a public humiliation. At the opera, where Beatrix sat in an obscure box, she exited and was recognized on the crowded staircase; whispers and stares left her isolated. La Palferine, graciously offering his arm, escorted her, sat with her in her carriage, and rode home beside her, to Calyste’s stupefaction. In the salon the count conversed brilliantly, ignoring Calyste, who seethed. La Palferine left on a witty note, ensuring the lovers’ quarrel.

Maxime, watching from the Jockey Club, checked on Aurelie’s progress. Madame Schontz, pleased with her strategy, had managed to eject Arthur: she led Fabien to believe she would reform and marry him, and Fabien, intoxicated and ambitious, embraced the project. Maxime promised Fabien the offices he’d been promised. Two days later Charles-Edouard told Maxime, “It is done.” Aurelie had turned Arthur out; Maxime consoled the marquis and suggested reconciliation could be bought with three hundred thousand francs and a new match arranged.

Meanwhile the duchess learned Calyste planned to leave for Italy; Sabine wanted to follow and even bought pistols. Maxime counselled d’Ajuda to remain silent, promising that Madame de Rochefide would not leave Paris but would soon separate from Calyste. He arranged for his own marriage to be accepted publicly and urged that the duchess travel to Italy soon.

Beatrix, now alternately consumed by passion and disdain, demanded that Calyste abandon Sabine to prove his devotion. Entrenched, she thought him unlikely to break with the Grandlieus because Sabine’s fortune mattered. Yet Calyste, driven by despair, obtained a passport and funds from his mother; nine days after La Palferine’s opening success he resolved to force a reconciliation. He arrived at Beatrix’s house intending to oust La Palferine and depart with her. Antoine, the old footman, sought to shield her by telling La Palferine she was out, but when La Palferine appeared Calyste watched him depart in a carriage; Beatrix, desperate, sent La Palferine a long letter of devoted confession—an unprecedented signed avowal.

When La Palferine called next, Beatrix, ensnared and overjoyed, embraced him. He set a condition: she must be publicly reconciled with Rochefide, restore her social honor, return to the Hôtel de Rochefide and play the grand lady in public; she must achieve this by political means and persistence. He had already arranged for the reconciliation; she had instructed him to insist on it. Calyste, repeatedly refused by Antoine and by La Palferine’s household, left letters and begged to be heard. Eventually La Palferine and Maxime confronted him and, with cold civility, showed him the six unopened letters he had sent: La Palferine had no wish to cause scandal. He disclosed that Beatrix had taken him up in her carriage after the opera and had insisted on bringing back Rochefide. Rochefide had been living with Madame Schontz, who had left Paris, and had returned with Aurelie to Nogent-sur-Marne while the Hôtel de Rochefide would be restored for the marquise’s triumphant return. La Palferine advised Calyste to resume life with Sabine after three months and not to regret Beatrix: she was vain, incomplete both for vice and virtue, and would have abandoned him without remorse.

Calyste left stunned but relieved to have his illusions pierced. Three days later the Duchesse de Grandlieu found Calyste content at home with Sabine planning the layette; Sabine whispered to the duchess, “I am loved, mother, and forever!”
