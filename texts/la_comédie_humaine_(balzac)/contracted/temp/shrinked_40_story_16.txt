In spring 1822 Parisian doctors sent M. le Baron Gaston de Nueil to Lower Normandy to recover from an inflammatory illness, probably from overwork. Seeking rest, light diet, and fresh air, he settled in Bayeux with welcoming relatives.

After a few evenings with his cousin, Mme. de Sainte‑Severe, Gaston met the local elite, who embodied provincial French types. One was an ancient but impoverished noble family: a patriarch, a resolute sportsman contemptuous of modernity and the new political order, and a loud, domineering wife who believed their daughters’ fortunes depended on the family name. They clung to obsolete customs—nobility without wealth or influence.

Opposite them stood a wealthier, more recent family attuned to Parisian trends: the wife a society woman a step behind the latest fashions, the husband a shrewd Departmental Council member who managed finances and trafficked in political gossip. Their Royalism was lukewarm; they embraced modern life while keeping ancestral ties.

The local bishop, a former Vicar‑General, mediated between factions—respected for his office but often reminded of plebeian origins. Retired officers preserved a veneer of aristocracy, women dressed for court yet lived by routine; a few wealthy townspeople were viewed skeptically. Ecclesiastics added wit and a bourgeois element. Overall conversation replayed the same themes, judgments were fixed, intellectual life stagnated, and critics were dismissed.

His cousin had mentioned that at twenty‑three Gaston had eighteen thousand livres from Auge valley land and that his father was bargaining for more, omitting that his elder brother was consumptive. Initially amused as he sketched their quirks, weeks of monotony left him weary and trapped; ennui and growing discontent threatened to fossilize him among the fossils.

One evening Gaston de Nueil sat between a dowager and a vicar-general in a gray-paneled drawing-room where portraits looked down on four card tables and sixteen people playing whist. After dinner he noted provincial habits—the old cards, threadbare cloths—and their philosophic indifference to luxury, which made Paris feel remote.

A casual remark about Mme. de Beauseant struck him: the Vicomtesse, unwell, had declined an invitation; gossip revealed her liaison with M. d’Ajuda-Pinto and her retreat to Courcelles. Though noble, she was shunned after separating from her husband and became for him a bright point amid provincial stagnation.

Obsessed, he wandered near Courcelles, imagining a chance meeting, then asked M. de Champignelles to arrange an introduction. The Marquis’s message met resistance but piqued her curiosity; after reflection she agreed to receive him. Excited and anxious, Gaston prepared and, when announced at Courcelles, entered ready to meet the enigmatic Vicomtesse.

A bright fire burned on the hearth despite mild weather, casting a soft light over a young woman in a high‑backed bergère. The Vicomtesse, graceful as she stirred the coals and picked up a fallen glove, motioned to a chair for M. de Nueil. Her poised, fastidious ease contrasted with the automata he had seen during two months’ exile in Normandy; in her presence he felt transported back to Paris.

Mme. de Beauseant’s fair hair, dark eyes, and pale complexion gave her a regal, almost fallen‑angel air; her hair braided in a crown intensified this bearing. Beneath that beauty lay sadness—traces of past unhappiness and lost love. Alone in her silent salon she seemed cut off, haunted by memories of a once‑vibrant youth now emptied. M. de Nueil was humbled and captivated by the blend of nobility and misfortune.

When she acknowledged him he felt the weight of his deception. He had come to deliver a message from M. de Champignelles, but awareness of his duplicity made him flush. Summoning courage he confessed, “Madame, I do not deserve the happiness of seeing you. I have deceived you basely.” Instead of mercy she rang for a servant to escort him out; her manner turned icy.

As he left he realized that going now would mean never returning and that she would regard him a fool. He hesitated and, on impulse, turned back, saying he had forgotten something; the servant followed unaware of the true reason. “Jacques lighted me to the door,” he said with a half‑sad smile, which softened Mme. de Beauseant. She invited him to sit; he did so eagerly. He then pleaded, admitting his blunder—blaming youthful enthusiasm and exile’s weariness, saying he had idealized her and, in delirious longing, committed a foolish intrusion. She rebuked him as ridiculous, insisted she would rather be scorned than pitied, warned of impropriety, and commanded him not to return. He begged indulgence for his fault.

An impassioned voice thawed Mme. de Beauseant’s chill solitude: she was moved by M. de Nueil’s sincerity and captivated by his noble youth. Gaston, in the bloom of life, seemed free of ambition and vanity. Both concealed and reflected on their feelings: he saw in the Vicomtesse not only beauty but depth and goodness; she discerned in him purity resisting society’s corruptions. His approach was instinctive, hers deliberate—she tested him with opposing opinions and put him at ease with wit and grace, reviving a sense of innocence in herself and offering, in effect, absolution from past shame.

When separation loomed, Mme. de Beauseant, smiling gently, insisted on preserving her solitary life, fearing any tie would tarnish her reputation. She spoke candidly of a forsaken love and the lessons solitude had taught her. Gaston longed to declare himself but feared rejection; torn between admiration and dread of appearing foolish, he finally confessed his love and vowed to heal her old wounds. She, moved yet resolute, insisted they remain strangers. She described the pain of being unable to follow a beloved man through life’s changes and reminded him she could not yield to blind impulse.

Gaston left unsettled, unsure whether her charm was genuine or coquetry. Diffident at twenty-three, he wrote faltering letters, then returned at night to Courcelles and sent a heartfelt plea. Mme. de Beauseant’s reply, sealed with the arms of Burgundy, was plain: she refused his advances—not to wound him but to be candid. Though she cared for him, she did not share his love and would not be moved by youthful passion: she was nearly thirty, he just over twenty, and feelings might change with age. Vows made lightly could become burdens; she feared he might one day resent the sacrifices she required, and she could not bear desertion. Her consolation was in God rather than earthly ties; she could offer only the friendship of an older woman. For both their sakes she urged him to remain in his country and not wreck his life for an illusion.

Gaston answered with fervor: if he could cease to love her and accept an ordinary life, he would deserve his fate; his oath of fidelity could be absolved only by death, and if she feared his remorse she might take his life. When the messenger returned, he reported he had delivered Mme. la Vicomtesse’s reply as she was leaving in a carriage. Realizing she was departing, Gaston followed her to Geneva, convinced that if she truly loved him she would seek the freedom of Switzerland.

Mme. de Beauseant had rented a small house by the lake. One summer evening Gaston surprised her; she dropped her book and embraced him as he explained he had followed her. For three years they lived in the villa on Lake Geneva—alone and apparently in a dreamlike happiness: a simple home, beautiful views, quiet days cherished together. Weighty obligations soon called Gaston back to France after the deaths of his father and brother. They refrained from selling the villa; instead they bought the family house, and Mme. de Beauseant sold her property to purchase an estate near Manerville adjoining Gaston’s lands. His mother took the family house at Manerville, and the lovers resumed the secluded happiness they had known.

For nine years they enjoyed almost indescribable bliss, but a legal obstacle remained: Mme. de Beauseant’s husband, the Marquis, was still alive, so any lawful union was impossible. The situation was precarious: Gaston’s mother, the dowager comtesse, would never accept Mme. de Beauseant; the Marquise feared the dowager’s influence might one day pull Gaston away. Though tempted to sell her estate and return to Geneva, Mme. de Beauseant could not act in a way that might betray distrust.

Their fragile peace was disturbed by the arrival nearby of a young heiress, Mlle. de la Rodiere, and by Gaston’s meeting with her. Mme. de Beauseant replied with a letter that laid bare her turmoil and pride. She confessed she had not known love until she met him and that, for nine years, she had felt no jealousy and had experienced infinite happiness. But now she anguished at the thought that, at forty, she might be a burden to his future: he was thirty and might desire a marriage that would bring children and security. Too proud to bind his future to her own fading life, she urged him to be frank: if he still loved her, he should come after reading the letter and she would love him more than ever; if he sought freedom, she would not display her suffering, because she loved him enough to put his happiness first.

When Mme. de Beauseant learned her letter had reached Gaston she was overwhelmed and stunned into numbness. Reading her words, M. de Nueil found himself in a painful dilemma—torn among passion, duty, pride, and the prospect of a conventional future.

Gaston had almost yielded to his mother’s urgings and to the attraction of Mlle. de la Rodiere, a plain young woman with a forty-thousand-livre dowry. Mme. de Nueil extolled the respectability of such a match, noted that future rents might raise his income to eighty thousand livres, and argued that if Mme. de Beauseant truly loved him she would encourage his marriage. Her pressure left him torn.

Then a letter from Mme. de Beauseant changed everything: Gaston resolved to end their liaison and marry. He told himself “one must live a man’s life,” conscious that his choice would wound her. Vanity and conscience deepened his sorrow; he felt real pity. He intended to prepare her gently, at first sacrificing Mlle. de la Rodiere to ease the transition and marrying later to respect Mme. de Beauseant’s wishes. He meant to send a calming letter, though he feared a woman of such perception might be devastated by a mere note.

When Jacques delivered the letter Mme. de Beauseant trembled, dread of loss closing in. If he did not come in person, she feared all was lost; yet a woman in love can endure much. Reading it, an inner cry—“He lies!”—rose within her: the message seemed calculated, not sincere. In despair she tore it and cast it into the fire, feeling betrayed and abandoned.

M. de Nueil fetched Jacques, who returned with a brief note: “Madame la Marquise has left the chateau.” Inside it M. de Nueil found his own letter from nine years earlier, in which he had sworn eternal fidelity; at the bottom Claire de Bourgogne had written, “Monsieur, you are free.” Stunned, he went to his mother and, within three weeks, married Mlle. Stéphanie de la Rodiere.

If the tale ended there it might seem trivial, but its notoriety strikes those who have known love’s deep joys and losses. After their parting Mme. de Beauseant remained at Valleroy in seclusion, rarely seen except by servants, attending mass daily.

Soon after the wedding M. de Nueil fell into emotional indifference—read as either contentment or misery. His wife was sweet but shallow; a month after the wedding she expected a child. Two months on, he grew increasingly pensive. Seven months later a small incident revealed deeper turmoil. Returning from a hunt he crossed Mme. de Beauseant’s park and had Jacques set aside whatever game he shot for her; for days he came home with an empty bag while Jacques secretly delivered the birds to the Marquise.

He dared to write her, but the letter was returned unopened. One evening, while his wife played the piano, he rushed to the Marquise’s room and found her in despair; she threatened to throw herself from the window if he approached. Terrified, he fled. Back home he wrote again, sent his servant, and an hour later the note was returned unopened. In despair M. de Nueil retired and shot himself.

The tragic end, though at odds with fashionable conventions, seems inevitable to those who have experienced a profound soul-connection. Love needs time and nurturing; abandoning a deep passion for a loveless, conventional marriage brings either despair or a cold, material existence. Mme. de Beauseant likely did not foresee his extreme act; she may have thought herself the greater sufferer, yet she rightly refused humiliation. A wife may compromise for social reasons, but a mistress cannot submit to degradation if the purity of her passion demands respect.

ANGOULEME, September 1832.
