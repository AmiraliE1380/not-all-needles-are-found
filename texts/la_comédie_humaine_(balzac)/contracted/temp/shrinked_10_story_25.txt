Early in the autumn of 1826 the Abbé Birotteau, the tale’s central figure, was caught in a downpour returning from an evening at a friend’s. He hurried, as his corpulence and gout allowed, across the deserted Cloister behind Saint-Gatien at Tours. Small, apoplectic and about sixty, he dreaded wet shoes because damp brought gout. Yet he bore the rain resignedly: he had won three francs ten sous at rubber with Madame de Listomère, wrapped himself in a canon’s fur cape, and was elated by talk that he might be named canon — a post he had long coveted.

On the Grand’Rue side of the Cloister a cluster of cathedral houses formed a Close that had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. Opposite, a single house, its walls overshadowed by Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, had long been occupied by abbés and belonged to an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Time had dimmed the cathedral and thrown the house into shadow; the dwelling, suitable only for those of absolute nullity or uncommon fortitude, had been bought by her father during the Terror and accepted without objection by the restored Church. Devout and careful, Mademoiselle Gamard boarded priests and kept the house with punctilious economy.

Birotteau had lived two years in that house and longed for its rooms as much as for a canonry. His two great desires — being Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and becoming a canon — reflected the ambitions of a provincial priest who sought decent lodging, good food, clean garments and the dignity of a title. The apartment he inhabited had been the object of a passion that began when he first admired the rooms of his friend, the Abbé Chapeloud, and which grew into an obsession.

For years Chapeloud, who became canon when worship was restored under Napoleon, had occupied the south-facing apartment, furnishing and renovating it with modest legacies and gifts: an oak bookcase from a demolished château, waxed floors, painted woodwork, a long ebony table, Boulle cabinets, folios and Fathers of the Church. The shabby rooms were transformed into a dignified oratory and a salon hung with red damask and mahogany. Birotteau, poor and the son of peasants like Chapeloud, admired these changes and secretly yearned to possess them. His desire increased until, when Chapeloud fell ill, his thoughts ran, “If Chapeloud dies I can have this apartment.” Yet he never contemplated any means to influence his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, indulgent and aware of his friend’s weakness, forgave his covetousness and, on his deathbed as Birotteau read the Quotidienne, said, “This time you will certainly get the apartment.” He bequeathed his library and furniture to Birotteau, who, though saddened, found consolation in the inheritance and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s cheerful agreement to take him as lodger. He moved into the apartment and enjoyed comforts he had long imagined: meticulous linen, fires, well-chosen wood, fragrant orris-root, and the small attentions for which Chapeloud had praised the spinster. Mademoiselle Gamard’s household had been celebrated, Chapeloud had often declared, for its perfect order and attentive service.

Yet having obtained his long-desired comforts, Birotteau soon replaced the old obsession with a new one: the canonry. Encouraged by Madame de Listomère’s conversations, he believed promotion was near. Absorbed in these hopes, he did not at once notice, on returning one evening, that he had left his umbrella and that some domestic attentions were missing. He found the outer door locked, Marianne the servant answering awkwardly that Mademoiselle had thought he was in; his candlestick had been moved to an ante-chamber; the fire in his room had not been burning; his slippers were not placed where they usually were. These trivial changes struck him as deliberate signs of displeasure. By a sort of clerical logic he concluded that Mademoiselle Gamard intended to chill and humiliate him. The small humiliations mortified him deeply.

Birotteau, kindly but simple-minded and ignorant of worldly calculations, lacked frank self-knowledge about what he had done to provoke dislike. He was a good man whose goodness verged on stupidity; his learning was shallow, and his life centered on mass and the confessional. He could not perceive how unconscious selfishness and provincial narrowness, common to many, had made him difficult. He was, in short, a clumsy egoist, unlike Chapeloud, who was a shrewd one.

Chapeloud had understood Mademoiselle Gamard: an old maid of about thirty-eight whose pretensions amounted to strong self-esteem. The canon judged she needed constant, precise attentions if he would live comfortably under her roof. So he kept limited contact with her, avoided breakfast and supper, and came to dinner a few minutes early to pay polite compliments about the food and service. By these attentions, and by never creating points of contact beyond politeness, Chapeloud secured a peaceful coexistence and won praise for being easy to live with.

Troubert, the other lodger, was her creature: obedient, patient and entirely under her rule, often suspected of coveting her property because of their intimate domestic life. When Chapeloud died the landlady at first considered offering the rooms to Troubert, but seeing Birotteau’s attachment to the apartment she accepted him as lodger for her own advantage and even improved the rooms: replacing brick with wooden floors and rebuilding the chimney.

But Birotteau, dazzled by his new condition, did not adopt Chapeloud’s circumspection. He fell into Mademoiselle Gamard’s life with the embarrassment and timidity of a man who fears seeming rude and stays too long in conversation. He began to spend his evenings there, and the spinster, whose real desire had long been to make her salon a weekly center of company, welcomed the chance to display more prominent visitors. Taking pride in entertaining, she boasted that thanks to Birotteau she could gather respectable company.

The vicar soon deserted her salon for Madame de Listomère’s and Mademoiselle Salomon’s, preferring aristocratic circles to which the landlady did not belong. His withdrawal gravely wounded Mademoiselle Gamard; she had hoped to use him to attract society. Her disappointment, joined to an old-maid’s craving for return on social investment and wounded pride, turned into resentment. The vicar, however, never understood this. He perceived, belatedly and clumsily, signs of slights—small omissions and unkindnesses—and concluded that ill will was directed at him though he could not see the reasons.

Mademoiselle Gamard, like many who never adapted their habits to changing lives, indulged a petty despotism over small things—the basket of counters, the arrangement of cards, the order of household routine. Such trifles she magnified into tests of obedience. The vicar, unlike Chapeloud, could not accept being persistently cinched by such crochets without protest; he withdrew rather than submitting, irritating her more by his absence than by any direct challenge.

Her friends, defending her, called Chapeloud amiable and blamed Birotteau for turning away from the old maid; society at large often sided, covertly, with those who escaped a nightly round in the Cloister. Mademoiselle Gamard enjoyed the condolence she received, and Troubert explained the departure by saying the abbe sought fashion, luxury and witty talk—thus flattering the spinster by implying he had higher tastes.

In truth, the vicar’s abandonment was a clumsy desertion, and his naïveté and lack of social tact made him gradually intolerable to Mademoiselle Gamard. Eighteen months after she had taken him in, when he mistook her silence for contentment, she had in fact begun a deliberate, underhand persecution. The locked door, the forgotten slippers, the unlit fire and the moved candlestick were the first signs. Birotteau, too innocent to see his own faults toward his landlady, could not understand why she had turned against him.

This prologue, recounting the small domestic developments that precede the greater drama, needs particulars. The little meannesses, jealousies and petty ambitions of bourgeois life inflict injuries as keen as those produced by grander interests, and their slow, minute growths require this account to explain the coming catastrophe.

The next morning Birotteau woke thinking so much of his prospective canonry that he forgot the four ominous signs of misfortune he had seen the night before. He did not get up without a fire; he rang for Marianne, then lay half-asleep in his usual musings. When Marianne did not appear after twenty minutes, he was about to ring again when he heard footsteps on the stairs. After a discreet knock, the Abbe Troubert, who visited him about once a month, came in. Birotteau at once called for Marianne sharply when he saw there was no fire, opened the window, and told her to come at once; then he turned to Troubert and, quite naively, related the steps he had taken and even the names of those Madame de Listomere was using in his favor—unaware that Troubert bore a grudge against that lady for having snubbed him, the same man twice proposed by the bishop as vicar-general.

Troubert and Birotteau presented many contrasts. Troubert, tall, lean, yellow-faced and bilious, had long, deeply wrinkled features that sometimes expressed sarcasm or contempt; he seldom spoke or laughed, often seemed grave and was suspected of ambition. Birotteau was plump, ruddy, kindly, talkative and simple—qualities that marked him permanently as vicar and nothing higher. Troubert’s imposing presence and cautious conduct, combined with a saintly life and failing health, had removed fears about his alleged ability; his chronic malady made it likely he would soon become vicar-general of the archbishopric. Birotteau’s robust appearance, his triple chin and even his gout, seemed to others guarantees of long life and offered no hope of elevation.

The Abbe Chapeloud, a sensible, amiable man popular in Tours, had quietly opposed Troubert’s rise by keeping him out of the best salons, though Troubert treated Chapeloud with great deference while Chapeloud remained unconvinced: “Distrust that lean stick of a Troubert,—Sixtus the Fifth reduced to the limits of a bishopric!” he had said to Birotteau.

Troubert had come that morning, the day after Mademoiselle Gamard—Birotteau’s landlady and the old maid who had seemingly declared war on the vicar—had behaved harshly toward him, simply to ask to borrow Birotteau’s Register of Bishops. Birotteau, pleased, sent him to the library while he dressed. Presently the breakfast bell rang; Birotteau, reflecting that without Troubert he would have had no fire, thought, “He’s a kind man.”

The two priests went downstairs carrying huge folios to lay on a dining-room table. Mademoiselle Gamard greeted them sharply. “I hope you are not going to litter up my dining-room with your old books!” she snapped at Birotteau. Troubert explained he had borrowed them from the vicar. Mademoiselle Gamard remarked contemptuously that Birotteau did not often read such large books.

Birotteau answered her with his usual mellifluous politeness. She replied that he had woken her the night before out of her first sleep and had kept her awake. Sitting down, she added, “Gentlemen, the milk is getting cold.” Stupefied at the ill-natured reception and alarmed at the prospect of a dispute, the timid vicar fell silent. He struggled inwardly between feeling he should not submit to discourtesy and his instinct to avoid quarrel.

To distract himself he examined the green lines painted on the oilcloth she habitually left on the breakfast table. They sat opposite each other; she dominated the head of the table from a high, cushioned chair near the stove. When Mademoiselle Gamard handed him his coffee, the vicar felt chilled by the silence and dared not meet Troubert’s dried features or the landlady’s threatening face. He turned, to keep up appearances, to the fat pug on a cushion by the stove, and addressed it, as he often did, to keep up conversation on the theory—his own—that speech favored digestion. The dog, once important in the household, now merely closed his eyes peevishly.

Mademoiselle Gamard believed in health maxims and in talking at meals; Birotteau had for some mornings strained to find topics to loosen her tongue. Their conversations, if recorded, would have illustrated provincial ignorance and credulous gossip—absurdities about Napoleon’s death, the Dauphin, taxes, and other political and medical superstitions. But that morning Birotteau felt tongue-tied; after a long pause he risked, “This coffee is excellent,” and later, “It will be finer to-day than yesterday.” At the second remark she cast her most gracious look toward Troubert, then turned on Birotteau with terrible severity.

Mademoiselle Sophie Gamard was the quintessential old maid. To understand her, the narrative pauses to outline the nature of such women. Society condemns the unproductive individual who consumes without producing; old maids fall under a similar stigma because they decline or fail to fulfill what society deems their role. Their celibacy—whether from ugliness, lack of charm, deliberate independence, or other causes—isolates them and corrupts their social standing. Deprived of the activity through which womanly qualities develop and are exercised, they are starved of purpose. Bitterness, jealousy and a wounded pride follow; they blame others for their condition, then themselves, and often turn to revenge. Their social failure shows in an oblique, ashamed glance, a hardness of manner, and a certain selfishness: used to being useless, they prefer what is convenient for themselves to what would please others. Habits of self-centeredness produce peevishness and lack of refinement; deprived of love’s practice, their faculties wither.

All these truths could be read in Mademoiselle Gamard’s dull gray eyes, the dark circles under them, the straight lines of her wrinkles. Her scattered hairs on the chin, thin lips barely covering long teeth, dark complexion, and the gaps where she poorly adjusted a false front revealed a carelessness born of inward conflict. Her thin, tight brown gowns, limp collar, and a neck thick and ribbed with age matched the hard, angular movements of a woman who had risen from rural origins—the daughter of a wood-merchant—and who carried in her face and gait the despotism of character and narrowness of mind. Her abrupt gestures, loud nasal blows on her handkerchief, and erect, blocklike stride betrayed a lack of grace. When in good humor she would recount missed chances of marriage and men’s lack of means, proof, to her, that worldly prudence had prevailed over the heart.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s dining-room showed her taste: varnished paper with Turkish landscapes, two pier tables, a barometer, little faded worsted cushions before each abbé’s chair. Her salon, “the yellow salon,” was yellow in curtains, furniture and walls; the mantelpiece with a gilt mirror, crystal candlesticks and clock seemed sharp and precise. Her private apartment was never shown; conjecture thought it full of odds, worn-out furniture, and things dear to an old maid.

Unable to expend womanly activity naturally, she had converted it into petty intrigues, provincial cabals and self-seeking schemes—the occupation of many old maids. Birotteau had developed in her the only strong feeling she could muster: hatred. This hatred, previously latent beneath provincial calm, became intense because expended on petty things within a narrow sphere. Birotteau’s timidity and incapacity to see the world’s malice made him especially helpless before her. He was the sort of man doomed to suffer because he could not avoid the harms that beset him.

After breakfast, Birotteau, unnerved by the silence, left the room with the coffee heavy in his stomach and strolled among the narrow, box-edged paths of the little garden, laid out in a star. Turning after a first round, he saw Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert standing motionless on the threshold; he felt as if they were watching and counting his steps. Nothing is so distressing to a timid person as being closely examined, more so when those eyes express hatred. Embarrassed, he fancied he was in their way and, moved by fear and kindness, left the garden for Saint-Gatien, where he found much to occupy him—funerals, a marriage, baptisms—and so forgot his pain. When his watch told him dinner should be ready he hurried back, mindful of Mademoiselle Gamard’s punctuality.

Passing the kitchen, he saw the first course had been removed. In the dining-room she said sourly and with evident pleasure at finding ground for reproach, “It is half-past four, Monsieur Birotteau. You know we are not to wait for you.” The vicar looked at the clock and noticed by the moved gauze that the hands had been set ahead of the cathedral clock. He said nothing; any remark would have led to one of her fierce expositions, in which women of her class could be eloquently censorious. Mademoiselle Gamard instinctively divined a thousand petty annoyances that might provoke or discomfort a timid man like Birotteau, and she delighted in plotting against his domestic comfort—always in a way that left her apparently never in the wrong. Her methods showed a profoundly malignant ingenuity: she could contrive to torment him while keeping the appearance of justice and propriety.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Eight days after the events that opened this history, changes in the household and in the relations between the Abbé Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard made the former perceive a plot that had been growing for six months.

At first the old maid’s vengeance had been covert and the vicar could ignore it; the harm was slight. But after the candlestick and the altered clock, Birotteau no longer doubted that hatred watched him. From then on he fell into despair, seeing Mademoiselle Gamard’s thin, clawlike fingers everywhere. She, delighting in revenge, hovered above him like a bird of prey preparing to pounce. She had long devised a plan which the simple priest could not imagine, and now unfolded it with the petty skill often shown by solitary people who, barred from large feelings, expend themselves on the details of outward devotion.

These small torments kept Birotteau, effusive and craving pity, from confiding in friends—another sting. His timidity made him dread seeming ridiculous over such pettiness. Yet these trifles composed his life—busy about nothing, finding happiness in the absence of passion. His paradise altered to hell; the fear of confronting Mademoiselle Gamard grew, and the secret distress harmed his health. One morning, while putting on mottled blue stockings, he noticed his calves had shrunk. Terrified, he resolved to ask Abbé Troubert to intervene officially between him and the landlady.

In Troubert’s bare, cheerless room the vicar, ashamed to bother so grave a man, after many hesitations, explained his case. The canon listened coldly, occasionally suppressing a smile. He blamed Birotteau’s failure to see the trouble on his preoccupation with elevated thoughts, remarked that former recluses thought little of food and lodging, and suggested modern priests can retire into the solitude of their hearts. He offered to act as arbitrator because his friendship with Mademoiselle Gamard had never exceeded Church limits, but justice required hearing both sides. Troubert professed to see no change in Gamard, attributing slight temper fluctuations to pulmonary suffering and recommending patience; if Birotteau stayed longer he would come to esteem her better.

Birotteau left confounded. Unable to consult anyone, he judged Mademoiselle Gamard alone and fancied that by leaving her house for a few days he might extinguish her dislike. He resolved to spend a week at Madame de Listomère’s country house, a practice he had often followed. Ironically, this was precisely what Gamard wished; only patient endurance could have foiled her plan. The vicar, incapable of divining this, was doomed to fall.

Madame de Listomère’s country-place, the Alouette, near Tours, combined town and country charms. About ten days after his arrival there, while breakfasting, Birotteau was told Monsieur Caron wished to speak with him. Caron was Mademoiselle Gamard’s lawyer. Agitated and surprised, Birotteau learned Caron had been sent to ask how long he intended to remain in the country. The original agreement had not foreseen a long absence and might lead to contest. Gamard, anxious to avoid dispute, had sent Caron to learn his plans. The abbé asked for time to take advice.

Back at table, Birotteau described his trouble. The company, bored with country-house monotony, eagerly sided against the old maid. Madame de Listomère surmised Troubert wanted Birotteau’s apartment; Monsieur de Listomère urged resistance. An old landowner, Monsieur de Bourbonne, suggested something more serious: that Gamard intended to turn Birotteau out, and those like her risked nothing in such games. De Bourbonne, thin, shrewd, practised in weighing words and measuring actions, had a reputation for cunning and decency that made him feared in local affairs.

Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived from Tours with news that changed everything. She reported the vicar-general was very ill and the archbishop had provisionally delegated his powers to Abbé Troubert, so the canonry would depend on him. At a recent gathering the Abbé Poirel had spoken of Birotteau’s alleged annoyances to Mademoiselle Gamard in a way that cast blame on the vicar. Madame de Listomère asked Birotteau whether he preferred to be made canon or to continue living with Gamard; in chorus they said canon. She suggested that by leaving Gamard’s house he might secure the canonry—“one good turn deserves another.” All applauded, except her nephew, the Baron, who joked about wishing to see a fight.

Despite their support, forces were unequal: the city’s best society had less power than Gamard backed by Troubert. By Madame de Listomère’s advice, a servant fetched Caron back. De Bourbonne counselled caution and delay, but passion prevailed. In a short conference Caron urged Birotteau to sign a paper declaring his relinquishment of domicile. Confused but overwhelmed, Birotteau read and mechanically signed it: he declared he left Gamard’s house of his own wish, that he had been properly fed and lodged. Caron asked where to send the abbé’s belongings; Birotteau said Madame de Listomère. De Bourbonne, suspicious, requested to see the paper and noted there must be prior documents between Birotteau and Gamard. The fatal deed, the vicar said, was in his library.

The helplessness of the poor man, innocent of worldly craft, moved Madame de Listomère and Mademoiselle Salomon, who soothed him like mothers consoling a child. They promised lodging and counsel, urged play to distract him, and spoke of asking Troubert for help. Birotteau, dazzled by the prospect of living with Madame de Listomère, temporarily forgot his ruined happiness. But that night the reality returned: the thought of moving, of his books and furniture scattered, of the end of long habits, overwhelmed him. He could not reconcile one sweet first year in Gamard’s house with the cruel second; the canonry seemed poor compensation. He clung to Mademoiselle Salomon’s friendship but dared not fully trust new attachments.

A digression notes that among spinsters some are heroic—those who sacrifice themselves nobly. Mademoiselle Salomon belonged to this class: once beloved and young, she had tended a lover who went mad, refusing to call him insane; she had devoted herself for five years to his mechanical well-being, and after his death settled in Tours to do good. Simple, frank, and of regular features, she attached herself to the feeble, which made her warmly interested in Birotteau.

Mademoiselle de Villenoix returned Birotteau to Tours the next morning and left him at the quay of the cathedral to go to the Cloister, bent on preserving the canonry and overseeing removal of his furniture. At the house he found his old apartment occupied: Marianne told him Troubert was in his old rooms. The sight of Troubert, comfortably seated in Chapeloud’s library and armchair, confirmed the depth of the conspiracy. Birotteau grasped, with a sinking vision, that Troubert intended to possess Chapeloud’s spoils. Facing Troubert’s sardonic glance, the abbé felt struck dumb.

Troubert coolly told him that Mademoiselle Gamard had informed him of Birotteau’s departure and that he had been installed out of necessity; if the furniture belonged to Birotteau, Gamard’s scrupulous honesty guaranteed its return; if Birotteau wished to return, Troubert would cede the apartment. The words were terrible. Birotteau ran to find Gamard and met her on the staircase. Bowing despite her derisive smile, he protested she should have waited to let him pack his books and furniture. She interrupted, asking if he had not read his deed, and then, with the triumphant clarity of hatred, reminded him of the clause: by agreement, if he left her house his furniture would indemnify her for the difference in board between what he paid and what the late Abbé Chapeloud had paid. With Poirel’s appointment as canon just made, Gamard exulted in the arrangement. At these words Birotteau faintly bowed and fled, fearing a complete breakdown if he stayed.

He found at Madame de Listomère’s house his linen, clothing, and papers packed in a trunk, and sank, hiding his face in his hands. Poirel had been made canon; Birotteau had no home, means, or furniture. Mademoiselle Salomon drove by; the porter noticed the priest’s despair and persuaded her to take him in her carriage. She, ignorant of the deed’s terms, believed his derangement due to the shock of Poirel’s nomination. Back at the Alouette, friends cared for him until evening, when he recounted the morning’s events.

De Bourbonne asked to see the deed. Birotteau produced it. De Bourbonne read the clause providing that, because of an eight-hundred-franc yearly difference between Chapeloud’s board and what Gamard charged, and because Birotteau was unable to pay full price, he engaged in consideration of sums lent by Gamard to leave her, as indemnity, all household property he might possess at death, or to transfer the same should he voluntarily give up the apartment. De Bourbonne cursed the agreement and declared Gamard armed with claws.

Birotteau had never imagined anything would separate him from the house where he hoped to live and die. He had not remembered the clause, which he had not discussed; in his eagerness to enter the house he would have signed any document. Madame de Listomère, in hot indignation, exclaimed she had made him sign and must restore his lost happiness. De Bourbonne advised law: the deed might constitute fraud. The Baron and others urged litigation. De Bourbonne privately advised a different path: that most now friendly would desert him soon, and if Birotteau wished peace he should resign the vicariate and leave Tours for a distant parish where Troubert could not pursue him. This prospect horrified the vicar—leaving Tours for him was like death; celibates replace feelings with habits, and Birotteau needed his routine: his walks, salons, and games.

Word quickly spread that Madame la Baronne de Listomère had invited Birotteau to stay. The baroness’s reception of him implicitly denied Gamard’s assertions and censured her conduct, so Gamard was deeply wounded. To understand how Gamard had resources, one must see the network that served Troubert. Accompanied by Troubert, Gamard made evening rounds to several houses where circles of old maids, a few men swayed by servants’ gossip, and other busybodies met. These women, ever sifting words and scrutinizing neighbors, kept exact accounts of fortunes and actions, retailing scandal and controlling local opinion. Spread through town like capillary fibres, they absorbed household secrets and transmitted them mechanically to Troubert. They formed a kind of police—dumb but perpetually talking—whose gossip could harm when it touched important interests. The quarrel between Birotteau and Gamard became the absorbing passion of these salons; a class sentiment underlay the conflict—a people-vs.-senate antipathy reduced to a provincial molehill.

Madame de Listomère returned to Tours and took Birotteau to her lawyer. He did not think the case easy. Friends who had believed justice certain had delayed bringing the suit until returning to town, and Gamard’s allies had taken the initiative, spreading the affair to Birotteau’s damage. The lawyer—whose practice lay among the devout—cautioned against litigation. According to the deed, Gamard had the law on her side; in equity, judges and respectable laymen might view Birotteau as having departed from his formerly peaceable, mild character. Gamard had lent him money to pay Chapeloud’s legacy duties without a receipt; Birotteau, not of age or character to sign without understanding, left Gamard’s house after two years while Chapeloud and Troubert had been there twelve and fifteen years respectively; the suit would appear like ingratitude. The lawyer declined to undertake it and besought Madame de Listomère to avoid embroiling herself.

That evening Birotteau could not refrain from telling friends the lawyer’s refusal. De Bourbonne advised caution and mentioned only a radical lawyer might take the case. The Baron, heatedly, insisted: Troubert’s involvement made it a public matter; valuable pictures and furniture had been involved, and Birotteau could not have intended to give away such wealth for two years of board. He proposed getting Monsieur Salmon, an expert from the Museum, to appraise the paintings and furniture.

Two days later the suit began. Choosing the radical lawyer harmed Birotteau’s cause: opponents of government and critics of religion seized on the case and the town buzzed. The Museum expert appraised the Virgin of Valentin and the Christ of Lebrun at eleven thousand francs; the bookshelves and Gothic furniture, increasingly fashionable in Paris, had immediate value of at least twelve thousand. The full appraisal exceeded thirty-six thousand francs. Clearly, Birotteau could not have intended to give Gamard such a sum under the deed; legally, he had equitable grounds to amend the agreement; if refused, Gamard’s conduct might constitute intentional fraud. The radical lawyer served a harsh writ on Gamard, a document of skillful legal argument supported by precedent and code citations; it so clearly condemned the old maid that many copies were maliciously distributed through the town.

################################################
A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, the Baron de Listomere, who expected promotion to corvette captain, received a letter warning he might be put on the retired list. Astonished, he went to Paris and to the minister, who laughed at his fears; but the next day, despite the minister’s assurances, the baron made inquiries. By an indiscretion a secretary showed him a document confirming the news, awaiting only the director’s signature.

The baron hurried to an uncle, a deputy who could see the minister at the Chamber, and begged him to learn the minister’s intentions in a matter that threatened his future. Waiting anxiously in his uncle’s carriage, he was told on the way home: the minister had first accused him of leading Radicals in Tours and of objectionable politics; but the real trouble, his uncle learned, was ecclesiastical. The baron had spoken slightingly of Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general, an influential man representing the Jesuits in the province. His uncle warned him to make peace with Troubert at once, saying such priests were men with whom one must live in harmony; Troubert was likely to be raised to a bishopric and could even harm the uncle’s prospects.

These words explained to the baron Troubert’s secret activities that Birotteau had often wondered about: “I can’t think what he does with himself—sitting up all night.” Troubert’s position, skill, and female network in Tours had made him the Congregation’s proconsul there; archbishop, general, prefect—all were under his covert influence. The Baron de Listomere decided on his course. “I shall take care not to get another round shot below my water-line,” he told his uncle.

Three days later the baron, returning hurriedly, told his aunt the family must abandon support of “that fool of a Birotteau.” He detained Monsieur de Bourbonne as the old man was leaving a rubber of whist; Bourbonne’s clear-sightedness seemed essential to navigate the reefs the Listomeres faced. The baron’s return and disquiet hinted he had met a check in his campaign against Gamard and Troubert. He revealed Troubert’s hidden power.

“I knew that,” said Bourbonne.

“Then why did you not warn us?” the baroness cried.

“Forget that I knew it, and I will forget that you did,” Bourbonne replied. “If we do not keep this secret now we shall be thought his accomplices. Pretend to be duped; but watch your steps. I warned you, but you would not understand me.”

Abandoning Birotteau was taken for granted by the three. “To beat a retreat with the honors of war has always been the triumph of the ablest generals,” said Bourbonne. “Bow to Troubert; if his hatred is less than his vanity you may win him; if you bow too low he will walk over you. Make believe you intend to leave the service, and you’ll escape him. Send away Birotteau, madame, and you will set things right with Mademoiselle Gamard. Ask Troubert, when you meet him at the archbishop’s, if he plays whist; he will say yes. Invite him to your salon; you are a woman and can win a priest. When the baron is promoted and his uncle made a peer, and Troubert a bishop, you can make Birotteau a canon if you choose. Meantime yield—but yield gracefully, with a slight menace. Your family can give him as much as he can give you. As for you, sailor, keep your deep-sea line about you.”

“Poor Birotteau?” said the baroness.

“Get rid of him at once,” Bourbonne replied. “If some clever Radical gets that empty head, he may cause trouble. The court might give a verdict in his favor, and Troubert must fear that. I have said my say.”

The next morning the baroness took Birotteau aside and, not without embarrassment, asked him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and to leave her house. As she spoke he turned pale. She explained, briefly and without disguising the gravity, the wide ramifications of the affair, making him see the net woven by revenge and the capacity and power of Troubert, whose hatred of Chapeloud now vented itself in seizing Chapeloud’s property and persecuting his friend. Birotteau clasped his hands and wept, listening as one at the edge of a precipice. The baroness ended: family duties must be considered before friendship; yield, and she would ensure he lacked nothing, arranging matters through Monsieur de Bourbonne to save appearances. “Grant me the right to abandon you,” she begged.

The bewildered abbe cried, “Chapeloud was right when he said that if Troubert could drag him by the feet out of his grave he would do it!” Yet he yielded. “I trust myself to you—I am but the stubble of the streets,” he said, using a local word for a pathetic bit of straw. He asked only for Chapeloud’s portrait, painted for him, and the baroness promised to see Mademoiselle Gamard herself and to consult the archbishop.

Birotteau left dismayed; Troubert now seemed to him a pyramid whose hands were in Paris and elbows in the cloister. The news of Birotteau’s removal from the Listomere house seemed mysterious: the baroness told people the nephew meant to marry and leave the navy, and wanted the vicar’s apartment. The Listomeres’ apparent neutrality implied recognition of the Congregation’s occult power. But the lawsuit still awaited judgment; their opponents yielded while threatening.

Then Mademoiselle Gamard took cold leaving the cathedral and was soon dangerously ill. The town burst into commiseration: “Mademoiselle Gamard’s sensitive nature could not bear the scandal; in spite of the justice of her cause she was likely to die of grief—Birotteau has killed his benefactress.” Such talk spread through the female conclave and the town.

Madame de Listomere went to pay her promised visit but the old maid was too ill to see her. She asked to speak to the vicar-general. Troubert, gratified to receive in Chapeloud’s library the woman who had hitherto ignored him, kept her waiting before admitting her. No courtier could have put more dissimulation into negotiating personal interests than the baroness and the priest did now.

Bourbonne had coached the baroness: “Don’t forget your cue. You are a mediator, not an interested party. Troubert is a mediator. Weigh your words; if he strokes his chin you have him.” Their conversation was a duel of implied meanings. Madame de Listomere began by expressing regret at Birotteau’s lawsuit and her desire to settle it.

“The harm is done, madame,” Troubert said gravely. “The pious Mademoiselle Gamard is dying.” (He intended to place the guilt on the baroness.)

“On hearing of her illness I entreated Monsieur Birotteau to relinquish his claims; I have brought the document intending to give it to that excellent woman,” she replied. (She meant to avoid compromising herself.) After a tense interchange of civilities and veiled thrusts about religion, politics, and the paintings, she laid the deed of relinquishment on the table and said she had come to reconcile two Christians.

“Your nephew has been to Paris, I believe,” Troubert said. “He returns tonight; the minister does not want him to leave the service.” “He has done us the service of getting a proper estimate on the value of those paintings,” Troubert added, eyeing the pictures. Madame de Listomere offered frames for Saint-Gatien if they were to be given to the chapel. He replied they did not belong to him. She handed him the relinquishment, saying it ended all discussion and that she had some compassion for Birotteau; he had asked, as the price of renunciation, for Chapeloud’s portrait. Troubert looked fixedly at her and went down to ask Mademoiselle Gamard.

He soon returned. “Madame,” he said, “I bring you the words of a dying woman. ‘The Abbe Chapeloud was so true a friend...that I cannot consent to part with his picture.’ As for me, if it were mine I would not yield it. My feelings to my late friend were so faithful that I should feel my right above others’.”

“That’s no need to quarrel over a bad picture,” the baroness said, offering to have a copy made and courteously inviting him to play whist at her house. Troubert, stroked his chin—caught by vanity—and promised he would oblige her for her sake.

Madame de Listomere went home hoping the archbishop would complete the peace. But Mademoiselle Gamard died the next day. Her will left everything to Abbe Troubert; the fortune was appraised at three hundred thousand francs. He sent the Listomeres invitations to the services and funeral; the baroness insisted they go. “We must go,” she said. Bourbonne added that the baron must go to the cemetery; the lieutenant, unluckily, had not left Tours.

The services were performed with unusual ecclesiastical magnificence. Only Birotteau wept, kneeling in a side chapel; he believed himself guilty and sincerely prayed for the deceased, lamenting that he had not obtained her forgiveness. Troubert followed the body to the grave and delivered a discourse that, by eloquence, enlarged the old maid’s life to monumental proportions. In the peroration he spoke of her life devoted to God and of Providence withdrawing her from trials; his tone suggested she rested innocent. After the sermon, in a gesture that betrayed more joy than grief, he sprinkled the coffin with holy water.

The next morning Mademoiselle Salomon came to tell Madame de Listomere that “our poor Abbe Birotteau has just received a frightful blow: he is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien.” Saint-Symphorien lay beyond the bridge, a suburb three miles away—an exile in plain sight of the town. The parsonage was cold and damp, the parish too poor, and the removal seemed an infamous plot.

To end the history: five months later Troubert was made Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity of fifteen hundred francs to Abbe Birotteau. The day her will’s dispositions were announced, Monseigneur Hyacinthe, Bishop of Troyes, about to leave Tours for his diocese, delayed when he received the news. Furious at being foiled by the baroness—whom he had lately received while she had secretly aided a man he counted as an enemy—Troubert again threatened the baron’s career and jeopardized his uncle’s peerage. He made one of those soft, vengeful priestly speeches in the archbishop’s salon. The Baron de Listomere went to him the next day and must have submitted to hard conditions, for his later conduct showed entire submission to the Jesuit’s will.

The new bishop deeded Mademoiselle Gamard’s house to the cathedral chapter, gave Chapeloud’s books and bookcases to the seminary, and presented the two disputed pictures to the Chapel of the Virgin—but he kept Chapeloud’s portrait. No one could explain this near-total renunciation of Gamard’s bequest; Bourbonne supposed the bishop had secretly kept invested monies to support his rank in Paris. Only the night before Troubert’s departure did Bourbonne unearth the hidden reason: the baron had contested Madame de Listomere’s legacy to Birotteau on grounds of undue influence. The deathblow to the feeblest victim was complete.

A few days after the case was brought the baron was promoted to captain. As ecclesiastical discipline, the curate of Saint-Symphorien—Birotteau—was suspended; his superiors judged him guilty. The murderer of Sophie Gamard was also a swindler; had Troubert kept the property openly, it would have been harder to censure Birotteau.

When Monseigneur Hyacinthe drove along the quay Saint-Symphorien in a post-chaise on his way to Paris, poor Birotteau was placed in an armchair in the sun on a terrace above the road. Pale and haggard, smitten by the archbishop, he was the skeleton of the man who less than a year earlier had rolled contentedly along the cloister. The bishop cast one look of pity and contempt, then went his way.

There is no doubt Troubert might once have been a Hildebrand or an Alexander VI. In our era the Church is no longer the political power it once was; celibacy concentrates a man’s faculties on a single passion—egotism—which makes celibates either useless or mischievous. We live when governments tend to make Man for Society rather than Society for Man. There is a perpetual struggle between the Individual and a social system that uses him while he tries to use it. In earlier times man was first father, then lived for clan or city; later, for caste or religion, and his range of interests widened. Today his life attaches to a vast country; perhaps someday his family may be the whole universe.

Will such moral cosmopolitanism—Christian Rome’s hope of Brotherhood—prove only a sublime error? It is natural to believe in such a noble vision, but human nature lacks such divine proportions. Souls vast enough to hold a world of feeling belong to great exceptions. Physiologists who say as the brain enlarges the heart narrows are mistaken. The apparent egotism of those who bear a science, a nation, or a code is a highest passion; it is the maternity of the masses. To create peoples and ideas they must unite in their mighty brains the tenderness of woman and the force of God. The histories of Innocent III, Peter the Great, and other great leaders show the same vast thought of which Troubert was a provincial representative.
