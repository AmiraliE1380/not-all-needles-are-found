**Colonel Chabert**  
*By Honoré de Balzac*  
*Translated by Ellen Marriage and Clara Bell*  

**DEDICATION**  
To Madame la Comtesse Ida de Bocarme née du Chasteler.

---

The story begins in a lawyer's office where clerk Simonnin mocks an old man in a shabby coat, calling him "old Box-coat." The clerks, busy with work and gossiping about clients, dismiss the old man, who is revealed to be Colonel Chabert, thought to be dead after the battle of Eylau.

Chabert, weary and desperate, seeks to speak with attorney M. Derville, but the clerks refuse to take him seriously, joking about his appearance and speculating on his past. Despite their mockery, Chabert insists on waiting for Derville, demonstrating his determination and the gravity of his situation.

When Chabert finally reveals his identity, the clerks are shocked. They had assumed he was a beggar, but now realize he is a significant figure. Their laughter turns to disbelief as they process his claim. Boucard, the head clerk, tries to dismiss Chabert, suggesting he return at a more convenient time, highlighting the clerks' indifference to his plight.

As Chabert leaves, the clerks continue their banter, debating whether he is truly the colonel or a pretender. They express skepticism about his story, particularly since Chabert's wife has remarried, leaving him in a vulnerable position. Their attitudes reflect a broader societal disdain for those who have fallen from grace, illustrating the harsh realities of post-war France.

The scene shifts back to the clerks, who are more concerned with trivial matters than the serious implications of Chabert's visit. They engage in lively discussions about theater and bets, showcasing their youthful exuberance and lack of empathy. The office, filled with the smell of food and cluttered with legal papers, serves as a backdrop to their indifference.

Ultimately, the story highlights the stark contrast between the struggles of individuals like Chabert and the callousness of the legal system and society. The clerks' laughter and lightheartedness poignantly remind us of the disconnect between those in power and the marginalized. Balzac vividly depicts a world where dignity is often stripped away, leaving only remnants of past glory.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At around one in the morning, Colonel Chabert arrived at the office of Maitre Derville, a young but renowned attorney in Paris. The porter informed him that Derville was not yet in, but Chabert insisted on his appointment and was shown upstairs. There, he found Derville's head clerk organizing case papers, who explained that Derville often worked late into the night to strategize for his cases.

When Derville finally arrived, he was taken aback by Chabert's ghostly appearance. The Colonel, thin and pale with a scarred head, introduced himself as the Colonel declared dead at the Battle of Eylau. Derville and his clerk exchanged skeptical glances, suspecting madness.

Chabert recounted his story: he had commanded a cavalry regiment at Eylau, where he was gravely injured and left for dead. Despite being reported dead, he survived, buried among corpses. He escaped and was rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery, he sought to reclaim his identity and fortune, only to find that his wife, Countess Ferraud, had remarried and inherited his estate.

Derville, realizing the gravity of Chabert's situation, listened intently as the Colonel described his struggles to prove his identity and reclaim his rights. Chabert expressed despair over being treated as a madman and his longing for vengeance against his wife, who had ignored him since his return.

Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offered to help him obtain the necessary documents to prove his identity and promised financial assistance while they pursued the case. Chabert, overwhelmed with gratitude, recognized Derville as a rare ally in his fight for justice.

As they discussed the legal complexities, Chabert's demeanor shifted from despair to determination. He expressed a willingness to fight for his rights, even if it meant facing significant challenges in court. Derville assured him he would take the case seriously and work diligently to help him reclaim his life.

After their meeting, Chabert left with a sense of hope, clutching the money Derville had given him. For the first time in years, he felt a glimmer of possibility in his otherwise bleak existence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Three months after an interview, Derville, a notary, is approached by Crottat, who requests the return of 600 francs given to Colonel Chabert, an old soldier. Derville is hesitant to continue supporting Chabert financially, having previously been misled by his sense of patriotism. He notices a German letter on his desk and instructs his clerk Boucard to translate it. The letter indicates that documents regarding Chabert's estate will soon arrive, confirming their legitimacy and the existence of witnesses in Prussia.

Derville recalls that Chabert’s estate was managed by Roguin, a disreputable lawyer who recently fled with clients' money. Crottat informs him that Chabert's widow, Rose Chapotel, inherited about 300,000 francs after a contested will. Derville learns that Chabert is living in poor conditions in Faubourg Saint-Marceau with a dairyman named Vergniaud.

Upon visiting Chabert, Derville finds him in squalor, yet the Colonel retains his dignity. Chabert expresses his desire to reclaim his life and fortune, believing he deserves recognition as a general. Derville explains the complexities of legally proving Chabert's identity, including the potential for a lengthy and costly court battle. He warns Chabert that his wife, now married to Ferraud, may contest his claims, especially since they have children together.

Chabert is disheartened by the prospect of a drawn-out legal struggle and its financial burden. Derville reassures him that they will pursue a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the marriage, aiming to restore Chabert's rights. Chabert agrees to trust Derville with his case.

As Derville leaves, he encounters Vergniaud, who expresses concern for Chabert's well-being and requests financial help to improve the Colonel's living conditions. Derville decides to assist but insists that Chabert should help Vergniaud.

Derville plans to confront Madame Ferraud, Chabert's widow, to gauge her reaction to the news of Chabert's survival. He reflects on the political and social dynamics surrounding the Ferrauds, particularly how Ferraud's ambitions could influence the outcome of Chabert's case.

When Derville meets Madame Ferraud, she initially dismisses the idea of Chabert being alive. However, Derville's serious demeanor and knowledge of the situation quickly shift the tone of the conversation. He warns her of the legal implications of Chabert's return and the potential for public opinion to turn against her if the case goes to court.

Madame Ferraud, realizing the gravity of her situation, becomes defensive but is intrigued by the possibility of leveraging Chabert's affection to negotiate a favorable outcome. Derville proposes a compromise, suggesting they could settle the matter amicably, benefiting both parties.

The conversation reveals underlying tensions in Madame Ferraud's life, including her fears of losing status and wealth. Derville's strategic approach highlights the complexities of the legal system and the personal stakes involved for all parties. Ultimately, he leaves the meeting confident in his ability to navigate the situation to Chabert's advantage.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, arrived at Derville's office from opposite ends of Paris. Chabert, dignified in a new outfit and wig, embodied the soldierly style he had regained, while the elegantly dressed Countess followed shortly after. The office remained unchanged, with clerks joking about the unusual situation.

Derville prepared a memorandum outlining the terms of their agreement regarding Chabert's identity and the conditions for their marriage. The Countess was initially resistant to a lawsuit to annul Chabert's death certificate, insisting she wanted to avoid legal proceedings. When Chabert entered, tension escalated, revealing the complexities of their relationship. The Countess feigned ignorance about Chabert's identity, but her reaction suggested otherwise.

As the discussion progressed, Chabert expressed anger over the Countess's reluctance to acknowledge him and her proposed financial terms. Feeling cornered, the Countess left abruptly, prompting Derville to caution Chabert about her potential traps. Despite his rage, Chabert was deeply affected and left the office in despair.

Later, the Countess approached Chabert and led him to her carriage, taking him to her country house. There, she expressed emotional turmoil over their situation and revealed her love for another man, Comte Ferraud, complicating their reunion. Chabert, though hurt, offered understanding and support, suggesting they maintain dignity and avoid public scandal.

During their time together, the Countess attempted to charm Chabert, seeking to erase the pain of their past. However, her ulterior motives became clear as she plotted to secure her position while keeping Chabert at bay. After several days, Chabert sensed her manipulations, leading to a confrontation with Delbecq, the Countess's secretary, who suggested exploiting Chabert's situation for financial gain.

Feeling betrayed, Chabert confronted the Countess, who tried to manipulate him further. He ultimately decided to sacrifice his claim to his identity for her happiness, but her true intentions were revealed when she discussed putting him in an asylum if necessary. This realization shattered Chabert's trust, leading him to reject her and resolve to disappear from her life.

Chabert's subsequent life was marked by hardship, falling into poverty and obscurity. Derville, unaware of Chabert's fate, assumed the couple had reached a compromise. He later discovered Chabert imprisoned as a vagabond, stripped of his dignity. Despite his circumstances, Chabert maintained pride and contempt for societal norms.

In a final act of integrity, Chabert sought to repay Derville for his assistance, demonstrating enduring loyalty despite betrayal. Derville, recognizing Chabert's plight, facilitated the payment owed to him by the Countess, highlighting the tragic consequences of their entangled lives. Ultimately, Chabert's story serves as a poignant reflection on identity, love, and the harsh realities of life after war.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In June 1840, attorney Godeschal visited Bicetre with his predecessor Derville, where they encountered an elderly pauper drying his handkerchief. Godeschal noted the man's resemblance to grotesque carvings, and Derville revealed he was Colonel Chabert, the estranged husband of the Comtesse Ferraud. Derville explained that Chabert had been sent to the workhouse due to reminders of their humble beginnings.

Two days later, they returned to find Chabert drawing in the sand. When addressed, he claimed to be "Hyacinthe," displaying a childlike innocence. Godeschal offered him money for snuff, which Chabert gratefully accepted, revealing a mix of military bravado and confusion.

An observing pauper remarked that despite Chabert's apparent childishness, he was clever and philosophical. He recalled how Chabert had impressed a Prussian officer with his military history. Derville lamented the fate of men like Chabert, who served Napoleon only to end up in a workhouse.

Derville reflected on the disillusionment faced by priests, doctors, and lawyers, particularly the latter, who witness the darkest aspects of human nature. He shared harrowing experiences from his legal career, including familial betrayals and moral corruption, concluding that the horrors he had seen surpassed any fictional accounts. Godeschal, having encountered similar cases, expressed a desire to escape the grim realities of Paris for a quieter life in the countryside.

The narrative highlights Colonel Chabert's tragic decline and critiques societal indifference towards those who have fallen from grace, emphasizing the darker truths of human relationships and the legal profession.
