“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!” cried the lawyer’s clerk Simonnin, leaning from a window and munching dry bread as he playfully shot a crumb at a stranger crossing the courtyard of Maître Derville’s office in the Rue Vivienne. “Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks on people, or I will turn you out of doors. However poor a client may be, he is still a man!” admonished the head clerk, who was busy with a bill of costs and tried to check the boy’s mischief. Simonnin, a typical messenger of thirteen or fourteen, was known for impudence and mischief; he and the other clerks, including the mischievous Godeschal, gossiped and joked about the shabby figure they called “old Box-coat,” even inventing tricks and false rules — Godeschal claiming M. Derville received clients only at odd hours — to make sport of him. The office buzzed with laughter and camaraderie: cluttered, dimly lit, the smell of food mingling with the musty odor of old papers, a disorder the clerks accepted as part of their chaotic, monotonous work. When the old man entered, looking weary, out of place and civil, he politely asked if M. Derville was at home; preoccupied, the clerks dismissed him. Boucard, the head clerk, brusquely told him to return at one in the morning, leaving the visitor bewildered. The clerks resumed their mockery, trading extravagant guesses about whether the man had once been a nobleman or a porter to explain his shabby coat; when the old man insisted he was Colonel Chabert, supposed to have died at Eylau, they erupted in laughter and treated the claim as a joke. Amid the jeering they argued over which theatre to attend that evening, their playful disputes underscoring a lively, youthful spirit. Finally Boucard quelled the chatter and refocused them on work, stressing efficiency; the scene captured their exuberant, irreverent, self-contained camaraderie in stark contrast to the old man’s somber, unsettling presence.

At about one in the morning Colonel Chabert knocked at the door of Maître Derville, a prominent Paris attorney. The porter said Derville had not returned, but Chabert insisted and was shown upstairs, where the head clerk, arranging papers, explained that Derville preferred to work at night and that the Colonel was only the third person to seek him so late.

When Derville arrived he was struck by the Colonel's strange appearance: thin, pale, his head scarred as if by a traumatic past. Chabert introduced himself as the Colonel presumed killed at Eylau, prompting Derville and the clerk to suspect madness, but Chabert was insistent and began to relate his story.

Accustomed to varied clients, Derville urged brevity. The Colonel recounted his service at Eylau, where he was gravely injured and buried among the dead. Miraculously he survived because his horse's body had shielded him; he described awakening in a grave, struggling to escape, and finally being rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery, Chabert discovered he had lost everything, including proof of his identity. With no documents, his attempts to reclaim his life met disbelief and ridicule. He wandered as a beggar, was often treated as a madman, and was confined in a mental institution for two years. Released but haunted by the past, he had lost his wife, the Countess Ferraud, who had remarried and borne children.

Derville, sensing the gravity of the claims, expressed skepticism but also a willingness to help. Chabert said he sought to reclaim his rights and fortune, which he believed had been unjustly taken, lamenting that no lawyer had taken him seriously enough to help prove his identity and recover his estate. Moved by Chabert's plight, Derville offered to obtain necessary documents from Germany and gave him a small sum to subsist on. Overwhelmed with gratitude, Chabert felt hope for the first time in years. He voiced a longing for vengeance against his wife, revealing his deep emotional turmoil.

Derville acknowledged the case's complexity and the likely legal obstacles. Chabert, resolute, declared that if he failed he would rather die than continue living in obscurity. Derville promised to treat the matter seriously and provided a letter to a notary for further assistance.

As Chabert left, he experienced a renewed sense of purpose and determination. He had received immediate aid and the promise of legal support in his quest to reclaim identity and rights. The encounter marked a turning point: after years of despair, the Colonel departed with a glimmer of hope for the future.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat, charged with advancing half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client, raised a serious matter and began by asking him to refund the six hundred francs the old soldier had received.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed; he was a young man who had recently taken over the office after his chief fled in bankruptcy.

“Thank you for reminding me of that affair,” Derville replied. “My philanthropy will not extend beyond twenty-five louis; I fear I have already been duped by my patriotism.”

As he spoke, Derville noticed papers on his desk, including a letter with stamps from various post offices.

“Ah ha! Here is the last act of the comedy; now we shall see if I have been taken in!” he exclaimed, opening the letter, but it was written in German.

“Boucard, have this translated immediately,” Derville ordered his head clerk.

The notary in Berlin informed Derville that the requested documents would arrive shortly and were regular and legally stamped. Most witnesses to the recorded facts were still in Eylau, Prussia, and the woman who saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg.

“This looks like business,” Derville said after Boucard translated the letter. He then asked Crottat for information from his office about Chabert’s estate, recalling the rogue Roguin.

“Let’s call him the unfortunate Roguin,” Crottat interjected with a laugh.

“Wasn’t it that ill-used man who absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of his clients’ money?” Derville continued. “I believe he handled Chabert’s estate.”

“Yes,” Crottat confirmed. “I copied the papers when I was third clerk. Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe, called Chabert, Count of the Empire, married without settlement, so they held all property in common. The personalty was about six hundred thousand francs. Before his marriage, Colonel Chabert made a will leaving one-quarter to the hospitals and the State to take the other quarter. The will was contested, leading to a forced sale, and the widow received the portion bequeathed to the treasury.”

“So Comte Chabert’s personal fortune was no more than three hundred thousand francs?” Derville asked.

“Exactly!” Crottat replied. “You lawyers can be quite clear-headed despite accusations of false practices.”

Colonel Chabert was lodging in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud. Derville had to walk because his coachman refused to navigate the unpaved street. He found shabby stone gate-posts with a sign, “Vergniaud, dairyman.” The yard was dismal; a dilapidated house seemed on the point of collapse.

The house was ill-built, with mismatched windows and a trodden-earth floor. Derville noted the squalor but also the Colonel’s calm demeanor when he emerged smoking a pipe, still displaying military coolness.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked, directing Derville to a better path to avoid the manure.

Derville entered the Colonel’s room, which contained only a chair and a bed of straw. The walls were damp; the atmosphere was grim, yet Chabert seemed content. Derville expressed concern over his living conditions.

“Here, we are not distinguished for luxury,” Chabert replied. “But I have done no one wrong, and I sleep in peace.”

Derville hesitated about the money he had advanced, instead asking why Chabert had not moved to Paris, where he could live better.

“The good folks here have taken me in and fed me for a year,” Chabert explained. “I cannot leave them now that I have a little money.”

Derville told him the papers from Heilsberg were expected and that the woman who saved him was alive.

“Curse the money! To think I haven’t got any!” Chabert exclaimed, throwing his pipe down in frustration.

Derville explained the legal complexities: proving Chabert’s identity would require lengthy, costly proceedings. He warned that the case could be prolonged by the bigamy issue with Comtesse Ferraud, who had remarried.

“Things are not done so in the legal world,” Derville cautioned. “You will face many challenges, and your opponents will demand inquiries that could take years.”

Chabert, overwhelmed by the prospect of a drawn-out lawsuit, expressed despair. Derville reassured him that the outcome could be favorable, suggesting a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the subsequent marriage.

“I will send you a power of attorney to sign,” Derville concluded, offering financial help.

Chabert, feeling hopeless, agreed to trust Derville completely. As Derville left, he encountered Louis Vergniaud, who expressed concern for Chabert’s well-being and requested a loan to improve the Colonel’s living conditions.

Derville agreed to provide the funds but insisted that Chabert should be the one to help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud, considering her position and the potential leverage he could gain.

Monsieur le Comte Ferraud, a former Councillor’s son, had returned to France after the Revolution and married Madame Chabert, who had turned her share of her husband’s fortune into a substantial income. Their marriage was socially accepted; Ferraud was politically ambitious but had begun to regret his union with Madame Ferraud.

Derville, reflecting on the situation, recognized the Countess’s precarious position. He arrived at the Hotel Ferraud and found her in luxurious surroundings, a stark contrast to Chabert’s squalor.

“Good-morning, Monsieur Derville,” she greeted him, but he quickly shifted to serious matters.

“I have come to speak with you on a very serious matter,” he stated, revealing that Colonel Chabert was alive.

The Countess laughed dismissively, but Derville’s serious demeanor silenced her. He warned her of the danger she faced if Chabert’s identity were proven, hinting at the potential legal ramifications.

She insisted her second marriage was secure because of her children, but Derville countered that the law might not favor her if Chabert proved his identity. He suggested Ferraud might not support her if he learned of the risk to his name and fortune.

Derville’s words struck a nerve; the Countess’s manner shifted as she considered the implications. He proposed a compromise, sensing her vulnerability.

“Does he still love me?” she asked, contemplating how to use Chabert’s affection to her advantage.

Derville left her with the suggestion to consider a compromise, confident he had gained the upper hand in the situation.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at their lawyer's office. Thanks to Derville's support, Chabert arrived in a dignified blue suit with the Legion of Honor ribbon, regaining a soldierly bearing and transforming from a shabby figure into one reflecting his past nobility.

When Chabert entered Derville's office, Madame Ferraud arrived shortly after, elegantly dressed to appear youthful. The office was unchanged; clerks made jesting remarks about his transformation. Aware of the couple's tension, Derville separated them to discuss terms and presented a memorandum: Chabert would formally acknowledge his identity in exchange for the financial support his wife would provide.

The Countess was taken aback by the terms, especially the financial obligations, and resisted letting the matter go to court. When Chabert returned he confronted her about their past, exposing the pain of their separation. She feigned surprise and attempted to leave, but Derville urged her to stay. Chabert, bitter and wounded, pressed for justice while Derville warned against rash action.

After a heated exchange, the Countess led Chabert to her carriage and they went to her country house in Groslay. During the ride her tone shifted, revealing turmoil: she confessed love for another man, Comte Ferraud, and wanted to preserve her dignity without public scandal. Chabert, though hurt, offered a measure of understanding, recognizing the complexities and social consequences she feared.

At the country house she treated him kindly, trying to efface past pain. They reminisced, and Chabert found temporary solace in her presence even as resentment and a sense of betrayal lingered. The Countess, meanwhile, worked discreetly to secure her position and extinguish Chabert's claim.

As days passed her unease grew; she wrote to Delbecq to manipulate the situation further. When Chabert discovered this, he felt deeply betrayed and resolved to confront her. Their relationship became a battleground of emotion, with Chabert torn between lingering affection and mounting resentment.

Ultimately he decided to sacrifice his identity for the sake of the Countess and their children, accepting anonymity to avoid scandal, but her continued manipulations revealed the depth of her betrayal. He confronted her, voiced his disdain, and chose to live without reclaiming his former name. The Countess, recognizing the consequences, understood she could live peacefully on his silence.

Chabert then vanished from society. Months later Derville learned of his fate when he encountered him in a courtroom, now reduced to a vagabond. Despite his circumstances, Chabert retained a measure of dignity and reflected on the indifference of the world. Derville, moved by his plight, sought to help, but Chabert's contempt for societal norms led him to reject material concerns.

In the end Chabert's story is one of loss and resilience, illustrating the complexities of love, betrayal, and the struggle for identity in a world that often overlooks the individual.

In June 1840, the attorney Godeschal accompanied Derville to Ris. Near Bicêtre they noticed an old pauper under an elm drying his handkerchief in the sun. In a shabby wrapper-coat with a striking look, the man prompted Godeschal’s comparison to grotesque German carvings; Derville recognized him as Comte Chabert, husband of the pious Comtesse Ferraud, who had likely had him sent to the workhouse because he reminded her of their past.

Intrigued, Godeschal urged Derville to tell Chabert’s story. Two days later they returned and found him sitting on a tree trunk, drawing in the sand. When addressed he insisted he was no longer Chabert but “Hyacinthe,” merely a number in the institution, and envied a condemned man for the freedom of not being married.

Godeschal offered him money for snuff; Chabert accepted eagerly, thanking them as “brave troopers,” playfully brandishing his stick and crying “Vive Napoleon!” Another pauper observed that Chabert, though sometimes childish, could be philosophical and imaginative, recalling how he had once impressed a Prussian officer with his military history.

Derville reflected on Chabert’s tragic trajectory: from foundling to Napoleonic soldier to the workhouse. He lamented the disillusionment suffered by the priest, the doctor, and especially the lawyer, who sees human nature at its worst—betrayal, greed, and moral decay. Derville cited cases of familial ruin: fathers abandoned by daughters and mothers who exploit their children for gain.

Godeschal confessed he had witnessed similar horrors in practice and voiced a wish to escape Paris’s corruption for a quieter rural life with his wife.
