“HULLO! There’s that Box-coat again!” cried Simonnin, leaning from a window and flinging a breadcrumb at a stranger crossing Maître Derville’s courtyard.

“Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks… However poor a client may be, he is still a man!” Boucard, head clerk, scolded over a bill. Simonnin, a saucy thirteen- or fourteen-year-old, and clerks, including Godeschal, mocked the shabby visitor as “old Box-coat”; Godeschal proposed a prank, saying Derville took clients only at odd hours.

The cluttered, dim office smelled of food and musty papers and buzzed with laughter. The weary stranger asked for Maître Derville but was brusquely told to return at one. When he insisted he was Colonel Chabert, reported dead at Eylau, they laughed. Boucard finally silenced the room, his sternness cutting through their irreverent gaiety.

About one in the morning Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the head clerk preparing papers explained Derville preferred to work at night and the Colonel was the third late caller.

When Derville arrived he was struck by the man’s thin, pale, scarred appearance. The stranger claimed to be the Colonel presumed killed at Eylau; though Derville and the clerk suspected madness, Chabert insisted on telling his story.

He recounted Eylau: gravely wounded and buried among corpses, he survived miraculously because his horse’s body shielded him. A woman rescued him; he spent months in hospital, and on recovery found he'd lost everything, especially proof of identity. Without papers, attempts to reclaim his life met disbelief and ridicule; he begged and spent two years in an asylum. Upon release his wife, Countess Ferraud, had remarried and borne children.

Sensing this, Derville offered help: obtain necessary documents from Germany, gave some money, and wrote a letter to a notary. Overwhelmed with gratitude, Chabert voiced longing for vengeance and refusal of obscurity; the meeting restored fragile hope.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat—who had advanced half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client—demanded the six hundred francs be refunded.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed; his sarcastic tone showed he was a young man who’d recently taken over after his chief fled in bankruptcy. Derville shrugged, joking his philanthropy would not exceed twenty-five louis.

Noticing a letter on his desk, Derville had his clerk Boucard translate. It was from Berlin: the requested documents would arrive, they were legally stamped, witnesses to the recorded facts still lived in Eylau, and the woman who had saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg. “This looks like business,” Derville said, and questioned Crottat about Chabert’s estate and the rogue Roguin.

“Let’s call him the unfortunate Roguin,” Crottat scoffed, then recalled the papers he’d copied as third clerk. Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe Chabert, had married without a settlement, so property had been common. The personalty had been about six hundred thousand francs. Before his marriage Colonel Chabert had made a will leaving one-quarter to the hospitals and another quarter to the State; the will was contested, forcing a sale that left the widow with the portion originally bequeathed to the treasury. Roguin later absconded with large sums—reports mentioned eight hundred thousand francs—and the net available to Chabert could be no more than three hundred thousand francs. “You lawyers can be clear-headed despite accusations,” Crottat added.

Derville then sought the Colonel. Chabert lodged in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with Vergniaud, an old quartermaster-turned-dairyman. The coachman refused the unpaved street, and Derville found a dismal, ill-built house: trodden-earth floor, damp walls, a straw bed. Yet when Chabert appeared, smoking his pipe, he retained the military calm of a man unshaken by hardship.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked. When Derville questioned his lodgings, Chabert replied that good folks had fed him for a year and that, having a little money, he could not leave them. Derville told him the Heilsberg papers were expected and that his savior was alive; Chabert, frustrated at the thought of money he did not yet possess, threw down his pipe.

Derville explained the legal complexities: proving identity would demand long, costly proceedings, complicated by the bigamy question since Comtesse Ferraud had remarried. Opponents would insist on inquiries, translations, and foreign formalities—years and expense. Overwhelmed, Chabert despaired; Derville reassured him the outcome might be favorable and proposed a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the subsequent marriage. He offered to send a power of attorney and to provide financial help. Trusting Derville completely, Chabert agreed.

As Derville left he met Louis Vergniaud, who asked for a loan to improve the Colonel’s lodging. Derville agreed but insisted Chabert should be the one to help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud.

Madame Ferraud, formerly Madame Chabert, had turned her share of the husband’s fortunes into substantial income; her second marriage to Ferraud, an ambitious man and son of a former councillor, was socially secure and brought her influence. At the Hotel Ferraud she was surrounded by luxury when Derville told her plainly that Colonel Chabert was alive. She laughed until his serious tone silenced her. He warned her of the legal and social danger if Chabert proved his identity and hinted Ferraud might not support her if his name and fortune were threatened. The Countess, shaken, asked whether Chabert still loved her and began to consider compromise. Derville left confident he had gained the upper hand.

A week after Derville’s visits, Colonel Chabert — restored and wearing his Legion of Honor — came to Derville’s office to meet his wife, the Countess. She appeared composed; sensing the tension, Derville separated them and proposed a memorandum: Chabert would acknowledge his identity for financial support. The Countess, fearing scandal, resisted.

When Chabert returned he confronted her, forcing the painful truth of their separation. She feigned surprise and tried to leave; Derville urged her to stay. She then took Chabert to her country house in Groslay and confessed she loved Comte Ferraud and wanted to avoid scandal. Though hurt, Chabert showed some understanding. She treated him kindly while secretly scheming to secure her position, writing to Delbecq to quash his claim. Discovering this betrayal, Chabert ultimately sacrificed his identity to protect the Countess and their children, renounced reclaiming his name, and disappeared. Months later Derville found him a destitute vagabond in a courtroom; still dignified, he scorned society’s indifference and refused charity.

In June 1840 Godeschal accompanied Derville to Ris. Near Bicêtre they found an old pauper under an elm—clad with a grotesque face. Derville recognized him as Comte Chabert, whom his pious wife Comtesse Ferraud had placed in the workhouse to bury past.

Two days later Chabert refused his name and called himself “Hyacinthe,” an institutional number who envied a condemned man. Godeschal gave him snuff and Chabert hailed them “brave troopers,” waved his stick and cried “Vive Napoleon!” A fellow pauper recalled Chabert impressing a Prussian officer with wartime recollections.

Derville reflected on Chabert’s fall—from foundling and Napoleonic soldier to inmate—and on priest, doctor, lawyer confronting betrayal, greed and moral decay. Godeschal admitted similar disillusionment and longed for quiet country life. An addendum lists Balzac’s characters and cross-appearances.
