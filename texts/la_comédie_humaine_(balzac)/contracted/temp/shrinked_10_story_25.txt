“HULLO! There is that old Box-coat again!” cried the lawyer’s clerk Simonnin, leaning from a window, tossing a breadcrumb at a stranger crossing Maître Derville’s courtyard.

“Come, Simonnin, don’t play tricks on people, or I will turn you out of doors. However poor a client is, he is still a man!” admonished the head clerk bent over a bill of costs.

Simonnin, a thirteen- or fourteen-year-old, was known for impudence. He and the other clerks, including Godeschal, mocked the shabby visitor as “old Box-coat.” Godeschal proposed a prank, claiming M. Derville received clients only at odd hours.

The office buzzed with laughter. Cluttered, dim, smelling of food and musty papers, its disorder was routine.

When the weary, out-of-place man entered and politely asked for M. Derville, he was dismissed. Boucard brusquely told him to return at one in the morning, leaving him bewildered.

The clerks resumed jeering, guessing—nobleman or porter—to explain his dress. When he insisted he was Colonel Chabert, reported dead at Eylau, they treated it as a joke and laughed. Amid chatter about the theatre, Boucard finally silenced them and refocused the room—its rowdy irreverence stark against the old man’s somber presence.

At about one in the morning Colonel Chabert knocked at Maître Derville’s door. The porter said Derville had not returned, but the head clerk—arranging papers for the next day—explained Derville preferred to work at night and that the Colonel was only the third late caller.

When Derville arrived he was struck by the man’s thin, pale appearance and scars suggesting past trauma. The stranger claimed to be the Colonel presumed killed at Eylau, prompting Derville and the clerk to suspect madness, but the Colonel insisted on telling his story.

Derville, used to varied clients, urged brevity. Chabert recounted Eylau: gravely wounded and buried among the corpses, he had miraculously survived because his horse’s body shielded him. He described awakening in a grave, struggling free, and being rescued by a woman who took him to a hospital. After months of recovery he found he had lost everything—especially proof of identity. Without documents, attempts to reclaim his life met disbelief and ridicule; he wandered as a beggar, was often treated as mad, and spent two years confined in a mental institution. Upon release he discovered his wife, the Countess Ferraud, had remarried and borne children.

Derville, sensing the gravity though skeptical, was willing to help. Chabert said he sought to reclaim rights and fortune he believed unjustly taken and lamented that no lawyer had taken him seriously. Moved, Derville offered to obtain necessary documents from Germany, gave him a small sum to subsist on, and provided a letter to a notary for further assistance.

Overwhelmed with gratitude, Chabert felt hope for the first time in years, voiced a longing for vengeance, and revealed his deep turmoil—declaring he would rather die than live in obscurity. Derville acknowledged the case’s complexity but promised to treat it seriously. As he left, Chabert experienced a renewed sense of purpose: the encounter was a turning point that gave him a glimmer of hope.

About three months after the interview, in Derville’s room, the notary Crottat—tasked with advancing half-pay to Derville’s eccentric client—raised a pointed demand: he wanted the six hundred francs the old soldier had received refunded.

“Are you amusing yourself with pensioning the old army?” Crottat laughed; he was a young man who had recently taken the office after his chief fled in bankruptcy.

“Thank you for reminding me of that affair,” Derville replied. “My philanthropy will not extend beyond twenty-five louis; I fear I have already been duped by my patriotism.”

Noticing papers on his desk, Derville opened a letter stamped from several post offices; it was written in German. He ordered his head clerk Boucard to translate. The Berlin notary informed Derville that the requested documents would arrive shortly and were legally stamped. Witnesses to the recorded facts still lived in Eylau, Prussia, and the woman who saved Comte Chabert was alive in Heilsberg.

“This looks like business,” Derville said after the translation. He questioned Crottat about Chabert’s estate and the rogue Roguin.

“Let’s call him the unfortunate Roguin,” Crottat interjected with a laugh.

“Wasn’t it that ill-used man who absconded with eight hundred thousand francs of his clients’ money?” Derville continued. “I believe he handled Chabert’s estate.”

“Yes,” Crottat confirmed. “I copied the papers when I was third clerk. Rose Chapotel, wife of Hyacinthe, called Chabert, Count of the Empire, married without settlement, so they held all property in common. The personalty was about six hundred thousand francs. Before his marriage, Colonel Chabert made a will leaving one-quarter to the hospitals and the State to take the other quarter. The will was contested, leading to a forced sale, and the widow received the portion bequeathed to the treasury.”

“So Comte Chabert’s personal fortune was no more than three hundred thousand francs?” Derville asked.

“Exactly!” Crottat replied. “You lawyers can be quite clear-headed despite accusations of false practices.”

Derville then sought the Colonel. Chabert lodged in the Faubourg Saint-Marceau with an old quartermaster named Vergniaud; Derville had to walk because his coachman refused the unpaved street. Stone gate-posts bore the sign “Vergniaud, dairyman.” The yard and house were dismal—ill-built, mismatched windows, a floor of trodden earth. Inside, a single chair and a straw bed, damp walls and a grim atmosphere testified to poverty.

Yet when Chabert emerged smoking a pipe he retained the military calm of a man unshaken by hardship.

“Why did you not write to me?” Chabert asked, directing Derville to avoid the manure.

“Here, we are not distinguished for luxury,” Chabert said when Derville questioned his lodgings. “But I have done no one wrong, and I sleep in peace.”

Derville hesitated about the money he had advanced and instead asked why Chabert had not moved to Paris to live better.

“The good folks here have taken me in and fed me for a year,” Chabert explained. “I cannot leave them now that I have a little money.”

Derville told him the papers from Heilsberg were expected and that the woman who saved him was alive. Chabert, frustrated at the thought of money he did not possess, threw down his pipe.

Derville then outlined the legal complexities: proving Chabert’s identity would require lengthy, costly proceedings. The case could be prolonged by the bigamy issue with Comtesse Ferraud, who had remarried; opponents would demand inquiries and formalities that might take years and drain resources. He warned that witnesses, translations, and foreign legal formalities would multiply both time and expense.

“You will face many challenges,” Derville cautioned. “Your opponents will demand inquiries that could take years.”

Overwhelmed by the prospect of a drawn-out lawsuit, Chabert expressed despair. Derville reassured him the outcome could be favorable and proposed a friendly suit to annul both the death certificate and the subsequent marriage. He offered to send a power of attorney for Chabert to sign and to provide financial help.

Chabert, feeling hopeless, agreed to trust Derville completely. As Derville left, he met Louis Vergniaud, who asked for a loan to improve the Colonel’s living conditions. Derville agreed but insisted Chabert should be the one to help his friends. He then planned to visit Comtesse Ferraud to assess her position and the leverage he might gain.

Monsieur le Comte Ferraud, a former Councillor’s son, had returned to France after the Revolution and married Madame Chabert, who had turned her share of her husband’s fortune into a substantial income. Their marriage was socially accepted; Ferraud was politically ambitious but had begun to regret his union. The Countess had become comfortable and influential, a sharp contrast to Chabert’s squalor.

Derville arrived at the Hotel Ferraud and found her surrounded by luxury. “Good-morning, Monsieur Derville,” she greeted him, but he quickly shifted to serious matters.

“I have come to speak with you on a very serious matter,” he stated, revealing that Colonel Chabert was alive.

The Countess laughed dismissively until Derville’s serious tone silenced her. He warned her of the danger she faced if Chabert’s identity were proven, hinting at the legal and social ramifications. She insisted her second marriage was secure because of her children, but Derville countered that the law might not favor her if Chabert proved his identity and that Ferraud might not support her if his name and fortune were at risk.

Derville’s words struck a nerve; the Countess began to consider the implications. He proposed a compromise, sensing her vulnerability. “Does he still love me?” she asked, contemplating how to use Chabert’s affection to her advantage.

Derville left her with the suggestion to consider a compromise, confident he had gained the upper hand.

A week after Derville's visits, Colonel Chabert and his wife, Madame la Comtesse Ferraud, set out from opposite ends of Paris to meet at their lawyer's office. With Derville's help Chabert arrived transformed: dignified in a blue suit and wearing the Legion of Honor ribbon, his soldierly bearing restored.

When Chabert entered Derville's office, Madame Ferraud shortly followed, elegantly made up to appear youthful. Clerks joked about his change, while Derville, aware of the couple's tension, separated them to discuss terms. He presented a memorandum: Chabert would formally acknowledge his identity in exchange for financial support from his wife. The Countess, startled and reluctant to see the matter public, resisted the obligation.

When Chabert returned he confronted her about their past, exposing the pain of their separation. She feigned surprise and tried to leave, but Derville urged her to stay. A heated exchange followed; despite warnings against rash action, Chabert pressed for justice. The Countess then led him to her carriage and took him to her country house in Groslay. During the ride she confessed turmoil: she loved another man, Comte Ferraud, and wished to preserve her dignity and avoid scandal. Chabert, hurt, offered a measure of understanding, recognizing the social consequences she feared.

At the country house she treated him kindly and they reminisced; Chabert found temporary solace even as resentment lingered. Meanwhile the Countess worked discreetly to secure her position and eliminate his claim, writing to Delbecq to manipulate affairs. When Chabert discovered this betrayal he resolved to confront her.

Ultimately he chose to sacrifice his identity to protect the Countess and their children, accepting anonymity to avoid scandal. Her continued machinations deepened his disdain; he renounced reclaiming his former name and vanished from society. Months later Derville found him reduced to a vagabond in a courtroom. Despite his poverty, Chabert retained dignity and scorn for society's indifference, refusing material aid. His story remains one of loss and resilience, tracing love, betrayal, and the struggle for identity in an indifferent world.

In June 1840 Godeschal, an attorney, accompanied Derville to Ris. Near Bicêtre they saw an old pauper under an elm, drying his handkerchief. In a shabby wrapper-coat he had a striking, almost carved look; Godeschal likened him to grotesque German carvings, while Derville recognized him as Comte Chabert, husband of the pious Comtesse Ferraud, who had likely put him in the workhouse because he reminded her of their past.

Curious, Godeschal urged Derville to tell Chabert’s story. Two days later they returned and found the old man drawing in the sand. When addressed he insisted he was no longer Chabert but “Hyacinthe,” merely a number in the institution; he even envied a condemned man for not being married. Godeschal gave him money for snuff; Chabert accepted eagerly, saluted them as “brave troopers,” waved his stick and cried “Vive Napoleon!” Another pauper remarked on his odd mix of philosophy and childishness and recalled how Chabert once impressed a Prussian officer with military recollections.

Derville reflected on Chabert’s tragic arc—from foundling to soldier under Napoleon to workhouse inmate—and on the disillusionment confronting priest, doctor, and lawyer, each exposed to human baseness: betrayal, greed, moral decay, fathers abandoned by daughters, mothers exploiting children. Godeschal admitted he’d seen similar horrors and longed to flee Parisian corruption for a quiet country life with his wife. An addendum lists characters from Balzac’s Human Comedy and their other appearances, including Napoleon and Comtesse Ferraud.
