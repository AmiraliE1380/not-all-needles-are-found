At Paris, balls commonly include two gatherings: the formal party, where guests trade superficial pleasantries, and a later, more intimate meeting where genuine conversation and laughter flourish. The latter, hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, preserves the old spirit of French wit and allows free-flowing ideas and camaraderie.

One memorable evening at Mademoiselle des Touches' salon, a group of notable figures, including the Minister de Marsay, engaged in lively discourse. The atmosphere brimmed with wit and ease, showcasing the conversational style Paris is known for. Anecdotes and confessions flowed, creating a delightful tapestry of social interaction.

As the night progressed, the talk turned to de Marsay's recollections of early love, especially a passionate affair with a woman six years his senior. He recounted youthful infatuation marked by intense devotion and secrecy. Despite his fervor, he kept up a facade of indifference to shield their relationship from society's gaze.

De Marsay described their love as a careful dance of appearances and feeling. He rode past her carriage, exchanged silent signals through the flowers she carried, and reveled in mutual admiration and the thrill of concealment, unaware of any betrayal.

A turning point came when he fell ill and sent her a note to cancel their meeting. Consumed by jealousy and suspicion, he decided to visit her despite his condition. On arriving, he found her with another man, which ignited a storm of emotion. Yet her calm demeanor and tender concern briefly soothed his doubts.

The narrative darkened as he grappled with the realization of betrayal. He felt a profound internal shift, marking the end of innocence and the onset of a more cynical view of love. He reflected on revenge and the complexities of passion, ultimately concluding that true love must transcend the desire for vengeance.

His account resonated with those present, prompting reflection on love, jealousy, and the social expectations shaping relationships. De Marsay's experience served as a poignant reminder of trust's fragility and love's transformative power, leaving a lasting impression on the salon.

Cured of my cold and my pure love, I began an affair with a charming heroine whose beauty sharply contrasted with that of my deceptive angel. I avoided quarrels with this clever woman, doubting whether true love could match the delights of her skilled deceit. Such refined hypocrisy can be as good as virtue.

I sought a hair artist in the Rue Boucher to make a keepsake for my new angel. He specialized in hair mementos and showed me work surpassing fairy tales, mentioning trends like marking linen with hair. Suspicion rose when I recognized a handkerchief he had worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. I left the shop believing in pleasure but skeptical about love.

Two months later, in the boudoir of the ethereal being, I held her hand as we explored the depths of sentiment. In a moment of tenderness, I seized the chance to draw out her most delightful lies. Charlotte professed love, insisting I was the only man in her world and that she had been seeking ways to bind me to her eternally.

The women listening to de Marsay seemed offended by his portrayal of their airs. Just as I might have believed her sweet falsehoods, I asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” The directness startled her; her reaction revealed guilt. She feigned astonishment, but I pressed on, suggesting she should marry him, as he was rich and devout.

Charlotte fell to her knees, insisting she loved no one but me. I urged her to be truthful, and she swore before God. I confessed doubts about her fidelity, which she quickly turned against me, claiming I no longer loved her. She sought to escape the confrontation, but curiosity compelled her to ask what I had seen. I revealed that I had noticed her attending mass with the Duke several times; she dismissed it as jealousy.

I explained that her marriage to the Duke would elevate her status and advised her to embrace the role, as he was virtuous and would expect propriety. Charlotte, in tears, lamented that if I had only spoken sooner we could have been happy. I maintained it was too late and she had gone too far with the Duke.

She worried about the Duke’s suspicions; I reassured her he likely had none but that she needed to prove devotion. As she paced the boudoir, she shifted demeanor, held out her hand, and declared I was noble and charming, vowing never to forget me.

Her tactics were admirable, and I played the distressed lover. She then asked what would become of me, and I revealed I had made advances to another woman. This startled Charlotte, who accused me of being unfaithful. I countered that she had too much wit for her heart to suffer.

Charlotte expressed disillusionment with love, claiming women could never be truly loved. I acknowledged her anger but pointed out that taking love seriously in society was like romanticizing an actress. She accused me of betrayal; I feigned submission and asked if she would remember her grievances once she became a duchess.

Her bitterness confirmed she still felt wronged, and I realized I had successfully distanced myself. I had studied the lives of successful men with women and believed I had mastered the art of retreat.

The women in the room reacted with sympathy for Charlotte, while de Marsay maintained a slight smile. Conversation shifted to the decline of social distinctions. Lady Dudley noted that women often did not see their marriages as acts of inconstancy. De Marsay lamented the erosion of the aristocracy, saying duchesses and marquises were disappearing and the Civil Code had leveled social hierarchies.

Blondet remarked that aristocracy now began with viscountesses, while de Marsay observed that countesses might survive in some form. He reflected on how the last great ladies had vanished with the splendor of the previous century, and the Code had inadvertently created a new class of women.

The Comte de Vandenesse commented on how the current structure allowed anyone to claim nobility, while Lord Dudley noted property had not been organized, accelerating the aristocracy’s decline.

Joseph Bridau observed that the beauty of womanhood had diminished and the great lady’s fan was broken. Daniel d’Arthez said the aristocracy had retreated, leaving behind women who might otherwise have shaped society.

The discussion turned to the modern “perfect lady,” a product of the era, embodying good taste and grace but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questioned the notion of progress, while Madame de Nucingen explained that a woman’s worth was no longer tied to lineage but to character and presentation.

Count Adam Laginski asked what defined a “perfect lady.” Émile Blondet described her as exuding elegance and refinement, effortlessly commanding respect and admiration. He painted a vivid picture of her bearing and demeanor, emphasizing grace and poise in various social settings.

The dialogue highlighted modern femininity’s complexities: the “perfect lady” navigates societal expectations with skill and charm, blending beauty and intellect to captivate those around her. Guests considered whether this new type of woman compensated for the vanished greatness of earlier aristocratic women, or merely reflected a reshaped society where merit and presentation replaced inherited splendour.

“You will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet remarked gravely. “In contrast, even if you know a lady is married, she will skillfully conceal her husband, making him hard to find. Often you only notice her looking at a middle-aged man with a decoration who bows and departs. She has ordered her carriage and leaves.

“You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose, and you go to bed beneath a golden canopy of a delightful dream, which may linger even after Sleep has opened the ivory gates.

“At home, the lady sees no one before four; she is shrewd enough to keep you waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; her luxury is for daily use and regularly renewed. You will find no dusty glass shades or remnants of wrappings. The staircase is warm, flowers abound—her only accepted gift and only from chosen people, since they symbolize a promise. Fashionable trinkets lie about, but not like museum pieces. She sits by the fire in a low chair, not rising to greet you. Her conversation at home differs from that at the ball; there, she was our creditor; here, she owes you the pleasure of her wit. These are nuances she masters. What she seeks is a man to widen her circle, an object for her attentions. To draw you into her drawing-room, she will be charming. This shows how isolated women are today and why they want a little world of their own where they can shine like a constellation. Conversation there often relies on generalities.

“Yes,” de Marsay agreed, “you have caught the flaw of our age. The epigram—a volume in a word—no longer strikes at people or things but at sordid events, fading quickly.”

“Hence,” Blondet continued, “a lady’s intelligence, if she has any, lies in doubting everything. Here is the difference between two women; the townswoman is virtuous, while the lady hesitates and contends with her virtue. This uncertainty is one of the last graces left to her by our dreadful times. She rarely goes to church yet will discuss religion; if you express free thought, she will try to convert you, sliding into the familiar phrases and gestures of such women: ‘For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering, and you deprive it of its support. Religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children! Let us not be selfish! Individualism is the disease of the age, and religion is the only remedy; it unites families which your laws put asunder,’ and so on. She then drifts into a neo-Christian discourse laced with political notions—neither Catholic nor Protestant—but moral? Oh, very moral!—a muddle of modern doctrines at odds with each other.”

The women laughed at Blondet’s satire.

“This explanation, dear Count Adam,” Blondet said to the Pole, “shows that the ‘perfect lady’ embodies both intellectual and political confusion, surrounded by flashy yet fleeting products of an industry that replaces its work with something new. When you leave her, you think: She certainly has superior ideas! You believe it more because she has delicately probed your heart and asked your secrets; she feigns ignorance to learn everything. There are things she never knows, even when she does. You alone feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart. The great ladies of old flaunted their affairs; today’s lady arranges her passions like a musical score, with notes and rests, pauses and sharps. A merely weak woman is anxious not to compromise her love, her husband, or her children’s future. Name, position, and fortune no longer shield her from scrutiny. The aristocracy no longer protects her. Unlike the great lady of the past, she lacks lofty antagonism; she cannot crush anything; rather, she would be crushed. Thus she masters Jesuitical compromises, moving between propriety and anonymous passion. She fears her servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. This woman—so free at a ball, so charming in a walk—is a slave at home; she is never independent except in privacy or theoretically. Her task is to preserve her position as a lady.

“For in our day, a woman repudiated by her husband, reduced to a meager allowance, without a carriage, luxury, or opera box, is neither wife nor maid nor townswoman; she is adrift, a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover still want her? That is the question. Thus, your perfect lady may give rise to calumny, but never to slander.”

“It is all so horribly true,” said the Princesse de Cadignan.

“And so,” Blondet continued, “our ‘perfect lady’ exists between English hypocrisy and the frankness of the eighteenth century—a bastard system symptomatic of an age where nothing that emerges resembles what has vanished, where transition leads nowhere, and everything is a matter of degree; all great figures fade into the background, and distinction becomes purely personal. I am convinced it is impossible for a woman, even if born near a throne, to acquire before age twenty-five the encyclopedic knowledge of trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the important small things, the musical tones of coloring, the angelic mischief and innocent cunning, the speech and silence, the seriousness and banter, the wit and obtuseness, the diplomacy and ignorance that make up the perfect lady.”

“And where, according to your sketch,” Mademoiselle des Touches asked Blondet, “would you classify the female author? Is she a perfect lady, a woman comme il faut?”

“When she has no genius, she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas,” Blondet replied, emphasizing the phrase with a glance that might be praise for Camille Maupin. “This epigram is not mine, but Napoleon’s,” he added.

“You need not hold a grudge against Napoleon for that,” Canalis interjected. “He was weak in being jealous of literary genius—he had his flaws. Who can ever explain or depict Napoleon? A man with arms folded who did everything, the greatest force known, the most concentrated, the most caustic; a singular genius who spread armed civilization without fixing it anywhere; a man who could do everything because he willed everything; a prodigious phenomenon of will, conquering illness through battle, yet destined to die of disease in bed after living amid balls and bullets; a man with a code and a sword in his brain, word and deed; a clear-sighted spirit that foreseen everything but his own fall; a capricious politician who risked men for economy yet spared three heads—Talleyrand, Pozzo de Borgo, and Metternich—whose deaths might have saved the Empire; a man to whom nature, as a rare privilege, gave a heart in a bronze frame; mirthful and kind at midnight among women, and the next morning manipulating Europe as a young girl might splash in her bath! Hypocritical and generous; loving tawdriness and simplicity; devoid of taste yet protecting the arts; and despite these contradictions, truly great by instinct or temperament; Caesar at twenty-five, Cromwell at thirty; and then, like my grocer buried in Père Lachaise, a good husband and father. In short, he improvised public works, empires, kings, codes, verses, a romance—with more range than precision. Did he not aim to make all Europe France? After making us weigh on the earth in such a way as to change the laws of gravitation, he left us poorer; he who had taken an empire by his name lost his name on the frontier of his empire in a sea of blood and soldiers. A man of thought and action, who understood Desaix and Fouché.”

“All despotism and all justice at the right moments. The true king!” de Marsay declared.

“Ah! what a pleasure it is to digest what you talk about,” Baron de Nucingen remarked.

“But do you think the treat we are giving you is common?” Joseph Bridau asked. “If you had to pay for the charms of conversation as you do for those of dancing or music, your fortune would be inadequate! There is no second performance of the same flash of wit.”

“And are we really so much diminished as these gentlemen think?” the Princesse de Cadignan asked, smiling skeptically at the women. “In these days, under a regime that makes everything small, you prefer small dishes, small rooms, small pictures, small articles, small newspapers, small books—does that prove women have grown smaller? Why should the human heart change because you change your coat? In all ages, passions remain the same. I know of beautiful devotion and sublime sufferings that lack the publicity—that glory, if you will—that once gave luster to the errors of some women. But even if one has not saved a King of France, one is not less an Agnès Sorel. Do you believe our Marquise d’Espard is not the equal of Madame Doublet or Madame du Deffant, in whose rooms so much evil was spoken and done? Is not Taglioni a match for Camargo? Or Malibran the equal of Saint-Huberti? Are not our poets superior to those of the eighteenth century? If, at this moment, due to the Grocers who govern us, we lack a style of our own, did not the Empire have its distinguishing stamp as the age of Louis XV. did, and was not its splendor fabulous? Have the sciences lost anything?”

“I completely agree, madame; the women of this age are truly great,” the Comte de Vandenesse replied. “When posterity looks back, will not Madame Récamier appear in proportions as fine as the most beautiful women of the past? We have made so much history that historians will be lacking. The age of Louis XIV had but one Madame de Sévigné; we have a thousand now in Paris who certainly write better than she did and do not publish their letters. Whether the Frenchwoman is called ‘perfect lady’ or great lady, she will always be the woman among women.

“Émile Blondet has painted the fascinations of a woman of the day; but, when needed, this creature who bristles or preens, who echoes the ideas of this or that man, can be heroic. And it must be said, your faults, mesdames, are all the more poetic because they are surrounded by greater perils. I have seen much of the world—perhaps too late; but where the illegality of your feelings might be excused, I have always observed the effects of what you may call Providence, inevitably overwhelming those we consider light women.”

“I hope,” Madame de Vandenesse said, “that we can be great in other ways——”

“Oh, let the Comte de Vandenesse preach to us!” Madame de Serizy exclaimed.

“With all the more reason because he has preached a great deal by example,” the Baronne de Nucingen added.

“On my honor!” General de Montriveau said, “in all the dramas—a word you are very fond of,” he said, glancing at Blondet—“in which the finger of God has been visible, the most frightful I ever knew was almost by my act——”

“Well, tell us all about it!” Lady Barimore cried. “I love to shudder!”

“It is the taste of a virtuous woman,” de Marsay replied, casting an eye at Lord Dudley’s lovely daughter.

“During the campaign of 1812,” General de Montriveau began, “I was the involuntary cause of a terrible disaster which may be of use to you, Doctor Bianchon,” turning to me, “since, while you devote yourself to the body, you concern yourself much with the mind; it may help solve some problems of the will.

“I was on my second campaign; I loved danger and laughed at everything, like the young and foolish lieutenant of artillery I was. When we reached the Beresina, the army had lost all discipline. It was a medley of men of many nations, instinctively making their way from north to south. Soldiers would drive a ragged, barefoot general away from their fire if he brought neither wood nor food. After crossing this river, disorder did not lessen. I had come quietly and alone, without food, from the marshes of Zembin, wandering in search of a house where I might be taken in. Finding none or being driven away, I finally saw a wretched Polish farm, like the wooden houses of Lower Normandy or poorest Beauce farms. These dwellings consist of a single room, one end divided by a partition as a forage store-room.

“In the twilight I saw faint smoke above the house. Hoping to find comrades more compassionate than those I had met, I approached. Inside, the table was laid. Several officers, with a woman—a common sight—were eating potatoes, some horseflesh broiled over charcoal, and frozen beetroots. I recognized artillery captains from my first regiment. I was welcomed with shouts, which would have amazed me on the other side of the Beresina; but at that moment the cold was less intense; my comrades were warm, they had food, and the straw-strewn floor promised a night’s rest. We did not ask for much then. My comrades could be philanthropists for free—one of the commonest ways of being philanthropic. I sat to eat on a straw bundle.

“At the end of the table, by the door to the hay-filled division, sat my old colonel, one of the most extraordinary men I ever met among the mixed collection of men I knew. He was Italian. Whenever human nature is truly fine in the South, it is sublime. I do not know if you have noticed the extreme fairness of Italians when fair. It is exquisite under artificial light. When I read the fantastical portrait of Colonel Oudet by Charles Nodier, I found my own sensations in Nodier’s elegant phrases. Italian, as were most officers of his regiment, borrowed from Eugene’s army, my colonel was tall, at least eight or nine inches above average, admirably proportioned—perhaps a little stout but prodigiously powerful, active, and clean-limbed like a greyhound. His abundant black curls contrasted with his complexion, as white as a woman’s; he had small hands, a shapely foot, a pleasant mouth, and an aquiline nose that would pinch and turn white when he was angry, which happened often. His irascibility was beyond belief; no one could remain calm in his presence. I alone perhaps was not afraid of him; he had such a singular fancy for me that he thought everything I did was right. When angry, his brow would knit, and the muscles of his forehead form a horseshoe; this mark was perhaps even more terrifying than the magnetic flashes of his blue eyes. His whole frame quivered, and his strength, great in repose, became almost unbounded.

“He spoke with a strong guttural roll. His voice, as powerful as Nodier’s Oudet’s, put incredible fullness into the syllable or consonant where this burr sounded. Though this faulty pronunciation could sometimes charm, when commanding his men or excited, you cannot imagine, unless you heard it, the force this accent expressed. When the Colonel was calm, his blue eyes were angelically sweet and his smooth brow had a charming look. On parade or with the army of Italy, no man could compare with him. Indeed, d’Orsay himself was eclipsed by our colonel at the last review Napoleon held before the invasion of Russia.

“Everything about this exceptional man was contrast. Passion thrives on contrast. Thus, you need not ask whether he exerted over women the irresistible influence to which our nature yields”—and the general looked at the Princesse de Cadignan—“as vitreous matter is molded under the pipe of the glass-blower; yet, by singular fatality—an observer might explain—the Colonel was neither a lady-killer nor indifferent to such successes.

“To illustrate his violence, I will tell of a brief incident. We were dragging our guns up a narrow road bordered by a high slope and thickets. Halfway up, we met another artillery regiment, its colonel at the head. That colonel wanted our captain to yield, but the captain refused. The other colonel ordered his foremost battery forward; despite the driver’s effort to keep among the scrub, the wheel of the first gun struck our captain’s right leg, breaking it and throwing him over on the near side of his horse. It happened in an instant. Our Colonel, nearby, sensed a quarrel; he galloped up, riding among the guns at the risk of falling, and reached the spot calling ‘Help!’ No, our Italian colonel was no longer human! Foam like champagne rose to his lips; he roared like a lion. Incapable even of a word or cry, he made a terrifying signal, pointing to the wood and drawing his sword. The two colonels stepped aside. In two seconds, we saw his opponent stretched on the ground, his skull split in two. The soldiers of that regiment backed away—yes, quickly.

“The captain, nearly crushed and yelping in the puddle where the gun carriage had thrown him, had an Italian wife, a beautiful Sicilian from Messina, who was not indifferent to our Colonel. This aggravated his rage. He felt pledged to protect the husband, bound to defend him as he would the woman herself.

“Now, in the hovel beyond Zembin where I was so well received, this captain sat opposite me, and his wife was at the other end facing the Colonel. This Sicilian was a little woman named Rosina, very dark, with the Southern sun’s fire in her black almond-shaped eyes. At this moment she was terribly thin; her face was dusted like fruit exposed on a highway. Scarcely clothed in rags, exhausted by marches, her hair disheveled under a shawl, she still had a woman’s graces; her movements were engaging, her small rose mouth and white teeth, the outline of her features and figure, charms which misery, cold, and neglect had not entirely erased. Rosina’s frame seemed fragile but was wiry and springy. Her husband, a gentleman from Piedmont, had an ironical simplicity in his face that somehow betrayed cruelty beneath phlegm and indolence. He seemed unaware of the connections between his wife and the Colonel for three years. I attributed this to Italian manners or some domestic secret; yet one feature in his countenance filled me with distrust—his thin, restless under lip turned down at the corners, which I thought revealed a streak of cruelty.

“The conversation was not lively when I arrived. My weary comrades ate in silence; they asked questions, shared misadventures, mixed with reflections on the campaign, generals, Russians, and the cold. A minute after I arrived, the colonel, having finished his meager meal, wiped his mustache, bade us goodnight, shot a black look at the Italian woman, saying, ‘Rosina?’ and without waiting, went into the little barn full of hay to sleep. The meaning of the Colonel’s call was clear. The young wife responded with an indescribable gesture, expressing all the annoyance she could not feel at seeing her thralldom flaunted without decency, and the offense to her dignity as a woman and to her husband. Yet there was, in her rigid features and knitted brows, a sort of presentiment; perhaps she foresaw her fate. Rosina remained quietly in her place.

“A minute later, apparently when the Colonel was settled in straw, he repeated, ‘Rosina?’

“The tone of this second call was even more brutally demanding. Rosina turned pale, rose, passed behind us, and went to the Colonel.

“All sat in utter silence; I, unfortunately, after looking at them, began to laugh, and then they all laughed too. ‘Tu ridi?—you laugh?’ said the husband.

“‘On my honor, old comrade,’ I replied, becoming serious again, ‘I confess I was wrong; I ask your pardon a thousand times, and if you are not satisfied with my apologies, I am ready to give you satisfaction.’

“‘Oh! it is not you who are wrong, it is I!’ he replied coldly.

“Thereupon we lay down in the room, and before long, all were sound asleep.

“Next morning, each one, without waking his neighbor or seeking companionship, set out again on his way, with that selfishness which made our rout one of the most horrible dramas of self-seeking, melancholy, and horror ever enacted under heaven. Nevertheless, about seven or eight hundred paces from our shelter, most of us met again and walked on together, like geese led in flocks by a child’s wilful tyranny. The same necessity urged us all.

“Having reached a knoll where we could still see the farmhouse, we heard sounds like the roar of lions, the bellowing of bulls—no, a cry beyond comparison. Mingled with this ominous roar, we heard a woman’s feeble scream. We all looked around, seized by terror; we no longer saw the house, but a huge bonfire. The farmhouse had been barricaded and was in flames. Swirls of smoke carried hoarse cries and an indescribable pungent smell. A few yards behind, the captain was quietly approaching to join our caravan; we gazed at him in silence, for no one dared question him; but he, understanding our curiosity, pointed to his breast with his right forefinger and, waving his left toward the fire, said, ‘Son’io.’

“We all walked on without a word to him.”

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked.

“It would be frightful to leave with this horrible picture in our memory,” Madame de Montcornet said. “I shall dream of it——”

“And what was the punishment of Monsieur de Marsay’s ‘First’?” Lord Dudley asked, smiling.

“When the English jest, their foils have the buttons on,” Blondet replied.

“Monsieur Bianchon can tell us, for he saw her dying,” de Marsay said, turning to me.

“Yes,” I replied; “and her end was one of the most beautiful I ever witnessed. The Duke and I had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow; pulmonary consumption, in its last stage, left no hope; she had taken the sacrament the day before. The Duke had fallen asleep. The Duchess, waking around four in the morning, signed to me with a friendly smile to leave him to rest, while she was about to die. She had become incredibly thin, but her face retained its sublime outline and features. Her pallor made her skin look like porcelain with a light within. Her bright eyes contrasted with this languidly elegant complexion, and her face was full of expressive calm. She seemed to pity the Duke, and this feeling sprang from a lofty tenderness that, as death approached, seemed boundless. The silence was absolute. The room, softly lit by a lamp, resembled every sickroom at the hour of death.

“At this moment the clock struck. The Duke awoke, despairing at having fallen asleep. I did not see the impatient gesture he made, expressing regret for having lost sight of his wife for a few of the last granted moments; but it is certain anyone but the dying woman might have misunderstood it. A busy statesman, always thinking of France’s interests, the Duke had a thousand odd surface ways which often lead a genius to be mistaken for a madman, explainable by the exacting needs of their intellect. He came to sit in an armchair by his wife’s side and looked fixedly at her. The dying woman reached out, took her husband’s hand and clasped it feebly; in a low but agitated voice she said, ‘My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?’ Then she died, looking at him.”

“The stories the doctor tells us,” the Comte de Vandenesse remarked, “always leave a deep impression.”

“But a sweet one,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, rising.
