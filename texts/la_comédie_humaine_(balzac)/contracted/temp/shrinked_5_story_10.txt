In Paris, social life divides between superficial official parties and intimate gatherings that foster genuine conversation. The latter, often hosted by women such as Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches, display authentic French wit in contrast with the cold luxury of the former.

At Mademoiselle des Touches’ graceful salon, noted for its freedom of expression, an evening assembly including the charismatic de Marsay debated how women in France had changed since the July Revolution. Lively repartee showcased the wit and intellect of Parisian society. As the night relaxed into camaraderie, guests shared personal stories; Mademoiselle des Touches insisted everyone remain at the table, fearing that moving to the drawing-room would spoil the mood.

De Marsay recounted a formative youth: his first love for an older widow, a secret passionate affair marked by jealousy and betrayal that shattered his idealism. The clandestine meetings and ultimate treachery forced him to reflect on love, trust, and the complexities of relationships, prompting broader discussion about fidelity and social expectations.

I, having recovered from unrequited love, began a new liaison with Charlotte, a charming woman whose beauty contrasted sharply with my former idol. I avoided quarrels with her; her skilled deception offered pleasures true love could not. Seeking an artist to make a keepsake from my hair, I learned he used false hair, which made me skeptical of love while still believing in pleasure.

Two months later in Charlotte's boudoir, tender moments turned tense when I asked whether she loved me. She answered with sweet lies, but when I probed about her relationship with the Duke my suspicions surfaced. Charlotte’s playful demeanor shifted to desperation: she claimed that if I had spoken sooner we might have been happy. I advised her to reconcile with God, noting the Duke awaited proof of her fidelity.

Though I felt betrayed, I found some amusement in her belief that I would be left heartbroken. I revealed I had pursued another woman; Charlotte panicked, lamenting that women could never be truly loved. I retorted that for men love was often a game.

The women present were offended by my portrayal. Lady Dudley defended Charlotte, insisting her marriage did not amount to infidelity. De Marsay lamented the erosion of social distinctions—duchesses and marquises fading—and the conversation turned to how women’s roles had changed, mourning the loss of the classical great lady.

The discussion emphasized that modern women, across classes, often seemed diminished compared to predecessors. Blondet observed bourgeois women tend to invoke their husbands, while upper-class women conceal theirs, underscoring women’s social isolation. The idealized "perfect lady" charms yet embodies hesitancy and moral ambiguity.

As debate continued over whether women had lost or retained greatness, the Princesse de Cadignan argued that despite societal change, passion and devotion endure. The conversation then shifted to a stark counterpoint: General de Montriveau recounted a disastrous 1812 campaign episode, a sobering reminder of war’s chaos that contrasted with the evening’s social talk.
