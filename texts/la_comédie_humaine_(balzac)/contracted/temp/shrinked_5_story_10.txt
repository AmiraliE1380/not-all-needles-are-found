Cured of my cold and my pure love, I embarked on an adventure with a charming heroine whose beauty sharply contrasted with that of my deceptive angel. I avoided quarreling with this clever woman, suspecting true love might not match the pleasures of her skilled deceit. Such refined hypocrisy can be as good as virtue.

When I recognized a handkerchief he had worked on, my doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections were confirmed. I left the shop still believing in pleasure but skeptical about love.

Two months later I was in the boudoir of this ethereal being, holding her hand as we explored sentiment. In a tender moment I seized the chance to elicit her most delightful lies. Charlotte professed her love, claiming I was the only man in her world and that she had sought ways to bind me to her eternally.

The women listening with de Marsay seemed offended by his portrayal of their airs. She feigned astonishment, but I pressed on, suggesting she should marry him, since he was rich and devout.

She worried about the Duke’s suspicions; I reassured her he likely had none, though she needed to prove her devotion. As she paced the boudoir her demeanor shifted: she held out her hand, called me noble and charming, and vowed never to forget me.

“Now, you will not talk for half an hour with a bourgeoise without her mentioning her husband,” Blondet remarked gravely. “In contrast, even if you know a lady is married, she will skillfully conceal her husband, making it a challenge to discover him. Often, you might only notice her gazing at a middle-aged man with a decoration who bows and leaves. She has ordered her carriage and departs.

“You are not the rose, but you have been with the rose, and you go to bed under a golden canopy of a delightful dream, which may linger even after Sleep has opened the ivory gates of dreams.

“At home, the lady sees no one before four; she is clever enough to keep you waiting. Her house is tastefully arranged; her luxury is for daily use and regularly refreshed. You will find no dusty glass shades or remnants of wrappings. The staircase is warm, and flowers abound—her only accepted gift, and only from select individuals, as they symbolize a promise. Fashionable trinkets are scattered about, but not in a way that resembles a museum. She will be seated by the fire in a low chair, not rising to greet you. Her conversation at home differs from that at the ball; there, she was our creditor; here, she owes you the pleasure of her wit. These are the nuances of which she is a master. What she seeks in you is a man to expand her circle, an object for her attentions. To draw you into her drawing-room, she will be enchantingly charming. This highlights how isolated women are today and why they desire a little world of their own, where they can shine like a constellation. Conversation often relies on generalities.

“Yes,” de Marsay agreed, “you have pinpointed the flaw of our age. The epigram—a volume in a word—no longer strikes at people or things but at sordid events, fading quickly.”

“Hence,” Blondet continued, “a lady’s intelligence, if she possesses any, lies in casting doubt on everything. Herein lies the difference between two women; the townswoman is virtuous, while the lady hesitates and struggles with her virtue. This uncertainty is one of the last graces left to her by our dreadful times. She rarely attends church but will discuss religion; if you express free thought, she will attempt to convert you, leading to the familiar phrases and gestures understood by these women: ‘For shame! I thought you had too much sense to attack religion. Society is tottering, and you deprive it of its support. Religion means you and me; it is property and the future of our children! Let us not be selfish! Individualism is the disease of the age, and religion is the only remedy; it unites families which your laws put asunder,’ and so forth. She then delves into a neo-Christian discourse laced with political notions that is neither Catholic nor Protestant—but moral? Oh, very moral!—a blend of modern doctrines at odds with one another.”

The women laughed at Blondet’s satirical portrayal.

“This explanation, dear Count Adam,” Blondet said to the Pole, “will show you that the ‘perfect lady’ embodies both intellectual and political muddle, surrounded by the flashy yet fleeting products of an industry that aims to destroy its work to replace it with something new. When you leave her, you think: She certainly has superior ideas! You believe it because she has delicately probed your heart and asked for your secrets; she feigns ignorance to learn everything. There are things she never knows, even when she does. You alone will feel uneasy, knowing nothing of her heart’s state. The great ladies of old flaunted their affairs, while today’s lady has her passions neatly arranged like a musical score, with its notes and rests, its pauses and sharps. A mere weak woman, she is anxious not to compromise her love, her husband, or her children’s future. Name, position, and fortune no longer shield her from scrutiny. The aristocracy no longer stands united to protect her. Unlike the great lady of the past, she lacks the demeanor of lofty antagonism; she can crush nothing underfoot; it is she who would be crushed. Thus, she is adept at Jesuitical compromises, navigating between propriety and anonymous passions. She fears her servants as an Englishwoman fears a divorce trial. This woman—so free at a ball, so charming while walking—is a slave at home; she is never independent except in perfect privacy or theoretically. Her task is to maintain her position as a lady.

“For in our day, a woman repudiated by her husband, reduced to a meager allowance, without a carriage, luxury, or opera box, is no longer a wife, maid, or townswoman; she is adrift, becoming a chattel. The Carmelites will not accept a married woman; it would be bigamy. Would her lover still want her? That is the question. Thus, your perfect lady may give rise to calumny, but never to slander.”

“It is all so horribly true,” said the Princesse de Cadignan.

“And so,” Blondet continued, “our ‘perfect lady’ exists between English hypocrisy and the frankness of the eighteenth century—a bastard system, symptomatic of an age where nothing that emerges resembles what has vanished, where transition leads nowhere, and everything is a matter of degree; all great figures fade into the background, and distinction becomes purely personal. I am convinced that it is impossible for a woman, even if born close to a throne, to acquire before the age of twenty-five the encyclopedic knowledge of trifles, the practice of maneuvering, the important small things, the musical tones and harmony of coloring, the angelic mischief and innocent cunning, the speech and silence, the seriousness and banter, the wit and obtuseness, the diplomacy and ignorance that make up the perfect lady.”

“And where, according to your sketch,” Mademoiselle des Touches asked Blondet, “would you classify the female author? Is she a perfect lady, a woman comme il faut?”

“When she has no genius, she is a woman comme il n’en faut pas,” Blondet replied, emphasizing the words with a glance that could be read as praise for Camille Maupin. “This epigram is not mine, but Napoleon’s,” he added.

“You need not hold a grudge against Napoleon for that,” Canalis interjected emphatically. “He was weak in being jealous of literary genius—he had his flaws. Who can ever explain, depict, or understand Napoleon? A man with arms folded, who did everything, the greatest force known, the most concentrated, the most caustic; a singular genius who spread armed civilization without fixing it anywhere; a man who could do everything because he willed everything; a prodigious phenomenon of will, conquering illness through battle, yet destined to die of disease in bed after living amid balls and bullets; a man with a code and a sword in his brain, word and deed; a clear-sighted spirit that foresaw everything but his own fall; a capricious politician who risked men by handfuls for economy, yet spared three heads—those of Talleyrand, Pozzo de Borgo, and Metternich, diplomats whose deaths would have saved the French Empire, and who seemed to him of greater weight than thousands of soldiers; a man to whom nature, as a rare privilege, gave a heart in a bronze frame; mirthful and kind at midnight among women, and the next morning manipulating Europe as a young girl might amuse herself by splashing water in her bath! Hypocritical and generous; loving tawdriness and simplicity; devoid of taste, yet protecting the arts; and despite these contradictions, truly great in everything by instinct or temperament; Caesar at twenty-five, Cromwell at thirty; and then, like my grocer buried in Père Lachaise, a good husband and father. In short, he improvised public works, empires, kings, codes, verses, a romance—and all with more range than precision. Did he not aim to make all Europe France? And after making us weigh on the earth in such a way as to change the laws of gravitation, he left us poorer than on the day he first laid hands on us; while he, who had taken an empire by his name, lost his name on the frontier of his empire in a sea of blood and soldiers. A man of thought and action, who understood Desaix and Fouché.”

“All despotism and all justice at the right moments. The true king!” de Marsay declared.

“Ah! what a pleasure it is to digest what you talk about,” Baron de Nucingen remarked.

“But do you think the treat we are giving you is a common one?” Joseph Bridau asked. “If you had to pay for the charms of conversation as you do for those of dancing or music, your fortune would be inadequate! There is no second performance of the same flash of wit.”

“And are we really so much diminished as these gentlemen think?” the Princesse de Cadignan asked, smiling skeptically at the women. “Because, in these days, under a regime that makes everything small, you prefer small dishes, small rooms, small pictures, small articles, small newspapers, small books, does that prove that women have grown smaller? Why should the human heart change because you change your coat? In all ages, passions remain the same. I know of beautiful devotion and sublime sufferings that lack the publicity—the glory, if you will—that once gave luster to the errors of some women. But even if one has not saved a King of France, one is not less an Agnès Sorel. Do you believe our dear Marquise d’Espard is not the equal of Madame Doublet or Madame du Deffant, in whose rooms so much evil was spoken and done? Is not Taglioni a match for Camargo? Or Malibran the equal of Saint-Huberti? Are not our poets superior to those of the eighteenth century? If at this moment, due to the Grocers who govern us, we lack a style of our own, did not the Empire have its distinguishing stamp as the age of Louis XV. did, and was not its splendor fabulous? Have the sciences lost anything?”

“I completely agree, madame; the women of this age are truly great,” the Comte de Vandenesse replied. “When posterity looks back, will not Madame Récamier appear in proportions as fine as those of the most beautiful women of the past? We have made so much history that historians will be lacking. The age of Louis XIV. had but one Madame de Sévigné; we have a thousand now in Paris who certainly write better than she did and do not publish their letters. Whether the Frenchwoman is called ‘perfect lady’ or great lady, she will always be the woman among women.

“Émile Blondet has painted a picture of the fascinations of a woman of the day; but, when needed, this creature who bristles or shows off, who echoes the ideas of Mr. This and Mr. That, could be heroic. And it must be said, your faults, mesdames, are all the more poetic because they are always surrounded by greater perils. I have seen much of the world, perhaps too late; but in cases where the illegality of your feelings might be excused, I have always observed the effects of what you may call Providence, inevitably overwhelming those we consider light women.”

“I hope,” Madame de Vandenesse said, “that we can be great in other ways——”

“Oh, let the Comte de Vandenesse preach to us!” Madame de Serizy exclaimed.

“With all the more reason because he has preached a great deal by example,” the Baronne de Nucingen added.

“On my honor!” General de Montriveau said, “in all the dramas—a word you are very fond of,” he said, looking at Blondet—“in which the finger of God has been visible, the most frightful I ever knew was very near being by my act——”

“Well, tell us all about it!” Lady Barimore cried. “I love to shudder!”

“It is the taste of a virtuous woman,” de Marsay replied, glancing at Lord Dudley’s lovely daughter.

“During the campaign of 1812,” General de Montriveau began, “I was the involuntary cause of a terrible disaster which may be of use to you, Doctor Bianchon,” turning to me, “since, while you devote yourself to the human body, you concern yourself a good deal with the mind; it may help solve some problems of the will.

“I was going through my second campaign; I enjoyed danger and laughed at everything, like the young and foolish lieutenant of artillery that I was. When we reached the Beresina, the army had lost all discipline and forgotten military obedience. It was a medley of men of all nations, instinctively making their way from north to south. Soldiers would drive a general in rags and barefoot away from their fire if he brought neither wood nor food. After crossing this famous river, disorder did not diminish. I had come quietly and alone, without food, out of the marshes of Zembin, wandering in search of a house where I might be taken in. Finding none or being driven away from those I encountered, I finally spotted a wretched little Polish farm, which you can only imagine if you have seen the wooden houses of Lower Normandy or the poorest farm buildings of la Beauce. These dwellings consist of a single room, with one end divided off by a wooden partition, the smaller division serving as a store-room for forage.

“In the twilight, I could see faint smoke rising above this house. Hoping to find comrades more compassionate than those I had previously encountered, I boldly approached the farm. Inside, I found the table laid. Several officers, along with a woman—a common sight—were eating potatoes, some horseflesh broiled over charcoal, and frozen beetroots. I recognized a few artillery captains from my first regiment. I was welcomed with a shout of acclamation, which would have amazed me on the other side of the Beresina; but at this moment, the cold was less intense; my fellow-officers were warm, they had food, and the room, strewn with straw, promised a delightful night. We did not ask for much in those days. My comrades could be philanthropists for free—one of the commonest ways of being philanthropic. I sat down to eat on one of the straw bundles.

“At the end of the table, by the door leading to the smaller room full of straw, sat my old colonel, one of the most extraordinary men I ever met among the mixed collection of men I encountered. He was Italian. Whenever human nature is truly fine in the South, it is sublime. I do not know if you have noticed the extreme fairness of Italians when they are fair. It is exquisite, especially under artificial light. When I read the fantastical portrait of Colonel Oudet sketched by Charles Nodier, I found my own sensations in every one of his elegant phrases. Italian, as were most of the officers of his regiment, which had been borrowed by the Emperor from Eugene’s army, my colonel was tall, eight or nine inches above average, admirably proportioned—perhaps a little stout, but prodigiously powerful, active, and clean-limbed like a greyhound. His abundant black curls contrasted with his complexion, as white as a woman’s; he had small hands, a shapely foot, a pleasant mouth, and a delicately formed aquiline nose, which would pinch and turn white when he was angry, which happened often. His irascibility was beyond belief; I will not elaborate. No one could remain calm in his presence. I alone, perhaps, was not afraid of him; he had taken such a singular fancy to me that he thought everything I did was right. When he was angry, his brow would knit, and the muscles of his forehead would set in a delta, or, to be more explicit, in Redgauntlet’s horseshoe. This mark was perhaps even more terrifying than the magnetic flashes of his blue eyes. His whole frame quivered, and his strength, great as it was in his normal state, became almost unbounded.

“He spoke with a strong guttural roll. His voice, at least as powerful as that of Charles Nodier’s Oudet, conveyed an incredible fullness of tone into the syllable or consonant in which this burr was sounded. Though this faulty pronunciation could sometimes be a grace, when commanding his men or when excited, you cannot imagine, unless you had heard it, the force expressed by this accent, which is so common in Paris. When the Colonel was calm, his blue eyes were angelically sweet, and his smooth brow had a charming expression. On parade, or with the army of Italy, no man could compare with him. Indeed, d’Orsay himself, the handsome d’Orsay, was eclipsed by our colonel during the last review held by Napoleon before the invasion of Russia.

“Everything about this exceptional man was in contrast. Passion thrives on contrast. Thus, you need not ask whether he exerted over women the irresistible influences to which our nature yields”—and the general looked at the Princesse de Cadignan—“as vitreous matter is molded under the pipe of the glass-blower; still, by a singular fatality—an observer might explain the phenomenon—the Colonel was not a lady-killer or indifferent to such successes.

“To illustrate his violence, I will recount a brief incident. We were dragging our guns up a narrow road bordered by a high slope on one side and thickets on the other. Halfway up, we encountered another regiment of artillery, its colonel marching at the head. This colonel wanted our captain to back down, but the captain refused. The other colonel ordered his foremost battery to advance, and despite the driver’s care to keep among the scrub, the wheel of the first gun struck our captain’s right leg, breaking it and throwing him over on the near side of his horse. All this happened in an instant. Our Colonel, who was nearby, sensed a quarrel; he galloped up, riding among the guns at the risk of falling with his horse’s four feet in the air, and reached the spot just as the captain fell, calling out ‘Help!’ No, our Italian colonel was no longer human! Foam like champagne rose to his lips; he roared inarticulately like a lion. Incapable of uttering a word or even a cry, he made a terrifying signal to his opponent, pointing to the wood and drawing his sword. The two colonels stepped aside. In two seconds, we saw our Colonel’s opponent stretched on the ground, his skull split in two. The soldiers of his regiment backed away—yes, by heaven, and quickly too.

“The captain, who had nearly been crushed and lay yelping in the puddle where the gun carriage had thrown him, had an Italian wife, a beautiful Sicilian from Messina, who was not indifferent to our Colonel. This circumstance aggravated his rage. He was pledged to protect the husband, bound to defend him as he would have defended the woman herself.

“Now, in the hovel beyond Zembin, where I was so well received, this captain was sitting opposite me, and his wife was at the other end of the table, facing the Colonel. This Sicilian was a little woman named Rosina, very dark, but with all the fire of the Southern sun in her black almond-shaped eyes. At this moment, she was terribly thin; her face was covered with dust, like fruit exposed to the drought of a highroad. Scarcely clothed in rags, exhausted by marches, her hair disheveled and clinging together under a piece of shawl tied over her head, she still possessed the graces of a woman; her movements were engaging, her small rose mouth and white teeth, the outline of her features and figure, charms which misery, cold, and neglect had not entirely defaced, still suggested love to any man who could think of a woman. Rosina had one of those frames that appear fragile but are wiry and full of spring. Her husband, a gentleman from Piedmont, had a face expressive of ironical simplicity, if it is permissible to combine those two words. Brave and well-informed, he seemed unaware of the connections that had existed between his wife and the Colonel for three years. I attributed this unconcern to Italian manners or some domestic secret; yet there was one feature in the man’s countenance that filled me with involuntary distrust. His thin, restless under lip turned down at the corners instead of up, which I thought betrayed a streak of cruelty in a character that seemed phlegmatic and indolent.

“As you can imagine, the conversation was not very lively when I arrived. My weary comrades ate in silence; they asked me some questions, and we shared our misadventures, mingled with reflections on the campaign, the generals, their mistakes, the Russians, and the cold. A minute after my arrival, the colonel, having finished his meager meal, wiped his mustache, bid us goodnight, shot a black look at the Italian woman, saying, ‘Rosina?’ and then, without waiting for a reply, went into the little barn full of hay to sleep. The meaning of the Colonel’s utterance was clear. The young wife responded with an indescribable gesture, expressing all the annoyance she could not feel at seeing her thralldom flaunted without human decency, and the offense to her dignity as a woman and to her husband. But there was also, in the rigid setting of her features and the tight knitting of her brows, a sort of presentiment; perhaps she foresaw her fate. Rosina remained quietly in her place.

“A minute later, and apparently when the Colonel was snug in his straw or hay bed, he repeated, ‘Rosina?’

“The tone of this second call was even more brutally demanding than the first. Rosina turned pale, but she rose, passed behind us, and went to the Colonel.

“All the party sat in utter silence; I, unfortunately, after looking at them all, began to laugh, and then they all laughed too. ‘Tu ridi?—you laugh?’ said the husband.

“‘On my honor, old comrade,’ I replied, becoming serious again, ‘I confess that I was wrong; I ask your pardon a thousand times, and if you are not satisfied with my apologies, I am ready to give you satisfaction.’

“‘Oh! it is not you who are wrong, it is I!’ he replied coldly.

“Thereupon we all lay down in the room, and before long, all were sound asleep.

“Next morning, each one, without waking his neighbor or seeking companionship, set out again on his way, with that selfishness which made our rout one of the most horrible dramas of self-seeking, melancholy, and horror ever enacted under heaven. Nevertheless, about seven or eight hundred paces from our shelter, we most of us met again and walked on together, like geese led in flocks by a child’s wilful tyranny. The same necessity urged us all.

“Having reached a knoll where we could still see the farmhouse where we had spent the night, we heard sounds resembling the roar of lions in the desert, the bellowing of bulls—no, it was a noise that can be compared to no known cry. Yet, mingling with this horrible and ominous roar, we could hear a woman’s feeble scream. We all looked around, seized by an impulse of terror; we no longer saw the house, but a huge bonfire. The farmhouse had been barricaded and was in flames. Swirls of smoke carried hoarse cries and an indescribable pungent smell. A few yards behind, the captain was quietly approaching to join our caravan; we gazed at him in silence, for no one dared question him; but he, understanding our curiosity, pointed to his breast with the forefinger of his right hand, and, waving his left in the direction of the fire, said, ‘Son’io.’

“We all walked on without saying a word to him.”

“There is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” de Marsay remarked.

“It would be frightful to let us leave with this horrible picture in our memory,” Madame de Montcornet said. “I shall dream of it——”

“And what was the punishment of Monsieur de Marsay’s ‘First’?” Lord Dudley asked, smiling.

“When the English jest, their foils have the buttons on,” Blondet replied.

“Monsieur Bianchon can tell us, for he saw her dying,” de Marsay said, turning to me.

“Yes,” I replied; “and her end was one of the most beautiful I ever witnessed. The Duke and I had spent the night by the dying woman’s pillow; pulmonary consumption, in its last stage, left no hope; she had taken the sacrament the day before. The Duke had fallen asleep. The Duchess, waking around four in the morning, signed to me in the most touching way, with a friendly smile, to leave him to rest, while she was about to die. She had become incredibly thin, but her face retained its sublime outline and features. Her pallor made her skin look like porcelain with a light within. Her bright eyes and color contrasted with this languidly elegant complexion, and her countenance was full of expressive calm. She seemed to pity the Duke, and this feeling stemmed from a lofty tenderness that, as death approached, seemed boundless. The silence was absolute. The room, softly lit by a lamp, resembled every sickroom at the hour of death.

“At this moment, the clock struck. The Duke awoke, despairing at having fallen asleep. I did not see the gesture of impatience he made, expressing regret for having lost sight of his wife for a few of the last moments granted to him; but it is certain that anyone but the dying woman might have misunderstood it. A busy statesman, always thinking of France’s interests, the Duke had a thousand odd ways on the surface, which often lead a genius to be mistaken for a madman, and whose explanation lies in the exacting needs of their intellect. He came to sit in an armchair by his wife’s side and looked fixedly at her. The dying woman reached out her hand, took her husband’s, and clasped it feebly; in a low but agitated voice, she said, ‘My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?’ Then she died, looking at him.”

“The stories the doctor tells us,” the Comte de Vandenesse remarked, “always leave a deep impression.”

“But a sweet one,” Mademoiselle des Touches said, rising.
