Some human experiences demand a delicate, almost anatomical rendering; portraits need a soul, and some truths arise only through subtle harmonies of time, circumstance, or moral inclination.

Such revelations are necessary for this simple story, intended for grave, reflective readers who live on tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, reveres the subject, the reader should share that feeling. The narrative speaks to those who know private grief—the luxury of tears, the silent pangs of memory, regret for loss, and smiles for past joys. Others may put the book down; those who have never buried a beloved will not grasp these pages. The writer assures the tale is true and worth the reader’s feeling.

In contemporary France, each speaker reshapes a person. Ask a Practical man about Madame Firmiani and he lists facts: a fine house in the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of a hundred thousand francs, and a husband once receiver-general. A Lounger praises her social standing; an Egotist dismisses her salons as trivial. A Student calls her enchanting; a Fop laments faded beauty yet admits charm. A Connoisseur praises her paintings; a Spiteful woman brands her a dangerous siren. Others—an Attache, a Distinguishing observer, a Duke, gossipy old ladies, an Original, an Observer, a Contradictor, an Envious critic—each tell a different tale.

Madame Firmiani thus emerges through many lenses, each account reflecting the observer as much as the woman, and pointing to deeper emotional truths.

In January 1824 opinions about Madame Firmiani diverged so widely that no clear portrait emerged: widow or wife, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain. The variety of interpretations showed how society distorted a reputation, like lithographic blocks producing different images from slight variations.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly magnate from Touraine, grew intrigued. Shrewd and honorable, he cherished his nephew Octave de Camps. When Octave sold an estate without consulting him and a distant relative claimed Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and was reduced to teaching mathematics, Bourbonne refused the worst and went to Paris to investigate.

Using his faubourg Saint‑Germain connections under the name de Rouxellay, he met Madame Firmiani while Octave was absent and confirmed his nephew’s distress. A former member of the King’s guard, he knew society’s manners. Though he had come with pity and anger, the salon’s atmosphere and Madame Firmiani’s presence—said to be twenty‑five but nearer twenty‑eight, married at sixteen, gifted in beauty, wealth, music, intelligence, and wit—softened him. Her acclaim had bred malicious, persistent gossip, revealing the hazards of societal suspicion and her complex character.

The former guardsman remained after the other guests left; Madame Firmiani found him still seated as the clock struck two. He announced bluntly that he was Monsieur Octave de Camps’s uncle and had heard a rumor that his nephew had ruined himself for her, living in a garret while she enjoyed wealth. Madame Firmiani, visibly affected, told him to stop: she knew the rumors and scorned money, having married without fortune. She insisted she welcomed Octave only as a friend she esteemed, and that her guardian angel kept her from dishonesty. When the uncle, trembling and almost a father to Octave, swore on his honor and asked one question—did she love Octave?—she refused to answer directly. “To confess love is the lover’s reward,” she said, implying such a declaration belonged to the lover alone, then withdrew.

The next morning Monsieur de Bourbonne found Octave in his garret and reproached him for squandering the estate the uncle meant to leave him. When he asked if Octave had ruined himself for Madame Firmiani, Octave admitted it and produced a letter she had given him. He read aloud a passage that revealed both the depth of her feeling and a grave moral test she had set before him: the letter began, “My beloved Husband,” and went on to explain that she felt dishonored by the fact that Octave’s father had unjustly deprived others of their property. She could not respect a man who knowingly kept ill-gotten gains; love for a woman implies boundless confidence and reverence, and she would love him the more if he came to her poor. She did not demand legal restitution but urged him to consult his conscience and perceive his faults himself.

The letter stunned them. Octave said it had sparked a revolution in him: haunted by conscience, every pleasure felt like theft. He rushed to Madame Firmiani; together they calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs, victims of his father’s injustice. Though his means were insufficient, Octave condemned himself to pay interest—three percent—and to make what restitution he could. Madame Firmiani, practical from having lived prudently after losing proof of her own husband’s death and will during the unrest in Greece, offered her savings and he accepted.

Octave explained he had been teaching mathematics to earn his keep rather than live on others. The act of restitution transformed him: finding the Bourgneufs wretched—a father who had been an agent, daughters keeping accounts and household, a sick mother—he felt immense joy in bringing relief, as if he were Providence. He called Madame Firmiani “my dear conscience”; her moral insistence had given him a delicacy of feeling he had lacked, and he hoped to become independent by his own industry.

As they spoke, the sound of a carriage announced Madame Firmiani. She entered somewhat annoyed to find the uncle but smiling, and explained she had come to entreat her husband humbly to accept her fortune. The Austrian Embassy had sent a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet had kept safe. Octave could accept her fortune, she said, but she added that he was richer already because of the treasures of the heart—her love—which no one could add to. She laid her head on his breast in a moment of unrestrained happiness.

Monsieur de Bourbonne, moved, reflected that whereas in his day men “made love,” in theirs they “love,” and he praised women as the best in humanity, innocent even of their faults, which come through men.
