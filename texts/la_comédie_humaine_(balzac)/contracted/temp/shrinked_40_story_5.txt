Many tales keep their charm, but some human experiences demand a delicate touch. Certain details—almost anatomical—require nuance. Portraits must carry a soul; some truths are expressed only through secret harmonies shaped by time, circumstance, or inner moral inclination.

These revelations are needed for this simple story, offered to the grave and reflective who find sustenance in tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, feels reverence for the subject, should not the reader share it? Those who have mourned a dear one, sit alone, or watch the day decline may continue; others should set this book aside. Readers who have never buried a beloved will not grasp these pages, which will seem fragrant to some and colorless to others. The reader must have known the luxury of tears and the silent pangs of memory. The writer assures this is a true story, worthy of feeling if the reader possesses it.

In contemporary France, speech reflects as many idioms as there are individuals; it is amusing to hear differing interpretations of the same person from various Parisians. Madame Firmiani, for example, is sketched in many partial but revealing ways:

- The Practical man: she is wealthy—fine house in the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of one hundred thousand francs, a husband once receiver-general.
- The Lounger: values her exclusive, respectable salon and excellent tea.
- The Egotist: dismisses such gatherings as trivial.
- The Student: finds her enchanting and eager to introduce you.
- The Fop: sees faded beauty but lingering charm.
- The Connoisseur: notes a fine collection of paintings.
- The Spiteful woman: calls her a dangerous siren.
- The Attache: remembers her beauty from ten years earlier.
- The Savvy observer: points to connections with the elite despite modest household circumstances.
- The Duke: claims not to know her, recognizing only court-presented names.
- The Ninny: wrongly insists she used to sing at the Opera.
- Two old ladies: discuss her ties to the noble Cadignan family.
- The Original: assures you her salon is safe—any scoundrels are of quality.
- Another observer: depicts a beautiful woman who rarely rises to greet guests, married to a mythical husband, admired by many but favored by none.
- The Contradictor and the Envious: spread rumor (Murat’s mistress) or reduce her wealth to coquetry that ruined a young man’s prospects.

Each portrait is partial: observers measure property, hospitality, intellect, romance, faded beauty, art, rivalry, memory, social ties, court recognition, ignorance, lineage, and rumor. Each view reveals as much about the speaker’s habits and biases as it does about Madame Firmiani. In sum, she appears complex, refracted through many lenses, inviting readers to consider these interpretations while reflecting on reverence, loss, memory, and the quiet harmonies that connect them.

In January 1824 opinion about Madame Firmiani was so divided that no clear portrait emerged: widow or wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or not, rich or poor, beautiful or plain. Such conflicting views showed how society distorts reputation, like lithographic blocks producing different images from slight variations.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly Touraine landowner, became intrigued. Practical and observant from managing an estate, he came to Paris to learn more, but gossip only deepened his confusion. A man of honor, he valued his nephew Octave de Camps and resented that Octave had sold an estate without consulting him. Worse, a relative claimed Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and was reduced to teaching mathematics—a report that alarmed Monsieur de Bourbonne.

Refusing to assume the worst, he resolved to investigate. Using connections in the faubourg Saint-Germain and assuming the name de Rouxellay, he sought the truth of Madame Firmiani’s relation to Octave. Choosing a moment when Octave would be occupied, he soon confirmed that Octave’s financial troubles were real.

De Rouxellay was no simple provincial: a former member of the King’s Guard, he knew society’s manners. Entering Madame Firmiani’s salon with the Marquise de Listomère, he lamented how a woman of such luxury could be linked to a man in such straits. Yet on seeing her his anger softened. Thought to be twenty-five but nearer twenty-eight, married at sixteen, she combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit. Admired even by rivals, her popularity invited envy and malicious gossip—transforming success into fodder for unfounded rumors and revealing a complex, intriguing nature.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############

The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had left, and Madame Firmiani found him still sitting before her; the clock struck two.

“Madame,” he began as she rose, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

She sat again, affected. Monsieur de Bourbonne, despite his experience, could not tell whether her pallor was shame or pleasure.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked.

“People in my province say my nephew has ruined himself for you, living in a garret while you enjoy wealth. Forgive my rustic frankness; you should know these rumors.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interrupted, hand raised. “I know of those rumors. You are too polite to press me if I ask you not to, and too gallant not to admit you have no right to question me. It would be absurd to defend myself. Believe me: I scorn money; if I married into wealth it was not for its sake. Octave’s fortune or poverty matters not: I welcome him as a friend, and I cannot accept anyone I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip.”

Her tone rose then softened into composed teasing.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Bourbonne said, trembling slightly, “I am almost a father to Octave. I implore your pardon for the question I will ask. I swear as a gentleman your answer will stay here.” He placed his hand on his heart. “Do you love Octave?”

“To another man I would answer with a look,” she replied. “To you, I ask what you would think of a woman who answered such a question. To confess love is an effort, a reward for him. To say it to another—” She stopped, rose, bowed, and withdrew to her private rooms, leaving the uncle bewildered.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought. “What a woman!” He left in his hired coach.

Next morning at eight Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed to Octave’s garret. The young professor, who had been up late, was astonished to see him so early.

“You rascal!” the uncle began, sitting down. “Since when is it fashionable to mock uncles with twenty-six thousand francs a year? Have I not been good to you? I’m making you an estate in Touraine. And what do you do? Sell your property and live like a lackey! Have you been gambling? Lost at the Bourse? Say ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie, I’ll sell my property and go back to my youth.”

“Uncle—” Octave began.

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” the old man continued. “She is charming. You have my approval if that helps. But have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! I’d have wagered it.” He laughed. “In my day women ruined men cleverly; this one is of another order.”

“My dear uncle, here is a letter that explains nearly everything.” Octave took a letter from the elegant portfolio Madame Firmiani had given him. “Read it and I’ll tell you the rest. You’ll know a Madame Firmiani unknown to the world.”

“I don’t have my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave read, faltering with feeling: “‘My beloved—’” The uncle interrupted, surprised by the intimacy.

“You are still intimate with her?” he asked.

“Of course. We are married.”

“Heavens!” cried the uncle. “Then why the garret?”

“Let me finish.” Octave continued: “‘My beloved Husband, you ask why I am sad. I cannot deceive you; a wife should be gay and caressing. I long to love you forever; a woman’s glory is in the man she loves. But a tale you told tarnished my joy. I feel humiliated by you—whom I thought most honorable—because of your father’s having unjustly deprived others of their property. I must have deep confidence to avow this: how can you knowingly keep such property? I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. If you had committed a crime I would hide you from justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love, for a woman, is boundless confidence plus reverence. I have never conceived of it otherwise. Come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Consult your conscience. An act of justice should not be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife, not your mistress. If I am mistaken, if you can rightly claim the property, reflect and decide from your own conscience. I know I have preached; scold me if you will.’”

“What!” the uncle exclaimed. “Surely you didn't tell her about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity,” Octave answered, and resumed reading a passage in which she insisted that ill-gotten gains, whether five francs or five times a hundred thousand, were equally dishonoring. She begged him to act from conscience.

“There’s more; finish it,” Monsieur de Bourbonne urged.

“Oh, the rest is for lovers’ eyes,” Octave smiled.

After a moment, Octave explained. The letter had changed him. He felt ashamed of living on his father’s questionable gains—“riding someone else’s horse, eating food not honestly mine.” He rushed to Madame Firmiani; together they calculated what was due the Bourgneufs and condemned themselves to pay interest. What he had was insufficient; she offered her savings, which he accepted.

“What! Besides her other virtues, does that adorable woman save money?” his uncle exclaimed.

“Don’t laugh; her position forced her to be careful. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died three years later. Legal proof of his death and his will—in which he left everything to her—were lost or stolen amid the Greek troubles; records there are not like France’s. Unsure of her title, she lived prudently. I want to buy property of my own to provide for her if she loses hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me? I love you enough to pay such debts. I’ll play the traditional uncle and help!”

“Ah! uncle, I want to be rich by my own industry. If you help, let it be an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year until I find a small enterprise needing capital. I teach myself to live honestly. If you’d seen my happiness in making restitution! The Bourgneufs lived miserably: an old father working as a lottery agent, daughters tending house by lamplight, a sick mother. My visit was like bringing Providence. My conscience felt cleansed; my father, if there is a Paradise, is happy there. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she inspires a delicacy of feeling I lacked. I call her my dear conscience. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich by my own industry. I have an industrial scheme and, if it succeeds, shall earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, eyes filling with memories.

At that moment, despite the garret’s distance from the street, a carriage halted.

“There she is!” he exclaimed. “I know her horses by the way they pull up.”

Madame Firmiani entered.

“Ah!” she said, surprised to see Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has sent me a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet kept safe for me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Overcome, she laid her head on his breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are the best in humanity; you are hardly guilty of fault, for they come through us.”
