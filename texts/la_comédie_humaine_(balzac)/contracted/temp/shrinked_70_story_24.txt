The French often hesitate to travel, believing that France's unique charm is irreplaceable. This sentiment was evident in 1836 when two Parisians visited a palazzo in Genoa, where they felt the essence of Paris among dinner guests, including the Consul-General and notable figures like Mademoiselle des Touches and Léon de Lora. The Consul-General, reminiscent of Lord Byron, had a complex relationship with his wife, Onorina Pedrotti. During dinner, discussions on literature and morality revealed the tension between public success and private anguish, particularly regarding Comte Octave, who lamented societal hypocrisy around adultery. His emotional pain stemmed from his wife's departure, which left him heartbroken. Octave's relationship with Honorine deteriorated after marriage, leading to her leaving him for unknown reasons. Despite his suffering, he continued to care for her from a distance, complicating matters when Maurice, a young man he enlisted as a liaison, developed feelings for her. The narrative explores themes of love, betrayal, and the quest for happiness within Parisian society.
