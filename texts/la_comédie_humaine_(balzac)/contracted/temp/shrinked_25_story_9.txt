**Study of a Woman**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*

**Dedication**  
To the Marquis Jean-Charles di Negro.

---

The Marquise de Listomere embodies the spirit of the Restoration, merging religious devotion with worldly elegance. Married for seven years to the unremarkable Marquis de Listomere, she upholds a virtuous facade to support her husband's political ambitions. Known as "dull weather," the marquis ensures a stable home life that aligns with her principles.

Despite her reserved nature, the Marquise commands respect and skillfully deflects flirtatious advances, preserving her dignity. She engages in conversations with agreeable men without attracting scandal, a challenging feat in her social circle. Her beauty is understated; her striking features emerge when animated, revealing her depth of character.

Recently, she danced with Eugène de Rastignac, a clever young man embodying charm and complexity. Their conversation, which shifted from light topics to women's roles, left a lasting impression. Rastignac, ambitious yet playful, was captivated by the Marquise but chose not to pursue her further that evening.

The next morning, Rastignac reflected on the Marquise while writing letters. His thoughts were interrupted by his friend Horace, leading to an unremarkable discussion about a potential expedition to Morea.

Meanwhile, the Marquise received a letter from Rastignac while preparing for the day. The romantic declarations prompted her to read on despite her initial impulse to discard it. As she absorbed its contents, she felt a mix of fatigue and intrigue, leading her to ponder Rastignac's bold approach.

This moment highlights her complexities—caught between societal expectations and personal desires, the letter challenges her perceptions of propriety and ignites curiosity about Rastignac's intentions.

Madame de Listomere decided to forbid her porter from admitting Monsieur de Rastignac, resolving to show disdain for his insolence. Although she initially considered keeping a love letter she received, she ultimately burned it, surprising her housemaid, Caroline.

At a party hosted by the Marquis de Beauseant, Madame de Listomere anticipated Rastignac's arrival. By 2 AM, realizing he would not come, her thoughts underwent a process Stendhal termed "crystallization."

Four days later, Rastignac scolded his valet, Joseph, for misplacing letters. Joseph revealed one was for Madame de Listomere, prompting Rastignac to realize he had mistakenly sent her a love letter not meant for her. This blunder led him to reflect on his mistakes, particularly not visiting her sooner, allowing her to dwell on the incident.

When Rastignac finally visited, the porter informed him that Madame de Listomere was out, but the marquis invited him in. Upon entering, Rastignac noticed Madame de Listomere blush at his presence, flattering his vanity. The marquis read the newspaper while Madame de Listomere maintained her composure despite the awkwardness.

Rastignac attempted to explain the misunderstanding about the letter, but Madame de Listomere, feigning offense, seemed amused. She insisted on forgetting the incident, but Rastignac clarified that no offense had occurred. Their conversation revealed underlying tensions, with Madame de Listomere expressing skepticism about his intentions.

As the discussion progressed, Rastignac inadvertently suggested that Madame de Nucingen was the intended recipient of the letter, causing a visible reaction in Madame de Listomere. She dismissed him coldly, pointing out the absurdity of the situation. Feeling foolish, Rastignac left after a few awkward exchanges.

Days later, Madame de Listomere withdrew from society, claiming illness, while the marquis attributed her absence to stomach inflammation. In reality, she was experiencing a nervous attack, using it as an excuse to avoid social interactions. The narrative highlights the complexities of social dynamics and misunderstandings in romantic pursuits among the young and inexperienced.
