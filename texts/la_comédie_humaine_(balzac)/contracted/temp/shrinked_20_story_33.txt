DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern type, signals the shift from material enterprise to intellectual force: he links isolated local power with universal influence, equalizing products and spreading ideas from the capital to the provinces. A self-taught scholar and mimic, he knows the world superficially, reads habits and solvency at a glance, and must judge quickly for his trade. Abroad such agents sometimes show truer diplomacy than ambassadors, negotiating commercial treaties in taverns rather than salons. By persuasive language they import Parisian innovations into provincial life.

One such traveller is Gaudissart. His olive-green overcoat and striped shirt announce his profession. An acrobat in commerce, he extracts wealth from the provinces with a flood of phrases. Persuasive, disarming, and eager for attention, he made his fortune in the hat trade, enchanting vendors and winning social favor. Jovial and festive, yet businesslike when needed, he masters diplomacy by adapting to his audiences.

Until 1830 Gaudissart stuck to hats; the upheaval that year turned ideas into commodities. Writers began to sell thought and speculators followed. Gaudissart seized the change, taking a life-insurance commission and quickly learning the business. His employers praised him; newspapers sought him to sell subscriptions too. Confident in his persuasive talents, he agreed to sell both life insurance and periodicals.

CHAPTER II

After a week steeped in Saint-Simonism and financial talk, Gaudissart prepared to travel in mid-April. Two commercial houses, however, tempted him back into hats with good commissions. Riding in a hackney with Jenny, a florist, he joked about refurnishing her room from his travels; she doubted such promises, prompting playful banter about his prospects.

Gaudissart assured her he would earn more than before, aiming for a substantial income from insurance and subscriptions and measuring himself against prominent figures in politics and commerce. The next morning he left for the towns assigned him, stopped in Blois, and wrote to Jenny boasting of selling life insurance and, especially, the profitable "Children’s Journal."

CHAPTER III

Arriving in Vouvray, Gaudissart expected his persuasive skills to prevail but met a provincial spirit resistant to new ideas. The Tourangians were witty, satirical, and hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, a local skeptical of commercial travellers and aware of Gaudissart’s reputation. Amused at the idea of humbling a top salesman, Vernier arranged a prank: he sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a local reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with his wine and newspapers, began a conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the madman’s eccentricities produced nonsensical replies. Gaudissart tried to make sense of Margaritis’s chatter and to sell his product, but met only distraction and mockery.

CHAPTER IV

Seated at home and drinking, Margaritis spoke delusionally while two women listened amused and Vernier watched, intent on humiliating Gaudissart. His erratic behavior—fixations on wine, newspapers, and peculiar views—thwarted any rational exchange. The talk devolved into misunderstandings and incoherent replies. Gaudissart patiently outlined the benefits of life insurance; Margaritis answered with unrelated absurdities, entertaining the onlookers and frustrating the salesman.

Despite the absurdity, Gaudissart persisted, but the lunatic’s unpredictability made success impossible. The scene exposed the limits of persuasive rhetoric when confronted with deliberate mockery and provincial antipathy.

CHAPTER V

After prolonged banter, the innkeeper disclosed that Margaritis was indeed a lunatic and that Vernier had engineered the encounter to trick Gaudissart. Enraged at the insult, Gaudissart demanded satisfaction. The townspeople enjoyed the spectacle; Vernier refused to apologize, and tension escalated toward a duel. The innkeeper, fearing trouble, proposed mediation over breakfast.

Ultimately the dispute was patched up: Gaudissart and Vernier reconciled, recognizing the quarrel’s absurdity. Gaudissart left Vouvray resolved to return to Paris and his usual work, with a renewed appreciation for provincial peculiarities and the necessity of reading local mindsets. On the journey back he reflected that commerce and human interaction require not only eloquence but discernment about whom one bargains with and when persuasion becomes folly.
