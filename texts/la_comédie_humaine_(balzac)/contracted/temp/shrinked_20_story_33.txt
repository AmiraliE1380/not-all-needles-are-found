THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

By Honore De Balzac

Translated By Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

THE ILLUSTRIOUS GAUDISSART

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller, a modern figure, symbolizes the transition from material enterprise to intellectual strength. This century connects isolated power with universal might, equalizing products and spreading them among the masses. The commercial traveller serves as a vehicle for ideas, bringing light from the center to the provinces. He is a scholar without formal education, a juggler who knows the world superficially, and a link between village and capital. He is a jester, adept at mimicking various political opinions, and must quickly assess people's habits and solvency for his trade.

He knows the haunts of vice and virtue across France and can persuade people with his eloquence. His activity is remarkable; he is quick to seize opportunities and outmaneuver rivals. Many such diplomats exist in various countries, negotiating for goods and often displaying more true diplomacy than ambassadors. The commercial traveller embodies the spirit of civilization, bringing Parisian innovations to provincial life, often using persuasive language to convince the populace.

A wise political economist noted that most policies in the provinces are collected through the efforts of agents who persuade those in arrears to renew their policies. The power of conversation is a form of seduction, and a nation with two Chambers and a woman who listens is easily swayed.

Let us examine the commercial traveller closely. His attire, including an olive green overcoat and a striped shirt, reflects his unique character. He is an acrobat of sorts, navigating the complexities of commerce with charm and wit. He extracts wealth from the provinces through intellectual persuasion, using a flood of phrases to engage the populace.

In Paris, there lives an exceptional commercial traveller named Gaudissart, who embodies all the qualities necessary for success. His speech is both persuasive and disarming, and he has gained fame in the hat trade, attracting the attention of various vendors. His life is filled with festivities, and he is celebrated wherever he goes, earning the title of "Illustrious."

Gaudissart's reputation precedes him, and he is welcomed in salons and taverns alike. He is jovial and free-spirited, yet capable of serious business when necessary. He has mastered the art of diplomacy, adapting his persona to suit different audiences. Until 1830, he remained loyal to the hat trade, but the upheaval of that year led him to abandon it for the realm of ideas and speculation.

The events of 1830 birthed a new era where ideas became commodities. Writers and thinkers began to sell their thoughts, and speculators sought to profit from them. Gaudissart, recognizing this shift, accepted an offer from a life-insurance company, which promised him a lucrative commission. He underwent training to understand the intricacies of the business and was soon celebrated for his talents.

The heads of the company boasted of Gaudissart's abilities, leading to offers from newspapers seeking subscribers. He agreed to work for both the life-insurance company and the newspapers, believing he could achieve great success.

CHAPTER II

For a week, Gaudissart immersed himself in the doctrines of Saint-Simonism and the intricacies of financial diplomacy. By mid-April, he was ready to embark on his journey. However, two commercial houses persuaded him to return to the hat trade, offering substantial commissions.

While in a hackney-coach with a florist named Jenny, Gaudissart expressed his intentions to refurnish her room with the profits from his travels. Jenny, however, was skeptical of his promises, leading to a playful exchange about his business.

Gaudissart assured her that he would earn significantly more than before, aiming for a substantial income from his various ventures. He expressed ambition, drawing parallels between himself and successful figures in politics and commerce.

The next morning, he set off to work the towns assigned to him, spending time in Blois and writing to Jenny about his progress. He boasted of his successes in selling life insurance and subscriptions to various newspapers, particularly the "Children's Journal," which he found to be a lucrative venture.

CHAPTER III

Gaudissart arrived in Vouvray, a region he believed to be ripe for his persuasive skills. However, he soon encountered the local mindset, which was resistant to new ideas. The Tourangians, known for their wit and satire, were particularly disdainful of Saint-Simonism.

At the Soleil d’Or inn, Gaudissart met Monsieur Vernier, a local figure who was skeptical of commercial travellers. Vernier, aware of Gaudissart's reputation, decided to play a trick on him by sending him to Margaritis, a local lunatic known for his eccentricities.

Margaritis, who was obsessed with his wine and newspapers, engaged Gaudissart in a conversation that quickly turned absurd. Despite Gaudissart's attempts to sell him on the life-insurance policy, Margaritis's madness led to a series of nonsensical exchanges.

CHAPTER IV

Margaritis, seated in his home, engaged Gaudissart in a conversation that revealed his delusions. The two women present listened in amusement, while Vernier plotted to make a fool of Gaudissart. Margaritis's erratic behavior and obsession with wine made it difficult for Gaudissart to maintain his composure.

As the conversation progressed, Margaritis's madness became more apparent, leading to a series of misunderstandings. Gaudissart attempted to explain the benefits of life insurance, but Margaritis's responses were nonsensical, leading to frustration on both sides.

Despite the absurdity of the situation, Gaudissart remained determined to sell Margaritis on the idea of insurance. However, the lunatic's unpredictable nature made it increasingly difficult for him to do so.

CHAPTER V

After a series of humorous exchanges, Gaudissart learned from the innkeeper that Margaritis was indeed a lunatic. Enraged by the trick played on him, he confronted Vernier, demanding satisfaction for the insult. The townspeople were amused by the unfolding drama, and Vernier's refusal to apologize only fueled Gaudissart's anger.

The situation escalated to the point where Gaudissart agreed to a duel with Vernier. However, the innkeeper, concerned about the potential consequences, sought to mediate the situation. He suggested that they settle their differences over breakfast instead.

Ultimately, Gaudissart and Vernier reconciled, realizing the absurdity of their conflict. Gaudissart left Vouvray, vowing to return to Paris and continue his work, but not without a newfound respect for the peculiarities of provincial life.

As he journeyed back, he reflected on the challenges he faced and the lessons learned from his encounters in Vouvray, particularly the importance of understanding the local mindset when navigating the complexities of commerce and human interaction.
