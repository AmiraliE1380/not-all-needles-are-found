DEDICATION

To Madame la Duchesse de Castries.

CHAPTER I

The commercial traveller marks a modern shift from mere trade to intellectual influence: he connects local power with universal markets, smooths regional differences, and brings metropolitan ideas to the provinces. Self-taught in reading surfaces, he judges people and places at a glance, knows where vice and virtue lie, and persuades with polished speech—often more effective than diplomats. Gaudissart embodies this type: an acrobat of commerce who extracts money from the provinces with a torrent of phrases. In an olive-green overcoat and striped shirt he charms and courts attention. Having prospered selling hats—winning social favor—he is jovial and adaptable, businesslike when needed, tailoring his approach to each audience.

After the Revolution of 1830 turned ideas into commodities, writers and speculators traded in thought; Gaudissart adapted. He took a commission selling life insurance, learned quickly, earned praise, and also sold newspaper subscriptions. Confident, he agreed to market both insurance and a profitable "Children’s Journal."

CHAPTER II

After a week of Saint-Simonism and finance, Gaudissart prepared to travel in mid-April. Tempting hat offers tried to lure him back, but he set off, joking with Jenny, a florist, about refurnishing her room from his earnings. She doubted him, prompting playful banter. He assured her he would profit handsomely from insurance and the journal, likening himself to successful politicians and merchants. The next morning he left for his towns, stopping in Blois and writing to Jenny, boasting of his sales.

CHAPTER III

In Vouvray Gaudissart expected triumph but met provincial resistance. The Tourangians, witty and satirical, were hostile to Saint-Simonism. At the Soleil d’Or inn he encountered Monsieur Vernier, skeptical of commercial travellers and bent on humiliating him. Vernier sent Gaudissart to Margaritis, a local reputed lunatic.

Margaritis, obsessed with wine and newspapers, turned the conversation absurd. Despite Gaudissart’s efforts to explain life insurance, the madman’s eccentricities and the watching townspeople thwarted him.

CHAPTERS IV–V

Seated with wine, Margaritis rambled while two women listened and Vernier watched, enjoying the spectacle. When the innkeeper revealed Vernier had engineered the encounter, Gaudissart, insulted, demanded satisfaction. The townspeople savored the quarrel; Vernier’s refusal threatened a duel until the innkeeper proposed mediation over breakfast. They patched up the absurd row, and Gaudissart left Vouvray, resolved to return to Paris and his usual work—reminded that eloquence alone cannot overcome native discernment and mockery.
