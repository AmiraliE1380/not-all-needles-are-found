DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati.

Madame, remembering the joy your conversation gave a traveler in Milan, I lay this work at your feet, hoping you will protect it with your name as you once shielded the tales of a beloved writer. You have a daughter, Eugenie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a joyful childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing of the Eugenie in these pages. While Frenchmen are often thought fickle, I remain Italian in my constancy and memory. Writing “Eugenie” recalls the laughter and warmth of our time in Milan; I now regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a luxurious boudoir in the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, two young women wept before a fireplace. The blue-velvet room, belonging to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who had married into an aristocratic family for a dowry, set the scene. Despite his fortune, du Tillet lacked true social standing.

Madame du Tillet held her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand as they exchanged intimate confessions. Raised by a strict, pious mother, they had entered marriage without ever enjoying youthful freedoms. Their upbringing, so severe and cloistered that it left them naive about life and love, had forged an intense bond between them. Forbidden the wider world and taught only religion and needlework, they retained innocence at the cost of understanding life’s complexities. Though marriage had seemed an escape, both found themselves unprepared for what awaited them.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse sought solace in her sister’s company, overwhelmed by marital troubles. She confessed despair over a husband who seemed indifferent to her happiness. Madame du Tillet, feeling helpless, listened as her sister lamented; their sisterly love transcended their husbands’ indifference.

Both revealed the emptiness of their marriages: du Tillet’s coldness and Félix’s inattentiveness. They voiced desires for passion and excitement, recognizing the limits society and marriage imposed. Each encouraged the other to seek contentment within those constraints while acknowledging their longing for more.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, had married hoping to recreate an idealized love he had once known. Initially content, Madame de Vandenesse began to feel monotony and lack of passion. Though her husband was kind and attentive, he could not satisfy her craving for deeper emotional connection. She grew aware of the allure of romance outside marriage, and a yearning for adventure took hold as duty and social obligation stifled their relationship.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an emerging literary figure, entered society with talent and audacity. Known for sharp wit and caustic humor, he charmed and provoked in equal measure. His unconventional appearance and manner set him apart and made him captivating to women.

At a gathering hosted by Madame de Montcornet, Nathan drew Madame de Vandenesse’s notice. Their initial interactions held an unspoken attraction. As Nathan managed a complex career and personal life, he became increasingly drawn to her, seeing her as a possible muse. Yet society’s norms and both their circumstances threatened the budding connection.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a talented actress and Nathan’s longtime companion, represented another side of his life. Their relationship mixed affection and practicality: she supported his spirits and career while relying on him for security and influence. The bond was complicated by public demands and private needs.

As Nathan’s ambitions grew, he found himself torn between loyalty to Florine and growing feelings for Madame de Vandenesse. Florine, aware of his infatuation, felt threatened by the countess and the potential shift in Nathan’s affections.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan’s relationship deepened, fueled by shared passion for art and literature. Their encounters became more intimate and exhilarating, but social expectations and their roles cast long shadows, creating tension and moral peril.

Madame de Vandenesse wrestled with guilt and desire, torn between loyalty to her husband and attraction to Nathan. The emotional turmoil intensified her longing for a life of passion, even as she feared scandal and consequence.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Nathan, under mounting pressure in career and life, sank into despair. The weight of ambition and fear of failure drove him toward suicide. A chance encounter with Madame de Vandenesse, however, rekindled his will to live.

Seeing his desperation, the countess resolved to help. She recognized the depth of his struggle and sought to encourage him, offering protection from his darkest impulses and from the destructive side of his ambitions.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

After Nathan’s near-suicide, Madame de Vandenesse became more involved in his life, balancing protection and her own feelings. Their relationship became both refuge and torment as they faced moral and practical realities under social scrutiny.

As Nathan’s work began to flourish, he remained torn between Florine’s pragmatic attachment and the countess’s inspiring influence. Florine, feeling endangered, grew increasingly possessive, heightening tensions among the three.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix de Vandenesse, aware of his wife’s attachment to Nathan, acted to preserve his marriage. Determined to uncover the truth and shield her from scandal, he sought to protect her honor and well-being. His resolve to defend their union displayed his love and commitment.

The story follows characters entangled by love, ambition, and societal expectation, leading to a dramatic conclusion that underscores the conflicts of desire and duty.
