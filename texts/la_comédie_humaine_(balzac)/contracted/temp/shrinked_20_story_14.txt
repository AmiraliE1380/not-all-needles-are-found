DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini.

Madame, recalling our conversation in Milan, I dedicate this work to you, hoping you will shelter it with your name as you once protected a beloved writer’s tales. You have a daughter, Eugénie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a happier upbringing than the rigid one of the Eugénie in these pages. Though Frenchmen are called fickle, I am Italian in my constancy and memory: writing “Eugénie” recalls Milan’s warmth, and I regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a blue-velvet boudoir on rue Neuve-des-Mathurins two young women wept by the fire. The room belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who bought social standing by marriage but lacked aristocratic ease. Madame du Tillet clasped her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand as they exchanged confidences. Raised by a strict, pious mother and married without youthful freedom, they had been taught only religion and needlework—innocent of adult passions—so that marriage, once imagined as escape, revealed unforeseen realities.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, distraught by marital troubles, poured out her despair over a husband indifferent to her inner life. Madame du Tillet, equally helpless, described her own cold, listless domesticity. Each admitted a craving for suppressed intensity and sought consolation in the other.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, had married hoping to recreate an idealized love. But the duties and restraints of his class cooled the relationship. Though kind and attentive, he could not supply the romantic intensity his wife desired; monotony provoked a longing for deeper emotional fulfilment and temptation beyond marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an ambitious literary figure, entered society with talent, audacity, and caustic wit. His unconventional charm unsettled as much as it attracted. At Madame de Montcornet’s salon he caught Madame de Vandenesse’s attention; their early exchanges hinted at an unspoken attraction. Nathan, amid a fraught career and private troubles, saw her as a potential muse, though social position and decorum barred any open liaison.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a gifted actress and Nathan’s long-term companion, combined affection with practicality: she sustained his spirits and career while relying on him for protection and status. As Nathan’s admiration for the countess grew, Florine felt the shift and grew insecure and jealous.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Nathan and Madame de Vandenesse bonded over art and literature. Their encounters grew intimate, bringing exhilaration and peril. She was torn—guilty yet enticed—between loyalty to her husband and the lure of passionate life, aware of the social ruin yielding would invite.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Crushed by setbacks, Nathan contemplated suicide. A chance meeting with Madame de Vandenesse revived his will to live; seeing his desperation, she resolved to protect and encourage him, countering his darkest impulses.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

She became more involved, balancing protection with her own feelings. Their relationship proved both strength and turmoil: secrecy, social risk, and Florine’s intensifying jealousy complicated matters. Nathan was torn between Florine’s practical devotion and the countess’s inspiring influence.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix, sensing his wife’s attachment, moved to preserve their marriage. Determined to protect her and their honor, he acted to discover the truth. Love, ambition, loyalty, and expectation became entangled, setting the stage for a dramatic clash between desire and duty.
