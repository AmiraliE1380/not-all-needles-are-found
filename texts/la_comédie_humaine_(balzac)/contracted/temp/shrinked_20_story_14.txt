DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati.

Madame, if you recall how your conversation in Milan recalled Paris to me, you will not be surprised that I thank you by placing one of my works at your feet and asking you to protect it with your name. You have an Eugenie whose intelligent smile promises inheritances of character you once showed me in that cool stuccoed salon and little garden in the Vicolo dei Cappucini. Though you have moved from the Corso to the Tre Monasteri, and seem more remote like a figure by Raffaelle or Titian, I remain grateful and faithful in memory.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a handsome house in the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, at half-past eleven, two young women sat before a blue-velvet boudoir fireplace. The room’s elaborate, tasteful decoration—blue cashmere, a silver lamp set with turquoise, a fluted silk ceiling, carved ebony furniture, Dresden figures on the mantel, a Venice mirror, exotic plants—spoke of wealth but cold order rather than domestic warmth. The occupants, though, were weeping.

The house belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, one of Paris’s richest bankers. He had married, in 1831, the daughter of the Comte de Granville, a magistrate who became a peer after 1830, and who had arranged a marriage contract acknowledging a dowry he had in fact not given—thus smoothing the union of finance and rank. The Comtesse du Tillet and her sister, Madame Felix de Vandenesse (born Emilie de Fontaine), were the two women in the room.

Madame de Vandenesse—Marie-Angelique—and her younger sister Marie Eugenie had been raised in a gloomy Marais household by a strict, devout mother. Their childhood was confined: religion and needlework filled their days; even reading was restricted to “authorized” books, and their lives were supervised by austere confessors. Their mother aimed to make angels of her daughters, sheltering them from worldly pleasures and instructing them in rigid piety; they left home untouched by romance or social experience, their education narrow and convent-like.

Music was the single permitted refuge. A German chapel-master, Schmucke, lively and devoted, became their companion in art, giving them a chaste but ardent love for melody and composition. The sisters embraced him as a kind of saint of music; through him they tasted a life beyond domestic strictures.

At sixteen they were taken to the ball only rarely and always accompanied by their mother. Their dress and manners were painfully modest, muslin high to the chin and sleeves to the wrist, forming pillars from which their tender faces emerged. Unsullied by dangerous ideas and robust in health, they were married virgins, obedient to the injunctions given them. Their pleasures and confidences were largely confined to each other.

The Comte de Granville, though present in the family, left his daughters largely to his wife’s control and saw them seldom, surrendering their immediate upbringing to the mother. This domestic division—sons with the father, daughters with the mother—intensified the sisters’ isolation and helped shape the futures that followed.

On the night described, both sisters mourned: each had a child—one a boy, the other a girl—and Angelique, the elder, seemed overwhelmed by trouble. Eugenie, who had been treated with special severity in childhood and was now the banker’s wife, tried to console her.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, exhausted and with flowers in her hair, had rushed to her sister’s boudoir after an appearance at the Opera. She revealed to Eugenie an urgent need—forty thousand francs—and pleaded for help. Eugenie, although married to a man of great wealth, hesitated, troubled both by the sum and by the implications of aiding someone who might be morally compromised.

The Comtesse’s complaint was both personal and social: she spoke of craving the life her husband gave—intellectual sympathy, the liberty to live emotions fully—but now winter had come, and monotony and age had intruded. She described her happiness in being part of a poet’s soul, living a double existence of public composure and private expansion, yet acknowledged the brevity of such youth.

Eugenie, who had a hard, suspicious husband, listened sympathetically. Ferdinand du Tillet entered at that moment. He was brusque and controlling; his tone toward his wife suggested he regarded her as a mere ornament of his ambition and suspected her of imprudence. The Comtesse de Vandenesse tried to ask Eugenie to aid her privately; du Tillet, however, mocked and probed. He dismissed the idea lightly, implying Eugenie’s wealth was common knowledge and making clear he would not permit his wife to meddle. When the Comtesse hinted she must borrow privately for a man on the brink of debtor’s prison, du Tillet forbade any intervention and implied he would prefer the man in prison, for reasons of his own financial interest and principle.

After du Tillet’s departure, the sisters’ plan to visit another woman who might help—the bold and wealthy Madame de Nucingen—was frustrated by time. The sisters’ trust in one another resurfaced even amid the banker’s icy surveillance. The scene ended with the hint of domestic tensions and the unspoken network of social and financial alliances that govern such crises.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

A compact account of Madame de Vandenesse’s life explains why the Comtesse sought aid and why her marriage, once content, had grown disquieting. Felix de Vandenesse had been removed to the Chamber of Peers during the Restoration; this change in circumstance inclined him to marry, and at thirty he sought a young woman steeped in strict Catholicism. Marie-Angelique de Granville fulfilled this ideal: raised under rigid maternal piety, she entered marriage pure and submissive.

Felix made himself the gentle tutor and guide of his wife. He introduced her to society, educated her in taste, literature, and the politics of rank, and took delight in shaping her as an intelligent companion and capable hostess. Their early years were tender and serene; Felix’s paternal indulgence and affectionate instruction produced a devoted wife who reciprocated grateful affection, fraternal love, and noble tenderness. He enjoyed instructing and forming her; the domestic scene was like an artist’s controlled creation rather than the raw material of passion.

By 1833, however, the marriage’s steady felicity had lost its edge. Habit and habit alone had replaced the stimulation that first animated their union. Marie-Angelique, stimulated by the new worlds Felix opened to her, discovered herself becoming curious and desiring variation. As she found social success—admiration from salon-goers, attention from clever women and artists—this fame fed a restlessness. Former lovers of Felix and cautious observers watched her ascendancy with envy; some looked on her as transformed from a once-childish bride into a leading, engaging salon figure. Her husband cautioned her against intimacies, but her experiences in society, and the flattering language of some, awakened a yearning for new sensations: the difference, she asked, between a husband and a lover—“We vegetate with a husband, we live with a lover”—a sentiment that, when repeated by those around her, inflamed curiosity.

Thus, social praise and idle talk began to destabilize the calm partnership. The seeds of discontent were small but significant: as her intelligence and social success grew, she found the monotony of conjugal life less satisfying, and the idea of a more passionate existence more alluring.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Among the men who turned the salons of Paris during that time was Raoul Nathan, a figure of rising fame who had pushed himself into notice by cultivating both talent and striking eccentricity. At Comtesse de Montcornet’s salon, where artists, financiers, and writers were strictly tested, Nathan became a remarkable presence. He combined gaunt, almost ascetic looks—haggard face, long disordered hair, hollow cheeks, and a furrowed skull—with an air of perpetual struggle, as if contending with angels and devils. His tall, thin frame and odd carriage, hands long and wiry, blue Napoleonic eyes, a crooked nose, and quick, incisive mouth gave him a compelling intensity; his beard was often untrimmed, his dress negligently romantic, and one hand habitually tucked into his waistcoat—a pose that aimed to contrast with conventional polish.

Nathan’s temperament matched his appearance: capricious, caustic, capable of sudden sweetness, contemptuous of society’s rituals yet able to charm women into overlooking his affectations. He began as a critic and published brilliant articles, then turned to drama and novels. Though he felt art’s demands intolerable and complained retrospectively of being cheated by the press, he achieved genuine success: a serious drama at the Theatre-Francais that won high critical notice and a novel hailed by some as one of the finest of the period. Yet necessity often drove him to lighter theatrical fare, collaborating with vaudevillists and sacrificing parts of his art for bread. Still, his confidence in his own genius remained unshaken; he moved through salons as a man convinced of his essential accomplishment and burning with creative impatience.

Nathan resembled a man of genius; had he marched to the scaffold, as he sometimes wished, he might have struck his brow like André Chénier. Seized with political ambition by the rise of a dozen authors, professors, metaphysicians, and historians who clustered on the machine during the turmoils of 1830 and 1833, he regretted not having written political rather than literary articles. He fancied himself superior to those parvenus, whose success filled him with consuming jealousy. He belonged to that class of minds ambitious of everything, capable of all things, from whom success seems stolen; they dash at a hundred bright points and settle on none, exhausting the goodwill of others.

At this time he was moving from Saint-Simonism toward republicanism, to return perhaps to ministerialism. He searched every corner for a bone to gnaw on, seeking a safe place where he could bark without being kicked and make himself feared. He was mortified to find himself of no account to de Marsay, then at the head of the government, who had no respect for authors—finding neither what Richelieu called a consecutive mind nor continuity of ideas in them—and who, as any minister would, counted on the constant embarrassment of Raoul’s affairs. Necessity would sooner or later force him to accept conditions instead of imposing them.

Raoul Nathan’s real but concealed character matches his public career. He is a comedian in good faith, selfish as if the State were himself, and a very clever orator. None knows better how to play on sentiments, assume false grandeur, deck himself with moral beauty, praise his nature in language, and pose like Alceste while behaving like Philinte. His egotism moves protected by this cardboard armor and often nearly reaches its aim. Lazy to a superlative degree, he does nothing until prodded by the bayonets of need. He is incapable of persistent labor to create a work; but in paroxysms of rage over wounded vanity, or when creditors press, he leaps barriers and attains some triumph of intellect. After which, wearied and surprised at his own creation, he drops into the marasmus of Parisian dissipation; wants become formidable; he lacks strength to face them; and then he descends from his pedestal and compromises.

Influenced by a false sense of grandeur and future—measured against the noble success of a former comrade, one of the few great talents uncovered by the revolution of July—he allows himself, to escape embarrassment, certain laxities of principle with friendly persons. These never surface publicly but remain buried in private life, where they go unnoticed. The shallowness of his heart and the impurity of his hand, which clasps that of all vices, treacheries, and opinions, have made him as inviolable as a constitutional king. Venial sins that would outrage a man of high character are forgiven in him; the world hastens to excuse them. Men who might otherwise despise him shake hands, fearing the day they may need him. He has, in fact, so many friends that he might wish for enemies.

Judged literarily, Nathan lacks style and cultivation. Like many young men hungry for literary fame, he vomits up today what he learned yesterday. He has neither the time nor the patience to write carefully; he does not observe, he listens. Incapable of constructing a solid plot, he sometimes compensates by impetuous ardor of sketching. He “does passion,” as the argot has it; but instead of arousing ideas, his heroes are magnified individuals eliciting only fugitive sympathy; they are not connected with life’s grander interests and thus represent nothing. Nevertheless Nathan holds his ground by quickness of mind and those lucky hits billiard-players call a “good stroke.” He is the cleverest shot at ideas on the fly in Paris. His fecundity is not his own but that of his epoch; he lives on chance events and distorts their meaning to control them. In short, he is not true; his presentation is false; as Comte Félix said, he is a born juggler. Moreover, his pen draws ink from an actress’s boudoir.

Raoul Nathan typifies Parisian literary youth—false grandeurs and real misery. He represents that youth by incomplete beauties and headlong falls, by the torrent of existence with sudden reverses and unforeseen triumphs. He is the child of a century consumed with envy—a century with a thousand rivalries under many systems, which feed to their profit that hydra of anarchy that wants wealth without toil, fame without talent, success without effort, but whose vices finally force it, after much rebellion and skirmishing, to accept the budget under the powers that be. When many young ambitions meet at the same point, there is contention of wills, extreme wretchedness, bitter struggles. In this dread battle the most overbearing or the most adroit selfishness wins; it is envied and applauded despite outcries, as Molière noted.

When, as an enemy to the new dynasty, Raoul was introduced in Madame de Montcornet’s salon, his apparent grandeurs flourished. He was accepted as the political critic of the de Marsays, the Rastignacs, and the Roche-Hugons who had risen to power. Émile Blondet, ever hesitating and repugnant to action that concerned only himself, continued the trade of scoffer, took no side, and kept well with all. He was friendly with Raoul, with Rastignac, with Montcornet.

“You are a political triangle,” de Marsay laughed at him at the Opera. “That geometric form, my dear fellow, belongs only to the Deity, who has nothing to do; ambitious men ought to follow curved lines, the shortest road in politics.”

From a distance Raoul Nathan was a fine meteor. Fashion accepted his ways and appearance. His borrowed republicanism gave him the Jansenist harshness assumed by defenders of the popular cause while they inwardly scoff—a quality not without charm to women. Women like to perform prodigies, break rocks, and soften natures that seem of iron.

Raoul’s moral costume matched his clothes. He was fitted to be what he became to the Eve bored in her rue du Rocher paradise—the fascinating serpent, the fine talker with magnetic eyes and harmonious motion who tempted the first woman. No sooner had the Comtesse Marie seen Raoul than she felt an inward emotion that caused a species of terror. The glance of that fraudulent great man exercised a physical influence on her, quivering in her heart and troubling it. But the trouble was pleasure. The purple mantle that momentarily draped Nathan dazzled the ingenuous young woman. When tea was served she rose from a knot of talking women where she had been striving to see and hear that extraordinary being. Her silence and absorption were noticed by false friends.

The countess approached the divan in the center of the room, where Raoul was perorating. She stood with her arm in that of Madame Octave de Camp, an excellent woman who kept the secret of Marie’s involuntary trembling, which betrayed the violent emotion. Though the eyes of a captivated woman shed wonderful sweetness, Raoul was too occupied then in letting off fireworks—epigrams like rockets, flaming portraits drawn in fire—to notice one little Eve’s naive admiration concealed among women. Marie’s curiosity—like that which would send all Paris to the Jardin des Plantes to see a unicorn—intoxicates secondary minds as much as it saddens great ones; Raoul was enchanted by it, though he was then too anxious to secure all women to care especially for one.

“Take care, my dear,” said Marie’s kind companion, “and go home.”

The countess looked at her husband for his arm; Félix took it and led her home.

“My dear friend,” Madame d’Espard whispered Raoul, “you are a lucky fellow. You have made more than one conquest to-night, among them the charming woman who left so abruptly.”

“Do you know what the Marquise meant?” Raoul asked Rastignac, when they were fairly alone between one and two in the morning.

“I’m told the Comtesse de Vandenesse has taken a violent fancy to you. You are not to be pitied!” said Rastignac.

“I did not see her,” Raoul said.

“Oh! but you will see her, you scamp!” cried Émile Blondet, standing by. “Lady Dudley will ask you to her grand ball so you may meet the pretty countess.”

Raoul and Blondet went off with Rastignac, who offered his carriage. All three laughed at the combination of an eclectic under-secretary of State, a ferocious republican, and a political atheist.

“Suppose we sup at the expense of the present order of things?” said Blondet, who wanted to revive suppers.

Rastignac took them to Véry’s, sent away his carriage, and all three sat down to analyze society with Rabelaisian laughs. During the supper, Rastignac and Blondet advised their provisional enemy not to neglect such a capital chance of advancement as this offered. The two “roués” satirically related the history of Madame Félix de Vandenesse, driving the scalpel of epigram and sharp points of wit into that innocent girlhood and happy marriage. Blondet congratulated Raoul on meeting a woman guilty of nothing worse so far than horrible red-chalk drawings, feeble water-colors, slippers embroidered for a husband, sonatas played with best intentions—a girl tied to her mother’s apron-strings till eighteen, trussed for religious practices, seasoned by Vandenesse, and cooked to a point by marriage. At the third bottle of champagne Raoul unbosomed himself as never before.

“My friends,” he said, “you know my relations with Florine; you know my life, and you will not be surprised to hear me say I am ignorant of what a countess’s love may be like. I have often felt mortified that I, a poet, could not give myself a Beatrice, a Laura, except in poetry. A pure and noble woman is like an unstained conscience—she represents us to ourselves in noble form. Elsewhere we may soil ourselves, but with her we are always proud, lofty, and immaculate. Elsewhere we lead ill-regulated lives; with her we breathe the calm, the freshness, the verdure of an oasis—”

“Go on, go on, my dear fellow!” cried Rastignac; “twang that fourth string with the prayer in ‘Moses’ like Paganini.”

Raoul remained silent, eyes fixed, apparently musing.

“This wretched ministerial apprentice does not understand me,” he said after a moment.

So, while the poor Eve in the rue du Rocher went to bed in the sheets of shame, frightened at the pleasure with which she had listened to that sham great poet, these three bold minds trampled with jests over the tender flowers of her dawning love. If women only knew the cynical tone such men, humble and fawning in their presence, take behind their backs—how they sneer at what they claim to adore! Fresh, pure, gracious being—the scoffing jester disrobes and analyzes her; but the more she loses veils, the more her beauty shines.

Marie at that moment compared Raoul and Félix without imagining the danger such comparisons might bring. Nothing presented a greater contrast than the disorderly, vigorous Raoul and Félix de Vandenesse, who cared for his person like a dainty woman, wore well-fitting clothes, had a charming desinvoltura, and practiced English nicety in which Lady Dudley had trained him. Marie, a good and pious woman, soon forbade herself even to think of Raoul, considering herself a monster of ingratitude for making the comparison.

“What do you think of Raoul Nathan?” she asked her husband at breakfast the next day.

“He is something of a charlatan,” Félix replied; “one of those volcanoes easily calmed by a little gold dust. Madame de Montcornet makes a mistake in admitting him.”

This annoyed Marie, especially as Félix supported his opinion with facts he knew of Raoul’s precarious life mixed with a popular actress.

“If the man has genius,” he concluded, “he certainly has neither the constancy nor the patience that sanctify it and make it divine. He strives to impose on the world by placing himself on a level he does nothing to maintain. True, painstaking and honorable talent follows its path courageously; it accepts its pains and penalties and does not cover them with tinsel.”

A woman’s thought is elastic. When she receives a blow she bends, seems crushed, and then renews her natural shape after a time.

“Félix is no doubt right,” she thought.

But three days later she was again thinking of the serpent, recalled by that singular, painful yet sweet emotion the first sight of Raoul had given her. The count and countess went to Lady Dudley’s grand ball, where, by the way, de Marsay appeared socially for the last time; he died about two months later, leaving the reputation of a great statesman because, as Blondet remarked, he was incomprehensible.

Vandenesse and his wife again met Raoul at this ball, which gathered several personages of the political drama who were astonished to find themselves together. It was one of the first solemnities of the great world. The salons presented a magnificent spectacle—flowers, diamonds, and brilliant head-dresses; every resource of the toilette was in play. The ballroom might be compared to a choice conservatory where rich horticulturists collect superb rarities—same brilliancy, same delicacy of texture. Here gauzes like dragonfly wings, crepes, laces, and tulles varied as entomological fantasies; dentelled, waved, scalloped; spider’s webs of gold and silver; silks embroidered by fairy fingers; tropical plumes drooping from haughty heads; pearls twined in braided hair; shot, ribbed, and brocaded silks as if arabesque genius presided over French manufacture—all this luxury harmonized with the beauties collected as if to realize a Keepsake. The eye received impressions of the whitest shoulders, some amber-tinted, others polished; some dewy, some plump and satiny, as though Rubens had prepared their flesh; in short, every shade known in white. Eyes sparkled like onyx or turquoise fringed with dark lashes; faces of varied outline presented graceful types of many lands; foreheads noble or softly rounded, as if thought or will reigned; beautiful bosoms shown boldly or pressed together after various fashions. The prettiest feet pointed for the dance, the slimmest waists encircled in the waltz, stimulated even indifferent gazes. The murmur of sweet voices, the rustle of gowns, the cadence of dances, and the whir of the waltz harmonized with music. A fairy’s wand seemed to command this revelry—melody of perfumes, iridescent lights from crystal chandeliers and candelabra. This assemblage of prettiest women in their prettiest dresses stood out against a gloomy background of men in black coats, among whom one noted the elegant profiles of nobles, the ruddy beards and grave faces of Englishmen, and the gracious faces of the French aristocracy. All the orders of Europe glittered on breasts and necks.

Examining this society carefully, one saw not only brilliant tones, colors, and outward adornment, but a soul—it lived, felt, thought. Hidden passions gave it a physiognomy; mischievous or malignant looks were exchanged; fair and giddy girls betrayed desires; jealous women told scandals behind their fans or paid exaggerated compliments. Society, anointed, curled, and perfumed, surrendered to gaiety that went to the brain like a heady liquor. Ideas and sentiments seemed to exhale from foreheads and hearts, condense, and stir even the coldest present. At the most animated moment of this intoxicating party, in a corner of a gilded salon where bankers, ambassadors, and the immoral old English earl, Lord Dudley, played cards, Madame Félix de Vandenesse was irresistibly drawn to converse with Raoul Nathan. Possibly she yielded to that ball-intoxication which sometimes wrings avowals from the most discreet.

Seeing such a fête and the splendors of a world new to him, Nathan was stirred by fresh ambition. He noted Rastignac—whose younger brother had become a bishop at twenty-seven, whose brother-in-law Martial de la Roche-Hugon was a minister, and who himself was under-secretary of State and rumored to be marrying the immense heiress, the Baron de Nucingen’s daughter—and saw in the diplomatic body an obscure writer he had known translating articles now made dynastic since 1830, and professors raised to the peerage. He felt anguished at being left behind by this new aristocracy of lucky talent, success, and real merit. Even Blondet, so used and abused in journalism but so welcomed here, who could enter public service through Madame de Montcornet's influence, struck Nathan as proof of social power. Secretly he resolved to play the game of political opinion like de Marsay, Rastignac, Blondet, Talleyrand—use facts, turn them to profit, treat his system as a weapon, and not interfere with a society so well constituted, shrewd, and natural.

“My influence,” he thought, “will depend on the influence of some woman belonging to this class of society.”

With that thought born of frenzied desire, he fell upon the Comtesse de Vandenesse like a hawk on prey. That charming young woman, in her marabout head-dress producing the delightful “flou” of Lawrence’s paintings and harmonizing with her gentle nature, was pierced by the foaming vigor of this poet wild with ambition. Lady Dudley, who observed everything, aided the tête-à-tête by throwing the Comte de Vandenesse with Madame de Manerville. Strong in her former ascendancy over him, Natalie de Manerville amused herself by leading Félix into mazes of witty teasing, blushing half-confidences, coy regrets cast like flowers at his feet, and recriminations in which she excused herself for being in the wrong.

These former lovers were speaking for the first time since their rupture; while her husband’s former love tried the embers for a spark, Madame Félix de Vandenesse felt those violent palpitations a woman experiences at the certainty of doing wrong and stepping on forbidden ground—emotions not without charm that awaken dormant faculties. Women love using Bluebeard’s bloody key, that fine myth for which we are indebted to Perrault.

The dramatist—who knew his Shakespeare—displayed his wretchedness, related his struggle with men and things, made his hearer aware of his baseless grandeur, his unrecognized political genius, his life without noble affections. Without a single definite word, he contrived to suggest to this charming woman that she should play the noble part of Rebecca in Ivanhoe, love and protect him. It was all, of course, in the ethereal regions of sentiment. Forget-me-nots are not more blue, lilies not more white than the images, thoughts, and radiantly illumined brow of this accomplished artist, who was likely to send his conversation to a publisher. He played his reptile part to this poor Eve so cleverly, made the fatal bloom of the apple dazzling to her eyes, that Marie left the ballroom filled with the species of remorse that resembles hope—flattered in her vanities, stirred in every corner of her heart, caught by her own virtues, allured by her native pity for misfortune.

Perhaps Madame de Manerville had led Vandenesse into the salon where his wife was talking with Nathan; perhaps he had come to fetch Marie and take her home; perhaps his conversation with his former flame had awakened slumbering griefs. Certain it is that when his wife took his arm to leave the ballroom she saw his face sad and his look serious. The countess wondered if he was displeased with her. No sooner were they seated in the carriage than she turned to Félix and said with a mischievous smile,—

“Did I not see you talking half the evening with Madame de Manerville?”

Félix was not out of the tangled paths into which his wife had led him by this charming little quarrel when the carriage turned into their court-yard. This was Marie’s first artifice dictated by her new emotion; she even took pleasure in triumphing over a man who, until then, had seemed to her so superior.

Between the rue Basse-du-Rempart and the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, Raoul maintained, on the third floor of an unattractive narrow house in the Passage Sandrie, a poor, cold, bare lodging ostensibly for the public—literary neophytes, creditors, and other nuisances he kept at the threshold of his private life. His true home, his fine existence, his presentation to friends, was in the house of Mademoiselle Florine, a second-rate comedy actress, where for ten years journalists, authors, and other literary figures amused themselves among actresses of equal distinction. Raoul had so closely attached himself to Florine that he spent over half his life with her; he took all his meals at her house unless he invited a friend or had some engagement elsewhere.

Florine completed this life of corruption with a lively wit honed by daily contact with artists. Contrary to the common belief that those who live by showing passions outwardly lack interior life, the comparatively small number of actors each century has produced many dramatic authors and fascinating women. The idea that exhibition destroys personal sentiment is refutable: dramatic artists contribute mind, memory, and imagination. Great artists, as Napoleon said, can cut the link Nature placed between senses and thought; Moliere and Talma, in old age, loved more intensely than many men do in a lifetime.

Used to journalists who suspected by adding two and two, to writers who anticipated, and to political figures who watched one another at her house, Florine presented a mixture of devil and angel that perfectly suited these roués. They delighted in her cool self-possession; her anomalies of mind and heart amused them. Her house, enriched by gallant tributes, displayed the extravagant magnificence of a woman who cared little for cost and valued objects by caprice—one who might break a fan fit for queens in a passion yet scream if a servant shattered a ten-franc saucer.

Florine’s dining-room reflected this pell-mell of regal and fantastic luxury. Oak panels with gilt lines framed reliefs of children playing with imaginary beasts; sketches by Bixiou and statuettes stood alongside a cast of an angel from Francois Souchet, one of Joseph Bridau’s coquettish paintings, and a gloomy Spanish alchemist by Hippolyte Schinner. Autographs and letters—Lord Byron to Lady Caroline Lamb, a letter from Napoleon to Josephine—hung casually. On the carved oak mantelpiece stood an ivory statuette attributed to Michael Angelo, supposedly Florentine, representing Pan discovering a woman under the skin of a young shepherd; renaissance candelabra flanked it. A Boule clock on a tortoiseshell stand inlaid with brass sparkled between statuettes that seemed rescued from a demolished abbey. Lamps of royal scale awkwardly occupied corners atop Japanese vases. On a sideboard gleamed a silver service given by an English lord and high-relief porcelains—such was the ostentation of an actress whose only real property was furniture.

Her bedroom, all in violet, was a debutante’s dream: violet velvet curtains lined with white silk looped over tulle; a white cashmere ceiling with violet satin rays; an ermine carpet beside a bed whose curtains resembled an inverted lily; a lantern in bed to read morning plaudits or criticisms. A yellow salon with Florentine-bronze trimmings harmonized with the rest—more detail would have read like an auction catalogue. For comparison one might look next door at a certain Rothschild house.

Sophie Grignault—Florine on the stage—had first appeared on poor boards despite her beauty. Her success and savings she owed to Raoul Nathan. This association, typical in dramatic and literary circles, did Raoul no harm; he observed outward conventions of a man of the world. Florine’s means were precarious; salary and leaves barely covered dress and household expenses. Nathan gave her perquisites, levied as a critic on new ventures of industrial art. Though always gallant and protective, his protection was irregular and fragile.

This uncertainty did not alarm Florine; she believed in her talent and beauty. Her robust faith amused those who heard her stake the future on it when remonstrated with.

“I can have income enough when I please,” she would say. “I have invested fifty francs on the Grand-livre.”

No one could understand how Florine, so handsome, had remained obscure for seven years. Enrolled as a supernumerary at thirteen and debuting at fifteen in a shabby boulevard theatre, she had been in her teens when neither beauty nor talent is fully formed. At twenty-eight—an age when French beauties often flourish—painters admired the luster of her white shoulders, tinted olive at the nape, firm and smooth like watered silk. When she turned her head, splendid folds formed about her neck, the delight of sculptors. She bore a small, Roman-empress–like head—delicate, round, and self-willed—set on a triumphant neck; a forehead cut with one chisel-stroke conveyed a woman who drives care away, who yields easily but can stubbornly resist. Her golden hair was raised in front in two equal masses and twisted up behind, prolonging the neck’s line.

Black, delicate eyebrows like a Chinese brush framed soft lids; pupils bright though streaked with brown gave her a cruel, fixed glance and hinted at the cold maliciousness of a courtesan. Her gray eyes, fringed with black lashes—a charming contrast—expressed contemplative voluptuousness; circles beneath them betrayed fatigue, but through stage artfulness she could make them extraordinary—terrible and soft. Rouge had fouled the diaphanous tints of her cheeks, yet the flesh remained delicate; though she no longer blushed easily, a thin nose with passionate nostrils and a sensual mouth spoke sarcasm and love of dissipation. A deep furrow united upper lip and nose; her chin, white and rather full, betrayed passion’s violence. Her hands and arms were fit for a sovereign.

Yet she bore a mark of low birth—short, fat feet—an affliction that distressed her most. She tried everything short of amputation. The feet were obstinate like the Breton stock she claimed; they resisted all remedies. Florine now wore long boots padded with cotton to give length and the illusion of an instep. Her figure was of medium height, tending toward corpulence but well-balanced.

Morally, she was a master of the postures and arts of her trade: quarrelling, alluring, cajoling, playing childlike innocence while slipping philosophical malignancies into artless speeches. Apparently giddy and ignorant, she was astute in money matters and commercial law—because she had known misery before her precarious success. She had climbed from garret to first floor through vicissitudes, knowing life from Brie cheese to pineapples, from cooks and washing in a garret to summoning armies of chefs and saucemakers. She had lived on credit and not been undone; she knew things honest women ignore. Experience had made her shrewd as any judge; she managed tradespeople, evaded snares, and understood prices like a public appraiser. Lying on her sofa like a bride, fresh and white, rehearsing a part, she seemed a child of sixteen—ingenuous, ignorant, weak; yet let a creditor enter and she rose like a startled fawn, swearing stoutly.

“Hey, my good fellow; your insolence is too dear an interest on the money I owe you,” she would cry. “I am sick of seeing you. Send the sheriff here; I’d prefer him to your silly face.”

Florine gave charming dinners, concerts, and well-attended soirées where play ran high. Her female friends were all handsome; no old woman entered her precincts. She was not jealous—jealousy would have seemed an admission of inferiority. She had known actresses of past fame and now knew Tullia, Euphrasie, Aquilina, Madame du Val-Noble, Mariette—those women who float through Paris, today queens, tomorrow slaves. She knew rivals and prima-donnas; that whole exceptional feminine society, graceful in its easy sans-souci, absorbed anyone seduced into its Bohemian whirl: eager abandonment and contempt for the future.

Though this Bohemian life at her house displayed tumultuous disorder amid artists’ laughter, Florine governed it with a better count than any guest. There secret Saturnalias of literature, art, politics, and finance were held; desire reigned; caprice and fancy were as sacred as bourgeois honor. Friends included Blondet, Finot, Etienne Lousteau, the feuilletonist Vernou, Couture, Bixiou, the banker Nucingen, du Tillet, Conti the composer—essentially that legion of selfish materialists who hovered around singers, actresses, and danseuses. They loved or hated each other as whims or circumstances dictated.

This Bohemian rendezvous, whose only ticket was celebrity, functioned as a Hades of the mind—a galleys of intellect. No one could enter without having conquered fortune, suffered ten years of misery, strangled two or three passions, and acquired some renown—by books or fine dress, dramas or carriages. Plots were hatched there; ways of making fortune were scrutinized; all things were debated. Yet each man left and resumed his livery of opinions; there he could, without compromise, criticize his own side, praise an opponent, admit thoughts none would formally own, and say all as if ready to act. Paris alone offers such eclectic houses where all tastes, vices, and opinions are received under decent guise. Thus it was uncertain that Florine would remain a second-class actress to the end.

Florine’s life was not idle or enviable. Many imagine that an actress lives in perpetual carnival because of the stage’s pedestal, but behind the scenes many poor girls return to garrets imagining pearls and embroidered gowns; little do they know the treadmill an actress endures—rehearsals under threat of fines, reading new pieces, constant study. At each performance Florine changed dress two or three times; she often came home exhausted and half-dead yet had to wash off cosmetics and clean powder from her hair, sometimes leaving no time even for supper. A performance could run past midnight; she might not get to bed before two, yet must rise early to study, order and try on dresses, breakfast, answer letters, arrange the claque’s placement for plaudits, pay for last month’s triumphs, and book those of the month ahead. Sometimes she feigned illness to escape a provincial longing for the country.

These mechanical labors were minor compared with intrigues and wounded vanity—preferences by authors, parts taken away or given to others, male actors’ exactions, rivals’ spite, managers’ nagging, struggles with journalists—all requiring another twelve hours a day. Beyond that lay the art of acting itself: mastering expression, positions, and gestures, minute watchfulness on stage where opera-glasses detect every lapse—labors that consumed Talma, Lekain, Baron, Contat, Clairon, Champmesle. In these infernal coulisses self-love has no sex; any triumphant artist, man or woman, finds all other artists arrayed against them. As to money, whatever the engagements, Florine’s salary did not cover the costs of stage costume, which requires a vast variety of gloves, shoes, and frippery beyond personal clothing. The first third of such a woman’s career is spent imploring; the next in getting a foothold; the last in defending it. Happiness is grasped frantically because it is rare, long desired, and found amid odious fictitious pleasures and smiles.

For Florine, Raoul’s influence in the press was a protecting sceptre: he spared her many cares and anxieties. She clung to him less as lover than as prop; she cared for him like a father, deceived him like a husband, and would willingly have sacrificed everything for him. Raoul could and did do everything for her vanity and stage future. Without a successful author there is no successful actress; Champmesle owed Racine as Mars owed Monvel. Florine could do little for Raoul, though she wished to be useful and necessary. She counted on habit’s charms to bind him and was ready to open salons and display her dinners for his friends and projects. She desired to be to him what Madame de Pompadour was to Louis XV. All actresses envied Florine’s position; some journalists envied Raoul’s influence.

Those who know the mind’s inclination toward chance and contrasts will understand that after ten years of lawless Bohemian life—ups and downs, fêtes and bailiffs, orgies and forced sobriety—Raoul was attracted by the idea of another love: the gentle, harmonious house and the presence of a great lady, just as Comtesse Félix desired intense emotion to break happiness’s monotony. This law of life—the dependence of art on contrast—applies to all arts. A work done without this incentive is the loftiest expression of genius, as the cloister is the highest Christian life.

Returning to his lodging after Lady Dudley’s ball, Raoul found a note from Florine brought by her maid; sleep had prevented him from reading it. He fell asleep dreaming of the gentle love his life had lacked. Hours later he opened the note and discovered important news that neither Rastignac nor de Marsay had let out: an indiscreet government member had revealed to the actress the coming dissolution of the Chamber after the present session. Raoul instantly went to Florine’s and summoned Blondet. In the actress’s boudoir, feet on the fender, Emile and Raoul analyzed France’s political situation in 1834. They considered on which side fortune most likely lay, reviewing every party and shade—pure republicans, presiding republicans, republicans without a republic, constitutionals without a dynasty, ministerial conservatives and absolutists; the Right in its various forms: aristocratic, legitimist, henriquinquist, Carlist. Between the party of resistance and that of action there was no debate; the choice was as stark as life and death.

A swarm of newspapers promoted every shade of opinion. Blondet, Raoul Nathan’s judicious but impractical friend, advised a gradual political shift rather than abrupt apostasy—becoming leader of the Left Centre instead of a sudden renunciation. Nathan, bent on founding a daily political journal to dominate the press, would not be dissuaded. He planned to gather collaborators—Massol, Finot, Pantin, Étienne Lousteau, Claude Vignon, Félicien Vernou, du Tillet—and asked Blondet to convene them. Blondet estimated heavy costs: three principal partners would need to put up a hundred thousand francs each to keep the paper solvent long enough for a rise and fall in Paris.

Florine, an actress and Nathan’s longtime lover, theatrically pretended distress to test friends and then sold nearly all her furniture and jewels cheaply to appear poor in pursuit of an English lord’s favor; the sale also furnished funds for Nathan’s enterprise. A festive launch dinner baptized the new journal; Florine, posing as generous, moved into Raoul’s rooms while he lodged at the new office. Her devotion was practical—she planned provincial tours to earn for him—while Nathan pursued ambition and glory.

After Lady Dudley’s ball Nathan fell under the spell of the innocent Countess Marie de Vandenesse. Both believed themselves loved: Marie, charmed by the heroic role she imagined for herself in the poet’s life; Raoul, weary of dissipation and eager for an ideal refuge. Their love was pure and enthusiastic. Marie delighted in adorning herself to please him and indulged the fantasy of inspiring his genius. Raoul, in turn, adopted gentility and restraint to please her, practicing civility in company and accepting society’s conventions when advised by Blondet.

At the Opera and in salons Raoul’s passion showed in looks, a single stolen kiss, and ardent letters. Yet his life was strained: he had to keep up an appearance of leisure in the Bois, attend society, run a newspaper demanding ceaseless work, write plays for money, and manage political ambitions. Marie, ignorant of these demands, misread his necessary absences as indifference until he explained his burdens. She responded with admiration and sacrifice, promising to wait and be his support; this deepened their devotion. Still, the obligations his passion imposed—daily riding, late nights, editorial toil, theatrical commitments—sapped the hours needed for his enterprise.

Back in the office, Nathan’s collaborators were not friends. Massol and du Tillet intended to use his talent and control or discard him when convenient. Du Tillet, a banker, secretly manipulated finance and politics: he and Nucingen conspired for advantage, and Rastignac maneuvered for his own position. The paper’s costs outran receipts. Du Tillet advanced a loan and renewed notes on short terms, keeping Nathan dependent. Florine contributed fifty thousand but used much to refurnish her house; later she added debt. Du Tillet’s leverage grew as subscriptions dwindled and expenses mounted.

In January, pressed for cash, Nathan borrowed from a usurer, Gigonnet, but the notes were protested; Florine obtained a brief extension. The ledger showed receipts covered only two-thirds of expenses. A final demand from du Tillet, who wished to block Nathan’s candidacy for the electoral college, led Gigonnet to sue. Arrest for debt would ruin Nathan politically. Crushed by shame at potential failure, surrounded by false friends and conspirators, and seeing no way to preserve his position without scandal, Nathan, faint and desperate, attempted suicide by charcoal. He was discovered in time at his office, carried away by the countess Marie, and revived in a hotel where he confessed his woes. Marie vowed to save him.

Eugénie du Tillet, Marie’s practical sister, suspected danger and took action. She urged Marie to help: Marie sold her diamonds rather than borrow from her husband or father, unwilling to compromise honor. Eugénie proposed a plan: have Madame de Nucingen provide a loan in exchange for endorsed notes. The sisters secretly arranged for Schmucke, an old German music master and former pupil, to sign four notes for ten thousand francs each as endorsements; Marie personally visited Schmucke’s squalid lodging and obtained his ingenuous signature. Schmucke, simple and adoring, signed without understanding the consequence, and Marie carried the papers to Madame de Nucingen, who—touched and discretely complicit—ordered the cashier to supply the money and keep the transaction secret.

When du Tillet learned the loan had been paid and that Nucingen’s wife had advanced the funds, he was furious—his scheme to have Nathan arrested and thus prevented from standing in elections had been foiled. Du Tillet accused his wife, who defied him, and a domestic scene followed. Despite his plotting, the immediate danger passed: the four notes and Marie’s guarantee were in Vandenesse’s hand. Félix de Vandenesse, prudent and magnanimous, drew forty thousand francs from the Bank of France to reimburse Madame de Nucingen and retrieved his wife’s guarantee. He intended not to punish Marie but to protect her and to recover Nathan’s letters to prevent scandal.

Felix then planned a controlled exposure. He decided to take Marie to a masked ball at the Opera where, disguised, they could confront Florine and Nathan and reclaim the letters—both to spare Marie further humiliation and to humble the journalist. At the ball Vandenesse contrived to involve Florine; in a private encounter he told the actress that Nathan had tried to kill himself for want of money and that Marie had carried him away to save him. He hinted that letters proving Nathan’s infatuation with the countess lay under Florine’s roof. Florine, confident and scornful of society women, denied Nathan would love a countess and challenged the plot’s truths. The count watched Nathan’s discomfort and prepared to bring the affair into the open, feeling that by revealing the deception he might both rescue his wife’s reputation and teach Nathan a salutary lesson.

Thus, in a world where journalism, politics, finance, love, vanity, and betrayal intertwine, Raoul is at once elevated and undone by his ambition and passions; Marie’s innocent, heroic devotion saves his life and summons schemes of money and influence; Florine’s theatrical constancy and du Tillet’s cold opportunism complicate every allegiance. The masked ball approaches as a stage for revelation, reprisal, and judgment.

Nathan, after hunting the foyer, returned to where the mask had addressed him. Seeing an expression he could not conceal, Florine planted herself before him and said imperiously that she did not wish him to leave her again; she had her reasons.

At her husband's prompt the countess approached Raoul and whispered, “Marie. Who is this woman? Leave her at once, and meet me at the foot of the grand staircase.” In that difficult moment Raoul let go of Florine’s arm; though she caught his hand and held it, she had to let him go. Nathan melted into the crowd.

“What did I tell you?” Felix murmured into Florine’s astonished ear, offering his arm.

“Come,” she replied. “Have you a carriage here?”

Vandenesse hurried Florine away, followed by his wife. A few moments later the three masks, driven by Vandenesse’s coachman, reached Florine’s house. As soon as she entered her apartments the actress unmasked. Madame de Vandenesse could not help a quiver of surprise at Florine’s beauty as she stood choking with anger—superb in her wrath and jealousy.

“There is, somewhere in these rooms,” Vandenesse said, “a portfolio, the key to which you never had; the letters are probably in it.”

“Good heavens; you know something I have been uneasy about,” cried Florine, rushing into the study for the portfolio.

Florine’s rooms betrayed more of her intimacy with Nathan than any ideal mistress would want revealed. A woman’s eye takes such truths at once, and the countess noticed vestiges of Nathan that confirmed Vandenesse’s suggestion. Florine returned with the portfolio.

“How am I to open it?” she asked.

She rang for the cook’s knife and brandished it, ironically remarking that it was used to cut the necks of “poulets,” a comment that chilled the countess and showed how close she had come to ruin. “What a fool I am!” Florine cried; “his razor will do better.” She fetched one of Nathan’s razors from his dressing-table and slit the portfolio’s leather cover; Marie’s letters spilled out. Florine snatched one and skimmed it.

“Yes, she must be well-bred. It looks to me as if there were no mistakes in spelling here.”

The count gathered the letters and handed them to his wife, who took them to a table as if to ensure they were all there.

“Now,” said Vandenesse to Florine, showing five bank-bills of ten thousand francs each, “will you let me have those letters for these? They’ll replace what you have paid for him.”

“Ah!” cried Florine. “Didn’t I kill myself body and soul in the provinces to get him money—I, who’d have cut my hand off to serve him? But that’s men! Damn your soul for them and they’ll march over you rough-shod! He shall pay me for this!”

Madame de Vandenesse was leaving with the letters.

“Hi! stop, my fine mask!” Florine called. “Leave me one to confound him with.”

“Not possible,” said Vandenesse.

“Why not?”

“That mask is your ex-rival; but you needn’t fear her now.”

“Well, she might have had the grace to say thank you,” Florine snapped.

“But you have the fifty thousand francs now,” Vandenesse added, bowing to her.

It is extremely rare for young men driven to suicide to try again if the first attempt fails; when it doesn’t end life, it destroys all desire for voluntary death. Raoul felt no wish to repeat his attempt when he found himself in a more painful position than the one from which he had just been rescued. He tried to see the countess and explain a love that now burned more vividly than ever. At their first meeting in society, however, Madame de Vandenesse gave him that fixed, contemptuous look that instantly and forever erects an impassable barrier between a man and a woman. Despite his assurances, Nathan never dared during the rest of the winter to speak to or even approach the countess.

He opened his heart to Blondet, comparing his Laura and Beatrice to Madame de Vandenesse, and even paraphrased Théophile Gautier:

“Ideala, flower of heaven’s blue, with a golden heart, whose fibrous roots, softer a thousandfold than fairy tresses, strike to our souls and drink their purest essence; flower most sweet and bitter! thou canst not be torn away without the heart’s blood flowing... Ah! cursed flower, why didst thou grow within my soul?”

Blondet cut him short. “My dear fellow, you are raving. It may have been a pretty flower, but it wasn’t ideal. Instead of singing like a blind man before an empty niche, wash your hands and submit to the powers. You are too much of an artist ever to be a good politician; you’ve been fooled by men worth only half as much as you are. Think about being fooled again—but elsewhere.”

“Marie cannot prevent my loving her,” Nathan insisted; “she shall be my Beatrice.”

“Beatrice,” Blondet replied, “was a child of twelve when Dante last saw her; otherwise she would not have been Beatrice. To make a divinity, it won’t do to see her one day in a mantle, the next in a low dress, and the third on the boulevard, cheapening toys for her last baby. When a man has Florine — who can be duchess, bourgeoise, Negress, marquise, colonel, Swiss peasant, virgin of the sun in Peru (the only way she can play the part) — I don’t see why he should go rambling after fashionable women.”

In Bourse terms, Du Tillet “executed” Nathan: lacking money, Nathan gave up his place on the newspaper; the celebrated man received only five votes in the electoral college where the banker was elected.

After a long, happy journey in Italy, the Comtesse de Vandenesse returned to Paris late the following winter, and her husband’s predictions proved true. Nathan had followed Blondet’s advice and negotiated with the government, which employed his pen. His affairs were in such disorder that one day on the Champs‑Élysées Marie saw her former suitor on foot in shabby clothes, giving his arm to Florine. When a man grows indifferent to the heart of a woman who once loved him, he often seems to her ugly, even horrible—especially when he resembles Nathan. Madame de Vandenesse felt personal humiliation at having once cared for him. Had she not already been cured of extra‑conjugal passion, the contrast between the count and this man—ever less worthy of public favor—would have sufficed to end any lingering feeling.

Today the ambitious Nathan, rich in ink and poor in will, has capitulated and settled into a sinecure like any commonplace man. After lending his pen to every disorganizing effort, he lives in peace under the protecting shade of a ministerial organ. The cross of the Legion of Honor, once the fruitful text of his satire, now adorns his button-hole. “Peace at any price,” formerly the stock-in-trade of his revolutionary editorship, is the topic of his laudatory articles. He once attacked heredity in Saint-Simonian phrases; he now defends it with solid arguments. This illogical conduct stems from the change of front performed by Raoul and many others during our recent political evolutions.
