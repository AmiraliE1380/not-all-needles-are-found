A START IN LIFE

CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS

Railroads will soon change travel to and from Paris, making some of the persons and things in this Scene seem antiquarian. The picturesque “coucous” that once stood on the Place de la Concorde and the Cours-la-Reine, common a century earlier and still numerous in 1830, were scarce by 1842 except as suburban curiosities. In 1820 many of the “Environs of Paris” lacked regular stage-coach service.

The Touchards, father and son, had built a near-monopoly of travel to towns within about forty-five miles from a large yard on the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis. Still, they faced stiff local competition from coucous on shorter routes. Parisian taste for the country kept local enterprises alive against the Petites Messageries—the Touchards’ designation to distinguish them from the Grandes Messageries of the rue Montmartre. From about 1822 speculators, inspired by Touchard success, set up rapid, commodious vehicles at fixed hours for nearly every small locality, provoking rivalry. Beaten on long distances, the coucous retreated to short trips and survived until omnibuses capable of carrying eighteen with two horses supplanted them; the remaining coucous might later seem curiosities.

These petty enterprises generally secured a foothold by local goodwill. Their proprietors were nearly always inn-keepers along the route, intimate with local interests. They handled commissions cleverly, charged less, and attracted more custom than the Touchard coaches; they bent rules, sometimes broke passenger-number laws, and kept the affection of the masses. Travellers often preferred a familiar coachman and would delay journeys to take the old coach even if it looked unpromising.

One much‑contested line the Touchards sought to monopolize ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, a profitable route served by three enterprises in 1822. Touchard lowered prices, built better coaches, and increased frequency, yet competition persisted because lines serving towns like Saint-Denis, Saint-Brice and villages—Pierrefitte, Groslay, Ecouen, Poncelles, Moisselles, Monsoult, Maffliers, Franconville, Presles, Nointel, Nerville—were productive. Touchard’s coaches extended to Chambly and rivals followed; later lines would go as far as Beauvais.

Toward the road to England, a turn at a place called the Cave leads through the pleasant Oise valley to Isle-Adam, once the seat of the Isle-Adam family and formerly occupied by the Bourbon-Contis. Isle-Adam and neighboring Nogent and Parmain are famed for quarries whose stone built many fine structures in Paris and abroad. Though the region had celebrated châteaux—Cassan, Stors, Le Val, Nointel, Persan—it escaped much competitive traffic in 1822 and was served by only two coaches that then worked in harmony.

A simple reason explains the exception: from the Cave the paved Conti highway turns to Isle-Adam, which lies six miles off the main line and is a terminus; no speculator would take such a detour. In 1822 the Conti road was the only approach to Isle-Adam. Pierrotin and his colleague therefore reigned from Paris to Isle-Adam, beloved by everyone along the way. Their vehicles and the men themselves were so well known that even those traveling on the main road as far as the Cave would use them; there was often a better chance of a seat than in the Beaumont coaches, which were nearly always full. Pierrotin and his rival were amiable rivals: when one left Isle-Adam the other returned from Paris, and vice versa.

Only Pierrotin figures in this story. The two proprietors lived in friendly understanding, vied loyally, and obtained customers honorably. In Paris they used the same yard, hotel, stable, coach-house, office, and clerk—an economy of good sense. The hotel they lodged in, at the corner of the rue d’Enghien, was the Lion d’Argent, long accustomed to accommodating coachmen and coaches; its proprietor drove for the well-established Daumartin company, so the Touchards never contemplated opening a rival on that line.

Though departures for Isle-Adam were advertised at fixed hours, Pierrotin and his colleague indulged peasants and thereby often embarrassed strangers. The afternoon four o’clock trip frequently waited until half-past, and the morning eight o’clock seldom left before nine. In summer the rule—strict with strangers—relaxed for country customers. This flexibility sometimes allowed Pierrotin to take two fares for one place if a countryman came early and sat in a seat booked by a late “bird of passage.” Moralists might frown, but Pierrotin excused it by “hard times,” winter losses, the need for new coaches, and the necessity of keeping up printed tariffs—which were seldom produced unless insisted upon.

Pierrotin was about forty, a father, discharged from the cavalry at the great disbandment of 1815, and successor to his father, who had driven a coucou between Paris and Isle-Adam for years. He married the daughter of a small inn-keeper and turned the business into a regular service noted for intelligence and a kind of military precision. Active and decisive, his vivacious face showed sly shrewdness beneath weathered features. He spoke with the roughened voice of one accustomed to “Gare!” shouted to horses but could soften it when addressing bourgeois. His work clothes were stout nailed boots from Isle-Adam, bottle-green velveteen trousers and waistcoat, and a blue blouse embroidered while working, topped by a visor cap. His military background taught him respect for social superiority and obedience to the upper classes; he mingled little with lower bourgeoisie and always respected women. By “trundling the world” he came to regard his passengers much like parcels—less important than packages, which were the essential object of a coaching business.

Aware of changes since the Peace that revolutionized his calling, Pierrotin was determined not to be outdone. Since the summer he had talked of a large coach ordered from Farry, Breilmann, and Company—the best makers of diligences—a purchase demanded by an influx of travellers. Pierrotin’s establishment comprised two vehicles. One, used in winter and the only one he reported to the tax-gatherer, was the coucou inherited from his father. Its rounded body allowed six travelers on two metallic-hard seats despite yellow Utrecht velvet coverings. Seats were divided by a removable wooden bar inserted at shoulder height, covered with velvet and called “a back” by Pierrotin; it was a constant annoyance—either rigid and uncomfortable or loose and making ingress and egress perilous, especially for women.

Though each seat legally carried three, it was not uncommon to see eight persons jammed on the two seats like herrings. Pierrotin argued travellers were more comfortable in a solid, immovable mass; when only three occupied a seat they constantly banged against each other and risked hitting their hats against the roof on rough roads. In front of the vehicle was a wooden bench where Pierrotin sat; three travelers could perch there and were called “rabbits.” Sometimes he placed four rabbits on the bench and sat at the side on a low box used as a foot-rest, always filled with straw or packages safe from damage. The coucou’s body was painted yellow with a band of barber’s blue along the top bearing in silvery letters “Isle-Adam, Paris,” and across the back “Line to Isle-Adam.”

The coucou could sometimes take three more passengers in a square compartment covered with an awning where trunks and packages were piled; this “hen-roost” was reserved for regular customers. Occupants of it were made to get down at any gendarmerie post, since overloading forbidden by law could be reported by the on-duty gendarme—who was often a friend to Pierrotin. Thus on certain busy nights and mornings Pierrotin’s coucou trundled fifteen travelers; then he paired his stout horse Rougeot with a small mare, Bichette, whom he praised passionately.

The other vehicle, a comical four-wheel coach, held seventeen travelers though legally limited to fourteen. It rumbled so noisily that Isle-Adam people often said, “Here comes Pierrotin!” when he was still in the forest. It had an “interior” with six passengers on two seats, a forward “coupe” closed with awkward glass sashes, and an “imperial” on top into which Pierrotin contrived to pack six, enclosed by leather curtains. Pierrotin sat on an almost invisible seat just below the coupe’s sashes.

Pierrotin paid tax only on his coucou, rated for six, obtaining special permits when he used the four-wheeler. When vehicle taxes were first imposed, enforcement was timid and proprietors often cheated their dues; the Treasury later forced public conveyances to carry certificates of weighing and tax payment. In 1822 those strictures were only beginning. Often the four-wheeler and coucou traveled together, carrying thirty-two passengers though Pierrotin paid tax on six. On lucky days the convoy left the faubourg Saint-Denis at half-past four and reached Isle-Adam by ten at night, boasting “We went at a fine pace!” To make the 27 miles in five hours he omitted certain stops—Saint-Brice, Moisselles, and La Cave.

The Lion d’Argent stands deep for its width; though its frontage shows few windows on the faubourg Saint-Denis, the building stretches back through a long courtyard to stables. Entry is through a passage beneath the second floor where two or three coaches could stand. In 1822 the offices of all lines leaving the Lion d’Argent were kept by the innkeeper’s wife, who kept as many books as there were lines. She received fares, booked passengers, and stowed packages in her vast kitchen, while travellers waited under the chimney, in the passage, or at the corner Cafe de l’Echiquier.

One early autumn Saturday in 1822 Pierrotin stood with his hands in his blouse beneath the porte-cochere of the Lion d’Argent, seeing diagonally into the kitchen and, through the courtyard, the stables. Daumartin’s diligence had just started far ahead of the Touchards’. It was past eight. Under the enormous porch the stablemen and porters watched the lively start that deceives travellers into thinking the horses will keep that speed.

“Shall I harness up, master?” asked Pierrotin’s hostler when nothing more appeared down the road.

“It is a quarter-past eight, and I don’t see any travellers,” Pierrotin replied. “Harness up all the same. And there are no parcels either! Twenty good Gods! a fine day like this, and I’ve only four booked! A pretty state of things for a Saturday! It is always the same when you want money! A dog’s life, and a dog’s business!”

“If you had more, where would you put them? There’s nothing left but the cabriolet,” the hostler said.

“You forget the new coach!” Pierrotin cried.

“Have you really got it?” laughed the man.

“You old good-for-nothing! It starts tomorrow; and I want at least eighteen passengers for it.”

“A fine affair; it’ll warm up the road,” the hostler said.

“A coach like Beaumont’s, painted to make Touchard burst with envy! It takes three horses! I have bought a mate for Rougeot, and Bichette will go finely in unicorn. Come, harness up!” Pierrotin added, glancing toward the street and stuffing tobacco into his pipe. Spotting a lady and child approaching with packages, he recognized an old customer.

This apparently trivial talk stirred anxieties in Pierrotin. What could trouble him about a fine new coach? He meant to shine on the road, rival Touchard, attract passengers who praised conveniences rather than grumble about his “sabots”—such was his laudable ambition. Hoping to outstrip his comrade and perhaps inherit the line if the latter retired, he had overreached. He ordered the coach from Farry, Breilmann & Co., who had just introduced square English springs; they would deliver only for coin. Not liking to build an undependable vehicle, they demanded a preliminary payment of two thousand francs. To satisfy this, Pierrotin had exhausted resources and credit: his wife, father-in-law, and friends had been bled. The coach he had seen at the painter’s the evening before needed only final payment to be delivered, but he lacked a thousand francs. He owed the Lion d’Argent and risked losing the two thousand already paid, not counting five hundred for Rougeot’s mate and three hundred for new harnesses on three months’ credit. Driven by despair and vanity, he had declared openly the new coach would start the morrow. By offering fifteen hundred instead of the two thousand five hundred still due, he hoped to soften the coach-builders. After a moment’s thought he cried, “No! they’re dogs! harpies! Suppose I appeal to Monsieur Moreau, the steward at Presles? He is such a kind man,” struck by a new plan. “Perhaps he would take my note for six months.”

Just then a footman in livery, carrying a leather portmanteau and coming from the Touchard establishment where he had been too late to secure places as far as Chambly, came up and asked, “Are you Pierrotin?”

“Yes?” replied Pierrotin.

“If you would wait a quarter of an hour, you could take my master. If not, I’ll carry back the portmanteau and try some other conveyance.”

“I’ll wait two, three quarters, and throw in a little besides, my lad,” said Pierrotin, eyeing the pretty trunk with a brass plate and coat of arms.

“Very good; then take this,” said the valet, handing over the trunk. Pierrotin inspected and weighed it.

“Wrap it up in soft hay and put it in the boot. There’s no name upon it.”

“Monseigneur’s arms are there,” replied the valet.

“Monseigneur! Come and take a glass,” said Pierrotin, nodding toward the Cafe de l’Echiquier, and led the valet there. “Waiter, two absinthes! Who is your master, and where is he going? I have never seen you before.”

“There’s a reason for that,” said the footman. “My master only comes to your parts about once a year, and then in his own carriage. He prefers the valley d’Orge, where he has the most beautiful park near Paris, a perfect Versailles; a family estate of which he bears the name. Don’t you know Monsieur Moreau?”

“The steward of Presles?”

“Yes. Monsieur le Comte is going down to spend a couple of days with him.”

“Ha! then I’m to carry Monsieur le Comte de Serizy!” cried Pierrotin.

“Yes, neither more nor less. But listen! here’s a special order. If you have any of the country neighbors in your coach you are not to call him Monsieur le Comte; he wants to travel ‘en cognito,’ and told me to say he would pay a handsome pourboire if not recognized.”

“So! Has this secret journey anything to do with the affair which Pere Leger, the farmer at the Moulineaux, came to Paris the other day to settle?”

“I don’t know,” the valet replied, “but the fat’s in the fire. Last night I was sent to the stable to order the Daumont carriage to be ready at seven this morning. But when seven o’clock came Monsieur le Comte countermanded it. Augustin, his valet de chambre, attributes the change to the visit of a lady who called last night and again this morning—he thought she came from the country.”

“Could she have told him anything against Monsieur Moreau?—the best of men, the most honest of men, a king of men, hey! He might have made a deal more than he has out of his position, if he’d chosen; I can tell you that.”

“Then he was foolish,” answered the valet sententiously.

“Is Monsieur le Serizy going to live at Presles at last?” asked Pierrotin; “for you know they have just repaired and refurnished the chateau. Do you think it is true he has already spent two hundred thousand francs upon it?”

“If you or I had half what he has spent upon it, we would be rich bourgeois. If Madame la comtesse goes there—ha! no more ease and comfort for the Moreaus,” said the valet mysteriously.

“He’s a worthy man, Monsieur Moreau,” remarked Pierrotin, thinking of the thousand francs he wanted. “He makes others work but doesn’t cheapen what they do; and he gets all he can out of the land—for his master. Honest man! He often comes to Paris and gives me a good fee: he has lots of errands for me; sometimes three or four packages a day—either from monsieur or madame. My bill for cartage alone comes to fifty francs a month, more or less. If madame does set up to be somebody, she’s fond of her children; and it is I who fetch them from school and take them back; and each time she gives me five francs—a real great lady couldn’t do better than that. And every time I have anyone in the coach belonging to them or going to see them, I’m allowed to drive up to the chateau—that’s all right, isn’t it?”

“They say Monsieur Moreau wasn’t worth three thousand francs when Monsieur le comte made him steward of Presles,” said the valet.

“Well, since 1806, there’s seventeen years, and the man ought to have made something at any rate.”

“True,” said the valet, nodding. “Anyway, masters are very annoying; and I hope, for Moreau’s sake, that he has made butter for his bread.”

“I have often been to your house in the rue de la Chaussee d’Antin to carry baskets of game,” Pierrotin continued, “but I’ve never had the advantage, so far, of seeing either monsieur or madame.”

“Monsieur le comte is a good man,” said the footman confidentially. “But if he insists on your helping to keep up his cognito there’s something in the wind. At any rate, so we think at the house; or else, why should he countermand the Daumont—why travel in a coucou? A peer of France might afford to hire a cabriolet to himself, one would think.”

“A cabriolet would cost him forty francs to go there and back; for let me tell you, if you don’t know it, that road was only made for squirrels—up-hill and down, down-hill and up!” said Pierrotin. “Peer of France or bourgeois, they are all looking after the main chance, and saving their money. If this journey concerns Monsieur Moreau, faith, I’d be sorry any harm should come to him! Twenty good Gods! hadn’t I better find some way of warning him?—for he’s a truly good man, a kind man, a king of men, hey!”

“Pooh! Monsieur le comte thinks everything of Monsieur Moreau,” replied the valet. “But let me give you a bit of good advice. Every man for himself in this world. We have enough to do to take care of ourselves. Do what Monsieur le comte asks you to do, and all the more because there’s no trifling with him. Besides, to tell the truth, the count is generous. If you oblige him so far,” said the valet, pointing halfway down his little finger, “he’ll send you on as far as that,” stretching his arm to its full length.

This prudent reflection, coming from a man who stood as high as second valet to the Comte de Serizy, cooled Pierrotin’s ardor for appealing to the steward at Presles.

A glance rapidly cast on the life of the Comte de Serizy, and on that of his steward, is here necessary to fully understand the little drama now about to take place in Pierrotin’s vehicle.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the celebrated président Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family arms are party per pale or and sable, with an orle and two lozenges counterchanged; the motto “i, semper melius eris,” with distaffs as supporters, reflects the modest pride of bourgeois families elevated when Orders held office—the pun on “eris” and the letters of “melius” yielding Serisy, the estate-name of the family.

The present count’s father had been a parliament president before the Revolution. The count himself, a councillor of State at the Grand Council in 1787 at twenty-two, wrote admirable memoranda on delicate diplomatic matters. He did not emigrate during the Revolution, spending those troubled years on his Serizy estate near Arpajon, where his father’s standing shielded him. After tending his dying father in 1794, he was elected to the Council of Five Hundred and accepted legislative duties to divert his grief. After 18th Brumaire, like many old parliamentary families, Monsieur de Serizy accepted Napoleon’s overtures. Appointed to the Council of State, he reconstructed a disorganized department and became useful to the regime.

Soon called to a ministry, he was made count and senator by the Emperor and served as proconsul to two kingdoms. In 1806, at forty, he married the sister of the former Marquis de Ronquerolles, a widow at twenty of General Gaubert, who had left her property. The marriage matched his rank, increased his fortune, and linked him to the former Marquis de Rouvre, later a count and chamberlain under the Emperor.

Weary and ill in 1814, the Comte de Serizy resigned, left his department, and came to Paris. Napoleon at first suspected defection but accepted the count’s illness as cause. Though never disgraced, the senator felt wronged by the Emperor. When the Bourbons returned, Louis XVIII, whom the count regarded as his legitimate sovereign, trusted him with private affairs and made him a cabinet minister. On March 20 the count did not go to Ghent; he told Napoleon he remained faithful to the Bourbons, would not accept a peerage during the Hundred Days, and stayed at his estate.

After Napoleon’s second fall he resumed office as privy councillor, became vice-president of the Council of State, and acted as liquidator of claims and indemnities demanded by foreign powers. Unambitious personally but influential, little of importance occurred without his counsel; he avoided court and was seldom seen in salons. A life of incessant work had formed his habit: rising at four to work until midday, attending duties in the afternoon, and sleeping by nine. He held the grand cross of the Legion of Honor and several foreign orders. No man was less conspicuous or more useful. Public honors mattered little to him; but such devotion rarely lacks a serious motive. His conduct had a cruel cause.

He had loved his wife before marrying her; the passion endured through the secret unhappiness of wedding a self-possessed widow who used her liberty, while he treated her with indulgent deference. His ceaseless toil shielded him from a pain he silenced with a diplomat’s care for secrets. He knew jealousy would seem ridiculous in a society that would not believe an old statesman’s conjugal passion. How his wife fascinated him, why he suffered without resistance, why he did not fight, and why he still hoped would require a long history; here they are only hinted at. One cruel fact was that his labor had caused a skin malady which repelled his wife. Kind and considerate, he permitted her to be mistress of herself and their home. She received all Paris, spent time in the country, and returned as though still a widow. He managed her fortune and supplied her luxury like a steward. The countess admired his mind and could please him by approval; an hour’s talk in his study made him pliable. Like old seigneurs, he protected her so loyally that any disrespect toward her would have seemed an unforgivable injury. The world admired him; Madame de Serizy benefited by that admiration. She was ungrateful, yet mingled charm with her ingratitude and sometimes soothed his wounds.

This explains the sudden journey and incognito of a minister of State.

A rich farmer of Beaumont-sur-Oise named Leger leased and cultivated a farm whose fields projected into and troubled the Comte de Serizy’s Presles estate. The farm belonged to a burgher, Margueron. The lease made to Leger in 1799, before the agricultural boom, was about to expire, and Margueron refused Leger’s offers to renew. Monsieur de Serizy wanted the farm to stop the annoyance of enclosed fields and heard Margueron’s chief ambition was to have his only son, then a mere tax-gatherer, appointed special collector of finances at Beaumont. Farmer Leger, knowing he could sell parcels to the count at a high price, planned to buy and resell for profit.

Two days before our narrative, anxious to settle the matter, Monsieur de Serizy summoned his notary Alexandre Crottat and his lawyer Derville to examine the situation. Though Derville and Crottat distrusted the count’s steward—prompted by a disturbing letter from him—the count defended Moreau, who had served him faithfully for seventeen years.

“Very well,” Derville said, “then go to Presles yourself and invite Margueron to dinner. Crottat will take a deed drawn, leaving only necessary lines blank for property description and titles. Take part of the purchase money in a Bank of France check, and the appointment of the son to the collectorship. If you don’t settle this now the farm will slip through your fingers. You don’t know the trickery of peasants. Peasants against diplomat, and the diplomat succumbs.”

Crottat agreed, and, as suggested by his valet Pierrotin, the count adopted the plan. The preceding evening he had sent Moreau a line by diligence to Beaumont, telling him to invite Margueron to dinner so they might close the purchase.

Before this affair, the count had ordered the château of Presles restored and refurnished; for a year Grindot, an architect then in fashion, had made weekly visits. While concluding the purchase the count intended to examine restorations and new furniture as a surprise for his wife when he brought her to Presles; he had put some personal pride into the work. Why the count, who planned to drive to Presles in his own carriage in the evening, should set out early the next morning incognito in Pierrotin’s coucou needs explanation.

A few words on the steward Moreau are necessary.

Moreau was the son of a provincial attorney who became syndic-attorney at Versailles during the Revolution. In that post Moreau the elder almost saved the Serizys’ lives and property; he belonged to Danton’s party, was pursued by Robespierre, and was executed at Versailles. Moreau the son, inheriting his father’s doctrines and friendships, was implicated in a conspiracy against the First Consul. Monsieur de Serizy, wishing to repay debts, enabled Moreau, under sentence of death, to escape; in 1804 he sought his pardon, obtained it, offered him a government post, and finally made him private secretary.

Some time after his patron’s marriage, Moreau fell in love with the countess’s waiting-woman and married her. To avoid the annoyances of that position at court, Moreau asked to manage the Presles estate, where his wife could play lady and neither would suffer wounded pride. The count wanted a trustworthy man at Presles because his wife preferred Serizy, an estate only fifteen miles from Paris. For three or four years Moreau thus held the key of the count’s affairs; he was intelligent, had studied law in his father’s office, and the count granted his request.

“You can never advance in life,” the count told Moreau, “for you have broken your neck; but you can be happy, and I will take care that you are so.”

He gave Moreau a salary of three thousand francs, a lodge near the château, all the wood needed from estate timber, oats, hay, and straw for two horses, and a right to the gardens’ produce. A sub-prefect was not as well provided for.

For the first eight years Moreau managed the estate conscientiously; he took interest in it. The count, visiting now and then to examine property and purchases, admired Moreau’s loyalty and rewarded him liberally.

But after the birth of Moreau’s third child—a daughter—he felt secure and ceased to see his advantages as gifts. About 1816, the steward, who had until then taken only what he needed, accepted twenty-five thousand francs from a wood-merchant as inducement to lease timber cutting for twelve years. Moreau argued that he could have no pension, was father of a family, and the count owed him that sum as a gift after ten years’ management; with sixty thousand francs already saved, this would let him buy a farm worth 125,000 francs in Champagne, above Isle-Adam on the Oise. Political events prevented the count and neighbors from noticing this investment, made in Madame Moreau’s name as if she had inherited from an aunt.

Once he tasted possession, Moreau, while keeping an appearance of integrity, seized opportunities to increase his fortune clandestinely; the interests of his three children served as a poultice to his conscience. Nevertheless, by local custom he shared with the count in profits due to his management. This swelling of his fortune troubled his conscience. Alert and thoroughly understanding the count’s interests, Moreau watched for good purchases, gaining a larger percentage. Presles returned a net revenue of seventy thousand francs. The countryside said for thirty miles: “Monsieur de Serizy has a second self in Moreau.”

Prudent, Moreau invested yearly after 1817, piling up savings secretly. He often played the poor man so well that the count sent his sons to the school Henri IV. At present Moreau held 120,000 francs invested in the Consolidated thirds, paying five percent and quoted at eighty francs. These hidden 120,000 and his Champagne farm, enlarged by purchases, amounted to about 280,000 francs, giving an income of some 16,000 francs.

Such was the steward’s position when the Comte de Serizy wished to buy the Moulineaux farm—ownership indispensable to his comfort. The farm consisted of ninety-six parcels bordering Presles and often running into it, causing disputes about hedges, ditches, and tree-cutting. Any other than a cabinet minister would probably have faced lawsuits. Pere Leger wished to buy the property only to resell it to the count at a handsome advance. To secure that sum he sought an understanding with Moreau. Three days before the crucial Sunday he talked with the steward and showed that Moreau could make the count invest at two and a half percent and thus pocket forty thousand francs off Leger’s offer.

“I tell you what,” Moreau said to his wife as he went to bed, “if I make fifty thousand francs out of the Moulineaux affair—and I certainly shall, for the count will give me ten thousand as a fee—we’ll retire to Isle-Adam and live in the Pavillon de Nogent.”

This “pavillon,” built by the Prince de Conti for a mistress, was well furnished.

“That will suit me,” his wife replied. “The Dutchman there has put it in order and now returns to India; he might let us have it for thirty thousand francs.”

“We shall be close to Champagne,” said Moreau. “I hope to buy the farm and mill of Mours for a hundred thousand francs. That would give us ten thousand a year. Nogent is one of the most delightful residences in the valley; and we should still have an income of ten thousand from the Grand-Livre.”

“But why not ask for the post of juge-de-paix at Isle-Adam? That would give influence and fifteen hundred a year.”

“Well, I did think of it.”

With these plans, Moreau, hearing the count was coming to Presles and wished him to invite Margueron to dinner on Saturday, sent an express to the count’s head-valet enclosing a letter to his master, which the messenger failed to deliver before Monsieur de Serizy retired. Augustin, however, placed it on his master’s desk in the morning. In the letter Moreau begged the count not to come, but to trust him entirely; he added that Margueron was no longer willing to sell the whole at once and talked of cutting the farm into lots. It was necessary to circumvent this plan, and perhaps employ a third party to make the purchase.

Everybody has enemies. The steward and his wife had offended a retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, and his wife, who lived near Presles. From pin-prick speeches matters had advanced to dagger thrusts. Monsieur de Reybert sought vengeance; he aimed to make Moreau lose his situation and gain it himself. Reybert had spied on the steward for two years. The same conveyance that took Moreau’s letter to the count carried Madame de Reybert to Paris, whom her husband had despatched. There she so earnestly asked to see the count that although sent away at nine, she was ushered into his study the next morning at seven.

“Monsieur,” she said to the cabinet minister, “we cannot write anonymous letters, so I have come in person. I am Madame de Reybert, née de Corroy. My husband is a retired officer with a pension of six hundred francs, and we live at Presles, where your steward has offered us insult after insult, though we are persons of good station. Monsieur de Reybert, who is not intriguing, retired in 1816 after twenty years—always at a distance from the Emperor. You know how difficult it is for soldiers not under the eye of their master to obtain promotion; my husband’s integrity displeased superiors. He has watched your steward for three years, aware of his dishonesty and intending to have him dismissed. We have been frank with you. Moreau plans to get an extra hundred thousand francs out of you, to be divided between the notary, the farmer Leger, and Moreau. You have written Moreau to invite Margueron, and you are going to Presles today; but Margueron will be ill, and Leger is so certain of buying he is now in Paris with the money. If we have enlightened you, and you want an upright steward, take my husband; though noble, he will serve you as he has served the State. Your steward has made two hundred and fifty thousand francs from his place; he is not to be pitied.”

The count thanked Madame de Reybert coldly, giving the holy-water of courts, for he despised backbiting; but he remembered Derville’s doubts and felt shaken. He then saw Moreau’s letter and read it. In its assurances of devotion and blaming implied distrust, he read truth.

“Corruption has come to him with fortune—as it always does!” he said.

The count then questioned Madame de Reybert, less for information than to observe her, and wrote a short note to his notary telling him not to send his head-clerk to Presles as requested, but to come himself in time for dinner.

“Though Monsieur le comte,” Madame de Reybert concluded, “may have judged me unfavorably for acting without my husband, he ought to be convinced we obtained this information in a natural and honorable manner; the most sensitive conscience cannot take exception to it.”

So saying, Madame de Reybert, née de Corroy, stood erect. She presented a face seamed with smallpox, a flat spare figure, two light eager eyes, fair hair plastered on an anxious forehead, a small drawn bonnet of faded green taffetas lined with pink, a white gown with violet spots, and leather shoes. The count recognized the wife of some poor, half-pay captain: a puritan, earnest in virtue but mindful of comfort and eager for a good situation.

“You say your husband has a pension of six hundred francs,” he remarked.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“You were born a Corroy?”

“Yes, monsieur,—a noble family of Metz, where my husband belongs.”

“In what regiment did Monsieur de Reybert serve?”

“The 7th artillery.”

“Good,” said the count, writing the number.

He had thought of entrusting estate management to some retired officer, about whom he could obtain exact information from the minister of war.

“Madame,” he resumed, ringing for his valet, “return to Presles this afternoon with my notary, who is going down for dinner and to whom I have recommended you. Here is his address. I am going myself secretly to Presles, and will send for Monsieur de Reybert to come and speak to me.”

Thus Monsieur de Serizy’s journey by public conveyance and his valet’s injunction to conceal his name and rank had not needlessly alarmed Pierrotin. That worthy had forebodings of a danger about to swoop down upon one of his best customers.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Pierrotin emerged from the Café de l’Échiquier after tending the valet, he saw in the gateway of the Lion d’Argent a lady and a young man he recognized as customers; the lady, with an outstretched neck and anxious face, looked for him. She wore a dyed black-silk gown, a brown bonnet, an old French cashmere shawl, raw-silk stockings, low shoes, and carried a straw bag. Once beautiful, she seemed about forty; her blue eyes, stripped of happiness’s fire, showed she had long renounced the world. Her dress and manner indicated a mother wholly devoted to home and son. The bonnet’s faded strings and worn shape betrayed its age; the shawl was fastened with a broken needle stuck into sealing-wax. She waited impatiently for Pierrotin, wishing to entrust her son—doubtless travelling for the first time—to his care.

The son completed the picture. If the mother mended gloves, the son wore an olive-green coat with sleeves too short, showing he had grown; his blue trousers, patched by his mother, revealed a brighter patch when the coat-tails parted.

“Don’t rub your gloves that way, you’ll spoil them,” she said as Pierrotin appeared. “Is this the conductor? Ah! Pierrotin, is it you?” she exclaimed, leaving her son and taking the coachman aside.

“I hope you’re well, Madame Clapart,” he replied, with a blend of respect and familiarity.

“Yes, Pierrotin, very well. Please take good care of my Oscar; he is travelling alone for the first time.”

“Oh! so he is going alone to Monsieur Moreau!” Pierrotin cried, to learn whether he was really going there.

“Yes,” said the mother.

“Then Madame Moreau is willing?” returned Pierrotin, with a sly look.

“Ah!” said the mother, “it will not be all roses for him, poor child! But his future absolutely requires that I should send him.”

This answer struck Pierrotin. He hesitated to reveal his fears for the steward to Madame Clapart, while she feared harming her son by asking for too much care. During this brief deliberation, covered by remarks on weather, journey, and stopping places, we must explain the ties that united Madame Clapart with Pierrotin and authorized their remarks.

Often—three or four times a month—Pierrotin, on his way to Paris, would meet the steward near La Cave. As soon as the vehicle arrived, Moreau would signal a gardener, who, with Pierrotin’s help, would put on the coach one or two baskets containing fruits, vegetables, chickens, eggs, butter, and game. The steward always paid the carriage and Pierrotin’s fee, adding money for the toll at the barrière if the baskets contained dutiable goods. These parcels were never addressed. On the first occasion, the steward had given Madame Clapart’s address by word of mouth to the discreet Pierrotin, requesting he never deliver the packages to others. Pierrotin, imagining an intrigue between the steward and some pretty girl, went to number 7 rue de la Cerisaie and found Madame Clapart instead of the beauty he expected.

Drivers of public conveyances enter many homes and learn many secrets; social accident often places them in intimate proximity, but being without education and poor observers, they are not usually dangerous. Nevertheless, after months Pierrotin was puzzled to explain Moreau and Madame Clapart’s exact relation from what he saw of the household. Though lodgings were not dear in the Arsenal quarter, Madame Clapart lived on a third floor at the end of a courtyard in a house once belonging to a great family when the nobility inhabited the old Tournelles and Saint-Paul sites. Toward the late sixteenth century those grandeurs were divided into the present streets—Cerisaie, Beautreillis, des Lions, etc. Madame Clapart’s flat, panelled with ancient carvings, consisted of three rooms—dining-room, salon, and bedroom; above was the kitchen and Oscar’s bedroom. Opposite the entrance, on the square landing, was a door opening into a rough stone tower built with a well for the staircase; that back room was where Moreau slept when in Paris.

Pierrotin had seen in the first room, where he deposited the hampers, six wooden chairs with straw seats, a table, and a sideboard; the windows had discolored curtains. In the salon he noticed old Empire furniture now shabby; the bedroom matched the salon and dining-room. The coarsely painted woodwork blurred the mouldings; the floors, never waxed, had the gray tone of boarding-schools. When Pierrotin visited Monsieur and Madame Clapart at meals he noted that the china and glass betrayed the utmost poverty; yet the forks and spoons were silver.

Monsieur Clapart, in a shabby surtout and broken slippers, always wore green spectacles and, when he removed his shabby cap, showed a pointed skull with a few dirty filaments hardly to be called hair. Timorous yet tyrannical, he looked wan.

In that dreary north-facing apartment, with only a vine and a well to look on, Madame Clapart bore herself like a queen and moved like a woman unaccustomed to walking. Often, while thanking Pierrotin, she gave him glances to touch a sensitive observer and would slip a twelve-sous piece into his hand; her voice then became charming. Pierrotin had never seen Oscar because the boy was always at school when his business took him to the house.

Here is the sad story Pierrotin could not have discovered by asking the portress, who only knew the Claparts paid 250 francs a year, had no servant but a charwoman coming a few hours each morning, that Madame Clapart did some washing herself, and paid postage daily as she could not let money accumulate.

There seldom exists a criminal wholly criminal; nor a dishonest nature entirely dishonest. A man may cheat his master yet do good at times. If Moreau’s faults can be excused, it might be for his persistent kindness to a woman whose favours he once claimed and in whose house he had hidden when condemned to death in 1797.

This woman, celebrated under the Directory for a liaison with a powerful protector, married through that protection a purveyor named Husson, who made millions from government contracts but whom Napoleon ruined in 1802. Husson became insane and threw himself into the Seine, leaving Madame Husson pregnant. Moreau, intimately allied with her, was then condemned to death and could not marry her; forced to leave France, he watched over her. Madame Husson, at twenty-two, married a clerk named Clapart, aged twenty-seven, rumored to be a rising man. But Clapart, though coarse and handsome, had no intelligence. Expecting wealth, he feigned passion but could not supply the wants she had known. He filled a Treasury post badly, giving a salary of eighteen hundred francs, which was all the household had to live on. When Moreau returned as secretary to the Comte de Serizy he learned of Madame Husson’s plight and obtained, before his own marriage, an appointment for her as head waiting-woman to Madame Mère, the Emperor’s mother. Despite such protection, Clapart was never promoted; his incapacity showed.

Ruined in 1815 by the fall of the Empire, the brilliant Aspasia of the Directory had no resources other than Clapart’s clerk’s salary of twelve hundred francs from a job procured by the Comte de Serizy. Moreau, the only protector of a woman he had known in opulence, obtained a half-scholarship for her son, Oscar Husson, at the school of Henri IV, and sent her regularly, by Pierrotin, such supplies from the estate at Presles as he could decently offer to a household in distress.

Oscar was his mother’s whole life and future. The poor woman’s only fault was exaggerated tenderness for her boy—the bête-noire of his stepfather. Oscar, unfortunately, had a foolish overweening conceit his mother did not perceive despite the stepfather’s sarcasms. This conceit so worried Monsieur Moreau that he begged Madame Clapart to send the boy to him for a month so he might study his character and find a suitable career; Moreau thought of proposing Oscar as his successor to the count.

To give each thing its due, we should show the causes of Oscar’s silly vanity: he was born in Madame Mère’s household, dazzled in childhood by imperial splendors. His imagination retained those images and nursed hopes of regaining a golden past. The natural boastfulness of schoolboys, resting on these memories and on maternal tales of former splendour, developed excessively. Oscar, leaving school, had borne many humiliations as a scholarship pupil among paying students unless the scholar can impose respect by physical superiority.

This mixture of departed splendor, faded beauty, blind maternal love, and heroic suffering made the mother a pathetic figure in Paris.

Incapable of understanding Moreau’s real attachment to her, and of that woman’s for the man who saved her in 1797, Pierrotin did not communicate his suspicion of danger to Moreau. His thought, “We have enough to do in this world to look after ourselves,” returned to mind, along with his obedience to what he called the “chefs de file”—men of rank. Besides, Pierrotin’s head was full of his own stings like coins in a purse. So his “Very good, madame,” “Certainly, madame,” in reply to the poor mother showed his desire to escape her long instructions.

“You will be sure to place the packages so they can’t get wet if the weather changes.”

“I’ve a hood,” replied Pierrotin. “Besides, see with what care they are placed.”

“Oscar, don’t stay more than two weeks, no matter how much they may ask you,” continued Madame Clapart, returning to her son. “You can’t please Madame Moreau, whatever you do; besides, you must be home by the end of September. We are to go to Belleville, you know, to your uncle Cardot.”

“Yes, mamma.”

“Above all,” she said in a low voice, “never speak about servants; keep thinking all the time that Madame Moreau was once a waiting-maid.”

“Yes, mamma.”

Like all youths whose vanity is excessively ticklish, Oscar seemed annoyed at being lectured on the threshold of the Lion d’Argent.

“Well, now good-bye, mamma. We shall start soon; there’s the horse all harnessed.”

The mother, forgetting she was in the street, embraced Oscar and smiling took a little roll from her basket.

“Tiens! you were forgetting your roll and the chocolate! My child, don’t take anything at the inns; they’ll charge you ten times the value.”

Oscar would have liked his mother farther off as she stuffed the roll and chocolate into his pocket. Two young men a few years older than Oscar, better dressed and without a mother hanging on them—complete in independence—witnessed the scene.

“He said mamma!” cried one, laughing.

The words reached Oscar and drove him to say, “Good-bye, mother!” in a tone of terrible impatience.

Madame Clapart spoke too loudly and seemed desirous to show her tenderness. “What is the matter with you, Oscar?” she asked. “I don’t know what to make of you,” she added severely, then resumed in her tender voice, “Listen, my Oscar—do not talk and show off; keep your tongue in check. You are not advanced enough to judge persons; there is nothing more dangerous than speaking in public conveyances. Besides, in a diligence well-bred persons always keep silence.”

The two young men had walked to the other end of the establishment and returned with boot-heels tapping. To rid himself of his mother, Oscar took an heroic measure that showed vanity stimulating intellect.

“Mamma,” he said, “you are standing in a draught, and you may take cold. Besides, I am going to get into the coach.”

The lad touched a tender spot; his mother caught him to her bosom, kissed him as if he were starting on a long journey, and went with him to the vehicle with tears. “Don’t forget to give five francs to the servants when you come away,” she said; “write me three times at least during the fifteen days; behave properly, and remember all I have told you. You have linen enough; don’t send any to the wash. And above all, remember Monsieur Moreau’s kindness; mind him as you would a father, and follow his advice.”

As he got into the coach Oscar’s blue woollen stockings became visible as his trousers pulled up, and the new patch showed through the parting coat-tails. The smiles of the two young men, on whom these signs of respectable indigence were not lost, were fresh wounds to his vanity.

“The first place was engaged for Oscar,” said the mother to Pierrotin. “Take the back seat,” she said to the boy with a loving smile.

Oh how Oscar regretted that trouble and sorrow had destroyed his mother’s beauty and that poverty prevented her being better dressed! One young man, wearing top-boots and spurs, nudged the other to notice Oscar’s mother; the other twirled his moustache as if to say, “Rather a pretty figure!”

“How shall I ever get rid of mamma?” thought Oscar.

“What’s the matter?” asked Madame Clapart.

He pretended not to hear. Perhaps she lacked tact; all absorbing sentiments have much egoism.

“Georges, do you like children when travelling?” asked one young man of the other.

“Yes, my good Amaury, if they are weaned, and are named Oscar, and have chocolate.”

These remarks, uttered in half-tones, allowed Oscar to hear or not; his face would indicate how much fun they might make of him. Oscar chose not to hear and looked to see if his mother, weighing on him like a nightmare, was still there. He compared his companion’s dress with his own and felt that his mother’s toilet counted for much in the youths’ smiles. “If they would only take themselves off!” he thought.

Instead, Amaury tapped the coucou’s wheel and remarked to Georges, “And so, my friend, you are really going to trust your future to this fragile bark?”

“I must,” replied Georges, fatalistically.

Oscar sighed as he noted the jaunty hat of his companion, revealing blond hair; he had black hair cut close by order of his stepfather. The chubby healthy face of the vain lad contrasted with the long pale one of the other. The latter’s broad forehead and filled chest showed a cashmere waistcoat; his tight iron-gray trousers and frogged overcoat looked elegant; the sight galled Oscar like an ugly woman seeing a pretty one. The click of the stranger’s boot-heels echoed in his heart; he felt hampered by his clothes—which were probably his stepfather’s—while the other seemed at ease.

“That fellow must have heaps of francs in his trousers pocket,” thought Oscar.

The young man turned. Oscar saw a gold chain and a watch; from then on the young man assumed the proportions of a personage.

Living in rue de la Cerisaie since 1815, taken to and from school by his stepfather and seldom seeing anything but his mother’s poverty, Oscar had little comparison. Brought up strictly on Moreau’s advice, he seldom went to the theatre except to the Ambigu-Comique. His stepfather still wore his watch in the fob with a heavy gold chain and various ornaments; Oscar considered that old-fashioned finery the “ne plus ultra.” He was bewildered by the revelation of superior, negligent elegance: spotless gloves, a gold-headed switch cane twirled with grace.

Oscar had reached that last quarter of adolescence when little things cause immense joys and miseries—a period when youth prefers misfortune to a ridiculous suit and torments itself about neckcloths and the desire to appear a man. Then the lad swells; his swagger is portentous because it boasts about nothings. Yet a lad who envies a well-dressed fool may also admire talent. Such defects, unless rooted in the heart, prove only youthful exuberance. For a nineteen-year-old only child kept at home by poverty and adored by an indulgent mother to envy a twenty-two-year-old in a frogged surtout is a peccadillo common to all ranks.

But Oscar passed from peccadillo to ill feeling. He felt humiliated, angry with the youth he envied, and secretly desired to show he was as good.

The two young fellows continued walking up and down from the gate to the stables; each time they turned they looked at Oscar curled in his corner. Oscar, convinced their jokes concerned him, affected indifference. He began to hum a chorus lately in vogue among liberals ending, “‘Tis Voltaire’s fault, ‘tis Rousseau’s fault.”

“Tiens! perhaps he is one of the chorus at the Opera,” said Amaury.

This exasperated Oscar, who bounded up, pulled out the wooden “back,” and called to Pierrotin: “When do we start?”

“Presently,” said Pierrotin, whip in hand, gazing toward rue d’Enghien.

At this moment a young man arrived with a true gamin followed by a porter dragging a hand-cart. The young man spoke confidentially to Pierrotin, who nodded and called his porter. The porter helped unload the cart, which contained two trunks, buckets, brushes, oddly shaped boxes, and innumerable packages that the gamin stowed with such speed Oscar, then smiling at his mother across the street, noticed none of the paraphernalia that might reveal his new companion’s profession.

The gamin, about sixteen, wore a gray blouse buckled by a leather belt, a cap jauntily perched, curly brown hair on his shoulders, a black-silk cravat around a very white neck, bright gray eyes, and a vivacious, mischievous face—Figaro-like. His gestures and mocking eye showed a lively intellect born of early vocation. Unconcerned about costume, he looked at his unpolished boots as a quizzical effect. “I’m in style,” he said, shaking himself and addressing his companion.

The companion’s glance showed authority over the adept; a practised eye would call the gamin a “rapin.” “Behave yourself, Mistigris,” said his master.

The master was a slight pale young man with thick black hair worn fantastically. His abundant hair suited an enormous head with a vast forehead proclaiming precocious intellect. His strained, harassed face was hollowed as if from chronic malady, poverty, or recent grief. His shabby American-green surtout was clean and brushed; a black waistcoat buttoned to the throat almost concealed a scarlet neckerchief; black trousers flapped his thin legs; muddy boots showed he had come on foot. With a rapid look he seized the Lion d’Argent scene, then glanced at Mistigris’s satirical stare.

“Charming!” said Mistigris.

“Yes, very,” replied the other.

“We seem to have got here too early,” continued Mistigris. “Couldn’t we get a mouthful somewhere? My stomach, like Nature, abhors a vacuum.”

“Have we time for a cup of coffee?” the artist asked Pierrotin.

“Yes, but don’t be long,” answered Pierrotin.

“Good; that means a quarter of an hour,” said Mistigris.

The pair disappeared. Nine o’clock was striking in the hotel kitchen. Georges thought it reasonable to remonstrate with Pierrotin.

“Hey! my friend; with such wheels as these (striking the clumsy tires with his cane) one might expect punctuality. I have devilishly pressing business or I wouldn’t trust my bones to it. And that horse you call Rougeot doesn’t look fit to make up for lost time.”

“We are going to harness Bichette while those gentlemen take their coffee,” replied Pierrotin. “Go and ask,” he said to his porter, “if Père Léger is coming with us—”

“Where is your Père Léger?” asked Georges.

“Over the way, at number 50. He couldn’t get a place in the Beaumont diligence,” said Pierrotin, making no answer to his customer; then he disappeared in search of Bichette.

Georges, after shaking hands with his friend, got into the coach, handling an important portfolio which he placed beneath the seat cushion. He took the opposite corner to Oscar on the same seat.

“This Père Léger troubles me,” he said.

“They can’t take away our places,” replied Oscar. “I have number one.”

“And I number two,” said Georges.

Just as Pierrotin reappeared having harnessed Bichette, the porter returned with a stout man whose weight could not be less than two hundred and fifty pounds. Père Léger belonged to the farmer species—square back, protuberant stomach, powdered pigtail, little blue linen coat, white gaiters above the knee fastened over velveteen breeches with silver buckles, and hob-nailed shoes. He held a small polished reddish stick with a large knob and a leather thong.

“And you are called Père Léger?” asked Georges very seriously as the farmer attempted to step up.

“At your service,” replied the farmer with a face like Louis XVIII, fat rubicund cheeks, and a large nose; his smiling eyes sunken in rolls of fat. “Come, a helping hand, my lad!” he said to Pierrotin.

The farmer was hoisted in by Pierrotin and the porter amid cries of “Houp la! hi! ha! hoist!” uttered by Georges.

“Oh! I’m not going far; only to La Cave,” said the farmer, good-humoredly.

In France everyone takes a joke.

“Take the back seat,” said Pierrotin, “there’ll be six of you.”

“Where’s your other horse?” demanded Georges. “Is it as mythical as the third post-horse.”

“There she is,” said Pierrotin, pointing to the little mare coming along alone.

“He calls that insect a horse!” exclaimed Georges.

“Oh! she’s good, that little mare,” said the farmer, now seated. “Your servant, gentlemen. Well, Pierrotin, how soon do you start?”

“I have two travellers in there after a cup of coffee,” replied Pierrotin.

The hollow-cheeked young man and his page reappeared.

“Come, let’s start!” was the general cry.

“We are going to start,” replied Pierrotin. “Now, then, make ready,” he said to the porter, who removed the stones that stopped the wheels.

Pierrotin took Rougeot by the bridle and gave that guttural cry, “Ket, ket!” to urge the two animals; though stiff, they pulled the coach to the Lion d’Argent door. Then Pierrotin gazed up rue d’Enghien and disappeared, leaving the coach to the porter.

“Ah ca! is he subject to such attacks—that master of yours?” said Mistigris to the porter.

“He has gone to fetch his feed from the stable,” replied the porter, versed in tricks to keep passengers calm.

“Well, after all,” said Mistigris, “‘art is long, but life is short’--to Bichette.”

At that time the studios fancied mutilating or transposing proverbs; it was a triumph to change letters or words to keep the semblance while making a fantastic meaning.

“Patience, Mistigris!” said his master; “‘come wheel, come whoa.’”

Pierrotin returned bringing the Comte de Serizy, who had come through rue de l’Échiquier and with whom Pierrotin had doubtless conversed.

“Père Léger,” said Pierrotin, looking into the coach, “will you give your place to Monsieur le comte? That will balance the carriage better.”

“We sha’n’t be off for an hour if you go on this way,” cried Georges. “We shall have to take down this infernal bar, which cost such trouble to put up. Why should everyone move for the man who comes last? We all have a right to the places we took. What place has monsieur engaged? Come, find that out! Haven’t you a way-book, a register, or something? What place has Monsieur Lecomte engaged—count of what, I’d like to know.”

“Monsieur le comte,” said Pierrotin, visibly troubled, “I am afraid you will be uncomfortable.”

“Why didn’t you keep better count of us?” said Mistigris. “‘Short counts make good ends.’”

“Mistigris, behave yourself,” said his master.

Monsieur de Serizy was evidently taken by people in the coach for a bourgeois named Lecomte.

“Don’t disturb anyone,” he said to Pierrotin. “I will sit with you in front.”

“Come, Mistigris,” said the master to his rapin, “remember the respect you owe to age; you don’t know how shockingly old you may be yourself some day. ‘Travel deforms youth.’ Give your place to monsieur.”

Mistigris opened the leather curtain and jumped out with frog-like agility.

“You mustn’t be a rabbit, august old man,” he said to the count.

“Mistigris, ‘ars est celare bonum,’” said his master.

“I thank you very much, monsieur,” said the count to Mistigris’s master, next to whom he now sat.

The minister of State cast a sagacious glance round the coach interior, affronting both Oscar and Georges.

“When persons want to be master of a coach they should engage all the places,” remarked Georges.

Certain now of his incognito, the Comte de Serizy made no reply but assumed a good-natured bourgeois air.

“Suppose you were late, wouldn’t you be glad the coach waited for you?” said the farmer to the two young men.

Pierrotin still looked up and down the street, whip in hand, apparently reluctant to mount the hard seat where Mistigris fidgeted.

“If you expect someone else, I am not the last,” said the count.

“I agree to that reasoning,” said Mistigris.

Georges and Oscar began to laugh impertinently.

“The old fellow doesn’t know much,” whispered Georges to Oscar, who was delighted at this apparent union between himself and the object of his envy.

“Parbleu!” cried Pierrotin, “I shouldn’t be sorry for two more passengers.”

“I haven’t paid; I’ll get out,” said Georges, alarmed.

“What are you waiting for, Pierrotin?” asked Père Léger.

Whereupon Pierrotin shouted a certain “Hi!” in which Bichette and Rougeot recognized a definitive resolution and sprang toward the faubourg at a pace soon to slacken.

The count had a burning red face with inflamed portions highlighted by snow-white hair. These blotches injured his naturally noble air; careful examination was needed to find in his green-gray eyes the shrewdness of a magistrate, the wisdom of a statesman, and a legislator’s knowledge. His face was flat, the nose depressed into it. The hat hid the grace of his forehead. There was enough odd contrast—silvery hair, burning face, thick black brows—to amuse youths.

He wore a long blue overcoat buttoned military-fashion to the throat, a white cravat, cotton wool in his ears, a high shirt-collar making a white patch on each cheek. His black trousers covered his boots whose toes were barely seen. No decoration adorned him; doeskin gloves concealed his hands. Nothing betrayed a peer of France and one of the kingdom’s most useful statesmen.

Père Léger had never seen the count, who knew the farmer only by name. When the count entered the carriage he cast a glance about him that affronted Georges and Oscar but was truly looking for the head-clerk of his notary in case he had been forced, like himself, into Pierrotin’s vehicle, intending to caution him about his incognito. Feeling reassured by Oscar, Père Léger, and the quasi-military air, waxed moustaches, and adventurer look of Georges, he concluded his note had reached his notary Alexandre Crottat in time to keep the clerk from leaving.

“Père Léger,” said Pierrotin when they reached the steep faubourg Saint-Denis at rue de la Fidélité, “suppose we get out, hey?”

“I’ll get out, too,” said the count, hearing Léger’s name.

“Goodness! if this is how we go we shall do fourteen miles in fifteen days!” cried Georges.

“It isn’t my fault,” said Pierrotin, “if a passenger wishes to get out.”

“Ten louis for you if you keep the secret of my being here as I told you before,” said the count in a low voice, taking Pierrotin by the arm.

“Oh, my thousand francs!” thought Pierrotin as he winked at Monsieur de Serizy, meaning, “Rely on me.”

Oscar and Georges stayed in the coach.

“Look here, Pierrotin, since Pierrotin you are,” cried Georges when they were stowed away again, “if you don’t mean to go faster than this, say so! I’ll pay my fare and take a post-horse at Saint-Denis; I have important business.”

“Oh! he’ll go well enough,” said Père Léger. “Besides, the distance isn’t great.”

“I am never more than half an hour late,” asserted Pierrotin.

“Well, you are not wheeling the Pope in this old barrow,” said Georges, “so, get on.”

“Perhaps he’s afraid of shaking monsieur,” said Mistigris looking at the count. “But you shouldn’t have preferences, Pierrotin, it isn’t right.”

“Coucous and the Charter make all Frenchmen equals,” said Georges.

“Oh! be easy,” said Père Léger; “we are sure to get to La Chapelle by mid-day,”—La Chapelle being the village beyond the Barrière of Saint-Denis.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Those who travel in public conveyances know strangers do not speak at once; first comes a period of mutual examination. Once each thinks he has sized up his companions, the most talkative starts, and the conversation livens. That was true in the French stage-coach, where everyone was eager to laugh and show wit.

A young man of twenty-two, Georges, was tempted to display his cleverness. He had judged his fellow passengers: the count as a second-rate manufacturer or chandler; the shabby young man with Mistigris as nobody; Oscar as a ninny; Pere Légèr as an excellent dupe. He resolved to amuse himself at their expense and, as the coach descended from La Chapelle toward Saint-Denis, ran over a catalogue of possible personae—Etienne, Béranger, a Carbonaro, the son of Marshal Ney, a disguised Russian prince, Cousin, Lord Byron—finally choosing a swaggering Eastern-adventurer role. Mistigris’s banter and Pierrotin’s exclamations soon got the talk going.

Georges affected having just returned from the Levant. Though pale, he claimed to have been ill for three months and to have just left his bed after a “germ” of suppressed plague, which produced alarm and gibes. He plunged into tales: he had been to Egypt and Greece; he had served under Ali, pacha of Janina; they had quarrelled; Oriental life had disorganized his liver. When Pere Légèr asked if he had been a soldier and how old he was, Georges answered twenty-nine and spun a patched military history: enlisted as a private in 1813, present at Hanau, promoted sergeant-major, made sub-lieutenant at Montereau and decorated by the Emperor; he implied service at Mont-Saint-Jean in 1815 and said he had been disgusted with France, then gone abroad with comrades to serve a pacha in Egypt. He declared he would not renounce his religion for Islam—and told of receiving a thousand talari from the pacha.

The passengers were captivated. Georges boasted of being the grandson of the famous Czerni-Georges, who had made war on the Porte; his father had died in Paris leaving his mother pregnant; their property was taken and she had married a purveyor, Monsieur Yung; now dead, the stepfather had left Georges estranged. That had driven him to the wars as a private in 1813. On returning to Greece, Ali Tebelen had welcomed him as the grandson of Czerni-Georges; Georges claimed Ali had given him a harem—five wives and ten slaves—but the women were stupid and the harem little better than a herd of goats; he preferred French grisettes.

They were entering Saint-Denis and stopped at a tavern famed for its cheese-cakes. The count, suspecting the youth had left something in the coach, returned and found beneath the cushion a portfolio labeled “Maitre Crottat, notary.” He took out the deed of sale for the farm at Moulineaux, pocketed it, and went into the inn, concluding that Georges was Crottat’s second clerk sent as head-clerk. Georges, meanwhile, had paid for cheese-cakes and wine for Pere Légèr and Oscar, basking in their admiration and trying to pick up Mistigris and his master.

When Pierrotin returned to the coach, Georges resumed his tales. He said he had been made commander of Ali’s cavalry and had distinguished himself at an action against Chosrew pacha, cutting his line and receiving Ali’s gratitude and costly gifts: yataghans, carbines, scimitars. Back at Janina Ali had proposed degrading acts—drowning a wife, renouncing Christianity—but Georges refused and fled while he could. He said Chosrew later captured Ali by sea with English help; Chosrew had been turned naval commander, and it was Chosrew who, when he recognized Georges at Smyrna, nearly had him executed. Monsieur de Rivière, the French ambassador, had intervened, saving him and getting back two thousand talari, though most of his treasures were taken by the admiral.

Georges claimed he had witnessed the execution of Smyrna’s governor and then gone to Spain, becoming aide-de-camp to Mina with the rank of colonel for the constitutional cause. The count reacted sharply, warning that the Court of Peers was then inquiring into conspiracies and dealing severely with French soldiers who bore arms against France or engaged in foreign intrigues. He added that if he were a magistrate he would have ordered the gendarmes at Pierrefitte to arrest the supposed aide-de-camp and summon the coach’s occupants to testify. The remark silenced Georges when they passed the gendarmerie guard-house.

Conversation shifted to cultivation in Eastern lands. Georges, with his usual flippancy, described them as too busy cutting each other’s heads off to farm in the French sense: onions, rice, tobacco, dates, rugs at Smyrna, Latakia tobacco, opium from poppies, and customs that left the fellahs with barely enough after taxes. Pere Légèr, puzzled, asked about sheep and fields; Georges evaded, saying most crops grew without much cultivation. He mocked bureaucracy and contrasted despotic plunder with French stamped papers. The count advised landowners to sell, warning that taxes in Italy were heavy.

Mistigris and Schinner—the supposed painter—joined in. Schinner had claimed to be a humble painter returned from Rome after winning the grand prix and sent to Italy at the government’s cost; he insisted on not being compromised and said he was going merely as a decorator to a château. The count, obliging the mock-importance of artists, urged that great ceilings and the love that inspired them were worth sacrifice. Oscar listened, eager to be somebody.

Pierrotin, the driver, joked about driving sovereigns and the pourboires he expected and fretted about a debt for a new coach. The passengers exchanged banter about ceilings, Venice, Rome, and a nearly murderous scrape Schinner claimed to have had there. Mistigris hinted that Lord Byron had been involved; Schinner demurred, not wishing to speak of Byron.

Schinner then told a long account of Dalmatia, Zara, and a Greek beauty named Zena. He had stayed with an apothecary, seen Zena on a neighboring balcony, fallen for her, and stolen walks with her despite an ugly duenna and a jealous seventy-year-old husband—a Uscoque pirate with millions, a missing ear and eye. Schinner recounted the dangerous delicacy of their flirtation: the ramparts, the duenna who wouldn’t leave, the tension. He had entered the pirate’s house and walked the ramparts with Zena. Late one day he returned to find the street full of people; he was seized, garroted, gagged, and dragged before magistrates accused of murdering the pirate. He was stoned and nearly killed by a maddened populace crying for his death.

He learned that Zena had poisoned the pirate with opium to free herself for walks with him; the duenna had overdone the dose, and Schinner was implicated. He insisted he knew nothing of the murder. The mayor and Austrian commissary of police released him with orders to return to Rome, while Zena, who had let the heirs take most of the Uscoque’s fortune, was sentenced to two years’ seclusion in a convent; Schinner hoped to paint her portrait someday. Mistigris complained that Schinner had left him without money in Venice, forcing him to paint cheap portraits to get to Rome.

Georges chimed in with comments on Dalmatian maraschino and the follies of youth. Pere Légèr repeatedly asked about farming and crops, receiving brief, often evasive replies. The count maintained a guarded attitude, sometimes smiling at the fashionable braggadocio and sometimes showing surprising knowledge—for instance, when Georges mentioned names connected to Smyrna that the count recognized, which made the youth seem less sure of his invented recollections.

Throughout the journey the characters parried between bluster and real information: Georges’s ostentatious Eastern adventures mingled fact and fabrication; the count, discreet but observant, suspected a clerk pretending to be a colonel; Schinner’s romance illustrated the dangers beneath exotic talk; Mistigris supplied cynical comments; Pierrotin displayed an eye for profit; Pere Légèr asked homely questions; Oscar gaped and yearned to be more. Conversation, prompted by the coach’s enforced intimacy and the French habit of talk, ran on—at times diverting, at times revealing—until they moved on toward Pierrefitte and beyond.

################################################
Pierrotin’s coach descended into the large village of Saint-Brice about half-past one, where the travelers usually stopped to rest the horses. The inn-keeper recognized Pere Leger and invited the party to breakfast.

“Always once a day,” said the fat farmer. Georges, twirling his cane, excited poor Oscar by taking a small straw case from his pocket, selecting a cigar, and lighting it on the threshold. He offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner; Oscar tried to imitate the others but his nausea and awkwardness made him suffer. Mistigris, taking Oscar’s cigar, smoked it off and mocked him.

Oscar brooded over Georges’s spending—expensive Alicante, cheesecakes, cigars, and a likely lavish breakfast—and sat embarrassed on a stone post, revealing the seam of his stocking to the group. Mistigris showed a similar tear in his trousers and joked: “We are brothers in socks.” The count, watching from under the porte-cochere, enjoyed their banter despite his statesmanlike demeanor.

In the kitchen, the inn-keeper and Pere Leger discussed Les Moulineaux and the Comte de Serizy. The inn-keeper wondered whether Pere Leger could fleece a peer and a minister like the Comte de Serizy. Pere Leger, low-voiced, admitted he had “done for him.” He explained a scheme: with forty thousand pictures-on-coin given to Moreau he could buy Les Moulineaux for 260,000 francs down; when the Comte found the sale made, he might be forced to buy it back for 360,000 rather than let the land be parceled out. Leger argued it was a solid investment—Les Moulineaux already produced 6,000 francs in rent, and he could take another lease at 7,500 for eighteen years. He would pose as a tenant proposed by the count to avoid involving Moreau, making the count appear to act for his master’s interest. If the count gave Moreau a ten-thousand-franc payment, Moreau would profit handsomely—fifty thousand in all. The inn-keeper approved, noting the count’s wealth and his restoration of the chateau for two hundred thousand francs.

The count, overhearing, suspected he’d found the very proofs he sought. He doubted, though, that his steward would collude. Concerned about indiscretions, he called Pierrotin aside. He had promised the coachman ten louis to keep his name secret; now he offered an extra inducement: if Pierrotin continued to conceal his identity (or make no sign revealing it) until that night, the count would give him, the next morning, the thousand francs Pierrotin needed for a new coach. As a precaution the count urged Pierrotin not to breakfast but to stay with the horses. Pierrotin promised and, to keep up appearances, told Pere Leger he must hurry—he had to deliver fish by three.

Pere Leger decided to harness a horse the inn-keeper wanted to sell, so they could breakfast and then overtake Pierrotin. Schinner and Mistigris walked on. Pierrotin unexpectedly rebridled his horses; shortly after he overtook the artists and mounted a hill that revealed Ecouen, Mesnil, and surrounding woods. Pere Leger and Czerni-Georges’s grandson joined in the coucou.

As they drove toward Moisselles, Georges praised the landscape and resumed cigars. Oscar, insulted at being called a “little young man,” tried to reclaim dignity. He boasted that his family destined him for a diplomatic career. The others laughed, but Georges regarded the claim seriously and teased him about his mother and “household.” Oscar defended his pedigree—“Husson de la Cerisaie”—and proclaimed he would be famous in ten years.

Conversation turned to Presles. Oscar announced he was going there to see the Comte de Serizy; he knew the count often, was a comrade of the count’s son, and visited the chateau. The others reacted with surprise. The count, listening, seized the chance: he wanted information about de Serizy for a claim against the American government and asked Oscar for help. Oscar, eager to show his knowledge, advised the count to go through the countess rather than the count—he claimed the count was hideous with a skin disease, addicted to sulphur and steam baths, and “shriveled and old,” and that his wife could not endure him. He suggested the Marquis d’Aiglemont could influence both husband and wife.

The others scolded Oscar for speaking ill of a woman in public. The count, suddenly stern, commanded silence about the Comtesse de Serizy, avowing friendship with her brother the Marquis de Ronquerolles and warning that anyone who disparaged the countess would “answer to me.” He boasted of connections—“I know the Keeper of the Seals,” he told Georges, and even the architect Grindot of Presles. He asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn; he wanted to leave the coach for a moment.

Pierrotin pulled up at Moisselles. Before he left, Pere Leger asked who the “young fool” had been; Pierrotin said he’d told the boy to get off near Maffliers and suspected him to be some local master, though Pierrotin didn’t know him. The three young men fell silent, realizing they had been boasting foolishly. The count returned and the journey continued in an awkward hush until they reached the Carreau woods.

As the coach neared the fine view of the chateau de Franconville, Oscar challenged the others to remember his name and future glory. Georges teased him, and Mistigris mocked his pedigree. They descended La Cave toward Presles. The count gave each a sardonic farewell—good luck to the colonel in reconciling with the King, congratulations to Schinner for his fame, a warning about domestic life, and mock praise for Oscar’s alleged political secrets. He advised Pere Leger to “pluck” the Comte de Serizy with a firm hand, then asked Pierrotin to set him down and pick him up the next day. He left the coach and took a path through the woods, leaving the others bewildered.

At the iron gate of Presles, Pierrotin drove in. Georges, Pere Leger, and the painter were stunned to discover they were at the chateau. Pere Leger, who owned Les Moulineaux, realized Georges had been boasting and slipped away so deftly the farmer couldn’t find him. Oscar, proud and unprepared, announced he would leave his packages with the steward and go straight to the chateau, plunging hurriedly into the narrow path. Pere Leger, watching him go, told him the right gate to use.

Pierrotin, proud and economical, delivered the artists’ packages and Oscar’s luggage to the concierge, including the pretty leather portmanteau he had carefully covered in hay. He then drove out through the forest road to Isle-Adam, his face radiant—he expected the thousand francs the count had promised and the profits of his errands. Pierrotin’s contentment came from the certainty of tomorrow’s payoff: the thousand francs that would buy him a new coach.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Oscar, abashed, lurked behind a clump of trees in the courtyard, watching what would become of his two traveling companions, when Monsieur Moreau appeared on the portico from the guard-room. He was neatly dressed in riding gear and carried a whip. Greeting Oscar and the painters, he summoned the concierge and had the two artists shown to rooms 14 and 15, instructing that fires be made and luggage taken up. He told them Madame Moreau would give them the keys and, addressing the painters, invited them to dine at five and enjoy the abundant hunting on the estate—Moreau boasting a license and twelve thousand acres of forest. Mistigris, the wittier of the pair, murmured, “‘Veni, vidi, cecidi’—I came, I saw, I slaughtered.”

Moreau hurried Oscar through the park. He sent a child to tell his wife that “little Husson” had arrived and that he must go to Les Moulineaux for a moment. The steward—about fifty, decisive, and a figure whose authority Oscar had been taught to magnify by his mother—made the boy feel small and uneasy; Oscar feared Moreau might harm him.

Moreau’s pavilion, attached to the estate, was a neat Louis XV freestone house with a salon, bedroom, dining-room, billiard-room, and a boudoir arranged in a tent style—comfortably furnished with chateau pieces and country luxury outside. Around the house Madame Moreau had laid out gardens, masked the outbuildings, and improved the entrance with an iron gateway.

The Moreaus had cleverly concealed their dependence on the count, living like independent managers. Perquisites from Monsieur de Serizy—milk, eggs, poultry, game, forage, vegetables, wood—kept horses and servants; they bought only butcher’s meat, wines, and colonial goods. Moreau skimmed small profits and supplies—stacking more hay than he accounted for, keeping farm pigs, using stable manure for the chateau gardens—accruing modest appropriations under plausible pretexts. Madame Moreau employed local girls and a discharged soldier; in neighboring towns people either did not know or pretended not to know her former status as a waiting-maid. Moreau’s influence had secured favors—appointments and decorations—that made the couple welcome in local bourgeois society; the rector and mayor played cards with them nightly.

Estelle, Madame Moreau, about thirty-six and fashionable, spent much of her meager toilette allowance to appear like a princess. She resented being “the steward’s wife at Presles” and wanted to be thought the chateau’s mistress. She pushed Moreau to partner with Léger at Les Moulineaux so they might retire to Isle-Adam rather than return to servitude. She nursed a rancorous grudge against the Reyberts, who had cut her family dead after she presumed precedence and had spread her past through the countryside; the Reyberts brooded on revenge.

Learning from Grindot the architect that two painters were coming to finish the chateau decorations (the principal work had been done by Schinner), Madame Moreau resolved to outshine Schinner by receiving and praising the artist she expected. For two days she prepared, reserving a fine Paris dress for a Saturday dinner she expected the artist to attend. Wearing a pink-striped gown and a superb Leghorn bonnet, she stationed herself near a flower-bed in the chateau courtyard, holding a rose-colored parasol to be seen as a mistress when the Beaumont coach arrived. When Pierrotin’s coach left only Mistigris’s packages at the concierge, she withdrew, disappointed, feeling too fine to sit idle in her salon.

The two artists—Joseph Bridau and his pupil Leon de Lora (Mistigris)—dressed and were shown to the steward’s lodge by Jacques Moreau. Madame Moreau received them with affected graciousness, boasting she and her husband loved art and had recently had Monsieur Schinner with them. Bridau and Mistigris, quick to amuse themselves at her clumsiness, parried her flattery with light satire. Bridau suggested a portrait of her; Leon, sliding to her sofa, indulged in compliments and asked for secret sittings. Bridau proposed painting the children. Madame Moreau coquetted and offered drives through the woods in her carriage, while the artists praised her as a sovereign of beauty.

Rosalie announced that the count had come to the chateau and wanted his trunk and the key to his apartment. The news unsettled Madame Moreau—Moreau was absent—and soon it became clear the count had also asked for Schinner and Mistigris to dine with him. The artists realized the man they had taken for an ordinary traveler in a coucou might be the Comte de Serizy. Oscar, who had been foolish enough to joke and speak indiscreetly about the count in the coach, went pale at the thought.

The count had indeed arrived; leaving his coach at the top of the hill, he had walked a path to the gamekeeper’s house, surprised the keeper, and sent the man with a folded note to Beaumont. He crossed the park, entering the chateau and confronting Moreau about betrayals Huntered to him by Pere Leger’s revelations and by gossip, he suspected Moreau of having revealed intimate secrets. When Moreau arrived at the lodge and learned of the count’s presence, he was dismayed.

Moreau’s life was a tangle of private appropriations and small deceits, but it was not the simple venality that outraged the count. The count’s grievance was a personal one: he had learned that Moreau had discussed his private ailments, his feelings for Madame de Serizy, and an intended sale at Les Moulineaux in the company of Madame Clapart and others; Oscar, moreover, had openly mocked the comtesse and revealed the count’s maladies in public during the coach journey. The count confronted Moreau in his dressing-room, dressed in evening white and covered with honors. He listened in cold fury to Moreau’s excuses, then, wounded by the indignity, declared that though he would leave Moreau his ill-gotten gains and forget him, the trust was gone. He ordered Moreau to set his affairs in order, to explain stewardship duties to Monsieur de Reybert, who would succeed him, and to keep on polite terms with the Reyberts. Above all, the count forbade Moreau from permitting Oscar to sleep at Presles; the boy must go to the inn, for the count might not answer for his temper should he see him again.

While this interview took place, the Beaumont coach deposited Maître Crottat, the notary, and his clerk Georges Marest, whom everyone recognized as the youth who had been talking and joking in the coach and thereby contributed to the chain of revelations. Inside the salon were the two painters, Monsieur de Reybert, old Margueron, and the notary with a bundle of deeds. Georges, a clerk and second to Crottat, had been mocking travelers and pretending to be a colonel; he had also carried the deed of sale for the farm at Moulineaux. The count, incisive and composed, handled the situation with both authority and irony. He teased Mistigris, welcomed Bridau warmly on account of his father, and reproved Georges for the negligence of leaving important papers unguarded and for vapid bravado. Georges was embarrassed and ashamed, recognizing he had made a foolish display.

The notaries and Margueron went to conclude the sale of Les Moulineaux before dinner, a business matter the count had pursued carefully and which had been endangered by loose tongues. The count retained possession of Georges’s deed—having already prevented Pere Leger from discovering the purchase plans—and adroitly secured the transaction. He invited the men to finish the business with a briskness to avoid further indiscretions before the meal.

When Moreau returned to his pavilion, the household buzzed. He entered white-faced; his wife, alarmed, demanded the cause. Moreau told her he was done—no longer steward, no longer in the count’s confidence. To Moreau the worst offense was not the small peculations; it was the betrayal of intimate trust. He accused Pere Leger of having informed the count about Les Moulineaux, but said the decisive injury had come from Oscar, who had insulted the comtesse and revealed the count’s maladies. Estelle gloated that the lad’s indiscretion had exposed them.

Moreau seized Oscar, who had been cowering, dragged him to a window, demanded to know what he had said in the coach, and in fury ordered him to go and beg the count’s pardon. Oscar, bewildered and terrified, could not obey. Moreau hauled him through the yards amid cries and sobs, and flung him into the salon at the count’s feet as the count was about to go to the dining-room with his guests.

“On your knees, wretched boy! and ask pardon of him who gave food to your mind by obtaining your scholarship,” Moreau roared. Oscar, face to the ground and sobbing, foamed and said nothing. The scene shocked the assembled company; Moreau was beside himself with anger and humiliation. The count, eyes on the boy, expressed the contempt of a man who saw vanity where there should have been humility: he declared the lad “a mere lump of vanity,” warned that he feared Oscar would never be much, and passed on to his dinner. The count’s dismissal was a cold end to Moreau’s stewardship and a brutal rebuke to the boy; Moreau took Oscar back and ordered the horses immediately put to Madame Moreau’s calèche, the household reeling from the abrupt rupture.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While the horses were being harnessed, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart:

My dear,—Oscar has ruined me. During his journey in Pierrotin’s coach he spoke of Madame de Serizy’s behavior to his Excellency, who was travelling incognito, and revealed the secret of his terrible malady. After dismissing me from my stewardship, the count told me not to let Oscar sleep at Presles, but to send him away immediately. Therefore, to obey his orders, the horses are being harnessed at this moment to my wife’s carriage, and Brochon, my stable-man, will take the miserable child to you to-night.

We are, my wife and I, in a distress of mind which you may perhaps imagine, though I cannot describe it to you. I will see you in a few days, for I must take another course. I have three children, and I ought to consider their future. At present I do not know what to do; but I shall certainly endeavor to make the count aware of what seventeen years of the life of a man like myself is worth. Owning at the present moment about two hundred and fifty thousand francs, I want to raise myself to a fortune which may some day make me the equal of his Excellency. At this moment I feel within me the power to move mountains and vanquish insurmountable difficulties. What a lever is such a scene of bitter humiliation as I have just passed through! Whose blood has Oscar in his veins? His conduct has been that of a blockhead; up to this moment when I write to you, he has not said a word nor answered, even by a sign, the questions my wife and I have put to him. Will he become an idiot? or is he one already? Dear friend, why did you not instruct him as to his behavior before you sent him to me? How many misfortunes you would have spared me, had you brought him here yourself as I begged you to do. If Estelle alarmed you, you might have stayed at Moisselles. However, the thing is done, and there is no use talking about it.

Adieu; I shall see you soon.

Your devoted servant and friend,

Moreau

That evening Madame Clapart, just returned from a walk with her husband, sat knitting winter socks for Oscar by candlelight. Monsieur Clapart, who avoided cafés and prided himself on hosting a friend for dominoes, feared Poiret had arrived while they were out.

“I’m afraid Poiret came while we were out,” he said.

“No, the portress would have told us,” she replied.

“She may have forgotten it,” he persisted.

They argued in the usual domestic way—he finding fault with Oscar’s character, she defending him—until the cracking of a postilion’s whip and the stoppage of a carriage outside made Clapart look out.

“They have sent Oscar back to you in a post-chaise,” he cried, partly relieved, partly uneasy.

“Good heavens! what can have happened to him?” Madame Clapart trembled.

Brochon entered, followed by Oscar and Poiret.

“I don’t know, but Monsieur Moreau is no longer steward of Presles, and they say your son has caused it. His Excellency ordered that he should be sent home to you. Here’s a letter from poor Monsieur Moreau, madame, which will tell you all. You never saw a man so changed in a single day.”

Madame Clapart flung herself into a chair to read the fatal letter. “Oscar,” she said, staggering toward his bed, “do you want to kill your mother? After all the cautions I gave you this morning—”

She fainted from distress. When she recovered, Clapart was shaking Oscar by the arm.

“Will you answer me?” he demanded.

“Go to bed, monsieur,” she said to her son. “Let him alone, Monsieur Clapart. Don’t drive him out of his senses; he is frightfully changed.”

Oscar had slipped away to bed the instant he got the order. After a day of such emotion, despite his faults, he slept the sleep of the just. The next day the violent mental suffering of the night had faded into the more practical worry of imminent want. Youth quickly shifts from one impression to another; corporal punishment might have fixed the lesson, but discretion in administering it is crucial.

Madame Clapart sent her husband out so she might be alone with Oscar. She was pitiable: eyes worn with tears, a face weary from sleeplessness, a feeble voice—everything showed suffering beyond what she could bear. She motioned him to sit beside her and spoke in a gentle, grieved voice of the benefits they had received from Monsieur Moreau. For six years they had lived on his delicate charity; Monsieur Clapart’s salary and Oscar’s "demi-bourse" were due to the Comte de Serizy. Most of this would now cease. Clapart had no claim to a pension; when he could no longer keep his place, what would become of them?

“For myself,” she said, “I could nurse the sick or be a housekeeper and support Monsieur Clapart; but you, Oscar, what could you do? You must earn your living. There are four careers for a young man like you—commerce, government employment, the professions, or the military. Commerce needs capital, and we have none. Public employment requires influence and a long probation—you have alienated our only protector. The professions demand years and fees, and again money for your maintenance while you learn. You are eighteen and strong; enlist in the army; it seems the only way you can earn your bread.”

Oscar, ignorant of life and of earning a living, listened submissively. The word “army,” his mother’s tears, and her admonitions finally made him cry. Seeing his tears, Madame Clapart softened, and gave the familiar peroration mothers use in such scenes.

“Well, Oscar, promise me you will be more discreet—repress your silly vanity,” she urged.

Oscar promised, and she kissed him to console him.

“In future,” she added, “you will follow my advice. We will go see your uncle Cardot; he owed much to your father, who gave him his sister Mademoiselle Husson with an enormous dowry, enabling him to make a fortune. Perhaps he might place you with Monsieur Camusot, his successor and son-in-law, in the rue des Bourdonnais. But your uncle has many claims on him—four children, the Cocon d’Or given to his eldest daughter, and other connections. He scarcely knows of our existence. Still, to-morrow we will go and see him; he is our last hope.”

Jean-Jérôme-Séverin Cardot had been a widower six years. Head-clerk of the old establishment Cocon d’Or, he had bought the business in 1793 with the dowry of Mademoiselle Husson and made a large fortune. To provide for his children he bought an annuity for himself and divided his capital among them; thus he now lived on a thirty-thousand-franc annuity and enjoyed the comfort of seeing them well provided.

Cardot lived simply in Belleville, in a first-floor apartment with a garden, for a thousand francs a year. Served by an old cook and a former maid of his late wife, he kept his apartment as she had furnished it. He dined in Paris five days a week and returned home at midnight in a hackney-coach. A little old man, neatly dressed and polite to women, he professed epicurean tastes, admired Voltaire and Béranger, and affected decorum. To his children he seemed grave and attentive, while he privately indulged pleasures and gaiety. He had not spoken of his income to his children, who supposed he had deprived himself for them; only his son-in-law Camusot knew the annuity, considering Cardot’s conduct a generous mix of family duty and later enjoyment.

Madame Clapart did not trouble the Cardots with complaints, seeing them only rarely. She had always clung to Uncle Cardot as a hope and took Oscar to see him during vacations. Cardot sometimes gave Oscar a dinner and a gaiety ticket and once had provided a school-suit and a silver cup and fork. Madame Clapart reminded him of these favors and of his nephew’s gratitude, though Cardot, shrewd and aware that Oscar was a posthumous child with no legal claim, had not been deeply moved.

“Your son bears the name of Husson,” Cardot said when Madame Clapart and Oscar visited. “If my dear deceased wife were living she would wish to do something for the name of her father and of her brother—”

“She loved her brother,” said Oscar’s mother.

“All my fortune is given to my children,” he continued, “and I have now only an annuity; but I know the path on which you ought to start this young fellow. Let him study law; I’ll pay the costs. Put him in a lawyer’s office and let him learn the business; if he does well, and if I am still alive, each of my children shall lend him a quarter of the cost of a practice; I will be security for him. You will only have to feed and clothe him. Discretion, honesty, and work—live at home, go straight to the law-school, from there to a lawyer’s office; drudge night and day, and study at home. Become, by twenty-two, a second clerk; by twenty-four, head-clerk; be steady, and you will win all. If you don’t like the profession you might enter my son the notary’s office. Work, patience, discretion, honesty—those are your landmarks.”

“God grant that you may live thirty years longer to see your fifth child realizing all we expect from him,” cried Madame Clapart, seizing Cardot’s hand.

“Now come to breakfast,” replied the kind old man, leading Oscar by the ear.

During the meal Cardot noted Oscar’s inexperience. “Send him here now and then,” he said to Madame Clapart as they parted; “I’ll form him for you.”

This visit calmed the poor mother’s anxieties. For the next fortnight she took Oscar walking every day and watched him closely. By the end of October, while the household breakfasted on a humble meal, the formidable ex-steward entered and surprised this scene of poverty.

“We are now living in Paris—but not as we lived at Presles,” said Moreau, explaining the change in their relations caused by Oscar’s folly. “I shall seldom be here myself; for I have gone into partnership with Père Légier and Père Margueron of Beaumont. We are speculating in land, and we have begun by purchasing the estate of Persan. I am the head of the concern, which has a capital of a million; part of which I have borrowed on my own securities. When I find a good thing, Père Légier and I examine it; my partners have each a quarter and I a half in the profits; but I do nearly all the work, and for that reason I shall be constantly on the road. My wife lives here, in the faubourg du Roule, very plainly. When we see how the business turns out, if we risk only the profits, and if Oscar behaves himself, we may, perhaps, employ him.”

“Ah! my friend, the catastrophe caused by my poor boy’s heedlessness may prove to be the cause of your making a brilliant fortune; for, really, you were burying your energy and your capacity at Presles.”

Madame Clapart related her visit to Cardot to show Moreau that she and her son need no longer be a burden.

“He is right, that old fellow,” said the ex-steward. “We must hold Oscar on that path with an iron hand, and he will end as a barrister or a notary. But he mustn’t leave the track; he must go straight through with it. Ha! I know how to help you. The legal business of land-agents is quite important, and I have heard of a lawyer who has just bought what is called a ‘titre nu’; that means a practice without clients. He is a young man, hard as an iron bar, eager for work, ferociously active. His name is Desroches. I’ll offer him our business on condition that he takes Oscar as a pupil; and I’ll ask him to let the boy live with him at nine hundred francs a year, of which I will pay three, so that your son will cost you only six hundred francs, without his living, in future. If the boy ever means to become a man it can only be under a discipline like that. He’ll come out of that office, notary, solicitor, or barrister, as he may elect.”

“Come, Oscar; thank our kind Monsieur Moreau, and don’t stand there like a stone post. All young men who commit follies have not the good fortune to meet with friends who still take an interest in their career, even after they have been injured by them.”

“The best way to make your peace with me,” said Moreau, pressing Oscar’s hand, “is to work now with steady application, and to conduct yourself in future properly.”

################################################
Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a twenty-six-year-old solicitor newly installed in the rue de Béthisy in a large apartment at the end of a narrow courtyard, for which he paid little. Born to poor parents and brought up sternly, Desroches knew of Oscar’s condition and felt interest in him, though his apparent harshness limited the help he could show. Oscar—gaunt, with muddy skin and hair cropped like a clothes-brush, speaking curtly with a piercing eye and a gloomy vivacity—was terrified by the sight of his new master.

“We work here day and night,” said the lawyer from his armchair behind a table piled with papers. “Monsieur Moreau, we won’t kill him; but he’ll have to go at our pace.” Though it was Sunday, the head-clerk appeared, pen in hand.

“Monsieur Godeschal, here’s the pupil I told you of. Monsieur Moreau takes the liveliest interest in him. He will dine with us and sleep in the small attic next to your chamber. You will allot the exact time it takes to go to the law-school and back, so he does not lose five minutes on the way. See that he learns the Code and is proficient; after his work here you will give him authors to read. He will be under your immediate direction, and I shall keep an eye on him. They want to make him what you have made yourself, a capable head-clerk, so he can take such a place himself. Go with Monsieur Godeschal, my young friend; he’ll show you your lodging, and you can settle down.”

Desroches praised his clerks—young men with only their ten fingers to rely upon—working as hard as a dozen ordinary fellows. He had taken Godeschal from Derville when the latter was only a second clerk, paying him a thousand francs a year with board, and admired his indefatigable honesty. “For the slightest fault of that kind a clerk leaves my office,” he declared. Moreau thought Oscar was in a good school.

For two years Oscar lived in the rue de Béthisy, a den of petty law. Under this strict and capable supervision he kept rigid hours; his Paris life resembled that of a monk. At five in the morning, in all weathers, Godeschal woke and took Oscar to the office, where their master was already at work. Oscar ran errands and prepared law-school lessons; Godeschal and often Desroches pointed out authors and difficulties. He was not allowed to leave a single section of the Code until he had mastered it; Godeschal gave him preliminary examinations more searching and longer than those of the law-school. After brief classes he returned to the office; occasionally sent to the Palais, but always under Godeschal’s thumb, until dinner.

Dinner was modest—one dish of meat, one of vegetables, and a salad; dessert, a piece of Gruyère cheese. After dinner Godeschal and Oscar returned to work until night. Once a month Oscar breakfasted with his uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother. From time to time Moreau, when in Paris, took Oscar to dine in the Palais-Royal or to the theater in the evening. Oscar had been so snubbed by Godeschal and Desroches for attempts at elegance that he no longer gave thought to his clothes.

“A good clerk,” Godeschal told him, “should have two black coats, one new, one old, a pair of black trousers, black stockings, and shoes. Boots cost too much; you can’t have boots till you are called to the bar. A clerk should never spend more than seven hundred francs a year. Good stout shirts of strong linen are what you want. Ha! when a man starts from nothing to reach fortune, he has to keep down to bare necessities. Look at Monsieur Desroches; he did what we are doing, and see where he is now.”

Godeschal preached by example. He practised strict principles of honor, discretion, and honesty without ostentation; such conduct was natural to him. Eighteen months after Oscar’s installation the second clerk was, for the second time, slightly wrong in his accounts. Godeschal said to him before the others:

“My dear Gaudet, go away of your own free will, that it may not be said that Monsieur Desroches has dismissed you. You have been careless or absent-minded, and neither of those defects can pass here. The master shall know nothing about the matter; that is all that I can do for a comrade.”

At twenty Oscar became third clerk in the office. Though he earned no salary, he was lodged and fed while doing the work of the second clerk. Desroches employed two chief clerks, and the second’s work was unremitting toil. By the end of his second year in the law-school Oscar knew more than most licensed graduates; he did work at the Palais intelligently and argued some cases in chambers. Godeschal and Desroches were satisfied. Yet Oscar still showed, from time to time, a hankering after pleasure and a suppressed desire to shine.

Moreau, satisfied with Oscar’s progress, relaxed his watchfulness; in July 1825, when Oscar passed his examinations with a spotless record, the land-agent gave him money to dress elegantly and Madame Clapart prepared his outfit splendidly.

In November, when the courts reopened, Oscar occupied the chamber of the second clerk and did that work wholly, with a salary of eight hundred francs plus board and lodging. Uncle Cardot, who had privately inquired at Desroches about his nephew, promised Madame Clapart to look out for a practice for Oscar if he continued to do well.

Despite these virtuous appearances, Oscar endured great inward strife. At times he thought of quitting a life so contrary to his tastes; he fancied galley-slaves happier. Galled by the collar of this iron system, wild desires seized him when he compared himself in the street with well-dressed young men. Sometimes he was driven toward women; then he resigned himself, only to fall into deeper disgust for life. Impelled by Godeschal’s example, he was forced rather than led into the rugged way.

Godeschal watched Oscar and, by principle, kept him from temptation. Generally the young clerk had so little money he could not give way to excess. During the last year Godeschal had made five or six pleasure parties with Oscar, defraying expenses, feeling the rope by which he tethered the young kid must be slackened. These “pranks,” as he called them, helped Oscar endure existence, for there was little amusement in breakfasting with Uncle Cardot or seeing his mother, who lived even more penuriously than Desroches. Moreau could not be as familiar with Oscar as Godeschal could; perhaps Godeschal, sincere friend to young Husson, had been behind Moreau in safe efforts to initiate the poor youth into life’s mysteries. Oscar, grown prudent, had come to see the extent and character of the fault committed on that luckless journey; but his repressed fancies and youthful follies might still get the better of him. Nevertheless, the more knowledge he got of the world and its laws, the better his mind would form itself, and, provided Godeschal never lost sight of him, Moreau flattered himself that between them they could bring the son of Madame Clapart through in safety.

“How is he getting on?” asked the land-agent of Godeschal on his return from a journey.

“Always too much vanity,” replied Godeschal. “You give him fine clothes and fine linen, he wears the shirt-fronts of a stockbroker, and so my dainty coxcomb spends his Sundays in the Tuileries, looking out for adventures. What else can you expect? That’s youth. He torments me to present him to my sister, where he would see actresses, ballet-dancers, elegant young fops, spendthrifts wasting fortunes! His mind, I’m afraid, is not fitted for law. He can talk well, though; and if we could make him a barrister he might plead cases that were carefully prepared for him.”

In November 1825, soon after Oscar had taken his new clerkship and was about to pass his licentiate examination, a new clerk arrived to fill the place vacated by Oscar’s promotion.

This fourth clerk, Frédéric Marest, intended to enter the magistracy and was in his third year at the law school. A fine young man of twenty-three, he inherited about twelve thousand francs a year from a bachelor uncle, and was the son of Madame Marest, widow of a wealthy wood-merchant. Desiring to understand procedure and train to be a head-clerk in two years, he had placed himself in Desroches’ office to study; by thirty he hoped to be “procureur du roi.” Though Frédéric was cousin-german to Georges Marest, Oscar did not connect him with the grandson of Czerni-Georges.

“Messieurs,” said Godeschal at breakfast, “I announce the arrival of a new jurisconsult; and as he is rich, rishissime, we will, I hope, make him pay a glorious entrance-fee.”

“Forward, the book!” cried Oscar, nodding to the youngest clerk, “and pray let us be serious.”

The youngest clerk climbed to the top shelf and produced a register covered with dust. We must explain this perennial joke of legal offices. In a clerical life where work is the rule, amusement is treasured; a hoax is a delightful rarity. The gravest clerk, at times, craves fun and quizzing, and a set of clerks will seize any hoax with keen appetite. This instinct explains Georges Marest’s behaviour in the coucou; the denizens of a studio and of a lawyer’s office are in this line superior to comedians.

When Desroches bought a practice without clients he began a new dynasty, breaking the usual customs of receiving new-comers. Having taken an office where legal documents had never been scribbled, he bought new tables and white boxes edged with blue. His staff, clerks from other offices without mutual ties, were surprised to find themselves together. Godeschal, who had apprenticed under maître Derville, would not allow the precious tradition of the “welcome” to be lost. This “welcome” is a breakfast every neophyte gives to the ancients of the office.

About the time Oscar arrived, during the first six months of Desroches’ installation, on a winter evening when work had been finished early and the clerks warmed themselves before the fire, Godeschal conceived and composed a Register “architriclino-basochien,” of great antiquity, contrived to appear genuine and saved from the fires of the Revolution. The work, approved by the clerks, began with a search through dealers in old paper for a proper register—paper with an eighteenth-century mark, bound in parchment, stamped like an order in council. The book was left about to gather mold and wear; the gilt edges were tarnished with surprising perfection. Then entries were forged. The first sixty pages were filled with fictional reports of receptions in an eighteenth-century legal style, as though the clerical guild of Basoche had been continuous since 1525.

The Register opened with a mock solemnity: an eighteenth-century-styled invocation to Sainte-Geneviève, an assertion that since 1525 the clerks of this Practice had existed under her protection, and a declaration by clerks of a predecessor’s office that they renewed and continued the register and the archives. Names and dates—Malin, Grévin, Athanase Féret, Jacques Héret, Regnault de Saint-Jean-d’Angély, Bedeau—were appended, and an account of a long breakfast at Courtille that lasted until seven the next morning.

Expertly engrossed, it contained twenty-seven reports of receptions of neophytes, the last in 1792. After a blank of fourteen years the register began again in 1806 with Bordin’s appointment as attorney before the first Court of the Seine. A deed proclaimed the reconstitution of the kingdom of Basoche, crediting Sainte-Geneviève and maître Bordin for the miraculous preservation of the archives, and recording clerks’ gratitude with a breakfast and theatre visit. The entries mixed legal formality with rollicking conviviality—detailed praise for dinners, wines, and the master’s generosity—creating the illusion of continuous clerical tradition.

Under date June 1822, when Desroches took the oath, Godeschal’s declaration appeared, boasting of recovering the famous archives architriclino-basochien, seeking out a document dated 1786 connected with other proofs going back to 1525 and celebrating the “worship” of the Goddess Bottle and good living. Godeschal recorded a breakfast at the “Cheval Rouge,” recounting twelve bottles of twelve different wines, melons, and dishes, and thanked his sister Mariette, a celebrated dancer, for providing orchestra seats. The page bore stains of wine, pâtés, and candle-grease.

Oscar’s own pretended reception, recorded with mock solemnity, lampooned his humble home repast with exaggerated praise: radishes, gherkins, anchovies, butter and olives for hors-d’œuvre; a “succulent soup of rice” credited to maternal solicitude and fine stews; a tongue of beef with tomatoes; a compote of pigeons; a timbale of macaroni with chocolate custards; and a dessert of eleven dishes, all washed down with sixteen bottles of choicest wines, Roussillon and Rhône outdoing Champagne and Burgundy. The statutes of their Order were parodied too: candidates must limit the magnificence of their welcome to the length of their purse; Madame Clapart’s liberality merited hurrahs. The entry purported to be signed by all the clerks.

Three clerks had already been deceived by the Book, and three real “receptions of welcome” were falsely recorded in it. After each arrival the little sub-clerk placed the Archives Architriclino-Basochiennes on the new-comer’s desk; the clerks enjoyed the look on his face as he studied its facetious pages. Inter pocula each candidate learned the secret of the farce, and the revelation inspired him to hoax his successor.

We see why Oscar, now a participator in the hoax, cried, “Forward, the book!”

Ten minutes later a handsome young man with a fine figure and pleasant face presented himself, asked for Monsieur Desroches, and gave his name without hesitation.

“I am Frédéric Marest,” he said, “and I come to take the place of third clerk.”

“Monsieur Husson,” said Godeschal, “show monsieur his seat and tell him about the customs of the office.”

The next day the new clerk found the register on his desk. He read a few pages, began to laugh, said nothing to the assembled clerks, and laid the book down again.

“Messieurs,” he said at five o’clock, “I have a cousin who is head-clerk of the notary Maître Léopold Hannequin; I will ask his advice as to what I ought to do for my welcome.”

“That looks ill,” cried Godeschal when Frédéric had gone, “he hasn’t the cut of a novice, that fellow!”

“We’ll get some fun out of him yet,” said Oscar.

The next day at two a young man entered the office whom Oscar recognized as Georges Marest, now head-clerk to the notary Hannequin.

“Ha! here’s the friend of Ali Pacha!” cried Oscar.

“Hey! you here, Monsieur l’ambassadeur!” returned Georges.

Godeschal asked if they knew each other. Georges explained they had “got into a scrape about two years ago” and had to leave Crottat for Hannequin. At Oscar’s signal he brushed it off as nothing. Producing the famous register, Oscar pointed to a sentence of banishment passed on a refractory in 1788.

Georges laughed, leafed through the archives and announced a fête. “Tomorrow, Sunday, you are bidden to the Rocher de Cancale at two. Afterwards I’ll take you to spend the evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, where we’ll play cards and you’ll see the elite of the women of fashion. Therefore, gentlemen of the lower courts,” he added with notarial airs, “behave yourselves, and carry your wine like seigneurs of the Regency.”

The office cheered. Desroches, coming from his private room, suspected a plan to demoralize his clerks. He opened his cash-box and gave Oscar five hundred francs. “Go to the Palais and get from the registrar a copy of the decision in Vandernesse against Vandernesse; it must be served to-night if possible. I promised Simon twenty francs. Wait if the copy is not ready. Don’t let Derville stick a spoke in our wheel; Count Félix de Vandernesse is more powerful than his brother, our client, the ambassador. Keep your eyes open; if there’s the slightest hitch come straight back.”

Oscar set off determined to distinguish himself in his first entrusted affair as second clerk.

After Georges and Oscar left, Godeschal interrogated the new clerk about the supposed marquise. Frédéric, composed like a king’s attorney, sustained his cousin’s hoax. By his manner he convinced the office that the marquise might indeed be a Mexican-born widow of a Spanish grandee, courted by wealthy Georges. “She loves to laugh, sing, drink like me,” he whispered, quoting Béranger. “Georges is very rich: he inherited eighteen thousand francs a year and with twelve thousand from an uncle he has thirty thousand. He pays his debts and gives up the law. He hopes to be Marquis de las Florentinas; the young widow can give her title to her husband.”

Though some clerks remained skeptical, the promise of a Rocher de Cancale breakfast and a fashionable evening put them in high spirits.

In truth the Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos was simply Mademoiselle Agathe‑Florentine Cabirolle, first danseuse at the Gaîté, whom the retired purveyor Cardot had taken up. After Madame Cardot’s death, Cardot encountered Florentine leaving Coulon’s dancing-class when she was about thirteen. Charmed, he followed her home, learned she was a portress’s daughter, and installed mother and daughter in a modest rue de Crussol apartment with proper furnishings and a pension. He sent her to Vestris; at sixteen she made her debut. Cardot raised her stipend, became a “friend for life,” and supported her career.

From 1820 to 1823 she moved among celebrated dancers. Cardot, older and semi-paternal, indulged and protected her, saving eighty thousand francs. Expecting her to need the luxuries of a theatrical star, he had spent forty‑five thousand francs fitting up the former apartment of the late Coralie for her; her lodgings on rue de Vendôme were magnificently furnished. She began to give dinners, spent on dress, kept a carriage and servants, and an engagement at the Opera was in prospect. Cardot intended to leave her a hundred thousand francs.

Georges Marest, with thirty thousand francs a year and a handsome face, courted Florentine. A danseuse always wants a young man to take her out—dinners, boxes, drives—and Georges, newly wealthy after his uncle’s death, stopped thinking of becoming a notary. Frédéric, by contrast, persisted in pursuing public office.

The clerks, sons of poor parents unused to the great world, dressed up to attend Georges’ breakfast and the promise of meeting the Mexican marquise. Oscar felt mixed resentment toward Georges for a prior slight—he still recalled Moreau having flung him at the count’s feet—but he eagerly prepared to display his new clothes. “What luck,” he told Godeschal, boasting of a new coat, trousers, waistcoat and six frilled shirts. Madame Clapart appeared with cravats and praised Godeschal as a model. Godeschal warned Oscar not to blunder as he had the previous night; Desroches had been furious that the judgment was not obtained. He had gone to the Palais himself at six that morning and secured a promise the copy would be ready by seven.

Madame Clapart, proud of her son’s appointment and savings, had given him a gold watch and told him Cardot promised money for a conscript substitute. She also brought a pocket‑book from their friend Moreau. Oscar insisted on carrying the five hundred francs Desroches had given him to fetch the judgment; his mother, alarmed, urged leaving it with Godeschal, but the latter had already gone.

Oscar lounged on the boulevards until Georges’ breakfast. Proud in his new clothes—blue waistcoat with palm pattern, black cashmere trousers, black coat, a saved gilt‑top cane—he enjoyed prospects of the gay world. He tried to keep his aversion for Georges in check—an instinct to avoid those who had once injured him—but at half‑past two, entering the Rocher de Cancale salon, he forgot it. Present were an old captain of dragoons named Giroudeau, Finot the journalist who might secure Florentine’s Opera engagement, and du Bruel the author and friend of Tullia. Georges made himself charming.

“You’ve taken to private diplomacy,” he said. “What difference between a lawyer and an ambassador? Ambassadors are attorneys of peoples. If I can ever be useful to you, let me know.”

Oscar confessed Georges had once done him great harm. “Pooh!” said Georges. “It was Monsieur de Sérizy who behaved badly. His wife—I wouldn’t have her; and the count, Minister of State and peer of France, has a small mind. I don’t care a fig for him.” Oscar welcomed such slurs, which diminished his own fault in his eyes.

The feast stretched long; courses took hours, dessert did not appear until eight. The wines were excellent. The clerks ate and drank like men newly freed from privation. Godeschal, after signing the report of the meal, left them to the wines and liqueurs. By half‑past ten Oscar was so intoxicated Georges packed him into a coach and sent him home, but ten others, also drunk, determined to walk to Florentine’s house. Only Georges, Giroudeau, du Bruel and Finot could keep pace; the rest were carried by carriages Georges sent for, and after an hour’s drive round the boulevards they returned by Bercy and the quays to rue de Vendôme.

Florentine’s salon sparkled with stage princesses who, knowing of Frédéric’s joke, affected the manners of good society as they ate ices. Wax candles burned; footmen in livery served dainties on silver; Lyonnaise hangings and carpets dazzled the newcomers. Through Georges’ presentation and their own intoxication, the clerks believed in the Marquise. Gold glittered on card‑tables in the bed‑chamber; in the salon women played vingt‑et‑un, kept by the celebrated Nathan.

Presented to Florentine by Georges, Oscar at first did not recognize the dancer he had seen at the Gaîté in this aristocratically décolleté and lace‑swaddled figure. She received him with gracious manners, then took him to a vingt‑et‑un table and presented him to Fanny Beaupré, who had just succeeded at the Porte‑Saint‑Martin.

“My dear,” said Florentine, “allow me to present a charming youth whom you can take as a partner in the game.”

“Ah! that will be delightful,” smiled the actress. “I am losing. Shall we go shares, monsieur?”

Oscar sat, ashamed to mix his small silver coins with the actress’s gold. She staked five gold pieces; he produced his hundred francs in silver. In ten throws the actress lost the two hundred francs. She became banker; Florentine lent her five hundred francs obtained from Georges. Nathan had won twelve hundred francs, and the actress whispered to Oscar to bring them luck.

Tempted by the opportunity and the atmosphere, Oscar took out the five hundred francs Desroches had given him. Early throws brought gains—two thousand francs—but a few turns later the gains vanished. He considered feigning illness and escaping, but “honor” held him. Three more throws lost their stake. He drank iced punch; then, desperate, he accepted Florentine’s loan of a thousand francs to win back his losses—she advised not to risk more than five hundred. She gave him the money to help him, yet Finot and Giroudeau won it from him. The company laughed over the “poor innocent.”

By dawn, after “character dancing,” Florentine, tired, went to bed, oblivious to Oscar still asleep in the boudoir.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About eleven the next morning a terrible noise roused the unlucky clerk. Hearing his uncle Cardot’s voice, he feigned sleep and buried his face in the yellow velvet cushions where he had passed the night.

“Really, my little Florentine,” said the old gentleman, “this is neither right nor sensible; you danced last evening in ‘Les Ruines,’ and you have passed the night in an orgy. That’s deliberately going to lose your freshness. Besides, it was ungrateful to inaugurate this beautiful apartment without even letting me know. Who knows what has been going on here?”

“Old monster!” cried Florentine. “Haven’t you a key that lets you in at all hours? My ball lasted till five in the morning, and you have the cruelty to come and wake me at eleven!”

“Half-past eleven, Titine,” Cardot corrected meekly. “I came out early to order a dinner fit for an archbishop at Chevet’s. Just see how the carpets are stained! What sort of people did you have here?”

“You needn’t complain, for Fanny Beaupré told me you were coming to dinner with Camusot, and to please you I invited Tullia, du Bruel, Mariette, the Duc de Maufrigneuse, Florine, and Nathan. So you’ll have the four loveliest creatures ever seen behind the foot-lights; we’ll dance you a ‘pas de Zéphire.’”

“It is enough to kill you to lead such a life!” cried Cardot. “And look at the broken glasses! What pillage! The antechamber actually makes me shudder—”

He stopped. Through the open door of the boudoir he saw the outline of a man in a black coat.

“Ah, Mademoiselle Cabirolle!” he said.

“Well, what?” she asked.

Florentine’s eyes followed Cardot’s; then, seeing the clerk, she burst into such fits of laughter that Cardot was nonplussed, and Oscar was compelled to rise. Florentine took him by the arm, still pealing at the conscience-stricken faces of the uncle and nephew.

“You here, nephew?”

“Nephew? so he’s your nephew?” cried Florentine, with another burst of laughter. “You never told me about him. Why didn’t Mariette carry you off?” she said to Oscar, who stood petrified. “What can he do now, poor boy?”

“Whatever he pleases!” said Cardot sharply, starting toward the door as if to go.

“One moment, papa Cardot. You will be good enough to get your nephew out of a scrape into which I led him; for he played his master’s money and lost it, and I lent him a thousand francs to win it back, and he lost that too.”

“Miserable boy! you lost fifteen hundred francs at play at your age?”

“Oh, uncle, uncle!” cried Oscar, plunged into despair, dropping on his knees, hands clasped. “It is twelve o’clock! I am lost, dishonored! Monsieur Desroches will have no pity! He gave me the money for an important affair, in which his pride was concerned. I was to get a paper at the Palais in the case of Vandernesse versus Vandernesse! What will become of me? Oh, save me for the sake of my father and aunt! Come with me to Monsieur Desroches, and explain it to him; make some excuse—anything!”

These sobs might have moved a sphinx. Florentine, scolding Cardot for cruelty, appealed to his sense of obligation: Oscar was the son of the man to whom Cardot owed his fortune. Cardot hesitated.

“Don’t you see that the reason he forgot to go for those papers was because he was drunk and overslept? Georges and his cousin Frédéric took all the clerks in his office to a feast at the Rocher de Cancale.”

Père Cardot looked at Florentine and hesitated.

“Come, come,” she said, “you old monkey, wouldn’t I have hidden him better if there had been any other business?”

“There, take your five hundred francs, you scamp!” said Cardot to his nephew, “and remember, that’s the last penny you’ll ever get from me. Go and make it up with your master if you can. I’ll return the thousand francs which you borrowed of mademoiselle; but I’ll never hear another word about you.”

Oscar fled, not wishing to hear more. Once in the street he knew not where to go.

Chance which destroys men and chance which saves them were both at work that morning. When Mariette reached home that night, alarmed for the youth in whom her brother took interest, she wrote a hasty note to Godeschal telling him what had happened to Oscar and enclosing a bank bill for five hundred francs to repair his loss. She charged her maid to carry the note to Desroches’ office before seven o’clock. Godeschal, rising at six and finding Oscar absent, guessed what had occurred. He took the five hundred francs from his own hoard and rushed to the Palais, where he obtained a copy of the judgment and returned in time to lay it before Desroches by eight o’clock.

Desroches, who always rose at four, was in his office by seven. Mariette’s maid, not finding her mistress’s brother in his bedroom, came down to the office and met Desroches, to whom she naturally offered the note.

“Is it about business?” he said; “I am Monsieur Desroches.”

“You can see, monsieur,” replied the maid.

Desroches opened the letter and read it. Finding the five-hundred-franc note, he went into his private office, furious with his second clerk. About half-past seven he heard Godeschal dictating to the second head-clerk a copy of the document in question, and a few moments later the good fellow entered his master’s office with an air of triumph.

“Did Oscar Husson fetch the paper this morning from Simon?” inquired Desroches.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“Who gave him the money?”

“Why, you did, Saturday,” replied Godeschal.

“Then it rains five-hundred-franc notes,” cried Desroches. “Look here, Godeschal, you are a fine fellow, but that little Husson does not deserve such generosity. I hate idiots, but I hate still more the men who will go wrong in spite of the fatherly care which watches over them.” He gave Godeschal Mariette’s letter and the banknote which she had sent. “You must excuse my having opened it,” he said, “but your sister’s maid told me it was on business. Dismiss Husson.”

“Poor unhappy boy! what grief he has caused me!” said Godeschal. “That tall ne’er-do-well of a Georges Marest is his evil genius; he ought to flee him like the plague; if not, he’ll bring him to some third disgrace.”

“What do you mean by that?” asked Desroches.

Godeschal briefly related the affair of the journey to Presles, reminding Desroches of a story Joseph Bridau had told and of favors since granted by Monsieur de Sérizy. At this moment Moreau, to whom the Vandernesse case was important, entered. The marquis wished to sell the land in parcels and the count opposed it; the land-agent bore the brunt of Desroches’ wrath as he thundered against his ex-second clerk and prophesied ill for the lad. The result of the morning’s events was that the man who had been a sincere friend and protector of Oscar concluded his vanity incorrigible.

“Make him a barrister,” said Desroches. “He has only his last examination to pass. In that line, his defects might prove virtues, for self-love and vanity give tongues to half the attorneys.”

Meanwhile Clapart, who was ill, was being nursed by his wife—a painful, unrewarded duty. The sick man tormented his poor creature; impoverished and crafty, he teased his wife maliciously. By the way she praised Oscar whenever he obtained the slightest success, Clapart knew the extent of her secret uneasiness and took pains to rouse it.

“Well, Madame,” Clapart would say, “Oscar is doing better than I even hoped. That journey to Presles was only a heedlessness of youth. Where can you find young lads who do not commit such faults? Poor child! he bears his privations heroically! If his father had lived, he would never have had any. God grant he may know how to control his passions!”

While these events occurred in the rue de Vendôme and the rue de Béthisy, Clapart, wrapped in an old dressing-gown, watched his wife in their bedroom as she prepared the family broth, Clapart’s “tisane,” and her breakfast.

“Mon Dieu! I wish I knew how the affair of yesterday ended. Oscar was to breakfast at the Rocher de Cancale and spend the evening with a marquise—”

“Don’t trouble yourself! Sooner or later you’ll find out about your swan,” said her husband. “Do you really believe in that marquise? Pooh! A young man who has senses and a taste for extravagance like Oscar can find such ladies as that on every bush—if he pays for them. Some fine morning you’ll find yourself with a load of debt on your back.”

“You are always trying to put me in despair!” cried Madame Clapart. “You complained that my son lived on your salary, and never has he cost you a penny. For two years you haven’t had the slightest cause of complaint against him; here he is second clerk, his uncle and Monsieur Moreau pay all expenses, and he earns, himself, a salary of eight hundred francs. If we have bread to eat in our old age we may owe it all to that dear boy. You are really too unjust—”

“You call my foresight unjust, do you?” replied the invalid, crossly.

Just then the bell rang. Madame Clapart ran to open the door, and remained in the outer room with Moreau, who had come to soften the blow which Oscar’s new folly would deal to the poor mother’s heart.

“What! he gambled with the money of the office?” she cried, bursting into tears.

“Didn’t I tell you so, hey?” said Clapart, appearing like a spectre at the salon’s door where curiosity had drawn him.

“Oh! what shall we do with him?” said Madame Clapart, whose grief made her impervious to Clapart’s taunts.

“If he bore my name,” replied Moreau, “I should wait composedly till he draws for the conscription, and if he gets a fatal number I should not provide him with a substitute. This is the second time your son has committed a folly out of sheer vanity. Well, vanity may inspire fine deeds in war and may advance him in the career of a soldier. Besides, six years of military service will put some lead into his head; and as he has only his last legal examination to pass, it won’t be much ill-luck for him if he doesn’t become a lawyer till he is twenty-six; that is, if he wants to continue in the law after paying, as they say, his tax of blood. By that time, at any rate, he will have been severely punished, he will have learned experience, and contracted habits of subordination. Before making his probation at the bar he will have gone through his probations in life.”

“If that is your decision for a son,” said Madame Clapart, “I see that the heart of a father is not like that of a mother. My poor Oscar a common soldier!--”

“Would you rather he flung himself headforemost into the Seine after committing a dishonorable action? He cannot now become a solicitor; do you think him steady and wise enough to be a barrister? No. While his reason is maturing, what will he become? A dissipated fellow. The discipline of the army will, at least, preserve him from that.”

“Could he not go into some other office? His uncle Cardot has promised to pay for his substitute; Oscar is to dedicate his graduating thesis to him.”

At that moment carriage-wheels were heard, and a hackney-coach containing Oscar and all his worldly belongings stopped before the door. The luckless young man came up at once.

“Ah! here you are, Monsieur Joli-Cœur!” cried Clapart.

Oscar kissed his mother and held out a hand to Moreau, which Moreau refused. Oscar replied with a reproachful look the boldness of which he had never shown before. Then he turned on Clapart.

“Listen to me, monsieur,” said the youth, transformed into a man. “You worry my poor mother devilishly, and that’s your right, for she is, unfortunately, your wife. But as for me, it is another thing. I shall be of age in a few months; and you have no rights over me even as a minor. I have never asked anything of you. Thanks to Monsieur Moreau, I have never cost you one penny, and I owe you no gratitude. Therefore, I say, let me alone!”

Clapart, hearing this apostrophe, slunk back to his sofa. The reasoning and inward fury of the young man, who had just received a lecture from his friend Godeschal, silenced the imbecile mind of the sick man.

“A momentary temptation, such as you yourself would have yielded to at my age,” said Oscar to Moreau, “has made me commit a fault which Desroches thinks serious, though it is only a peccadillo. I am more provoked with myself for taking Florentine of the Gaieté for a marquise than I am for losing fifteen hundred francs after a little debauch in which everybody, even Godeschal, was half-seas over. This time, at least, I’ve hurt no one but myself. I’m cured of such things forever. If you are willing to help me, Monsieur Moreau, I swear to you that the six years I must still stay a clerk before I can get a practice shall be spent without—”

“Stop there!” said Moreau. “I have three children, and I can make no promises.”

“Never mind, never mind,” said Madame Clapart to her son, casting a reproachful glance at Moreau. “Your uncle Cardot—”

“I have no longer an uncle Cardot,” replied Oscar, relating the scene at the rue de Vendôme.

Madame Clapart, feeling her legs give way, staggered to a chair and fell as if struck by lightning.

“All the miseries together!” she said, and fainted.

Moreau took her in his arms and carried her to bed. Oscar remained motionless, as if crushed.

“There is nothing left for you,” said Moreau when he returned, “but to make yourself a soldier. That idiot of a Clapart looks to me as though he couldn’t live three months, and then your mother will be without a penny. Ought I not, therefore, to reserve for her the little money I am able to give? It was impossible to tell you this before her. As a soldier, you’ll eat plain bread and reflect on life such as it is to those who are born into it without fortune.”

“I may get a lucky number,” said Oscar.

“Suppose you do, what then? Your mother has well fulfilled her duty towards you. She gave you an education; she placed you on the right road, and secured you a career. You have left it. Now, what can you do? Without money, nothing; as you know by this time. You are not a man who can begin a new trade by taking off your coat and going to work in your shirt-sleeves with the tools of an artisan. Besides, your mother loves you, and she would die to see you come to that.”

Oscar sat down and no longer restrained his tears. At last he understood this language, so completely unintelligible to him since his first fault.

“Men without means ought to be perfect,” added Moreau, not suspecting the profundity of that cruel sentence.

“My fate will soon be decided,” said Oscar. “I draw my number the day after to-morrow. Between now and then I will decide upon my future.”

Moreau, deeply distressed in spite of his stern bearing, left the household in the rue de la Cerisaie to its despair.

Three days later Oscar drew the number twenty-seven. In his interest the former steward of Presles had courage to go to the Comte de Sérizy and ask for his influence to get Oscar into the cavalry. It happened that the count’s son, having left the École Polytechnique rather low in his class, had been appointed, as a favor, sub-lieutenant in a regiment of cavalry commanded by the Duc de Maufrigneuse. Oscar was thus, through the Comte de Sérizy’s intercession, drafted into that regiment, with the promise of promotion to quartermaster within a year. Chance had placed the ex-clerk under the command of the son of the Comte de Sérizy.

Madame Clapart, after languishing for several days, became a victim to remorse. She attributed the sorrows of her second marriage and the misfortunes of her son to retribution for the pleasures of her youth. This belief hardened into conviction. The poor woman went, for the first time in forty years, to confess herself to the Abbé Gaudron, vicar of Saint-Paul’s, and took up devotion. But so ill-used and loving a soul as Madame Clapart’s could never be anything but simply pious. The Aspasia of the Directory wanted to expiate her sins in order to draw down God’s blessing on Oscar, and she henceforth vowed herself to works of the purest piety. She believed she had won heaven’s attention when she saved the life of Monsieur Clapart, who, thanks to her devotion, lived on to torture her; yet she chose to see his tyranny as a trial inflicted by One who loveth while He chasteneth.

Oscar meantime behaved so well that by 1830 he was first sergeant of the company of the Vicomte de Sérizy, which gave him the rank of sub-lieutenant of the line. Oscar Husson was by that time twenty-five. As the Royal Guard, to which his regiment was attached, was always in or near Paris, he came to see his mother from time to time and tell her his griefs; he had sense enough to see he could hardly become an officer as matters then were. The cavalry grades were being taken up by the younger sons of noble families, and men without titles found promotion difficult. Oscar’s sole ambition was to leave the Guards and be appointed sub-lieutenant in a regiment of the line. In February 1830 Madame Clapart obtained this promotion for her son through the influence of Madame la Dauphine, granted to the Abbé Gaudron, now rector of Saint-Pauls.

Although Oscar outwardly professed devotion to the Bourbons, in the depths of his heart he was a liberal. Therefore, in the struggle of 1830, he went over to the side of the people. This desertion, significant because of the crisis, brought him into public notice. During the triumphal excitement in August he was promoted lieutenant, received the cross of the Legion of Honor, and was attached as aide-de-camp to La Fayette, who made him captain in 1832. When La Fayette was removed from command of the National Guard, Oscar, whose devotion to the new dynasty amounted to fanaticism, was appointed major of a regiment sent to Africa at the time of the Prince-Royal’s first expedition. The Vicomte de Sérizy chanced to be lieutenant-colonel of this regiment. At the affair of the Makta, where the field had to be abandoned to the Arabs, Monsieur de Sérizy was left wounded under a dead horse. Oscar, discovering this, called to the squadron:

“Messieurs, it is going to death, but we cannot abandon our colonel.”

He dashed upon the enemy, and his electrified soldiers followed him. The Arabs, astonished by this furious return, allowed Oscar to seize the vicomte, whom he flung across his horse and carried off at full gallop, receiving two slashes from yataghans on his left arm.

Oscar’s conduct was rewarded with the officer’s cross of the Legion of Honor and promotion to lieutenant-colonel. He took most affectionate care of the Vicomte de Sérizy, whose mother met him on the regiment’s arrival at Toulon, where, as we know, the young man died of his wounds.

The Comtesse de Sérizy had not separated her son from the man who had shown him such devotion. Oscar himself was so seriously wounded that the surgeons whom the countess had brought from Paris deemed it best to amputate his left arm.

Thus the Comte de Sérizy was led not only to forgive Oscar for his painful remarks on the journey to Presles, but to feel himself indebted to him on behalf of his son, now buried in the chapel of the château de Sérizy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Years after the affair at Makta, an old lady in black, leaning on the arm of a man of about thirty-four—his arm gone and a Legion of Honor rosette showing his rank as a retired officer—waited one May morning under the porte-cochere of the Lion d’Argent, rue de Faubourg Saint-Denis, for a diligence. Pierrotin, master of the coaches through the valley of the Oise, sending one by Saint-Leu-Taverny and Isle-Adam to Beaumont, would scarcely have recognized in this bronzed, maimed officer the little Oscar Husson he had taken to Presles. Madame Husson, now a widow, was hardly recognizable either. Clapart, a victim of Fieschi’s machine, had by death done his wife more service than in life; the idle lounger whose presence caused the explosion was gone. The poor widow had been placed on a pension of fifteen hundred francs a year.

The coach, with four iron-gray horses of the Messageries-royales, was divided into coupe, interieur, and rotonde, with an imperiale above. Solid, light, and well kept, lined with blue cloth, Moorish-pattern blinds and red morocco cushions, the “Swallow of the Oise” could carry nineteen passengers. Pierrotin, about fifty-six, little changed, still in a blue blouse over a black suit, smoked his pipe and supervised two liveried porters stowing luggage on the imperiale.

“Are your places taken?” he asked Madame Clapart and Oscar, eyeing them like a man trying to recall a likeness.

“Yes—two places in the interieur in the name of my servant, Bellejambe,” said Oscar; “he must have taken them last evening.”

“Ah! monsieur is the new collector of Beaumont,” said Pierrotin. “You take the place of Monsieur Margueron’s nephew?”

“Yes,” Oscar replied, pressing his mother’s arm as she was about to speak. The officer wished to remain unknown for a time.

Suddenly Oscar thrilled at the well-remembered voice of Georges Marest calling from the street: “Pierrotin, have you one seat left?”

“It seems to me you could say ‘monsieur’ without cracking your throat,” Pierrotin snapped.

By voice alone Oscar would not have recognized the man whose jest had once been fatal to him. Georges, almost bald, kept tufts above his ears to hide his bare skull. A pear-shaped stomach marred his former elegance. Disasters in love and debauchery showed in blotched skin, bloated, vinous features and dull eyes. Careless of clothes, he wore shabby trousers with boot straps and thick-soled, ill-blacked boots. A faded waistcoat and a pretentiously tied cravat of worn foulard betrayed his fall. An evening coat chosen instead of a surtout—seams whitening, greasy collar, frayed sleeve-edges—spoke poverty. Still he ostentatiously wore yellow kid gloves, a signet ring, and a gold-ringed cravat holding a silk watch chain. His battered cane, blue trousers, cheap waistcoat, sky-blue cravat and pink-striped cotton shirt revealed a latent desire to show off that was both spectacle and lesson.

“And that is Georges!” thought Oscar—“the man I left with thirty thousand francs a year!”

“Has Monsieur de Pierrotin a place in the coupe?” Georges asked, answering Pierrotin’s rebuff.

“No; my coupe is taken by a peer of France, Monsieur le Baron de Canalis, his wife, and his mother-in-law. I have nothing left but one place in the interieur.”

“The devil! so peers of France still travel in your coach, do they?” said Georges, recalling his adventure with the Comte de Serizy. “Well, I’ll take that place in the interieur.”

He glanced at Oscar and his mother but did not recognize them. Oscar’s skin was bronzed by Africa; his moustache thick, whiskers ample; hollows in his cheeks and strongly marked features suited his military bearing. The Legion rosette, missing arm, and strict propriety of dress would have diverted Georges’ recollection of his former victim if he had any. Madame Clapart, scarcely seen by Georges, had been transformed by ten years of severe piety; no one would guess the Aspasia of 1797 hid beneath that gray sister.

An enormous old man, simply dressed though in good clothes—Pere Leger—came slowly along and nodded to Pierrotin, who paid him the respect due millionaires.

“Ha! ha! here’s Pere Leger! more and more preponderant!” cried Georges.

“To whom have I the honor of speaking?” asked Leger curtly.

“What! you don’t recognize Colonel Georges, the friend of Ali Pacha? We traveled together once, with the Comte de Serizy.”

One folly of those fallen in the world is the thirst for recognition.

“You are much changed,” said the ex-farmer, now twice a millionaire.

“All things change,” Georges shrugged. “Look at the Lion d’Argent and Pierrotin’s coach; they are not like fourteen years ago.”

“Pierrotin now controls the whole Valley of the Oise service,” Leger replied, “and sends out five coaches. He is the bourgeois of Beaumont; he keeps a hotel where all the diligences stop, and has a wife and daughter who are not a bad help to him.”

An old man of seventy came from the hotel and joined them.

“Come along, Papa Reybert,” said Leger, “we are only waiting now for your great man.”

“Here he comes,” said the steward of Presles, pointing to Joseph Bridau.

Neither Georges nor Oscar recognized the illustrious artist, whose face bore the worn lines of fame and whose bearing showed success. The Legion ribbon adorned his black coat; his elegant dress suited a rural fête.

A clerk with a paper came from the office, now in the former kitchen of the Lion d’Argent, and stood before the empty coupe.

“Monsieur and Madame de Canalis, three places,” he said. At the interieur door: “Monsieur Bellejambe, two places; Monsieur de Reybert, three places; Monsieur—your name, if you please?” he said to Georges.

“Georges Marest,” said the fallen man, low.

The clerk moved to the rotunde, where nurses, country folk and small shopkeepers gathered; after bundling in six passengers, he called four young men to the imperiale; then cried: “Start!” Pierrotin mounted beside his driver, who called to the horses: “Pull!” The vehicle, drawn by four horses brought at Roye, climbed the faubourg Saint-Denis at a slow trot.

Past Saint-Laurent it raced like a mail-cart to Saint-Denis, reached in forty minutes. No stop was made at the cheese-cake inn; the coach took the road through the valley of Montmorency.

At the turn into this road Georges broke the silence the travelers had kept while observing each other.

“We go a little faster than fifteen years ago, hey, Pere Leger?” he said, pulling out a silver watch.

“Persons are usually good enough to call me Monsieur Leger,” the millionaire corrected.

“Why, here’s our blagueur of the famous journey to Presles,” cried Joseph Bridau. “Have you made any new campaigns in Asia, Africa, or America?”

“Sacrebleu! I’ve made the revolution of July, and that’s enough for me—it ruined me.”

“Ah! you made the revolution of July!” cried the painter, laughing. “Well, I always said it never made itself.”

“How people meet again!” Leger said to Monsieur de Reybert. “This, papa Reybert, is the clerk of the notary to whom you doubtless owe the stewardship of Presles.”

“We lack Mistigris, now famous under his own name of Leon de Lora,” said Joseph Bridau. “And the little young man who was stupid enough to talk to the count about those skin diseases which are now cured, and about his wife, whom he has recently left so he may die in peace.”

“And the count himself, you lack him,” said old Reybert.

“I’m afraid,” said Joseph Bridau sadly, “that the last journey the count will ever take will be from Presles to Isle-Adam, to be present at my marriage.”

“He still drives about the park,” said Reybert.

“Does his wife come to see him?” asked Leger.

“Once a month,” Reybert replied. “She is never happy out of Paris. Last September she married her niece, Mademoiselle du Rouvre, whom, since their son’s death, she has befriended, to a very rich young Pole, the Comte Laginski.”

“To whom,” asked Madame Clapart, “will Monsieur de Serizy’s property go?”

“To his wife, who will bury him,” replied Georges. “The countess is still fine-looking for fifty-four. She is very elegant and gives one the illusion—”

“She will always be an illusion to you,” said Leger, avenging his old hoaxer.

“I respect her,” Georges said. “But what became of that steward the count turned off?”

“Moreau?” Leger replied. “He’s the deputy from the Oise.”

“Ha! the famous Centre man; Moreau de l’Oise?” cried Georges.

“Yes,” Leger returned, “Moreau de l’Oise. He did more than you for the revolution of July, and has since bought the beautiful estate of Pointel, between Presles and Beaumont.”

“Next to the count’s,” said Georges. “I call that bad taste.”

“Don’t speak so loud,” said Monsieur de Reybert, “for Madame Moreau and her daughter, the Baronne de Canalis, and the Baron himself, the former minister, are in the coupe.”

“What ‘dot’ could he have given his daughter to induce our great orator to marry her?” Georges asked.

“Something like two millions,” old Leger said.

“He always had a taste for millions,” remarked Georges. “He began his pile surreptitiously at Presles—”

“Say nothing against Monsieur Moreau,” Oscar cried hastily. “You ought to have learned by now to hold your tongue in public conveyances.”

Joseph Bridau looked at the one-armed officer for several seconds, then smiled.

“Monsieur is not an ambassador, but his rosette tells us he has made his way nobly; my brother and General Giroudeau have repeatedly named him in their reports.”

“Oscar Husson!” cried Georges. “Faith! if it hadn’t been for your voice I should never have known you.”

“Ah! it was monsieur who so bravely rescued the Vicomte Jules de Serizy from the Arabs?” asked Reybert, “and for whom the count has obtained the collectorship of Beaumont while awaiting that of Pontoise?”

“Yes, monsieur,” said Oscar.

“I hope you will give me the pleasure, monsieur,” the great painter said, “of being present at my marriage at Isle-Adam.”

“Whom do you marry?” asked Oscar after accepting.

“Mademoiselle Leger,” Joseph Bridau replied, “the granddaughter of Monsieur de Reybert. Monsieur le comte arranged the marriage for me. As an artist I owe him a great deal, and he wished, before his death, to secure my future, about which I did not think myself.”

“Whom did Pere Leger marry?” asked Georges.

“My daughter,” replied Monsieur de Reybert, “and without a ‘dot.’”

“Ah!” Georges said, assuming a more respectful manner toward Leger. “I am fortunate in having chosen this particular day to do the valley of the Oise. You can all be useful to me, gentlemen.”

“How so?” asked Monsieur Leger.

“In this way,” Georges replied. “I am employed by the ‘Esperance,’ a company just formed, its statutes approved by royal ordinance. This institution gives dowries to girls after ten years, annuities to old men; it pays children’s education and takes charge, in short, of everybody’s fortunes.”

“I can well believe it,” said Pere Leger with a smile. “In a word, you are a runner for an insurance company.”

“No, monsieur. I am the inspector-general; charged with establishing correspondents and appointing agents of the company throughout France. I operate only until agents are selected; it is as delicate as it is difficult to find honest agents.”

“But how did you lose your thirty thousand a year?” asked Oscar.

“As you lost your arm,” replied Georges curtly.

“Then you must have shared in some brilliant action,” Oscar remarked, with sarcasm and bitterness.

“Parbleu! I’ve too many—shares! that’s just what I wanted to sell.”

They arrived at Saint-Leu-Taverny, where all disembarked while the coach changed horses. Oscar admired Pierrotin’s liveliness in unhooking the traces while the driver cleared the reins.

“Poor Pierrotin,” he thought; “he has stuck like me,—not far advanced in the world. Georges has fallen low. All the others, by speculation and talent, have made fortunes. Do we breakfast here, Pierrotin?” he said aloud, slapping him on the shoulder.

“I am not the driver,” Pierrotin answered.

“What are you, then?” asked Colonel Husson.

“The proprietor,” replied Pierrotin.

“Come, don’t be vexed with an old acquaintance,” Oscar said, motioning to his mother, but still retaining a patronizing air. “Don’t you recognize Madame Clapart?”

It was noble of Oscar to present his mother to Pierrotin, for at that moment Madame Moreau de l’Oise, alighting from the coupe, overheard the name and stared disdainfully at Oscar and his mother.

“My faith! madame,” Pierrotin said, “I should never have known you; nor you, either, monsieur; the sun burns black in Africa, doesn’t it?”

This pity Oscar felt for Pierrotin was the last blunder vanity led him to commit, and like his other faults it was punished, but gently: two months after his official installation at Beaumont-sur-Oise, Oscar was paying addresses to Mademoiselle Georgette Pierrotin, whose ‘dot’ was one hundred and fifty thousand francs, and he married the pretty daughter of the proprietor of the stage-coaches of the Oise toward the close of winter, 1838.

The adventure of the journey to Presles taught Oscar Husson discretion; his disaster at Florentine’s card-party strengthened his honesty; the hardships of his military career taught him to understand social hierarchy and yield obedience to his lot. Becoming wise and capable, he was happy. The Comte de Serizy, before his death, obtained the collectorship at Pontoise for him. The influence of Monsieur Moreau de l’Oise and of the Comtesse de Serizy and the Baron de Canalis later secured him a receiver-generalship, and the Camusot family now recognize a relation in him.

Oscar is a commonplace man—gentle, without assumption, modest, always keeping, like his government, to a middle course. He excites neither envy nor contempt. In short, he is the modern bourgeois.
