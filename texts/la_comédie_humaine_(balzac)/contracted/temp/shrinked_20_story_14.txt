DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini.

Madame, recalling our Milan conversation, I dedicate this work to you, hoping you will shelter it with your name as you once protected a beloved writer’s tales. You have a daughter, Eugénie, whose beauty and intelligence promise a freer life than the rigid fate of the Eugénie in these pages. Though Frenchmen are deemed fickle, I am Italian in constancy and memory: the name “Eugénie” brings Milan’s warmth, and I regard you as a distant, treasured masterpiece.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a blue-velvet boudoir on rue Neuve-des-Mathurins two young women weep by the fire. The room belongs to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who bought social standing by marriage but lacks aristocratic ease. Madame du Tillet holds her sister Madame Félix de Vandenesse’s hand as they confide. Raised by a strict, pious mother and married without youthful freedom, they learned only religion and needlework—ignorant of adult passions—so marriage, once imagined as escape, reveals harsh realities.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, distraught by marital troubles, confesses despair over a husband indifferent to her inner life. Madame du Tillet, equally helpless, describes her own cold domesticity. Both admit a craving for intense feeling and seek consolation in each other.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Félix de Vandenesse, of noble lineage, married hoping to recreate an idealized love. But the duties and restraints of his class cool their relationship. Though kind and attentive, he cannot provide the romantic intensity his wife desires; monotony breeds longing and the temptation to look beyond marriage.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, an ambitious literary figure, enters society with talent, audacity, and caustic wit. His unconventional charm unsettles and attracts. At Madame de Montcornet’s salon he catches Madame de Vandenesse’s attention; their exchanges hint at a growing, if unspoken, attraction. Nathan, amid career strain and private troubles, sees her as a possible muse, though social bounds bar any open liaison.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a gifted actress and Nathan’s long-term companion, combines affection with practicality: she sustains him and relies on him for protection and status. As Nathan’s admiration for the countess grows, Florine becomes insecure and jealous.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Nathan and Madame de Vandenesse bond over art and literature; their meetings grow intimate, bringing exhilaration and peril. She is torn—guilty yet enticed—between loyalty to her husband and the lure of a passionate life, aware that yielding risks social ruin.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

Crushed by setbacks, Nathan contemplates suicide. A chance encounter with Madame de Vandenesse revives his will to live; seeing his despair, she vows to protect and encourage him, countering his darkest impulses.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

She becomes more involved, balancing protection with her own feelings. Their secrecy, social risk, and Florine’s intensifying jealousy complicate matters. Nathan is torn between Florine’s practical devotion and the countess’s inspiring influence.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Félix, sensing his wife’s attachment, moves to preserve their marriage. Determined to protect her and their honor, he seeks the truth. Love, ambition, loyalty, and expectation entwine, setting the stage for a dramatic clash between desire and duty.
