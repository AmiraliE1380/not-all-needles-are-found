A DAUGHTER OF EVE

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati.

Madame, recalling the joy your conversation brought to a traveler in Milan, I lay this work at your feet, hoping you will protect it with your name, as it once shielded the tales of a beloved writer. You have a daughter, Eugenie, whose beauty and intelligence promise her a joyful childhood, unlike the rigid upbringing of the Eugenie in these pages. While Frenchmen are often seen as fickle, I remain Italian in my loyalty and memory. Writing “Eugenie” brings me back to the laughter and warmth of our time together in Milan, though I now think of you as a distant figure, like a masterpiece from a great artist.

Your devoted servant,
De Balzac.

A DAUGHTER OF EVE

CHAPTER I. THE TWO MARIES

In a luxurious boudoir in the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, two young women sat weeping before a fireplace. The room, adorned with blue velvet and fine furnishings, belonged to Ferdinand du Tillet, a wealthy banker who had married the daughter of a prominent magistrate. Despite his wealth, du Tillet lacked social standing, having married into the aristocracy through a dowry agreement.

Madame du Tillet, holding her sister Madame Felix de Vandenesse’s hand, listened to her sister’s heartfelt confessions. The two sisters, raised under a strict and pious mother, had married without ever experiencing the joys of youth. Their upbringing had been so severe that they entered marriage utterly naive about life and love.

The sisters shared a deep bond, having grown up in a repressive household where their mother’s rigid control stifled their spirits. They had never been allowed to experience the world outside their home, and their education was limited to religious instruction and needlework. Their innocence was preserved, but at the cost of their understanding of life’s complexities.

As they confided in each other, the sisters reflected on their childhood and the oppressive atmosphere created by their mother. They had longed for freedom and love, and marriage was seen as their escape. However, they were unaware of the challenges that awaited them in their new lives.

CHAPTER II. A CONFIDENCE BETWEEN SISTERS

Madame de Vandenesse, overwhelmed by her troubles, sought solace in her sister’s company. She expressed her despair over her marriage to a man who seemed to care little for her happiness. Madame du Tillet, feeling helpless, listened as her sister lamented her situation. The sisters’ love for each other was evident, and they shared a bond that transcended their husbands’ indifference.

Madame du Tillet revealed her own struggles, feeling trapped in a marriage devoid of affection. She described her husband’s coldness and the emptiness of her life, contrasting it with the warmth of their sisterly bond. The sisters’ shared experiences of repression and longing for love deepened their connection.

As they spoke, Madame de Vandenesse confided her desire for a life filled with passion and excitement, while Madame du Tillet encouraged her to seek happiness. They both recognized the limitations imposed by their marriages and the societal expectations that constrained them.

CHAPTER III. THE HISTORY OF A FORTUNATE WOMAN

Felix de Vandenesse, a man of noble lineage, had married Madame de Vandenesse, hoping to find happiness in a union with a pure and innocent woman. He had experienced love in its most ideal form and sought to recreate that happiness in his marriage. However, as time passed, he realized that the constraints of society and the expectations placed upon them were stifling their relationship.

Madame de Vandenesse, initially content in her marriage, began to feel the weight of monotony and the absence of passion. She longed for excitement and adventure, feeling trapped in a life that had become predictable. Her husband, while kind and attentive, could not fulfill her desire for deeper emotional connection.

As the couple navigated their lives, Madame de Vandenesse found herself drawn to the idea of love outside her marriage. She became aware of the allure of romantic passion and the excitement it promised, leading her to question her own happiness.

CHAPTER IV. A CELEBRATED MAN

Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, entered the social scene with a unique blend of talent and ambition. He was known for his sharp wit and caustic humor, which endeared him to some while alienating others. His unconventional appearance and demeanor set him apart, making him a captivating figure in the eyes of women.

At a gathering hosted by Madame de Montcornet, Nathan caught the attention of Madame de Vandenesse, who was intrigued by his charisma. Their initial interactions were charged with unspoken attraction, and both felt a magnetic pull toward one another.

As Nathan navigated the complexities of his career and personal life, he found himself increasingly drawn to Madame de Vandenesse. Their connection deepened, and he began to see her as a muse, inspiring his creative endeavors. However, the societal constraints surrounding them posed challenges to their budding romance.

CHAPTER V. FLORINE

Florine, a talented actress and Nathan’s long-time companion, represented a different side of his life. Their relationship was marked by a blend of affection and practicality, as Florine provided Nathan with emotional support while also relying on him for her own success. Their dynamic was complex, with both individuals navigating the demands of their respective careers.

As Nathan’s ambitions grew, he found himself torn between his loyalty to Florine and his burgeoning feelings for Madame de Vandenesse. The actress, aware of Nathan’s infatuation with the countess, felt threatened by the potential shift in their relationship.

CHAPTER VI. ROMANTIC LOVE

Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan’s relationship blossomed, fueled by their shared passion for art and literature. Their encounters became increasingly intimate, and both felt a sense of exhilaration in their connection. However, the societal expectations and the realities of their lives loomed over them, creating tension in their romance.

As their love deepened, Madame de Vandenesse grappled with her feelings of guilt and desire. She found herself caught between her loyalty to her husband and her growing affection for Nathan. The emotional turmoil she experienced only intensified her longing for a life filled with passion and excitement.

CHAPTER VII. SUICIDE

As Nathan faced mounting pressures in his career and personal life, he found himself overwhelmed by despair. The weight of his ambitions and the fear of failure led him to contemplate suicide. However, a chance encounter with Madame de Vandenesse reignited his hope and desire to live.

The countess, determined to save Nathan from his self-destructive tendencies, took it upon herself to support him. She recognized the depth of his struggles and sought to provide him with the encouragement he needed to overcome his challenges.

CHAPTER VIII. A LOVER SAVED AND LOST

In the aftermath of Nathan’s near-suicide, Madame de Vandenesse became increasingly involved in his life. She sought to protect him from the dangers of his ambitions while also navigating her own feelings for him. Their relationship became a source of both strength and turmoil, as they grappled with the complexities of love and societal expectations.

As Nathan’s career began to flourish, he found himself torn between his loyalty to Florine and his growing affection for Madame de Vandenesse. The actress, feeling threatened by the countess, became increasingly possessive of Nathan, leading to tension in their relationship.

CHAPTER IX. THE HUSBAND’S TRIUMPH

Felix de Vandenesse, aware of the growing bond between his wife and Nathan, took action to protect his marriage. He sought to uncover the truth behind their relationship while also ensuring that Madame de Vandenesse remained safe from scandal. His determination to shield his wife from potential harm demonstrated his love and commitment to her.

As the story unfolds, the characters navigate the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations, ultimately leading to a dramatic conclusion that highlights the challenges faced by those caught in the web of desire and duty.
