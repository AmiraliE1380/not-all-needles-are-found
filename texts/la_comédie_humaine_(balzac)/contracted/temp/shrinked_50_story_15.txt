On a bright April morning in 1813, a cabriolet arrives at the Tuileries, where an anxious father helps his daughter, Julie, out of the carriage. Captivated by a military review commanded by Napoleon, Julie gains entry thanks to an officer who recognizes her and becomes infatuated with Colonel Victor d’Aiglemont, worrying her father.

By March 1814, Julie and Victor are married but strained. Their loveless marriage takes a toll on Julie's health, despite the joy of their daughter, Helene. Victor neglects her emotional needs, leading to a rift. During this time, Julie meets Lord Grenville, who becomes infatuated with her. Torn between duty and feelings for Grenville, she struggles with guilt.

In late 1820, the Marquise d’Aiglemont retreats to the desolate chateau of Saint-Lange, grappling with grief over her husband's absence. Isolated and skeptical of societal norms, particularly marriage, she finds some distraction in managing her estate, highlighting the complexities of grief and societal pressures on women.

Madame Firmiani hosted a ball where M. Charles de Vandenesse, a young diplomat, prepared to leave Paris for the Congress of Laybach. Although he enjoyed the city's pleasures, he felt disconnected from its superficiality, prioritizing ambition over romance at thirty. At the ball, he met the reclusive Marquise d’Aiglemont, whose beauty and depth captivated him, contrasting sharply with the frivolity around them. Their conversation shifted from light banter to profound discussions about love, leading Vandenesse to request another visit.

During subsequent meetings, Vandenesse learned of the Marquise's tragic past, including a lost love and an unhappy marriage. She revealed that a man had died for her honor, deepening Vandenesse's admiration for her noble spirit. However, the Marquise struggled with her feelings, torn between loyalty to her deceased lover and her growing affection for Vandenesse, who fell deeply in love, experiencing the intensity of first passion. The story explores the complexities of love, loss, and the quest for personal fulfillment amid social expectations.

Years later, the Marquis de Vandenesse hosted a notary at dinner, monopolizing the conversation and frustrating the Marquise, who longed to join her children at the theater. When the children returned, revealing they had left due to a distressing scene, the atmosphere shifted dramatically. Helene's tears hinted at deeper family tragedy, causing the Marquise’s composure to crack. In anger, the Marquis chastised the notary for his insensitivity, leading to a bewildering confrontation.

Set in spring at Versailles, the narrative follows a General, a former Napoleon officer, who enjoys solitude at his country house. On a winter Christmas evening, a stranger, pursued by unknown assailants, arrives seeking refuge. The General offers sanctuary but faces a moral dilemma when gendarmes arrive searching for the fugitive, who is revealed to be a murderer. 

As night deepens, Helene encounters the fugitive, creating a charged connection. Upon discovering this, the General confronts the stranger, demanding he leave. In a climactic moment, Helene declares her intention to leave with the fugitive, asserting her autonomy and rejecting her mother’s authority. The General feels a profound sense of loss as Helene embraces a life defined by passion and danger.

In June 1844, the Marquise d’Aiglemont, around fifty, walks through her daughter’s mansion garden in Paris. After losing her two sons, she has devoted her life to her youngest child, the Comtesse Moïna de Saint-Héreen. This intense affection contrasts with her muted feelings for her deceased children, leading society to view her devotion as both admirable and questionable. Moïna's flirtation with Alfred de Vandenesse troubles the Marquise, who feels powerless to intervene.

As the Marquise reflects on her life, she feels sorrow and resignation, recognizing the toll her emotional struggles have taken on her health. When she tries to discuss Moïna's relationship with Alfred, she faces resistance, deepening her anguish. In despair, the Marquise collapses in the garden, and her lifeless body is carried inside. Initially self-focused, Moïna begins to understand the depth of her mother’s love and sacrifices, ultimately mourning the mother she took for granted. The story emphasizes themes of love, regret, and complex familial relationships.
