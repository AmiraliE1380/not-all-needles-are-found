In Alencon, Chevalier de Valois, a 58-year-old gentleman claiming to be 50, is known for his social skills and anecdotes from Louis XV's reign. Living modestly in a rented room from Madame Lardot, he dreams of marrying a wealthy old maid to elevate his status. 

One spring morning, Suzanne, a young girl working for Madame Lardot, seeks to escape provincial life for Paris. The chevalier encourages her to pursue local bachelor Monsieur du Bousquier, whom she plans to manipulate for financial support, contrasting with the chevalier's genteel approach and highlighting Alencon's social dynamics.

Meanwhile, Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension, struggles to support her son Athanase, who feels stifled in his job at the mayor’s office and is in love with his wealthy cousin, Mademoiselle Cormon. His feelings evolve from financial interest to genuine affection, complicated by societal expectations and rivals.

At a dinner hosted by Cormon, tensions rise as Madame Granson seeks to use Suzanne's situation against du Bousquier. Athanase hopes to express his feelings to Cormon while burdened by his circumstances. This chapter delves into the complexities of love, ambition, and societal pressures, particularly for women like Cormon, who fear being married for their wealth and face isolation as they age.

Penelope, the old brown-bay mare, was a focal point of affection, having served the family for eighteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon, childless and single, projected her maternal instincts onto the mare, which became a source of constant conversation and care among the household members.
