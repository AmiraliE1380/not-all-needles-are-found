In various provinces of France, many Chevaliers de Valois exist, including one in Alencon, the focus of this tale. This chevalier, fifty-eight but claiming to be fifty, is a tall, thin gentleman of modest means, known for his excellent manners and social skills. He dines out daily, sharing witty anecdotes from the reign of Louis XV and the early Revolution. Despite his age, he appears youthful, aided by a vigorous constitution and a notable nose. His legendary appetite is attributed to a "hot liver," justifying his voracious eating habits. 

Living modestly in a rented room from Madame Lardot, the town's washerwoman, his life revolves around social engagements and card games, with a modest annuity supplemented by occasional winnings. Beloved for his kindness and discretion, he harbors a secret ambition to marry a wealthy old maid to elevate his social standing.

One spring morning, a young girl named Suzanne, a work-girl at Madame Lardot's, enters his room with news that could impact both their lives. Ambitious and clever, she seeks to escape her provincial life and considers moving to Paris. The chevalier encourages her ambitions while advising her to pursue a wealthy man, suggesting Monsieur du Bousquier, a local bachelor. 

Suzanne plans to manipulate du Bousquier into supporting her move to Paris, presenting herself as a victim to extract money from him. Caught off guard by her boldness, du Bousquier ultimately agrees to provide funds, revealing his insecurities. As Suzanne leaves, she feels empowered by her ability to navigate relationships, contrasting with the chevalier's genteel approach, highlighting the social dynamics in Alencon where both men and women seek to secure their futures through marriage.

In many second-class prefectures of France, salons serve as gathering places for influential community members outside the elite. These salons, frequented by lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates, wield significant influence, prioritizing land acquisition over luxury and resisting outside influences, particularly from Paris.

Gossip holds considerable weight in this society, and young women are rarely present unless seeking validation. The dominance of one household can incite envy, prompting gatherings at more neutral homes.

For years, the upper classes of Alencon gathered at Mademoiselle Cormon's home, an old maid whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two bachelors. Despite her social standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried, complicated by her desire to marry a nobleman and societal pressures. Over time, her reputation as an old maid became a source of ridicule, despite her charitable nature. Mademoiselle Cormon's life exemplified the struggles of a woman caught between societal expectations and personal desires, leading to profound loneliness and unfulfilled aspirations.

Penelope, the old brown-bay mare, was a focal point of affection, having served the family for eighteen years. Mademoiselle Cormon, childless and single, projected her maternal instincts onto the mare, which became a source of constant conversation and care among the household members.

The four servants operated with mechanical precision, but Mademoiselle Cormon, agitated by her fixed ideas, became increasingly difficult to please. She fixated on trivial matters, such as napkin arrangements or Penelope’s meal timings, leading to petty quarrels that provided her with a sense of purpose. These disputes, while seemingly trivial, were cathartic for her, allowing her to vent frustrations.

Mademoiselle Cormon’s life revolved around her household, her uncle, her health, and her religious duties. Her obsession with well-being led her to frequently consult doctors, humorously attributing minor ailments to her diet, which sparked amusing exchanges with her servants.

Every season, Mademoiselle Cormon visited her estate at Prebaudet, tending to her apple trees and preparing for harvest. Her departures drew attention and speculation from townsfolk, particularly regarding her marital status and her relationship with Monsieur du Bousquier, a rejected suitor.

On the day of her farewell, the town buzzed with gossip, especially concerning the scandal involving Suzanne and du Bousquier. Mademoiselle Cormon, preoccupied with packing, was unaware of the brewing scandal.
