THE VICAR OF TOURS

In the autumn of 1826, Abbé Birotteau, short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend's house and hurried across the deserted Cloister behind Saint‑Gatien cathedral. He hated wet shoes, yet was in high spirits after winning at cards with Madame de Listomère and nourished by the hope of obtaining a long‑desired canonry at the cathedral. That ambition absorbed him so completely he neglected his umbrella.

The Cloister, once home to Chapter dignitaries, had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house long occupied by priests and owned by the devout, discreet old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, who preserved the property through the Restoration by taking priests as boarders. Gamard could take only two lodgers; for twelve years she had sheltered Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau—who had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits—succeeded him.

Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau both bereaved and relieved. He inherited Chapeloud’s tasteful library and furniture, rewards for which he had long envied him. To Birotteau, becoming Gamard’s boarder and securing the canonry represented the comforts and status he craved. Installing himself in the rooms he had coveted, he enjoyed the attentions and comforts he had imagined; but gratitude soon yielded to renewed obsession with the canonry.

One rainy evening he returned to find the house door locked and had to ring repeatedly; his servant Marianne explained Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed other small omissions—no slippers waiting, no fire—and felt slighted. These petty neglects pricked his vanity and suggested intentional disrespect. Innocent and lacking worldly tact, he could not see that these lapses were deliberate. Where Chapeloud had handled Gamard’s temperament with worldly shrewdness, Birotteau’s simplicity left him blind to a quietly mounting hostility.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had built during Birotteau’s eighteen months of tenancy. She prized social recognition and dreamed of cultivating a salon—ambitions frustrated by his indifference and his evenings with friends. As he distanced himself, she felt her plans slipping away and began to nurse a quiet vengeance. Her coolness—locked doors, omitted attentions—was the first sign.

Absorbed by hopes of preferment, Birotteau confronted symptoms, not causes, convincing himself he must somehow have displeased her. The contrast between the two priests is telling: Chapeloud navigated Gamard’s vanity with tact; Birotteau’s guilelessness made him an easy target. Thus small domestic grievances escalate into a deeper conflict—unnoticed slights, unacknowledged ambitions, and provincial social maneuvering—that serves as a prologue to a fuller exploration of pride, naiveté, and intrigue to follow.

The next morning Birotteau woke absorbed in thoughts of a possible canonry, briefly forgetting the previous night’s ill omens. He rang for Marianne, who did not appear; before he could ring again Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected. Troubert noted the absent fire, called for Marianne, and asked after Birotteau’s health and news of the canonry. Unaware that Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him to her house, Birotteau described his efforts to obtain the position.

The two abbés contrasted sharply. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, with a wrinkled face suggestive of sarcasm and contempt; Birotteau was plump, round-faced, and cheerful. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dulled intellect some attributed to subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard; Birotteau’s joviality made him popular. Neither seemed likely to rise far—Troubert because he had tempered his ambitions with a pious life and, at fifty, showed failing health that might still win him vicar-general; Birotteau because amiability kept him comfortable. Abbé Chapeloud quietly opposed Troubert’s promotion, but Troubert treated him respectfully despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

Troubert asked to borrow the Register of Bishops, which Birotteau lent. Carrying heavy books downstairs, they met Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval; she scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room and stung him with her ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness.

Breakfast was tense. Gamard complained of being disturbed the previous night; weighed down by her displeasure, Birotteau could not engage. Her bitter demeanor—shaped by the resentment and social frustration of an unmarried woman—showed in sharp features and abrupt movements. Feeling oppressed, Birotteau left for the garden, only to find Gamard and Troubert watching him, increasing his discomfort. He escaped into church duties and forgot his troubles temporarily; on returning he discovered he had missed dinner—Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting, a petty manipulation protecting her propriety at his expense.

Eight days after the events began, Abbé Birotteau discovered that Mademoiselle Gamard’s hostility—visible since the candlestick-and-clock incident—was a six-month plot against him. Previously oblivious, he now felt suffocated by her malice as she savored revenge while he, absorbed by minor worries, confided in no one.

His life became torment. Anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened his health, so he sought Abbé Troubert’s help. Embarrassed to complain, he described Gamard’s provocations; Troubert listened with restrained amusement and advised understanding her, attributing her behavior to ill health.

Desperate, Birotteau hoped a temporary stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house would ease tensions, not realizing Gamard could exploit his absence. After ten days, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, reported she was alarmed by his prolonged leave and wanted clarity to avoid disputes. Back among friends, Birotteau recounted his troubles and speculation followed. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. An old landowner warned this was only the start. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix brought news that the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed, potentially giving him appointment power.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to consider the canonry rather than endure Gamard. Friends proposed yielding to Gamard and Troubert if it aided his advancement. But the struggle intensified. Caron returned asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence; pressed by friends, he signed.

Despair deepened when he learned he had signed away possessions Gamard claimed as compensation for board differences. Confronted, she coldly cited the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère and found his belongings packed. Mademoiselle Salomon, fearing for his sanity, took him back to the Alouette, where he confided in friends.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed, found it exploitative, and urged legal action. The group debated suing to recover property versus compromising to avoid scandal; they resolved to support Birotteau, though Bourbonne warned many would desert him if the fight grew bitter. Gossip, political divisions, and public suspicion of the clergy complicated matters. An expert appraisal revealed the true value of his goods, showing Gamard’s claims fraudulent. As legal proceedings opened, town gossip intensified the conflict; the stakes were high and the outcome uncertain as Birotteau faced the formidable alliance of Gamard and Troubert determined to preserve their power.

A few days after Birotteau’s quarrel with the old maid began, Baron de Listomere—expecting promotion to corvette captain—received a letter warning he might be retired. Alarmed, he hurried to Paris; the naval minister dismissed his fears, but inquiries found a document confirming the retirement pending the director’s signature.

Desperate, the baron consulted his deputy uncle, who said the minister believed he had sided with Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbé Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle urged reconciliation with Troubert, stressing that good relations with the clergy were vital to the baron’s career. Seeing the danger, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later, fearing political fallout from supporting Birotteau, the family followed Monsieur de Bourbonne’s advice to appease Troubert and distance themselves from Birotteau as a liability. The baroness confronted Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Distressed, he agreed and asked for the return of Chapeloud’s portrait; the baroness promised to negotiate.

Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill; gossip blamed Birotteau’s suit. The baroness sought out Troubert; their meeting was a duel of polite insinuations. She presented Birotteau’s withdrawal; Troubert feigned indifference but was privately pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to surrender the portrait; the baroness accepted it to keep the peace.

Gamard died, leaving her fortune to Troubert, worsening the Listomeres’ plight. At her funeral Troubert delivered a grand eulogy while Birotteau mourned alone. Troubert became Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving Birotteau an annuity, but Troubert’s influence continued to threaten the baron and contest Birotteau’s legacy. Ultimately Birotteau was sent to a distant parish, ill and grieving—Troubert’s ascent exposing the moral costs of ambition and clerical power.
