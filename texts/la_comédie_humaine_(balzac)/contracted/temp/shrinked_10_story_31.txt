DEDICATION

To David, Sculptor:

The permanence of the work on which I inscribe your name—twice made illustrious in this century—is very problematical; whereas you have graven mine in bronze which survives nations—if only in their coins. The day may come when numismatists, discovering amid the ashes of Paris existences perpetuated by you, will wonder at the number of heads crowned in your atelier and endeavor to find in them new dynasties.

To you, this divine privilege; to me, gratitude.

De Balzac.

THE VICAR OF TOURS

I

In the autumn of 1826 Abbé Birotteau, the story’s protagonist, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend’s house. He hurried across the deserted square called “The Cloister,” behind the cathedral of Saint-Gatien in Tours.

Short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, Birotteau hated wet shoes. Yet he was in good spirits—he had won at cards with Madame de Listomère and was buoyed by hope of a long-desired canonry at the cathedral. This ambition consumed him, making him almost oblivious to rain and discomfort.

The Cloister had once housed Chapter dignitaries but, after ecclesiastical confiscations, became the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house, shadowed by the cathedral and long occupied by priests; its owner, the old maid Mademoiselle Gamard, was devout and discreet and had kept the property through the Restoration by taking in priests as boarders.

Birotteau had lived there two years. To be Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and to secure a canonry were his two chief ambitions, representing the comforts and status he craved. His desire for the apartment had become an obsession, pursued with a mix of hope and impatience.

Gamard could take only two lodgers. For twelve years she had housed the Abbé Troubert and the late Abbé Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau took his place; he had long admired the apartment’s furnishings during his visits. Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau bereaved but also relieved to inherit the library and furniture he had coveted.

Chapeloud had gradually enriched the rooms with books and tasteful furniture, inciting Birotteau’s envy and longing. The vicar’s wish for that comfort grew until it bordered on obsession; he sometimes found himself wishing for Chapeloud’s passing to obtain the apartment, though he never schemed about his friend’s will. Chapeloud, aware of these feelings, forgave him and, just before dying, told him, “This time you will certainly get the apartment. I feel it is all over with me now.” After the funeral, Birotteau set to arranging his inherited possessions and felt contented despite being passed over for the canonry.

Mademoiselle Gamard welcomed him as boarder; he enjoyed the attentions and comforts he had long imagined. Yet his gratitude soon shifted back to the canonry, which again obsessed him. Preoccupied, he left his umbrella in the rain. Arriving home, he found the door locked and had to ring several times. His servant Marianne said Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed the absence of usual attentions—no slippers, no fire—and felt slighted.

These small neglects pricked his vanity and made him suspect intentional disrespect. He reflected on possible causes and convinced himself he must have displeased her somehow. His innocence and lack of worldly experience left him vulnerable; he failed to perceive that Gamard’s coolness was growing into subtle persecution. Where Chapeloud had managed the relationship with shrewd tact, Birotteau’s simplicity left him blind to mounting resentment.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had been building over the eighteen months of his tenancy. She prized social recognition and had hoped to create a salon, ambitions frustrated by Birotteau’s indifference. As he resumed former social habits and spent evenings with friends who provided the engagement she desired, he further distanced himself from her. She felt her plans slipping away and began to nurse a quiet vengeance.

The neglect he experienced—the locked door, the omission of small domestic attentions—manifested her growing animosity. Birotteau, naive and self-absorbed in his desire for preferment, remained oblivious to the calculations behind these omissions. He confronted only the symptoms, not their cause, and hoped for clarity in the morning, unaware the reasons for Gamard’s hostility would remain hidden to him.

The contrast between the two priests was clear: Chapeloud had navigated Gamard’s vanity with worldly tact; Birotteau’s simplicity made him an easy target for her petty reprisals. As the vicar sank into discontent, he sought solace in outside company, further alienating Gamard who, thwarted in her ambitions, began to plot revenge. Thus small domestic grievances escalated into a deeper conflict, revealing how unnoticed slights and unacknowledged ambitions can shape human relationships in a provincial setting. The events of these nights are only a prologue to a fuller exploration of the intricacies of pride, naiveté, and social maneuvering that will follow.

The next morning Birotteau awoke preoccupied with thoughts of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the ominous signs from the night before. He rang for Marianne to light a fire; when she did not appear he was about to ring again when Abbé Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected; Troubert noticed the absence of a fire, called for Marianne, then inquired about Birotteau’s health and any news of the canonry. Birotteau, unaware that Troubert resented Madame de Listomère for not inviting him to her home, shared details of his efforts to secure the position.

The two abbés presented a sharp contrast. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, his wrinkled face hinting at sarcasm and contempt; Birotteau was plump, with a round, cheerful face. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dullness some attributed to his subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard, while Birotteau’s jovial nature made him well liked. Neither seemed likely to rise above his present station—Troubert because of restrained ambition and Birotteau because of amiability.

At fifty, Troubert had smoothed his ambitions by adopting a saintly life, and his declining health suggested he might soon be appointed vicar-general. Birotteau’s robust health made him seem likely to endure. Abbé Chapeloud, a respected diocesan figure, had quietly opposed Troubert’s elevation, yet Troubert treated him with respect despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

During the visit Troubert asked to borrow the “Register of Bishops,” which Birotteau gladly supplied. They went downstairs together, each carrying a large book, and were met with Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval. She scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room; he felt the sting of her ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness.

Breakfast was tense. Mademoiselle Gamard complained about being disturbed the previous night, and Birotteau, weighed by her displeasure, struggled to engage. He tried to break the silence with remarks about the coffee and the weather, but met disdain. Her demeanor reflected the bitterness of an unmarried woman who, feeling unfulfilled and rejected, harbored resentment toward others; lives without romantic fulfillment bred in her both self-loathing and an ungracious manner. Her sharp features and abrupt movements mirrored that inner turmoil, and having never married she bore the social stigma of perceived failure.

Feeling the oppressive atmosphere, Birotteau left the dining room with a heavy heart and wandered the garden, only to find Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert watching him, which intensified his discomfort. Distracted by their scrutiny, he sought refuge in church activities and momentarily forgot his troubles. When he returned home he discovered he had missed dinner: Mademoiselle Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting for him. This manipulation exemplified her maliciousness—she took pleasure in undermining Birotteau’s comfort while maintaining an appearance of propriety.

Eight days after the affair began, the relationship between Abbé Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard revealed a six-month plot. Birotteau had been blind to Gamard’s malice, but after the candlestick-and-clock incident he finally recognized her hostility. The discovery devastated him; her presence became suffocating. Gamard, savoring vengeance, had meticulously plotted against the unsuspecting priest, who was too absorbed in petty concerns to confide in friends.

Birotteau’s once-mundane life turned to torment. Anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened his health, and he sought help from Abbé Troubert, though he felt embarrassed to discuss Gamard’s provocations with someone so grave. Troubert listened coolly, tamping down a smile, and suggested understanding Gamard as a health-related problem.

Desperate and bewildered, Birotteau thought a temporary departure from Gamard’s house might calm things. He planned to stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house, not seeing how this would play into Gamard’s hands. After ten days at the Alouette, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, informed him she was anxious about his prolonged absence. Startled, Birotteau insisted he intended to return, but Caron said Gamard wanted clarification to avoid disputes.

Back in the dining room, Birotteau recounted his troubles to friends intrigued by the drama. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. The group parsed Gamard’s motives; an old landowner warned this was only the start of long troubles. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived with news that the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed in his place, a development that would give Troubert appointment power.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to consider the canonry rather than endure Gamard. The group rallied, proposing he let Gamard and Troubert have their way if it might advance him. But tensions rose, and friends summoned Caron again to clarify matters. Caron returned with a document asking Birotteau to relinquish his residence; pressed by friends, he signed.

Birotteau’s despair deepened when he realized the document also cost him his belongings, which Gamard claimed as compensation for board price differences. Confronting her, he found she coldly cited the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère, where Mademoiselle Salomon, fearing for his sanity, took him back to the Alouette to confide in friends.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed and deemed it exploitative. Outraged, he urged legal action. The group debated suing while others favored compromise to avoid scandal. They resolved to support Birotteau against Gamard and Troubert, though Bourbonne warned many would abandon him once the struggle intensified.

Madame de Listomère’s invitation for Birotteau to stay at her house provoked gossip and divided the town; Gamard, feeling slighted, sought to undermine his reputation. Friends began gathering evidence for a lawsuit, complicated by political divisions and anti-clerical sentiment. An expert appraisal of Birotteau’s belongings revealed their true value, suggesting Gamard’s claims were fraudulent.

As the legal battle opened, Birotteau’s position grew precarious, town gossip fueling the conflict. Stakes were high and the outcome uncertain as he faced the formidable alliance of Gamard and Troubert determined to maintain their power.

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere, who expected promotion to captain of a corvette, received a letter warning of possible retirement. Alarmed, he rushed to Paris to see the minister of the Navy, who laughed off his concerns. Further inquiries, however, produced a document confirming his impending retirement, pending the director's signature.

Desperate, the baron sought help from his uncle, a deputy, who learned the minister believed the baron had sided with the Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle advised reconciling with Troubert, stressing the importance of good relations with the clergy for the baron’s career. Realizing the gravity, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later he told his aunt their family was precarious because of their support of Birotteau. They consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who advised retreating gracefully from the dispute with Troubert: appease the vicar while distancing themselves from Birotteau, now a liability.

The baroness approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Distressed, Birotteau agreed, believing himself a burden. He requested the return of a portrait of Chapeloud that belonged to him; the baroness promised to negotiate with Gamard.

Soon Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill, and the town buzzed that Birotteau’s lawsuit had caused her distress. The baroness visited but could not see her, then sought an audience with Troubert, where they engaged in a duel of insinuations and civility, each trying to outmaneuver the other.

The baroness presented Birotteau’s relinquishment document; Troubert affected indifference but was secretly pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to part with Chapeloud’s portrait, which the baroness accepted to preserve peace. Gamard died shortly after, leaving her fortune to Troubert, further complicating matters for the Listomeres.

At Gamard’s funeral, Troubert delivered a grand eulogy that masked his true feelings. Birotteau, guilt-ridden over her death, mourned alone. The Listomeres struggled with the fallout and Troubert’s growing power.

Five months later Troubert was appointed Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity to Birotteau. Troubert continued to manipulate events to his advantage, ensuring Birotteau’s legacy would be contested.

In the end Birotteau was exiled to a distant parish, ill and grieving. Troubert’s ascent, marked by cunning and manipulation, reflected broader struggles within society and the Church. The narrative closes on a meditation about power, individualism, and the moral complexities of human relationships, suggesting the pursuit of personal ambition often exacts a heavy cost from others.
