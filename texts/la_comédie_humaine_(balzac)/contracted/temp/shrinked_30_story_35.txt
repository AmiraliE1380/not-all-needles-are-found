Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; this tale focuses on the one in Alençon. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers mostly did not know one another and had accepted the Bourbon monarchy after the Valois line died out. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose youth in Paris and romantic pursuits had been interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was known for his manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed to be fifty, keeping a youthful air despite age. He had a prominent nose and a pale complexion that reddened on one side — his excuse for the flush, and for a voracious appetite, was a “hot liver.”

Seemingly frail but vigorous, he bore the marks of a lived life. He groomed himself carefully and kept his worn clothes clean, maintaining aristocratic standards. Living modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, he dined out daily on a small annuity. Beloved in Alençon for card-playing and amiability, he mingled with all ranks and was sought for conversation. Yet curiosity shadowed his private life, including persistent rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine. Financially precarious, he managed provincial life with dignity.

One spring morning Suzanne, a young laundry girl, interrupted him with news that might bring pleasure or pain; she feigned indifference about his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne aspired to leave Alençon for Paris, convinced she could better herself. The chevalier, struck by her charm and drive, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised courting Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest.

Du Bousquier, by contrast, bore a dubious reputation: once wealthy and influential, his role as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished him. Returned to Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism contrasted with the chevalier’s elegance; their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne’s ambitions, set in motion an intricate dance of desire, ambition, and social expectation in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension with a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who earned only a meager salary at the mayor’s office. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, secretly loved his cousin Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. What began as a practical hope to secure a better life for himself and his mother grew into genuine affection. Constrained by circumstance, he admired her from afar while his mother, aware of his feelings, saw the match’s advantages despite his lack of fortune.

Suzanne’s arrival altered the dynamics. She felt an immediate bond with Athanase and understood his struggles, yet her ambition pushed her toward deception as a way out of poverty. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes; the community lamented her departure. As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon—encouraged by his mother—their fates diverged: Suzanne headed for Paris to remake her life, while Athanase remained in Alençon, weighed down by unfulfilled dreams and provincial pressures.

In many second‑class French prefectures a salon gathered influential—but not elite—locals. Hosted by prominent residents, it included lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who kept a formal distance from the highest echelons. The salon shaped local life: members prized practicality over luxury, tended land and provincial concerns, resisted Parisian fashions, and used gossip as communal judgment. Young women rarely appeared except to seek validation.

In Alençon the upper classes met at the house of Mademoiselle Rose‑Marie‑Victoire Cormon, an unmarried heiress courted by relatives and two aged bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand‑vicar, she descended from a family long in service to the nobility and—through advantageous marriages—had acquired an aristocratic air despite middle‑class origins. Her house, dating from Henri IV, provoked envy with its prime location and careful upkeep: a neat courtyard, flowerbed, solid architecture, and period decor signaled meticulousness and status. Rooms were arranged for reception—a portrait‑hung salon for cards and local talk, and a dining room of provincial charm. Her circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, rooted in provincial life and rarely traveling.

Wednesdays were notable: dinners drew larger assemblies where modestly dressed women spoke familiarly about gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior—topics reflecting the community’s concerns and values.

Despite wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried. She had hoped to wed a nobleman, but the upheavals of 1788–1815 left few suitable matches; many were too old, too young, or otherwise unsuitable. Fearing suitors sought her fortune, she set subtle tests. As she aged her longing for love intensified—especially for Athanase Granson—exposing her innocence and naivety. Pious and virtuous, she stiffened under social expectation and became a local byword; occasional returning soldiers offered hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned to despair, revealing the limits placed on women by rigid class conventions.

Completing the household were the Abbé de Sponde and loyal servants Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette, who ensured comfort. Jacquelin, short and stout, had served twenty‑two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the family mare Pénélope. He loved Josette, a thirty‑year‑old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if Josette married him; the couple quietly saved, hoping Mademoiselle’s marriage would free them to wed. Childless, Mademoiselle directed maternal affection toward Pénélope. Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to inspect apple trees and mark the harvest with a farewell dinner—an Alençon event—where gossip about suitors, notably the rejected M. du Bousquier, swirled. At one such dinner she awkwardly defended du Bousquier against rumors involving Suzanne; an unexpected remark won laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation in Alençon.

The next day Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prébaudet, unaware an event there would hasten her marriage. The morning after their arrival, Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of the abbé’s friend, wished to stay in Alençon and required hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency and hopeful for marriage, Mademoiselle Cormon returned to town at once despite the rain.

Their sudden arrival provoked gossip; Josette and Jacquelin managed the household while Mademoiselle Cormon prepared for the viscount, fretting over her old-fashioned home and appearance and even sacrificing prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was greeted by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. Distinguished and courteous, he praised her house and the region in a way that made her feel noticed and desirable.

The evening changed when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests arrived. During introductions the viscount casually revealed he was married, devastating Mademoiselle Cormon; she fainted. Du Bousquier carried her to her room amid watching townspeople, and news of the fainting and the viscount’s marriage swept Alençon.

To restore her standing—and with Du Bousquier and the Chevalier now seeking her favour—she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on the condition he consent to a public falsehood about their engagement, which he agreed to. The Chevalier remained unaware; the evening closed with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling mingled triumph and uncertainty as the town buzzed about her supposed engagement.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house while his mother played cards. Having long placed his hopes on Mademoiselle Cormon and schooled in an Imperial fatalism, he sank into indifference; his mother mistook his pallor for work preoccupation.

Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding bitter consolation in its neglected beauty, and withdrew from politics and society. Townspeople, unable to grasp his suffering, lost interest; his mother fretted over imagined enterprises rather than his true despair. Stirred by romantic tales, Suzanne returned to Alençon determined to help him financially, though they never met.

Gossip swirled about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match—motives and legitimacy debated. When the contract was signed and the wedding held, ominous signs—most notably a priest accidentally reading from the De Profundis—set an uneasy tone. The union with du Bousquier lacked celebration; the couple soon retired to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier began modernizing the estate, alarming locals fearful of losing provincial charm.

Consumed by grief, Athanase took his own life, leaving a letter his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than despair. His suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson mourned privately, bearing a grief others barely comprehended; the Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy.

Madame du Bousquier returned and met Madame Granson in a tense, wordless encounter. Madame Granson arranged a discreet burial to avoid scandal, the quiet ceremony reflecting the community’s wish to contain the episode. Over time the marriage became gossip and a cautionary tale: Madame du Bousquier, though outwardly advantaged, proved powerless and unfulfilled—childless and subject to her husband’s domestic tyranny—while political upheavals and the Chevalier’s death closed an era. Suzanne honored the past with a small memento from the Chevalier’s estate, leaving Madame du Bousquier to endure the consequences of constrained choices.
