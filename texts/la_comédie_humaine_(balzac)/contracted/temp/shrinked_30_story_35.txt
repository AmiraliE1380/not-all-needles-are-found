Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; this tale centers on the one in Alençon. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers were mostly strangers to one another and had accepted the Bourbons after the Valois produced no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose Parisian youth and romantic adventures were interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was noted for his manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A social fixture, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he insisted he was fifty, keeping a youthful air. He had a prominent nose and a pale complexion that flushed on one side—he blamed a “hot liver” and a voracious appetite. Though seemingly frail, he remained vigorous and kept his person and worn clothes carefully maintained, upholding aristocratic standards. Modest in means, he rented a room above Madame Lardot’s laundry and lived on a small annuity. Beloved for card-playing and conversation, he mingled across classes, yet curiosity surrounded his private life, including rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine.

One spring morning Suzanne, a young laundress at Madame Lardot’s, interrupted him with news. Ambitious and clever, she aspired to leave Alençon for Paris. The chevalier, struck by her charm, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised her to court Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest.

Du Bousquier, however, had a dubious reputation: once prosperous, his service as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished him. Back in Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism contrasted with the chevalier’s elegance; their rivalry over Cormon, along with Suzanne’s ambitions, set in motion a delicate dance of desire and social expectation.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension with a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old clerk. Athanase secretly loved his cousin Cormon, believing her wealthy; what began as practical hope became genuine affection. Suzanne felt an immediate bond with Athanase and understood his struggles, yet her ambition pushed her toward deception. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes, bound for Paris, while Athanase remained in Alençon, preparing a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon and weighed down by unfulfilled dreams.

In many second-rank French prefectures a local salon gathered influential—but not top-tier—residents. Hosted by prominent locals, it included lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who took part in events while avoiding the highest circles and communicating formally. The salon shaped local affairs: members valued practicality over luxury, focused on land and community matters, resisted Parisian fashions, and used gossip as judgment. Young women rarely appeared except to seek social validation.

In Alençon the circle centered on Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, an unmarried woman whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two aging bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, she descended from a family long in service to the nobility and, through advantageous marriages, had acquired a semblance of aristocracy despite middle-class origins. Her house, dating from Henri IV, occupied a prime site and was kept with meticulous care: a neat courtyard, flowerbed, solid architecture, and period decor. Reception rooms were arranged for social life—a portrait-hung salon for cards and local talk, and a dining room of provincial charm. Her circle numbered about 150, mostly mature provincials who rarely traveled.

Wednesdays were notable: dinners attracted larger assemblies where modestly dressed women spoke familiarly about gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior—topics reflecting communal concerns and values.

Despite wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained single. She had hoped to marry a nobleman, but the upheavals of 1788–1815 left few suitable matches; many were too old, too young, or otherwise unsuitable. Guarding her fortune, she set subtle tests for suitors. As she aged her longing for love intensified, especially for Athanase Granson, exposing her innocence. Pious and virtuous, she tightened under social expectation and became a local byword; returning soldiers briefly raised hopes, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned to despair, revealing the limits placed on women by rigid class conventions.

The household completed the picture: the Abbé de Sponde and servants Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette ensured comfort. Jacquelin, short and stout with a ruddy face, had served twenty-two years and cared for everything from table service to the family mare Pénélope. He loved Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married; the couple quietly saved, hoping Mademoiselle’s marriage would free them to wed. Mariette, cook of fifteen years, prepared local dishes. Routine held while Mademoiselle grew harder to please, obsessing over linens, Pénélope’s meals, minor ailments, and household minutiae.

Each season she visited her Prébaudet estate to inspect apple trees and mark the harvest with a farewell dinner that became an Alençon event. Town gossip about suitors—notably M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected—swirled. At the dinner she tried to engage but often lacked wit; when she clumsily defended du Bousquier against rumors involving Suzanne, a remark unexpectedly won laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation in Alençon.

Mademoiselle Cormon and Josette went to Prebaudet, unaware an event would hasten her marriage. The morning after, Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, his friend's grandson, sought hospitality. Alarmed she returned to town hopeful of marriage.

Gossip swirled as Josette and Jacquelin ran the house and Cormon prepared for the viscount, sacrificing liqueurs to impress. When Monsieur de Troisville arrived she felt attracted when he praised her home. The mood shifted when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests came and the viscount revealed he was married—Cormon fainted and Du Bousquier carried her to her room.

As gossip grew and both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier sought her favor, she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on condition of a sham public engagement; he agreed, leaving her triumphant yet uncertain.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his pain. He first learned of it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house while his mother played cards. Having long clung to hope and schooled in Imperial fatalism, he sank into indifference; his mother noticed his pallor but assumed he was merely preoccupied with work.

After the announcement, Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding bitter solace in its neglected beauty. He withdrew from politics and society and became little noticed by townspeople, who did not grasp the depth of his suffering. His mother fretted, imagining he might be planning some grand project, unaware of the true cause.

Suzanne, stirred by romantic tales, returned to Alençon determined to help Athanase financially, though they never met. The town buzzed with gossip about Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. When the marriage contract was signed and the wedding held, ominous signs—like a priest accidentally reading from the De Profundis—set an uneasy tone.

The union lacked celebration; the couple retreated to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier began modernizing the estate, alarming locals who feared losing provincial charm. Consumed by grief, Athanase ultimately killed himself, leaving a letter his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than despair.

His suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson mourned privately, bearing a grief others barely understood. The Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. To avoid scandal, Madame Granson arranged a discreet burial, the quiet ceremony reflecting the community’s wish to contain the episode.

Over time Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became gossip and a cautionary tale: outwardly advantageous but childless and tyrannical, it left her powerless and unfulfilled. The Chevalier’s death and political change closed an era; Suzanne honored the past with a small memento. Madame du Bousquier’s life thus reflects the complexities of love, duty, and constrained choice.
