Across France there were many Chevaliers de Valois; the one in Alençon is this tale’s focus. Though sharing the name, these chevaliers mostly did not know one another; they accepted the Bourbon monarchy because the Valois line had no male heir. The Alençon chevalier was an elderly, tall, thin gentleman whose youth in Paris and romantic pursuits had been interrupted by the Revolution.

Well regarded among the local aristocracy, he was noted for his manners, wit, and anecdotes from Louis XV’s reign. A regular at social gatherings, his charm and discretion made him a favorite. At fifty-eight he claimed to be fifty, preserving a youthful air despite age. He had a prominent nose and a pale complexion that reddened on one side—his excuse for the flush, and for a voracious appetite, was a “hot liver.” Though seemingly frail, he remained vigorous and passionate; his face bore the marks of a lived life. He groomed himself carefully and kept his clothes clean though worn, maintaining aristocratic standards. Living modestly in a rented room above Madame Lardot’s laundry, he dined out daily on a small annuity. Beloved in Alençon for card-playing and amiability, he was a master of conversation who mingled with all ranks and whose presence was sought. Yet curiosity attended his private life, including persistent rumors of a secret marriage to a woman named Césarine. Financially precarious, he managed provincial life with dignity.

One spring morning Suzanne, a young girl from Madame Lardot’s laundry, interrupted him with news that might bring pleasure or pain; she feigned indifference to his rumored link with Césarine. Ambitious and clever, Suzanne aspired to leave Alençon for Paris, convinced she could better herself. The chevalier, struck by her charm and drive, encouraged her in a paternal tone and advised courting Monsieur du Bousquier, a wealthy bachelor who had shown interest.

Du Bousquier, by contrast, bore a dubious reputation: once wealthy and influential, his role as a purveyor during the Revolution had tarnished him. Returned to Alençon, he hoped to marry Mademoiselle Cormon, a rich old maid, to restore his fortunes. His brusque opportunism contrasts with the chevalier’s elegance; their rivalry over Mademoiselle Cormon, and Suzanne’s ambitions, set in motion an intricate dance of desire, ambition, and societal expectation in a changing world.

Madame Granson, a widow on a modest pension and a small property, struggled to support her son Athanase, who earned only a meager salary at the mayor’s office. Athanase, a pale, introspective twenty-three-year-old, secretly loved his cousin Mademoiselle Cormon, whom he believed to be wealthy. What began as a practical hope to secure a better life for himself and his mother grew into genuine affection. Madame Granson, aware of his feelings, saw the match’s advantages despite Athanase’s lack of fortune and the cousin’s age; she waited patiently while Athanase dreamed of a future together.

The arrival of Suzanne, even poorer but bold, changed the dynamics. She felt an immediate bond with Athanase and understood his struggles, yet her ambition pushed her toward deception as a way out of poverty. After a brief meeting with Madame Granson, Suzanne left town with money and new clothes; the community lamented her departure. As Athanase prepared for a dinner with Mademoiselle Cormon—encouraged by his mother—their fates diverged: Suzanne headed for Paris to remake her life, while Athanase remained in Alençon, weighed down by unfulfilled dreams and provincial pressures.

In many second-class French prefectures a salon gathered influential—but not elite—locals. Hosted by prominent residents, it included lesser nobility, clergy, and magistrates who were invited to events but did not mix with the highest echelons; the latter kept their distance and communicated formally. The salon had real local influence: members prized practicality over luxury, focused on land and local affairs, resisted Parisian fashions, and used gossip as a form of judgment. Young women rarely appeared except to seek validation.

In Alençon the upper classes met at the house of Mademoiselle Rose-Marie-Victoire Cormon, an old maid whose fortune was coveted by relatives and two aging bachelors. Living with her uncle, a former grand-vicar, she came from a family long in service to the nobility and, through advantageous marriages, had acquired a semblance of aristocracy despite middle-class origins. Her house, dating from Henri IV, provoked envy by its prime location and careful upkeep: a neat courtyard, flowerbed, solid architecture, and period decor signaled meticulousness and historical standing. Rooms were arranged for reception: a portrait-hung salon where card games and local conversation took place, and a dining room that exuded provincial charm. Her circle numbered about 150 people, mostly mature, rooted in provincial life and rarely traveling.

Wednesdays were notable: dinners drew larger assemblies where modestly dressed women spoke in a familiar, routine atmosphere about local gossip, land disputes, and judgments of aristocratic behavior—topics reflecting the community’s concerns and values.

Despite wealth and standing, Mademoiselle Cormon remained unmarried. She had hoped to wed a nobleman, but the upheavals of 1788–1815 left few suitable matches; many were too old, too young, or otherwise unsuitable. Fearing marriage for her fortune, she set subtle tests for suitors. As she aged her longing for love intensified—especially for Athanase Granson—exposing her innocence and naivety. Pious and virtuous, she stiffened under social expectation and became a local byword; occasional returning soldiers offered hope, but none met her standards. Her yearning for companionship and motherhood turned to despair, revealing the limits placed on women by rigid class conventions.

Completing the household picture: the Abbé de Sponde and loyal servants Jacquelin, Josette, and Mariette ensured the uncle and niece’s comfort. Jacquelin, a short, stout man of ruddy face, had served twenty-two years, doing everything from waiting at table to grooming the family mare, Pénélope. He loved Josette, a thirty-year-old maid whom Mademoiselle would dismiss if she married him; the couple quietly saved, hoping Mademoiselle’s eventual marriage would free them to wed. Josette, plump and practical, seemed to rule her mistress. Mariette, the cook of fifteen years, prepared local delicacies. Pénélope received Mademoiselle’s maternal affections.

The servants and mare kept a steady routine while Mademoiselle grew harder to please. With an idle mind she fixed on trifles—linens, the timing of Pénélope’s meals—turning small disputes into purpose. Her life centered on the household, her uncle, and religious duties, which magnified everyday minutiae; worried about her health, she obsessed over minor ailments and diet.

Each season she visited her estate at Prébaudet to inspect the apple trees and prepare for harvest, marking the occasion with a farewell dinner that was an Alençon event. Town gossip swirled about suitors, notably M. du Bousquier, whom she had rejected. At the dinner she tried to engage but often lacked wit, amusing sharper guests. When talk turned to du Bousquier’s rumored involvement with Suzanne, her clumsy defense produced a remark that unexpectedly drew laughter and admiration, leaving her to ponder her place amid love, ambition, and social expectation in Alençon.

The next day Mademoiselle Cormon, packed into her old carriole with Josette, set off for Prebaudet, unaware an event would hasten her marriage. The morning after their arrival, Jacquelin brought a letter from her uncle, the Abbé de Sponde: Monsieur de Troisville, a grandson of one of the abbé’s friends, wished to stay in Alençon and needed hospitality. Alarmed by the urgency, she returned to town to help her uncle receive the guest.

Despite the rain she insisted on leaving at once, driven by excitement and the prospect of marriage. Their arrival provoked town gossip; Josette and Jacquelin managed the household while Mademoiselle Cormon prepared for the viscount. Anxious about her old-fashioned home and appearance, she even sacrificed prized liqueurs to make a better impression.

When Monsieur de Troisville arrived he was greeted by the abbé and Mademoiselle Cormon. He looked distinguished and she felt an immediate attraction. Their dinner conversation flowed: the viscount praised her home and the region, making her feel noticed and desirable. The mood changed when the Chevalier de Valois and other guests arrived and the viscount casually mentioned he was married, devastating Mademoiselle Cormon, who fainted. Du Bousquier carried her to her room amid speculation.

As she recovered and accepted that her hopes were gone, gossip spread through Alençon. To restore her standing—and with both Du Bousquier and the Chevalier seeking her favor—she accepted Du Bousquier’s proposal on the condition he consent to a public, misleading engagement. He agreed; the evening ended with Mademoiselle Cormon feeling both triumphant and uncertain as the town buzzed over the new chapter.

The news of Mademoiselle Cormon’s marriage devastated Athanase Granson, though he concealed his turmoil. He first heard it at Chief Justice du Ronceret’s house while his mother played cards. Having long relied on hope and schooled in an Imperial fatalism, he sank into indifference and despair; his mother noticed his pallor but assumed he was merely preoccupied with work.

After the announcement, Athanase wandered the Sarthe countryside, finding bitter solace in its overlooked beauty. He withdrew from politics and society and became of little interest to townspeople, who did not grasp the depth of his suffering. His mother fretted and imagined he might be planning some major undertaking, unaware of the true cause of his withdrawal.

Suzanne, stirred by romantic tales of adventure, returned to Alençon determined to help Athanase financially, though they never met. The town buzzed with gossip about Mademoiselle Cormon’s match, with conflicting reports about its legitimacy. Eventually the marriage contract was signed and the wedding took place amid ominous signs—a priest accidentally read from the De Profundis—setting an uneasy tone.

The union lacked celebration; the couple soon retreated to Prébaudet. Du Bousquier began modernizing the Cormon estate, alarming locals who feared losing their provincial charm. Consumed by grief, Athanase ultimately took his own life, leaving a letter that his mother misread as evidence of newfound independence rather than the despair it revealed.

Athanase’s suicide shocked Alençon. Madame Granson was left to mourn privately, carrying a grief others barely comprehended. The Chevalier de Valois disclosed Athanase’s unrequited love for Mademoiselle Cormon, deepening the town’s sense of tragedy. Madame Granson bore the true weight of her son’s struggles in silence and arranged a discreet burial to avoid scandal; the ceremony was quietly conducted, reflecting the community’s wish to contain the episode.

Over time, Madame du Bousquier’s marriage became gossip and a cautionary tale. Though she initially appeared content, she was powerless and unfulfilled. Du Bousquier, outwardly successful and politically ambitious, proved a domestic tyrant; the marriage brought social advantage but no children, deepening her longing for motherhood. The Chevalier’s embittered plots against du Bousquier widened the rift with local aristocracy and intensified animosity. Public mockery shifted toward sympathy as her hardships emerged.

She lived amid contradictions: performing the duties of a devoted wife while inwardly resenting her husband and mourning the life she might have had. The Chevalier’s death and sweeping political changes marked the end of an era; Suzanne, honoring the past, purchased a small memento from the chevalier’s estate. Madame du Bousquier’s life thus illustrates the complexities of love, duty, and social expectation—pitied and admired, she endures the consequences of choices made within constraining social limits.
