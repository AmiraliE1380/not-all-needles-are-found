In September 1835 Clémentine du Rouvre, daughter of a spendthrift marquis, married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. Settlements from her mother's family secured her income. Adam, without French support, faced Parisian prejudice—Polish nobles scorned and émigré community split—and was mocked.

He bought a house on rue de la Pépinière in 1834 and established himself in Paris.

Clémentine noticed Captain Paz, a discreet factotum who ran the house and stables yet remained unobtrusive. A poor veteran relation of Adam’s, Paz became a close friend; he refused a gift, showing loyalty and equality. As they left, Clémentine's affection for both men grew, suggesting ties that transcended social norms.

Thaddeus lived reclusively in Paris until the Comtesse Clémentine Laginska was seen riding with him in the Bois de Boulogne and insisted he dine with her and Adam. He dressed for the occasion but behaved like a subordinate, answering little while listening to Clémentine; Adam urged him to be livelier. Though reserved outwardly, Thaddeus was painfully aware of his love for Clémentine and struggled to conceal it.

At the Opera he stayed distant; Clémentine felt slighted and Adam excused him as disagreeable. His reserve sprang from unrequited love, deepening after her return from a trip. He took a bittersweet pleasure in being indispensable to their household—knowing it would falter without him even as he suffered. Clémentine, newly confident and intent on her Parisian standing, left little room for him, though once she noticed him while driving home and invited him along—an invitation that flustered him. Their interactions were charged with unspoken feeling: he felt gratitude and longing but kept his distance, finding solace in managing their affairs and reflecting on his pain.

When Clémentine hosted a reception his absence went unnoticed; he retreated into silent devotion. Her ceaseless struggle for attention, rivals, and the strain of maintaining status led her to increasingly neglect Adam. In May, preparing to visit her uncle’s estate, she saw Thaddeus again and insisted he join dinner. He engaged more; her interest grew, and after dinner she confessed dissatisfaction with Adam and a desire for passion. Torn between love and loyalty, Thaddeus nearly confessed but instead invented a romance with Malaga, a circus performer, to deflect. He provided for Malaga and visited her without intimacy; misunderstandings followed until her despairing letter longing for his companionship reminded him that his devotion to Clémentine remained constant amid his sacrifice.

Clementine returned home beautiful. After dinner they discussed Thaddeus and Malaga: Adam feared Thaddeus’s misfortune would compromise him, while Clementine scorned Malaga and Adam defended Thaddeus’s sacrifices.

Paz, who had asked Adam for money, seemed a threat; under Clementine’s contempt he sank into despair yet admired her, tended the household, and fell ill.

After Adam lost heavily gambling he asked Thaddeus to cover the loss by blaming Malaga; Thaddeus reluctantly complied. Clementine, angered by his generosity to Paz, questioned his loyalty, so Thaddeus taught her household management. He nursed Adam through a grave illness; Adam recovered and praised him. Tormented by unrequited love, Thaddeus left for the Caucasus after sending a confession neither Clementine nor Adam grasped. Years later she remembered him as a bittersweet, unfulfilled love.
