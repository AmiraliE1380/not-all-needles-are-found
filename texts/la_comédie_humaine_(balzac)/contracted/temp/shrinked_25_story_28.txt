Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the revolution of 1830? Balzac contends the true Salon has been overwhelmed by quantity. Where a strict selection once showcased the finest works and provoked debate—helping form reputations like Delacroix and Ingres—exhibitions now drown in numbers. Before 1817 only a few paintings were shown; today galleries overflow, individual works lose impact, shows close quickly, and the Salon’s role as arbiter of taste has become a tumultuous bazaar. Great artists are obscured by mediocrity, and talk of unrecognized genius grows when the Academy’s discerning judgment is absent.

Amid this swelling catalogue stands Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lives in a cramped rue de Navarin apartment with a modest studio overlooking Montmartre. Tidy and careful, Grassou is a genre painter who avoids grand historical subjects, painting easel-sized works suited to his limited means.

One December morning the dealer Elie Magus visits. Habitually buying Grassou’s pictures cheaply, Magus proposes a commission: paint portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family, who wish to enter the art world. Their daughter brings a substantial dowry, and Magus hints that marriage might secure Grassou’s future. Startled by the suggestion, Grassou nevertheless sees in the commission a chance to establish himself as a portraitist and improve his status.

Balzac traces Grassou’s past: he studied under masters like Servin and Schinner but never mastered their techniques; his education brought little recognition and frequent ridicule for mediocrity. Gentle and diligent, he failed to stand out. His first Salon attempt—a village-wedding scene in 1819—was rejected; Schinner told him to give up. Persisting, Grassou refined the piece and sold it to Magus for a meager sum, beginning a long career of perseverance.

Grassou’s story embodies many contemporaries’ plight: ambition checked by limited talent, hopes of recognition, and the reality that circumstance and market forces often outweigh true merit.

For several days Pierre Fougeres waited near Elie Magus's shop hoping his painting would attract notice, but it was ignored. After a week it disappeared; when Fougeres asked, Elie said he had sold it and would buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated and missed the ironic tone a more cynical artist might have noticed.

He met his friend Joseph Bridau but, consumed with painting, could not enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft. After two months he produced four paintings. Schinner and Bridau called them derivative and urged literature, but Fougeres retouched the canvases and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs apiece, barely breaking even. He noticed a strange mist over his paintings in the shop but dismissed it.

Over seven years he developed competent, if unremarkable, work. Elie sold the pieces, and Fougeres earned a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends' help, he entered a striking piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Dou—which drew attention even from Charles X. Madame bought the painting and commissioned more.

Fougeres’s success rested more on imitation than innovation, yet his modesty, perseverance, punctuality, and steadiness won supporters. He furnished a studio, completed commissions reliably, and by thirty-seven had produced around two hundred paintings in a style that appealed to the bourgeoisie. Friends often preferred his character to his art and regretted his career choice, though he offered sound advice and maintained a keen appreciation for beauty.

After the July Revolution he kept submitting to the Salon, lived frugally, and managed finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable artistic life and aspired to join the Institute. One day the wealthy Vervelle family came for portraits; the father admired Fougeres’ Legion of Honor cross, the family liked his work, Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie, and they began to see him as a suitable match, blending personal hopes with professional ambitions.

When Fougeres began painting Mademoiselle Virginie, Grassou had become part of the Vervelle household, treating their neat studio as a second home. Near the end of the sitting Joseph Bridau burst in, disheveled, begged five hundred francs to meet creditors, and criticized Grassou’s style, urging acceptance of nature’s imperfections. Bridau left; Madame Vervelle feared he had spoiled the portrait, but Grassou assured her he had improved it.

Flattered, Grassou accepted an invitation to the Vervelles’ country house at Ville d’Avray. They lavished hospitality—admiring grounds, a grand dinner, and fawning praise as if he were celebrated—then proudly showed their gallery, certain it rivaled the king’s. Examining the canvases, Grassou was shocked to find many were his own works, long since sold to the dealer Elie Magus. Vervelle boasted the collection’s value at three hundred thousand francs; Grassou dryly noted he had sold those same pictures for far less. Vervelle promised to double his daughter’s dowry if Grassou could prove the claim. The revelation raised Grassou’s standing; when he later completed their portraits he gifted them, securing their favor.

Today Pierre Grassou is a competent portrait painter, earning about twenty thousand francs a year, living on his wife’s six-thousand-franc dowry, painting bourgeois portraits for five hundred francs apiece, and even holding the Legion of Honor. Yet he remains insecure—sure other artists mock him and critics ignore him—still craving Academy recognition and secretly swapping weaker works in the Ville d’Avray gallery for those by celebrated painters. His mediocrity, tempered by kindness and generosity, renders him complex and oddly sympathetic.
