To souls given to effusiveness, there is a particular twilight hour when day yields to night. Its dimming light and quiet draw artists into reflection on their work and its meaning. In that hour a young painter, finishing a large canvas, falls from his ladder, strikes his head, and loses consciousness.

He is roused by the voices of a young woman and her mother who rushed to his aid. As he recovers he notices the girl’s beauty—the delicate features matching the feminine ideal he had imagined—and the older woman’s refined, marquise-like air. The mother tends him carefully and offers gentle advice on his recovery. The painter thanks them, but the two, naturally reserved, withdraw without pressing for acquaintance.

After they leave, the porter’s wife tells him the visitors are Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, long-time residents of the building. The painter, Hippolyte Schinner, has just moved into the attic studio and begun to earn some recognition. He is determined to secure a better life for his own mother, who sacrificed much for him. Despite modest success, he remains guarded and does not reveal his private affairs easily.

During convalescence Hippolyte replays the encounter, especially the image of the young girl, which lingers. Returning to his studio he is eager to visit them, feeling a kinship born of imagined shared misfortune; this sense of common ground draws him to them.

In their modest home he notices signs of poverty but feels only empathy, not disdain. Mademoiselle Leseigneur greets him with a polite bow; he notes the contrast between their humble surroundings and the elegance they maintain. The room holds faded traces of past luxury—worn finery kept meticulously clean.

Conversation begins awkwardly but soon turns to art, allowing Hippolyte to speak openly about his work and feelings. Madame Leseigneur reveals a cherished portrait of her late husband, a naval officer, is decaying. Hippolyte offers to paint a new portrait, hoping the gesture will deepen their acquaintance. They accept with grace, and he senses the emotional weight behind the request.

While they talk, the young woman, Adelaide, briefly leaves and returns with candles to brighten the room. The mood shifts when a visitor arrives—someone who clearly knows Adelaide. His presence awakens a quick jealousy in Hippolyte, who wants to understand their relationship.

The newcomer is an older gentleman in a coat recalling a bygone fashion; his confident entry suggests nobility mixed with libertinism and hints at a complex past. The exchange between him and the women reveals layers of history and social position, leaving Hippolyte to consider the dynamics at play.

Throughout the evening Hippolyte wrestles with his feelings for Adelaide—torn between admiration and uncertainty. The contrast between their social standings weighs on him, yet he is drawn to her spirit and grace. As night deepens he becomes increasingly aware of the fragile boundary between their worlds and of the possibility of connection amid the shadows of their circumstances.

A remarkable figure appeared beside the “Louis XIV’s light infantry” specimen, a Bonapartist nickname for these remnants of the Monarchy. This lean, aged man was a shadow of the first: coat worn, powdered hair faded, intellect feebler—Rivarol’s quip about Champcenetz applied: a faded reflection.

The silent old man puzzled the painter Chevalier; neither he nor anyone addressed him. His relation to the gallant man—friend, relative, or companion—was unclear, and his presence raised questions about any past link to the gallant’s deceased wife without offering answers.

One of the least dilapidated men approached Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat beside her; the other bowed and took a nearby seat. Adelaide leaned over the old gentleman’s chair in a pose recalling a famous painting, though his familiarity seemed to annoy her.

“Are you sulky with me?” he asked, casting a shrewd glance at Schinner the painter, as if gauging his status.

“This is our neighbor,” Madame de Rouville said, introducing Hippolyte, the celebrated painter. The old man noted Hippolyte’s red ribbon of distinction and contrasted it with his own hard-earned honors.

Hippolyte, captivated by Adelaide’s beauty, became lost in admiration and momentarily forgot the squalor around them. Absorbed in contemplation, he answered briefly. The old gentleman lamented his losing streak at cards; Adelaide offered help, but he insisted she stay where he could see her.

When the game ended, the gentleman voiced frustration at his losses. The silent figure echoed the hour, prompting Hippolyte to take his leave, still entranced by budding feelings.

The next day Hippolyte longed to see Adelaide again. He delayed until afternoon before visiting Madame de Rouville, awkwardly requesting the Baron’s portrait. Adelaide, alone in the drawing-room, asked him to fetch it, revealing her modesty. Their shared glance spoke volumes; Hippolyte took the portrait, promising to return it soon.

In the following days their relationship blossomed with laughter and shared thoughts. Schinner, eager to learn piquet, often lost, while the unspoken bond between him and Adelaide deepened. They became inseparable; Adelaide’s affection grew and Hippolyte’s jealousy flared when the old Count showed familiarity with her.

Adelaide worried that Hippolyte’s social life harmed his health and expected more from him. Their love flourished without grand gestures, relying on mutual trust and delicate exchanges. Within two months their lives were intertwined: Hippolyte visited daily, their connection deepened, and the old gentleman’s visits faded as Hippolyte took his place at the card table and shared in the losses.

Still, Hippolyte could not shake concern about Madame de Rouville’s finances. He spent two months on the Baron’s portrait, considering it among his finest works, but the Baroness’s silence about payment puzzled him.

One day Adelaide visited his studio and was awestruck by the portrait; their moment brimmed with unspoken feeling. Overwhelmed, Hippolyte expressed his desire to marry her, but their moment was interrupted by the arrival of the old vice-admiral, who praised the portrait and hinted at financial support for both commissions.

That evening Hippolyte noticed Madame de Rouville’s unusual excitement during cards, stirring suspicions about her gambling habits and the nature of her dealings. After a night of turmoil he discovered his purse missing and, in his pain, suspected Adelaide and her mother of theft.

Despite heartbreak he clung to hope for a misunderstanding. The next day a friend mentioned rumors surrounding Adelaide and her mother, unsettling him further. Torn between love and doubt, he resolved to confront his feelings and visit Madame de Rouville.

Seeing Adelaide, he was struck by her pallor and sadness; their conversation revealed mutual longing, and he felt ashamed of his earlier suspicions. The evening turned tender as they played cards, but his fears reemerged when he noticed Adelaide’s secretive glances at her mother.

During the game Adelaide discreetly placed a new purse before Hippolyte, a thoughtful gesture to replace the one he had lost. Overwhelmed, he realized the depth of their connection and impulsively declared his intention to marry her.

Just as they began to discuss their future, the old vice-admiral arrived with news of a promotion and expressed support for the young couple. He praised the portrait and offered assistance, dispelling lingering doubts. The evening concluded with a renewed sense of unity as the families came together.

This tale of love, trust, and misunderstanding follows characters navigating the complexities of their relationships and, through gestures of faith and reconciliation, finding solace in shared bonds and the promise of a brighter future.
