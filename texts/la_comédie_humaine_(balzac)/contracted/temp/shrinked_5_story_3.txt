In the enchanting twilight, artist Hippolyte Schinner, finishing a painting, fell from his ladder and lost consciousness. He awoke to the soothing voices of Adelaide and her mother, immediately captivated by Adelaide’s beauty. Newly settled in a modest Paris studio, Hippolyte had gained fame and financial stability through his art but stayed humble and devoted to his mother.

After recovering, he visited Adelaide and Madame Leseigneur in their small apartment, struck by the contrast between their cramped living conditions and Adelaide’s grace. The warm atmosphere suggested better days; Madame Leseigneur welcomed him, and a closer bond formed when he offered to paint Adelaide’s portrait. She told him of her past: a late husband, a naval officer killed in battle, and Hippolyte felt compelled to help.

As his admiration grew, their conversation was interrupted by two men, including an older gentleman, adding intrigue. His worries about their finances intensified when he discovered his missing purse—fifteen louis d’or—shattered his idealized view of them.

Despite this heartbreak, he remained drawn to Adelaide. Rumors about Madame de Rouville surfaced, prompting Hippolyte to probe further. On a later visit he found Adelaide distressed; during a card game she discreetly returned his lost purse, rekindling hope. The arrival of a vice‑admiral, an old friend of Madame de Rouville, defended her against gossip and shifted the mood. The story culminates with Hippolyte’s heartfelt proposal to Adelaide, joyfully accepted, as they embrace intertwined fates amid lingering doubts.
