For souls to whom effusiveness comes easily there is a delightful hour between day and night; twilight throws softened lights and reflections on every object and favors a dreamy mood that weds itself to the play of light and shade. The prevailing silence makes it especially dear to artists, who grow contemplative, step back from pictures they can no longer work on, and pass judgment on them, rapt by meanings that then flash on the inner eye of genius. He who has never stood pensive by a friend in such a poetic hour can hardly grasp its inexpressible soothingness. Favored by the clear-obscure, material skill used to produce illusion entirely disappears. If the work is a picture, the figures seem to speak and walk; shade is shadow, light is day; flesh breathes, eyes move, blood flows, and fabrics change sheen. Imagination heightens every realistic detail and sees only the work’s beauties. At that hour illusion reigns despotically; perhaps it wakens at nightfall. Is not illusion a sort of night to the mind, peopled by dreams? It bears the soul aloft to a world of fancies where the artist forgets the real world—yesterday, tomorrow, the future—everything, its miseries and its goods alike.

At this magic hour a young painter of talent, who saw in art nothing but Art itself, was perched on a step-ladder working on a large, now nearly finished painting. Criticising himself, admiring himself honestly, floating on the current of his thoughts, he lost himself in one of those meditative moods which elevate and soothe the soul. His reverie lasted long. Night fell. Whether he meant to come down, or made some ill-judged movement believing himself on the floor—the cause escaped his memory—he fell, struck his head on a footstool, lost consciousness, and lay motionless for a time of which he knew not the length.

A sweet voice roused him from his stunned state. When he opened his eyes a bright light made him close them again; through the mist he heard two women whispering and felt two timid hands supporting his head. He soon recovered; by the light of an old-fashioned Argand lamp he saw the most charming girl’s face he had ever seen, a head that for an artist realized the ideal beauty he had formed for himself. Her features resembled the refined delicacy of Prudhon’s school and the poetic sentiment of Girodet: fresh temples, regular eyebrows, pure outline, virginal innocence stamped on every feature. Her figure was slight and graceful. Her dress, simple and neat, revealed neither wealth nor penury.

As he came to, the painter expressed admiration, fumbling thanks. A handkerchief pressed to his forehead; amid the studio smell he recognized the strong odor of ether, applied to revive him. He saw an old woman, looking like a marquise of the old school, who held the lamp and advised the young girl.

“Monsieur,” said the younger woman in answer to a few questions the painter asked while still vague from the shock, “my mother and I heard the noise of your fall and fancied we heard a groan. The silence after the crash alarmed us, and we hurried up. Finding the key in the latch, we entered and found you lying motionless. My mother fetched what was needed to bathe your head and revive you. You have cut your forehead—there. Do you feel it?”

“Yes, I do now,” he replied.

“Oh, it will be nothing,” said the old mother. “Happily your head rested against this lay-figure.”

“I feel infinitely better,” replied the painter. “I need nothing further but a hackney cab to take me home. The porter’s wife will go for one.”

He tried to repeat his thanks, but the elder lady interrupted him at every sentence: “Tomorrow, monsieur, pray be careful to put on leeches, or to be bled, and drink a few cups of something healing. A fall may be dangerous.”

The young girl stole glances at the painter and the pictures. Her expression revealed perfect propriety; her curiosity seemed rather an absence of mind, and her eyes spoke the natural interest women feel in suffering. The two strangers seemed to forget the painter’s works in his mishap. When he reassured them they left, looking at him with anxious kindness free from insistence or familiarity, without indiscreet questions or invitations. Their noble and simple manners made a quiet impression on the painter; later, recalling details of the incident, he was greatly struck by them.

On the landing the old lady gently observed, “Adelaide, you left the door open.”

“That was to hear me fall,” said the painter with a grateful smile.

“You came down just now, mother,” replied the young girl with a blush.

“Would you like us to accompany you downstairs? The stairs are dark.”

“No, thank you, madame; I am much better.”

“Hold tightly by the rail.”

The two women remained to light him, listening to his steps.

To appreciate the interest this scene aroused, it must be said the painter had only a few days earlier established his studio in the attics of this house in the darker, muddier part of the Rue de Suresnes, almost opposite the Church of the Madeleine and near his rooms in the Rue des Champs-Elysees. Fame had recently made him one of the artists most dear to his country; he was beginning to feel free from want, and, as he put it, was enjoying his last privations. Instead of working in cheaper studios near the city gates he had spared himself the long walk and the loss of time now more valuable than before.

No man would have inspired greater interest than Hippolyte Schinner if he would ever consent to make acquaintance; but he did not lightly entrust the secrets of his life. He was the idol of a necessitous mother who had raised him amid severe privations. Mademoiselle Schinner, daughter of an Alsatian farmer, had never married. Her gentle soul had been cruelly crushed long ago by a rich man who showed no delicacy in his love affairs. The day she lost her illusions was an age of reflection, religious thought, and resignation. She refused the betrayer’s alms, renounced the world, and made a glory of her shame. She devoted herself to maternal love, sought in it her joys instead of social pleasures, worked, and saved a treasure for her son. In after years one day amply repaid her for those sacrifices.

At the last exhibition her son received the Cross of the Legion of Honor. Newspapers hailed an unknown genius, artists acknowledged Schinner as a master, and dealers covered his canvases with gold pieces. At twenty-five Hippolyte, to whom his mother had transmitted her woman’s soul, understood his standing. Eager to restore to his mother the pleasures society had denied her, he lived for her, hoping that fame and fortune would one day make her happy, rich, respected, and surrounded by people of mark. Schinner chose friends among the honorable and distinguished; fastidious in selection, he desired to raise still further a position his talent had placed high. His solitary work from boyhood had left him beautiful beliefs that grace early days. His adolescent soul was not closed to any of the shy emotions that make a young man an apart being, full of poetry and virginal hopes, puerile to the worldly but deep for their singleness.

He had gentle, polite manners that spoke to the soul and fascinated even those who did not understand them. His voice, from the heart, stirred noble sentiments and testified to his modesty by ingenuous tones. Those who saw him felt an attraction of moral nature they could not analyze; they might call it galvanism or some other current, and try to express it in formulas.

These details help explain why, in the absence of the porter whom he had sent to the end of the Rue de la Madeleine for a coach, Hippolyte did not question the porter’s wife closely about the two women who had tended him. He replied to her natural inquiries about his accident and the fourth-floor tenants but could not stop her chatter, led by lodge policy and gossip.

“Ah,” she said, “they were, no doubt, Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, who have lived here four years. We do not know exactly what these ladies do; in the morning, till noon, an old woman who is half deaf comes in to help them; in the evening, two or three old gentlemen with ribbon loops, like you, monsieur, come to see them and often stay very late. One comes in a carriage with servants; they say he has sixty thousand francs a year. However, they are very quiet tenants, as you are, monsieur; and economical! they live on nothing, and pay as soon as a letter is brought. It is queer, monsieur, the mother’s name is not the same as the daughter’s. Ah, but when they go for a walk in the Tuileries, mademoiselle is very smart, and she never goes out without being followed by a lot of young men; but she shuts the door in their face, and she is quite right. The proprietor would never allow—”

The coach came; Hippolyte heard no more and went home. His mother dressed his wound and would not allow him to return to the studio the next day. After advice and treatments, Hippolyte remained at home three days. During this enforced idleness his fancy recalled, bit by bit, the scene of his fainting fit. The young girl’s profile returned clearly against the darkness of his inward vision; he saw the mother’s faded features again, felt Adelaide’s touch. He remembered some gesture whose grace memory enhanced; then an attitude, a tone of voice rising like objects from deep waters to the surface.

When he could resume work he went early to his studio; but the real cause of his haste was the visit he felt entitled to make to his neighbors. At the moment passion throws off its swaddling clothes, inexplicable pleasures are felt; some readers will understand why he mounted the fourth-floor stairs slowly, and why his heart throbbed as he neared the humble brown door of Mademoiselle Leseigneur. He fancied similarity between their positions, imagined misfortunes of birth akin to his own. He lingered at the studio to think of them, dined there, then about seven o’clock went to call.

No painter of manners has sufficiently told the curious privacy of certain Parisian existences: fresh elegant toilettes and brilliant women whose homes betray doubtful comfort. If my picture is sometimes bold, blame the truth; the appearance of his neighbors’ rooms greatly influenced Hippolyte Schinner’s feelings and hopes.

The house belonged to one of those proprietors who abhor repairs and decoration, who regard being a Paris homeowner as business. Between miser and usurer, such men are allergic to moving a cupboard or opening an air-hole; when chimneys fall they deprive themselves of theatre nights “on account of repairs.” Hippolyte, who had seen a comical scene about decorative repairs, was not surprised by dark greasy paint, oily stains, and other disagreeable woodwork signs. These stigmata of poverty are not without a sort of poetry to an artist.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door. Recognizing the young artist she bowed and, with Parisian tact and pride, turned to shut a glass partition through which Hippolyte might have seen linen hung over patent ironing stoves, an old camp-bed, charcoal and irons, a filter, household crockery—utensils of a small household. Muslin curtains screened this capharnaum, lighted by windows facing a neighboring yard.

With an artist’s quick eye Hippolyte saw the uses, furniture, and general condition of this cut-in-half first room. The more honorable half, serving as ante-room and dining-room, was hung with old salmon-rose paper with a flock border patched with wafers. Prints of the battles of Alexander, in gilding-rubbed frames, were arranged on the walls. In the middle stood a massive, old-fashioned mahogany table worn at the edges. A small stove with a thin pipe stood before the chimney, whose hearth was blocked by a cupboard. Chairs showed remains of former splendor—carved mahogany, red morocco seats, gilt nails—scarred like a veteran.

This room was a museum of objects that show both luxury and penury: a splendidly finished telescope hung over a discolored glass on the chimney. Between chimney and partition was a wretched painted sideboard pretending to be mahogany. Slippery red quarries, shabby rugs, and all the furniture shone with hard cleanliness that accentuated oldness. An indescribable odor mixed exhalations from the lumber-room, dining-room vapors, and stair smells, though the window was partly open. Street air fluttered dusty curtains, carefully drawn to hide a window bay where former tenants left ornamental additions—a sort of domestic fresco.

Adelaide hastened to open the inner room door and announced the painter with obvious pleasure. Hippolyte, who had earlier seen similar poverty in his mother’s home, noted these signs with an impression vivid as earliest memories and entered with sympathy rather than scorn.

“Well, monsieur, I hope you no longer feel the effects of your fall,” said the old lady, rising from an antique armchair and offering a seat.

“No, madame. I have come to thank you for the kind care you gave me, and above all mademoiselle, who heard me fall.”

As he spoke—words stamped with the exquisite awkwardness of first love—Hippolyte looked at the young girl. Adelaide lit the Argand lamp, replacing a tallow candle in a large copper candlestick, then placed the lamp on the chimney shelf and sat a little behind the painter to look at him at her ease while appearing interested in the lamp. The flame sputtered in the damp chimney. Seeing the large mirror on the chimney-piece, Hippolyte fixed his eyes on it to admire Adelaide; her stratagem only embarrassed them both.

While talking with Madame Leseigneur—Hippolyte called her so—he examined the room unobtrusively. Egyptian figures on iron fire-dogs were scarcely visible under cinders; two brands struggled before a sham log. An old Aubusson carpet, faded and mended, left the tiled floor cold. Walls were hung with reddish paper imitating figured silk. Opposite the windows he saw a crack and the paper outline of double doors closing a recess where Madame Leseigneur likely slept, poorly disguised by a sofa. Above a mahogany chest of drawers hung a portrait of an officer of rank, so dim he could not judge it well; from what he saw it must have been painted in China. Window-curtains of red silk were as faded as the furniture in red-and-yellow worsted work. On the chest’s marble top was a costly malachite tray with a dozen magnificently painted coffee cups, perhaps Sevres. On the chimney shelf stood an Empire clock—a warrior driving four horses on a chariot whose wheel bore the hours. Tall candlesticks held smoke-yellowed tapers; porcelain vases crowned with dusty artificial flowers stood in the corners.

In the middle of the room a card-table with new packs suggested a painful dilemma: either these two women were perfectly honest or they lived by intrigue and gambling. Yet in looking at Adelaide, so pure-minded, Schinner could not but believe in her innocence and attribute the furniture’s incoherence to honorable causes.

“My dear,” said the old lady to the young one, “I am cold; make a little fire, and give me my shawl.”

Adelaide fetched a cashmere shawl—once costly, now faded and darned—and wrapped her mother in it. She then brought a bundle of small wood from the lumber-room and gallantly threw it on the fire to revive it.

It is hard to reproduce the ensuing conversation. Guided by the tact born of early hardship, Hippolyte did not remark on his neighbors’ poverty; any direct question would be indiscreet. The simplest pity can offend, so his thoughts and words diverged. The two ladies began by talking of painting; women easily guess the embarrassment of a first call and supply devices to end it. Questioning him about his art emboldened him to talk, and the chat’s light nothings led Hippolyte to reflections that revealed his habits and mind. Trouble had prematurely faded the old lady’s face; only prominent features remained, an outline suggesting shrewdness with grace in the eyes—an expression of the old Court that can hint either at artifice or refined delicacy.

A woman’s face hides mystery: the line between frankness and duplicity, intrigue and heart, is inscrutable. A penetrating eye might discern differences; such appreciation lies in intuition alone. The old lady’s face, like her room, made it hard to tell whether the squalor covered vice or highest virtue. But at Schinner’s age the heart’s first impulse is to believe in goodness. As he studied Adelaide’s noble, almost haughty brow and soulful eyes, he breathed the modest fragrance of virtue. He seized an opportunity to discuss portraits and thus examine the faded pastel whose chalk had fallen away.

“You are attached to that picture for the sake of the likeness, no doubt, mesdames, for the drawing is dreadful?” he said, looking at Adelaide.

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” replied the mother, her voice agitated.

She gazed at the formless sketch with the absorption memories of happiness bring; in her face were traces of perennial regret. The painter interpreted her attitude as the mark of a life once happy. He then sat down beside her.

“Madame,” he said, “in a very short time the colors of that pastel will disappear. The portrait will only survive in your memory. Will you allow me to reproduce the likeness on canvas? It will be more permanently recorded. Grant me, as a neighborly favor, the pleasure of doing you this service. There are times when an artist is glad of a respite from greater undertakings by doing work of less lofty pretension; it will be a recreation for me.”

The old lady flushed at the offer, and Adelaide shot a glance of deep feeling. Hippolyte wanted some tie to his neighbors, to gain a right to mingle in their life. His offer appealed to the heart’s affections, gratified his pride as an artist, and could not wound the ladies. Madame Leseigneur accepted, without eagerness or reluctance, with the self-possession of a noble soul, aware of the character of bonds formed by such an obligation.

“I fancy,” said the painter, “that the uniform is that of a naval officer.”

“Yes,” she said, “that of a captain in command. Monsieur de Rouville—my husband—died at Batavia from a wound received in a fight with an English ship off the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal, but he defended his ship bravely, held out till nightfall and got away. When I returned to France Bonaparte was not yet in power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, quite lately, I was sternly informed that if the Baron de Rouville had emigrated I should not have lost him; that by this time he would have been a rear-admiral; finally, his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I took this step, urged by friends, only for Adelaide’s sake. I have always hated holding out my hand as a beggar in the name of a grief that deprives a woman of voice and strength. I do not like this money valuation for blood irreparably spilt—”

“Dear mother, this subject always does you harm.”

The Baronne Leseigneur bowed and was silent.

“Monsieur,” said the young girl, “I had supposed a painter’s work was generally fairly quiet?”

Schinner colored, remembering the noise he had made. Adelaide rose at the sound of a carriage stopping at the door. She went into her own room and returned with a pair of tall gilt candlesticks with partly burnt wax candles, which she quickly lit. Without waiting for the bell she opened the outer door where she set the lamp down. The sound of a kiss given and received touched Hippolyte’s heart. His impatience to see the man who treated Adelaide familiarly was not immediately gratified; the newcomers conversed in undertone with the young girl.

At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose costumes and countenances need a long tale. The first, about sixty, wore one of those coats fashioned for Louis XVIII., solving the sartorial compromise of the time: neither civil nor military, it might pass for either. Fleurs-de-lis embroidered the lapels and gilt buttons bore the same. Shoulder straps begged for useless epaulettes; a buttonhole blossomed with many colored ribbons. He probably always carried his hat—three-cornered, with a gold cord—for his powdered hair showed no traces of pressure. He might be taken for fifty and seemed robust. His frank, loyal emigre expression hinted also at libertine habits, the reckless gallantry of the Musketeers. His gestures and manner showed no intention of renouncing royalism, religion, or love affairs.

A really fantastic figure came in behind this specimen of “Louis XIV.’s light infantry”—a nickname the Bonapartists gave these venerable survivors of the Monarchy. To do it justice it ought to be the principal object in the picture, and it is but an accessory. Imagine a lean, dry man, dressed like the former, but seeming only his reflection or his shadow. The coat, new on the first, on the second was old; the powder in his hair looked less white, the gold of the fleurs-de-lis less bright, the shoulder straps more hopeless and dog’s-eared; his intellect seemed feebler, his life nearer the fatal term. In short, he realized Rivarol’s witticism on Champcenetz, “He is the moonlight of me.” He was simply his double, a paler and poorer double, the difference between the first and last impressions of a lithograph.

This speechless old man was a mystery to the painter, and always remained a mystery. The Chevalier, for he was a Chevalier, did not speak; nobody spoke to him. Was he a friend, a poor relation, a man who followed at the old gallant’s heels as a lady companion does at an old lady’s? Did he fill a place midway between a dog, a parrot, and a friend? Had he saved his patron’s fortune, or only his life? Was he the Trim to another Captain Toby? Elsewhere, as at the Baronne de Rouville’s, he always piqued curiosity without satisfying it. Who, after the Restoration, could remember the attachment which, before the Revolution, had bound this man to his friend’s wife, dead now these twenty year?

The leader, least dilapidated of these wrecks, came gallantly up to Madame de Rouville, kissed her hand, and sat down by her. The other bowed and placed himself not far from his model, at a distance represented by two chairs. Adelaide came behind the old gentleman’s armchair and leaned her elbows on the back, unconsciously imitating the attitude given to Dido’s sister by Guerin in his famous picture.

Though the gentleman’s familiarity was that of a father, his freedom seemed at the moment to annoy the young girl.

“What, are you sulky with me?” he said.

Then he shot at Schinner one of those side-looks full of shrewdness and cunning, diplomatic looks, whose expression betrays the discreet uneasiness, the polite curiosity of well-bred people, and seems to ask, when they see a stranger, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbor,” said the old lady, pointing to Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter, whose name must be known to you in spite of your indifference to the arts.”

The old man saw his friend’s mischievous intent in suppressing the name, and bowed to the young man.

“Certainly,” said he. “I heard a great deal about his pictures at the last Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, observing the artist’s red ribbon. “That distinction, which we must earn at the cost of our blood and long service, you win in your youth; but all glory is of the same kindred,” he said, laying his hand on his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured a few words of acknowledgment, and was silent again, satisfied to admire with growing enthusiasm the beautiful girl’s head that charmed him so much. He was soon lost in contemplation, completely forgetting the extreme misery of the dwelling. To him Adelaide’s face stood out against a luminous atmosphere. He replied briefly to the questions addressed to him, which, thanks to a singular faculty, he heard, as if the soul sometimes has a double consciousness. Who has not known what it is to sit lost in sad or delicious meditation, listening within, while attending to a conversation or to reading? An admirable duality which often helps us to tolerate a bore! Hope, prolific and smiling, poured out before him a thousand visions of happiness; he refused to analyze the little incidents of the evening.

After a short time he perceived that the old lady and her daughter were playing cards with the old gentleman. As to the satellite, faithful to his function as a shadow, he stood behind his friend’s chair watching his game, answering the player’s mute inquiries by little approving nods, repeating the other’s gestures.

“Du Halga, I always lose,” said the gentleman.

“You discard badly,” replied the Baronne de Rouville.

“For three months now I have never won a single game,” said he.

“Have you the aces?” asked the old lady.

“Yes, one more to mark,” said he.

“Shall I come and advise you?” said Adelaide.

“No, no. Stay where I can see you. By Gad, it would be losing too much not to have you to look at!”

At last the game was over. The gentleman pulled out his purse, and, throwing two louis d’or on the table, not without temper—

“Forty francs,” he exclaimed, “the exact sum.--Deuce take it! It is eleven o’clock.”

“It is eleven o’clock,” repeated the silent figure, looking at the painter.

The young man, hearing these words rather more distinctly than the others, thought it time to retire. Coming back to ordinary ideas, he found a few commonplace remarks to make, took leave of the Baroness, her daughter, and the two strangers, and went away, wholly possessed by the first raptures of true love, without attempting to analyze the little incidents of the evening.

On the morrow the young painter felt the most ardent desire to see Adelaide once more. If he had followed the call of his passion, he would have gone to his neighbor’s door at six in the morning, when he went to his studio. However, he still was reasonable enough to wait till the afternoon. As soon as he thought he could present himself to Madame de Rouville, he went downstairs, blushing like a girl, and shyly asked Mademoiselle Leseigneur, who came to let him in, to let him have the portrait of the Baron.

“But come in,” said Adelaide, who had no doubt heard him come down from the studio.

The painter followed, bashful and out of countenance, not knowing what to say; happiness had so dulled his wit. To see Adelaide, to hear the rustle of her skirt, after longing for a whole morning to be near her, to have started up a hundred times—“I will go down now”—and not gone; this was to him life so rich that such sensations, too greatly prolonged, would have worn out his spirit. The heart gives extraordinary value to mere nothings. What joy to a traveler to treasure a blade of grass if he has risked his life to pluck it! It is the same with the trifles of love.

The old lady was not in the drawing-room. When the young girl found herself there alone with the painter, she brought a chair to stand on to take down the picture; but perceiving she could not unhook it without setting her foot on the chest of drawers, she turned to Hippolyte, and said with a blush:

“I am not tall enough. Will you get it down?”

A feeling of modesty, betrayed in her expression and tone, was the real motive of her request; and the young man, understanding this, gave her one of those glances of intelligence which are the sweetest language of love. Seeing that the painter had read her soul, Adelaide cast down her eyes with the instinct of reserve which is the secret of a maiden’s heart. Hippolyte, finding nothing to say, and feeling almost timid, took down the picture, examined it gravely, carried it to the light of the window, and then went away, saying only, “I will return it soon.”

During this brief moment they both underwent one of those storms of agitation whose effects in the soul may be compared to a stone flung into a deep lake. The most delightful waves of thought rise and follow each other, repeated and aimless, tossing the heart like the circular ripples which long fret the waters, starting from the point where the stone fell.

Hippolyte returned to the studio bearing the portrait. His easel was ready with a fresh canvas, his palette set, brushes cleaned, the light carefully chosen. Till dinner he worked with the ardor artists throw into their whims. He went again that evening to the Baronne de Rouville’s, and remained from nine till eleven. Excepting different topics of conversation, this evening was like the last. The two old men arrived at the same hour, the same game of piquet was played, the same speeches made by the players, the sum lost by Adelaide’s friend was not less considerable than on the previous evening; only Hippolyte, a little bolder, ventured to chat with the young girl.

A week passed thus, and in it the painter’s feelings and Adelaide’s underwent the slow and delightful transformations that bring two souls to perfect understanding. Every day the look with which the girl welcomed her friend grew more intimate, more confiding, gayer, more open; her voice and manner became more eager and familiar. They laughed and talked, telling each other their thoughts, speaking of themselves with the simplicity of two children who have made friends in a day, as if they had met constantly for three years. Schinner wished to be taught piquet. Being ignorant and a novice, he made blunder after blunder, and like the old man, he lost almost every game. Without speaking a word of love the lovers knew they were all in all to one another. Hippolyte enjoyed exerting his power over his gentle little friend, and many concessions were made to him by Adelaide, who, timid and devoted, was deceived by assumed fits of temper that the least skilled lover and the most guileless girl can affect; they constantly play off such caprices as spoilt children abuse the power they owe to a mother’s affection. Thus all familiarity between the girl and the old Count was soon put a stop to. She understood the painter’s melancholy, the thoughts hidden in the furrows on his brow, from the abrupt tone of the few words he spoke when the old man unceremoniously kissed Adelaide’s hands or throat.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur, on her part, soon expected her lover to give an account of all his actions; she was so unhappy and restless when Hippolyte did not come, she scolded him so effectually for his absence that the painter had to give up seeing his other friends, and now went nowhere. Adelaide allowed the natural jealousy of women to be perceived when she heard that sometimes at eleven o’clock, on quitting the house, the painter still had visits to pay, and was seen in the most brilliant drawing-rooms of Paris. This mode of life, she assured him, was bad for his health; then, with the intense conviction to which the accent, emphasis and look of one we love lend weight, she asserted that a man who was obliged to expend his time and charms on several women at once could not be the object of very warm affection. Thus the painter was led, as much by the tyranny of his passion as by the exactions of a girl in love, to live exclusively in the little apartment where everything attracted him.

And never was there a purer or more ardent love. On both sides the same trustfulness, the same delicacy, gave their passion increase without the aid of those sacrifices by which many persons try to prove their affection. Between these two there was such a constant interchange of sweet emotion that they knew not which gave or received the most.

A spontaneous affinity made the union of their souls close. The progress of this true feeling was so rapid that two months after the accident which brought the painter to know Adelaide, their lives were one life. From early morning the young girl, hearing footsteps overhead, could say to herself, “He is there.” When Hippolyte went home to his mother at dinner he never failed to look in on his neighbors, and in the evening he flew there at the accustomed hour with lover’s punctuality. Thus the most tyrannical or most ambitious woman in love could find no fault with the young painter. And Adelaide tasted unmixed and unbounded happiness as she saw the realization of the ideal of which, at her age, it is natural to dream.

The old gentleman now came more rarely; Hippolyte, who had been jealous, had taken his place at the green table, and shared his constant ill-luck at cards. And sometimes, in the midst of his happiness, thinking of Madame de Rouville’s disastrous position—for he had had more than one proof of her extreme poverty—an importunate thought would haunt him. Several times he had said to himself as he went home, “Strange! twenty francs every evening?” and he dared not confess to himself his odious suspicions.

He spent two months over the portrait, and when it was finished, varnished, and framed, he looked upon it as one of his best works. Madame la Baronne de Rouville had never spoken of it again. Was this from indifference or pride? The painter would not allow himself to account for this silence. He joyfully plotted with Adelaide to hang the picture in its place when Madame de Rouville should be out. So one day, during the walk her mother usually took in the Tuileries, Adelaide for the first time went up to Hippolyte’s studio, on the pretext of seeing the portrait in the good light it had been painted in. She stood speechless and motionless, but in ecstatic contemplation, in which all a woman’s feelings were merged. For are they not all comprehended in boundless admiration for the man she loves? When the painter, uneasy at her silence, leaned forward to look at her, she held out her hand, unable to speak a word; two tears fell from her eyes. Hippolyte took her hand and covered it with kisses; for a minute they looked at each other in silence, both longing to confess their love, and not daring. The painter kept her hand in his, the same glow, the same throb, told them that their hearts both beat wildly. The young girl, too greatly agitated, gently drew away and said, with utmost simplicity:

“You will make my mother very happy.”

“What, only your mother?” he asked.

“Oh, I am too happy.”

The painter bent his head and remained silent, frightened at the vehemence of the feelings her tones stirred in his heart. Then, both understanding the perils of the situation, they went downstairs and hung up the picture in its place. Hippolyte dined for the first time with the Baroness, who, greatly overcome and drowned in tears, embraced him.

In the evening the old emigre, the Baron de Rouville’s old comrade, paid the ladies a visit to announce he had just been promoted to vice-admiral. His voyages by land over Germany and Russia had been counted as naval campaigns. On seeing the portrait he cordially shook the painter’s hand, and exclaimed, “By Gad! though my old hulk does not deserve to be perpetuated, I would gladly give five hundred pistoles to see myself as like as that is to my dear old Rouville.”

At this hint the Baroness looked at her young friend and smiled, while her face lighted up with sudden gratitude. Hippolyte suspected the old admiral wished to offer him the price of both portraits while paying for his own. His pride as an artist, no less than his jealousy, took offence at the thought, and he replied:

“Monsieur, if I were a portrait-painter I should not have done this one.”

The admiral bit his lip, and sat down to cards.

The painter remained near Adelaide, who proposed a dozen hands of piquet, to which he agreed. As he played he observed in Madame de Rouville an excitement over her game which surprised him. Never before had the old Baroness manifested so ardent a desire to win, or so keen a joy in fingering the old gentleman’s gold pieces. During the evening evil suspicions troubled Hippolyte’s happiness, and filled him with distrust. Could it be that Madame de Rouville lived by gambling? Was she playing at this moment to pay off some debt, or under pressure of necessity? Perhaps she had not paid her rent. The old man seemed shrewd enough not to allow his money to be taken with impunity. What interest attracted him to this poverty-stricken house, he who was rich? Why, when he had formerly been so familiar with Adelaide, had he given up the rights he had acquired, and which were perhaps his due?

These involuntary reflections prompted him to watch the old man and the Baroness, whose meaning looks and certain sidelong glances cast at Adelaide displeased him. “Am I being duped?” was Hippolyte’s last idea—horrible, scathing, for he believed it just enough to be tortured by it. He determined to stay after the departure of the two old men, to confirm or dissipate his suspicions. He drew out his purse to pay Adelaide; but carried away by his poignant thoughts, he laid it on the table, falling into a brief reverie; then, ashamed of his silence, he rose, answered some commonplace question from Madame de Rouville, and went close up to her to examine the withered features while he talked.

He went away, racked by a thousand doubts. He had gone down but a few steps when he turned back to fetch the forgotten purse.

“I left my purse here!” he said to the young girl.

“No,” she said, reddening.

“I thought it was there,” and he pointed to the card-table. Not finding it, in his shame for Adelaide and the Baroness, he looked at them with a blank amazement that made them laugh, turned pale, felt his waistcoat, and said, “I must have made a mistake. I have it somewhere no doubt.”

In one end of the purse there were fifteen louis d’or, and in the other some small change. The theft was so flagrant, and denied with such effrontery, that Hippolyte no longer felt doubt as to his neighbors’ morals. He stood still on the stairs, and got down with some difficulty; his knees shook, he felt dizzy, in a cold sweat, he shivered, and found himself unable to walk, struggling with the agonizing shock caused by the destruction of all his hopes. At this moment he recalled a number of observations, trifling in themselves, but which corroborated his frightful suspicions, and which, by proving the certainty of this last incident, opened his eyes as to the character and life of these two women.

Had they really waited till the portrait was given them before robbing him of his purse? In such a combination the theft was more odious. The painter recollected that for the last two or three evenings Adelaide, while seeming to examine with a girl’s curiosity the particular stitch of the worn silk netting, was probably counting the coins in the purse, while making some light jests, quite innocent in appearance, but no doubt with the object of watching for a moment when the sum was worth stealing.

“The old admiral has perhaps good reasons for not marrying Adelaide, and so the Baroness has tried----”

But he checked himself, not finishing his thought, which was contradicted by a just reflection, “If the Baroness hopes to get me to marry her daughter,” thought he, “they would not have robbed me.”

Then, clinging to his illusions and the love that already had taken such deep root, he tried to find a justification in some accident. “The purse must have fallen on the floor,” said he to himself, “or I left it lying on my chair. Or perhaps I have it about me—I am so absent-minded!” He searched himself with hurried movements, but did not find the ill-starred purse. His memory cruelly retraced the fatal truth, minute by minute. He distinctly saw the purse lying on the green cloth; but then, doubtful no longer, he excused Adelaide, telling himself that persons in misfortune should not be hastily condemned. There was, of course, some secret behind this apparently degrading action. He would not admit that that proud and noble face was a lie.

At the same time the wretched rooms rose before him, denuded of the poetry of love which beautifies everything; he saw them dirty and faded, emblematic of an inner life devoid of honor, idle and vicious. Are not our feelings written, as it were, on the things about us?

Next morning he rose, not having slept. The heartache, that terrible malady of the soul, had made rapid inroads. To lose the bliss we dreamed of, to renounce our whole future, is a keener pang than that caused by the loss of known happiness; for is not Hope better than Memory? The thoughts into which our spirit is suddenly plunged are like a shoreless sea. It is a frightful death. Are not our feelings the most glorious part of our life? This partial death which, in certain delicate or powerful natures, leads to the terrible ruin produced by disenchantment, by hopes and passions betrayed. Thus it was with the young painter. He went out at a very early hour to walk under the fresh shade of the Tuileries, absorbed in his thoughts, forgetting everything.

There by chance he met one of his most intimate friends, a school-fellow and studio-mate, with whom he had lived on better terms than with a brother.

“Why, Hippolyte, what ails you?” asked Francois Souchet, the young sculptor who had just won the first prize, and was soon to set out for Italy.

“I am most unhappy,” replied Hippolyte gravely.

“Nothing but a love affair can cause you grief. Money, glory, respect—you lack nothing.”

Insensibly the painter was led into confidences, and confessed his love. The moment he mentioned the Rue de Suresnes, and a young girl living on the fourth floor, “Stop, stop,” cried Souchet lightly. “A little girl I see every morning at the Church of the Assumption, and with whom I have a flirtation. But, my dear fellow, we all know her. The mother is a Baroness. Do you really believe in a Baroness living up four flights of stairs? Brrr! Why, you are a relic of the golden age! We see the old mother here, in this avenue, every day; her face, her appearance, tell everything. What, have you not known her for what she is by the way she holds her bag?”

The two friends walked up and down for some time, and several young men who knew Souchet or Schinner joined them. The painter’s adventure, which the sculptor regarded as unimportant, was repeated by him.

“So he, too, has seen that young lady!” said Souchet.

And then there were comments, laughter, innocent mockery, full of the liveliness familiar to artists, but which pained Hippolyte frightfully. A certain native reticence made him uncomfortable as he saw his heart’s secret so carelessly handled, his passion torn to tatters, a young and unknown girl, whose life seemed modest, the victim of condemnation, right or wrong, but pronounced with such reckless indifference. He pretended to be moved by a spirit of contradiction, asking each for proofs of his assertions, and their jests began again.

“But, my dear boy, have you seen the Baroness’ shawl?” asked Souchet.

“Have you ever followed the girl when she patters off to church in the morning?” said Joseph Bridau, a young dauber in Gros’ studio.

“Oh, the mother has among other virtues a certain gray gown, which I regard as typical,” said Bixiou, the caricaturist.

“Listen, Hippolyte,” the sculptor went on. “Come here at about four o’clock, and just study the walk of both mother and daughter. If after that you still have doubts! well, no one can ever make anything of you; you would be capable of marrying your porter’s daughter.”

Torn by conflicting feelings, the painter parted from his friends. It seemed to him that Adelaide and her mother must be superior to these accusations, and at the bottom of his heart he was filled with remorse for having suspected the purity of this beautiful and simple girl. He went to his studio, passing the door of the rooms where Adelaide was, and conscious of a pain at his heart no man can misapprehend. He loved Mademoiselle de Rouville so passionately that, in spite of the theft of the purse, he still worshiped her. His love was that of the Chevalier des Grieux admiring his mistress, and holding her as pure, even on the cart which carries such lost creatures to prison. “Why should not my love keep her the purest of women? Why abandon her to evil and to vice without holding out a rescuing hand to her?”

The idea of this mission pleased him. Love makes a gain of everything. Nothing tempts a young man more than to play the part of a good genius to a woman. There is something inexplicably romantic in such an enterprise which appeals to a highly-strung soul. Is it not the utmost stretch of devotion under the loftiest and most engaging aspect? Is there not something grand in the thought that we love enough still to love on when the love of others dwindles and dies?

Hippolyte sat down in his studio, gazed at his picture without doing anything to it, seeing the figures through tears that swelled in his eyes, holding his brush in his hand, going up to the canvas as if to soften an effect, but not touching it. Night fell, and he was still in this attitude. Roused by the darkness, he went downstairs, met the old admiral on the way, looked darkly at him as he bowed, and fled.

He had intended going in to see the ladies, but the sight of Adelaide’s protector froze his heart and dispelled his purpose. For the hundredth time he wondered what interest could bring this old prodigal, with his eighty thousand francs a year, to this fourth story, where he lost about forty francs every evening; and he thought he could guess what it was.

The next days Hippolyte threw himself into his work, and to try to conquer his passion by the swift rush of ideas and the ardor of composition. He half succeeded. Study consoled him, though it could not smother the memories of so many tender hours spent with Adelaide.

One evening, as he left his studio, he saw the door of the ladies’ rooms half open. Somebody was standing in the recess of the window, and the position of the door and the staircase made it impossible that the painter should pass without seeing Adelaide. He bowed coldly, with a glance of supreme indifference; but judging of the girl’s suffering by his own, he felt an inward shudder as he reflected on the bitterness that look and coldness must produce in a loving heart. To crown the most delightful feast which ever brought joy to two pure souls, by eight days of disdain, of the deepest contempt!--A frightful conclusion. And perhaps the purse had been found, perhaps Adelaide had looked for her friend every evening.

This simple and natural idea filled the lover with fresh remorse; he asked whether the proofs of attachment given him by the young girl, the delightful talks, full of love, did not deserve at least inquiry; were not worthy of some justification. Ashamed of having resisted his heart for a whole week, and feeling almost a criminal in this mental struggle, he called that evening on Madame de Rouville.

All his suspicions vanished at the sight of the young girl, who had grown pale and thin.

“Good heavens! what is the matter?” he asked her, after greeting the Baroness.

Adelaide made no reply, but gave him a look of deep melancholy, a sad, dejected look which pained him.

“You have, no doubt, been working hard,” said the old lady. “You are altered. We are the cause of your seclusion. That portrait had delayed some pictures essential to your reputation.”

Hippolyte was glad to find so good an excuse for his rudeness.

“Yes,” he said, “I have been very busy, but I have been suffering—”

At these words Adelaide raised her head, looked at her lover, and her anxious eyes had no hint of reproach.

“You must have thought us quite indifferent to any good or ill that may befall you?” said the old lady.

“I was wrong,” he replied. “Still, there are forms of pain we know not how to confide to any one, even to a friendship of older date than that with which you honor me.”

“The sincerity and strength of friendship are not to be measured by time. I have seen old friends who had not a tear to bestow on misfortune,” said the Baroness, nodding sadly.

“But you—what ails you?” the young man asked Adelaide.

“Oh, nothing,” replied the Baroness. “Adelaide has sat up late for some nights to finish some little piece of woman’s work, and would not listen to me when I told her that a day more or less did not matter—”

Hippolyte was not listening. As he looked at these two noble, calm faces, he blushed for his suspicions, and ascribed the loss of his purse to some unknown accident.

This was a delicious evening to him, and perhaps to her too. There are some secrets which young souls understand so well. Adelaide could read Hippolyte’s thoughts. Though he could not confess his misdeeds, the painter knew them, and he had come back to his mistress more in love and more affectionate, trying thus to purchase her tacit forgiveness. Adelaide was enjoying such perfect, such sweet happiness, that she did not think she had paid too dear for it with all the grief that had so cruelly crushed her soul. Yet this true concord of hearts, this understanding so full of magic charm, was disturbed by a little speech of Madame de Rouville’s.

“Let us have our little game,” she said, “for my old friend Kergarouet will not let me off.”

These words revived all the young painter’s fears; he colored as he looked at Adelaide’s mother, but he saw nothing in her countenance but the expression of the frankest good-nature; no double meaning marred its charm; its keenness was not perfidious, its humor seemed kindly, and no trace of remorse disturbed its equanimity.

He sat down to the card-table. Adelaide took his side, saying he did not know piquet, and needed a partner.

All through the game Madame de Rouville and her daughter exchanged looks of intelligence, which alarmed Hippolyte all the more because he was winning; but at last a final hand left the lovers in the old lady’s debt.

To feel for some money in his pocket the painter took his hands off the table, and then saw before him a purse Adelaide had slipped in front of him without his noticing it; the poor child had the old one in her hand, and, to keep her countenance, was looking into it for the money to pay her mother. The blood rushed to Hippolyte’s heart with such force that he was near fainting.

The new purse, substituted for his own, and which contained his fifteen gold louis, was worked with gilt beads. The rings and tassels bore witness to Adelaide’s good taste, and she had no doubt spent all her little hoard in ornamenting this pretty piece of work. It was an impossible more delicate way to say the painter’s gift could only be repaid by some proof of affection.

Hippolyte, overcome with happiness, turned to look at Adelaide and her mother, and saw that they trembled with pleasure and delight at their little trick. He felt mean, sordid, a fool; he longed to punish himself, to rend his heart. A few tears rose to his eyes; by an irresistible impulse he sprang up, clasped Adelaide in his arms, pressed her to his heart, and stole a kiss; then with the simple heartiness of an artist, “I ask for her for my wife!” he exclaimed, looking at the Baroness.

Adelaide looked at him with half-wrathful eyes, and Madame de Rouville, somewhat astonished, was considering her reply, when the scene was interrupted by a ring at the bell. The old vice-admiral came in, followed by his shadow, and Madame Schinner. Having guessed the cause of the grief her son vainly endeavored to conceal, Hippolyte’s mother had made inquiries among her friends concerning Adelaide. Very justly alarmed by the calumnies which weighed on the young girl, unknown to the Comte de Kergarouet, whose name she learned from the porter’s wife, she went to report them to the vice-admiral; and he, in his rage, declared “he would crop all the scoundrels’ ears for them.”

Then, prompted by his wrath, he went on to explain to Madame Schinner the secret of his losing intentionally at cards, because the Baronne’s pride left him none but these ingenious means of assisting her.

When Madame Schinner had paid her respects to Madame de Rouville, the Baroness looked at the Comte de Kergarouet, at the Chevalier du Halga—the friend of the departed Comtesse de Kergarouet—at Hippolyte, and Adelaide, and said, with the grace that comes from the heart, “So we are a family party this evening.”


PARIS, May 1832
