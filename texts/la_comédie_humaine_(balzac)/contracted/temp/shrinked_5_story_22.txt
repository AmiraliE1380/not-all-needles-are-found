MODESTE MIGNON

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To a Polish Lady.

Daughter of an enslaved land, angel through love, witch through fancy, child by faith, aged by experience, man in brain, woman in heart, giant by hope, mother through sorrows, poet in thy dreams,—to thee belongs this book, in which thy love, thy fancy, thy experience, thy sorrow, thy hope, thy dreams, are the warp through which is shot a woof less brilliant than the poesy of thy soul, whose expression, when it shines upon thy countenance, is, to those who love thee, what the characters of a lost language are to scholars.

De Balzac.

MODESTE MIGNON

CHAPTER I. THE CHALET

In October 1829, Monsieur Simon Babylas Latournelle, a notary, walked from Havre to Ingouville with his son Exupère, his wife, and their head clerk, Jean Butscha. At a bend in the road Latournelle lowered his voice to instruct Exupère in a small stratagem involving Mademoiselle Modeste Mignon and her family: Exupère was to pay respects to the Mignons and then, after a short walk, return to whisper to Monsieur Dumay that “the young man has come,” signaling romantic interest.

Exupère was about to leave for Paris to study law, so Latournelle involved him in the scheme. Butscha, curious, asked if Modeste had a lover, but Madame Latournelle hushed him. Madame Latournelle, pretentious and devoted to social standing, was a comic figure; Latournelle himself was trustworthy though odd, notable for his green goggles. Butscha, a hunchbacked clerk who adored Modeste, completed the household circle.

Ingouville, a picturesque suburb of Havre, was where wealthy merchants built homes to escape the city. The Chalet, a charming cottage with a garden and a beloved hot-house, was the home of Dumay, the Mignon family’s cashier. He held a lease that annoyed the new owner, Monsieur Vilquin. Dumay, fiercely loyal to the Mignons, refused Vilquin’s lucrative offer to vacate, determined to protect Modeste from suitors.

CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE

The Latournelles arrived at the Chalet and found Gobenheim, a young banker, already present. Pale and serious, Gobenheim visited the Mignons more from self-interest than affection. Family dynamics were tense; Dumay prepared secretly for a confrontation with any of Modeste’s suitors.

Blind and dignified, Madame Mignon was the household’s center. Modeste embroidered beside her as the atmosphere brimmed with unspoken anxiety. Dumay’s preparations promised an evening meant to test Modeste’s affections.

CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES

Jean François Bernard Dumay, a former soldier, had become devoted cashier to the Mignon family after misfortunes. His loyalty to Colonel Mignon, exiled to Siberia, was unwavering. Dumay’s wife, who had lost her children, poured maternal instincts into Modeste.

The family had fallen after a financial disaster, and Dumay and Latournelle worked to settle debts while Modeste and her mother retreated to the Chalet. Madame Mignon, though blind, sensed a change in Modeste and believed her daughter might be in love. Dumay, protective and suspicious, vowed to guard Modeste’s honor.

CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY

The Mignons’ downfall began with business failure and Colonel Mignon’s departure. Dumay and Latournelle labored to settle obligations; the women of the family lived quietly at the Chalet. Madame Mignon’s health weakened under grief, and Modeste’s innocence troubled Dumay, who took his protective role seriously. Their life was marked by routine and the absence of male suitors, increasing anxiety about Modeste’s future.

CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED

As Modeste’s beauty and charm drew speculation, her mother’s fears grew. The household women became vigilant, suspecting Modeste might harbor romantic feelings. Dumay, ever watchful, resolved that no man would approach her without his knowledge.

Despite guardians’ scrutiny, Modeste’s heart brimmed with dreams of love and adventure, shaped by her readings and her sister’s tragic fate. Tension mounted as the women conspired to shelter Modeste from the dangers of passion.

CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE

Modeste’s imagination flourished through literature; she dreamed of a romantic life far removed from her reality, longing to be the muse of a great poet. Her fantasies drew on her sister’s tragedy and her yearning for something beyond provincial life.

She wrote to the poet Canalis, pouring out her heart in a letter that sought a connection to lift her from obscurity. The anticipation of his response filled her with hope, though she understood the risks of addressing a man of his stature.

CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL

Canalis received Modeste’s letter with both amusement and condescension. He perceived her innocence but feared misunderstanding. His reply mixed flattery with caution, warning her against romanticizing a poet’s life.

Reading his response, Modeste felt disappointment and a renewed resolve. The reality of Canalis’s temperament clashed with her idealized vision, prompting her to prove him wrong. She resolved to press the correspondence further, driven to assert her identity and desires.

Through the exchange with Canalis, Modeste navigates the complexities of love, ambition, and self-discovery, setting the scene for a dramatic exploration of her character and the social expectations that surround her.

CHAPTER VIII. BLADE TO BLADE

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Monsieur, you are a great poet and an honest man. After your frankness to a young girl on the brink of despair, can you answer me truthfully? Would your response to my letter have been the same if you had known that Mademoiselle O. d’Este M. has six million francs and seeks a fool for a husband?

Consider this for a moment. Be as honest with me as you are with yourself; fear nothing. I am wiser than my years; nothing frank can harm you in my eyes. If you share your thoughts, I will respond to your first letter.

Having admired your sublime talent, I must also pay tribute to your integrity, which compels me to remain,

Your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

Ernest de La Brière held this letter and walked long on the boulevards, his mind in turmoil. Most young Parisians would dismiss such an appeal as a flirtation; but for a man of honor like him, it summoned the judges within: Honor, Truth, and Justice.

Truth accused him of hypocrisy; Justice defended the girl’s rebellion against materialism; Honor demanded sincerity. La Brière delayed his reply, then six days later sent:

Mademoiselle, you are not a D’Este. The name is a disguise. Do I owe a truth to someone who lies about herself? Are you of an illustrious, noble, or middle-class family? Ethics are constant but obligations vary by rank. A daughter of Este worth six million can boldly confess an interest in poetry; a daughter of the people would be ridiculed. What class are you? Answer frankly, and I will respond to your question.

Modeste replied:

To Monsieur de Canalis, you grow more sensible, my dear poet. My father is a count. Our family’s chief glory was a cardinal, and I am the last of our line. We are equal to the Canalis. You will excuse me from sending our arms. Please answer me as truthfully as I have answered you. I await your response to know if I can sign myself as I do now,

Your servant, O. d’Este M.

La Brière smiled at her mischief yet admired her frankness. Four years as a private secretary in Paris had not left him naive about intrigue. He booked a seat in the mail-coach for Havre and wrote that his reply would be delayed by his duties.

He obtained a note from the post-office director to ensure secrecy and watched as Francoise Cochet collected letters, following her discreetly to Ingouville. There he glimpsed Modeste at the Chalet window. Struck by her beauty, he inquired about the estate and learned it belonged to Monsieur Vilquin, the richest shipping merchant in Havre. The Vilquins, he discovered, were enormously wealthy; one daughter married to M. Althor. Rumors of a d’Herouville family visit and a possible marriage between a young duke and a Mademoiselle Vilquin reached him.

Having seen enough to dream, La Brière continued the correspondence.

We must pity French girls who try to conceal a romance; civilization tracks every detail. Chance had spared Modeste from discovery—La Brière regretted the reconnaissance—yet Parisian curiosity seldom rests. He wrote a letter shaped by conscience and passion; Modeste read it by her window on a fine summer day.

Mademoiselle, without hypocrisy, yes: had I known of your wealth I would have acted differently. Men confuse happiness with its means; money becomes a religion. In the face of a prize, a young man pursues it. Had I known you were rich I might have wooed you merely from social impulse and flattery, perhaps losing my esteem for you and yours for me. A husband won that way might despise or distrust you; poets complain where strong men forgive. That honesty compels me to say so.

Your letter forced me to reflect deeply—on you and on myself. You stirred in me many base thoughts, from which something generous emerged, and I salute you. I wished to know who you are. I went to Havre, followed Francoise to Ingouville; you are as beautiful as a poet’s dream. I blush at such spying and stopped. You have modified my opinion: you are not merely a woman, you are a poetic Ideal. Exceptional souls may transcend the laws that bind the crowd. But if your letter sprang from lofty reveries and a desire for a friendship beyond ordinary relations, then you are an exception; yet you may have done too much or not enough.

Accept my thanks for forcing me to sound my heart. You corrected my false idea that marriage should be for fortune. I swore to make my own fortune and not choose a wife from cupidity. I have also reproached myself for the curiosity you provoked. You are not worth six million; such a fortune could not be hidden in Havre. I now fix in my mind the rule: seek a partner made by God; do not marry a dunce. If I were rich and you poor, I would lay my heart and fortune at your feet; you seem loyal and rich in soul.

This letter comforted Modeste; she altered her plan of concealment, giving Francoise envelopes of her own so letters could be slipped back into the box and resolved to meet the postman herself.

The feelings La Brière’s reply stirred in Modeste were manifold: delight at having touched a noble soul, joy at sensing that hearts of worth might still match genius, and triumph at following intuition. Her life was now animated; the wires of her cage seemed broken. “Oh, father!” she cried to the horizon; “come back and make us rich and happy.”

Ernest de La Brière received another letter five days later that revealed more than any analysis.

CHAPTER IX. THE POWER OF THE UNSEEN

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My friend, I call you that; you have delighted me. I would not change your image derived from your first letter—oh, may it not be the last! Who but a poet could have so delicately excused and understood a young girl on the brink?

I admit with joy I am not the Vilquin nor the withered d’Herouville; my ancestors are respectable on both sides and my precautions are taken—I will remain veiled. As to my person and “belongings,” be easy: I am at least as beautiful as the girl whose light you saw, and not poverty-stricken. I have seen the humiliating comedy of heiresses being hunted; make no attempt to find me. I am free as air yet watched—by myself and by resolute people who would not hesitate to stab a man who threatened my retreat. I do not say this to quicken your courage; I merely warn you.

Now to your sermon. When I read your distrust, I thought Muses may have led you astray. You are safe with me: I love poetry but do not practice hypocritical airs. Should I ever bid you come, I promise no old maid or poor wretch will appear. I regret that you came to Havre; you have lowered the charm of the secret meeting I dreamed—God alone knew the treasure I reserved for a man noble, trusting, perspicacious enough to come. You have spoiled it, but I forgive you; Paris breeds a man in every poet.

Do you think me a gullible child? When your first letter reached me I did not fling it like a pebble; my net was carefully cast by a fisherman expecting a miraculous catch. You have my approval in what you say about family. The man who pleases me will have my heart with my parents’ consent; I will neither grieve nor surprise them. I am guarded by devotion and disdain for calculating motives. I worship the ideal without being romantic; but I recognize social truths.

For now we must be two friends. Why seek an unseen friend? Your person may be unknown, but your mind and heart please me; an infinity of thoughts in me need a man of genius to share them. I do not want my poem wasted; I want it known to you as to God. A true comrade’s confidences are precious; accept these leaflets of a young girl’s thoughts. Later, if our souls match, a white-haired servant may guide you to whatever dwelling fate holds for us—but not now. Come to me when you are unhappy; I have balms for your ills. I am twenty but feel fifty; I have known through another the horrors and delights of love and have no illusions but a religion and beliefs. Whoever I marry—if I choose him—will find me faithful and constant. I will not follow my husband blindly; I will be the divinity of his hearth. But I must test and choose the man who will be life’s companion.

Your letter was precious. Yes, it held those prosaic, necessary truths for family life. Act as an honest man, think as a poet, love as women love—that is what I long for. Adieu, my friend. I am poor at present; that is why I hide. I have read your verses in the Revue with delight now that I know your inner loftiness. Will it grieve you that a young girl prays for you, that you are her solitary thought? Send me the counterpart of these pages; your confidences—if full and true—will make me happy.

O. d’Este M.

La Brière, surprised to find he had lingered over her letter, wondered if he was in love already. She thought him the great poet; could he continue the deception? He was fascinated by the charm of the unseen—an obscurity that stirs fancy. In a life like Canalis’s such a romance might be fleeting; in La Brière’s emptier life, it became an obsession. He answered with a pretentiously studied letter where passion began to leak from pique.

Mademoiselle,—Is it loyal of you to enthrone yourself in a poor poet’s heart with an intention of abandoning him if he is not what you wish? I was short-sighted in seeking your letter; a man can become enamored of a mysterious unknown combining fearlessness and originality. Who would not wish to know you after your confidence? You leave little of yourself but much of your charm. I offer humble remonstrances.

Do you believe letters—those momentary expressions—can replace the knowledge gained by daily intercourse? Man is dual: an invisible life of heart and a material life. Letters may suffice for the first but cannot reconcile both. A solitary, educated, chaste homage is one of those celestial flowers that console literary men. I thank you as one equal might thank another.

But after this exchange, what next? I am neither Byron nor a colossus. I might have vanity, discordant character, and ambition; I need a career. I have sufficient means and the external bearing of an ordinary man. I can be neither martyr nor hero. Your project is a plagiarism, perhaps like the German girl who idolized Goethe and later married someone younger and handsomer. If you make yourself the handmaid of a man of genius and worship him till death, you might live forever in his glory; if you attach to me that ideal now, I might, by my more modest gifts, disappoint you. The compact is madness: in exchange for your reveries you risk the dissatisfaction of a life that will not be wholly yours. If we continue, I might love you wildly—but if I pleased you, the tale would likely end in the common fashion: marriage, house, children. Therefore, adieu.

CHAPTER X. THE MARRIAGE OF SOULS

To Monsieur de Canalis:

My friend, your letter gives me both pain and pleasure; perhaps someday all will be pleasure. I want the soul’s answers you seek from God. Cannot the friendship of Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne be revived in us? Remember Sismondi’s lovely household or the Marquis de Pescaire’s; can two hearts vibrate in unison across distance? Man is both harp, musician, and listener. Will you find me jealous like ordinary women? I know you go into the world; I know your beauties and wits. Yet I seek a flower that blooms on Alpine peaks—men of genius—and I wish to cultivate it. I am no commonplacid creature; I would not belong to a ninny or a merchant who could ruin us. My father adores my wishes and will not oppose them. If I please my poet and he pleases me, we will build our love high and secure.

I am an eaglet; I want to be imprisoned by love as I now am by my father’s will. Listen to my argument: a young imagination locked in a tower longs to run in its neighbor’s park—your genius. I ask: why complain? Parents arrange marriages by appearances and formalities; a young girl is often denied the right to choose. I want a long marriage of souls before any legal pledge. My mother followed instinct and beauty in marriage and prospered; I follow my will. I will not bind us prematurely to a vulgar experience of addresses. If our correspondence reveals true sympathy, we may be spared the common follies and reach graveside hand in hand. I await your orders, monseigneur, and am with all my heart,

Your handmaiden,

O. d’Este M.

To Mademoiselle O. d’Este M.—You are a witch and I love you! Is that what you desire, most original girl? Perhaps you merely amuse provincial leisure with a folly to make a poet act rashly. If so, you have done wrong. Your letters have mischief. But I am no longer master of myself; my life depends on your answer. If a certainty of unbounded affection that disregards social conventions touches you—if you will let me seek you—say so. If your reply is favorable I change my life; I shall renounce the trivial pleasures we call happiness. For me, happiness is a fusion of feeling, a harmony of souls, an imprint of a noble ideal on daily life—a constancy of heart more precious than fidelity. We may call such devotion sacrifice, but is it not the dream of poets and maidens? I cannot speak more of myself—my past nor my filial affection, which you have altered. Say one word and I will love you until death. Either write me no more or give me hope. Let me see you, or let me go. Must I bid you adieu? Will you permit me to sign myself,

Your Friend?

To Monsieur de Canalis,—What flattery! How quickly the grave man becomes Leander! To what should I attribute such change—my ink upon your paper, your fancy, or a memory of the girl at the window? Your letter has given me elating pleasures outside family affection. What are blood ties compared to those forged by mysterious sympathies? Bless you for the happiness you have given me. You explain some injustices of social life. Glory is dazzling and belongs mostly to men; God gives love to women to soothe them. I have felt my mission and you confirm it.

Sometimes I wake in inexpressible sweetness, a little German waking opposed to my Southern sunsets; after your feverish letter I felt heavenly freshness, destined to die for one I love. Your poem “The Maiden’s Song” paints these moments; I think you worthy to be me. Your letter lets me read within you: I have guessed your struggles, curiosity, and plans, but do not yet know you well enough to satisfy you. The mystery that shrouds me permits me to say: if we meet, our mutual understanding will end. Shall we make a compact? Your first disadvantage brought esteem; that is precious. Write me your life briefly—tell me what you do in Paris day by day, as to an old friend—and then I will see you. That is a great step.

This is no intrigue: it involves my life and my parents’, whom I adore; my choice must please them and they must find a son in you. Tell me, to what extent can superb spirits bend to family yokes and endure little miseries? I meditated long on your nature. You, who send airy visions, have had romances; still I cried “Onward!” because I studied the geography of great summits. You said Goethe and Byron were egotists; you erred, perhaps from modesty. Great minds are slaves of ideas; their life is a sacrifice as a mother sacrificially devotes herself to a child. The visible egoism of genius hides abnegation. Who can say that a man who gives gifts to his epoch is selfish? The poet’s life needs a vast organization to bear pleasures and sorrows alike; consider Molière, who suffered when he sought to live feeling’s keenest moments—his comedy seems horrible when measured by his private life.

I place you among those whose generosity borders on the divine. Had I found self-interest and ambition where I now find noble flowers of the soul, I would have been anguished. I once met disappointment before sixteen; what would have become of me had I learned at twenty that fame is a lie? I should have surrendered to my father and asked him to choose a son-in-law. I would have become a resigned servant to a dull man; my soul would have been suicided. But I have you; I believe in you. That belief straightens my thoughts and fancies. Sometimes I wish I were in the middle of the book we are beginning; I feel persistence, strength to love, constancy tempered by reason, a heroism for duty.

If you could follow me to the exquisite retreat I fancy, if you knew my plans, the word “folly” might escape you unjustly. I wish to be an inexhaustible spring for the twenty years nature allows me to shine; to vary happiness, to put intelligence into tenderness, and give piquancy to fidelity. I wish to be courageous for my friend, to abnegate for the nest like no bird can. Tell me, do you now think me to blame for my first letter? The mysterious wind of will drove me to you, as tempest drives the rose-tree to the pollard window. In your letter I hold your cry: “God wills it.”

Ah, you will call me a chatterbox; the people around say I am taciturn.

O. d’Este M.

To Mademoiselle O. d’Este M.,—You are a witch, a spirit, and I love you! Is that what you desire of me, most original of girls? Perhaps you are amusing yourself at a provincial’s expense; if so, you have done wrong. But I am no longer master of myself; my life, my future depend on your answer. Tell me if certain and unbounded affection, unconcerned with social conventions, will touch you—if you will allow me to seek you. If you answer favorably, I change my life; I renounce the little pleasures we call happiness. Happiness is what you dream: a fusion of feelings, accord of souls, the imprint of a noble ideal on daily life, constancy of heart above fidelity. We call such devotion sacrifice; still it is the dream of poets and maidens. Say one word and I will love you to death. Either write no more or give me hope. Let me see you, or let me go. Will you permit me to sign myself,

Your Friend?

To Monsieur de Canalis,—What flattery! How rapidly is Anselme transformed into Leander! Is it my ink, your fancy, or the memory of the girl at the window? Your letter has given me elating pleasures; what are the ties of blood compared with those forged by mysterious sympathies? Bless you for the happiness you have given me. You explain the injustices of social life; glory belongs chiefly to men, while love is women’s portion. I have felt my mission; you confirm it.

Often I rise in a state of inexpressible sweetness, little German wakings opposed to Southern sunsets. After your feverish letter, I felt heavenly freshness. “The Maiden’s Song” is a favorite. I think you worthy to be me. Your letter lets me read within you: I have guessed your turmoil, but I do not yet know you. Let us make a compact: write your life in a few words, tell me of your days in Paris as to an old friend, and then I will see you. This involves my life and my parents’—they must find a son in you. How far can superb spirits bend to family life? I have meditated this long.

You said Goethe and Byron were colossi of egoism; you erred. Poets are slaves of their ideas; their sacrifice may seem egoism but is often maternity of genius. If I had found selfishness where I found flowers, I would have been ruined. I suffered before sixteen; had I found fame a lie at twenty, I would have abandoned myself. But I have you. I believe in you.

If you could follow to my retreat and know my plans, you might not call me foolish. I wish to be a perpetual spring for my friend’s life, to vary happiness, to put intelligence into tenderness, to conjure away griefs by gentle abnegation, to guard a nest a lifetime. Do you blame me for my first letter? The mysterious will drove me to you. In your letter I have seen God’s answer—“God wills it.”

O. d’Este M.

The visit from the renowned surgeon profoundly affected Modeste; she admired his selflessness and dedication to relieving suffering. That evening in the salon, guests praised the Mignon family for the hope Desplein inspired. Conversation turned to Desplein’s genius: the medical community acknowledged it, but Canalis dismissed it as mere application rather than invention. Modeste defended him, arguing genius should not be judged by narrow standards.

Canalis insisted true genius means invention, invoking figures like Napoleon and Scott; Modeste countered that practical contributions by Parmentier and Jacquart also deserve the term. The discussion grew heated—Modeste scorned purely utilitarian views while her father and others stressed utility’s role in art and life. Canalis, stung, tried to elevate himself with eloquence, revealing his vanity.

Modeste’s flirtation with Canalis irritated La Briere, who felt overshadowed; he left convinced he had lost her to the poet. Her bold challenge to her father surprised the adults and exposed her growing independence.

The next day, Modeste’s interactions with Canalis grew more flirtatious and provocative, fueling La Briere’s jealousy. Canalis sensed competition and staged grand gestures to win her, but she remained skeptical of his motives. Tension mounted as Modeste asserted her independence and pushed back against social expectations.

Approaching the hunt, Modeste’s feelings for La Briere deepened while her rapport with Canalis became strained. The arrival of the Duc d’Herouville and the promise of a grand hunt raised the stakes: Modeste’s beauty and fortune attracted attention and shifted the suitors’ dynamics as they vied for her favor.

The hunt served as backdrop to unfolding drama, placing Modeste between her affection for La Briere and Canalis’s allure. The social intricacies of the gathering exposed the characters’ true natures, and Modeste had to navigate the expectations tied to being a young woman of wealth and beauty.

Ultimately, Modeste moved toward a deeper understanding of love and ambition, trying to balance personal desire with the realities of her social standing. The conclusion suggests the complexities of relationships and the sacrifices involved in pursuing happiness, prompting reflection on the nature of love and ambition within a society governed by expectation.
