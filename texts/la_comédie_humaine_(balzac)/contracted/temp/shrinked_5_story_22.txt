EUGENIE GRANDET

I

There are houses in some provincial towns whose aspect inspires a
melancholy like that of sombre cloisters, dreary moorlands, or ruins.
Within them silence and barrenness prevail; life seems stagnant until a
pale, motionless face peers from a window at an unaccustomed step.

Such sadness marked a dwelling in Saumur at the upper end of the steep
street leading to the chateau. This street—now little frequented, hot
in summer, cold in winter, and dark in places—is notable for the
resonance of its pebbled pavement, its narrow tortuous roadway, and the
quiet of houses overtopped by the ramparts. Many are three centuries
old, built of wood, their ends carved into fantastic figures, blackened
by time. Slate-covered beams trace a bluish line along frail walls;
rotten shingles bend beneath the years. Blackened window-sills with
faint sculpture barely bear the clay pots of poor working-women. Doors
studded with enormous nails show domestic hieroglyphics of a lost
meaning: here a Protestant attested his belief, there a Leaguer cursed
Henry IV., elsewhere a bourgeois carved the insignia of his trade—the
whole history of France is written there.

Next to a tottering artisan’s house rose a country gentleman’s mansion,
whose stone arch still held battered armorial bearings. In this hilly
street the ground-floors are neither shops nor warehouses; lovers of
the Middle Ages will find the ouvrouir of our forefathers in all its
naive simplicity. These low rooms, without show-windows, are deep, dark,
and unadorned. Their doors open in two parts; the upper half is fastened
back, the lower half, fitted with a spring-bell, swings continually.
Air and light reach the damp den through the upper door or an open space
between the ceiling and a low front wall, closed by solid shutters put
up each evening and held by heavy iron bars.

This wall serves as a counter. No delusive display: only samples—tubs
of cod and salt, bundles of sail-cloth, cordage, copper wire, iron hoops,
a few cloth pieces on shelves. Enter; a neat girl in a white kerchief
drops her knitting and calls her father or mother, who sells you what you
want, phlegmatically, civilly, or arrogantly. A cooper may sit twirling
his thumbs, seeming to own little beyond a few boat-ribs and laths, yet
his wood-yard below supplies the cooperage trade of Anjou. A hot season
makes him rich, a rainy one ruins him; puncheons worth eleven francs
may fall to six in a morning.

Here, as in Touraine, atmospheric vicissitudes control commerce. Wine-growers,
proprietors, wood-merchants, coopers, inn-keepers, mariners—all watch the
sun. They dread frost, rain, wind, drought; a perpetual duel goes on
between the heavens and terrestrial interests. The barometer smooths,
saddens, or brightens their countenances. From end to end of this street,
formerly the Grand’Rue de Saumur, men call: “Here’s golden weather,” or
“It rains louis,” knowing what sun or rain will bring them.

On fine Saturdays, these traders, each with vineyards, fields, and
provisions provided for, close shop and spend two days in the country.
Purchases and profits arranged, they have hours for pleasure, observation,
criticism, and spying. A housewife cannot buy a partridge without
neighbors asking if it was cooked right; a girl is seen by idling groups
if she peeps at a window. Consciences are held in the light; the dark,
silent houses hide no mysteries. Life is mostly in the open air; every
household sits at its threshold to breakfast, dine, and quarrel. No one
passes without being examined; formerly a stranger entering a provincial
town was bantered from door to door—the origin of many good stories and
the nickname copieux for Angers folk who excelled in such urban sarcasm.

The ancient mansions of Saumur’s old town are at the top of this street,
once occupied by neighborhood nobility. The melancholy dwelling where
the following events occur is one of these venerable relics of a simpler
age. Follow the picturesque thoroughfare and you will find the dark
recess where the door of Monsieur Grandet’s house is hidden. To explain
that provincial expression—the house of Monsieur Grandet—we must give
the biography of Monsieur Grandet himself.

Monsieur Grandet enjoyed a reputation in Saumur whose causes and
effects are best understood provincially. In 1789—still called by some
le Pere Grandet—he was a master-cooper, able to read, write, and cipher.
When the Republic sold church property in the arrondissement, the forty-year-old
cooper had just married the daughter of a rich wood-merchant. With about
two thousand louis-d’or—his own fortune and his wife’s dowry—and
help from two hundred double louis given by his father-in-law to the
surly republican presiding over sales, Grandet bought for a song one of
the finest vineyards in the arrondissement, an old abbey, and several farms.
The inhabitants, so little revolutionary, thought Pere Grandet bold and
patriotic; in truth his thought was open only to vineyards. Appointed to
the administration of Saumur, his pacific influence showed itself
politically and commercially: he protected the ci-devant nobles and
prevented, where possible, the sale of emigres’ lands; he furnished the
Republican armies with thousands of puncheons of white wine and took
payment in splendid fields reserved for sale.

Under the Consulate Grandet became mayor, governed wisely, and reaped
better pickings. Under the Empire he was called Monsieur Grandet; Napoleon
superseded him in office but Grandet left without regret. He had built
roads to his properties; his house and lands, assessed advantageously,
paid moderate taxes; and his vineyards, through his care, became the
“head of the country,” a local term for the finest yielders. He might
well have asked for the Legion of Honor.

In 1806 Monsieur Grandet was fifty-seven, his wife thirty-six, and an only
daughter, the fruit of their love, was ten. That year he inherited three
fortunes—those of Madame de la Gaudiniere, née de la Bertelliere, of old
Monsieur de la Bertelliere (her grandfather), and of Madame Gentillet,
her grandmother—a hoard amassed by avarice. Old Monsieur de la Bertelliere
called investment extravagance and took keener interest in the sight of
his gold than in usury. The inhabitants estimated his savings according
to “the revenues of the sun’s wealth,” as they said.

Monsieur Grandet thus obtained a modern sort of nobility. He became the
most imposing personage in the arrondissement. He farmed a hundred acres
of vineyard, which in good years yielded seven or eight hundred hogsheads
of wine; he owned thirteen farms, an old abbey whose windows he had
walled to save expense, one hundred and twenty-seven acres of meadow
with three thousand poplars planted in 1793, and the house in which he
lived. This was his visible estate; as to other property, only two men
could hazard a guess at its value: Monsieur Cruchot, a notary who handled
Grandet’s usurious investments, and Monsieur des Grassins, Saumur’s
richest banker, in whose profits Grandet had a secret share.

Though Cruchot and des Grassins were discreet, their public respect for
Grandet led observers to estimate his wealth by their obsequiousness.
No one in Saumur doubted Monsieur Grandet had a private treasure, a
hiding-place of louis he nightly gazed upon. Avaricious people saw proof
in his eyes, which seemed tinged by yellow metal. The glance of a man
accustomed to draw enormous interest acquires furtive, eager, mysterious
movements—an unspoken language of passion. Grandet inspired respect as
one who owed no man anything: skilful cooper and experienced wine-grower,
he could tell with precision whether to make a thousand puncheons or
five hundred; he never failed in speculation and always had casks when
casks were dear. He could store his vintage and sell at two hundred
francs when small proprietors took five louis. His famous 1811 vintage,
judiciously stored and slowly sold, brought him over two hundred and
forty thousand francs.

Financially, Grandet was part tiger, part boa-constrictor. He could lie
in wait, spring on his prey, swallow masses of louis, and rest like a
snake digesting—impassible, methodical, and cold. No one passed without
admiration mingled with fear: had not many felt the rending of those
polished steel claws? For one man Cruchot procured money at eleven per
cent; for another des Grassins discounted bills at frightful deductions.
Few days passed without Grandet’s name in market talk. To some, his
fortune was an object of patriotic pride. Innkeepers and merchants would
tell strangers: “We have two or three millionaire establishments; but as
for Monsieur Grandet, he does not himself know how much he is worth.”

In 1816 Saumur’s best reckoners put his landed property near four
millions; having made on average a hundred thousand francs a year from
1793 to 1817, it was fair to presume he held cash nearly equal to his
estate. After a game of boston or talk on vines, people said: “Le Pere
Grandet? le Pere Grandet must have at least five or six millions.”

“You are cleverer than I; I have never been able to find out the
amount,” Cruchot or des Grassins would answer when overheard.

If some Parisian mentioned Rothschild or Lafitte, Saumur folk asked if
he were as rich as Monsieur Grandet. If the Parisian affirmed, they
shook their heads incredulously. Such a fortune explained all his
actions. Early peculiarities that once provoked ridicule had long died
away. His slightest actions, like the blinking of his eyes, were law to
the countryside, where men had studied him like a naturalist studies
instinct.

“It will be a hard winter,” said one; “Pere Grandet has put on his fur
gloves.”

“Pere Grandet is buying quantities of staves; there will be plenty of
wine this year.”

Monsieur Grandet never bought bread or meat: his farmers supplied
capons, chickens, eggs, butter, and his tithe of wheat. He owned a mill;
the tenant took grain as part of rent and returned flour and bran.
La Grande Nanon, his only servant, baked bread every Saturday. Grandet
arranged with kitchen-gardeners, his tenants, to supply vegetables. He
sold most of his fruits in the market. His firewood came from hedgerows
and half-rotten sheds whose planks farmers carted to town, cut, and
stacked in his wood-house, receiving his thanks. His only known expenses
were consecrated bread, clothing for his wife and daughter, hire of
their chairs in church, La Grande Nanon’s wages, tinning saucepans,
lights, taxes, repairs, and industrial costs. He had six hundred acres
of woodland recently purchased, which he put under a neighbor’s care
for a promised indemnity. After acquiring this, he ate game for the
first time.

Monsieur Grandet’s manners were simple. He spoke little, in short
sententious phrases in a soft voice. Early on he stuttered when required
to speak at length; this incoherence, attributed to defects of
education, was really assumed and will be explained later. Four brief
sentences sufficed him: “I don’t know; I cannot; I will not; I will see
about it.” He never said yes or no, and never committed himself to
writing. If people talked, he listened coldly, chin in his right hand,
right elbow resting on the left, forming opinions he never changed.
He reflected long before any business agreement; when his opponent
confidently revealed purposes, Grandet answered: “I can decide nothing
without consulting my wife.” His wife, reduced to helpless slavery, was
a useful screen in business. He went nowhere among friends; he neither
gave nor accepted dinners; he made no stir, economizing even in motion.
He never disturbed others’ things out of respect for property, yet at
home coarse language and habits of his nature surfaced where he
controlled himself less.

Physically, Grandet was five feet high, thick-set, square-built, with
calves twelve inches in circumference, knotted knees, and broad
shoulders; his face was round, tanned, pitted by small-pox; his chin
straight, lips uncurved, teeth white; his eyes had the calm, devouring
expression of a basilisk; his forehead showed transverse wrinkles and
significant protuberances; his yellow-gray hair was mockingly called
silver and gold by young jesters. His nose, thick at the end, bore a veined
wen the common people said was full of malice. His whole countenance
showed dangerous cunning, an integrity without warmth, and the egotism
of a man long used to concentrate every feeling on avarice’s enjoyments
and upon the only human being who mattered to him—his daughter and sole
heiress, Eugenie. Attitude, manners, bearing, all testified to the
self-belief habit of success gives.

Thus, though outwardly unctuous, Monsieur Grandet’s nature was iron.
His dress never varied since 1791: stout shoes tied with thongs, thick
woollen stockings, short breeches of coarse maroon cloth with silver
buckles, a velvet waistcoat of yellow and puce stripes, a large maroon
coat with wide flaps, a black cravat, and a quaker’s hat. His gloves,
thick as a gendarme’s, lasted twenty months; to preserve them he always
laid them on his hat in one particular spot. Saumur knew nothing further.

Only six persons had the right of entrance to Grandet’s house. The most
important of the first three was a nephew of Monsieur Cruchot. Since his
appointment as president of the Civil courts of Saumur this young man
had added Bonfons to Cruchot, signing himself C. de Bonfons. Any litigant
so ill-advised as to call him Monsieur Cruchot would soon feel his folly
in court. The magistrate protected those who called him Monsieur le
president, but favored with smiles those who addressed him as Monsieur
de Bonfons. Monsieur le president was thirty-three, owned Bonfons worth
seven thousand francs a year; he expected to inherit his uncle the
notary’s property and that of another uncle, Abbe Cruchot, a dignitary
of Saint-Martin de Tours. These three Cruchots, backed by cousins and
allies, formed a party like the Medici.

Madame des Grassins, mother of a son of twenty-three, came assiduously
to play cards with Madame Grandet, hoping to marry her Adolphe to
Mademoiselle Eugenie. Monsieur des Grassins promoted his wife’s schemes
by services constantly rendered to the old miser, arriving in time upon
the field of battle. The three des Grassins had adherents and cousins.
On the Cruchot side, the abbe—the family Talleyrand—backed by his brother
the notary, sharply contested every inch with his female adversary and
tried to obtain the heiress for his nephew the president.

This secret warfare for Eugenie’s hand kept Saumur agitated. Would
Mademoiselle Grandet marry Monsieur le president or Monsieur Adolphe des
Grassis? Some said Monsieur Grandet would give his daughter to neither.
The old cooper, eaten with ambition, was said to seek a peer of France,
to whom an income of three hundred thousand francs would make the
Grandet casks acceptable. Others replied the des Grassins were nobles
and rich; Adolphe was personable; unless the old man had a papal nephew
at his beck, such an alliance should satisfy a man who came from
nothing—whom Saumur remembered with an adze in his hand and the bonnet
rouge. Certain wise heads noted Cruchot de Bonfons had the right of
entry at all times whereas his rival was received only on Sundays; others
trusted Madame des Grassins’ intimacy with the women. “It is diamond
cut diamond,” said a Saumur wit.

The oldest, wiser inhabitants declared the Grandets knew better than to
let the property go out of the family, and that Mademoiselle Eugenie
Grandet of Saumur would marry the son of Monsieur Grandet of Paris, a
wealthy wholesale wine-merchant. To this the Cruchotines and Grassinists
replied: the two brothers had seen each other only twice in thirty years;
Monsieur Grandet of Paris, mayor and deputy and colonel of the National
Guard, disowned the Saumur Grandets and meant to ally with some ducal
family favored by Napoleon. In short, nothing was not said of the heiress,
talked of for fifty miles and even on conveyances from Angers to Blois.

At the beginning of 1811, the Cruchotines won an advantage. The estate
of Froidfond, notable for its park, mansion, farms, streams, ponds, and
forests, worth about three millions, was put up for sale by the young
Marquis de Froidfond, who had to liquidate. Maitre Cruchot, the president,
and the abbe, aided by adherents, prevented sale in small lots. The
notary concluded a bargain for the whole property, payable in gold,
persuading the marquis that suits against purchasers of small lots would
delay collection; it was better to sell the whole to Monsieur Grandet,
solvent and able to pay in ready money. The marquisate of Froidfond was
accordingly conveyed down the gullet of Monsieur Grandet, who, to Saumur’s
astonishment, paid for it, under proper discount, with the usual formalities.

This affair echoed from Nantes to Orleans. Monsieur Grandet took advantage
of a cart returning by way of Froidfond to see his chateau. After casting
a master’s eye over the property, he returned to Saumur, satisfied he had
invested at five per cent, and seized by the stupendous thought of
extending the marquisate by concentrating his property there. Then, to
fill his coffers, now nearly empty, he resolved to thin out his woods
and sell the poplars in the meadows.

One now understands the full meaning of “the house of Monsieur Grandet”—that cold, silent, pallid dwelling above the town, sheltered by the ruined ramparts. The two pillars and arch of the porte-cochere were built of tufa, a Loire stone so soft it scarcely lasts two centuries. Irregular holes, bored or eaten by weather, gave the arch and side walls the look of vermiculated stonework, like a jail gateway. Above the arch was a long bas-relief in hard stone representing the four seasons, faces already crumbling and blackened. On the projecting plinth above had sprung chance growths—pellitory, bindweed, convolvuli, nettles, plantain, and even a little cherry-tree.

The archway door was solid oak, brown, shrunken, split; frail though it looked, iron bolts held it in symmetrical patterns. A small square grating with close bars, red with rust, filled the middle panel and served as the knocker’s aperture; a ring struck upon the grinning head of a huge nail. This jaquemart-like knocker resembled an enormous exclamation point; an antiquary might have seen traces of the burlesque figure it once represented. Through this grating—originally for recognizing friends in civil war—curious persons could perceive, at the far end of the dark slimy vault, a few broken steps leading to a garden shut in by thick damp walls whose moisture nourished tufts of sickly herbage. These walls were the ruined ramparts under which stretched the gardens of several neighboring houses.

The most important room on the ground floor was a large hall entered directly from beneath the porte-cochere vault. Few know the importance of a hall in the little towns of Anjou, Touraine, and Berry. The hall is simultaneously antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining-room; it is the theatre of domestic life. There the barber came twice a year to cut Monsieur Grandet’s hair; there farmers, the cure, the under-prefect, and the miller’s boy came on business. This room, with two windows looking on the street, was entirely of wood. Gray panels with ancient mouldings covered the walls from top to bottom; the ceiling showed its beams, painted gray, the spaces between washed white and yellowed with age. An old brass clock inlaid with arabesques adorned the mantel of the ill-cut white stone chimney-piece, above which hung a greenish mirror whose bevel reflected a thread of light along a gothic damascened steel frame. Two copper-gilt candelabra decorated the chimney-piece corners; by removing their side branches each main stem, fastened to a bluish marble pedestal tipped with copper, served as a single candlestick on ordinary occasions. Antique chairs were covered with tapestry of La Fontaine’s fables; faded colors and darning blurred the figures.

At the four corners of the hall were closets, or buffets, surmounted by dirty shelves. An old marquetry card-table, its top a chessboard, stood between the windows. Above it hung an oval barometer with a black border enlivened by gilt bands, on which flies had so disported that the gilding was problematical. Opposite the chimney-piece were two pastel portraits, supposed to represent Madame Grandet’s grandfather, old Monsieur de la Bertelliere, as a lieutenant in the French guard, and the deceased Madame Gentillet in the guise of a shepherdess. The windows were draped with red gros de Tours curtains held back by silken cords with ecclesiastical tassels. This little luxury, ill-matched to Monsieur Grandet’s habits, had been included in the purchase of the house along with the steel pier-glass, the tapestries, and the rosewood buffets.

By the window nearest the door stood a straw chair on castors to lift Madame Grandet high enough to see passers-by. A stained cherry-wood work-table filled the embrasure, and Eugenie Grandet’s little armchair stood beside it. Here their lives had flowed peacefully for fifteen years in a round of work from April to November. On the first of November they took their winter station by the chimney. Grandet permitted a fire only from November 1 to March 31, regardless of early spring or wintry autumn chills. A foot-warmer filled with embers la Grande Nanon contrived to save mother and daughter from chilly mornings and evenings in April and October. Mother and daughter managed the family linen, spending their days in labor; if Eugenie wished to embroider a collar for her mother she had to take time from sleep and deceive her father to obtain light. For a long time the miser dispensed the tallow candle to his daughter and la Grande Nanon just as he did bread and other daily necessities.

La Grande Nanon was perhaps the only human being capable of willingly accepting her master’s despotism. The whole town envied Monsieur and Madame Grandet for possessing her. La Grande Nanon, so called for her height of five feet eight inches, had lived with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years. Though she received only sixty francs a year, she was reputed one of Saumur’s richest serving-women; those sixty francs, accumulated over thirty-five years, had enabled her to invest four thousand francs in an annuity with Maitre Cruchot. This result of long, persistent economy seemed gigantic. Every servant in town, seeing the poor sexagenarian secure of bread for her old age, was jealous and never thought of the hard slavery through which it had been won.

At twenty-two she had been unable to find a situation because her face repelled most; unjustly, for it would have been admired on a grenadier’s shoulders. Forced to leave a farm where she kept cows because the dwelling burned down, she came to Saumur, full of robust courage that shrinks from no labor. Le Pere Grandet then thought of marriage and setting up his household. He espied the girl, rejected from door to door. A good judge of strength from his trade as a cooper, he guessed the work to be got from a female shaped like a Hercules—firm on her feet, strong in the hips, square in the back, with hands of a cartman and honesty as sound as her unblemished virtue. Neither the warts on her martial visage, nor the brick tints of her skin, nor her sinewy arms, nor her ragged garments dismayed the cooper, who was still of an age when the heart shudders. He fed, shod, and clothed her, paid wages, and set her to work without treating her too roughly. Seeing herself thus welcomed, la Grande Nanon wept secretly and attached herself sincerely to her master, who from that day ruled and worked her with feudal authority. Nanon did everything. She cooked, made lye, washed linen in the Loire and brought it home on her shoulders; she rose early and slept late; she prepared the vine-dressers’ food during harvest, watched the market-people, protected her master’s property like a faithful dog, and, in blind confidence, obeyed his absurd exactions without murmur.

In the famous year 1811, when the grapes were gathered with unheard-of difficulty, Grandet resolved to give Nanon his old watch—the first present in twenty years of service. Though he turned over his old shoes (which fitted her), it is impossible to consider that quarterly benefit a gift, for the shoes were always thoroughly worn out. Necessity had made the poor girl so niggardly that Grandet had grown to love her as one loves a dog, and Nanon allowed him to fasten a spiked collar round her throat whose spikes no longer pricked. If Grandet cut bread with parsimony, she made no complaint; she gladly shared hygienic benefits from the household’s severe regime, in which no one was ever ill. Nanon was, in fact, one of the family; she laughed when Grandet laughed, felt gloomy or chilly, warmed herself, and toiled as he did. Never did the master need to find fault with the servant for pilfering grapes, plums, or nectarines. “Come, fall-to, Nanon!” he would say in years when branches bent under fruit and farmers handed it to the pigs.

To the poor peasant who had earned nothing but harsh treatment, Grandet’s ambiguous laugh was like a sunbeam. Nanon’s simple heart and narrow head could hold only one feeling and one idea. For thirty-five years she never ceased to see herself before Monsieur Grandet’s wood-yard, ragged and barefoot, and to hear him say, “What do you want, young one?” Her gratitude was ever new. Sometimes Grandet, reflecting that she had never heard a flattering word and might one day appear before God more chaste than the Virgin Mary, would say, looking at her, “Poor Nanon!” The exclamation was always followed by an undefinable look from the old servant. The words, uttered from time to time, formed a chain of friendship nothing ever parted, each exclamation adding a link. Such compassion, arising in the miser and accepted gratefully by the old spinster, had something inconceivably horrible about it. This cruel pity, recalling a thousand pleasures to the cooper, was to Nanon the sum total of happiness. Who does not likewise say, “Poor Nanon!” God will recognize his angels by the inflexions of their voices and by their secret sighs.

Many households in Saumur treated servants better but received less satisfaction in return. Thus people asked: “What have the Grandets ever done to make their Grande Nanon so attached? She would go through fire and water for them!” Her kitchen, whose barred windows looked into the court, was always clean, neat, cold—a true miser’s kitchen where nothing went to waste. When Nanon had washed dishes, locked up dinner remains, and put out her fire, she left the kitchen, separated by a passage from the living-room, and went to spin hemp beside her masters. One tallow candle sufficed the family for the evening. The servant slept at the end of the passage in a closet lit only by a fan-light. Her robust health enabled her to live in this hole; she could hear the slightest noise through the deep silence that reigned day and night. Like a watchdog, she slept with one ear open and took her rest alert.

A description of other parts of the dwelling will appear with the events of this history, though this sketch of the hall, where the household’s whole luxury appears, may enable the reader to surmise the nakedness of the upper floors.

In 1819, at the beginning of an evening in mid-November, la Grande Nanon lighted the fire for the first time. The autumn had been fine. This day was a fête well known to the Cruchotines and the Grassinists. The six antagonists, armed at all points, prepared to meet at the Grandets and surpass each other in testimonials of friendship. That morning all Saumur had seen Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet, accompanied by Nanon, on their way to Mass, and everyone remembered the day as Mademoiselle Eugenie’s birthday. Calculating when the family dinner would end, Maitre Cruchot, the Abbe Cruchot, and Monsieur C. de Bonfons hastened to arrive before the des Grassins and be first to pay compliments. All three brought enormous bouquets gathered in their greenhouses. The president’s flower stalks were ingeniously wound with a white satin ribbon fringed with gold. In the morning Monsieur Grandet, following his custom on Eugenie’s birth and fête days, went to her bedside and solemnly presented his paternal gift—which for thirteen years consisted regularly of a curious gold-piece. Madame Grandet gave her a winter or summer dress as needed. These two dresses and the gold pieces, supplemented by two more on New Year’s day and her father’s fête, gave Eugenie a little revenue of about a hundred crowns, which Grandet loved to see her amass. Was it not putting money from one strongbox to another, and training his heiress’s parsimony? He sometimes demanded an account of her treasure, formerly increased by the Bertelliere gifts, saying: “It is to be your marriage dozen.”

The “marriage dozen” is an old custom still in force in parts of central France. In Berry and Anjou, when a young girl marries, her family, or that of the husband, gives her a purse containing, according to means, twelve pieces, or twelve dozen, or twelve hundred pieces of gold. The poorest shepherd-girl never marries without her dozen, even if only coppers. They still tell in Issoudun of a “dozen” presented to a rich heiress containing one hundred and forty-four portugaises d’or. Pope Clement VII., uncle of Catherine de’ Medici, gave her when he married her to Henri II. a dozen antique gold medals.

During dinner, pleased to see Eugenie in a new gown, Grandet exclaimed, “As it is Eugenie’s birthday let us have a fire; it will be a good omen.”

“Mademoiselle will be married this year, that’s certain,” said la Grande Nanon, carrying away the remains of the goose—the pheasant of tradesmen.

“I don’t see anyone suitable for her in Saumur,” said Madame Grandet, glancing timidly at her husband, revealing the conjugal slavery under which she languished.

Grandet looked at his daughter and exclaimed gaily, “She is twenty-three years old to-day; we must soon begin to think of it.”

Eugenie and her mother silently exchanged a glance of intelligence.

Madame Grandet was a dry, thin woman, as yellow as a quince, awkward, slow—one born to be down-trodden. She had big bones, a big nose, a big forehead, big eyes, and bore a vague resemblance to mealy fruits without savor. Her teeth were black and few, her mouth wrinkled, her chin long and pointed. She was an excellent woman, a true la Bertelliere. L’abbe Cruchot occasionally told her she had not done ill, and she believed him. Angelic sweetness, resignation like that of an insect tortured by children, rare piety, a good heart, and unalterable equanimity made her universally pitied and respected. Her husband never gave her more than six francs at a time for personal expenses. Ridiculous as it seems, this woman, who by fortune and inheritances had brought Pere Grandet more than three hundred thousand francs, felt so profoundly humiliated by dependence and slavery—against which her gentleness prevented revolt—that she had never asked for a penny or remarked on the deeds Maitre Cruchot brought for her signature. This foolish secret pride, this nobility of soul perpetually misunderstood and wounded by Grandet, ruled her conduct.

Madame Grandet habitually wore a greenish levantine silk gown, making it last nearly a year; a large white cotton kerchief, a bonnet of plaited straw, and almost always a black-silk apron. As she seldom left the house she wore out few shoes and never asked anything for herself. Grandet, seized with occasional remorse when he remembered how long since he gave her the last six francs, always stipulated “the wife’s pin-money” when he sold his yearly vintage. The four or five louis presented by the Belgian or Dutchman who purchased wine were the chief visible signs of Madame Grandet’s annual revenues. But after receiving the five louis, her husband would often say, as though their purse were common, “Can you lend me a few sous?” and the poor woman, glad to help a man her confessor upheld as her lord and master, returned several crowns in winter out of the “pin-money.” When Grandet drew from his pocket the five-franc piece he allowed monthly for his daughter’s minor expenses—thread, needles, and toilet—he never failed to ask as he buttoned his breeches’ pocket, “And you, mother, do you want anything?”

“My friend,” Madame Grandet would answer, moved by maternal dignity, “we will see about that later.”

Wasted dignity! Grandet thought himself generous to his wife. Philosophers who meet the like of Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie have surely a right to say that irony lies at the bottom of Providence.

After the dinner at which Eugenie’s marriage was first mentioned, Nanon fetched a bottle of black-currant ratafia from Monsieur Grandet’s bed-chamber and nearly fell coming down the stairs.

“You great stupid!” said her master; “are you going to tumble about like other people, hey?”

“Monsieur, it was that step on your staircase which has given way.”

“She is right,” said Madame Grandet; “it ought to have been mended long ago. Yesterday Eugenie nearly twisted her ankle.”

“Here,” said Grandet, seeing Nanon pale, “as it is Eugenie’s birthday, and you came near falling, take a little glass of ratafia to set you right.”

“Faith! I’ve earned it,” said Nanon; “most people would have broken the bottle; but I’d sooner have broken my elbow holding it up.”

“Poor Nanon!” said Grandet, filling a glass.

“Did you hurt yourself?” asked Eugenie kindly.

“No, I didn’t fall; I threw myself back on my haunches.”

“Well! as it is Eugenie’s birthday,” said Grandet, “I’ll have the step mended. You people don’t know how to set your foot in the corner where the wood is still firm.”

Grandet took the candle, leaving his wife, daughter, and servant with only the hearthlight, and went into the bakehouse to fetch planks, nails, and tools.

“Can I help you?” cried Nanon, hearing him hammer.

“No, no! I’m an old hand at it,” he answered.

While Grandet mended the worm-eaten staircase and whistled the songs of his youth, the three Cruchots knocked.

“Is it you, Monsieur Cruchot?” asked Nanon, peeping through the grating.

“Yes,” answered the president.

Nanon opened the door, and the hearth’s light enabled the Cruchots to find their way into the room.

“Ha! you’ve come a-greeting,” said Nanon, smelling the flowers.

“Excuse me, messieurs,” cried Grandet, recognizing their voices; “I’ll be with you in a moment. I’m not proud; I am patching up a step on my staircase.”

“Go on, go on, Monsieur Grandet; a man’s house is his castle,” said the president sententiously.

Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet rose. The president, profiting by the darkness, said to Eugenie, “Will you permit me, mademoiselle, to wish you, on this your birth-day, a series of happy years and the continuance of health?” He offered a huge bouquet of rare flowers and, taking the heiress by the elbows, kissed each side of her neck with complacency that made her blush. The president, who looked like a rusty iron nail, felt his courtship progressing.

“Don’t stand on ceremony,” said Grandet, entering. “How well you do things on fête-days, Monsieur le président!”

“When it concerns mademoiselle,” said the abbe, armed with his own bouquet, “every day is a fête-day for my nephew.” The abbe kissed Eugenie’s hand. Maitre Cruchot boldly kissed her on both cheeks, remarking, “How we sprout up, to be sure! Every year is twelve months.”

As he replaced the candlestick beside the clock, Grandet, who never forgot his jokes and repeated them when he fancied them funny, said, “As this is Eugenie’s birthday let us illuminate.” He carefully removed the candelabra branches, put a socket on each pedestal, took from Nanon a new tallow candle with paper twisted around its end, set it in the hollow, made it firm, lit it, and sat down beside his wife, looking alternately at friends, his daughter, and the two candles. The Abbe Cruchot, a plump puffy man with a red wig plastered down and a face like an old female gambler, stretched his feet well shod in stout shoes with silver buckles and asked, “The des Grassins have not come?”

“Not yet,” said Grandet.

“But are they coming?” asked the old notary, twisting his face, which had as many holes as a colander, into a queer grimace.

“I think so,” answered Madame Grandet.

“Are your vintages all finished?” asked Monsieur de Bonfons.

“Yes, all of them,” said the old man, rising to walk up and down with chest swelling with pride as he repeated the words, “all of them.” Through the passage door he saw la Grande Nanon sitting by her fire with a candle, preparing to spin so as not to intrude among guests.

“Nanon,” he said, going into the passage, “put out that fire and that candle, and come and sit with us. Pardieu! the hall is big enough for all.”

“But monsieur, you are to have the great people.”

“Are not you as good as they? They are descended from Adam, and so are you.”

Grandet returned to the president and said, “Have you sold your vintage?”

“No, not I; I shall keep it. If the wine is good this year, it will be better two years hence. The proprietors, you know, have agreed to keep up the price; and this year the Belgians won’t get the better of us. Suppose they are sent off empty-handed for once, faith! they’ll come back.”

“Yes, but let us mind what we are about,” said Grandet in a tone that made the president tremble.

“Is he driving some bargain?” thought Cruchot.

At that moment the knocker announced the des Grassins, interrupting a conversation begun between Madame Grandet and the abbe.

Madame des Grassins was one of those lively, plump little women with pink-and-white skins who, thanks to provincial calm and virtuous habits, keep their youth past forty. She was like the last rose of autumn—pleasant though the petals had a frostiness and slight perfume. She dressed well, took fashions from Paris, set the tone in Saumur, and gave parties. Her husband, formerly a quartermaster in the Imperial guard, wounded at Austerlitz and since retired, still retained the seeming frankness of an old soldier.

“Good evening, Grandet,” he said, holding out his hand and affecting a superiority with which he always crushed the Cruchots. “Mademoiselle,” he added to Eugenie after bowing to Madame Grandet, “you are always beautiful and good; I do not know what to wish you.” So saying, he offered a little box from his servant containing a Cape heather—a recently imported, very rare flower.

Madame des Grassins kissed Eugenie affectionately, pressed her hand, and said, “Adolphe wishes to make you my little offering.”

A tall, blond young man, pale and slight, with tolerable manners and seeming shy though he had spent eight or ten thousand francs over his allowance in Paris while studying law, came forward and kissed Eugenie on both cheeks, offering her a workbox with silver-gilt utensils—mere show trumpery, despite the monogram E.G. in well-engraved gothic letters. As she opened it, Eugenie felt one of those unexpected perfect delights that make a young girl blush and tremble with pleasure. She turned her eyes to her father as if asking permission, and Monsieur Grandet replied, “Take it, my daughter,” in a tone that would have made an actor illustrious.

The three Cruchots felt crushed as they saw the joyous look cast upon Adolphe des Grassins by the heiress, to whom such riches were unheard-of. Monsieur des Grassins offered Grandet snuff, took some himself, shook off grains that fell on the Legion of Honor ribbon in his buttonhole, then looked at the Cruchots with an air that seemed to say, “Parry that thrust if you can!” Madame des Grassins cast her eyes on the blue vases holding the Cruchot bouquets, regarding the enemy’s gifts with the pretended interest of a satirical woman. At this delicate juncture the Abbe Cruchot left the company seated in a circle round the fire and joined Grandet at the lower end of the hall. As they reached the far window embrasure the priest said in the miser’s ear, “Those people throw money out of the windows.”

“What does that matter if it gets into my cellar?” retorted the old wine-grower.

“If you want to give gilt scissors to your daughter, you have the means,” said the abbe.

“I give her something better than scissors,” answered Grandet.

“My nephew is a blockhead,” thought the abbe as he looked at the president, whose rumpled hair added to the ill grace of his brown countenance. “Couldn’t he have found some little trifle which cost money?”

“We will join you at cards, Madame Grandet,” said Madame des Grassins.

“We might have two tables, as we are all here.”

“As it is Eugenie’s birthday you had better play loto all together,” said Pere Grandet; “the two young ones can join,” and the old cooper, who never played, motioned to his daughter and Adolphe. “Come, Nanon, set the tables.”

“We will help you, Mademoiselle Nanon,” said Madame des Grassins, quite joyous at the joy she had given Eugenie.

“I have never in my life been so pleased,” the heiress said to her; “I have never seen anything so pretty.”

“Adolphe brought it from Paris, and he chose it,” Madame des Grassins whispered.

“Go on! go on! damned intriguing thing!” thought the president. “If you ever have a suit in court, you or your husband, it shall go hard with you.”

The notary, sitting in his corner, looked calmly at the abbe, thinking, “The des Grassins may do what they like; my property and my brother’s and my nephew’s amount in all to eleven hundred thousand francs. The des Grassins, at most, have not half that; besides, they have a daughter. They may give presents; heiress and presents will be ours one of these days.”

At half-past eight the two card-tables were set. Madame des Grassins succeeded in seating her son beside Eugenie. The actors in this scene, so full of interest though commonplace, were provided with bits of pasteboard striped in many colors and numbered, and counters of blue glass. They seemed to listen to the notary’s jokes, who never drew a number without a remark, while in fact all thought of Monsieur Grandet’s millions. The old cooper, with inward self-conceit, contemplated Madame des Grassins’ pink feathers and fresh toilette, the banker’s martial head, the faces of Adolphe, the president, the abbe, and the notary, saying to himself:—

“They are all after my money. Hey! neither the one nor the other shall have my daughter; but they are useful—useful as harpoons to fish with.”

This family gaiety in the old gray room dimly lighted by two tallow candles; this laughter, accompanied by the whirr of Nanon’s spinning-wheel, sincere only on Eugenie’s and her mother’s lips; this triviality mingled with important interests; this young girl, who, like certain birds made victims by the price put on them, was now lured by proofs of friendship of which she was the dupe—all contributed to make the scene a melancholy comedy. It is, moreover, a drama of all times and places, though here in simplest expression. The figure of Grandet, playing his own game with false friendship and reaping enormous profits, dominates the scene and throws light upon it. The modern god—the only god in whom faith is preserved—money, is here, in all its power, manifested in a single countenance. Tender sentiments hold a secondary place; only three pure simple hearts—Nanon’s, Eugenie’s, and her mother’s—were inspired by them. How much ignorance there was in their simplicity! Eugenie and her mother knew nothing of Grandet’s wealth; they estimated life’s things by the glimmer of their pale ideas, and neither valued nor despised money because they were used to do without it. Their feelings, bruised though they did not know it, ever-living, were the secret spring of their existence and made them curious exceptions among lives purely material. Frightful condition of the human race! There is no joy that does not come from some species of ignorance.

At the moment Madame Grandet had won a loto of sixteen sous—the largest ever pooled in that house—and while la Grande Nanon laughed watching Madame pocket her riches, the knocker resounded on the house-door so loudly the women jumped in their chairs.

“There is no man in Saumur who would knock like that,” said the notary.

“How can they bang in that way!” exclaimed Nanon; “do they want to break in the door?”

“Who the devil is it?” cried Grandet.

Nanon took a candle and went to open the door, followed by her master.

“Grandet! Grandet!” cried his wife, frightened, running to the room’s door.

All the players looked at one another.

“Suppose we all go?” said Monsieur des Grassins; “that knock strikes me as ill-intentioned.”

A young man’s figure appeared, accompanied by a coach-office porter with two trunks and a carpet-bag. Monsieur Grandet turned sharply on his wife and said,—

“Madame Grandet, go back to your loto; leave me to speak with monsieur.”

He shut the door; the excited players returned to their seats but did not resume the game.

“Is it anyone from Saumur, Monsieur des Grassins?” asked his wife.

“No, it is a traveller.”

“He must have come from Paris.”

“Just so,” said the notary, pulling out his thick watch; “it’s nine o’clock; the Grand Bureau diligence is never late.”

“Is the gentleman young?” inquired the Abbe Cruchot.

“Yes,” answered Monsieur des Grassins, “and he has luggage which must weigh nearly three tons.”

“Nanon does not come back,” said Eugenie.

“It must be one of your relations,” remarked the president.

“Let us go on with our game,” said Madame Grandet gently. “I know from Monsieur Grandet’s tone that he is annoyed; perhaps he would not like to find us talking of his affairs.”

“Mademoiselle,” said Adolphe to his neighbor, “it is no doubt your cousin Grandet,—a very good-looking young man; I met him at the ball of Monsieur de Nucingen.” His mother trod on his toes; then, asking aloud for two sous to add to her stake, she whispered: “Will you hold your tongue, you great goose!”

Grandet returned without la Grande Nanon; the porter’s and Nanon’s steps were heard ascending the staircase. He was followed by the traveller—his arrival seemed as out of place as a peacock among poultry.

“Sit down near the fire,” said Grandet.

Before seating himself, the young stranger saluted the company gracefully. The men inclined their heads; the women made ceremonious bows.

“You are cold, no doubt, monsieur,” said Madame Grandet; “you have perhaps travelled from—”

“Just like all women!” said the old wine-grower, looking up from a letter. “Do let monsieur rest himself!”

“But, father, perhaps monsieur would like to take something,” said Eugenie.

“He has got a tongue,” said the old man sternly.

The newcomer, surprised by the curt domestic tone, rose, turned his back to the fire, lifted one foot to warm his boot, and said to Eugenie,—

“Thank you, my cousin, but I dined at Tours. And,” he added to Grandet, “I need nothing; I am not even tired.”

“Monsieur has come from the capital?” asked Madame des Grassins.

Monsieur Charles—son of Monsieur Grandet of Paris—took an eye-glass on a chain, applied it to his right eye to inspect the table and the persons around it. He ogled Madame des Grassins impertinently, and said,—

“Yes, madame. You are playing at loto, aunt. Do not let me interrupt you; it is too amusing to leave.”

“I was certain it was the cousin,” thought Madame des Grassins, glancing repeatedly at him.

“Forty-seven!” cried the old abbe. “Mark it down, Madame des Grassins. Isn’t that your number?”

Monsieur des Grassins put a counter on his wife’s card, who watched the cousin and Eugenie with uneasy curiosity. From time to time the young heiress glanced furtively at her cousin; the banker’s wife noted a rising curiosity in her.

Monsieur Charles Grandet, a handsome twenty-two-year-old, contrasted with the provincial company, who, disgusted by his aristocratic manners, studied him sarcastically. At twenty-two one is near childhood and often childish; out of a hundred, ninety-nine would likely have acted like him.

His father had told him to spend months with his uncle at Saumur; Charles, thinking perhaps of Eugenie, affected the superiority of a man of fashion. He brought a complete hunting-costume, the finest gun and knife, various waistcoats and collars, Buisson’s coats, fine linen, a gold toilet-set from his mother, dandy knick-knacks, and a pretty desk from a lady called Annette—Parisian frivolities, from a little whip to chased pistols.

Told to travel modestly, he had nevertheless taken the coupe of the diligence alone, pleased to avoid sharing the carriage. At Tours a hairdresser re-curled his chestnut locks; he changed linen, put on a black satin cravat and a round collar framing his fair, smiling face. A travelling great-coat, half buttoned, showed crossed cashmere waistcoats; his watch hung by a short gold chain; gray trousers had black silk seams. He twirled a cane with a chased gold knob and wore gray gloves—only a Parisian could dress thus without seeming ridiculous; his foppery was carried off with a dashing air.

Picture the Cruchots to understand the mutual amazement. All three took snuff, had ceased to hide their sniveling, and wore dingy shirts and yellowing collars. Their flabby cravats twisted into ropes; their linen, rarely washed, allowed grimy stains to settle. There was perfect unison of ill-grace and senility. A horror of fashion was the only point on which the Grassinists and the Cruchotines agreed.

When the Parisian took up his eye-glass to examine the joists, woodwork color, and fly-specks, the loto-players lifted their noses and looked at him as at a giraffe. Monsieur des Grassins and his son, acquainted with fashion, were astonished; with satirical glances they seemed to say, “That is what you see in Paris!”

They examined Charles at leisure without fearing to displease the master, who remained absorbed in the long letter he held, taking the only candle on the card-table and paying no heed to his guests. Eugenie, to whom such perfection of dress and person was unknown, thought she beheld a seraphic being. She inhaled the fragrance of his curls, longed to touch his soft gloves, envied his small hands and refined features. To a girl who mended her father’s clothes, Charles seemed like a fancy engraving in a keepsake. When he drew from his pocket a handkerchief embroidered by a great lady traveling in Scotland, Eugenie watched to see if he would use it. His manners, eye-glass, and contemptuous glance at the coffer delighted her; she dreamed long dreams of her phoenix cousin.

The loto-numbers were drawn slowly; the game ended. La Grande Nanon entered: “Madame, I want the sheets for monsieur’s bed.”

Madame Grandet followed her out. Madame des Grassins whispered: “Let us keep our sous and stop playing.” Each took two sous from the chipped saucer; the party moved toward the fire.

“Have you finished your game?” said Grandet, without looking up from his letter.

“Yes, yes!” replied Madame des Grassins, taking a seat near Charles.

Eugenie, moved by the fresh sentiment newly stirring in her, left the room to help her mother and Nanon. Had a confessor questioned her she might have admitted she neither thought of her mother nor Nanon but felt a keen desire to attend to her cousin’s room: to remedy any neglect, make it elegant. She arrived and found everything to be done. She suggested warming-pans, covered the old table with a cloth and had Nanon change it each morning; she convinced her mother to light a good fire and persuaded Nanon to bring a great pile of wood into the corridor without telling her father. She fetched a lacquer tray, a six-sided crystal goblet, a little gilt spoon, an antique flask engraved with cupids, and set these triumphantly on her cousin’s chimney-piece. More ideas surged through her mind in a quarter of an hour than she had ever had.

“Mamma,” she said, “my cousin will never bear the smell of a tallow candle; suppose we buy a wax one?” She darted to get the five-franc piece she had for monthly expenses. “Here, Nanon,” she cried, “quick!”

“What will your father say?” Madame Grandet remonstrated, seeing her daughter with an old Sevres sugar-basin Grandet had brought from Froidfond. “And where will you get the sugar? Are you crazy?”

“Mamma, Nanon can buy sugar as well as the candle.”

“But your father?”

“Surely his nephew ought not to go without a glass of eau sucrée? Besides, he will not notice it.”

“Your father sees everything,” said Madame Grandet, shaking her head.

Nanon hesitated; she knew her master.

“Come, Nanon, go,—because it is my birthday.”

Nanon laughed at the first jest her young mistress had ever made, and then obeyed.

While Eugenie and her mother prepared the bedroom, Madame des Grassins paid attention to Charles, seemingly setting her cap at him.

“You are very courageous, monsieur,” she said, “to leave the pleasures of the capital at this season and take up your abode in Saumur. If we do not frighten you away, you will find some amusements even here.”

She cast the ogling glance of provincial coquetry. Charles, out of his element and far from the chateau life he had imagined, perceived a dim likeness to Parisian faces and responded gracefully, beginning a conversation where Madame des Grassins gradually lowered her voice to confidences. After coquettish phrases and light jesting she thought herself unheard and said,—

“Monsieur, if you will do us the honor to come and see us, you will give as much pleasure to my husband as to myself. Our salon is the only one in Saumur where the higher business circles mingle with the nobility. My husband—I say it with pride—is as much valued by one class as by the other. We will try to relieve the monotony of your visit here. If you stay all the time with Monsieur Grandet, good heavens! what will become of you? Your uncle is a sordid miser who thinks of nothing but his vines; your aunt is a pious soul who can’t put two ideas together; and your cousin is a little fool, without education, perfectly common, no fortune, who will spend her life in darning towels.”

“She is really very nice, this woman,” thought Charles as he responded to Madame des Grassins’ coquetries.

“It seems to me, wife, that you are taking possession of monsieur,” said the stout banker, laughing.

The notary and the president uttered a few significant words; the abbe, looking slyly at them, focused their thoughts by taking a pinch of snuff and saying as he handed round his box: “Who can do the honors of Saumur for monsieur so well as madame?”

“Ah! what do you mean by that, monsieur l’abbe?” demanded Monsieur des Grassins.

“I mean it in the best possible sense for you, for madame, for the town of Saumur, and for monsieur,” said the wily old man, turning to Charles.

The Abbe Cruchot had guessed the conversation without seeming to pay attention.

“Monsieur,” said Adolphe to Charles with an air he tried to make free and easy, “I don’t know whether you remember me, but I had the honor of dancing as your vis-à-vis at a ball given by the Baron de Nucingen,—and—”

“Perfectly; I remember perfectly, monsieur,” answered Charles, pleased to be the object of attention.

“Monsieur is your son?” he asked Madame des Grassins.

The abbe looked at her maliciously.

“Yes, monsieur,” she answered.

“Then you were very young when you were in Paris?” said Charles to Adolphe.

“You must know, monsieur,” said the abbe, “that we send them to Babylon as soon as they are weaned.”

Madame des Grassins examined the abbe with a penetrating glance.

“It is only in the provinces,” he continued, “that you will find women of thirty and more as fresh as madame here, with a son about to take his degree. I almost fancy myself back in the days when the young men stood on chairs in the ball-room to see you dance, madame,” he added to his female adversary. “To me, your triumphs are but of yesterday—”

“The old rogue!” thought Madame Grassins; “can he have guessed my intentions?”

“It seems that I shall have a good deal of success in Saumur,” thought Charles as he unbuttoned his great-coat, put a hand into his waistcoat, and cast a distant glance to imitate the attitude of a dashing poet.

Pere Grandet’s preoccupation did not escape the notary and the president, who tried to guess the letter’s contents by the almost imperceptible motions of the miser’s face under the candlelight. He maintained his habitual calm with difficulty; we may picture the countenance such a man endeavored to preserve as he read the fatal letter which follows.

The letter from Victor-Ange-Guillaume Grandet to his brother was a few pages long. He declared he could no longer survive the disgrace of bankruptcy: the double failures of his broker and his notary had carried off his last resources and left him owing nearly four millions, with assets not more than twenty-five percent of that sum. The wines in his warehouses suffered from the fall in prices caused by the abundance and quality of the other Grandet’s vintage. Three days hence Paris would cry out that he was a knave. He could not endure that infamy. He bewailed the ruin he brought upon his son, whom he idolized, and, foreseeing his own end, begged his elder brother to be a father to Charles. He entreated that the boy be treated gently—not torn at once from his idle life—and be given means to venture in trade or the Indies. He confessed he had nothing to leave and asked his brother to obtain from Charles a renunciation of any claim to his estate as his inheritance. If the brother refused, remorse would be lasting; if he accepted, a voice would ever pray for him. The letter closed with a blessing and the dying man’s trust that his brother would accept guardianship.

“So you are talking?” said Pere Grandet as he folded the letter and put it into his waistcoat-pocket. He looked at his nephew with a humble, timid air, beneath which he hid his feelings and his calculations. “Have you warmed yourself?” he asked.

“Thoroughly, my dear uncle.”

“Well, where are the women?” said his uncle, already forgetting that his nephew was to sleep at the house. Eugenie and Madame Grandet returned.

“Is the room all ready?” asked Grandet, recovering composure.

“Yes, father.”

“Well then, my nephew, if you are tired, Nanon shall show you your room. It isn’t a dandy’s room; but you will excuse a poor wine-grower who never has a penny to spare. Taxes swallow up everything.”

“We do not wish to intrude, Grandet,” said the banker; “you may want to talk to your nephew, and therefore we will bid you good-night.”

At these words the assembly rose and made parting bows fitting their characters. The old notary fetched his lantern and offered to accompany the des Grassins. Madame des Grassins had not foreseen the incident that ended the evening prematurely; her servant had not arrived.

“Will you do me the honor to take my arm, madame?” said the abbe.

“Thank you, monsieur l’abbe, but I have my son,” she answered dryly.

“Ladies cannot compromise themselves with me,” said the abbe.

“Take Monsieur Cruchot’s arm,” said her husband.

The abbe walked off with the pretty lady so quickly they were soon ahead of the caravan.

“That is a good-looking young man, madame,” he said, pressing her arm. “Good-by to the grapes; the vintage is done. It is all over with us. We may as well say adieu to Mademoiselle Grandet. Eugenie will belong to the dandy. Unless this cousin is enamored of some Parisian woman, your son Adolphe will find another rival in—”

“Not at all, monsieur l’abbe. This young man cannot fail to see that Eugenie is a little fool,—a girl without the least freshness. Did you notice her to-night? She was as yellow as a quince.”

“Perhaps you made the cousin notice it?”

“I did not take the trouble—”

“Place yourself always beside Eugenie, madame, and you need never take the trouble to say anything to the young man against his cousin; he will make his own comparisons, which—”

“Well, he has promised to dine with me the day after to-morrow.”

“Ah! if you only would, madame—” said the abbe.

“What do you wish me to do, monsieur l’abbe? Do you mean to offer me bad advice? I have not reached thirty-nine, without a stain upon my reputation, thank God! to compromise myself now, even for the empire of the Great Mogul. You and I know the meaning of words. For an ecclesiastic, you have ideas that are very incongruous. Fie! it is worthy of Faublas!”

“You have read Faublas?”

“No, monsieur l’abbe; I meant the Liaisons dangereuses.”

“Ah! that book is infinitely more moral,” said the abbe, laughing. “But you make me out as wicked as a young man; I only meant—”

“Do you dare to tell me you were not thinking of putting wicked things into my head? Isn’t it clear? If this young man—who I admit is very good-looking—were to make love to me, he would not think of his cousin. In Paris, I know, good mothers devote themselves to their children’s happiness; but we live in the provinces, monsieur l’abbe.”

“Yes, madame.”

“And,” she continued, “I do not want, and Adolphe himself would not want, a hundred millions brought at such a price.”

“Madame, I said nothing about a hundred millions; that temptation might be too great for either of us to withstand. Only, I think an honest woman may permit herself, in all honor, certain harmless little coquetries which are part of her social duty and which—”

“Do you think so?”

“Are we not bound, madame, to make ourselves agreeable to each other? Permit me to blow my nose. I assure you, madame,” he resumed, “that the young gentleman ogled you through his glass in a more flattering manner than he looked at me; but I forgive him for doing homage to beauty in preference to old age—”

“It is quite apparent,” said the president loudly, “that Monsieur Grandet of Paris has sent his son to Saumur with matrimonial intentions.”

“But in that case the cousin wouldn’t have fallen among us like a cannon-ball,” answered the notary.

“That doesn’t prove anything,” said Monsieur des Grassins; “the old miser is always making mysteries.”

“Des Grassins, my friend, I have invited the young man to dinner. You must go and ask Monsieur and Madame de Larsonniere and the du Hautoys, with the beautiful demoiselle du Hautoy, of course. I hope she will be properly dressed; that jealous mother of hers does make such a fright of her! Gentlemen, I trust you will all do us the honor to come,” she added, stopping the procession to address the two Cruchots.

“Here you are at home, madame,” said the notary.

After bowing to the three des Grassins, the three Cruchots returned home, applying their provincial genius for analysis to study the great event of the evening, which undoubtedly changed the positions of Grassinists and Cruchotines. The admirable common-sense guiding these machinators made each side feel the necessity of a momentary alliance against a common enemy. Must they not mutually hinder Eugenie from loving her cousin, and the cousin from thinking of Eugenie? Could the Parisian resist the influence of treacherous insinuations, soft-spoken calumnies, slanders full of faint praise and artless denials, which should be made to circle incessantly about him and deceive him?

When the four relations were left alone, Monsieur Grandet said to his nephew,—

“We must go to bed. It is too late to talk about the matters which have
brought you here; tomorrow we will take a suitable moment. We breakfast
at eight; at midday we eat a little fruit or a bit of bread, and drink
a glass of white wine; and we dine, like the Parisians, at five. That’s
the order of the day. If you like to go and see the town and the
environs you are free to do so. You will excuse me if my occupations do
not permit me to accompany you. You may perhaps hear people say that I
am rich,—Monsieur Grandet this, Monsieur Grandet that. I let them talk;
their gossip does not hurt my credit. But I have not a penny; I work in
my old age like an apprentice whose worldly goods are a bad plane and two
good arms. Perhaps you’ll soon know yourself what a franc costs when you
have got to sweat for it. Nanon, where are the candles?”

“I trust, my nephew, that you will find all you want,” said Madame
Grandet; “but if you should need anything else, you can call Nanon.”

“My dear aunt, I shall need nothing; I have, I believe, brought
everything with me. Permit me to bid you good-night, and my young cousin
also.”

Charles took a lighted wax candle from Nanon’s hand—an Anjou candle,
yellow and so shopworn it looked like tallow; Monsieur Grandet, who
could not imagine such magnificence under his roof, did not notice.

“I will show you the way,” he said.

Instead of leaving by the door under the archway, Grandet went through
the passage dividing the hall from the kitchen. A swing-door with a
large oval pane shut this passage from the staircase to keep out the
cold. But the north wind whistled none the less; despite sand-bags at
the bottom of the living-room doors, the temperature within was poorly
kept. Nanon bolted the outer door; then she closed the hall and let loose
a wolf-dog whose strangled bark made it seem nearly voiceless. This
animal, noted for ferocity, recognized no one but Nanon; the two idle
servants understood each other.

When Charles saw the yellow, smoke-stained stairwell, each worm-eaten
step shaking under his uncle’s heavy foot-fall, his expectations
sobered. He fancied himself in a hen-roost. His aunt and cousin, used to
the staircase, took his inquiring look for friendliness and answered by
a smile that made him despair.

“Why the devil did my father send me to such a place?” he thought.

At the first landing he saw three doors painted Etruscan red and without
casings—doors sunk in dusty walls and provided with iron bolts each
ending with a flame pattern. The first door at the top of the
staircase, opening into a room directly above the kitchen, was evidently
walled up; the only entrance was through Grandet’s bedchamber. The
single window on the side of the court was protected by a lattice of
strong iron bars. No one, not even Madame Grandet, had permission to
enter it. The old man chose to be alone, like an alchemist in his
laboratory. There, no doubt, some hiding-place had been ingeniously
constructed; there the title-deeds were stored; there hung the scales to
weigh the louis; there, by night and secretly, were devised the
estimates, profits, receipts, so that business men, finding Grandet
prepared at all points, imagined he took his cue from fairies. No doubt,
while Nanon’s loud snoring shook the rafters, while the wolf-dog yawned
in the courtyard, while Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet slept, the old
cooper came to cuddle, caress, and clasp his gold. The walls were thick,
the screens sure. He alone had the key of this laboratory, where—so
people declared—he studied the maps of his fruit-trees, calculated his
profits to a vine and almost to a twig.

Eugenie’s chamber faced the walled-up entrance. At the other end of the
landing were the married couple’s apartments, occupying the whole front
of the house. Madame Grandet had a room next to Eugenie’s, entered
through a glass door. The master’s chamber was separated from his wife’s
by a partition, and from the mysterious strong-room by a thick wall.
Pere Grandet lodged his nephew on the second floor, in the high mansarde
attic above his own bedroom, so he might hear him if the young man took
to go and come. When Eugenie and her mother reached the middle of the
landing they kissed for good-night; then with a few words of adieu to
Charles, cold upon the lips but warm in the young girl’s heart, they
withdrew into their chambers.

“Here you are in your room, my nephew,” said Pere Grandet as he opened
the door. “If you need to go out, call Nanon; without her, beware! the
dog would eat you up without a word. Sleep well. Good-night. Ha! why,
they have made you a fire!” he cried.

At that moment Nanon appeared with the warming-pan.

“Here’s something more!” said Monsieur Grandet. “Do you take my nephew
for a lying-in woman? Carry off your brazier, Nanon!”

“But, monsieur, the sheets are damp, and this gentleman is as delicate
as a woman.”

“Well, go on, as you’ve taken it into your head,” said Grandet,
pushing her by the shoulders; “but don’t set things on fire.” So saying,
the miser went downstairs, grumbling.

Charles stood aghast among his trunks. After looking at the attic walls
covered with yellow paper sprinkled with bouquets, at the chilling
ribbed stone fireplace, the yellow-wood chairs with varnished cane
seats, the capacious open night-table, the meagre rag-carpet, and the
tester whose cloth valance shook as if devoured by moths, he turned to
la Grande Nanon and said,—

“Look here! my dear woman, just tell me, am I in the house of Monsieur
Grandet, formerly mayor of Saumur, and brother to Monsieur Grandet of
Paris?”

“Yes, monsieur; and a very good, a very kind, a very perfect gentleman.
Shall I help you to unpack your trunks?”

“Faith! yes, if you will, my old trooper. Didn’t you serve in the
marines of the Imperial Guard?”

“Ho, ho, ho!” laughed Nanon. “What’s that,—the marines of the guard? Is
it salt? Does it go in the water?”

“Here, get me my dressing-gown out of that valise; there’s the key.”

Nanon was wonder-struck by the sight of a green silk dressing-gown
brocaded with gold flowers.

“Are you going to put that on to go to bed with?” she asked.

“Yes.”

“Holy Virgin! what a beautiful altar-cloth it would make for the
parish church! My dear monsieur, give it to the church, and you’ll save
your soul; if you don’t, you’ll lose it. Oh, how nice you look in it! I
must call mademoiselle to see you.”

“Come, Nanon, if Nanon you are, hold your tongue; let me go to bed. I’ll
arrange my things tomorrow. If my dressing-gown pleases you so much,
you shall save your soul. I’m too good a Christian not to give it to
you when I go away, and you can do what you like with it.”

Nanon stood rooted, gazing at Charles and unable to put faith in his
words.

“Good night, Nanon.”

“What in the world have I come here for?” thought Charles as he went to
sleep. “My father is not a fool; my journey must have some object.
Pshaw! put off serious thought till the morrow, as some Greek idiot
said.”

“Blessed Virgin! how charming he is, my cousin!” Eugenie was saying,
interrupting her prayers, which that night at least were never finished.

Madame Grandet had no thoughts at all as she went to bed. She heard the
miser walking up and down his room through the door of communication in
the partition. Like all timid women, she had studied the character of
her lord. Just as the petrel foresees the storm, she knew by imperceptible
signs when an inward tempest shook her husband; and at such times, to
use an expression of her own, she “feigned dead.”

Grandet gazed at the door lined with sheet-iron which he had lately put
to his sanctum, and said to himself,—

“What a crazy idea of my brother to bequeath his son to me! A fine
legacy! I have not fifty francs to give him. What are fifty francs to a
dandy who looked at my barometer as if he meant to make firewood of it!”

In thinking over the consequences of that legacy of anguish Grandet was
perhaps more agitated than his brother had been at the moment of writing
it.

“I shall have that golden robe,” thought Nanon, who went to sleep tricked
out in her altar-cloth, dreaming for the first time in her life of
flowers, embroidery, and damask, just as Eugenie was dreaming of love.

In the pure and monotonous life of young girls there comes a delicious
hour when the sun sheds its rays into their soul, when the flowers
express their thoughts, when the throbbings of the heart send upward to
the brain their fertilizing warmth and melt all thoughts into a vague
desire—day of innocent melancholy and dulcet joys! When babes begin to
see, they smile; when a young girl first perceives the sentiment of
nature, she smiles as she smiled when an infant. If light is the first
love of life, is not love a light to the heart? The moment to see within
the veil of earthly things had come for Eugenie.

An early riser, like all provincial girls, she was up betimes and said
her prayers, and then began the business of dressing—a business which
henceforth was to have a meaning. First she brushed and smoothed her
chestnut hair and twisted its heavy masses to the top of her head,
preventing loose tresses from straying and giving her head a symmetry
which heightened the timid candor of her face. As she washed her hands
again and again in the cold water that reddened the skin, she looked at
her handsome round arms and asked herself what her cousin did to make
his hands so softly white, his nails so delicately curved. She put on
new stockings and her prettiest shoes. She laced her corset straight,
without skipping a single eyelet. And then, wishing for the first time
in her life to appear to advantage, she felt the joy of having a new
gown, well made, which rendered her attractive.

As she finished her toilet the clock of the parish church struck the
hour; to her surprise it was only seven. The desire for plenty of time
had led her to get up too early. Ignorant of the art of retouching every
curl and studying every effect, Eugenie simply crossed her arms, sat by
the window, and looked at the courtyard, the narrow garden, and the high
terraced walls that over-topped it: a dismal, hedged-in prospect, yet not
wholly devoid of the mysterious beauties of solitary nature. Near the
kitchen was a well with a pulley fastened to a bent iron rod clasped by
a vine whose leaves were withered and reddened by the season. The
tortuous shoots straggled to the wall and ran the house’s length, ending
near the wood-pile, where the logs were arranged with as much precision
as the books in a library. The pavement showed black stains produced by
lichens and the absence of movement. The thick walls wore a coating of
green moss streaked with brown lines, and the eight stone steps at the
bottom of the courtyard which led up to the garden gate were disjointed
and hidden beneath tall plants, like a tomb half buried. Above a
moss-grown foundation was a trellis of rotten wood, half fallen, over
which a mass of creepers clambered. On each side of the latticed gate
stretched the crooked arms of two stunted apple-trees. Three parallel
gravelled walks, separated by square beds edged with box, made the
garden, which ended beneath a terrace of old walls in a group of
lindens. At the farther end were raspberry-bushes; near the house an
immense walnut-tree drooped its branches almost into the miser’s sanctum
window.

A clear day and the autumnal sun common to the Loire began to melt the
hoar-frost that night had laid on these objects. Eugenie found a novel
charm in the aspect of things lately so insignificant to her. A thousand
confused thoughts came to birth in her mind and grew there, as the sun
grew along the wall. She felt that impulse of delight, vague and
inexplicable, which wraps the moral being as a cloud wraps the physical
body. Her thoughts matched the details of this strange landscape, and
the harmonies of her heart blended with those of nature. When the sun
reached an angle of the wall where the “Venus-hair” drooped its thick,
pigeon-breast colored leaves, rays of hope illumined the future to her
eyes, and thenceforth she loved to gaze upon that piece of wall with its
pale flowers and wilting herbage, mingling memories as tender as those
of childhood. The noise made by each falling leaf answered the secret
questionings of the young girl, who could have stayed there all day
without perceiving time. Then came tumultuous heavings of the soul. She
rose often, went to her glass, and looked at herself, as an author in
good faith looks at his work to criticize and blame it.

“I am not beautiful enough for him!” Such was Eugenie’s thought—a
humble thought, fertile in suffering. The poor girl did not do herself
justice; modesty, or rather fear, is among the first of love’s virtues.
Eugenie belonged to the type of sturdy children common among the lesser
bourgeoisie, whose beauties often seem a little vulgar; and yet, though
she resembled the Venus of Milo, the lines of her figure were ennobled
by the softer Christian sentiment which purifies womanhood and gives it
a distinction unknown to the sculptors of antiquity. She had an
enormous head, with the masculine yet delicate forehead of the Jupiter
of Phidias, and gray eyes filled with the light of her chaste life. The
features of her round face, once swollen by small-pox which had marred
the skin but left no other traces, were soft; her cheek was so delicate
that her mother’s kiss made a momentary red mark upon it. Her nose was
somewhat thick but harmonized with a vermilion mouth, whose lips, creased
in many lines, were full of love and kindness. The throat was exquisitely
round. The bust, well curved and carefully covered, attracted the eye
and inspired reverie. It lacked, perhaps, the grace which a fitting
dress can bestow; but to a connoisseur the non-flexible form had its
charm. Eugenie, tall and strongly made, had none of the prettiness that
pleases the masses; but she was beautiful with a beauty the spirit
recognizes, and none but artists truly love. A painter seeking a type of
Mary’s celestial purity, the proud modest eyes Raphael divined, the
virgin lines due to Christian life, would have found in Eugenie the
innate nobleness that is ignorant of itself; he would have seen beneath
the calm brow a world of love; he would have felt, in the eyes and
eyelids, the presence of the nameless something we call divine. Her
features, the contour of her head, which pleasure had never altered,
were like the lines of the distant horizon softly traced across tranquil
lakes. That calm, rosy countenance, margined with light, rested the mind,
held the eye, and imparted the charm of the conscience reflected there.
Eugenie stood on the shore of life where young illusions flower, where
daisies are gathered with delights soon to be unknown; and thus she
said, looking at her image in the glass, unconscious yet of love: “I am
too ugly; he will not notice me.”

Then she opened the door of her chamber which led to the staircase, and
stretched out her neck to listen for household noises. “He is not up,”
she thought, hearing Nanon’s morning cough as the good soul went and
came, sweeping the halls, lighting her fire, chaining the dog, and
speaking to the beasts in the stable. Eugenie went down and ran to
Nanon, who was milking the cow.

“Nanon, my good Nanon, make a little cream for my cousin’s breakfast.”

“Why, mademoiselle, you should have thought of that yesterday,” said
Nanon, bursting into a loud laugh. “I can’t make cream. Your cousin is a
darling, a darling! oh, that he is! You should have seen him in his
dressing-gown, all silk and gold! I saw him, I did! He wears linen as
fine as the surplice of monsieur le cure.”

“Nanon, please make us a galette.”

“And who’ll give me wood for the oven, and flour and butter for the
cakes?” said Nanon, who in her function of prime-minister to Grandet
assumed at times enormous importance in Eugenie’s and her mother’s eyes.
“Mustn’t rob the master to feast the cousin. You ask him for butter and
flour and wood: he’s your father, perhaps he’ll give you some. See! there
he is now, coming to give out the provisions.”

Eugenie escaped into the garden, quite frightened as she heard the
staircase shaking under her father’s step. Already she felt that virgin
modesty and special consciousness of happiness which make us fancy our
thoughts are written on our foreheads and open to all. Perceiving for
the first time the cold nakedness of her father’s house, the poor girl
felt a sort of rage that she could not put it in harmony with her
cousin’s elegance. She felt the need of doing something for him—what,
she did not know. Ingenuous and truthful, she followed her angelic
nature without mistrusting her impressions or her feelings. The mere
sight of her cousin had wakened natural yearnings of a woman—yearnings
likely to develop ardently because, at twenty-three, she was in the
plenitude of intelligence and desire. For the first time her heart was
full of terror at the sight of her father; in him she saw the master of
fate, and she fancied herself guilty of hiding certain thoughts. She
walked with hasty steps, surprised to breathe purer air, to feel the
sun’s rays quickening her pulses, to absorb a moral warmth and new life.
As she planned some stratagem to get the cake, a quarrel—an event as
rare as the sight of swallows in winter—broke out between la Grande
Nanon and Grandet. Armed with his keys, the master had come to dole out
provisions for the day.

“Is there any bread left from yesterday?” he said.

“Not a crumb, monsieur.”

Grandet took a large round loaf, well floured and moulded in one of the
flat baskets used in Anjou, and was about to cut it, when Nanon said,—

“We are five, to-day, monsieur.”

“That’s true,” said Grandet, “but your loaves weigh six pounds; there’ll
be some left. Besides, these young fellows from Paris don’t eat bread,
you’ll see.”

“Then they must eat frippe?” said Nanon.

“No,” answered Grandet, “they eat neither bread nor frippe; they are
something like marriageable girls.”

After ordering the meals for the day with his usual parsimony, the
goodman, having locked the closets containing the supplies, was about to
go to the fruit-garden, when Nanon stopped him to say,—

“Monsieur, give me a little flour and some butter, and I’ll make a
galette for the young ones.”

“Are you going to pillage the house on account of my nephew?”

“I wasn’t thinking more of your nephew than I was of your dog—not more
than you think yourself; for, look here, you’ve only forked out six
bits of sugar. I want eight.”

“What’s all this, Nanon? I have never seen you like this. What have you
got in your head? Are you the mistress here? You sha’n’t have more than
six pieces of sugar.”

“Well, then, how is your nephew to sweeten his coffee?”

“With two pieces; I’ll go without myself.”

“Go without sugar at your age! I’d rather buy you some out of my own
pocket.”

“Mind your own business.”

In spite of the recent fall in prices, sugar was still in Grandet’s eyes
the most valuable of colonial products; to him it was always six francs
a pound. The necessity of economizing it, acquired under the Empire,
had grown to be the most inveterate of his habits. All women know how to
dodge to get their ends; Nanon abandoned the sugar for the sake of
getting the galette.

“Mademoiselle!” she called through the window, “do you want some
galette?”

“No, no,” answered Eugenie.

“Come, Nanon,” said Grandet, hearing his daughter’s voice. “See here.”
 He opened the cupboard where the flour was kept, gave her a cupful, and
added a few ounces of butter to the piece he had already cut off.

“I shall want wood for the oven,” said the implacable Nanon.

“Well, take what you want,” he answered sadly; “but in that case you
must make us a fruit-tart, and you’ll cook the whole dinner in the
oven. In that way you won’t need two fires.”

“Goodness!” cried Nanon, “you needn’t tell me that.”

Grandet cast a look that was well-nigh paternal upon his faithful
deputy.

“Mademoiselle,” she cried, when his back was turned, “we shall have the
galette.”

Pere Grandet returned from the garden with the fruit and arranged a
plateful on the kitchen-table.

“Just see, monsieur,” said Nanon, “what pretty boots your nephew has.
What leather! why it smells good! What does he clean it with, I wonder?
Am I to put your egg-polish on it?”

“Nanon, I think eggs would injure that kind of leather. Tell him you
don’t know how to black morocco; yes, that’s morocco. He will get you
something himself in Saumur to polish those boots with. I have heard
that they put sugar into the blacking to make it shine.”

“They look good to eat,” said the cook, putting the boots to her nose.
“Bless me! if they don’t smell like madame’s eau-de-cologne. Ah! how
funny!”

“Funny!” said her master. “Do you call it funny to put more money into
boots than the man who stands in them is worth?”

“Monsieur,” she said, when Grandet returned the second time, after
locking the fruit-garden, “won’t you have the pot-au-feu put on once
or twice a week on account of your nephew?”

“Yes.”

“Am I to go to the butcher’s?”

“Certainly not. We will make the broth of fowls; the farmers will bring
them. I shall tell Cornoiller to shoot some crows; they make the best
soup in the world.”

“Isn’t it true, monsieur, that crows eat the dead?”

“You are a fool, Nanon. They eat what they can get, like the rest of the
world. Don’t we all live on the dead? What are legacies?”

Monsieur Grandet, having no further orders to give, drew out his watch,
and seeing that he had half an hour to dispose of before breakfast, he
took his hat, went and kissed his daughter, and said to her:

“Do you want to come for a walk in the fields, down by the Loire? I have
something to do there.”

Eugenie fetched her straw bonnet, lined with pink taffeta; then the
father and daughter went down the winding street to the shore.

“Where are you going at this early hour?” said Cruchot, the notary,
meeting them.

“To see something,” answered Grandet, not duped by the matutinal
appearance of his friend.

When Pere Grandet went to “see something,” the notary knew by
experience there was something to be got by going with him; so he went.

“Come, Cruchot,” said Grandet, “you are one of my friends. I’ll show you
what folly it is to plant poplar-trees on good ground.”

“Do you call the sixty thousand francs that you pocketed for those that
were in your fields down by the Loire, folly?” said Maitre Cruchot,
opening his eyes. “What luck you have had! To cut down your trees at the
very time they ran short of white-wood at Nantes, and to sell them at
thirty francs!”

Eugenie listened, without knowing that she approached the most solemn
moment of her whole life, and that the notary was about to bring down
upon her head a paternal and supreme sentence. Grandet had now reached
the magnificent fields he owned on the banks of the Loire, where thirty
workmen were employed in clearing, filling, and levelling the spots
formerly occupied by poplars.

“Maitre Cruchot, see how much ground this tree once took up! Jean,” he
cried to a laborer, “m-m-measure with your r-r-rule, b-both ways.”

“Four times eight feet,” said the man.

“Thirty-two feet lost,” said Grandet. “I had three hundred poplars in
this one line, isn’t that so? Well, then, three h-h-hundred times
thir-thirty-two lost m-m-me five hundred in h-h-hay; add twice as much
for the side rows—fifteen hundred; the middle rows as much more. So we
may c-c-call it a th-thousand b-b-bales of h-h-hay—”

“Very good,” said Cruchot to help out his friend; “a thousand bales are
worth about six hundred francs.”

“Say t-t-twelve hundred, be-c-cause there’s three or four hundred francs
on the second crop. Well, then, c-c-calculate that t-twelve thousand
francs a year for f-f-forty years with interest c-c-comes to—”

“Say sixty thousand francs,” said the notary.

“I am willing; c-c-comes t-t-to sixty th-th-thousand. Very good,”
continued Grandet, without stuttering: “two thousand poplars forty years
old will only yield me fifty thousand francs. There’s a loss. I have
found that myself,” said Grandet, getting on his high horse. “Jean, fill
up all the holes except those at the bank of the river; there you are
to plant the poplars I have bought. Plant ’em there, and they’ll get
nourishment from the government,” he said, turning to Cruchot and giving
a slight motion to the wen on his nose, which expressed more than the
most ironical of smiles.

“True enough; poplars should only be planted on poor soil,” said
Cruchot, amazed at Grandet’s calculations.

“Y-y-yes, monsieur,” answered the old man satirically.

Eugenie, gazing at the sublime scenery of the Loire and paying little
attention to her father’s reckonings, presently turned an ear when she
heard Cruchot say,—

“So you have brought a son-in-law from Paris. All Saumur is talking
about your nephew. I shall soon have the marriage-contract to draw up,
hey! Pere Grandet?”

“You g-g-got up very early to t-t-tell me that,” said Grandet,
accompanying the remark with a motion of his wen. “Well, old
c-c-comrade, I’ll be frank, and t-t-tell you what you want t-t-to know.
I would rather, do you see, f-f-fling my daughter into the Loire than
g-g-give her to her c-c-cousin. You may t-t-tell that everywhere,—no,
never mind; let the world t-t-talk.”

This answer dazzled and blinded the young girl with sudden light. The
distant hopes upspringing in her heart bloomed suddenly, became real,
tangible, like a cluster of flowers, and she saw them cut down and
wilting on the earth. Since the previous evening she had attached
herself to Charles by those links of happiness which bind soul to soul;
from henceforth suffering was to rivet them. Is it not the noble destiny
of women to be more moved by the dark solemnities of grief than by the
splendors of fortune? How was it that fatherly feeling had died out of
her father’s heart? Of what crime had Charles been guilty? Mysterious
questions! Already her dawning love, a mystery so profound, was wrapping
itself in mystery. She walked back trembling in all her limbs; and when
she reached the gloomy street, lately so joyous to her, she felt its
sadness, she breathed the melancholy time and events had printed there.
A few steps from their own door she went on before her father and
waited at the threshold. But Grandet, who saw a newspaper in the notary’s
hand, stopped short and asked,—

“How are the Funds?”

“You never listen to my advice, Grandet,” answered Cruchot. “Buy soon;
you will still make twenty per cent in two years, besides getting an
excellent rate of interest—five thousand a year for eighty thousand
francs fifty centimes.”

“We’ll see about that,” answered Grandet, rubbing his chin.

“Good God!” exclaimed the notary.

“Well, what?” cried Grandet; and at the same moment Cruchot put the
newspaper under his eyes and said:

“Read that!”

  “Monsieur Grandet, one of the most respected merchants in Paris,
  blew his brains out yesterday, after making his usual appearance
  at the Bourse. He had sent his resignation to the president of the
  Chamber of Deputies, and had also resigned his functions as a
  judge of the commercial courts. The failures of Monsieur Roguin
  and Monsieur Souchet, his broker and his notary, had ruined him.
  The esteem felt for Monsieur Grandet and the credit he enjoyed
  were nevertheless such that he might have obtained the necessary
  assistance from other business houses. It is much to be regretted
  that so honorable a man should have yielded to momentary despair,”
   etc.

“I knew it,” said the old wine-grower to the notary.

The words sent a chill through Maitre Cruchot, who, despite his
impassibility, felt cold run down his spine as he thought Grandet of
Paris had possibly implored in vain the millions of Grandet of Saumur.

“And his son, so joyous yesterday—”

“He knows nothing as yet,” answered Grandet, with the same composure.

“Adieu! Monsieur Grandet,” said Cruchot, who now understood the state of
the case, and went off to reassure Monsieur de Bonfons.

On entering, Grandet found breakfast ready. Madame Grandet, round whose
neck Eugenie had flung her arms, kissing her with the quick effusion of
feeling often caused by secret grief, was already seated in her chair on
castors, knitting sleeves for the coming winter.

“You can begin to eat,” said Nanon, coming downstairs four steps at a
time; “the young one is sleeping like a cherub. Isn’t he a darling with
his eyes shut? I went in and I called him: no answer.”

“Let him sleep,” said Grandet; “he’ll wake soon enough to hear
ill-tidings.”

“What is it?” asked Eugenie, putting into her coffee the two little bits
of sugar weighing less than half an ounce which the old miser amused
himself by cutting up in his leisure hours. Madame Grandet, who did not
dare to ask, gazed at her husband.

“His father has blown his brains out.”

“My uncle?” said Eugenie.

“Poor young man!” exclaimed Madame Grandet.

“Poor indeed!” said Grandet; “he isn’t worth a sou!”

“Eh! poor boy, and he’s sleeping like the king of the world!” said Nanon
in a gentle voice.

Eugenie stopped eating. Her heart was wrung, as the young heart is when
pity for one she loves overflows, for the first time, the whole being of
a woman. The poor girl wept.

“What are you crying about? You didn’t know your uncle,” said her
father, giving her one of those hungry, tigerish looks he doubtless threw
upon his piles of gold.

“But, monsieur,” said Nanon, “who wouldn’t feel pity for the poor young
man, sleeping there like a wooden shoe, without knowing what’s coming?”

“I didn’t speak to you, Nanon. Hold your tongue!”

Eugenie learned at that moment that the woman who loves must be able to
hide her feelings. She did not answer.

“You will say nothing to him about it, Ma’ame Grandet, till I return,”
 said the old man. “I have to go and straighten the line of my hedge
along the high-road. I shall be back at noon, in time for the second
breakfast, and then I will talk with my nephew about his affairs. As
for you, Mademoiselle Eugenie, if it is for that dandy you are crying,
that’s enough, child. He’s going off like a shot to the Indies. You will
never see him again.”

The father put on his gloves, pushed them in place by shoving the fingers
of both hands together, and went out.

“Mamma, I am suffocating!” cried Eugenie when she was alone with her
mother; “I have never suffered like this.”

Madame Grandet, seeing her turn pale, opened the window and let her
breathe fresh air.

“I feel better!” said Eugenie after a moment.

This nervous excitation in a nature hitherto, to all appearance, calm
and cold, reacted on Madame Grandet; she looked at her daughter with the
sympathetic intuition mothers have for the objects of their tenderness,
and guessed all. Their life was intimate as the Hungarian sisters’: always
together in the window embrasure, sleeping in the same atmosphere.

“My poor child!” said Madame Grandet, taking Eugenie’s head and laying
it upon her bosom.

At these words the young girl raised her head, questioned her mother by
a look, and seemed to search out her inmost thought.

“Why send him to the Indies?” she said. “If he is unhappy, ought he not
to stay with us? Is he not our nearest relation?”

“Yes, my child, it seems natural; but your father has his reasons: we
must respect them.”

The mother and daughter sat in silence; the former upon her raised seat,
the latter in her little armchair, and both took up their work.
Swelling with gratitude for her mother’s understanding, Eugenie kissed
the dear hand, saying,—

“How good you are, my kind mamma!”

The words sent a glow into the motherly face, worn and blighted by many
sorrows.

“You like him?” asked Eugenie.

Madame Grandet only smiled. Then, after a moment’s silence, she said in a
low voice: “Do you love him already? That is wrong.”

“Wrong?” said Eugenie. “Why is it wrong? You are pleased with him, Nanon
is pleased with him; why should he not please me? Come, mamma, let us
set the table for his breakfast.”

She threw down her work, and her mother did the same, saying, “Foolish
child!” But she sanctioned the folly by sharing it. Eugenie called Nanon.

“What do you want now, mademoiselle?”

“Nanon, can we have cream by midday?”

“Ah! midday, to be sure you can,” answered the old servant.

“Well, let him have his coffee very strong; I heard Monsieur des
Grassins say that they make the coffee very strong in Paris. Put in a
great deal.”

“Where am I to get it?”

“Buy some.”

“Suppose monsieur meets me?”

“He has gone to his fields.”

“I’ll run, then. But Monsieur Fessard asked me yesterday if the Magi
had come to stay with us when I bought the wax candle. All the town will
know our goings-on.”

“If your father finds it out,” said Madame Grandet, “he is capable of
beating us.”

“Well, let him beat us; we will take his blows on our knees.”

Madame Grandet raised her eyes to heaven. Nanon put on her hood and went
off. Eugenie got out some clean table-linen, and went to fetch a few
bunches of grapes she had hung on a string across the attic; she walked
softly along the corridor so as not to waken her cousin, and could not
help listening at the door to his quiet breathing.

“Sorrow is watching while he sleeps,” she thought.

She took the freshest vine-leaves and arranged her dish of grapes as
coquettishly as a practised housekeeper might, and placed it triumphantly
on the table. She laid hands on the pears counted out by her father, and
piled them in a pyramid mixed with leaves. She went and came, and skipped
and ran. She would have liked to lay under contribution everything in
her father’s house; but the keys were in his pocket. Nanon came back
with two fresh eggs. At sight of them Eugenie almost hugged her round
the neck.

“The farmer from Lande had them in his basket. I asked him for them,
and he gave them to me, the darling, for nothing, as an attention!”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After two hours of care, during which Eugenie rose twenty times to see if the coffee boiled or to listen to her cousin dressing, she prepared a simple breakfast—cheap, but breaking household custom. Midday breakfast was always standing: a slice of bread, a little fruit or some butter, and a glass of wine. As Eugenie looked at the table by the fire—an arm-chair before her cousin’s plate, two dishes of fruit, an egg-cup, a bottle of white wine, bread, and sugar piled in a saucer—she trembled at the thought of her father’s look if he should come in. She glanced often at the clock to see if Charles could eat before the master’s return.

“Don’t be troubled, Eugenie; if your father comes in, I will take it all upon myself,” said Madame Grandet.

Eugenie could not repress a tear.

“Oh, my good mother!” she cried, “I have never loved you enough.”

Charles, who had been pacing his room singing, came down at eleven. A true Parisian, he had put as much dandyism into his dress as if at a noble lady’s château and entered with the smiling, courteous manner of youth, which made Eugenie’s heart beat with mournful joy. He treated the destruction of his castles in Anjou as a joke and approached his aunt gaily.

“Have you slept well, dear aunt? and you, too, my cousin?”

“Very well, monsieur; did you?” said Madame Grandet.

“I? perfectly.”

“You must be hungry, cousin,” said Eugenie; “will you take your seat?”

“I never breakfast before midday; I never get up till then. However, I fared so badly on the journey that I am glad to eat something at once. Besides—” here he pulled out a pretty Breguet watch. “Dear me! I am early; it is only eleven o’clock!”

“Early?” said Madame Grandet.

“Yes; but I wanted to put my things in order. Well, I shall be glad to have anything to eat—anything, it doesn’t matter what, a chicken, a partridge.”

“Holy Virgin!” exclaimed Nanon, overhearing.

“A partridge!” thought Eugenie; she would gladly have given her little hoard for one.

“Come and sit down,” said his aunt.

The young dandy let himself drop into an easy-chair with the grace of a pretty woman on a sofa. Eugenie and her mother took plain chairs near the fire.

“Do you always live here?” asked Charles, finding the room uglier by daylight.

“Always,” Eugenie answered, “except during the vintage. Then we help Nanon, and live at the Abbaye des Noyers.”

“Don’t you ever take walks?”

“Sometimes on Sunday after vespers, when the weather is fine,” said Madame Grandet, “we walk on the bridge, or we go and watch the haymakers.”

“Have you a theatre?”

“Go to the theatre!” exclaimed Madame Grandet. “Why, monsieur, don’t you know it is a mortal sin?”

“See here, monsieur,” said Nanon, bringing in the eggs, “here are your chickens—in the shell.”

“Oh! fresh eggs,” said Charles, who, used to luxury, had already forgotten his partridge; “that is delicious: now, if you will give me the butter, my good girl.”

“Butter! then you can’t have the galette.”

“Nanon, bring the butter,” cried Eugenie.

Eugenie watched her cousin cut his sippets with the pleasure of a grisette at a melodrama where innocence triumphs. Brought up by a charming mother and trained by a woman of fashion, Charles had the elegant, dainty movements of a coxcomb. The compassionate sympathy and tenderness of the two women flowed toward him; he melted under their attention and gave Eugenie a bright, caressing look full of kindness, which seemed itself a smile. His eyes lingered on the harmony of her features, the grace of her attitude, the clear eyes where young love sparkled without knowing it.

“Ah! my dear cousin, if you were in full dress at the Opera, I assure you my aunt’s words would come true—you would make men commit the mortal sin of envy, and women the sin of jealousy.”

The compliment struck Eugenie’s heart, though she half understood it.

“Oh! cousin,” she said, “you are laughing at a poor little country girl.”

“If you knew me, my cousin, you would know I abhor ridicule; it withers the heart and jars upon all my feelings.” Here he swallowed his buttered sippet very gracefully. “No, I really have not enough mind to make fun of others; and doubtless it is a great defect. In Paris, when they want to disparage a man, they say: ‘He has a good heart.’ The phrase means: ‘The poor fellow is as stupid as a rhinoceros.’ But as I am rich, and known to hit the bull’s-eye at thirty paces with any kind of pistol, and even in the open fields, ridicule respects me.”

“My dear nephew, that bespeaks a good heart,” said Madame Grandet.

“You have a very pretty ring,” said Eugenie; “is there any harm in asking to see it?”

Charles loosened the ring and held out his hand; Eugenie blushed as she touched his pink nails with her fingertips.

“See, mamma, what beautiful workmanship.”

“My! there’s a lot of gold!” said Nanon, bringing in the coffee.

“What is that?” exclaimed Charles, laughing, as he pointed to an oblong brown earthenware pot, glazed inside, fringed with ashes, from which coffee-grounds bubbled up in the boiling liquid.

“It is boiled coffee,” said Nanon.

“Ah! my dear aunt, I shall at least leave one beneficent trace of my visit here. You are indeed behind the age! I must teach you to make good coffee in a Chaptal coffee-pot.”

He tried to explain the Chaptal process.

“Gracious! if there are so many things as all that to do,” said Nanon, “we may as well give up our lives to it. I shall never make coffee that way; I know that! Pray, who is to get the fodder for the cow while I make the coffee?”

“I will make it,” said Eugenie.

“Child!” said Madame Grandet, looking at her daughter.

The word recalled the sorrow that would soon fall upon the young man; the three women fell silent, looking at him with commiseration that caught his attention.

“Is anything the matter, my cousin?” he said.

“Hush!” Madame Grandet checked Eugenie, who was about to answer; “you know, my daughter, that your father charged us not to speak to monsieur—”

“Say Charles,” said young Grandet.

“Ah! you are called Charles? What a beautiful name!” cried Eugenie.

Presentiments of evil are almost always justified. At this moment Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie—each thinking with a shudder of the old man’s return—heard the knock whose echoes they knew too well.

“There’s papa!” said Eugenie.

She removed the saucer of sugar, leaving a few pieces on the tablecloth; Nanon carried off the egg-cup; Madame Grandet sat up like a frightened hare. Charles, wholly unable to understand, was amazed.

“Why! what is the matter?” he asked.

“My father has come,” answered Eugenie.

“Well, what of that?”

Monsieur Grandet entered, threw his keen eye upon the table and on Charles, and saw the whole thing.

“Ha! ha! so you have been making a feast for your nephew; very good, very good!” he said without stuttering. “When the cat’s away, the mice will play.”

“Feast!” thought Charles, ignorant of the house rules.

“Give me my glass, Nanon,” said the master.

Eugenie brought the glass. Grandet drew a horn-handled knife with a big blade from his breeches’ pocket, cut a slice of bread, took a small bit of butter, spread it carefully, and ate standing. Charles was sweetening his coffee. Père Grandet saw the bits of sugar, looked at his wife, who turned pale, made three steps forward, leaned to her ear, and asked,—

“Where did you get all that sugar?”

“Nanon fetched it from Fessard’s; there was none.”

It is impossible to picture the intense interest the three women took in this mute scene. Nanon had left her kitchen and stood looking into the room. Charles, having tasted his coffee and found it bitter, glanced for the sugar, which Grandet had already put away.

“What do you want?” said his uncle.

“The sugar.”

“Put in more milk,” answered the master; “your coffee will taste sweeter.”

Eugenie took the saucer Grandet had put away and placed it on the table, looking calmly at her father. The courage of that small act was as proud as any lover’s reward in other tales. Charles never guessed the cruel agitation in his cousin’s heart under the miser’s look.

“You are not eating your breakfast, wife.”

The poor helot came forward, cut herself a piece of bread, and took a pear. Eugenie boldly offered her father some grapes.

“Taste my preserves, papa. My cousin, you will eat some, will you not? I went to get these pretty grapes expressly for you.”

“If no one stops them, they will pillage Saumur for you, nephew. When you have finished, we will go into the garden; I have something to tell you which can’t be sweetened.”

Eugenie and her mother cast a look at Charles that he could not mistake.

“What is it you mean, uncle? Since the death of my poor mother”—his voice softened—“no other sorrow can touch me.”

“My nephew, who knows by what afflictions God is pleased to try us?” said his aunt.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta,” said Grandet. “There’s your nonsense beginning. I am sorry to see those white hands of yours, nephew”—and he showed his own shoulder-of-mutton fists—“there’s a pair of hands made to pick up silver pieces. You’ve been brought up to put your feet in the kid out of which we make the purses we keep our money in. A bad look-out! Very bad!”

“What do you mean, uncle? I’ll be hanged if I understand a single word of what you are saying.”

“Come!” said Grandet.

The miser closed his knife, drank the last of his wine, and opened the door.

“My cousin, take courage!”

The young girl’s tone struck terror into Charles, who followed his uncle, a prey to disquieting thoughts. Eugenie, her mother, and Nanon went into the kitchen, driven by curiosity to watch the scene about to take place in the garden, where at first the uncle walked silently ahead. Grandet found little difficulty in telling Charles of his father’s death; what he struggled with was compassion for a penniless nephew and finding words to soften the cruel truth. “You have lost your father” seemed slight; “you are absolutely without means” summed up all misfortunes. Grandet walked round the garden three times, the gravel crunching under his heavy step.

In crucial moments our minds fasten on the locality where sorrow overwhelms us. Charles noticed the box-borders, the yellow leaves fluttering down, the dilapidated walls, and the gnarled fruit-trees—images destined to remain with the memory of that solemn hour.

“It is very fine weather, very warm,” said Grandet, drawing a long breath.

“Yes, uncle; but why—”

“Well, my lad,” answered his uncle, “I have some bad news to give you. Your father is ill—”

“Then why am I here?” said Charles. “Nanon,” he cried, “order post-horses! I can get a carriage somewhere?” he added, turning to his uncle.

“Horses and carriages are useless,” answered Grandet, looking at Charles, who stood silent, eyes fixed. “Yes, my poor boy, you guess the truth—he is dead. But that’s nothing; there is something worse: he blew out his brains.”

“My father!”

“Yes, but that’s not the worst; the newspapers are all talking about it. Here, read that.”

Grandet, who had borrowed the fatal article from Cruchot, thrust the paper under his nephew’s eyes. The young man, still a child emotionally, burst into tears.

“That’s good!” thought Grandet; “his eyes frightened me. He’ll be all right if he weeps. That is not the worst, my poor nephew,” he added aloud, not noticing if Charles heard, “that is nothing; you will get over it: but—”

“Never, never! My father! Oh, my father!”

“He has ruined you, you haven’t a penny.”

“What does that matter? My father! Where is my father?”

His sobs resounded horribly against the dreary walls and through the echoes. The three women, filled with pity, wept too; tears are contagious. Charles, not listening further, ran through the court and up to his chamber, threw himself on the bed and hid his face in the sheets to weep in peace.

“The first burst must have its way,” said Grandet, returning to the living-room where Eugenie and her mother had resumed their seats and were sewing with trembling hands after wiping their eyes. “But that young man is good for nothing; his head is more taken up with the dead than with his money.”

Eugenie shuddered at her father’s comment on the most sacred grief. From then on she began to judge him. Charles’s muffled sobs still sounded through the house; his deep groans, seeming to come from the earth, ceased only toward evening, growing feebler.

“Poor young man!” said Madame Grandet.

Fatal exclamation! Père Grandet looked at his wife, at Eugenie, and at the sugar-bowl. He recollected the extraordinary breakfast prepared for the unfortunate youth, and took his position in the middle of the room.

“Listen to me,” he said, with his usual composure. “I hope you will not continue this extravagance, Madame Grandet. I don’t give you MY money to stuff that young fellow with sugar.”

“My mother had nothing to do with it,” said Eugenie; “it was I who—”

“Is it because you are of age,” said Grandet, interrupting her, “that you choose to contradict me? Remember, Eugenie—”

“Father, the son of your brother ought to receive from us—”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” exclaimed the cooper on four chromatic tones; “the son of my brother this, my nephew that! Charles is nothing at all to us; he hasn’t a farthing, his father has failed; and when this dandy has cried his fill, off he goes from here. I won’t have him revolutionize my household.”

“What is ‘failing,’ father?” asked Eugenie.

“To fail,” answered her father, “is to commit the most dishonorable action that can disgrace a man.”

“It must be a great sin,” said Madame Grandet, “and our brother may be damned.”

“There, there, don’t begin with your litanies!” said Grandet, shrugging. “To fail, Eugenie,” he resumed, “is to commit a theft which the law, unfortunately, takes under its protection. People entrusted their property to Guillaume Grandet trusting his honor; he has made away with it all, and left them nothing but their eyes to weep with. A highway robber is better than a bankrupt: the one attacks you and you can defend yourself, he risks his life; but the other—well, in short, Charles is dishonored.”

The words weighed heavily on the poor girl. Upright and delicate, ignorant of worldly sophisms, she believed her father’s atrocious explanation, unable to distinguish involuntary failure from intentional fraud.

“Father, could you not have prevented such a misfortune?”

“My brother did not consult me. Besides, he owes four millions.”

“What is a ‘million,’ father?” she asked, with childlike simplicity.

“A million? why, it is a million pieces of twenty sous each, and it takes five twenty sous pieces to make five francs.”

“Dear me!” cried Eugenie, “how could my uncle possibly have had four millions? Is there any one else in France who ever had so many millions?” Père Grandet stroked his chin, smiled, and his wen seemed to dilate. “But what will become of my cousin Charles?”

“He is going off to the West Indies by his father’s request, and he will try to make his fortune there.”

“Has he got the money to go with?”

“I shall pay for his journey as far as—yes, as far as Nantes.”

Eugenie sprang into his arms.

“Oh, father, how good you are!”

She kissed him with warmth that almost made Grandet ashamed; his conscience galled him a little.

“Will it take much time to amass a million?” she asked.

“Look here!” said the old miser, “you know what a napoleon is? Well, it takes fifty thousand napoleons to make a million.”

“Mamma, we must say a great many neuvaines for him.”

“I was thinking so,” said Madame Grandet.

“That’s the way, always spending my money!” cried the father. “Do you think there are francs on every bush?”

At that moment a muffled cry more distressing than the others echoed through the garrets and struck chill into Eugenie’s and her mother’s hearts.

“Nanon, go upstairs and see that he does not kill himself,” said Grandet. “Now, then,” he added, looking at his wife and daughter, who had turned pale at his words, “no nonsense, you two! I must leave you; I have got to see about the Dutchmen who are going away today. And then I must find Cruchot, and talk with him about all this.”

He departed. As soon as he had shut the door, Eugenie and her mother breathed more freely. Until that morning the young girl had never felt constrained in her father’s presence; but in these hours her feelings and ideas began to change.

“Mamma, how many louis are there in a cask of wine?”

“Your father sells his from a hundred to a hundred and fifty francs, sometimes two hundred—at least, so I’ve heard.”

“Then papa must be rich?”

“Perhaps he is. But Monsieur Cruchot told me he bought Froidfond two years ago; that may have pinched him.”

Eugenie, unable to understand her father’s fortune, stopped her calculations.

“He didn’t even see me, the darling!” said Nanon returning. “He’s stretched out like a calf on his bed and crying like the Madeleine, and that’s a blessing! What’s the matter with the poor dear young man!”

“Let us go and console him, mamma; if anyone knocks, we can come down.”

Madame Grandet yielded to the sweet persuasion of her daughter’s voice. Eugenie was sublime: she had become a woman. They went up to Charles’s room. The door was open. The young man saw and heard nothing; plunged in grief, he uttered inarticulate cries.

“How he loves his father!” said Eugenie in a low voice.

In that utterance it was impossible to mistake the hopes of a heart that, unknown to itself, had suddenly become passionate. Madame Grandet cast a mother’s look upon her daughter and whispered,—

“Take care, you will love him!”

“Love him!” answered Eugenie. “Ah! if you did but know what my father said to Monsieur Cruchot.”

Charles turned over and saw his aunt and cousin.

“I have lost my father, my poor father! If he had told me his secret troubles we might have worked together to repair them. My God! my poor father! I was so sure I should see him again that I think I kissed him quite coldly—”

Sobs cut off his words.

“We will pray for him,” said Madame Grandet. “Resign yourself to the will of God.”

“Cousin,” said Eugenie, “take courage! Your loss is irreparable; therefore think only of saving your honor.”

With the delicate instinct of a woman who puts her mind into all things, even while offering consolation, Eugenie sought to turn his thoughts inward.

“My honor?” exclaimed Charles, tossing aside his hair and sitting up, crossing his arms. “Ah! that is true. My uncle said my father had failed.” He uttered a heart-rending cry and hid his face in his hands. “Leave me, leave me, cousin! My God! my God! forgive my father, for he must have suffered sorely!”

There was something terribly attractive in this young sorrow, sincere without reasoning. It was a virgin grief that the simple hearts of Eugenie and her mother could comprehend, and they obeyed his sign to leave him. They went downstairs in silence and sat by the window sewing for nearly an hour without exchanging a word. Eugenie had seen, in a furtive glance around his room—the pretties of his dressing-case, the scissors, the razors embossed with gold—a gleam of luxury across his grief that made him more interesting by contrast. Never before had such a serious event touched the imaginations of these two passive beings, hitherto sunk in the stillness of solitude.

“Mamma,” said Eugenie, “we must wear mourning for my uncle.”

“Your father will decide that,” answered Madame Grandet.

They relapsed into silence. Eugenie drew her stitches with a uniform motion that revealed the teeming thoughts of her meditation. The first desire of her heart was to share her cousin’s mourning.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
About four o’clock an abrupt knock at the door struck sharply on Madame Grandet’s heart.

“What can have happened to your father?” she said to her daughter.

Grandet entered joyously. After taking off his gloves he rubbed his hands hard; his tanned skin saved them from injury. Presently his secret escaped him.

“Wife,” he said without stuttering, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I walked about the market-place before their inn, pretending to do nothing. That Belgian fellow—you know who I mean—came up to me. The owners of the good vineyards have kept back their vintages, intending to wait; well, I didn’t hinder them. The Belgian was in despair; in a minute the bargain was made. He takes my vintage at two hundred francs the puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; the notes are drawn. Here are six louis for you. In three months wines will have fallen.”

These words, quietly uttered and bitterly sarcastic, would have chilled Saumur’s market-place had its inhabitants heard them: their panic would have destroyed prices.

“Did you have a thousand puncheons this year, father?”

“Yes, little one.”

That term for his daughter was the superlative expression of the old miser’s joy.

“Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?”

“Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s rage, cold and concentrated, was fiercer than any spectacle of Babylon’s doom. He had forgotten his nephew until his daughter put him into the account.

“What’s this? Ever since that dandy set foot in my house everything goes wrong! You behave as if you had a right to buy sugar-plums and make feasts and weddings. I won’t have that sort of thing. I hope I know my duty at my time of life! I certainly sha’n’t take lessons from my daughter, or from anybody else. I shall do for my nephew what is proper, and you have no need to poke your nose into it. As for you, Eugenie,” he added, facing her, “don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon, see if I don’t; and no later than to-morrow either, if you disobey me! Where is that fellow, has he come down yet?”

“No, my friend,” answered Madame Grandet.

“What is he doing then?”

“He is weeping for his father,” said Eugenie.

Grandet looked at his daughter without finding a word; after all, he was a father. He made a couple of turns up and down the room, then hurried away to his secret den to think over an investment he was meditating in the public Funds. The thinning of his two thousand acres of forest had yielded six hundred thousand francs; adding sales of poplars and other gains he had nine hundred thousand francs, not counting the two hundred thousand from the sale just concluded. The twenty percent Cruchot promised in the Funds tempted him. He figured on the margin of the newspaper that gave his brother’s death account, all the while hearing his nephew’s moans but not listening. Nanon knocked on the wall to summon him to dinner. On the last step he was saying to himself—

“I’ll do it; I shall get eight per cent interest. In two years I shall have fifteen hundred thousand francs, which I will then draw out in good gold,—Well, where’s my nephew?”

“He says he doesn’t want anything to eat,” answered Nanon; “that’s not good for him.”

“So much saved,” retorted her master.

“That’s so,” she said.

“Bah! he won’t cry long. Hunger drives the wolves out of the woods.”

Dinner was eaten in silence.

“My good friend,” said Madame Grandet when the cloth was removed, “we must put on mourning.”

“Upon my word, Madame Grandet! what will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, and not in the clothes.”

“But mourning for a brother is indispensable; and the Church commands us to—”

“Buy your mourning out of your six louis. Give me a hat-band; that’s enough for me.”

Eugenie raised her eyes to heaven without words. Her generous instincts, long repressed and now suddenly awakened, were galled. The evening passed as other monotonous evenings, yet it was certainly the most horrible. Eugenie sewed without lifting her head; Madame Grandet knitted; Grandet twirled his thumbs for four hours, absorbed in calculations whose results would astonish Saumur the next day. No one visited; the town buzzed with news of Grandet’s sale, his brother’s failure, and the arrival of his nephew. The upper and middle-class wine-growers gossiped at Monsieur des Grassins, fulminating imprecations against the ex-mayor. Nanon spun; the whirr of her wheel was the only sound beneath the gray rafters.

“We don’t waste our tongues,” she said, showing her teeth like peeled almonds.

“Nothing should be wasted,” answered Grandet, rousing from reverie. He saw a perspective of eight millions in three years and sailed along that sheet of gold. “Let us go to bed. I will bid my nephew good-night for the rest of you, and see if he will take anything.”

Madame Grandet remained on the first landing to hear the coming conversation; Eugenie went up two stairs.

“Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you, remember that. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” (Wine costs nothing in Saumur.) “Why!” added Grandet, “you have got no light! That’s bad, very bad; you ought to see what you are about,” and he walked to the chimney-piece. “What’s this?” he cried. “A wax candle! How the devil did they filch a wax candle? The spendthrifts would tear down the ceilings of my house to boil the fellow’s eggs.”

Hearing this, mother and daughter slipped back into their rooms and burrowed in bed like frightened mice getting to their holes.

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” said the man, coming into his wife’s chamber.

“My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” said the poor mother in a trembling voice.

“The devil take your good God!” growled Grandet.

Misers have little belief in a future life; the present is their all. This thought casts a terrible light upon an epoch in which money sways laws, politics, and morals. Institutions, books, and men conspire to undermine belief in a future life—a belief on which the social edifice rested for eighteen hundred years. The grave, once a feared transition, is now less so; the future has been imported into the present. To obtain, by any means, a terrestrial paradise of luxury and enjoyment, hardening the heart and macerating the body for fleeting possessions, is now a widespread thought—written even in laws that ask, “What do you pay?” instead of “What do you think?” When this doctrine passes from bourgeoisie to populace, where will the country be?

“Madame Grandet, have you done?” asked the old man.

“My friend, I am praying for you.”

“Very good! Good-night; to-morrow morning we will have a talk.”

The poor woman slept like a schoolboy who, unprepared, fears his master’s anger. As she drew the sheet over her to stifle sound, Eugenie, in her night-gown and with bare feet, ran to her and kissed her brow.

“Oh! my good mother,” she said, “to-morrow I will tell him it was I.”

“No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me.”

“Do you hear, mamma?”

“What?”

“He is weeping still.”

“Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold in your feet: the floor is damp.”

* * * * * *

Thus passed the solemn day which was to weigh upon the whole life of the rich and poor heiress, whose sleep was never again so calm or so pure. Certain actions seem improbable because we omit to turn psychological light upon the impulses that cause them. Perhaps Eugenie’s deep passion should be analyzed in its fibres; it became a malady that influenced her existence. Many prefer to deny results rather than estimate the force of hidden ties that link facts in the moral order. Here, Eugenie’s past life offers an explanation of her naive want of reflection and sudden emotions. The more tranquil her life, the more vivid her pity, the simpler the sentiments now developed.

Restless from the day’s events, she woke at intervals to listen for her cousin, thinking she heard his sighs. Sometimes she saw him dying or fainting from hunger. Towards morning she was certain she heard a cry. She dressed and ran, in the dawning light, to his chamber, whose door he had left open. The candle had burned to the socket. Charles, overcome, slept dressed in an armchair beside the bed, his head on the bedpost; he dreamed as men dream on an empty stomach. Eugenie might weep at her ease; she could admire the handsome face blotted with grief, the eyes swollen as if with tears. Charles felt her presence; he opened his eyes and saw her pity.

“Pardon me, my cousin,” he said, evidently not knowing the hour or place.

“There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus.”

“That is true.”

“Well, then, adieu!”

She escaped, ashamed and happy. Innocence alone can be so bold. Once enlightened, virtue calculates like vice. Eugenie, who had not trembled beside her cousin, could scarcely stand when she regained her chamber. Her ignorant life had suddenly ended; she reproached herself.

“What will he think of me? He will think that I love him!”

That was what she most wished him to think. An honest love presages itself. What an event for this poor solitary girl to have entered a young man’s chamber! There are acts in love that to certain souls have the meaning of espousals. An hour later she dressed her mother as usual. They sat before the window waiting for Grandet with the cruel anxiety that freezes or warms the heart when a scene is feared. The goodman came down; he spoke to his wife absent-mindedly, kissed Eugenie, and sat to table without appearing to remember his threats.

“What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble.”

“Monsieur, he is asleep,” answered Nanon.

“So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” said Grandet with a jeer.

This clemency and bitter gaiety struck Madame Grandet; she studied her husband. The goodman—who in regions like Touraine and Anjou is called “goodman” at a certain age whether kind or harsh—took his hat and gloves saying as he went out,—

“I am going to loiter about the market-place and find Cruchot.”

“Eugenie, your father certainly has something on his mind.”

Grandet, a poor sleeper, spent half his nights in preliminary calculations, giving accuracy to his schemes and assuring their success. All human power is a compound of time and patience. The miser’s life is constant exercise of power for self: self-love and self-interest, compact and intelligent, form egotism. From this arises the persistent curiosity and the habit of trading on others. Where is the man without desire? and what social desire can be satisfied without money?

Grandet had “something on his mind.” Like all misers, he craved to play a commercial game with men and win their money legally. To impose upon others was a sign of power, a proof he had the right to despise those who let themselves be preyed upon. Oh! who has ever truly understood the lamb lying peacefully at the feet of God?—the emblem of terrestrial victims. The miser fattens and slaughters the lamb, then despises it. His pasture is money and disdain. During the night his ideas had taken another course, explaining his sudden clemency. He had hatched a plot to trick the Parisians, to decoy and snare them, to make them sweat and hope—a plot to amuse himself, the old provincial cooper, sitting under his gloomy rafters. His nephew occupied his mind. He wished to save his dead brother’s honor without spending a penny on the son or himself. His own funds he would invest for three years; he needed only to manage his Saumur property. Finding nutriment for mischief in his brother’s failure, he resolved to crush the Parisians on Charles’s behalf and to play the good brother at the cheapest terms. Family honor counted so little that his intentions resembled a gambler’s interest in seeing a game well played without stake. The Cruchots were necessary; but he would not seek them—he resolved to make them come to him and to stage, that evening, a comedy whose plot he had conceived, which would make him the town’s admired man on the morrow without costing him a penny.

In his absence Eugenie had the happiness of openly busying herself with her cousin, spending upon him fearlessly the treasures of her pity—woman’s sublime superiority, the sole she desires to have recognized, the sole she pardons man for letting her assume. Three or four times she went to listen to his breathing to see if he slept, then when he rose she turned to his breakfast—the cream, eggs, fruits, the plates and glasses—each item a special care. At length she ran up the old staircase to hear the noise he made. Was he dressing? Did he still weep? She reached the door.

“My cousin!”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Will you breakfast downstairs, or in your room?”

“Where you like.”

“How do you feel?”

“Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry.”

This conversation, held through the closed door, was like an episode in a poem to Eugenie.

“Well, then, we will bring your breakfast to your own room, so as not to annoy my father.”

She flew to the kitchen.

“Nanon, go and do his room!”

That staircase, often traversed and echoing to the slightest noise, lost its decaying aspect in Eugenie’s eyes. It grew luminous; it had a voice and spoke to her; it was young—young like the love it was now serving. Her kind indulgent mother lent herself to the caprices of the child’s love, and after the room was put in order both went to sit with the unhappy youth and keep him company. Does not Christian charity make consolation a duty? The two women drew little sophistries from religion to justify their conduct. Charles became the object of tender and loving care. His saddened heart felt the sweetness of the gentle friendship these two souls, crushed under restraint, displayed when, for an instant, they were unfettered in the regions of suffering.

Claiming relationship, Eugenie began to fold linen and arrange the toilet articles Charles had brought; she could marvel at ease over each luxurious bauble and knick-knack of silver and gold, holding them under pretext of examination. Charles could not see without emotion the generous interest his aunt and cousin felt; he knew Paris society and, placed as he was, expected indifference. Eugenie thus appeared in splendor, and he admired the innocence of life and manners he had been inclined to ridicule. When Eugenie took from Nanon the bowl of coffee and cream and poured it with real feeling, giving him a kindly glance, the Parisian filled with tears; he took her hand and kissed it.

“What troubles you?” she said.

“Oh! these are tears of gratitude,” he answered.

Eugenie abruptly turned to the chimney-piece to take the candlesticks.

“Here, Nanon, carry them away!” she said.

When she looked again she blushed, but her looks could at least deceive, not betray the excess of joy that flooded her heart; yet the eyes of both expressed the same sentiment—the future was theirs. This soft emotion was precious to Charles amid heavy grief because it was wholly unexpected. The knocker recalled the women to duty; happily they ran downstairs with speed to be seated at their work when Grandet entered; had he met them under the archway his suspicions might have been roused. After breakfast, which the goodman took standing, the keeper from Froidfond, whose promised indemnity had not yet been paid, appeared with a hare and partridges from the park, eels and two pike sent as tribute by the millers.

“Ha, ha! poor Cornoiller; here he comes, like fish in Lent. Is all that fit to eat?”

“Yes, my dear, generous master; it has been killed two days.”

“Come, Nanon, bestir yourself,” said Grandet; “take these things, they’ll do for dinner. I have invited the two Cruchots.”

Nanon opened her eyes, stupefied, and looked at everybody.

“Well!” she said, “and how am I to get the lard and the spices?”

“Wife,” said Grandet, “give Nanon six francs, and remind me to get some of the good wine out of the cellar.”

“Well, then, Monsieur Grandet,” said the keeper, prepared to settle the indemnity, “Monsieur Grandet—”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet; “I know what you want to say. You are a good fellow; we will see about it to-morrow, I’m too busy to-day. Wife, give him five francs,” he added to Madame Grandet as he decamped.

The poor woman was too happy to buy peace at eleven francs. She knew Grandet would let her alone for a fortnight after he had thus taken back, franc by franc, the money he had given her.

“Here, Cornoiller,” she said, slipping ten francs into his hand, “some day we will reward your services.”

Cornoiller could say nothing and went away.

“Madame,” said Nanon, who had donned her black coif and taken her basket, “I want only three francs. You keep the rest; it’ll go fast enough somehow.”

“Have a good dinner, Nanon; my cousin will come down,” said Eugenie.

“Something very extraordinary is going on, I am certain,” said Madame Grandet. “This is only the third time since our marriage that your father has given a dinner.”

* * * * * *

About four o’clock, just as Eugenie and her mother had finished setting the table for six and the master had brought up a few bottles of the exquisite wine provincials cherish, Charles came down into the hall. He was pale; his gestures, expression, glance, and voice had a sadness full of grace. He truly suffered; the veil of pain gave him an air dear to women. Eugenie loved him the more for it. Sorrow had leveled the distance between them—Charles was no longer the rich, distinguished young man above her sphere, but a relation plunged into frightful misery. Misery begets equality. Women, like angels, claim suffering humanity. Charles and Eugenie spoke only with their eyes; the poor fallen dandy, orphaned and impoverished, sat apart, proud, calm, silent. From time to time the gentle caressing glance of the young girl shone upon him and drew him from sad thoughts into fields of hope, where she loved to hold him at her side.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At this moment Saumur was more excited about the dinner Grandet gave the Cruchots than the previous night's sale of his vintage, though that sale had affronted the wine-growers. Had the politic old miser entertained for a noble motive, he might have been praised; believing himself superior to the townspeople, he cared little for their opinion.

The des Grassins had soon learned of Guillaume Grandet’s failure and violent death and resolved to call that evening to commiserate and discover why he had invited the Cruchots. At precisely five Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle the notary arrived in their Sunday clothes. They dined with appetites; Grandet was grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, Madame Grandet reserved. When they rose, Charles said he must retire to undertake a long and painful correspondence.

As soon as the aunt and uncle were sure Charles could not overhear, Grandet dismissed his wife and daughter and, alone with the two Cruchots, assumed the cunning that had earned him the nickname “the old dog.” He affected a stutter and slight deafness—tricks to manage conversation and dodge responsibility—to keep his intentions hidden.

He began, stammering, to raise the subject of bankruptcy. The president, flattered by Grandet’s awkwardly respectful address, took the lead and explained how a threatened merchant might avoid dishonor by liquidating rather than being declared insolvent. He described how creditors might buy up notes in the market at a reduction so that, if one held title to the debts and paid them off by agreement, the estate would be honorably released. Grandet, feigning ignorance, professed he knew nothing of such Paris tricks and that he lived in Saumur attending to vines and drains. He admitted he might be able to help his nephew, but must consider, for he had no time or knowledge to engage personally.

The president—smooth, sniffing snuff—went on about notes as merchandise, invoking Bentham’s reasoning, and suggested debts might be bought for twenty-five percent. The notary laughed; the president added practicalities: a friend could go to Paris, buy up the notes at a reduction, obtain extensions from creditors, and thus save the family’s honor. Grandet pretended to be distressed by the complexity and expense, repeatedly claiming he was only a poor wine-grower; but his eye betrayed eagerness.

The notary, uneasy, warned his nephew to let Monsieur Grandet explain his intentions. Before Cruchot could resume, the des Grassins arrived with expressions of condolence. The notary took the opportunity to announce Grandet’s honorable intention to liquidate his brother’s affairs, and that the president had offered to go to Paris to negotiate with the creditors. Des Grassins, who had been talking along the way about the old man’s avarice and had almost accused him of fratricide, were astonished and delighted.

“Grandet is honorable to the backbone,” cried the banker, embracing him. The scene allowed the des Grassins to present themselves as friends with business sense. The banker suggested that such a commercial matter required a consummate business man and offered to take charge, since he was going to Paris and could attend to it while on his own business.

Grandet, dropping some assumed hesitancy, confided privately to des Grassins that he had more confidence in the banker than in the president and had other plans. He wanted to buy a few thousand francs in the Funds while the price was low; des Grassins knew about such things and could make the purchase by the end of the month. He signaled that none of this should be told to the Cruchots. The banker, pleased, promised to start by the mail-post and to take final directions at five o’clock, just before dinner.

The parties lingered; des Grassins struck Grandet on the shoulder, murmuring it was fortunate to have such a relation. Grandet, rubbing his wen, declared his love for his brother and his intention to prove it—unless costs prevented him. Des Grassins had cut the Cruchots out of the commission; the president, disappointed at being sidelined, complained to his uncle that he had begun by being “de Bonfons” and ended as nothing but Cruchot. The notary advised modesty: let the des Grassins take this tack, and Eugenie would remain the president’s wife all the same.

In a short time news of Grandet’s magnanimity spread through three houses and then the town. Everyone forgave his sale made in defiance of pledged good faith and lauded his apparent generosity; such sudden enthusiasm is a French tendency, quick to forget and quick to praise.

When Père Grandet shut the door he called Nanon and instructed her not to let the dog loose or go to bed; they had work to do. At eleven Cornoiller would bring the chariot from Froidfond; Nanon must tell him to come softly, for there must be no nocturnal racket and no neighborly curiosity. Grandet returned to his private room, moving cautiously so as not to wake his wife and daughter and, above all, not to rouse his nephew, whom he had already begun to condemn for his pale, despairing looks.

About the middle of the night Eugenie, anxious for Charles, fancied she heard a cry like that of a dying person. She wrapped herself in a pelisse and, thinking of the light under her cousin’s door, was about to go out when she saw Nanon’s heavy steps and heard horses. Peering into the corridor she met her father’s vague, unnoticing gaze and was terrified. Grandet and Nanon were linked by a stout stick; a thick rope over their shoulders carried a small keg like those Grandet made in the bakehouse—filled, as Nanon complained, with heavy copper sous. Grandet cautioned them to be careful of the candlestick; the scene was lit by a single candle on the staircase.

Cornoiller had come with horses; Grandet asked if he had pistols—there was nothing to fear—and told Nanon to say to his wife that he had gone into the country and would be back to dinner. The chariot drove off with all possible secrecy; Nanon bolted the great door, let the dog loose, and went to bed with a bruised shoulder, the neighbors unaware of either the departure or its purpose. Grandet’s precautions and reticence were habitual; no one had ever seen a penny in that house, though it was filled with gold.

In the morning, gossip at the port reported that exchange on gold had doubled because of military preparations at Nantes and that speculators had come to Angers to buy coin. The old wine-grower, having borrowed horses from his farmers, had seized the chance: he had gone to sell his gold and to bring back treasury notes, converting the sum he intended to put into the Funds and swelling it by advantage of the exchange.

################################################
“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie, who had heard it from the head of the stairs. Silence fell; the rumble of the carriage died away. A cry came from her cousin’s room; a thin strip of light showed under the door and crossed the rotten stair.

“He suffers!” she said, rushing up. A moan brought her to the landing. The door stood ajar; she pushed it open. Charles slept with his head drooped over the old chair’s arm, the pen fallen from his hand nearly touching the floor. His strained posture alarmed her; she entered.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, and glanced at a dozen sealed letters on the table. She read the addresses—carriage-makers, a tailor—and guessed he had been settling affairs to leave France. Her eyes fell on two open letters. The words “My dear Annette” at the head of one stunned her. Her heart beat fast; her feet felt nailed.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” She saw the words everywhere. “I ought to go away—What if I do read it?”

She looked at Charles, then gently set his head against the chair back; he let her, like a sleeping child. She raised his drooping hand and kissed his chestnut hair. “Dear Annette!” a demon shrieked in her ear.

“I am doing wrong; but I must read it,” she decided, torn by honor and curiosity. Passion and curiosity triumphed. As she read, her heart swelled; the keen glow of first love made every feeling precious.

My dear Annette,—Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has fallen on me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are irretrievably lost. I am an orphan at an age when, by my education, I am still a child; yet I must lift myself as a man out of the abyss. If I wish to leave France an honest man—I am without a hundred francs to try my luck in the Indies or America. Yes, Anna, I must seek my fortune in those deadly climates. I cannot remain in Paris. Neither my nature nor my face are made to bear the affronts, neglect, disdain shown to a ruined man, the son of a bankrupt! Good God! think of owing two millions! I should be killed in a duel the first week; therefore I shall not return. Your love—the most tender and devoted love—cannot draw me back. I have no money to go to you, to give and receive a last kiss that might strengthen me.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him,” thought Eugenie.

She wiped her eyes and read on.

I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. If I had the hundred louis for the journey, I have not a sou for an outfit. But no—I have not the hundred louis, not even one. I don’t know what will be left after I have paid my debts in Paris. If I have nothing, I shall go quietly to Nantes and ship as a common sailor; I will begin in the new world like other men who started with nothing and made fortunes. I have faced my future coolly. It seems more horrible to me because I was so petted by a mother who adored me, so indulged by a kindly father, so blessed to have known the love of an Anna. The flowers of life are all I have known. Such happiness could not last. Nevertheless I feel more courage than is supposed for a careless young man—above all, one used to caressing; oh, my father! Annette, he is dead!

I have thought of my position and yours. I have grown old in twenty-four hours. Dear Anna, if to keep me with you in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, your dress, your opera-box, we should still not have enough for my extravagant ways. Besides, I would never accept such sacrifices. No, we must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy. Charles moved; terror chilled her. He did not wake, and she resumed reading.

When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European who works hard. In ten years your daughter will be eighteen; she may be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, daughters sometimes cruel; remember four years of happiness and, if you can, be faithful to my memory. I cannot demand faithfulness, for I must adapt to my new life and take a commonplace view of it. Therefore marriage becomes a necessity; I have found here in Saumur, in my uncle’s house, a cousin whose face, manners, mind, and heart would please you, and who, besides, seems to me—

“He must have been very weary to have ceased writing to her,” thought Eugenie as the letter broke off.

She excused him. How could an innocent girl suspect cold-heartedness in such a letter? Young, religiously brought up girls see love’s light everywhere; they reflect their warmth on their lover and attribute to him their highest thoughts. A woman’s errors often spring from belief in good and trust in truth. Eugenie’s simple heart heard “My dear Annette” like the sweetest words; the tears on the young man’s lashes seemed noble. How could she know that Charles, though he loved his father and mourned him, was moved more by paternal indulgence than by heroic resolve? Monsieur and Madame Guillaume Grandet had gratified every fancy of their son and lavished pleasures on him, keeping him from the hard reckonings other sons face.

Still, Charles was a child of Paris, schooled by society and by Annette to calculate; already, beneath youthful grace, he had learned the bitter prudence of social life, where in one evening more crimes of thought and speech are committed than justice punishes; where to “see clear” one must believe in nothing and weigh a friend’s purse daily—self-interest the mainspring of all things. After folly and adulation, the great lady—Annette—had made him think soberly; with a perfumed hand among his curls she spoke to him of his future, taught worldly prudence, effeminized and materialized him—a refined but real corruption.

Nearly all young girls yield to the promises such outward appearance offers: even had Eugenie been as prudent as many provincial girls, she would have found it hard to distrust a cousin whose manners and words matched youthful hopes. A fatal chance laid before her Charles’s last effusions; she heard the last stirrings of his conscience. She laid down the letter—so full of love—and smiled, watching him sleep; the fresh illusions of life were still on his face; she vowed to love him always. Then she glanced at the other letter, reading it for new proofs of noble qualities she already believed him to possess.

My dear Alphonse,—When you receive this letter I shall be without friends; but while I doubt the friendship of the world, I have never doubted yours. I beg you to settle my affairs and get as much as you can out of my possessions. You know my situation. I have nothing left, and I intend to go at once to the Indies. I have written to those I think I owe money, and enclose a list. My books, furniture, pictures, horses, etc., ought to pay my debts. I do not wish to keep anything, except perhaps a few baubles for my outfit. I will send a power of attorney so you can make these sales. Send me all my weapons. Keep Briton for yourself; nobody would pay his value, and I would rather give him to you—like a mourning-ring bequeathed by a dying man to his executor. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a travelling-carriage not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it and not ask for payment. If they refuse, do what you can and avoid anything dishonorable. I owe the British Islander six louis lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, throwing down the letter and running softly back to her room, carrying a lighted candle. A thrill passed as she opened the drawer of an old oak cabinet topped with the faint royal salamander. She took out a large red velvet purse edged with gold fringe—a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it proudly and began counting the forgotten hoard: portugaises, genovines, Spanish quadruples, Dutch ducats of 1756, Indian rupees, and a napoleon of forty francs received the day before. Her father loved these coins for rarity, but Eugenie thought only of Charles. After a few miscalculations she made out about five thousand eight hundred francs in intrinsic value—nearly six thousand for collectors. She clapped her hands like a child. Father and daughter had each counted their fortune—he, to sell his gold; she, to fling hers into the sea of affection. She put the coins back, took the purse, and ran upstairs without hesitation. The hidden misery of her cousin made her forget hour and propriety; she was strong in conscience, devotion, and happiness.

At the threshold, holding the candle and purse, Charles woke and was speechless with surprise. Eugenie set the candle on the table.

“My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me—if you—will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” asked Charles, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles colored.

“How did it happen?” she continued; “how came I here? Truly, I do not know. I am tempted not to regret too much that I have read them; they have made me know your heart, your soul, and—”

“And what?” asked Charles.

“Your plans, your need of a sum—”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush, hush! not so loud; we must not wake others. See,” she said, opening her purse, “here are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I did not value money; you have taught me. It is but a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow your sister’s purse.”

Eugenie, both woman and girl, never dreamed of refusal; but Charles remained silent.

“Oh! you will not refuse?” cried Eugenie, whose heart could be heard beating in the silence.

His hesitation mortified her, but the need of his position became clearer, and she knelt.

“I will never rise till you have taken that gold!” she said. “My cousin, I implore you, answer me! let me know if you respect me, if you are generous, if—”

Moved, Charles took her hands to keep her from kneeling; his tears fell on them. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents on the table.

“Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she sobbed. “Fear nothing, my cousin, you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; some day you shall bring it back to me—are we not partners? I will obey all conditions. But you should not attach such value to the gift.”

Charles could finally speak.

“Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she said, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square box covered by a leather case on the drawers. “There,” he continued, “is something as precious to me as life itself. This box was a present from my mother. All day I have thought that if she could rise from her grave, she would herself sell the gold which her love for me lavished on this dressing-case; but were I to do so, it would seem a sacrilege.” Eugenie pressed his hand. “No,” he added after a pause with a tender look, “no, I will neither sell it nor risk its safety on my journey. Dear Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the case and revealed a richly wrought dressing-case with gold ornaments. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, pushing a spring that revealed a hidden drawer. He drew out two portraits, masterpieces by Madame Mirbel, richly set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he smiled; “this is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you on my knees, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your little fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. To you alone could I leave these portraits; you are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent. “Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he added with winning grace.

Hearing his words as if he addressed himself, she turned to him with a look of love—the first look of womanhood, half coquette, half depth. He kissed her hand.

“Angel of purity! between us two money is nothing, never can be anything. Feeling and sentiment must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, dropping her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She disengaged her hand; he lighted her way to her room. At the threshold—

“Ah!” he said, “why am I ruined?”

“What matter?—my father is rich; I think so,” she answered.

“Poor child!” said Charles, stepping in and leaning against the wall, “if that were so, he would never have let my father die; he would not let you live in this poor way; he would live otherwise himself.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” said Charles disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year do you suppose you would live in this cold, barren room?” he added, edging forward. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said, glancing at the old cabinet, as if to conceal his thoughts.

“Go and sleep,” she said, hindering his entrance into the disordered room.

They bade each other good-night with a mutual smile.

Both fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment the youth began to wear roses with his mourning. The next morning, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found her daughter in the garden with Charles. The young man was still sad, fitting for one plunged in misfortune and measuring his future.

“My father will not be home till dinner-time,” said Eugenie, seeing her mother’s anxious face.

There was an evident unison of thought between her and her cousin. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected. Everyone busied themselves with tasks. Grandet had left affairs unattended, and several people came on business—the plumber, mason, slater, carpenter, diggers, farmers—some to drive bargains about repairs, others to pay or be paid. Nanon put away produce brought as tribute, always waiting for her master’s orders before deciding to use or sell. It was the goodman’s custom, like many country gentry, to drink bad wine and eat spoiled fruit.

Toward five in the afternoon Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs by the exchange on his gold, bringing treasury-notes bearing interest until he should invest them in the Funds. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, nearly foundered, with orders to bring them home slowly after they were rested.

“I have got back from Angers, wife,” he said; “I am hungry.”

Nanon called from the kitchen: “Haven’t you eaten anything since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he answered.

Nanon brought in the soup. Des Grassins arrived to take his client’s orders just as the family sat down. Grandet had not even observed his nephew.

“Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker; “we can talk. Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” said Grandet; “they have got enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from such a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where the devil have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” answered Grandet in a low voice.

The banker started. A whispered conversation began between the two, during which they frequently looked at Charles. Presently des Grassins gave a start of astonishment; probably Grandet was then instructing him to invest the sum which was to give him a hundred thousand francs a year in the Funds.

“Monsieur Grandet,” said the banker to Charles, “I am starting for Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” answered Charles.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” said Charles eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle, with well-acted pride, “are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, seized Pere Grandet, kissed him, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-by, des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead ’em by the nose.”

The two diplomats shook hands. The old cooper accompanied the banker to the front door. After closing it, he came back and plunged into his armchair, saying to Nanon,—

“Get me some black-currant ratafia.”

Too excited to sit long, he got up, looked at the portrait of Monsieur de la Bertelliere, and began to sing, performing what Nanon called his dancing steps—

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie looked at each other in silence. The master’s hilarity always frightened them when it reached its climax. The evening soon passed. Pere Grandet chose to go to bed early, and when he went to bed everybody else was expected to do the same. Nanon, Charles, and Eugenie were as tired as the master. Madame Grandet lived always according to her husband’s will. During the two hours consecrated to digestion the cooper, more facetious than ever, uttered a number of his apothegms—one of which gives the measure of his mind. When he had drunk his ratafia he looked at his glass and said,—

“You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.”

He was jovial and benevolent. When Nanon came with her spinning-wheel, “You must be tired,” he said; “put away your hemp.”

“Ah, bah! then I shall get sleepy,” she answered.

“Poor Nanon! Will you have some ratafia?”

“I won’t refuse a good offer; madame makes it a deal better than the apothecaries. What they sell is all drugs.”

“They put too much sugar,” said the master; “you can’t taste anything else.”

The following day the family met at eight for the early breakfast, showing an intimate domestic picture. Grief had united Madame Grandet, Eugenie, and Charles; even Nanon sympathized without knowing why. The four now formed one household. The old man, whose avarice and assurance that he would soon rid himself of the dandy nephew for merely the cost of his journey to Nantes made him nearly indifferent, left the two children—Charles and Eugenie—to behave as they pleased under Madame Grandet’s supervision, in whom he trusted for public and religious morality. He occupied himself with straightening field boundaries, tending poplars by the Loire, winter vineyard work, and affairs at Froidfond.

For Eugenie the springtime of love had come. Since the night she gave her little treasure to her cousin, her heart had followed it. Confederates in the same secret, they exchanged looks and a mutual intelligence that deepened their communion, removing them from ordinary life. Their near relationship licensed gentleness and tender glances. Eugenie took pleasure in soothing her cousin’s pain with the childish delights of new love. There are sweet parallels between the birth of love and the birth of life: one rocks the babe with songs and soft looks, tells of a golden future, and Hope spreads her wings. Childhood and love were one to Eugenie and Charles; it was a first passion, childlike play—the more caressing because wrapped in sadness. Struggling at birth against the gloom of mourning, their love matched the simplicity of that gray, ruined house. As they exchanged few words beside the well, lingered in the garden at sunset on a mossy seat saying the infinite nothings of love, or mused between the house and the ramparts as under church arches, Charles learned the sanctity of love; his great lady Annette had taught him only its stormy troubles. He left vain worldly passion for true, pure love. He loved even the house, whose customs no longer seemed ridiculous. He rose early to speak with Eugenie before her father distributed provisions; when Grandet’s steps sounded he escaped to the garden. The small illicitness of these morning tête-à-têtes, which Nanon pretended not to see, gave their innocent love the charm of forbidden joy.

After breakfast, when Grandet had gone to his fields, Charles stayed with the mother and daughter, enjoying holding their skeins, watching them work, and listening to their quiet prattle. The simplicity of this half-monastic life, revealing the beauty of these souls, touched him. He had believed such morals impossible in France, admitting their existence only in Germany; even so, they seemed fabulous, as in La Fontaine. Soon Eugenie became to him a Margaret of Goethe—before her fall. Day by day his words and looks enraptured the poor girl, who yielded with delicious non-resistance to love’s current; she caught her happiness like a swimmer seizes an overhanging branch. Were they saddened by dread of coming absence? Daily some small circumstance reminded them of the impending parting.

Three days after des Grassins’ departure, Grandet took his nephew to the Civil courts with the solemnity country people attach to legal acts, so he might sign a deed surrendering his rights in his father’s estate. Terrible renunciation! Charles also went before Maitre Cruchot to make two powers of attorney—one for des Grassins, the other for the friend charged with selling his belongings. He handled formalities for a passport; when he received mourning clothes from Paris, he sent for the Saumur tailor and sold his useless wardrobe. That pleased Grandet exceedingly.

“Ah! now you look like a man prepared to embark and make your fortune,” he said when Charles appeared in a plain black surtout. “Good! very good!”

“I hope you will believe, monsieur,” answered his nephew, “that I shall always try to conform to my situation.”

“What’s that?” said his uncle, eyes lighting on a handful of gold Charles carried.

“Monsieur, I have collected all my buttons and rings and other superfluities which may have some value; not knowing anyone in Saumur, I wanted to ask you to—”

“To buy them?” interrupted Grandet.

“No, uncle; only to tell me of an honest man who—”

“Give me those things; I will estimate their value,” said Grandet, taking the mass of gold.

“Cousin,” said Grandet, “may I offer you these two buttons? They can fasten ribbons round your wrists; that sort of bracelet is fashionable now.”

“I accept,” she answered, giving him an understanding look.

“Aunt, here is my mother’s thimble; I have always kept it carefully,” said Charles, presenting a pretty gold thimble to Madame Grandet, who longed for one.

“I cannot thank you; no words are possible, my nephew,” said the poor mother, with tears. “Night and morning in my prayers I shall add one for you, the most earnest of all—for those who travel. If I die, Eugenie will keep this treasure for you.”

“They are worth nine hundred and eighty-nine francs, seventy-five centimes,” said Grandet. “To save you the pain of selling them, I will advance the money—in livres.”

On the Loire littoral the word livres meant crown prices of six livres were accepted as six francs without deduction.

“I dared not propose it,” Charles answered; “but it was repugnant to me to sell my jewels to some second-hand dealer in your town. People should wash their dirty linen at home, as Napoleon said. I thank you for your kindness.”

Grandet scratched his ear; silence followed.

“My dear uncle,” resumed Charles uneasily, “my aunt and cousin have accepted a trifling remembrance of me. Will you allow me to give you these sleeve-buttons, now useless? They will remind you of a poor fellow who, far away, will always think of those who are henceforth all his family.”

“My lad, my lad, you mustn’t rob yourself! Let me see, wife, what have you got?” he added, turning eagerly to her. “Ah! a gold thimble. And you, little girl? Diamond buttons? Yes, I’ll accept your present, nephew,” he answered, shaking Charles’s hand. “But—you must let me—pay—your—yes, your passage to the Indies. I wish to pay your passage because, d’ye see, my boy, in valuing your jewels I estimated only the weight of the gold; very likely the workmanship is worth something. So let us settle that I give you fifteen hundred francs—in livres; Cruchot will lend them to me. I haven’t got a copper farthing here—unless Perrotet, behindhand with his rent, should pay up. By the bye, I’ll go and see him.”

He took his hat, put on his gloves, and went out.

“Then you are really going?” said Eugenie to her cousin, with a sad, admiring look.

“I must,” he said, bowing his head.

For days Charles’s bearing, manners, and speech took on the dignity of a man who, despite profound affliction, feels obligations and draws courage from misfortune. He no longer repined; he became a man. Eugenie never thought better of his character than when she saw him in plain black that suited his pale face. That day the two women put on mourning, and all three attended a Requiem in the parish church for the late Guillaume Grandet.

At the second breakfast Charles received letters from Paris and began to read them.

“Well, cousin, are you satisfied with the management of your affairs?” said Eugenie in a low voice.

“Never ask such questions, my daughter,” said Grandet. “What the devil! do I tell you my affairs? Why do you poke your nose into your cousin’s? Let the lad alone!”

“Oh! I haven’t any secrets,” said Charles.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, nephew; you’ll soon find out that you must hold your tongue in business.”

When the two lovers were alone in the garden, Charles drew Eugenie down on the old bench beneath the walnut-tree and said,—

“I did right to trust Alphonse; he has done famously. He has managed my affairs with prudence and good faith. I now owe nothing in Paris. All my things have been sold; he took advice of an old sea-captain and spent three thousand francs on a commercial outfit of European curiosities sure to be in demand in the Indies. He has sent my trunks to Nantes, where a ship is loading for San Domingo. In five days, Eugenie, we must bid each other farewell—perhaps forever, at least for years. My outfit and ten thousand francs, which two of my friends send me, are a very small beginning. I cannot look to return for many years. My dear cousin, do not weigh your life in the scales with mine; I may perish; some good marriage may be offered to you—”

“Do you love me?” she said.

“Oh, yes! indeed, yes!” he answered, with a depth that revealed equal feeling.

“I shall wait, Charles—Good heavens! there is my father at his window,” she said, repulsing him who leaned forward to kiss her.

She ran under the archway. Charles followed. At the foot of the staircase she opened the swing-door and, scarcely knowing where she was going, reached the corner near Nanon’s den, in the darkest end of the passage. There Charles caught her hand and drew her to his heart. Passing his arm about her waist, he made her lean upon him. Eugenie no longer resisted; she received and gave the purest, sweetest, most unreserved of kisses.

“Dear Eugenie, a cousin is better than a brother, for he can marry you,” said Charles.

“So be it!” cried Nanon, opening the door of her lair.

The two lovers, alarmed, fled into the hall, where Eugenie took up her work and Charles began to read the litanies of the Virgin in Madame Grandet’s prayer-book.

“Mercy!” cried Nanon, “now they’re saying their prayers.”

As soon as Charles announced his immediate departure, Grandet moved to show interest. He became liberal of what cost him nothing; took pains to find a packer; declared the price too high; insisted on making the cases himself out of old planks; rose early to plane and nail strips into strong boxes in which he packed all Charles’s effects; he also sent them by boat down the Loire, insured them, and got them to Nantes in time.

After the kiss in the passage, hours fled for Eugenie with frightful rapidity. Sometimes she thought of following him. Those who have known that most endearing of passions—the one shortened by time, distance, mortal illness, and chances—will understand her tortures. She wept as she walked in the garden, now so narrow; the court, the house, the town seemed confined. She launched in thought upon the wide ocean he was about to traverse. At last the eve of his departure came. That morning, in the absence of Grandet and Nanon, the case containing the two portraits was solemnly placed in the only drawer of the old cabinet that could be locked, where the now empty velvet purse lay. This deposit was not made without many tears and kisses. When Eugenie placed the key within her bosom she had no courage to forbid the kiss with which Charles sealed the act.

“It shall never leave that place, my friend,” she said.

“Then my heart will be always there.”

“Ah! Charles, it is not right,” she said, as though blaming him.

“Are we not married?” he said. “I have thy promise—then take mine.”

“Thine; I am thine forever!” they each said, repeating the words twice.

No promise made upon this earth was ever purer. The innocent sincerity of Eugenie sanctified for a moment the young man’s love.

On the morrow the breakfast was sad. Nanon herself, despite the gold-embroidered robe and Jeannette cross bestowed by Charles, had tears in her eyes.

“The poor dear monsieur who is going on the seas—oh, may God guide him!”

At half-past ten the whole family started to escort Charles to the diligence for Nantes. Nanon let loose the dog, locked the door, and insisted on carrying the young man’s carpet-bag. All the tradesmen in the tortuous old street were on their sills to watch the procession, which was joined in the market-place by Maitre Cruchot.

“Eugenie, be sure you don’t cry,” said her mother.

“Nephew,” said Grandet in the doorway of the inn from which the coach started, kissing Charles on both cheeks, “depart poor, return rich; you will find the honor of your father safe. I answer for that myself, I—Grandet; for it will only depend on you to—”

“Ah! my uncle, you soften the bitterness of my departure. Is it not the best gift you could make me?”

Not understanding his uncle’s broken words, Charles shed tears of gratitude upon the tanned cheeks of the old miser, while Eugenie pressed her cousin’s and father’s hands with all her strength. The notary smiled, admiring the sly speech of the old man, which he alone had understood. The family stood about the coach until it started; then as it disappeared on the bridge and its rumble faded, Grandet said:

“Good-by to you!”

Happily no one but Maitre Cruchot heard the exclamation. Eugenie and her mother had gone to a corner of the quay from which they could still see the diligence and wave their white handkerchiefs, to which Charles answered by displaying his.

“Ah! mother, would that I had the power of God for a single moment,” said Eugenie, when she could no longer see his handkerchief.

To avoid interrupting the Grandet family story, it is necessary to glance at the operations the goodman carried on in Paris through Monsieur des Grassins. A month after the latter’s departure from Saumur, Grandet obtained a certificate of a hundred thousand francs a year from his investment in the Funds, bought at eighty francs net. The particulars revealed at his death by the inventory of his property shed no clear light on the methods his suspicious nature used to remit the price and receive the certificate. Maitre Cruchot thought that Nanon, unknown to herself, was the trusty instrument by which the money was transported; about this time she was absent five days under pretext of putting things to rights at Froidfond—as if the goodman were capable of leaving anything out of order!

In matters concerning Guillaume Grandet’s house the old cooper’s intentions were fulfilled. The Bank of France has exact information about large fortunes. The names des Grassins and Felix Grandet of Saumur were known there; they enjoyed esteem among financial celebrities whose wealth comes from unencumbered territorial possessions. The Saumur banker’s arrival to honorably liquidate Guillaume Grandet of Paris averted the shame of protested notes. The seals on the property were removed before the creditors, and the notary employed by Grandet began the inventory. Des Grassins called a meeting of the creditors, who unanimously elected him, together with Francois Keller, head of a rich banking-house and a principal interested party, as liquidators with full power to protect family honor and claimants’ interests. The credit of Grandet of Saumur and the hopes he inspired through des Grassins facilitated the transactions. Not a single creditor proved recalcitrant; each said confidently, “Grandet of Saumur will pay.”

Six months passed. Parisians redeemed the notes as they fell due and kept them locked. First result sought by the old cooper! Nine months after the preliminary meeting, the two liquidators distributed forty-seven per cent to each creditor on his claim. This sum was obtained by selling securities and property of the late Guillaume Grandet and was paid with scrupulous fidelity. Unimpeachable integrity was shown. The creditors acknowledged the remarkable honor displayed by the Grandets. When praise circulated, creditors asked for the rest of their money. It became necessary to write a collective letter to Grandet of Saumur.

“Here it comes!” said the old man as he threw the letter into the fire. “Patience, my good friends!”

In answer, Grandet demanded that all vouchers for claims against his brother’s estate be deposited with a notary, together with acquittances for the forty-seven per cent already paid; he made this demand under pretense of sifting accounts and finding the estate’s exact condition. It raised difficulties. Generally, the creditor is a species of maniac—agreeable one day, fiery the next, later amicable again. Grandet had studied creditors’ atmospheric variations, and his brother’s creditors justified his calculations. Some were angry and refused to hand in their vouchers.

“Very good; so much the better,” said Grandet, rubbing his hands over des Grassins’ letter.

Others agreed only if their rights were fully guaranteed; they renounced none and even reserved the power to compel failure. This began long correspondence, ending with Grandet of Saumur agreeing to all conditions. By this concession placable creditors were able to bring the dissatisfied to reason. The deposit was then made, but not without complaints.

“Your goodman,” they said to des Grassins, “is tricking us.”

Twenty-three months after Guillaume Grandet’s death many creditors, swept away by more pressing Paris business, had forgotten their claims or only thought to say: “I begin to believe that forty-seven per cent is all I shall ever get.”

The old cooper had counted on the power of time, which, as he used to say, is a pretty good devil. By the end of the third year des Grassins wrote that he had brought creditors to accept ten per cent on the two million four hundred thousand francs still due by the house of Grandet. Grandet answered that the notary and broker whose failures caused his brother’s death were still living, that they might have recovered their credit and could be sued to recover something toward the deficit.

By the end of the fourth year liabilities were definitively estimated at twelve hundred thousand francs. Many negotiations took place between creditors and liquidators, and between liquidators and Grandet. To cut a long story short, about the ninth month of the fourth year Grandet told the liquidators his nephew had made a fortune in the Indies and was disposed to pay his father’s debts in full; therefore he could not settle without consulting him; he had written and awaited an answer. The creditors were held in check until the middle of the fifth year by the words “payment in full,” which the wily miser put out from time to time, smiling and cursing, “Those Parisians!”

The creditors were reserved for an unprecedented fate in commerce. When events bring them back into notice, they will be found still in the position Grandet had resolved from the first.

As soon as the Funds reached a hundred and fifteen, Pere Grandet sold out and withdrew two million four hundred thousand francs in gold, to which he added the six hundred thousand francs compound interest from the capital. Des Grassins lived in Paris. First he had been made a deputy; then he became infatuated (father of a family though he was, and bored by provincial Saumur) with a pretty actress at the Theatre de Madame, known as Florine, and relapsed into old army habits. His conduct was considered profoundly immoral in Saumur. His wife was fortunate in having her property settled on herself and in possessing ability to keep up the Saumur banking-house, which was managed in her name and repaired the breach caused by her husband’s extravagance. The Cruchotines made much talk about the quasi-widow’s false position; she married her daughter badly and gave up hope of an alliance between Eugenie Grandet and her son. Adolphe joined his father in Paris and became, it was said, worthless. The Cruchots triumphed.

“Your husband hasn’t common sense,” said Grandet as he lent Madame des Grassins money on a note securely endorsed. “I am very sorry for you, for you are a good little woman.”

“Ah, monsieur,” said the poor lady, “who could have believed that when he left Saumur to go to Paris on your business he was going to his ruin?”

“Heaven is my witness, madame, that up to the last moment I did all I could to prevent him from going. Monsieur le president was most anxious to take his place; but he was determined to go, and now we all see why.”

In this way Grandet made it plain he was under no moral obligation to des Grassins.

In all situations women have more cause for suffering than men, and they suffer more. Man has strength and the power of exercising it; he acts, moves, thinks, occupies himself; he looks ahead, and sees consolation in the future. It was thus with Charles. But the woman stays at home; she faces the grief from which nothing distracts her; she goes down to the abyss that yawns before her, measures it, and often fills it with her tears and prayers. Thus did Eugenie. To feel, to love, to suffer, to devote herself—is not this the sum of woman’s life? Eugenie was to be in all things a woman, except in the one thing that consoles for all. Her happiness, picked up like nails scattered on a wall—to use Bossuet’s simile—would never so much as fill the hollow of her hand. Sorrows are never long in coming; for her they came soon. The day after Charles’s departure the house resumed its ordinary look in the eyes of all except Eugenie, to whom it grew suddenly empty. She wished, if it could be done unknown to her father, that Charles’s room be kept as he had left it. Madame Grandet and Nanon were willing accomplices in this statu quo.

“Who knows but he may come back sooner than we think?” she said.

“Ah, don’t I wish I could see him back!” answered Nanon. “I took to him! He was such a dear, sweet young man—pretty too, with his curly hair.” Eugenie looked at Nanon. “Holy Virgin! don’t look at me that way, mademoiselle; your eyes are like those of a lost soul.”

From that day the beauty of Mademoiselle Grandet took on a new character. The solemn thoughts of love which filled her soul, and the dignity of the woman beloved, gave her features an illumination such as painters render by a halo. Before her cousin, Eugenie might be compared to the Virgin before the conception; after he had gone, she was like the Virgin Mother—she had given birth to love. These two Marys, so different, so well represented by Spanish art, embody one of those shining symbols with which Christianity abounds.

Returning from Mass the morning after Charles’s departure—having vowed to hear it daily—Eugenie bought a map of the world which she nailed beside her looking-glass, that she might follow her cousin westward, put herself day by day into the ship that bore him, and ask a thousand questions—“Art thou well? Dost thou suffer? Dost thou think of me when the star, whose beauty and usefulness thou hast taught me to know, shines upon thee?” In the mornings she sat pensive beneath the walnut-tree on the worm-eaten bench covered with gray lichens where they had said so many precious things, where they had built pretty castles of a future home. She thought of the future as she looked upward to the bit of sky the high walls allowed; then she turned her eyes to the angle where the sun crept, and to the roof above the room in which he had slept. Hers was the solitary, persistent love that glides into every thought and becomes the tissue of life. When Pere Grandet’s would-be friends came in the evening for their game at cards, she was gay and dissembling; but all the morning she talked of Charles with her mother and Nanon. Nanon had brought herself to pity her young mistress without failing in duty to the old master, and she would say to Eugenie,—

“If I had a man for myself I’d—I'd follow him to hell; yes, I’d exterminate myself for him; but I’ve none. I shall die and never know what life is. Would you believe, mamz’elle, that old Cornoiller (a good fellow all the same) is always round my petticoats for the sake of my money—just like the rats who come smelling after the master’s cheese and paying court to you? I see it all; I’ve got a shrewd eye, though I am as big as a steeple. Well, mamz’elle, it pleases me, but it isn’t love.”

Two months passed. Domestic life, once monotonous, quickened with the intense interest of a secret that bound the women together. For them Charles lived beneath the grim gray rafters of the hall. Night and morning Eugenie opened the dressing-case and gazed at her aunt’s portrait. One Sunday morning her mother discovered her absorbed in searching Charles’s features in his mother’s face. Madame Grandet was then for the first time drawn into the terrible secret: Charles had been given Eugenie’s treasure.

“You gave him all!” cried the poor mother, terrified. “What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?”

Eugenie’s eyes fixed, and the two women lived through mortal terror for more than half the morning. They were so troubled they missed high Mass and only went to the military service. In three days 1819 would end. In three days a terrible drama would begin — a bourgeois tragedy without poison or dagger, but, regarding the actors, more cruel than any fabled horror in the house of Atreus.

“What will become of us?” said Madame Grandet, letting her knitting fall. She had been so anxious for two months that the woollen sleeves she needed were unfinished. “I thought, my poor child, if you had confided in me we could have written to Monsieur des Grassins in Paris. He might have sent us gold pieces like yours; though Grandet knows them all, perhaps—”

“Where could we have got the money?” Eugenie answered.

“I would have pledged my property. Besides, Monsieur des Grassins would have—”

“It is too late,” said Eugenie in a broken voice. “Tomorrow morning we must go and wish him a happy New Year in his chamber.”

“But, my daughter, why not consult the Cruchots?”

“No, no; it would be delivering me up to them and putting ourselves in their power. Besides, I have chosen my course. I have done right and repent nothing. God will protect me. His will be done! Ah! mother, if you had read his letter, you would have thought only of him.”

January 1, 1820 dawned. The winter was one of the coldest of the epoch; snow lay thick on the roofs. The fear of Grandet’s inspection suggested a natural excuse to escape the formal entrance into his chamber.

Madame Grandet called when she heard him stirring. “Grandet, will you let Nanon light a fire here for me? The cold is so sharp I am freezing. Eugenie shall come and dress here; the poor child might get ill dressing in her cold room. Then we will go and wish you a happy New Year by the hall fire.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta, what a tongue! a pretty way to begin the new year, Madame Grandet!” he answered. After a pause he consented, with a gruff jest about the Bertellieres, coughed, and entered his wife’s room fully dressed. “Yes, it is cold enough to freeze you. We shall have a fine breakfast, wife. Des Grassins has sent me a truffled pate-de-foie-gras! I am going now to get it at the coach-office. There’ll be a double napoleon for Eugenie in the package,” he whispered. “I have no gold left, wife. I had a few stray pieces—but I had to let them go in business.”

He kissed her on the forehead and went out. The two women breathed a little.

Nanon entered to light the fire. “First he said, ‘Good-morning; happy New Year, you big fool! Go and light my wife’s fire, she’s cold’; and then he gave me a six-franc piece, which isn’t worn one bit. Oh, the kind man! He is a good man, that’s a fact. He’s getting soft, improving with time, like your ratafia! He’s a good, good man—”

Grandet’s joy sprang from the complete success of his speculation. Monsieur des Grassins, after deducting what Grandet owed for discounting a hundred and fifty thousand francs in Dutch notes and for the surplus he had advanced to make up the sum required for an investment yielding a hundred thousand francs a year, had sent him thirty thousand francs in silver, the remainder of his first half-year’s interest, and informed him the Funds had risen. They were then quoted at eighty-nine; the shrewdest bought later at ninety-three. Grandet had thus gained twelve percent on his capital in two months; he had simplified accounts and would henceforth receive fifty thousand francs interest every six months without taxes or repair costs. He understood investing in public securities; at the end of five years he found himself master of six million francs capital, which increased without much effort and, joined to his land, made him enormously wealthy. The six francs for Nanon were perhaps the reward for some small service rendered unknowingly.

“Oh! oh! where’s Pere Grandet going? He has been scurrying about since sunrise,” said the tradespeople as they opened shops. When they saw him returning from the wharf followed by a porter with a barrow laden with sacks, they commented on his gold. “Silver sous,” whispered the porter. “If you want me to take care of you, keep your tongue between your teeth,” Grandet warned as they reached the door. He handed twenty sous to someone and bade them be off. The sacks were carried to his inner room, where he shut himself in. “When breakfast is ready, knock on the wall,” he said, then disappeared.

The family breakfasted at ten. “Your father will not ask to see your gold downstairs,” Madame Grandet hoped after Mass. “You must pretend to be very chilly. We may have time to replace the treasure before your fête-day.”

Grandet came down thinking of his speculation and wondering how to metamorphose his Parisian silver into solid gold; he resolved to invest everything until the Funds reached par. Fatal reverie for Eugenie. As soon as he came in, the two women wished him a happy New Year—his daughter by putting her arms round his neck and caressing him; Madame Grandet gravely.

“Ha! ha! my child,” he said, kissing both her cheeks. “I work for you, don’t you see? I think of your happiness. Must have money to be happy. Here! there’s a new napoleon for you. I sent to Paris for it. On my word of honor, it’s all the gold I have; you are the only one that has any gold. I want to see your gold, little one.”

“Oh! it is too cold; let us have breakfast,” Eugenie answered.

“Well, after breakfast, then; it will help digestion. That fat des Grassins sent me the pate. Eat as much as you like, my children, it costs nothing. Des Grassins is getting along very well. I am satisfied with him. The old fish is doing Charles a good service, and gratis too. He is making a very good settlement of that poor deceased Grandet’s business. Hoo! hoo!” he muttered with his mouth full, “how good it is! Eat, wife; that will feed you for at least two days.”

“I am not hungry. I am very poorly; you know that.”

“Ah, bah! you can stuff yourself as full as you please without danger, you’re a Bertelliere; they are all hearty. You are a bit yellow, that’s true; but I like yellow, myself.”

The expectation of ignominious and public scrutiny is perhaps less horrible to a condemned criminal than the anticipation of what was coming after breakfast to Madame Grandet and Eugenie. The more gleefully the old man talked and ate, the more their hearts shrank. Eugenie, however, had an inward prop—she drew strength through love.

“For him! for him!” she cried within her, “I would die a thousand deaths.”

At this thought she shot a glance at her mother that flamed with courage.

“Clear away,” Grandet said to Nanon about eleven, “but leave the table. We can spread your little treasure upon it,” he said, looking at Eugenie. “Little? Faith! no; it isn’t little. You possess, in actual value, five thousand nine hundred and fifty-nine francs and the forty I gave you just now. That makes six thousand francs, less one. Well, now see here, little one! I’ll give you that one franc to make up the round number. Hey! what are you listening for, Nanon? Mind your own business; go and do your work.”

Nanon disappeared.

“Now listen, Eugenie; you must give me back your gold. You won’t refuse your father, my little girl, hein?”

The two women were dumb.

“I have no gold myself. I had some, but it is all gone. I’ll give you in return six thousand francs in livres, and you are to put them just where I tell you. You mustn’t think anything more about your ‘dozen.’ When I marry you (which will be soon) I shall get you a husband who can give you the finest ‘dozen’ ever seen in the provinces. Now attend to me, little girl. There’s a fine chance for you; you can put your six thousand francs into government funds, and you will receive every six months nearly two hundred francs interest, without taxes, or repairs, or frost, or hail, or floods, or anything else to swallow up the money. Perhaps you don’t like to part with your gold, hey, my girl? Never mind, bring it to me all the same. I’ll get you some more like it—like those Dutch coins and the portugaises, the rupees of Mogul, and the genovines—I’ll give you some on your fête-days, and in three years you’ll have got back half your little treasure. What’s that you say? Look up, now. Come, go and get it, the precious metal. You ought to kiss me on the eyelids for telling you the secrets and the mysteries of the life and death of money. Yes, silver and gold live and swarm like men; they come, and go, and sweat, and multiply—”

Eugenie rose; but after a few steps toward the door she turned abruptly, looked her father in the face, and said,—

“I have not got my gold.”

“You have not got your gold!” cried Grandet, starting up like a horse that hears a cannon.

“No, I have not got it.”

“You are mistaken, Eugenie.”

“No.”

“By the shears of my father!”

Whenever the old man swore that oath the rafters trembled.

“Holy Virgin! Madame is turning pale,” cried Nanon.

“Grandet, your anger will kill me,” said the poor mother.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! nonsense; you never die in your family! Eugenie, what have you done with your gold?” he cried, rushing upon her.

“Monsieur,” said the daughter, falling at Madame Grandet’s knees, “my mother is ill. Look at her; do not kill her.”

Grandet, frightened by his wife’s pallor, was momentarily softened. “Nanon, help me to bed,” she murmured; “I am dying—” Nanon gave her an arm, Eugenie another; with difficulty they got her upstairs; she fell with exhaustion at every step. Grandet remained. In a few moments he went up six or eight stairs and called,—

“Eugenie, when your mother is in bed, come down.”

“Yes, father.”

She soon came after reassuring her mother.

“My daughter,” Grandet said, “you will now tell me what you have done with your gold.”

“My father, if you make me presents of which I am not the sole mistress, take them back,” she answered coldly, picking up the napoleon from the chimney-piece and offering it to him.

Grandet seized the coin and slipped it into his breeches pocket. “I shall certainly never give you anything again. Not so much as that!” he said, clicking his thumb-nail against a front tooth. “Do you dare despise your father? have you no confidence in him? Don’t you know what a father is? If he is nothing for you, he is nothing at all. Where is your gold?”

“Father, I love and respect you, in spite of your anger; but I humbly ask you to remember I am twenty-three. You have told me I have attained my majority, and I do not forget it. I have used my money as I chose, and you may be sure it was put to a good use—”

“What use?”

“That is an inviolable secret,” she answered. “Have you no secrets?”

“I am the head of the family; I have my own affairs.”

“And this is mine.”

“It must be something bad if you can’t tell it to your father, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“It is good, and I cannot tell it to my father.”

“At least you can tell me when you parted with your gold?”

Eugenie shook her head.

“You had it on your birthday, hein?”

She repeated the negative sign, crafty through love as he was through avarice.

“Was there ever such obstinacy! It’s a theft,” cried Grandet, his voice rising through the house. “What! here, in my own home, under my very eyes, somebody has taken your gold—the only gold we have—and I’m not to know who has got it! Gold is a precious thing. Virtuous girls go wrong sometimes, and give—I don’t know what; they do it among the great people, and even among the bourgeoisie. But give their gold—for you have given it to some one, hein?”

Eugenie was silent and impassive.

“Was there ever such a daughter? Is it possible that I am your father? If you have invested it anywhere, you must have a receipt—”

“Was I free—yes or no—to do as I would with my own? Was it not mine?”

“You are a child.”

“Of age.”

Dumbfounded by his daughter’s logic, Grandet turned pale and stamped. When at last he found words, he cried: “Serpent! Cursed girl! Ah, deceitful creature! You know I love you, and you take advantage of it. She’d cut her father’s throat! Good God! you’ve given our fortune to that ne’er-do-well—that dandy with morocco boots! By the shears of my father! I can’t disinherit you, but I curse you—you and your cousin and your children! Nothing good will come of it! Do you hear? If it was to Charles—but, no; it’s impossible. What! has that wretched fellow robbed me?—”

He looked at his daughter, who continued cold and silent.

“She won’t stir; she won’t flinch! She’s more Grandet than I’m Grandet! Ha! you have not given your gold for nothing? Come, speak the truth!”

Eugenie looked at her father with a sarcastic expression that stung him.

“Eugenie, you are here, in my house—in your father’s house. If you wish to stay here, you must submit to me. The priests tell you to obey me.” Eugenie bowed. “You affront me in all I hold most dear. I will not see you again until you submit. Go to your chamber. You will stay there till I give you permission to leave it. Nanon will bring you bread and water. You hear me—go!”

Eugenie burst into tears and fled to her mother. Grandet, after marching two or three times round the garden in the snow without heeding the cold, suspected his daughter had gone to her mother; only too happy to find her disobedient, he climbed the stairs with the agility of a cat and appeared in Madame Grandet’s room just as she was stroking Eugenie’s hair, the girl’s face hidden on her motherly bosom.

“Be comforted, my poor child,” she was saying; “your father will get over it.”

“She has no father!” said the old man. “Can it be you and I, Madame Grandet, who have given birth to such a disobedient child? A fine education—religious, too! Well! why are you not in your chamber? Come, to prison, to prison, mademoiselle!”

“Would you deprive me of my daughter, monsieur?” said Madame Grandet, now red with fever.

“If you want to keep her, carry her off! Clear out—out of my house, both of you! Thunder! where is the gold? what’s become of the gold?”

Eugenie rose, looked proudly at her father, and withdrew to her room. Grandet turned the key.

“Nanon,” he cried, “put out the fire in the hall.”

Then he sat down in an armchair beside his wife’s fire and said to her,—

“Undoubtedly she has given the gold to that miserable seducer, Charles, who only wanted our money.”

“I knew nothing about it,” she answered, turning away from his savage glances. “I suffer so much from your violence that I shall never leave this room, if I trust my presentiments, till I am carried out in my coffin. You ought to have spared me this suffering, monsieur—you, to whom I have caused no pain, I think. Your daughter loves you. I believe her to be as innocent as an unborn babe. Do not make her wretched. Revoke your sentence. The cold is very severe; you may give her some serious illness.”

“I will not see her, neither will I speak to her. She shall stay in her room, on bread and water, until she submits to her father. What the devil! shouldn’t a father know where the gold in his house has gone to? She owned the only rupees in France, perhaps, and the Dutch ducats and the genovines—”

“Monsieur, Eugenie is our only child; and even if she had thrown them into the water—”

“Into the water!” cried her husband; “into the water! You are crazy, Madame Grandet! What I have said is said; you know that well enough. If you want peace in this household, make your daughter confess, pump it out of her. Women understand how to do that better than we do. Whatever she has done, I sha’n’t eat her. Is she afraid of me? Even if she has plastered Charles with gold from head to foot, he is on the high seas, and nobody can get at him, hein!”

“But, monsieur—” Excited by the crisis and by her daughter’s fate, Madame Grandet suddenly observed a frightful movement of her husband’s wen, and, in replying, she changed her speech without changing tone—“But, monsieur, I have not more influence over her than you have. She has said nothing to me; she takes after you.”

“Tut, tut! Your tongue is hung in the middle this morning. Ta, ta, ta, ta! You are setting me at defiance, I do believe. I daresay you are in league with her.”

He looked fixedly at his wife.

“Monsieur Grandet, if you wish to kill me, you have only to go on like this. I tell you, monsieur—and if it were to cost me my life, I would say it—you do wrong by your daughter; she is more in the right than you are. That money belonged to her; she is incapable of making any but a good use of it, and God alone has the right to know our good deeds. Monsieur, I implore you, take Eugenie back into favor; forgive her. If you do this you will lessen the injury your anger has done me; perhaps you will save my life. My daughter! oh, monsieur, give me back my daughter!”

“I shall decamp,” he said; “the house is not habitable. A mother and daughter talking and arguing like that! Broooouh! Pouah! A fine New Year’s present you’ve made me, Eugenie.” He lectured on Charles’s worth, his morocco boots and airs, and the ruin that would come.

When the street-door was shut, Eugenie came out of her room and went to her mother.

“What courage you have had for your daughter’s sake!” she said.

“Ah! my child, see where forbidden things may lead us. You forced me to tell a lie.”

“I will ask God to punish only me.”

“Is it true,” cried Nanon, rushing in alarm, “that mademoiselle is to be kept on bread and water for the rest of her life?”

“What does that signify, Nanon?” said Eugenie tranquilly.

“Goodness! do you suppose I’ll eat frippe when the daughter of the house is eating dry bread? No, no!”

“Don’t say a word about all this, Nanon,” Eugenie begged.

“I’ll be as mute as a fish; but you’ll see!”

Grandet dined alone for the first time in twenty-four years.

“So you’re a widower, monsieur,” said Nanon; “it must be disagreeable to be a widower with two women in the house.”

“I did not speak to you. Hold your jaw, or I’ll turn you off! What is that I hear boiling in your saucepan on the stove?”

“It is grease I’m trying out.”

“There will be some company tonight. Light the fire.”

The Cruchots, Madame des Grassins, and her son arrived at the usual hour of eight, surprised to see neither Madame Grandet nor her daughter.

“My wife is not very well, and Eugenie is with her,” said the old wine-grower, whose face betrayed no emotion.

After an hour of idle conversation, Madame des Grassins, who had gone up to see Madame Grandet, came down, and all asked, “How is Madame Grandet?”

“Not at all well,” she answered; “her condition seems really alarming. At her age you ought to take every precaution, Papa Grandet.”

“We’ll see about it,” said the old man absently.

They all wished him good-night. When the Cruchots reached the street, Madame des Grassins said, “There is something going on at the Grandets. The mother is very ill without her knowing it. The girl’s eyes are red, as if she had been crying all day. Can they be trying to marry her against her will?”

When Grandet had gone to bed Nanon came softly to Eugenie’s room in her stockinged feet and showed her a pate baked in a saucepan.

“See, mademoiselle,” said the good soul, “Cornoiller gave me a hare. You eat so little that this pate will last you a full week; in such frosty weather it won’t spoil. You sha’n’t live on dry bread, I’m determined; it isn’t wholesome.”

“Poor Nanon!” said Eugenie, pressing her hand.

“I’ve made it downright good and dainty, and he never found it out. I bought the lard and the spices out of my six francs: I’m the mistress of my own money”; and she disappeared rapidly, fancying she heard Grandet.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For months the old wine-grower constantly visited his wife’s room, never mentioning his daughter, seeing her, or alluding to her. Madame Grandet stayed in her chamber and daily grew worse. Nothing softened the old man; he remained harsh, cold as granite. He continued his business but ceased to stutter, spoke less, became more obdurate in transactions, and often erred in adding up his figures.

“Something is going on at the Grandets,” said the Grassinists and the Cruchotines.

“What has happened in the Grandet family?” became a fixed question at Saumur’s little evening-parties. Eugenie went to Mass escorted by Nanon. If Madame des Grassins spoke to her coming out of church, she answered evasively. After two months it became impossible to hide from the three Cruchots or Madame des Grassins that Eugenie was in confinement. Pretexts failed to explain her perpetual absence. Then, though it was impossible to discover who had betrayed the secret, all the town learned that since New Year’s day Mademoiselle Grandet had been kept in her room without fire, on bread and water, by her father’s orders, and that Nanon cooked little dainties and took them to her secretly at night. It was even known that the young woman could not see or care for her mother except at times when her father was out.

Grandet’s conduct was severely condemned. The town, remembering his treachery and hard-heartedness, more or less outlawed him. When he passed along the streets, people pointed and muttered. When his daughter came down the winding street, accompanied by Nanon, to Mass or Vespers, inhabitants ran to the windows and examined with intense curiosity the bearing of the rich heiress and her countenance, stamped by angelic gentleness and melancholy. Her imprisonment and her father’s condemnation were as nothing to her. Had she not a map of the world, the little bench, the garden, the angle of the wall? Did she not taste upon her lips the honey that love’s kisses left there? For a time she was unaware that the town talked about her, just as Grandet himself was. Pious and pure before God, her conscience and her love helped her suffer patiently her father’s wrath.

One deep grief silenced all others. Her mother, that gentle, tender creature, beautified by the inward light that shone as she approached the tomb, was perishing. Eugenie often reproached herself as the innocent cause of the slow malady wasting her away. This remorse, though soothed by her mother, bound her still closer to her love. Every morning, as soon as her father left the house, she went to her mother’s bedside, and Nanon brought her breakfast. The poor girl, sad and suffering through her mother’s pain, would turn her face to the old servant with a mute gesture, weeping, and yet not daring to speak of her cousin. Madame Grandet first found courage to say,—

“Where is he? Why does he not write?”

“Let us think about him, mother, but not speak of him. You are ill—you, before all.”

“All” meant “him.”

“My child,” said Madame Grandet, “I do not wish to live. God protects me and enables me to look with joy to the end of my misery.”

Every utterance of this woman was unfalteringly pious and Christian. Sometimes, during the first months of the year, when her husband came to breakfast and tramped about the room, she would speak a few religious words, always with angelic sweetness, yet with the firmness of one to whom approaching death lends courage she had lacked in life.

“Monsieur, I thank you for the interest you take in my health,” she would answer when he made some commonplace inquiry; “but if you really desire to render my last moments less bitter and ease my grief, take back your daughter: be a Christian, a husband, and a father.”

When he heard these words, Grandet would sit by the bed with the air of a man who sees rain coming and quietly shelters himself till it is over. After these touching, tender, religious supplications, he would say,—

“You are rather pale to-day, my poor wife.”

Absolute forgetfulness of his daughter seemed graven on his stony brow, on his closed lips. He was unmoved by the tears that flowed down his unhappy wife’s white cheeks as she listened to his meaningless answers.

“May God pardon you,” she said, “even as I pardon you! You will some day stand in need of mercy.”

Since Madame Grandet’s illness he had not dared to use his terrible “Ta, ta, ta, ta!” Yet his despotic nature was not disarmed by this angel of gentleness, whose ugliness daily decreased, driven out by the expression of moral qualities which shone upon her face. She was all soul. The spirit of prayer seemed to purify her and refine those homely features and make them luminous. This transformation wrought by the struggle that consumed the last shreds of her life did somewhat affect the old cooper, though feebly—for his nature was iron; if his language ceased to be contemptuous, an imperturbable silence, which saved his dignity as master, took its place and ruled his conduct.

When the faithful Nanon appeared in the market, many quips and complaints about the master whistled in her ears; but however loudly public opinion condemned Monsieur Grandet, the old servant defended him for the honor of the family.

“Well!” she would say to his detractors, “don’t we all get hard as we grow old? Why shouldn’t he? Stop telling lies. Mademoiselle lives like a queen. She’s alone, that’s true; but she likes it. Besides, my masters have good reasons.”

At last, towards the end of spring, Madame Grandet, worn out by grief even more than by illness, having failed, in spite of her prayers, to reconcile father and daughter, confided her troubles to the Cruchots.

“Keep a girl of twenty-three on bread and water!” cried Monsieur de Bonfons; “without any reason, too! That constitutes wrongful cruelty; she can contest, as much in as upon—”

“Come, nephew, spare us your legal jargon,” said the notary. “Set your mind at ease, madame; I will put a stop to such treatment to-morrow.”

Eugenie, hearing herself mentioned, came out of her room.

“Gentlemen,” she said with a proud step, “I beg you not to interfere. My father is master in his own house. As long as I live under his roof I must obey him. His conduct is not subject to the world’s approbation or disapprobation; he is accountable to God only. I appeal to your friendship to keep total silence in this affair. To blame my father is to attack our family honor. I thank you for your interest; you will do me an additional service if you put a stop to the offensive rumors current in the town, of which I am accidentally informed.”

“She is right,” said Madame Grandet.

“Mademoiselle, the best way to stop such rumors is to procure your liberty,” answered the old notary respectfully, struck with the beauty which seclusion, melancholy, and love had stamped upon her face.

“Well, my daughter, let Monsieur Cruchot manage the matter if he is so sure of success. He understands your father, and how to manage him. If you wish to see me happy for my few remaining days, you must, at any cost, be reconciled to your father.”

On the morrow Grandet, following a custom since Eugenie’s imprisonment, took a certain number of turns in the little garden; he chose the hour when Eugenie brushed and arranged her hair. When the old man reached the walnut-tree he hid behind its trunk and remained a few moments watching his daughter’s movements, hesitating between his obstinacy and his natural desire to embrace his child. Sometimes he sat on the rotten bench where Charles and Eugenie had vowed eternal love; she, too, looked at her father secretly in the mirror before which she stood. If he rose and continued his walk, she sat obligingly at the window and looked at the angle of the wall where pale flowers hung, where Venus-hair grew from crevices with bindweed and sedum. Maitre Cruchot came early and found the old wine-grower sitting in the fine June weather on the little bench, his back against the division wall, watching his daughter.

“What may you want, Maitre Cruchot?” he said, perceiving the notary.

“I came to speak on business.”

“Ah! ah! have you brought some gold in exchange for my silver?”

“No, not about money; it is about your daughter Eugenie. All the town is talking of her and you.”

“What does the town meddle for? A man’s house is his castle.”

“Very true; and a man may kill himself if he likes, or, what is worse, he may fling his money into the gutter.”

“What do you mean?”

“Why, your wife is very ill, my friend. You ought to consult Monsieur Bergerin; she is likely to die. If she does die without proper care you will not be easy, I take it.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! you know a deal about my wife! These doctors, if they once get their foot in, will come five and six times a day.”

“Of course you will do as you think best. We are old friends; there is no one in Saumur who takes more interest in what concerns you. I was bound to tell you this. However, happen what may, you have the right to choose your own course. Besides, that is not what brings me here. There is another thing which may have serious results for you. After all, you can’t wish to kill your wife; her life is too important to you. Think of your situation in connection with your daughter if Madame Grandet dies. You must render an account to Eugenie, because you enjoy your wife’s estate only during her lifetime. At her death your daughter can claim a division of property, and she may force you to sell Froidfond. In short, she is her mother’s heir, and you are not.”

These words fell like a thunderbolt on the old man, who was not as wise about law as in business. He had never thought of a legal division.

“Therefore I advise you to treat her kindly,” added Cruchot.

“But do you know what she has done, Cruchot?”

“What?” asked the notary, curious to hear the truth and find the cause of the quarrel.

“She has given away her gold!”

“Well, wasn’t it hers?” said the notary.

“They all tell me that!” exclaimed the old man, letting his arms fall with a movement truly tragic.

“Are you going—for a mere nothing,” resumed Cruchot, “to put obstacles in the way of concessions which you will be obliged to ask from your daughter as soon as her mother dies?”

“Do you call six thousand francs a mere nothing?”

“Hey! my old friend, do you know what the inventory of your wife’s property will cost, if Eugenie demands division?”

“How much?”

“Two, three, four thousand francs, perhaps! The property would have to be put up at auction and sold to get its actual value. Instead of that, if you are on good terms with—”

“By the shears of my father!” cried Grandet, turning pale as he suddenly sat down, “we will see about it, Cruchot.”

After a moment’s silence, full of anguish perhaps, the old man looked at the notary and said,—

“Life is very hard! It has many griefs! Cruchot,” he continued solemnly, “you would not deceive me? Swear to me upon your honor that all you’ve told me is legally true. Show me the law; I must see the law!”

“My poor friend,” said the notary, “don’t I know my own business?”

“Then it is true! I am robbed, betrayed, killed, destroyed by my own daughter!”

“It is true that your daughter is her mother’s heir.”

“Why do we have children? Ah! my wife, I love her! Luckily she’s sound and healthy; she’s a Bertelliere.”

“She has not a month to live.”

Grandet struck his forehead, went a few steps, came back, cast a dreadful look at Cruchot, and said,—

“What can be done?”

“Eugenie can relinquish her claim to her mother’s property. Should she do this you would not disinherit her, I presume?—but if you want such a settlement, you must not treat her harshly. What I am telling you, old man, is against my own interests. What do I live by, if it isn’t liquidations, inventories, conveyances, divisions of property?—”

“We’ll see, we’ll see! Don’t let’s talk any more about it, Cruchot; it wrings my vitals. Have you received any gold?”

“No; but I have a few old louis, a dozen or so, which you may have. My good friend, make it up with Eugenie. Don’t you know all Saumur is pelting you with stones?”

“The scoundrels!”

“Come, the Funds are at ninety-nine. Do be satisfied for once in your life.”

“At ninety-nine! Are they, Cruchot?”

“Yes.”

“Hey, hey! Ninety-nine!” repeated the old man, accompanying the notary to the street-door. Too agitated to stay, he went up to his wife’s room and said,—

“Come, mother, you may have your daughter to spend the day with you. I’m going to Froidfond. Enjoy yourselves, both of you. This is our wedding-day, wife. See! here are sixty francs for your altar at the Fete-Dieu; you’ve wanted one for a long time. Come, cheer up, enjoy yourself, and get well! Hurrah for happiness!”

He threw ten silver pieces of six francs each upon the bed, and took his wife’s head between his hands and kissed her forehead.

“My good wife, you are getting well, are you not?”

“How can you think of receiving the God of mercy in your house when you refuse to forgive your daughter?” she said with emotion.

“Ta, ta, ta, ta!” said Grandet in a coaxing voice. “We’ll see about that.”

“Merciful heaven! Eugenie,” cried the mother, flushing with joy, “come and kiss your father; he forgives you!”

But the old man had disappeared. He was going as fast as his legs could carry him toward his vineyards, trying to arrange his confused ideas. Grandet had entered his seventy-sixth year. During the last two years his avarice had increased, as persistent passions do at a certain age. As with other monomaniacs, his affections had fastened on one special symbol—gold. The sight and possession of gold had become a monomania. His despotic spirit had grown with his avarice, and to part with any control of his property at his wife’s death seemed to him “against nature.” To declare his fortune to his daughter, to inventory his property for division—

“Why,” he cried aloud in the midst of a field where he pretended to examine a vine, “it would be cutting my throat!”

He returned to Saumur in time for dinner, resolved to unbend to Eugenie and coax her, that he might die regally, holding the reins of his millions so long as he had breath. As he entered the house with his pass-key and climbed the stair stealthily to his wife’s room, Eugenie had brought the beautiful dressing-case from the oak cabinet and placed it on her mother’s bed. Mother and daughter, in Grandet’s absence, allowed themselves the pleasure of looking for a likeness to Charles in the portrait of his mother.

“It is exactly his forehead and his mouth,” Eugenie was saying as the old man opened the door. At the look which his glance cast upon the gold, Madame Grandet cried,—

“O God, have pity upon us!”

The old man sprang upon the box as a famished tiger might spring upon a sleeping child.

“What’s this?” he said, snatching the treasure and carrying it to the window. “Gold, good gold! All gold—it weighs two pounds! Ha, ha! Charles gave you that for your money, did he? Hein! Why didn’t you tell me so? It was a good bargain, little one! Yes, you are my daughter, I see that—” Eugenie trembled in every limb. “This came from Charles, of course, didn’t it?” continued the old man.

“Yes, father; it is not mine. It is a sacred trust.”

“Ta, ta, ta, ta! He took your fortune, and now you can get it back.”

“Father!”

Grandet took his knife to pry out some of the gold; he placed the dressing-case on a chair. Eugenie sprang forward to recover it; but her father, who had his eye on her and on the treasure, pushed her back so violently that she fell upon her mother’s bed.

“Monsieur, monsieur!” cried the mother, lifting herself.

Grandet had opened his knife and was about to apply it to the gold.

“Father!” cried Eugenie, falling on her knees and dragging herself close to him with clasped hands, “father, in the name of all the saints and the Virgin! in the name of Christ who died upon the cross! in the name of your eternal salvation, father! for my life’s sake, father—do not touch that! It is neither yours nor mine. It is a trust placed in my hands by an unhappy relation: I must give it back to him uninjured!”

“If it is a trust, why were you looking at it? To look is as bad as touching it.”

“Father, don’t destroy it, or you will disgrace me! Father, do you hear?”

“Oh, have pity!” said the mother.

“Father!” cried Eugenie in so startling a voice that Nanon ran upstairs terrified. Eugenie sprang upon a knife that was close at hand.

“Well, what now?” said Grandet coldly, with a callous smile.

“Oh, you are killing me!” said the mother.

“Father, if your knife so much as cuts a fragment of that gold, I will stab myself with this one! You have already driven my mother to her death; you will now kill your child! Do as you choose! Wound for wound!”

Grandet held his knife over the dressing-case and hesitated as he looked at his daughter.

“Are you capable of doing it, Eugenie?” he said.

“Yes, yes!” said the mother.

“She’ll do it if she says so!” cried Nanon. “Be reasonable, monsieur, for once in your life.”

The old man looked at the gold and then at his daughter alternately for an instant. Madame Grandet fainted.

“There! don’t you see, monsieur, that madame is dying?” cried Nanon.

“Come, come, my daughter, we won’t quarrel for a box! Here, take it!” he cried hastily, flinging the case upon the bed. “Nanon, go and fetch Monsieur Bergerin! Come, mother,” said he, kissing his wife’s hand, “it’s all over! There! we’ve made up—haven’t we, little one? No more dry bread; you shall have all you want—Ah, she opens her eyes! Well, mother, little mother, come! See, I’m kissing Eugenie! She loves her cousin, and she may marry him if she wants; she may keep his case. But don’t die, mother; live a long time yet, my poor wife! Come, try to move! Listen! you shall have the finest altar that ever was made in Saumur.”

“Oh, how can you treat your wife and daughter so!” said Madame Grandet in a feeble voice.

“I won’t do so again, never again,” cried her husband; “you shall both be happy. Hey, wife?”

“Alas! I wish I could, if it would give you pleasure,” said the dying woman; “but I cannot rise.”

“Poor mother,” said Grandet, “you don’t know how I love you! and you too, my daughter!” He took her in his arms and kissed her. “Oh, how good it is to kiss a daughter when we have been angry with her! There, mother, don’t you see it’s all over now? Go and put that away, Eugenie,” he added, pointing to the case. “Go, don’t be afraid! I shall never speak of it again, never!”

Monsieur Bergerin, the celebrated doctor of Saumur, presently arrived. After an examination, he told Grandet positively that his wife was very ill; but that perfect peace of mind, a generous diet, and great care might prolong her life until the autumn.

“Will all that cost much?” said the old man. “Will she need medicines?”

“Not much medicine, but a great deal of care,” answered the doctor, who could scarcely restrain a smile.

“Now, Monsieur Bergerin,” said Grandet, “you are a man of honor, are you not? I trust to you! Come and see my wife how and when you think necessary. Save my good wife! I love her,—don’t you see?—though I never talk about it; I keep things to myself. I’m full of trouble. Troubles began when my brother died; I have to spend enormous sums on his affairs in Paris. Why, I’m paying through my nose; there’s no end to it. Adieu, monsieur! If you can save my wife, save her. I’ll spare no expense, not even if it costs me a hundred or two hundred francs.”

In spite of Grandet’s fervent wishes for his wife’s health, in spite of the consideration he now showed and the tender care Eugenie lavished, Madame Grandet rapidly approached her end. Every day she grew weaker and visibly wasted, as women of her age attacked by serious illness do. She was fragile as autumn foliage; the radiance of heaven shone through her as the sun gilds withering leaves. It was a death worthy of her life—and is not that sublime? In October, 1822, her virtues, angelic patience, and love for her daughter seemed to find special expression; then she passed away without a murmur. Lamb without spot, she went to heaven, regretting only the sweet companion of her cold and dreary life, for whom her last glance seemed to prophesy a destiny of sorrows. She shrank from leaving her ewe-lamb, white as herself, alone in the midst of a selfish world that sought to strip her of her fleece and grasp her treasures.

“My child,” she said as she expired, “there is no happiness except in heaven; you will know it some day.”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after his death Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to the house of her birth, where she had suffered so much and where her mother had just died. She could not see the window or the chair on its castors without weeping. She thought she had mistaken the heart of her old father when she found herself the object of his tenderest care. He came in the morning and gave her his arm to breakfast; he looked at her with a kind eye and brooded over her as though she had been gold. The old man trembled so often before his daughter that Nanon and the Cruchotines, observing his weakness, attributed it to age and feared his faculties were failing. But the day the family put on mourning, after dinner, and Maitre Cruchot (the only one who knew his secret) had been invited, the old miser’s conduct was explained.

“My dear child,” he said to Eugenie when the table had been cleared and the doors carefully shut, “you are now your mother’s heiress, and we have a few little matters to settle between us. Isn’t that so, Cruchot?”

“Yes.”

“Is it necessary to talk of them to-day, father?”

“Yes, yes, little one; I can’t bear the uncertainty. I think you don’t want to give me pain?”

“Oh! father—”

“Well then! let us settle it to-night.”

“What is it you wish me to do?”

“My little girl, it is not for me to say. Tell her, Cruchot.”

“Mademoiselle, your father does not wish to divide the property, nor sell the estate, nor pay enormous taxes on the ready money which he may possess. Therefore, to avoid all this, he must be released from making the inventory of his whole fortune, part of which you inherit from your mother, and which is now undivided between you and your father—”

“Cruchot, are you quite sure of what you are saying before you tell it to a mere child?”

“Let me tell it my own way, Grandet.”

“Yes, yes, my friend. Neither you nor my daughter wish to rob me,—do you, little one?”

“But, Monsieur Cruchot, what am I to do?” said Eugenie impatiently.

“Well,” said the notary, “it is necessary to sign this deed by which you renounce your rights to your mother’s estate and leave your father the use and disposition, during his lifetime, of all the property undivided between you, of which he guarantees you the capital.”

“I do not understand a word of what you are saying,” returned Eugenie; “give me the deed, and show me where I am to sign it.”

Pere Grandet looked alternately at the deed and at his daughter, undergoing such violent emotion that he wiped the sweat from his brow.

“My little girl,” he said, “if, instead of signing this deed, which will cost a great deal to record, you would simply agree to renounce your rights as heir to your poor dear, deceased mother’s property, and would trust to me for the future, I should like it better. In that case I will pay you monthly the good round sum of a hundred francs. See, now, you could pay for as many masses as you want for anybody—Hein! a hundred francs a month—in livres?”

“I will do all you wish, father.”

“Mademoiselle,” said the notary, “it is my duty to point out to you that you are despoiling yourself without guarantee—”

“Good heavens! what is all that to me?”

“Hold your tongue, Cruchot! It’s settled, all settled,” cried Grandet, taking his daughter’s hand and striking it with his own. “Eugenie, you won’t go back on your word?—you are an honest girl, hein?”

“Oh! father—”

He kissed her effusively and pressed her in his arms till he almost choked her.

“Go, my good child, you restore your father’s life; but you only return to him that which he gave you: we are quits. This is how business should be done. Life is a business. I bless you! you are a virtuous girl, and you love your father. Do just what you like in future. To-morrow, Cruchot,” he added, looking at the horrified notary, “you will see about preparing the deed of relinquishment, and then enter it on the records of the court.”

The next morning Eugenie signed the papers by which she completed her own spoliation. Yet in the first year, in spite of his bargain, the old man had not paid her one sou of the hundred francs he had pledged. When Eugenie reminded him, he colored, went to his secret hiding-place, brought down about a third of the jewels he had taken from his nephew, and threw them into her apron.

“There, little one,” he said in a sarcastic tone, “do you want those for your twelve hundred francs?”

“Oh! father, truly? will you really give them to me?”

“I’ll give you as many more next year,” he said, rubbing his hands, delighted to speculate on his daughter’s feelings.

Nevertheless, though still robust, he felt it necessary to initiate her into the secrets of his thrift. For two years he made her order the household meals in his presence and receive the rents; he taught her the names and yield of his vineyards and farms. By the third year his avaricious methods had become her habits, and he could leave the household keys in her charge without anxiety, installing her as mistress of the house.

Five years passed without an event to relieve the monotonous existence of Eugenie and her father. The same actions were performed daily with clockwork regularity. The deep sadness of Mademoiselle Grandet was known to all; but if others surmised the cause, she herself never uttered a word to justify the suspicions about her heart. Her only society was the three Cruchots and a few of their friends whom they had, little by little, introduced into the Grandet household. They had taught her to play whist and came every night for their game. In 1827 her father, feeling his infirmities, further instructed her in the management of his landed property and told her to have recourse to Maitre Cruchot in difficulty.

Towards the end of that year the old man, then eighty-two, was seized by paralysis which advanced rapidly. Dr. Bergerin gave him up. Eugenie, feeling she would soon be alone, clung more tightly to the last living link of affection. To her, love was life. Charles was absent, and she devoted all her care to her father, whose faculties weakened though his avarice remained instinctively acute. The dying man had his chair placed facing the door of the secret room, and he asked for an explanation of every noise, even the watch-dog’s yawn. He woke at the day rents were due, or when accounts had to be settled; then he made his chair face the inner room, made his daughter open it, watched while she placed the bags of money in his secret receptacle, and fingered the key in his waistcoat pocket.

His friend the notary, sure that the heiress would marry his nephew the president if Charles did not return, redoubled his attentions: he took Grandet’s orders, ran errands to Froidfond, sold the vintages, and turned everything into gold and silver which found their way in sacks to the hiding-place.

At last the last struggle came. The old man rolled up all his coverings and said to Nanon, “Put them away, lock them up, for fear they should be stolen.” So long as he could open his eyes his whole being fixed on the door behind which his treasures lay, asking, “Are they there? are they there?” in a tone of panic.

“Yes, my father,” she would answer.

“Take care of the gold—put gold before me.”

Eugenie spread coins on a table before him, and he sat for hours with his eyes fixed on them, like a child newly seeing. “It warms me!” he sometimes said. When the cure came to administer the last sacraments, the old man’s eyes kindled at the sight of the cross and holy vessels of silver; he gazed fixedly, moved his wen for the last time, and, when the priest put the crucifix to his lips, made a frightful gesture as if to seize it; that effort cost him his life. He called Eugenie, whom he did not see though she knelt beside him bathing his stiffening hand.

“My father, bless me!” she entreated.

“Take care of it all. You will render me an account yonder!” he said, proving by these last words that Christianity must always be the religion of misers.

Eugenie Grandet was now alone in that gray house, with none but Nanon to whom she could turn with certainty of being heard and understood—Nanon, the sole being who loved her for herself and with whom she could speak of her sorrows. La Grande Nanon was a providence; not merely a servant but a humble friend. After her father’s death Eugenie learned from Maitre Cruchot that she possessed an income of three hundred thousand francs from landed and personal property in the arrondissement of Saumur; six millions invested at three percent in the Funds (bought at sixty, then worth seventy-six franc); two millions in gold coin, and a hundred thousand francs in silver crown-pieces, besides unpaid interest. The sum total reached seventeen millions.

“Where is my cousin?” was her sole thought.

The day Maitre Cruchot handed her a clear schedule of the whole inheritance, Eugenie sat with Nanon by the vacant hall fireplace, where everything now was memory, from her mother’s chair on castors to the glass from which her cousin had drunk.

“Nanon, we are alone—”

“Yes, mademoiselle; and if I knew where he was, the darling, I’d go on foot to find him.”

“The ocean is between us,” she said.

While the poor heiress wept with an old servant in that cold, dark house which was to her the universe, the whole province, from Nantes to Orleans, rung with the news of her seventeen millions. Among her first acts she settled an annuity of twelve hundred francs on Nanon, who, already possessed of six hundred more, became a rich and enviable match. Less than a month later that good soul passed from single life to wedded life under the protection of Antoine Cornoiller, appointed keeper of Mademoiselle Grandet’s estates. Madame Cornoiller, though fifty-nine, looked not more than forty; her strong features resisted time. Thanks to semi-conventual habits she laughed at old age with rosy skin and iron constitution. Perhaps she never looked so well as on her marriage-day. Big, fat, and strong, she gave an appearance of happiness that made Cornoiller’s bargain envied.

“Fast colors!” said the draper.

“Quite likely to have children,” said the salt merchant. “She’s pickled in brine, saving your presence.”

“She is rich, and that fellow Cornoiller has done a good thing for himself,” said a third man.

On her way to church, Nanon, beloved by the neighborhood, received many compliments. Eugenie had given her three dozen silver forks and spoons as a wedding present. Cornoiller, amazed, spoke of his mistress with tears in his eyes; he would willingly have been hacked in pieces for her. Madame Cornoiller, appointed housekeeper to Mademoiselle Grandet, took as much happiness from her new function as from her husband: she took charge of weekly accounts, locked up provisions and gave them out daily in the old manner; she ruled two servants, a cook and a maid to mend linen and make dresses. Cornoiller combined the functions of keeper and bailiff. The women-servants chosen were “perfect treasures.” Thus Mademoiselle Grandet had four devoted servants. The farmers perceived no change; the usages established by Monsieur Grandet were scrupulously carried out by Monsieur and Madame Cornoiller.

At thirty Eugenie knew none of life’s joys. Her pale, sad childhood had glided beside a mother whose heart, always wounded, had known only suffering. The mother, leaving life joyfully, pitied her daughter and left in her some fugitive remorse and many lasting regrets. Eugenie’s first and only love had been a wellspring of sadness. Meeting her lover for a few days she gave him her heart between two kisses; then he left and a whole world lay between them. This love, cursed by her father, had cost her mother’s life and brought Eugenie only sorrow and frail hopes. Her upward spring toward happiness had wasted her strength and given nothing in exchange. The life of the soul needs to absorb another soul’s sentiments and assimilate them; without that respiration the heart perishes. Eugenie had begun to suffer. Wealth was neither power nor consolation; she could not live except through love, religion, and faith in the future. Love explained to her the mysteries of eternity. Her heart and the Gospel taught her to know two worlds; she bathed in two infinite thoughts which may have had but one meaning. She drew back within herself, loving and believing herself beloved. For seven years her passion invaded everything. Her treasuries were not the millions but Charles’s dressing-case, the portraits above her bed, the jewels recovered from her father and spread upon wool in a drawer, the thimble of her aunt used by her mother, which she wore while embroidering—a Penelope’s web begun to present that gold upon her finger.

It seemed unlikely Mademoiselle Grandet would marry during mourning. Her genuine piety was known. The Cruchots, guided by the old abbé, contented themselves with surrounding the heiress and paying affectionate attentions. Every evening the hall filled with devoted Cruchotines who sang the praises of their mistress in every key. She had her doctor in ordinary, her grand almoner, her chamberlain, her first lady of honor, her prime minister; above all, her chancellor, a chancellor who would fain have said much to her. If she had wished for a train-bearer, one would have been found. She was a queen, obsequiously flattered. Flattery never comes from noble souls; it is the gift of little minds who belittle themselves to worm into a person’s favor. So the people who nightly assembled in Mademoiselle Grandet’s house (they called her Mademoiselle de Froidfond) outdid each other in expressions of admiration. The novelty made her blush; but insensibly she grew accustomed to praise, and if any newcomer seemed to find her plain she would have felt the reproach far more than she might have eight years earlier. She ended by loving the incense she secretly laid at the feet of her idol. By degrees she grew used to being treated as a sovereign and to seeing her court press around her each evening.

Monsieur de Bonfons was the hero of the little circle: his wit, person, education, and amiability were perpetually praised. One or another remarked that in seven years he had largely increased his fortune, that Bonfons brought in at least ten thousand francs a year, and was surrounded, like the other possessions of the Cruchots, by the vast domains of the heiress.

“Do you know, mademoiselle,” said a habitual visitor, “that the Cruchots have an income of forty thousand francs among them!”

“And then, their savings!” exclaimed Mademoiselle de Gribeaucourt.

“A gentleman from Paris has offered Monsieur Cruchot two hundred thousand francs for his practice,” said another. “He will sell it if appointed juge de paix.”

“He wants to succeed Monsieur de Bonfons as president of the Civil courts, and is taking measures,” replied Madame d’Orsonval. “Monsieur le president will certainly be made councillor.”

“Yes, he is a very distinguished man,” said another—“don’t you think so, mademoiselle?”

Monsieur de Bonfons tried to keep pace with the role he sought. In spite of forty years and withered features, he dressed in youthful fashions, toyed with a bamboo cane, avoided snuff in Mademoiselle de Froidfond’s house, and came in a white cravat and a pleated shirt frill, giving him a family resemblance to turkeys. He addressed the heiress familiarly as “Our dear Eugenie.” Except for more visitors, the change from loto to whist, and the absence of Monsieur and Madame Grandet, the scene remained as when this story opened. The pack still pursued Eugenie and her millions; but the hounds, more numerous, lay better on the scent and beset the prey more unitedly. If Charles had dropped from the Indian Isles he would have found the same people and the same interests. Madame des Grassins, to whom Eugenie was courteous, still tormented the Cruchots. Eugenie remained the central figure; and Charles, as before, would still have been sovereign. Yet there had been small progress. The flowers which the president formerly presented on her birthdays had become a daily institution. Every evening he brought the rich heiress a huge bouquet, which Madame Cornoiller placed conspicuously in a vase and secretly threw into a corner of the court-yard when the visitors had departed.

Early in spring Madame des Grassins tried to disturb the Cruchotines by speaking to Eugenie of the Marquis de Froidfond, whose ancient and ruined family might be restored if the heiress returned his estates through marriage. Madame des Grassins rang the changes on peerage and the marquise title until, mistaking Eugenie’s disdainful smile for acquiescence, she went about proclaiming that the marriage with “Monsieur Cruchot” was not as certain as people thought.

“Though Monsieur de Froidfond is fifty,” she said, “he does not look older than Monsieur Cruchot. He is a widower and has children, that’s true. But then he is a marquis; he will be peer of France; and in times like these where will you find a better match? I know for a fact that Pere Grandet, when he put all his money into Froidfond, intended to graft himself upon that stock; he often told me so. He was a deep one, that old man!”

“Ah! Nanon,” said Eugenie one night as she was going to bed, “how is it that in seven years he has never once written to me?”

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
While these events happened in Saumur, Charles was making his fortune in the Indies. His commercial outfit sold well; he realized six thousand dollars. Crossing the line cleared many cobwebs from his brain; he perceived that the surest way to fortune in the tropics, as in Europe, was to buy and sell men. He went to the African coast and bought Negroes, combining this traffic with other merchandise. He plunged into the business tirelessly, driven by the desire to return to Paris with the prestige of a large fortune and the hope of regaining an even more brilliant position than the one he had lost.

Jostling with men, traveling through many lands, and observing conflicting customs modified his ideas and made him skeptical. He ceased to have fixed principles, since what one country called crime another praised as virtue. In the perpetual struggle of selfish interests his heart grew cold and contracted. The blood of the Grandets did its work: Charles became hard and eager for prey. He sold Chinamen, Negroes, bird-nests, children, artists; practiced usury on a large scale; his habit of defrauding custom-houses made him less scrupulous about others’ rights. He went to St. Thomas and bought, for a song, goods captured by pirates and took them to ports where he could sell them well. If Eugenie’s pure face had accompanied him on his first voyage, like an image of the Virgin on a mast, and if he attributed his first success to her prayers, later women—blacks, mulattoes, whites, Indian dancing-girls—orgies and adventures effaced recollection of his cousin, Saumur, the house, the bench, the stolen kiss. He remembered only the little garden behind the crumbling wall, where he had learned his fate; but he rejected his family. His uncle was an old dog who had filched his jewels; Eugenie had no place in his heart, though she remained in his accounts as a creditor for six thousand francs.

Such conduct explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies, at St. Thomas, on the African coast, at Lisbon, and in the United States, the adventurer used the name Shepherd so as not to compromise his family. As Charles Shepherd he could be indefatigable, bold, grasping, and greedy, like a man resolved to snatch his fortune quibuscumque viis and get the villainy over with to live honestly thereafter.

With such methods prosperity was rapid; and in 1827 Charles returned to Bordeaux on the brig Marie Caroline, belonging to a royalist house. He brought nineteen hundred thousand francs worth of gold-dust, from which he expected another seven or eight per cent at the Paris mint. On the brig he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman-in-ordinary to Charles X., a worthy old man who had married a fashionable woman with a fortune from the West Indies. To meet Madame d’Aubrion’s extravagance he had gone to the Indies to sell property and was now returning with his family.

Monsieur and Madame d’Aubrion, of the old southern family d’Aubrion de Buch, were reduced to about twenty thousand francs income and had an ugly daughter whom the mother was resolved to marry off without a dot—the family fortune barely sufficing for her own expenses in Paris. The mother almost despaired of placing such a girl, but she made the best of her. Mademoiselle d’Aubrion was long, spare, with a disdainful mouth and a nose too long, thick at the end, sallow normally and very red after a meal—an unpleasant feature in a pale, dull face. Madame d’Aubrion made her daughter appear distinguished: hygienic treatment kept the nose manageable, she taught the art of dressing, charming manners, melancholy glances that interest a man, and the coquettish peep of the foot beneath a petticoat at the moment the nose reddened. By full sleeves, deceptive pads, puffed dresses, and tight corsets she had achieved strange feminine developments worth showing as an example to other mothers.

Charles became intimate with Madame d’Aubrion precisely because she sought his intimacy. Persons on the brig said the handsome Madame neglected no means to secure so rich a son-in-law. Landing at Bordeaux in June 1827, Monsieur, Madame, Mademoiselle d’Aubrion, and Charles lodged at the same hotel and set out together for Paris. The hotel d’Aubrion was hampered with mortgages; Charles was to free it. The mother told him she would gladly give the ground-floor to a son-in-law. Not sharing Monsieur d’Aubrion’s prejudices about nobility, she promised Charles that she would obtain a royal ordinance from Charles X. allowing him to take the d’Aubrion name and arms and, by buying the entailed estate for thirty-six thousand francs a year, to succeed to the titles of Captal de Buch and Marquis d’Aubrion. By uniting fortunes, living together, and profiting by sinecures, the two families might occupy the hotel d’Aubrion with an income exceeding a hundred thousand francs.

“And when a man has a hundred thousand francs a year, a name, a family, and a position at court—for I will get you appointed gentleman-of-the-bedchamber—you can do what you like,” she told Charles. “You can become master of the rolls, prefect, secretary to an embassy, ambassador if you like. Charles X. is fond of d’Aubrion; they have known each other from childhood.”

Intoxicated with ambition, Charles toyed with the hopes thus presented in confidences poured from heart to heart. Believing his father’s affairs settled by his uncle, he imagined himself anchored in the Faubourg Saint-Germain—reappearing as the Comte d’Aubrion, like the Dreux reappearing in Bréze. Dazzled by the Restoration’s prosperity, which had been tottering when he left France, fascinated by aristocratic splendor, his intoxication, begun on the brig, increased after he reached Paris, and he resolved to take the course his would-be mother-in-law indicated. His cousin counted for no more than a speck in this brilliant prospect; but he went to see Annette. A woman of the world, Annette advised him to marry and promised her support in his ambitious projects. In her heart she was delighted to fasten an ugly, uninteresting girl on Charles, whose West Indies life had made him very attractive: bronzed complexion, bold, decisive manners like a man used to sharp decisions, to rule, and to succeed. Charles breathed easier in Paris, conscious of his part to play.

Des Grassins, hearing of his return, his approaching marriage, and his fortune, came to see him and inquired about the three hundred thousand francs still required to settle his father’s debts. He found Grandet conferring with a goldsmith about jewels for Mademoiselle d’Aubrion’s corbeille. Charles had brought magnificent diamonds; the setting with plate and jewelry for the new establishment amounted to over two hundred thousand francs. When des Grassins, who had already called several times, presented himself, Charles treated him with the impertinence of a young man of fashion conscious of having killed four men in as many duels in the Indies. Coldly, and without fully grasping the plea, Charles replied:

“My father’s affairs are not mine. I am much obliged, monsieur, for the trouble you have been good enough to take—by which, however, I really cannot profit. I have not earned two millions by the sweat of my brow to fling them at the head of my father’s creditors.”

“But suppose that your father’s estate were within a few days to be declared bankrupt?”

“Monsieur, in a few days I shall be called the Comte d’Aubrion; you will understand, therefore, that what you threaten is of no consequence to me. Besides, you know as well as I do that when a man has an income of a hundred thousand francs his father has never failed.” So saying, he edged Monsieur des Grassins to the door.

At the beginning of August that year, Eugenie was sitting on the little wooden bench where her cousin had sworn to love her eternally, where she usually breakfasted when the weather was fine. The poor girl was happy for the moment in the fresh summer air, letting memory recall the great and little events of her love and the catastrophes that followed. The sun had just reached the angle of the ruined wall full of chinks that no one, by the mistress’s caprice, was allowed to touch, though Cornoiller often told his wife it would fall and crush somebody one day. At that moment the postman knocked and gave a letter to Madame Cornoiller, who ran into the garden, crying:

“Mademoiselle, a letter!” She gave it to her mistress, adding, “Is it the one you expected?”

The words rang as loudly in Eugenie’s heart as they echoed from wall to wall of the court and garden.

“Paris—from him—he has returned!”

Eugenie turned pale and held the letter for a moment. She trembled and could not break the seal. La Grande Nanon stood before her, hands on her hips, her joy showing in the lines of her face.

“Read it, mademoiselle!”

“Ah, Nanon, why did he return to Paris? He went from Saumur.”

“Read it, and you’ll find out.”

Eugenie opened the letter with trembling fingers. A cheque on the house of “Madame des Grassins and Coret, of Saumur,” fluttered out. Nanon picked it up.

My dear Cousin,—

“No longer ‘Eugenie,’” she thought, and her heart quailed.

You—

“He once said ‘thou.’” She folded her arms and dared not read further; great tears gathered.

“Is he dead?” asked Nanon.

“If he were, he could not write,” said Eugenie.

She read the whole letter, which ran as follows:

My dear Cousin,—You will, I am sure, hear with pleasure of the success of my enterprise. You brought me luck; I have come back rich, and I have followed my uncle’s advice, whose death, together with my aunt’s, I have just learned from Monsieur des Grassins. The death of parents is in the course of nature, and we must succeed them. I trust you are by this time consoled. Nothing can resist time, as I know. Yes, my dear cousin, the day of illusions is, unfortunately, gone for me. How could it be otherwise? Traveling through many lands, I have reflected on life. I was a child when I went away—I have come back a man. Today I think of many I did not dream of then. You are free, my dear cousin, and I am free still. Nothing apparently hinders the realization of our early hopes; but my nature is too loyal to hide from you the situation in which I find myself. I have not forgotten our relations; I have always remembered, throughout my long wanderings, the little wooden seat—

Eugenie rose as if sitting on live coals and went to the stone steps of the court.

—the little wooden seat where we vowed to love each other forever, the passage, the gray hall, my attic chamber, and the night when, by your delicate kindness, you made my future easier. Yes, these recollections sustained my courage; I said in my heart that you were thinking of me at the hour we had agreed upon. Have you always looked at the clouds at nine o’clock? Yes, I am sure of it. I cannot betray so true a friendship—no, I must not deceive you. An alliance has been proposed to me which satisfies all my ideas of matrimony. Love in marriage is a delusion. My experience warns me that in marrying we are bound to obey social laws and meet the world’s conventional demands. Between you and me there are differences which might affect your future more than mine. I will not speak here of your customs, inclinations, education, nor your habits, none of which fit Parisian life or the future I have marked out. My intention is to keep my household stately, to receive much company—in short, to live in the world; and I think I remember that you love a quiet and tranquil life. I will be frank and make you judge my situation; you have the right to understand and judge it.

I possess now an income of eighty thousand francs. This fortune enables me to marry into the Aubrion family, whose heiress, nineteen years old, brings me a title, a place as gentleman-of-the-bedchamber, and a brilliant position. I will admit, my dear cousin, that I do not love Mademoiselle d’Aubrion; but in marrying her I secure to my children a social rank whose advantages will become incalculable: monarchical principles are gaining favor. Thus my son, when he becomes Marquis d’Aubrion, having an entailed estate with a rental of forty thousand francs a year, can obtain any State position he chooses. We owe ourselves to our children.

You see, my cousin, with what good faith I lay my heart, hopes, and fortune before you. Possibly, after seven years’ separation, you have yourself forgotten our youthful loves; but I have never forgotten your kindness or my own words. I remember all, even lightly uttered words—words by which a less conscientious man, with a heart less upright, would scarcely feel bound. In telling you the marriage I propose is solely one of convenience, that I still remember our childish love, am I not putting myself entirely in your hands and making you the mistress of my fate? Am I not telling you that if I must renounce my social ambitions, I shall willingly content myself with the pure and simple happiness of which you have shown me so sweet an image?

“Tan, ta, ta—tan, ta, ti,” sang Charles Grandet to the air of Non più andrai, as he signed himself,—

Your devoted cousin, Charles.

“Thunder! that’s doing it handsomely!” he said, looking for the cheque; having found it, he added:

P.S.—I enclose a cheque on the des Grassins bank for eight thousand francs to your order, payable in gold, which includes the capital and interest of the sum you were kind enough to lend me. I am expecting a case from Bordeaux which contains a few things which you must allow me to offer you as a mark of my unceasing gratitude. You can send my dressing-case by the diligence to the hotel d’Aubrion, rue Hillerin-Bertin.

“By the diligence!” said Eugenie. “A thing for which I would have laid down my life!”

Terrible and utter disaster! The ship had gone down, leaving not a spar, not a plank, on a vast ocean of hope. Some women, abandoned, will try to tear a lover from a rival, commit crimes, or rush to the ends of the earth. Others bow their heads and suffer in silence; they go their way dying, resigned, weeping, forgiving, praying, and recollecting until the last breath. This was Eugenie’s love after she read that dreadful letter. She raised her eyes to heaven, thinking of her dying mother’s last words, who, with the prescience of death, had looked into the future: Eugenie, remembering that prophetic life, measured her destiny. Nothing was left for her; she could only unfold her wings, stretch upward, and live in prayer until deliverance.

“My mother was right,” she said, weeping. “Suffer—and die!”

Eugenie came slowly back from the garden to the house, avoiding the corridor. The memory of her cousin lingered in the gray old hall and on the chimney-piece, where stood a saucer and the Sèvres sugar-bowl she used each morning.

This day was to be solemn and eventful. Nanon announced the parish cure, connected to the Cruchots and allied with Monsieur de Bonfons’s interests. For some time the old abbé had urged him, from a religious point of view, to speak to Mademoiselle Grandet about the duty of marriage for a woman in her position. When she saw her pastor, Eugenie supposed he had come for the thousand francs she gave monthly to the poor, and she told Nanon to fetch them; the cure only smiled.

“To-day, mademoiselle,” he said, “I have come to speak to you about a poor girl in whom the whole town of Saumur takes an interest, who, through lack of charity to herself, neglects her Christian duties.”

“Monsieur le curé, you have come to me at a moment when I cannot think of my neighbor; I am filled with thoughts of myself. I am very unhappy; my only refuge is in the Church; her bosom is large enough to hold all human woe, her love so full that we may draw from its depths and never drain it dry.”

“Mademoiselle, in speaking of this young girl we shall speak of you. Listen! If you wish to insure your salvation you have only two paths—either leave the world or obey its laws. Obey either your earthly destiny or your heavenly destiny.”

“Ah! your voice speaks to me when I need to hear a voice. Yes, God has sent you to me; I will bid farewell to the world and live for God alone, in silence and seclusion.”

“My daughter, you must think long before you take so violent a step. Marriage is life, the veil is death.”

“Yes, death,—a quick death!” she said, with dreadful eagerness.

“Death? but you have great obligations to society, mademoiselle. Are you not the mother of the poor, to whom you give clothes and wood in winter and work in summer? Your great fortune is a loan which you must return, and you have sacredly accepted it as such. To bury yourself in a convent would be selfishness; to remain an old maid is to fail in duty. In the first place, can you manage your vast property alone? May you not lose it? You will have lawsuits, you will find yourself surrounded by inextricable difficulties. Believe your pastor: a husband is useful; you are bound to preserve what God has bestowed upon you. I speak to you as a precious lamb of my flock. You love God too truly not to find your salvation in the midst of his world, of which you are a noble ornament and to which you owe your example.”

At this moment Madame des Grassins was announced. She came driven by vengeance and despair.

“Mademoiselle,” she said—“Ah! here is monsieur le curé; I am silent. I came to speak to you on business; but I see that you are conferring with—”

“Madame,” said the curé, “I leave the field to you.”

“Oh! monsieur le curé,” said Eugenie, “come back later; your support is very necessary to me just now.”

“Ah, yes, indeed, my poor child!” said Madame des Grassins.

“What do you mean?” asked Eugenie and the curé together.

“Don’t I know about your cousin’s return, and his marriage with Mademoiselle d’Aubrion? A woman doesn’t carry her wits in her pocket.”

Eugenie blushed and was silent for a moment. From this day forth she assumed the impassible countenance for which her father had been remarkable.

“Well, madame,” she presently said, ironically, “no doubt I carry my wits in my pocket, for I do not understand you. Speak, say what you mean, before monsieur le curé; you know he is my director.”

“Well, then, mademoiselle, here is what des Grassins writes me. Read it.”

Eugenie read the letter:

  My dear Wife,—Charles Grandet has returned from the Indies and has been in Paris about a month—

“A month!” thought Eugenie, her hand falling to her side. After a pause she resumed the letter,—

  I had to dance attendance before I was allowed to see the future Vicomte d’Aubrion. Though all Paris is talking of his marriage and the banns are published—

“He wrote to me after that!” thought Eugenie. She did not cry out, as a Parisian woman would have done, “The villain!” but though she said it not, contempt was none the less present in her mind.

  The marriage, however, will not come off. The Marquis d’Aubrion will never give his daughter to the son of a bankrupt. I went to tell Grandet of the steps his uncle and I took in his father’s business, and the clever manoeuvres by which we had managed to keep the creditor’s quiet until the present time. The insolent fellow had the face to say to me—to me, who for five years have devoted myself night and day to his interests and his honor!—that his father’s affairs were not his! A solicitor would have had the right to demand fees amounting to thirty or forty thousand francs, one per cent on the total of the debts. But patience! there are twelve hundred thousand francs legitimately owing to the creditors, and I shall at once declare his father a bankrupt.

  I went into this business on the word of that old crocodile Grandet, and I have made promises in the name of his family. If Monsieur le vicomte d’Aubrion does not care for his honor, I care for mine. I shall explain my position to the creditors. Still, I have too much respect for Mademoiselle Eugenie (to whom under happier circumstances we once hoped to be allied) to act in this matter before you have spoken to her about it—

There Eugenie paused, and coldly returned the letter without finishing it.

“I thank you,” she said to Madame des Grassins.

“Ah! you have the voice and manner of your deceased father,” Madame des Grassins replied.

“Madame, you have eight thousand francs to pay us,” said Nanon, producing Charles’s cheque.

“That’s true; have the kindness to come with me now, Madame Cornoiller.”

“Monsieur le curé,” said Eugenie with a noble composure, inspired by the thought she was about to express, “would it be a sin to remain a virgin after marriage?”

“That is a case of conscience whose solution is not within my knowledge. If you wish to know what the celebrated Sanchez says of it in his treatise ‘De Matrimonio,’ I shall be able to tell you to-morrow.”

The curé went away; Mademoiselle Grandet withdrew to her father’s secret room and spent the day there alone, not coming down to dinner despite Nanon’s entreaties. She appeared in the evening at the hour when the usual company began to arrive. Never was the old hall so full. The news of Charles’s return and his treachery had spread through the whole town. But however watchful the visitors might be, they were left unsatisfied. Eugenie, expecting scrutiny, allowed none of the cruel emotions that wrung her soul to appear. She met mournful looks and melancholy speeches with a smiling front, hiding misery behind courtesy. Towards nine the games ended and the players left, paying their losses and discussing the play. At the moment when the whole party rose to take leave, an unexpected event occurred, which resounded through Saumur and beyond.

“Stay, monsieur le président,” said Eugenie to Monsieur de Bonfons as she saw him take his cane.

There was not a person in that assembly who was unmoved by these words. The president turned pale and was forced to sit down.

“The president gets the millions,” said Mademoiselle de Gribeaucourt.

“It is plain enough; the president marries Mademoiselle Grandet,” cried Madame d’Orsonval.

“All the trumps in one hand,” said the abbé.

“A love game,” said the notary.

Each made his pun and looked at the heiress mounted on her millions. The drama begun nine years before had reached its conclusion. In provincial towns social conventionalities are rigidly enforced; an infraction like this constituted a solemn promise.

“Monsieur le président,” said Eugenie in a voice of some emotion when they were left alone, “I know what pleases you in me. Swear to leave me free during my whole life, to claim none of the rights which marriage will give you over me, and my hand is yours. Oh!” she added, seeing him about to kneel, “I have more to say. I must not deceive you. In my heart I cherish one inextinguishable feeling. Friendship is the only sentiment which I can give to a husband. I wish neither to affront him nor to violate the laws of my own heart. But you can possess my hand and my fortune only at the cost of doing me an inestimable service.”

“I am ready for all things,” said the president.

“Here are fifteen hundred thousand francs,” she said, drawing from her bosom a certificate of a hundred shares in the Bank of France. “Go to Paris—not to-morrow, but instantly. Find Monsieur des Grassins, learn the names of my uncle’s creditors, call them together, pay them in full all that was owing, with interest at five per cent from the day the debt was incurred to the present time. Be careful to obtain a full and legal receipt, in proper form, before a notary. You are a magistrate, and I can trust this matter in your hands. You are a man of honor; I will put faith in your word, and meet the dangers of life under shelter of your name. Let us have mutual indulgence. We have known each other so long that we are almost related; you would not wish to render me unhappy.”

The president fell at the feet of the rich heiress, his heart beating and wrung with joy.

“I will be your slave!” he said.

“When you obtain the receipts, monsieur,” she resumed, with a cold glance, “you will take them with all the other papers to my cousin Grandet, and you will give him this letter. On your return I will keep my word.”

The president understood that he owed her acquiescence to some bitterness of love, and he hurried to obey, lest time effect a reconciliation between the pair.

When Monsieur de Bonfons left her, Eugenie fell back in her chair and burst into tears. All was over.

The president took the mail-post and reached Paris the next evening. The morning after his arrival he went to see des Grassins, and together they summoned the creditors to meet at the notary’s office where the vouchers had been deposited. Not a single creditor failed to be present. Creditors though they were, justice must be done to them—they were all punctual. Monsieur de Bonfons, in the name of Mademoiselle Grandet, paid them the amount of their claims with interest. The payment of interest was remarkable in Parisian commerce of that day. When the receipts were legally registered, and des Grassins had received for his services the fifty thousand francs allowed him by Eugenie, the president went to the hôtel d’Aubrion and found Charles just entering his apartment after a serious encounter with his prospective father-in-law. The old marquis had told him plainly that he should not marry his daughter until all the creditors of Guillaume Grandet had been paid in full.

The president gave Charles the following letter:

  My Cousin,—Monsieur le président de Bonfons has undertaken to place in your hands the acquittance for all claims upon my uncle, also a receipt by which I acknowledge having received from you the sum total of those claims. I have heard of a possible failure, and I think that the son of a bankrupt may not be able to marry Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Yes, my cousin, you judged rightly of my mind and of my manners. I have, it is true, no part in the world; I understand neither its calculations nor its customs; and I could not give you the pleasures that you seek in it. Be happy, according to the social conventions to which you have sacrificed our love. To make your happiness complete I can only offer you your father’s honor. Adieu! You will always have a faithful friend in your cousin

Eugenie.

The president smiled at the exclamation which the ambitious young man could not repress as he received the documents.

“We shall announce our marriages at the same time,” remarked Monsieur de Bonfons.

“Ah! you marry Eugenie? Well, I am delighted; she is a good girl. But,” added Charles, struck with a luminous idea, “she must be rich?”

“She had,” said the president, with a mischievous smile, “about nineteen millions four days ago; but she has only seventeen millions to-day.”

Charles looked thunderstruck.

“Seventeen mil—”

“Seventeen millions; yes, monsieur. We shall muster, Mademoiselle Grandet and I, an income of seven hundred and fifty thousand francs when we marry.”

“My dear cousin,” said Charles, recovering a little assurance, “we can push each other’s fortunes.”

“Agreed,” said the president. “Here is also a little case which I am charged to give into your own hands,” he added, placing on the table the leather dressing-case.

“Well, my dear friend,” said Madame d’Aubrion, entering the room without noticing the president, “don’t pay any attention to what poor Monsieur d’Aubrion has just said to you; the Duchesse de Chaulieu has turned his head. I repeat, nothing shall interfere with the marriage—”

“Very good, madame. The three millions which my father owed were paid yesterday.”

“In money?” she asked.

“Yes, in full, capital and interest; and I am about to do honor to his memory—”

“What folly!” exclaimed his mother-in-law. “Who is this?” she whispered in Grandet’s ear, perceiving the president.

“My man of business,” he answered in a low voice.

The marquise bowed superciliously to Monsieur de Bonfons.

“We are pushing each other’s fortunes already,” said the president, taking up his hat. “Good-by, cousin.”

“He is laughing at me, the old cockatoo! I’d like to put six inches of iron into him!” muttered Charles.

The president was out of hearing. Three days later Monsieur de Bonfons, on his return to Saumur, announced his marriage with Eugenie. Six months after the marriage he was appointed councillor in the Cour royale at Angers. Before leaving Saumur Madame de Bonfons had the gold of certain jewels, once so precious to her, melted up, and put, together with the eight thousand francs paid back by her cousin, into a golden pyx, which she gave to the parish church where she had so long prayed for him. She now spent her time between Angers and Saumur. Her husband, who had shown some public spirit on a certain occasion, became a judge in the superior courts, and finally, after a few years, president of them. He was anxiously awaiting a general election, in the hope of being returned to the Chamber of Deputies. He hankered after a peerage; and then—

“The king will be his cousin, won’t he?” said Nanon, la Grande Nanon, Madame Cornoiller, bourgeoise of Saumur, as she listened to her mistress, who was recounting the honors to which she was called.

Nevertheless, Monsieur de Bonfons (he had finally abolished his patronymic of Cruchot) did not realize his ambitions. He died eight days after his election as deputy of Saumur. God, who sees all and never strikes amiss, punished him, no doubt, for his sordid calculations and the legal cleverness with which, accurante Cruchot, he had drawn up his marriage contract, in which husband and wife gave to each other, “in case they should have no children, their entire property of every kind, landed or otherwise, without exception or reservation, dispensing even with the formality of an inventory; provided that said omission of said inventory shall not injure their heirs and assigns, it being understood that this deed of gift is, etc., etc.” This clause explains the profound respect which monsieur le président always testified for the wishes, and above all, for the solitude of Madame de Bonfons. Women cited him as the most considerate and delicate of men, pitied him, and even went so far as to find fault with the passion and grief of Eugenie, blaming her, as women know so well how to blame, with cruel but discreet insinuation.

“Madame de Bonfons must be very ill to leave her husband entirely alone. Poor woman! Is she likely to get well? What is it? Something gastric? A cancer?”—“She has grown perfectly yellow. She ought to consult some celebrated doctor in Paris.”—“How can she be happy without a child? They say she loves her husband; then why not give him an heir?—in his position, too!”—“Do you know, it is really dreadful! If it is the result of mere caprice, it is unpardonable. Poor president!”

Endowed with the delicate perception which a solitary soul acquires through constant meditation and the clear-sightedness with which a mind aloof from life fastens on all that falls within its sphere, Eugenie, taught by suffering and by later education to divine thought, knew well that the president desired her death that he might step into possession of their immense fortune, augmented by the property of his uncle the notary and his uncle the abbé, whom it had lately pleased God to call to himself. The poor solitary pitied the president. Providence avenged her for the calculations and the indifference of a husband who respected the hopeless passion on which she spent her life because it was his surest safeguard. To give life to a child would give death to his hopes—the hopes of selfishness, the joys of ambition, which the president cherished as he looked into the future.

God thus flung piles of gold upon this prisoner to whom gold was a matter of indifference, who longed for heaven, who lived, pious and good, in holy thoughts, succoring the unfortunate in secret, and never wearying of such deeds. Madame de Bonfons became a widow at thirty-six. She is still beautiful, but with the beauty of a woman nearly forty. Her face is white, placid and calm; her voice gentle and self-possessed; her manners simple. She has the noblest qualities of sorrow, the saintliness of one who has never soiled her soul by contact with the world; but she also retains the rigid bearing of an old maid and the petty habits inseparable from the narrow round of provincial life. In spite of her vast wealth, she lives as the poor Eugenie Grandet once lived. The fire is never lighted on her hearth until the day when her father allowed it to be lighted in the hall, and it is put out in conformity with the rules which governed her youthful years. She dresses as her mother dressed. The house in Saumur, without sun, without warmth, always in shadow, melancholy, is an image of her life. She carefully accumulates her income, and might seem parsimonious did she not disarm criticism by a noble employment of her wealth. Pious and charitable institutions, a hospital for old age, Christian schools for children, a public library richly endowed, bear testimony against the charge of avarice which some persons lay at her door. The churches of Saumur owe much of their embellishment to her. Madame de Bonfons (sometimes ironically spoken of as mademoiselle) inspires for the most part reverential respect: and yet that noble heart, beating only with tenderest emotions, has been, from first to last, subjected to the calculations of human selfishness; money has cast its frigid influence upon that hallowed life and taught distrust of feelings to a woman who is all feeling.

“I have none but you to love me,” she says to Nanon.

The hand of this woman stanches the secret wounds in many families. She goes on her way to heaven attended by a train of benefactions. The grandeur of her soul redeems the narrowness of her education and the petty habits of her early life.

Such is the history of Eugenie Grandet, who is in the world but not of it; who, created to be supremely a wife and mother, has neither husband nor children nor family. Lately there has been some question of her marrying again. The Saumur people talk of her and of the Marquis de Froidfond, whose family are beginning to beset the rich widow just as, in former days, the Cruchots laid siege to the rich heiress. Nanon and Cornoiller are, it is said, in the interests of the marquis. Nothing could be more false. Neither la Grande Nanon nor Cornoiller has sufficient mind to understand the corruptions of the world.
