The book is dedicated to a Polish lady, celebrating diverse qualities.

In October 1829 Simon Babylas Latournelle, a notary, walks with his family and clerk Jean Butscha to Chalet in Ingouville. He instructs his son Exupère to carry out a secret plan involving Mademoiselle Modeste Mignon, hinting at her romantic inclinations. Madame Latournelle is humorous; Exupère prepares to leave for law studies. Chalet reflects the Mignon family’s decline.

The Latournelles visit the Mignons, where Modeste, embroidering beside her blind mother, becomes the focus. Tension mounts as Dumay, the cashier, prepares to confront Modeste’s suitor. Dumay’s loyalty, forged under Colonel Mignon, compels him to protect her honor. The family’s downfall and Bettina’s tragic death expose societal gossip and their struggle for dignity. Madame Mignon’s comic attempts to discern Modeste’s feelings reveal her emotional state. Modeste’s romantic fantasies, fed by reading, end in disillusionment after corresponding with the poet Canalis. Themes of love, social expectation, and the clash of reality and idealism shape Modeste’s journey.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Monsieur, you are a great poet and an honest man. After your candor with a young girl in despair, can you answer me truthfully? Would your response to my letter have been the same if you had known that Mademoiselle O. d’Este has six million francs and seeks a fool for a husband?

I admire your talent and integrity. I remain your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

---

Ernest de La Brière pondered this letter as he walked the boulevards, uneasy. He admitted he would not have replied to a wealthy heiress with the same candor.

Six days later Modeste received a reply from him:

Mademoiselle, you are not a D’Este. Your name is a disguise. Do I owe revelations to someone who is untruthful about herself? Answer me sincerely, and I will respond to your question.

---

La Brière conceded that knowledge of her fortune might have altered his behavior. He prized honesty and sought to keep his self-respect. Still captivated by Modeste’s beauty, he resolved to continue their correspondence.

---

Buoyed by his reply, Modeste felt joy and confusion. She arranged to receive letters secretly so her family would not discover the correspondence.

---

In her next letter she spoke openly, revealing her noble lineage and wealth, and begged La Brière to understand her heart—seeking a connection beyond social constraints.

---

La Brière struggled with his feelings, torn between admiration and their differing social positions. He decided to meet her father, the Comte de La Bastie, and declare his love.

---

Complications arose when the Duc d’Herouville arrived as a suitor, intensifying family tensions. Modeste prepared to meet suitors including La Brière and Canalis, each embodying different hopes and fears.

---

Modeste’s journey becomes one of self-discovery, negotiating romantic desire and social reality, building toward a climax that will determine her fate and affect those around her.
