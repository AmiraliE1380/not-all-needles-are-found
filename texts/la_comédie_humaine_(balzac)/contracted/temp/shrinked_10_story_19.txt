Bianchon, a physician famed for theoretical physiology and early recognition at the Paris medical school, began as a surgeon before practicing medicine. His early studies were guided by the illustrious Desplein, one of France’s greatest surgeons, who shone briefly but brilliantly. Desplein had a unique, incommunicable method; like many geniuses, he left no heirs and no lasting school. The glory of surgeons, Balzac notes, is transient—heroic in their moment but not always immortalized beyond their specialty.

Desplein’s brilliance was clear yet circumscribed. He possessed a godlike eye and an intuition—whether natural or acquired—that let him read the sufferer and disease, choose the precise moment to operate, and account for temperament and atmosphere. He seemed to perceive the human frame through time, attending to present needs. Yet he did not embody universal science as Hippocrates, Galen, or Aristotle did, nor did he found a school to transform knowledge. Everything in him was intensely personal; his egoism isolated him and now diminishes his posthumous fame. There is no triumphant monument to secure his discoveries for posterity.

His genius shaped his beliefs. He pictured the atmosphere as a generative envelope and the earth as an egg within a shell; unable to decide whether egg or hen came first, he rejected both antecedent animals and any surviving spirit. Accustomed to dissecting humans before, during, and after life, he found no evidence of an individual soul; observing cerebral and nervous centers and a center for blood aeration, and detecting what he called two “souls” in man, he embraced a confident atheism—typical of many scientific minds—dying impenitent as many great intellects do.

His greatness was marred by contradictions his enemies called meannesses. Envious critics, ignorant of superior spirits’ motives, seize inconsistencies to condemn. Even a man whose deeds vindicate his methods can be misunderstood. Desplein’s moods and temper invited attack: sometimes elegantly dressed, sometimes indifferent; sometimes rough, sometimes kind. Capable of ostentatious covetousness yet ready to offer his whole fortune to exiled patrons, he provoked conflicting judgments. He could drop a prayer-book at court to win a ribbon while privately mocking everything; after studying humanity from every angle, he felt profound contempt for men, seeing their true nature in both lofty and base acts.

Genius often carries a federative quality; great talent can uplift wit beyond that of the merely clever. Moral insight accompanies true genius: one who sees a flower sees the sun. Desplein’s repartee—his capacity to be medically precise and verbally sharp—made his claims credible to students who believed his pretensions, even that he might have been as great a minister as a surgeon.

Among Desplein’s puzzles is one Balzac highlights and resolves at the end, vindicating him against foolish charges.

Of all Desplein’s students, Horace Bianchon was most warmly attached. Before becoming house surgeon at the Hôtel-Dieu, Bianchon had been a poor medical student lodging at the Maison Vauquer in the Latin Quarter. He knew the hunger of poverty, a crucible that refines talent. Tested by passions, such men acquire honesty and the habit of steady work to survive life’s battles.

Horace was upright, incapable of dishonor, direct, ready to stake his cloak for a friend and lend his time and nights. He expected nothing in return yet trusted that time would repay him. Friends respected his modest virtue and feared his censure. Neither pedant nor preacher, he could swear gracefully while giving counsel and was always ready for good company. Frank, open, and content, he was a loyal friend—the Pylades of many Oresteses—undaunted by creditors, whom Balzac likens to modern Furies.

He bore poverty cheerfully, a sign of courage, and incurred few debts. Sober and active, he was steadfast in thought and conduct.

His fortune turned when Desplein noticed him—both his virtues and his faults. When a leading clinician takes a young man under his wing, doors open. Desplein made Bianchon his assistant in affluent houses where the student received fees and glimpses of Parisian life’s mysteries. He kept him at consultations, occupied him, sometimes sent him with rich patients to spas—effectively building a practice. The tyrant of surgery thus gained a devoted ally. The two men—one at the summit of honor, the other humble and poor—became intimate.

Desplein confided in Bianchon, who knew which women had sat near the master, who had slept on his famous surgical couch, and the temperament—part lion, part bull—that swelled until it caused the man’s death by heart degeneration. Bianchon noted the schemes of sordid avarice and the politician behind the man of science; he foresaw the mortifications awaiting the solitary sentiment hidden in a steeled heart.

On one occasion, Bianchon mentioned a poor water-carrier from Saint-Jacques with a horrible disease brought on by fatigue and want; the man had eaten only potatoes all winter of 1821. Desplein left his rounds, risking his horse to rush with Bianchon to remove the man to a sick house founded by Dubois in the Faubourg Saint-Denis. After curing him, Desplein gave him money to buy a horse and barrel. The Auvergnat later returned to bring a sick friend, saying, “I could not have borne to let him go to any one else!” Desplein clasped his hand and said, “Bring them all to me.”

Bianchon noticed his chief’s predilection for Auvergnats and water-carriers, but since Desplein prided himself on cures at the Hôtel-Dieu, the pupil found nothing odd.

One morning, as he crossed the Place Saint-Sulpice, Bianchon saw Desplein enter the church about nine, slipping in by the door on Rue du Petit-Lion as if into a suspect house. Curious, Bianchon followed and was astonished to see the great atheist kneeling humbly in the Lady Chapel, remaining through mass, giving alms for the service and the poor, looking as serious as in an operation.

“He has certainly not come to clear up the question of the Virgin's delivery,” Bianchon thought. The sight of Desplein attending mass alone at that hour seemed a marvel.

At dinner that day, Bianchon, who had a Cabaniste leaning himself, steered conversation to the mass, calling it a farce. Desplein replied with characteristic invective: the mass, he said, had cost Christendom more blood than Napoleon’s battles and Broussais’ leeches. He called it a papal invention not older than the sixth century, based on Hoc est corpus, and recounted the violent controversies it provoked—Corpus Christi, the wars against the Albigenses, the Vaudois’ resistance—displaying his Voltairean satire.

Bianchon wondered where his master’s worshiper had gone. He doubted his own sight; Desplein would not lie. Three months passed. Bianchon did not press the matter though it lingered in his mind. One day at the Hôtel-Dieu a colleague bluntly asked Desplein what he had been doing at Saint-Sulpice. Desplein replied he had gone to see a priest with a diseased knee-bone, recommended by the Duchesse d'Angoulême. That answer satisfied others but not Bianchon; he interpreted it as a cover for attending mass.

Resolved to watch, Bianchon remembered the date and hour he'd seen Desplein and vowed to be there the next year. By then he was no longer Desplein’s house surgeon. The following year, at that hour, he saw Desplein’s cab on Rue de Tournon; the surgeon crept along Saint-Sulpice's wall and once more attended mass at the Virgin’s altar. The regularity deepened the mystery. After Desplein left, Bianchon asked the sacristan whether the gentleman was a regular. “For twenty years that I have been here,” the man replied, “M. Desplein has come four times a year to attend this mass. He founded it.”

“A mass founded by him!” Bianchon said as he left. The puzzle—how an outspoken atheist could have established and regularly attend a mass—remained as startling to him as any theological paradox.

Some time elapsed before Doctor Bianchon, though so intimate a friend, found an opportunity to speak to Desplein of this incident in his life. Though they met in consultation or society it was hard to find a confidential hour when two men could tell each other their secrets. At last, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, when the mob invaded the Archbishop's residence and Republican agitators urged them to destroy the gilt crosses flashing like streaks of lightning over the city, Bianchon once more detected Desplein going into Saint-Sulpice. The doctor followed him and knelt down by him without any notice or demonstration of surprise. They both attended the mass he had founded.

"Will you tell me, my dear fellow," said Bianchon as they left the church, "the reason for your fit of monkishness? I have caught you three times going to mass—You! You must account to me for this mystery; explain such a flagrant disagreement between your opinions and your conduct. You do not believe in God, and yet you attend mass? My dear master, you are bound to give me an answer."

"I am like a great many devout people—men who on the surface are deeply religious, but quite as much atheists as you or I can be."

He then poured out a torrent of epigrams on certain political personages; but Bianchon would not be baffled. "All that has nothing to do with my question," he said. "I want to know why you have just been doing that, and why you founded this mass."

"Faith! my dear boy," said Desplein, "I am on the verge of the tomb; I may safely tell you about the beginning of my life."

They were in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, one of the worst streets in Paris. Desplein pointed to the sixth floor of a house, its narrow door opening into a passage with a winding staircase, windows aptly called 'borrowed lights'—jours de souffrance. Throwing up his arm with a vehement gesture, he exclaimed, "I lived up there for two years."

"I know; Arthez lived there; we used to call it the pickle-jar of great men! What then?"

"The mass I have just attended is connected with events which took place when I lived in that garret—the one with the clothes-line and a pot of flowers. My early life was so hard, my dear Bianchon, that I may dispute the palm of Paris suffering with any man living. I endured everything: hunger and thirst, want of money, clothes, shoes, linen—every cruelty that penury can inflict. I have blown on my frozen fingers in that pickle-jar of great men. I worked through a whole winter, my head steaming in the cold. I do not know where a man finds the fulcrum that enables him to hold out against such a life.

"I was alone, with no one to help me, no money to buy books or pay medical training; I had not a friend; my irascible temper was against me. No one understood that this irritability was the distress and toil of a man at the bottom of the social scale struggling to reach the surface. Still, I had, if I may say so to you, a ground-bed of good feeling and keen sensitiveness which must be the birthright of any man strong enough to climb after long tramping in the bogs of poverty. I could obtain nothing from my family beyond an inadequate allowance. At that time I breakfasted on a roll the baker sold me cheap because it was left over, which I crumbled into milk; thus my morning meal cost but two sous. I dined only every other day in a boarding-house where the meal cost sixteen sous. You know what care I took of my clothes and shoes. I hardly know whether in later life we feel grief so deep on detecting the mocking smile of a gaping seam in a shoe, or hearing the armhole of a coat split. I drank nothing but water; a café seemed a promised land where none but the Lucullus of the pays Latin had a right of entry. 'Shall I ever take a cup of coffee there with milk in it?' I said to myself, 'or play a game of dominoes?'

"I threw into my work the fury I felt at my misery. I tried to master positive knowledge to acquire value and merit a position as soon as I could escape from nothingness. I consumed more oil than bread; the light I burned during those endless nights cost me more than food. It was a long duel, obstinate, with no consolation. To have friends, must we not form connections with young men, have a few sous to go tippling with them, and meet where students congregate? And I had nothing! And no one in Paris can understand that nothing means nothing. When I even thought of revealing my beggary, I had that nervous contraction of the throat which makes a sick man believe a ball rises from the oesophagus into the larynx.

"In later life I have met people born to wealth who, never having wanted anything, have never even heard this problem in the rule of three: A young man is to crime as a five-franc piece is to X.—These gilded idiots say to me, 'Why did you get into debt? Why involve yourself in such onerous obligations?' They remind me of the princess who, on hearing that the people lacked bread, said, 'Why do not they buy cakes?' I should like to see one of these rich men, who complain that I charge too much for an operation—yes, I should like to see him alone in Paris without a sou, without a friend, without credit, and forced to work with his five fingers to live at all! What would he do? Where would he go to satisfy his hunger?

"Bianchon, if you have sometimes seen me hard and bitter, it was because I added my early sufferings to the insensibility and selfishness I have seen thousands of times in the highest circles; or perhaps I was thinking of the obstacles which hatred, envy, jealousy, and calumny raised up between me and success. In Paris, when certain people see you ready to set your foot in the stirrup, some pull your coat-tails, others loosen the buckle of the strap that you may fall and crack your skull; one wrenches off your horse's shoes, another steals your whip, and the least treacherous of them all is the man whom you see coming to fire his pistol at you point-blank.

"You yourself, my dear boy, will become acquainted with the odious and incessant warfare waged by mediocrity against the superior man. If you should drop twenty-five louis one day, you will be accused of gambling on the next, and your best friends will report that you have lost twenty-five thousand. If you have a headache, you will be considered mad. If you are a little hasty, no one can live with you. If, to make a stand against this armament of pigmies, you collect your best powers, your best friends will cry out that you want everything, that you aim at domineering and tyranny. In short, your good points become your faults, your faults vices, and your virtues crime.

"If you save a man, you will be said to have killed him; if he reappears, it will be positive that you secured the present at the cost of the future. Stumble, and you fall! Invent anything and claim your rights, you will be called crotchety, cunning, ill-disposed to rising younger men.

"So, you see, if I do not believe in God, I believe still less in man. But do you not know in me another Desplein, altogether different from the Desplein whom everyone abuses?—However, we will not stir that mud-heap.

"Well, I was living in that house, working hard to pass my first examination, and I had no money at all. I had come to one of those moments of extremity when a man says, 'I will enlist.' I had one hope. I expected from my home a box full of linen, a present from one of those old aunts who, knowing nothing of Paris, think of your shirts while imagining that their nephew with thirty francs a month is eating ortolans. The box arrived while I was at the schools; it had cost forty francs for carriage. The porter, a German shoemaker living in a loft, had paid the money and kept the box. I walked up and down the Rue des Fosses-Saint-Germain-des-Pres and the Rue de l'Ecole de Medecine without hitting on any scheme to release my trunk without payment of the forty francs, which of course I could pay as soon as I should have sold the linen. My stupidity proved to me that surgery was my only vocation. Refined souls, whose powers move in a lofty atmosphere, have none of that spirit of intrigue that is fertile in resource and device; their good genius is chance; they do not invent, things come to them.

"At night I went home just as my fellow lodger also came in—a water-carrier named Bourgeat, a native of Saint-Flour. We knew each other as two lodgers do who have rooms off the same landing, and who hear each other sleeping, coughing, dressing, and so at last become used to one another. My neighbor informed me that the landlord, to whom I owed three quarters' rent, had turned me out; I must clear out next morning. He himself was also turned out on account of his occupation. I spent the most miserable night of my life. Where was I to get a messenger to carry my few chattels and my books? How could I pay him and the porter? Where would I go? I repeated these unanswerable questions in tears. I fell asleep; poverty has for its friends heavenly slumbers full of beautiful dreams.

"Next morning, just as I was swallowing my little bowl of bread soaked in milk, Bourgeat came in and said in his Auvergne accent:

"'Mouchieur l'Etudiant, I am a poor man, a foundling from the hospital at Saint-Flour, without either father or mother, and not rich enough to marry. You are not fertile in relations either, nor well supplied with the ready? Listen, I have a hand-cart downstairs which I have hired for two sous an hour; it will hold all our goods; if you like, we will try to find lodgings together, since we are both turned out of this. It is not the earthly paradise, when all is said and done.'

"'I know that, my good Bourgeat,' said I. 'But I am in a great fix. I have a trunk downstairs with a hundred francs' worth of linen in it, out of which I could pay the landlord and the porter, and I have not a hundred sous.'

"'Pooh! I have a few dibs,' replied Bourgeat joyfully, and he pulled out a greasy old leather purse. 'Keep your linen.'

"Bourgeat paid our arrears and settled with the porter. Then he put our furniture and my box of linen in his cart, and pulled it along the street, stopping in front of every house where there was a notice board. I went up to see whether the rooms to let would suit us. At midday we were still wandering about the neighborhood without having found anything. The price was the great difficulty. Bourgeat proposed that we should eat at a wine-shop, leaving the cart at the door. Towards evening I discovered, in the Cour de Rohan, Passage du Commerce, at the very top of a house next the roof, two rooms with a staircase between them. Each of us was to pay sixty francs a year. So there we were housed, my humble friend and I. We dined together. Bourgeat, who earned about fifty sous a day, had saved a hundred crowns or so; he would soon be able to gratify his ambition by buying a barrel and a horse. On learning of my situation—for he extracted my secrets with a quiet craftiness and good nature, of which the remembrance touches my heart to this day—he gave up for a time the ambition of his whole life; for twenty-two years he had been carrying water in the street, and he now devoted his hundred crowns to my future prospects."

Desplein clutched Bianchon's arm tightly. "He gave me the money for my examination fees! That man, my friend, understood that I had a mission, that the needs of my intellect were greater than his. He looked after me, he called me his boy, he lent me money to buy books, he would come in softly sometimes to watch me at work, and took a mother's care in seeing that I had wholesome and abundant food instead of the bad and insufficient nourishment I had been condemned to. Bourgeat, a man of about forty, had a homely, mediaeval type of face, a prominent forehead, a head a painter might have chosen as Lycurgus'. The poor man's heart was big with affections seeking an object; he had never been loved but by a poodle that had died some time since, of which he would talk to me, asking whether I thought the Church would allow masses for the repose of its soul. His dog, he said, had been a good Christian, who for twelve years had accompanied him to church, never barking, listening to the organ without opening his mouth, crouching beside him as though praying too.

"This man centered all his affections in me; he looked upon me as a forlorn and suffering creature, and he became to me the most thoughtful mother and considerate benefactor, the ideal of the virtue that rejoices in its own work. When I met him in the street he would throw me a glance of intelligence full of unutterable dignity; he would affect to walk as though he carried no weight and seemed happy in seeing me in good health and well dressed. It was, in fact, the devoted affection of the lower classes, the love of a girl of the people transferred to a loftier level. Bourgeat did all my errands, woke me at night at any fixed hour, trimmed my lamp, cleaned our landing; as good a servant as he was a father, and as clean as an English girl. He did all the housework. Like Philopoemen, he sawed our wood, and gave to all he did the grace of simplicity while preserving his dignity, for he seemed to understand that the end ennobles every act.

"When I left this good fellow to be house surgeon at the Hotel-Dieu, I felt an indescribable dull pain, knowing that he could no longer live with me; but he comforted himself with the prospect of saving enough for me to take my degree, and he made me promise to go to see him whenever I had a day out: Bourgeat was proud of me. He loved me for my own sake and for his own. If you look up my thesis, you will see that I dedicated it to him.

"During the last year of my residence as house surgeon I earned enough to repay all I owed to this worthy Auvergnat by buying him a barrel and a horse. He was furious with rage at learning that I had deprived myself of spending my money, and yet delighted to see his wishes fulfilled; he laughed and scolded, looked at his barrel and his horse, and wiped away a tear, saying, 'It is too bad. What a splendid barrel! You really ought not. Why, that horse is as strong as an Auvergnat!'

"I never saw a more touching scene. Bourgeat insisted on buying for me the case of instruments mounted in silver which you have seen in my room and which is to me the most precious thing there. Though enchanted with my first success, never did the least sign escape him implying, 'This man owes all to me!' Yet, but for him, I should have died of want; he had eaten bread rubbed with garlic that I might have coffee to enable me to sit up at night.

"He fell ill. As you may suppose, I passed my nights by his bedside, and the first time I pulled him through; but two years after he had a relapse; in spite of the utmost care and exertions of science, he succumbed. No king was ever nursed as he was. Yes, Bianchon, to snatch that man from death I tried unheard-of things. I wanted him to live long enough to see his work accomplished, to realize his hopes, to give expression to the only gratitude that ever filled my heart, to quench a fire that burns in me to this day.

"Bourgeat, my second father, died in my arms," Desplein went on after a pause, visibly moved. "He left me everything he possessed by a will he had had made by a public scrivener, dating from the year when we had gone to live in the Cour de Rohan.

"This man's faith was perfect; he loved the Holy Virgin as he might have loved his wife. He was an ardent Catholic, but never said a word to me about my want of religion. When he was dying he entreated me to spare no expense that he might have every possible benefit of clergy. I had a mass said for him every day. Often, in the night, he would tell me of his fears as to his future fate; he feared his life had not been saintly enough. Poor man! he was at work from morning till night. For whom, then, is Paradise—if there be a Paradise? He received the last sacrament like the saint that he was, and his death was worthy of his life.

"I alone followed him to the grave. When I had laid my only benefactor to rest, I looked about to see how I could pay my debt to him; I found he had neither family nor friends, neither wife nor child. But he believed. He had a religious conviction; had I any right to dispute it? He had spoken to me timidly of masses said for the repose of the dead; he would not press it on me as a duty, thinking it a form of repayment for his services. As soon as I had money enough I paid to Saint-Sulpice the requisite sum for four masses every year. As the only thing I can do for Bourgeat is thus to satisfy his pious wishes, on the days when that mass is said at the beginning of each season of the year, I go for his sake and say the required prayers; and I say with the good faith of a sceptic—'Great God, if there is a sphere which Thou hast appointed after death for those who have been perfect, remember good Bourgeat; and if he should have anything to suffer, let me suffer it for him, that he may enter all the sooner into what is called Paradise.'

"That, my dear fellow, is as much as a man who holds my opinions can allow himself. But God must be a good fellow; He cannot owe me any grudge. I swear to you, I would give my whole fortune if faith such as Bourgeat's could enter my brain."

Bianchon, who was with Desplein through his last illness, dares not affirm to this day that the great surgeon died an atheist. Will not those who believe like to fancy that the humble Auvergnat came to open the gate of Heaven to his friend, as he did that of the earthly temple on whose pediment are the words—"A grateful country to its great men."

PARIS, January 1836.
