DEDICATION

To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.

Among all the pupils of the Oratorian school at Vendome, we are, I think, the only two who have afterwards met in mid-career of a life of letters. When we met, you were engaged upon your noble works on German philosophy, and I on this study. So neither of us has missed his vocation; and you, when you see your name here, will feel as much pleasure as I do in inscribing my work to you. Your old schoolfellow, 1840 De Balzac.

GOBSECK

It was one o’clock in the morning during the winter of 1829-30, and in the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon two guests not of her family remained. A young man took his leave; as the departing carriage died away, the Vicomtesse turned to her daughter Camille, who stood by the chimney-piece, ostensibly looking at a fire-screen while listening to the sounds from the courtyard, stirring maternal anxiety.

“Camille,” the Vicomtesse said, “if you continue to treat young Comte de Restaud as you have this evening, I shall have to prevent him from visiting. Trust my love and let me guide you. At seventeen one cannot judge the past or the future or social standings. M. de Restaud has a mother, Mlle. Goriot, who squandered her father’s fortune and is of no birth. Though he behaves admirably toward her and his siblings, any family would hesitate to entrust their daughter’s future to him while she lives.”

“I overheard part of your conversation with Mlle. de Grandlieu,” interposed Derville, a family friend. “I have won, M. le Comte,” he added to the departing man. “I shall assist your niece.”

“See what it is to have an attorney’s ears!” exclaimed the Vicomtesse. “How could you know what I was saying to Camille?”

“I knew it from your looks,” Derville replied, settling by the fire.

Camille’s uncle approached, and the Vicomtesse sat on a hearth-stool between them.

“It’s time for a story that may change your view of Ernest de Restaud’s prospects,” Derville said.

“A story?” Camille cried. “Please begin!”

Derville’s look at the Vicomtesse showed the tale was meant for her. The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu was a leading figure in the Faubourg Saint-Germain by birth and fortune. That a Paris attorney could be so intimate with her seemed odd until one knew the facts.

After returning to France with the Royal family, the Vicomtesse had lived on a pension from Louis XVIII, a state she found intolerable. The Hotel de Grandlieu had been sold during the Revolution. Derville discovered defects in the title, pursued legal action, and reclaimed it for her. He also secured judgments that restored significant property, raising her income to about sixty thousand francs plus indemnities. Derville, a man of integrity and modesty, became a close family confidant.

His management of the Vicomtesse’s affairs gained him esteem in the Faubourg Saint-Germain and brought many aristocratic clients. Yet he did not exploit this popularity for personal advancement. The Vicomtesse urged him to accept a magistracy; he declined, preferring to keep his practice and social ties. His steadfast service revealed his talents, and since Ernest de Restaud’s arrival—especially as Camille showed interest—Derville had been visiting the Hotel de Grandlieu more often.

At a recent ball Derville had taunted Camille: “It’s a pity that young man doesn’t have two or three million francs, isn’t it?”

“Is it a pity? I don’t think so,” she answered. “M. de Restaud is capable and well educated. He will have as much money as he wishes when he comes into power.”

“But what if he were already rich?” Derville persisted.

“Rich already?” Camille blushed. “All the girls would be vying for him,” she said, watching the dancers.

“Then Mlle. de Grandlieu might not be the one he favors,” Derville teased. “That blush says it all! You like him, don’t you? Speak up.”

Camille suddenly rose to leave.

“She loves him,” Derville thought.

Since that evening Camille had been unusually attentive to Derville, who approved of her feelings for Ernest. Though she felt deep gratitude for his services to her family, she had previously shown respect rather than warmth toward him, keeping a polite distance. Gratitude often weighs heavily on the next generation, which may seek to escape it.

“This adventure,” Derville began, “brings to mind the one romantic event in my life. You may find it ridiculous that an attorney speaks of romance, but once I was twenty-five and had seen some strange things. I should start by telling you about someone you could not possibly know—a usurer.

“Can you picture his sallow, wan face? I wish the Academie would allow me to call such faces the lunar type. His hair was iron-gray, sleek, and carefully combed; his features were as impassive as Talleyrand’s. His little yellow eyes, devoid of eyelashes, peered from under a shabby cap, as if afraid of the light. He had thin lips like Rembrandt’s alchemists and a sharp nose like a gimlet. His voice was low and suave; he never lost his temper. It was hard to tell his age; he seemed either prematurely aged or curiously preserved.

“His room was as clean and threadbare as that of an elderly spinster. In winter the fire smoldered in a bank of ashes, never producing a flame. He moved through his day with the regularity of a pendulum, stopping mid-sentence when a cart passed to save his voice. He was thrifty with his pulse, concentrating all sensibility within himself.

“His life flowed soundlessly, like the sands of an hourglass. His victims sometimes erupted in rage, then fell silent, much like a kitchen after a fowl’s neck has been wrung.

“Toward evening this bill of exchange incarnate would take on a human shape, and his metals would transform into a human heart. When satisfied with his day’s business, he would rub his hands and let his inward glee escape through the wrinkles of his face, akin to a soundless laugh. Even in joy his conversation was limited to monosyllables, and he kept a non-committal expression.

“This was the neighbor Chance found for me in the Rue de Gres, where I lived as a second clerk finishing my studies. The house was damp and dark, with all windows facing the street, resembling an old convent. It was so gloomy that the gaiety of eldest sons vanished before they reached my neighbor’s door. He and his house were alike; even so does the oyster resemble its rock.

“I was the only person he communicated with; he would borrow a light or a book, and in the evenings he would allow me to visit. These gestures resulted from four years of neighborly interaction and my sober conduct. I lived much like he did due to lack of funds. Did he have relatives or friends? Was he rich or poor? No one could answer. I never saw money in his room; his capital was likely in the Bank. He collected his debts himself, running all over Paris on skinny legs. Once, when gold fell from his pocket, another lodger returned it.

“‘That isn’t mine!’ he exclaimed, surprised. ‘If I were rich, would I live as I do?’

“He made his coffee every morning on a cast-iron chafing dish; his dinner came from a cookshop, and our old porter’s wife tidied his room at the prescribed hour. A whimsical chance named him Gobseck. When I later did business for him, I learned he was about seventy-six when we met. Born around 1740 near Antwerp to a Dutch father and a Jewish mother, his name was Jean-Esther Van Gobseck. You may recall the murder case of La belle Hollandaise? I mentioned it to him, and he replied without interest, ‘She is my grandniece.’

“That was the only remark he made about the death of his sole surviving relative. Reports said La belle Hollandaise was named Sara Van Gobseck. When I asked how his grandniece bore his surname, he smiled, ‘The women never marry in our family.’

“He had never cared to trace relatives beyond four generations on the female side. The thought of heirs was abhorrent; the idea of his wealth passing to others after his death was inconceivable.

“He was ten when his mother sent him as a cabin boy to the Dutch Straits Settlements, where he spent twenty years. The inscrutable lines on his sallow forehead concealed secrets of horrible adventures, sudden panic, unanticipated luck, and lost fortunes. He had known notable figures: Admiral Simeuse, M. de Lally, Tippoo Sahib. A long stay in St. Thomas brought him into contact with notorious pirates. He had left no stone unturned in pursuit of fortune, even trying to find treasure in Buenos Ayres. He had personal knowledge of the American War of Independence. Yet when he spoke of the Indies or America, he seemed to view it as indiscretion and would repent afterward. If humanity and sociability are a religion, Gobseck might be considered an infidel; despite my efforts to understand him, his nature remained impenetrable.

“Did he adhere to his mother’s religion? Did he view Gentiles as legitimate prey? Had he converted to Catholicism, Lutheranism, or something else? I never learned his religious opinions; he appeared indifferent rather than incredulous.

“One evening I visited this man who had turned himself to gold, the usurer whom his clients called Daddy Gobseck, perhaps ironically. He sat motionless in his armchair, staring at the mantel-shelf, seemingly reading his statements. A lamp burned, but his face appeared paler against the background. He gestured to a chair for me but said nothing.

“What thoughts could this being have?” I wondered. “Does he know God exists? Does he know about feeling, women, happiness?” I felt pity for him, akin to pitying a diseased creature. Yet I knew that while he had millions at his command, he possessed the world in idea—having explored, ransacked, weighed, appraised, and exploited it.

“‘Good day, Daddy Gobseck,’ I began.

“He turned his face toward me, slightly contracting his bushy black eyebrows, a look that conveyed as much as a jubilant smile on a Southern face.

“‘You look as gloomy as you did when you heard about the failure of that bookseller you admired, though you were one of his victims.’

“‘One of his victims?’ he repeated, astonished.

“‘Yes. You refused to accept composition at the creditors’ meeting until he promised to pay you in full, and he gave you bills accepted by the bankrupt firm. When he resumed business, he paid you the dividend on those bills.’

“‘He was sharp, but I got it out of him.’

“‘Do you have bills to protest? Today is the 30th, I believe.’

“It was the first time I mentioned money to him. He looked at me ironically, then replied blandly, ‘I am amusing myself.’

“‘So you amuse yourself now and again?’

“‘Do you think the only poets are those who print their verses?’ he asked, pityingly.

“‘Poetry in that head!’ I thought, knowing little of his life.

“‘What life could be as glorious as mine?’ he continued, his eyes lighting up. ‘You are young; your visions are colored by youthful blood. You see women’s faces in the fire, while I see only coals. You have beliefs; I have none. Keep your illusions if you can. I will show you life with the discount taken off. You can go wherever you like or stay at home with your wife, but eventually you settle into a groove. Happiness consists in applying your faculties to realities. Anything more is false. My principles have varied with different men; I had to change them with every latitude. What we admire in Europe is punishable in Asia, and a vice in Paris becomes a necessity elsewhere. There are no hard-and-fast rules, only conventions adapted to climate. Throw a man into various social melting pots, and convictions and moral systems become meaningless. The only constant is self-interest. If you lived as long as I have, you would know that the only concrete reality worth caring about is GOLD. Gold represents every form of human power. I have traveled and found either hills or plains everywhere; place is irrelevant. Manners are universal; the battle between the poor and the rich is inevitable. It is better to exploit than to be exploited. Everywhere, you find the laboring man and the idle man; pleasures are the same, and when all sensations are exhausted, only Vanity remains. Vanity is satisfied only by gold. Our dreams require time and means to realize. Gold contains everything in embryo; it realizes all things for us.

“‘Only fools and invalids find pleasure in shuffling cards for a few pence. Only dupes think they are useful laying down political rules amid unpredictable events. Only simpletons delight in discussing stage players and repeating their sayings. What is all this but Parisian life summed up? Let us seek a higher outlook. Happiness consists in strong emotions that drain our vitality or in methodical occupation that makes existence like clockwork. A higher happiness is noble curiosity, a wish to learn Nature’s secrets, or to imitate Nature. In two words: Science and Art, or passion and calm. Every human passion, heightened in the struggle for existence, parades before me while I live in calm. As for scientific curiosity, I replace it with insight into the springs of action in man and woman. In summary, the world is mine without effort, and it has no hold on me. Listen,’ he continued, ‘I will tell you about my morning, and you will understand my pleasures.’

“He got up, bolted the door, drew a tapestry curtain across it, and sat down again.

“‘This morning,’ he said, ‘I had only two amounts to collect; the rest of the bills due I gave away instead of cash yesterday. So much saved, you see, for when I discount a bill, I always deduct two francs for a hired brougham—collection expenses. It would be absurd for my clients to make me trudge all over Paris for a few francs of discount when no man is my master, and I only pay seven francs in taxes.

“‘The first bill for a thousand francs was presented by a young fellow, a dandy with a spangled waistcoat, eyeglass, tilbury, and English horse. The bill bore the signature of one of the prettiest women in Paris, married to a Count, a great landowner. How did that Countess come to sign a bill of exchange, legally worthless but practically good business? These women, poor things, fear the scandal of a protested bill and would rather pay than fail. I wanted to find out what that bill truly represented—stupidity, imprudence, love, or charity?

“‘The second bill, signed “Fanny Malvaut,” came from a linen-draper on the brink of bankruptcy. No one with bank credit comes to me. The first step to my door means desperation; the news of his failure will soon emerge, and he has been everywhere else first. The stag is always at bay when I see him, and a pack of creditors is on his tail. The Countess lived in the Rue du Helder, and my Fanny in the Rue Montmartre. I made many conjectures as I set out this morning. If these two women could not pay, they would show me more respect than their fathers. What tricks would the Countess try for a thousand francs? She would be so nice to me, speaking in that ingratiating tone of endorsers, pouring out coaxing words, while I...’ (here the old man turned his pale eyes on me)—‘and I would remain unmoved, inexorable! So much for hypotheses. I reached the house.

“‘“Madame la Comtesse is asleep,” said the maid.

“‘“When can I see her?”

“‘“At twelve o’clock.”

“‘“Is Madame la Comtesse ill?”

“‘“No, sir, but she returned home at three o’clock this morning from a ball.”

“‘“My name is Gobseck; tell her I will call again at twelve,” and I left, leaving muddy footprints on the carpet. I enjoy leaving mud on a rich man’s carpet; it’s not spite, but a reminder of Necessity. In the Rue Montmartre, I entered a poor-looking house and looked into a dark courtyard where sunlight never shone. The porter’s lodge was grimy, the window like a shabby gown—greasy, dirty, and full of holes.

“‘“Mlle. Fanny Malvaut?”

“‘“She has gone out; but if you’ve come about a bill, the money is waiting for you.”

“‘“I will look in again,” I said.

“‘As soon as I knew the porter had the money, I wanted to know what the girl was like; I pictured her as pretty. The rest of the morning, I spent looking at prints in shop windows along the boulevard; then, just as it struck twelve, I entered the Countess’ antechamber.

“‘“Madame has just rung for me,” said the maid; “I don’t think she can see you yet.”

“‘“I will wait,” I said, sitting in an easy chair.

“‘Venetian shutters opened, and the maid hurried back.

“‘“Come in, sir.”

“‘From the sweet tone of the girl’s voice, I knew the mistress was not ready to pay. What a handsome woman I saw! She had thrown an Indian shawl over her bare shoulders, covering herself while revealing the outlines beneath. She wore a loose gown trimmed with ruffles, indicating her laundress’ bills amounted to about two thousand francs a year. Her dark curls escaped from a bright handkerchief knotted carelessly around her head. The bed lay in disorder, suggesting broken slumber. A painter would have paid to see the scene: a crushed pillow in an eider-down quilt, satin slippers gleaming on a bear-skin rug, a crumpled gown, twisted stockings, ribbon garters, a half-open fan on the chimney-piece. Drawers stood open; flowers, diamonds, gloves, a bouquet, a girdle were scattered about. The room was filled with a vague sweet perfume. Beneath all the luxury and disorder, I saw Misery lurking—she felt the edge of those fangs. Her tired face epitomized the room strewn with relics of past revelry. The scattered gewgaws, pitiable this morning, had turned heads the night before.

“‘What efforts to drink from the Tantalus cup of bliss I could read in these traces of love struck by remorse—in this visible representation of a life of luxury, extravagance, and riot. There were faint red marks on her young face, signs of fine skin; her features were coarsened, and dark circles surrounded her eyes. Yet Nature was vigorous in her; these traces of past folly did not mar her beauty. Her eyes glittered. She resembled a da Vinci Herodias, magnificently full of life and energy; nothing was starved or stinted in feature or outline; she awakened desire; it seemed there was a passion in her stronger than love. I was taken with her. It had been long since my heart throbbed; I was paid then and there—for I would give a thousand francs for a sensation that brought back memories of youth.

“‘“Monsieur,” she said, finding a chair for me, “will you be so good as to wait?”

“‘“Until this time tomorrow, madame,” I replied, folding the bill again. “I cannot legally protest this bill any sooner.” And within myself, I thought—“Pay the price of your luxury, your name, your ease, the monopoly you enjoy! The rich have invented judges and courts to secure their goods, and the guillotine—where many lie in silk, under silken coverlets, there is remorse and grinding of teeth beneath a smile, and those fantastical lions’ jaws are gaping to sink their fangs into your heart.”

“‘“Protest the bill! Can you mean it?” she cried, her eyes on me; “could you have so little consideration for me?”

“‘“If the King himself owed me money and did not pay, I would summon him even sooner than any other debtor.”

“‘While we spoke, someone tapped gently at the door.

“‘“I cannot see anyone,” she cried imperiously.

“‘“But, Anastasie, I particularly wish to speak to you.”

“‘“Not just now, dear,” she answered in a milder tone, but with no sign of relenting.

“‘“What nonsense! You are talking to someone,” said the voice, and in came a man who could only be the Count.

“‘The Countess glanced at me. I saw how it was; she was thoroughly in my power. There was a time when I was young and might have been foolish enough not to protest the bill. At Pondicherry in 1763, I let a woman off, and she paid me back nicely. I deserved it; what reason had I to trust her?

“‘“What does this gentleman want?” asked the Count.

“‘I could see the Countess trembling; the white satin skin of her throat was rough, turned to goose flesh. I laughed inwardly without moving a muscle.

“‘“This gentleman is one of my tradesmen,” she said.

“‘The Count turned his back on me; I drew the bill halfway out of my pocket. After that inexorable movement, she came over and placed a diamond in my hands. “Take it,” she said, “and be gone.”

“‘We exchanged values, and I bowed and left. The diamond was worth twelve hundred francs to me. Outside, I saw a swarm of flunkeys brushing their liveries, waxing their boots, and cleaning sumptuous equipages.

“‘“This is what brings these people to me!” I thought. “They steal millions with all due formalities to maintain this lifestyle. The great lord and the little man who apes him bathe in mud to save themselves a splash or two when they walk through the streets.”

“‘Just then, the great gates opened to admit a cabriolet. It was the same young fellow who had brought the bill to me.

“‘“Sir,” I said as he alighted, “here are two hundred francs, which I beg you to return to Mme. la Comtesse, and please tell her I hold the pledge she deposited with me this morning for a week.”

“‘He took the two hundred francs, and an ironical smile crossed his face, as if to say, “Aha! So she has paid, has she? ... Faith, so much the better!” I read the Countess’ future in his face. That good-looking, fair-haired young man is a heartless gambler; he will ruin himself, her, her husband, and the children, devouring their portions and wreaking more havoc in Parisian salons than a battery of howitzers.

“‘I returned to see Mlle. Fanny in the Rue Montmartre, climbed a steep, narrow staircase, and reached a two-room dwelling on the fifth floor. Everything was as neat as a new ducat. I saw no dust on the furniture in the first room, where Mlle. Fanny sat. She was a young Parisian girl, quietly dressed, with a delicate fresh face and a winning look. The arrangement of her neatly brushed chestnut hair in a double curve on her forehead lent a refined expression to her blue eyes, clear as crystal. The daylight streaming through the short curtains softened the light on her girlish face. A pile of shaped linen indicated she was a sempstress. She looked like a spirit of solitude. When I held out the bill, I noted she had not been home when I called in the morning.

“‘“But the money was left with the porter’s wife,” she said.

“‘I pretended not to understand.

“‘“You go out early, mademoiselle, it seems.”

“‘“I very seldom leave my room; but when you work all night, you must take a bath sometimes.”

“‘I looked at her. A glance revealed her life. Here was a girl condemned by misfortune to toil, from honest farmer folk, with a freckle or two indicating her country birth. There was an indefinable atmosphere of goodness about her; I felt as if I were breathing sincerity and frank innocence. It was refreshing. Poor innocent child, she had faith in something; a crucifix and a sprig of green box adorned her little painted wooden bedstead. I felt touched, inclined to offer to charge no more than twelve percent, helping her establish a good business.

“‘But maybe she has a cousin who would raise money on her signature and sponge on her,’ I thought.

“‘So I left, keeping my generous impulses in check; I have often observed that when benevolence harms no one who gives it, it ruins those who take it. When you came in, I was thinking that Fanny Malvaut would make a nice little wife; I was considering the contrast between her pure, lonely life and the Countess’s life—she has already sunk as low as a bill of exchange; she will sink to the lowest depths of degradation before she is done!’ I scrutinized him during the deep silence that followed, but he spoke again. ‘Well,’ he said, ‘do you think it is nothing to have this power of insight into the deepest recesses of the human heart, to embrace so many lives, to see the naked truth underlying it all? There are no two dramas alike: hideous sores, deadly chagrins, love scenes, misery that will soon lie under the Seine, young men’s joys leading to the scaffold, laughter of despair, and sumptuous banquets. Yesterday was a tragedy. A father drowned himself because he could not support his family. Tomorrow will be a comedy; some young man will try to rehearse the scene of M. Dimanche, brought up to date. You have heard people extol the eloquence of our latter-day preachers; I have wasted time listening to them; they changed my opinions, but never my conduct. Well, well; these good priests and your Mirabeaus and Vergniauds are mere stammering beginners compared to my orators.

“‘Often it is a girl in love, a gray-headed merchant on the verge of bankruptcy, a mother concealing her son’s wrongdoing, a starving artist, or a great man whose influence wanes, who pleads for me. Their power of pleading makes me shudder. Sublime actors play for me, for an audience of one, and they cannot deceive me. I can see their inmost thoughts, read them as God reads them. Nothing is hidden from me. The holder of the purse-strings can loose and bind. I am rich enough to buy the consciences of those who control ministers, from their office boys to their mistresses. Is that not power? I can possess the fairest women, receive their softest caresses; is that not Pleasure? And is not your whole social economy summed up in terms of Power and Pleasure?

“‘There are ten of us in Paris, silent, unknown kings, arbiters of your destinies. What is life but a machine set in motion by money? Know this for certain—methods are always confounded with results; you will never separate the soul from the senses, spirit from matter. Gold is the spiritual basis of existing society. The ten of us are bound by common interest; we meet certain days at the Cafe Themis near the Pont Neuf, revealing the mysteries of finance. No fortune can deceive us; we possess family secrets in all directions. We keep a kind of Black Book, noting the most important bills issued, drafts on public credit, or on banks, or given and taken in business. We are the Casuists of the Paris Bourse, a kind of Inquisition weighing and analyzing every man of fortune’s actions, and our forecasts are infallible. One of us oversees the judicial world, another the financial, another the administrative, and yet another the business world. I keep an eye on eldest sons, artists, people in high society, and gamblers—on the most sensational side of Paris. Everyone who comes to us reveals their neighbor’s secrets. Thwarted passion and mortified vanity are great babblers. Vice, disappointment, and vindictiveness are the best detectives. My colleagues and I have enjoyed all things, are sated with all things, and have reached the point where power and money are loved for their own sake.

“‘Here,’ he said, indicating his bare, chilly room, ‘the most high-mettled gallant, who chafes at a word and draws swords for a syllable elsewhere, will entreat with clasped hands. No city merchant is so proud, no woman so vain of her beauty, no soldier so bold, but they all entreat me here, with tears of rage or anguish in their eyes. Here they kneel—the famous artist, the man of letters whose name will go down to posterity. Here, in short, all inheritances and concerns of all Paris are weighed in the balance. Are you still of the opinion that there are no delights behind the blank mask that has often amazed you with its impassiveness?’ he asked, stretching out that livid face that reeked of money.

“I returned to my room, feeling stupefied. The little, wizened old man had grown great. He had transformed into a strange visionary symbol; he had become the power of gold personified. I shrank from life and my kind.

“‘Is it really so?’ I thought; ‘must everything be resolved into gold?’

“I remember it was long before I slept that night. I envisioned heaps of gold surrounding me. My thoughts were filled with the lovely Countess; I confess, to my shame, that the vision eclipsed another quiet, innocent figure—the woman who had entered a life of toil and obscurity. But the next morning, through the clouds of slumber, Fanny’s sweet face rose before me in all its beauty, and I thought of nothing else.”

“Will you take a glass of eau sucrée?” asked the Vicomtesse, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it.”

“But I see nothing here that concerns us,” said Mme. de Grandlieu, ringing the bell.

“Sardanapalus!” cried Derville, invoking his favorite phrase. “Mademoiselle Camille’s happiness once depended on Daddy Gobseck; but with his death at ninety, M. de Restaud will soon inherit a fortune. This needs explanation. As for poor Fanny Malvaut, you know her; she is my wife.”

“Poor fellow, he would admit that, with his usual frankness, in front of a crowd!” said the Vicomtesse.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” said the attorney.

“Go on, drink your glass, my poor Derville. You will always be the happiest and best of men.”

“I left you in the Rue du Helder,” remarked the uncle, waking from a gentle doze. “You had gone to see a Countess; what became of her?”

“A few days after my conversation with the old Dutchman,” Derville continued, “I submitted my thesis, became a licentiate in law, and then an advocate. The old miser’s opinion of me improved. He consulted me (for free) on tricky matters which would have seemed risky to any ordinary practitioner. This man, who seemed immune to influence, listened to my advice with respect, and it often turned out well.

“Eventually, I became head-clerk in the office where I had worked for three years and then moved into my employer’s house. I had board and lodging and a salary of one hundred and fifty francs per month. It was a great day for me!

“When I went to bid the usurer goodbye, he showed no emotion, neither cordial nor regretful, and did not ask me to visit him again, only giving me a glance that seemed to reveal a second sight.

“A week later, my old neighbor came to see me with a thorny business matter, an expropriation, and continued to seek my advice as if I were still his clerk.

“My principal was a man of pleasure and expensive tastes; by the end of the second year (1818–1819), he found himself in difficulties and had to sell his practice. Back then, a professional connection didn’t fetch the exorbitant prices of today, and my principal asked for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. An active, competent man might hope to pay off the capital in ten years, living respectably in the meantime—if he could command confidence. But as the seventh child of a small tradesman from Noyon, I had no money and no connections except Daddy Gobseck. An ambitious idea and a glimmer of hope encouraged me. I went to Gobseck, my heart pounding as I knocked on his door in the gloomy house, recalling his warnings about the anguish awaiting those who crossed his threshold. Now, I was about to beg like so many others.

“‘Well, no, not that,’ I told myself; ‘an honest man must keep his self-respect. Success is not worth cringing for; let’s show him a front as determined as his own.’

“Daddy Gobseck had taken my room since I left, so as to have no neighbor; he had even added a grated window to his door and didn’t open until he had looked at me.

“‘So your principal is selling his practice?’ he said.

“How did you know that?” I asked, surprised.

“‘Nothing else would have brought you here,’ he replied dryly.

“‘Listen to me, M. Gobseck,’ I began, trying to maintain my composure. ‘I am a penniless clerk, and only you can understand my future. Let’s leave feelings aside. My principal’s practice is worth about twenty thousand francs per annum; in my hands, I believe it could bring in forty thousand. He is willing to sell it for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. If you would lend me the purchase money, I could pay it off in ten years.’

“‘Come, that is plain speaking,’ said Daddy Gobseck, shaking my hand. ‘Nobody has stated their motives more clearly. Guarantees?’ he asked, scanning me. ‘None to give,’ he added after a pause. ‘How old are you?’

“‘Twenty-five in ten days,’ I replied.

“‘Precisely. Well?’

“‘It is possible.’

“‘We must be quick about it, or I shall have someone buying over my head.’

“‘Bring your birth certificate tomorrow morning, and we will talk. I will think it over.’

“Next morning, at eight o’clock, I stood in the old man’s room. He took the document, put on his spectacles, coughed, and read it through. Then he said, ‘We will try to arrange this business.’

“I trembled.

“‘I make fifty percent on my capital,’ he continued, ‘sometimes I make a hundred, two hundred, five hundred percent.’

“I turned pale.

“‘But as we are acquaintances, I shall be satisfied to take twelve and a half percent per—well, yes, from you I would be content to take thirteen percent per annum. Will that suit you?’

“‘Yes,’ I answered.

“‘But if it is too much, stand up for yourself, Grotius!’ (a name he jokingly gave me). ‘When I ask for thirteen percent, it is all in the way of business; look into it, see if you can pay it; I don’t like a man to agree too easily. Is it too much?’

“‘No,’ I said, ‘I will make up for it by working a little harder.’

“‘Gad! your clients will pay for it!’ he said, looking at me wickedly.

“‘No, by all the devils in hell!’ I cried, ‘it shall be I who pays. I would sooner cut my hand off than flay people.’

“‘Good-night,’ said Daddy Gobseck.

“‘Fees are all according to scale,’ I added.

“‘Not for compromises and settlements out of court, and cases where litigants come to terms,’ he replied. ‘You can send in a bill for thousands of francs, six thousand even at a swoop, depending on the case’s importance. A man must learn to look out for business of this kind. I will recommend you as a competent attorney. I will send you so much work that your colleagues will be fit to burst with envy. Werbrust, Palma, and Gigonnet, my cronies, shall hand over their expropriations to you; they have plenty of them! So you will have two practices—the one you are buying and the other I will build up for you. You ought almost to pay me fifteen percent on my loan.’

“‘So be it, but no more,’ I said firmly.

“Daddy Gobseck’s face relaxed; he looked pleased.

“‘I shall pay the money over to your principal myself, to establish a lien on the purchase and caution-money.’

“‘Oh, anything you like in the way of guarantees.’

“‘And you will give me bills for the amount made payable to a third party (name left blank), fifteen bills of ten thousand francs each.’

“‘Well, so long as it is acknowledged in writing that this is a double—’

“‘No!’ Gobseck interrupted. ‘Why should I trust you any more than you trust me?’

“I kept silent.

“‘Furthermore,’ he continued, ‘you will give me your advice without charging fees as long as I live, will you not?’

“‘So be it; as long as there is no outlay.’

“‘Precisely,’ he said. ‘Ah, by the by, you will allow me to visit you?’ (The old man found it hard to assume an air of good humor.)

“‘I shall always be glad.’

“‘Ah! yes, but it would be difficult to arrange in the morning. You will have your affairs to attend to, and I have mine.’

“‘Then come in the evening.’

“‘Oh, no!’ he replied briskly, ‘you ought to go into society and see your clients, and I have my friends at my café.’

“‘His friends!’ I thought. ‘Very well,’ I said, ‘why not come at dinner-time?’

“‘That is the time,’ said Gobseck, ‘after “Change,” at five o’clock. Good, you will see me Wednesdays and Saturdays. We will talk over business like friends. Aha! I am gay sometimes. Just give me the wing of a partridge and a glass of champagne, and we will chat. I know many things that can be told now at this distance of time; I will teach you to know men, and what is more—women!’

“‘Oh! a partridge and a glass of champagne if you like.’

“‘Don’t do anything foolish, or I shall lose my faith in you. And don’t set up housekeeping grandly. Just one old general servant. I will come and see that you keep your health. I have capital invested in your head, he! he! so I must look after you. There, come round in the evening and bring your principal with you!’

“‘Would you mind telling me, if there is no harm in asking, what was the good of my birth certificate in this business?’ I asked as we stood on the doorstep.

“Jean-Esther Van Gobseck shrugged his shoulders, smiled maliciously, and said, ‘What blockheads youngsters are! Learn, master attorney (for learn you must if you don’t want to be taken in), that integrity and brains in a man under thirty are commodities that can be mortgaged. After that age, there is no counting on a man.’

“And with that, he shut the door.

“Three months later, I was an attorney. Soon after, madame, I had the good fortune to undertake the suit for the recovery of your estates. I won, and my name became known. Despite the exorbitant interest, I paid off Gobseck in less than five years. I married Fanny Malvaut, whom I loved deeply. Our parallel lives, our hard work and luck, strengthened our bond. One of her uncles, a wealthy farmer, died and left her seventy thousand francs, which helped clear the loan. Since then, my life has been nothing but happiness and prosperity. Nothing is more utterly uninteresting than a happy man, so let’s return to the other characters.

“About a year after purchasing the practice, I was dragged into a bachelor breakfast-party given by one of our number who had lost a bet to a young man greatly in vogue in fashionable circles. M. de Trailles, the epitome of dandyism, enjoyed a prodigious reputation.”

“But he is still enjoying it,” interjected the Comte de Born. “No one wears clothes with a finer air, nor drives a tandem with better grace. Maxime can gamble, eat, and drink more gracefully than anyone. He is a judge of horses, hats, and pictures. All the women lose their heads over him. He spends about a hundred thousand francs a year, and no one can discover that he has an acre of land or a single dividend warrant. The typical knight-errant of our salons, our boudoirs, our boulevards—Maxime de Trailles is a singular being, fit for anything, and good for nothing, capable of both noble and base acts, more often bespattered with mire than blood, knowing more anxiety than remorse, more concerned with his digestion than any mental process, shamming passion, feeling nothing. Maxime de Trailles is a brilliant link between the hulks and the best society; he belongs to the eminently intelligent class from which a Mirabeau, or a Pitt, or a Richelieu springs at times, though it more often produces Counts of Horn, Fouquier-Tinvilles, and Coignards.”

“Well,” Derville continued, “I had heard much about this individual from poor old Goriot, a client of mine, and I had already taken pains to avoid the dangerous honor of his acquaintance, as I sometimes encountered him in society. Still, my friend was so insistent about this breakfast-party that I could hardly refuse without earning a reputation for squeamishness. Madame, you could hardly imagine what a bachelor’s breakfast-party is like. It means superb display and a studied refinement seldom seen; the luxury of a miser when vanity leads him to be sumptuous for a day.

“You are surprised as you enter the room at the neatness of the table, dazzling with silver, crystal, and damask linen. Life is in full bloom; the young men are graceful, smiling, and speaking in low, demure voices like brides; everything about them looks girlish. Two hours later, you might mistake the room for a battlefield after a fight. Broken glasses, crumpled serviettes lie strewn among the nauseous remnants of food. There is an uproar of jesting toasts, a fire of witticisms and bad jokes; faces are flushed, eyes inflamed and expressionless, unintentional confidences reveal the truth. Bottles are smashed, songs are sung amidst a diabolical racket; men call each other out, hang on each other’s necks, or fall to fisticuffs; the room is filled with a horrid scent made up of a hundred odors, and noise enough for a hundred voices. No one knows what he is eating, drinking, or saying. Some are depressed, others babble, one turns monomaniac, repeating the same word like a bell; another tries to keep the tumult in check; the steadiest proposes an orgy. If a sober person were to enter, he would think he had interrupted a Bacchanalian rite.

“It was in the midst of such chaos that M. de Trailles tried to ingratiate himself with me. My head was clear, and I was on guard. He pretended to be drunk, but was perfectly cool and knew what he was about. Somehow, by the end of the evening, I had promised to introduce him to our Papa Gobseck. The words ‘honor,’ ‘virtue,’ ‘countess,’ ‘honest woman,’ and ‘ill-luck’ were mingled in his discourse with a magical potency, thanks to his golden tongue.

“When I awoke the next morning, trying to recollect the previous day, I struggled to piece together my impressions. Eventually, it seemed that the daughter of one of my clients was in danger of losing her reputation and her husband’s love unless she could gather fifty thousand francs that morning. There had been gaming debts and other accounts. My charming companion had impressed upon me that she was rich enough to cover these reverses with a few years of economy. Only then did I begin to grasp the urgency of his request. I confess, to my shame, that I had no doubt it was crucial for Daddy Gobseck to reconcile with this dandy. I was dressing when the young gentleman appeared.

“‘M. le Comte,’ I said after the usual greetings, ‘I fail to see why you need me to introduce you to Van Gobseck, the most civil and smooth-spoken of capitalists. Money will be forthcoming if he has any, or rather, if you can provide adequate security.’

“‘Monsieur,’ he replied, ‘I do not wish to force you to do me a service, even though you have given your word.’

“‘Sardanapalus!’ I thought, ‘am I going to let him think I won’t keep my word?’

“‘I told you yesterday,’ he said, ‘that I had fallen out with Daddy Gobseck most inopportunely; and as there is scarcely another man in Paris who can provide a hundred thousand francs at the end of the month, I begged you to make my peace with him. But let us say no more about it—’

“M. de Trailles looked at me with a civil insult, making as if to leave.

“‘I am ready to go with you,’ I said.

“When we reached the Rue de Gres, my dandy looked around with a circumspection and unease that puzzled me. His face changed color, and by the time we reached Gobseck’s door, sweat stood in drops on his forehead. Just as we were getting out of the cabriolet, a hackney cab turned into the street. His hawk eye detected a woman inside. His face lit up with a gleam of almost savage joy; he called to a little boy passing by and gave him his horse to hold. Then we approached the old bill discounter.

“‘M. Gobseck,’ I said, ‘I have brought one of my closest friends to see you (whom I trust as I would trust the Devil,’ I added for the old man’s private ear). ‘To oblige me, you will do your best for him (at the ordinary rate), and pull him out of his difficulty (if it suits your convenience).’

“M. de Trailles made his bow to Gobseck, took a seat, and listened with a courtier-like attitude; its charming humility would have touched your heart. But Gobseck sat impassively by the fireside, resembling the statue of Voltaire under the peristyle of the Théâtre-Français. He had partly risen as if to bow, and the skull cap covering his head, along with the narrow strip of sallow forehead, completed his likeness to the man of marble.

“‘I have no money to spare except for my own clients,’ he said.

“‘So you are cross because I may have tried in other quarters to ruin myself?’ laughed the Count.

“‘Ruin yourself!’ Gobseck repeated ironically.

“‘Were you about to say that it is impossible to ruin a man who has nothing?’ the dandy asked. ‘I defy you to find a better stock in Paris!’ he cried, turning on his heels.

“This half-earnest buffoonery produced no effect on Gobseck.

“‘Am I not on intimate terms with the Ronquerolles, the Marsays, the Franchessinis, the two Vandenesses, the Ajuda-Pintos—all the most fashionable young men in Paris? A prince and an ambassador (you know them both) are my partners at play. I draw my revenues from London and Carlsbad and Baden and Bath. Is not this the most brilliant of all industries!’

“‘True.’

“‘You make a sponge of me, begad! You encourage me to swell myself in society, so you can squeeze me when I am hard up; but you yourselves are sponges, just as I am, and death will squeeze you someday.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘If there were no spendthrifts, what would become of you? The pair of us are like soul and body.’

“‘Precisely so.’

“‘Come, now, give us your hand, Grandaddy Gobseck, and be magnanimous if this is “true” and “possible” and “precisely so.”’

“‘You come to me,’ the usurer answered coldly, ‘because Girard, Palma, Werbrust, and Gigonnet are full up of your paper; they are offering it at a loss of fifty percent; and as they likely only gave you half the figure on the face of the bills, they are not worth twenty-five percent of their supposed value. I am your most obedient! Can I in common decency lend a stiver to a man who owes thirty thousand francs and has not one farthing?’ Gobseck continued. ‘The day before yesterday, you lost ten thousand francs at a ball at the Baron de Nucingen’s.’

“‘Sir,’ said the Count, with rare impudence, ‘my affairs are no concern of yours,’ and he scrutinized the old man. ‘A man has no debts until payment is due.’

“‘True.’

“‘My bills will be duly met.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘And at this moment, the question between you and me is simply whether the security I am going to offer is sufficient for the sum I have come to borrow.’

“‘Precisely.’

“A cab stopped at the door, and the sound of wheels filled the room.

“‘I will bring something directly which perhaps will satisfy you,’ cried the young man, and he left the room.

“‘Oh! my son,’ exclaimed Gobseck, rising and stretching out his arms to me, ‘if he has good security, you have saved my life. It would be the death of me. Werbrust and Gigonnet thought they were going to trick me; and now, thanks to you, I shall have a good laugh at their expense tonight.’

There was something frightful about the old man’s ecstasy. It was the one occasion he opened his heart to me; that flash of joy, swift though it was, will never be effaced from my memory.

“‘Favor me by staying here,’ he added. ‘I am armed and a sure shot. I have gone tiger-hunting and fought on deck when there was nothing for it but to win or die; but I don’t care to trust yonder elegant scoundrel.’

He sat down again in his armchair before his bureau, and his face grew pale and impassive as before.

“‘Ah!’ he continued, turning to me, ‘you will see that lovely creature I once told you about; I can hear a fine lady’s step in the corridor; it is she, no doubt;’ and indeed, the young man returned with a woman on his arm. I recognized the Countess, one of old Goriot’s two daughters.

The Countess did not see me at first; I stayed in the window bay, my face against the pane; but I saw her give Maxime a suspicious glance as she entered the money-lender’s damp, dark room. She was so beautiful that, despite her faults, I felt sorry for her. There was a storm of anguish in her heart; her proud features were drawn with pain she strove to disguise. The young man had become her evil genius. I admired Gobseck, whose perspicacity had foreseen their future four years ago at the first bill she endorsed.

“‘Probably,’ I thought, ‘this monster with the angel face controls every possible spring of action in her: rules her through vanity, jealousy, pleasure, and the current of life in the world.’”

The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu interrupted the story.

“Why, the woman’s very virtues have been turned against her,” she exclaimed. “He has made her shed tears of devotion, then abused her kindness and made her pay dearly for unhallowed bliss.”

Derville did not understand the signs Mme. de Grandlieu made to him.

“I confess,” he said, “that I had no inclination to shed tears over the lot of this unhappy creature, so brilliant in society, so repulsive to eyes that could read her heart; I shuddered rather at the sight of her murderer, a young angel with a clear brow, red lips, white teeth, and a winning smile. There they stood before their judge, he scrutinizing them much as a fifteenth-century Dominican inquisitor might have peered into the dungeons of the Holy Office while the torture was administered to two Moors.

“The Countess spoke tremulously. ‘Sir,’ she said, ‘is there any way of obtaining the value of these diamonds, and of keeping the right of repurchase?’ She held out a jewel-case.

“‘Yes, madame,’ I interjected, stepping forward.

“She looked at me, and a shudder ran through her as she recognized me, giving me the glance that means, ‘Say nothing of this,’ all over the world.

“‘This,’ I said, ‘constitutes a sale with faculty of redemption, a formal agreement to transfer and deliver a piece of property, either real estate or personalty, for a given time, after which the previous owner recovers their title upon payment of a stipulated sum.’

“She breathed more freely. The Count looked black; he doubted whether Gobseck would lend much on the diamonds after such a fall in their value. Gobseck, impassive as ever, took up his magnifying glass and scrutinized the jewels. If I were to live for a hundred years, I would never forget the sight of his face at that moment. There was a flush in his pale cheeks; his eyes seemed to catch the sparkle of the stones, for there was an unnatural glitter in them. He rose and went to the light, holding the diamonds close to his toothless mouth, as if he meant to devour them; mumbling vague words over them, holding up bracelets, sprays, necklaces, and tiaras one after another, to judge their quality; taking them out of the jewel-case and putting them back, letting the light bring out all their fires. He was more like a child than an old man; or rather, childhood and dotage seemed to meet in him.

“Fine stones! This set would have fetched three hundred thousand francs before the Revolution. What water! Genuine Asiatic diamonds from Golconda or Visapur. Do you know their worth? No one in Paris but Gobseck can appreciate them. In the Empire, such a set would have cost another two hundred thousand francs!”

He shrugged. “But now diamonds are losing value daily. The Brazilians have flooded the market since the Peace; Indian stones are a better color. Others wear them now besides court ladies. Does madame go to court?”

While he spoke he examined each stone with rapture. “Flawless!” he cried. “Here is a speck!... here is a flaw!... A fine stone that!”

His gaunt face, lit by the jewels, recalled a dull inn mirror that reflects every bright image but reveals nothing of the light.

“Well?” asked the Count, clapping Gobseck on the shoulder.

The old man trembled, set down the case, and resumed his money-lender persona—hard, cold, precise. “How much do you want?”

“One hundred thousand francs for three years,” the Count said.

“That is possible,” Gobseck replied, producing scales and weighing the diamonds, delight and severity contending on his face. The Countess seemed stupefied, perhaps contemplating how far she had fallen. I sensed a remnant of remorse and resolved to help.

“Are the diamonds your personal property, madame?” I asked plainly.

“Yes, monsieur,” she answered, meeting my gaze with pride.

“Make out the deed of purchase with power of redemption, chatterbox,” Gobseck instructed, giving up his chair at the bureau.

“Madame is undoubtedly a married woman?” I pressed.

She nodded.

“Then I will not draw up the deed,” I said.

“And why not?” asked Gobseck.

I drew him aside. “This woman is under her husband’s control; the agreement would be void in law. You could not ignore that fact recorded in the document. You’d be compelled to return the diamonds according to their description.”

Gobseck nodded and turned to the guilty couple. “He is right! That changes everything. Eighty thousand francs down, and you leave the diamonds with me,” he added in a huskier voice. “Possession is as good as title.”

“But—” the young man objected.

“You can take it or leave it,” Gobseck replied, returning the jewel-case to the lady.

“Too many risks to run,” I whispered to her. Gobseck seemed to understand my lip movements. The Count went livid; the Countess faltered. Maxime approached her softly, “Adieu, dear Anastasie, may you be happy! As for me, by tomorrow my troubles will be over.”

“Sir!” cried the lady, turning to Gobseck. “I accept your offer.”

“Come now,” Gobseck replied. “You have taken long enough to decide.”

He drew a cheque for fifty thousand francs on the Bank of France and handed it to the Countess. “Now,” he continued with a smile of mockery, “I will give you the rest in bills—thirty thousand francs’ worth of paper as good as bullion.” He produced drafts bearing the Count’s signature, all protested the day before and likely transferred to him at a reduction.

The young man muttered something about an “old scoundrel!” Gobseck remained unfazed and even produced two pistols from a pigeonhole. “As the insulted man, I fire first.”

“Maxime, you owe this gentleman an explanation,” the trembling Countess urged.

“I had no intention of giving offense,” Maxime stammered.

“Quite sure of that,” Gobseck replied dryly; “you had no intention of meeting your bills, that was all.”

The Countess rose, bowed, and left; M. de Trailles followed with, “If either of you gentlemen forget yourselves, I will have his blood, or he will have mine.”

“Amen!” Gobseck called, putting his pistols away. “But a man must have blood in his veins before he can risk it, my son, and you have nothing but mud in yours.”

When the door closed and the vehicles departed, Gobseck pranced about. “I have the diamonds! I have the diamonds!” he cried, exulting at having obtained them “tolerably cheaply.” His dark glee and ferocious delight at possessing a few white stones made me shudder.

“Aha! There you are, my boy!” he said. “We will dine together. We will have some fun at your place, for I haven’t a home of my own, and these restaurants would poison the Devil himself.”

Something in my face brought back his usual cold impassivity. He sat by the hearth, put a tin saucepan of milk on the brazier, and asked if I would breakfast with him.

“Thanks,” I replied, “I do not breakfast till noon.”

Footsteps hurried in the passage. A stranger knocked with rage in his manner. Gobseck peered through the grating and opened to a man of about thirty-five—the Count, an aristocratic figure like many statesmen.

“Sir,” he said roughly, “did my wife just leave this house?”

“That is possible,” Gobseck answered.

“Do you not understand my meaning?”

“I have not the honor of knowing your lady wife,” Gobseck replied. “I have had many visitors this morning. I could hardly say—”

“A truce to jesting, sir! I mean the woman who just left you.”

“How can I know whether she is your wife or not? I never had the pleasure of seeing you before.”

“You are mistaken, M. Gobseck. We have met before, one morning in my wife’s bedroom. You came to demand payment for a bill—no bill of hers.”

“It was no business of mine to inquire what value she had received for it,” Gobseck returned, casting a malignant look. “I had the bill in the way of business. At the same time, monsieur,” he added, quietly pouring coffee into his bowl of milk, “your right to enter my house and expostulate with me is far from proven. I came of age in the sixty-first year of the preceding century.”

“You have just bought family diamonds that do not belong to my wife for a mere trifle,” the Count roared.

“If Mme. la Comtesse has taken your diamonds, you should have sent a circular to all jewelers, warning them not to buy them; she might have sold them separately,” Gobseck retorted.

“You know my wife, sir!” the Count cried.

“True.”

“She is under her husband’s power.”

“That is possible.”

“She had no right to dispose of those diamonds—”

“Precisely.”

“Very well, sir?”

“Very well, sir. I knew your wife, and she is in her husband’s power; I am quite willing; she is in the power of many people; but—I—do—not—know—your diamonds. If Mme. la Comtesse can put her name to a bill, she can go into business, of course, and buy and sell diamonds on her own account. The thing is plain on the face of it!”

“Good day, sir!” the Count cried, pale with rage. “There are courts of justice.”

“Quite so.”

“This gentleman here,” indicating me, “was a witness to the sale.”

“That is possible.”

As the Count turned to leave, I stepped between them. “M. le Comte,” I said, “you are right, and M. Gobseck is by no means wrong. You could not prosecute the purchaser without bringing your wife into court, and the odium would fall on her. I am an attorney, and I must declare that the diamonds were purchased by M. Gobseck in my presence; but it would be unwise to dispute the sale legally, since the goods are not easily identifiable. In equity you might contend; in law your case would collapse. My advice is to agree with M. Gobseck: he can plead he bought in good faith; in any case you’d have to return the purchase money. Consent to a settlement, with power to redeem after seven, eight, or twelve months, or repurchase them outright and give security.”

Gobseck, dipping bread into his coffee, looked at me when I said “come to terms,” as if impressed; I returned the look. The business was shady, and coming to terms was prudent—especially since I would appear as a witness. The Count thanked me with a smile. In the ensuing negotiation Gobseck displayed enough greed and skill to baffle diplomats; ultimately I drew an instrument in which the Count acknowledged receipt of eighty-five thousand francs, interest included, in consideration of which Gobseck undertook to return the diamonds.

“How wasteful!” the Count exclaimed as he signed. “How is it possible to bridge such a gulf?”

“Have you many children, sir?” Gobseck asked gravely.

The Count winced. The old money-lender had touched the sore point. Gobseck, reading the silence as answer, joked aloud about the woman’s nature and the Count’s affection, advised ways to preserve an estate via friends or fictitious sale, and said to me, “You call that a fidei commissum, don’t you?” The Count seemed absorbed. “You shall have your money tomorrow; have the diamonds in readiness,” he said, then left.

“There goes one who looks to me to be as stupid as an honest man,” Gobseck said coolly after the Count had gone.

“Say rather stupid as a man of passionate nature,” I replied.

“The Count owes you your fee for drawing up the agreement!” Gobseck called after me as I left.

A few days later the Count came to my office. “I have come to consult you on a matter of grave moment,” he said, and told me he trusted me. He had made inquiries about Gobseck and wanted to transfer his property to him. “Draw up the necessary papers; I am going to transfer my property to Gobseck. This transfer shall be a simulated sale, and Gobseck, as trustee, will administer my estate and make over my fortune to my eldest son when he comes of age. I cannot keep the document myself. My boy loves his mother, and I cannot trust him with it. Dare I beg you to keep it for me? In case of death, Gobseck would make you legatee of my property. Every contingency is provided for.”

He paused, greatly agitated. “I am in great pain and have grave misgivings about my health,” he said.

I thanked him for his trust but warned that such a counter-deed would disinherit his other children. “They bear your name. Merely as the children of a once-loved wife, they have a claim to an assured existence. I cannot accept your trust unless their future is secured.”

The Count trembled and wept. “We will make provision for the children in the counter-deed,” he promised.

I accompanied him to the door and left feeling the act would be just.

Three months later, with the deed signed but not delivered to me, Gobseck dined at my house. I asked why I had heard nothing more of M. de Restaud. “There are excellent reasons for that,” he said; “the noble Count is at death’s door.” Gobseck implied he and I were the only ones in whom the Count had confidence—because we had trusted him without finesse.

Though this encouraged me to believe Gobseck might act fairly if the counter-deed were lost, I resolved to visit the Count. I went to the Rue du Helder and was shown into a room where the Countess sat playing with her children. On hearing my name she sprang up, then sat and pointed to a chair by the fire. Trouble had withered her face; little of its beauty remained.

“It is essential, madame, that I speak to M. le Comte—”

“If so, you would be more favored than I am,” she interrupted. “M. de Restaud will see no one. He will hardly allow his doctor to come; he will not be nursed even by me. When people are ill, they have such strange fancies!”

“Perhaps, like children, they know very well what they want,” I said. The Countess reddened. I tried to steer the conversation back to business; she would not allow me to see her husband. She affected civility but dissimulated perfectly; I suspected she was resolved to guard secrets closely. I left.

I later learned more. When the Count seemed to abandon himself to dissipation, something passed between husband and wife, and the Count regarded his wife more harshly. Once bedridden, he expressed aversion for the Countess and the two youngest children, forbade them to enter his room, and any attempt to disobey brought on dangerous attacks, so that even the doctor implored the Countess to submit.

Mme. de Restaud saw her family’s estate and house pass into Gobseck’s hands, who appeared monstrous in his handling of their wealth. She partly understood what her husband was doing: he was taking precautions against her, at Gobseck’s suggestion. M. de Trailles was traveling in England and could not explain that the Count had secured the fortune. It is said the Countess held out for long before signing for the sale of property; yet the Count gained his point. She came to believe her husband had safely realized his fortune and had deposited papers or notes somewhere—a notary’s box, a bank, or a safe place. She thought he must have a document by which remaining property could be recovered for his son.

So she kept strict watch over the sick-room, ruling the house, submitting everything to feminine espionage. She sat in the salon adjoining his room all day to hear every syllable, had a bed put there, and did not sleep much. She feigned devotion so perfectly that some even thought she had expiated her sins. Always she saw the peril of destitution on the Count’s death; so the wife, repulsed but near, crouched like an ant-lion at the bottom of a pit, watching every fall of sand.

Yet the Countess pushed maternal sentiment to extremes. Her children were young; she sought to secure their future. Gobseck joked about her devotion. She had discovered Maxime’s baseness and seemed to be expiating past sins in bitter tears. Hateful as her measures to regain control of the money were, they might spring from a mother’s love and desire to repair wrongs. Perhaps she only learned the worth of virtue when she reaped the bitter harvest of error.

Each time little Ernest came from his father’s room she questioned him closely about what he had heard. The boy, eager to please, told her more than she asked. My visits were rays of light to the Countess; she wanted me as instrument of vengeance and resolved I should not be allowed near the dying man. I feared for the counter-deed. If it fell into her hands she might use it, and endless litigation would follow between her and Gobseck. I knew the usurer well enough to think he would never yield; a tangle of transfers would ensue, and only I knew the full story. I resolved to forestall disaster.

I sued the Comte de Restaud for a sum ostensibly due to Gobseck and gained judgment, so I could affix seals to his property on his death. I bribed a servant to inform me when his master was at death’s door, so I could intervene, threaten to affix seals, and secure the counter-deed. I later learned the Countess was studying the Code with her husband’s moans in her ears. Money, always the motive, shaped schemes round the deathbed.

For two months the Count lay on his bed, sapped by mortal disease. He would not allow anyone to set his room in order; the furniture was out of place, dainties covered in dust and cobwebs. Bottles, crumpled linen, broken plates lay about. He delighted in the comfortless look; rows of empty and full medicine bottles and an unremoved bath testified to his apathy. He could not bear daylight; shutters were closed; his face was wasted, hair uncut; he looked like a religious fanatic in the desert. Mental suffering had extinguished most human instincts in the man once brilliant and successful.

One morning in early December 1824 he looked up at Ernest. “Are you in pain?” the little Vicomte asked.

“No,” said the Count, with a ghastly smile, “it all lies here and about my heart!” He pointed to his forehead and then to his hollow chest. Ernest began to cry.

“How is it that M. Derville does not come to me?” the Count asked his servant. “I have sent for my attorney seven or eight times during the last fortnight, and he does not come!” He called for me with urgency and insisted; when the servant prevaricated, the dying man seemed greatly agitated. “Oh God!” he cried. “I put my trust in none but Thee.”

He turned to his son. “Ernest, my boy, you are very young; but you have a good heart; you can understand that a promise given to a dying man is sacred. Do you feel you can keep a secret, so closely that even your mother shall not know you have a secret to keep?”

“Yes, father,” answered the child.

“In a minute I will give you a sealed packet that belongs to M. Derville; you must take such care of it that no one can know you have it; then you must slip out of the house and put the letter into the post-box at the corner.”

“Yes, father.”

“Will you do it?”

“Yes, father.”

“Come and kiss me. You have made death less bitter to me, dear boy. In six or seven years’ time you will understand the importance of this secret, and you will be well rewarded then.”

Ernest kissed him and went out. In the next room he found his mother.

“Ernest,” she said, “come here.” She drew him to her knees and held him. “Your father said something to you just now.”

“Yes, mamma.”

“What did he say?”

“I cannot repeat it, mamma.”

“Ah!” cried the Count. He had opened the door and stood there, a half-naked apparition, thin as a skeleton.

His smothered cry produced a terrible effect. The Countess, in sudden stupor, was motionless. Her husband, white and wasted, confronted her. “You have filled my life full of trouble, and now you are trying to vex my deathbed, to warp my boy’s mind, and make a depraved man of him!” he cried.

She flung herself at his feet, heaving and sobbing. “Mercy! mercy!” she cried. “If you condemn me, condemn me; the children? Condemn your widow to a convent; I will obey and do anything, anything you bid me, to expiate the wrong I have done you, if that so the children may be happy!”

“I have only one child,” said the Count, stretching out a wasted arm toward his son.

“Pardon a penitent woman,” wailed the Countess. “Pardon me...” She could not speak for sobbing.

“You dare to talk of penitence after all you said to Ernest!” he cried, shaking her off. “You have been a bad daughter; you have been a bad wife; you will be a bad mother.”

The Countess fainted. The dying man returned to his bed, sank unconscious. The priests administered the sacraments.

At midnight he died. I arrived with Gobseck. The house was in confusion. We walked into the little salon adjoining the death-chamber. The three children were there in tears with two priests. Ernest approached and said that his mother desired to be alone in the Count’s room. “Do not go in,” he said, and I admired the child’s tone. Gobseck let out one of his soundless laughs. When Ernest saw us move toward the door he planted himself before it, crying, “Mamma, here are some gentlemen in black who want to see you!”

Gobseck lifted Ernest aside as if the child were a feather and opened the door.

What a scene! The room was in frightful disorder—clothes, papers, and rags tossed about in the presence of Death; the Countess stood in disheveled despair. The Count had scarcely breathed his last before she forced open drawers and the desk; the carpet was strewn with litter, furniture and boxes broken; signs of violence were everywhere. If her search had at first proved fruitless, her excitement suggested she had found the documents at last. The mattress had been flung aside; the Count’s body lay face down like one of the paper envelopes strewn on the carpet. The dying man must have hidden the counter-deed under his pillow; his wife must have guessed and seized upon it. The pillow lay on the floor; I could see the print of her heel. At her feet lay a paper with the Count’s arms on the seal; I snatched it up and saw it was addressed to me. I looked at the Countess with the pitiless clarity of an examining magistrate. The contents were blazing in the grate; she had thrown them on the fire at our approach, thinking, from a hasty glance, that she was destroying a will that disinherited her children. Tormented conscience had taken away her power of reflection. Caught in the act, she, perhaps foreseeing punishment, was almost paralyzed.

There she stood gasping, waiting for us to speak, staring at us with haggard eyes. I pulled at the grate and found an unburned fragment. “Ah, madame!” I exclaimed, “you have ruined your children! Those papers were their titles to their property.”

Her mouth twitched; she seemed near paralysis. Gobseck’s harsh laugh grated on us. He turned to me calmly: “Do you intend Mme. la Comtesse to suppose that I am not the rightful owner of the property sold to me by her late husband? This house belongs to me now.”

A sudden blow would have caused less astonishment. The Countess saw the hesitation on my face.

“Monsieur!” she cried.

“You are a trustee, are you not?” I asked.

“That is possible.”

“Then do you mean to take advantage of this crime of hers?”

“Precisely.”

I left her sitting by her husband’s bedside, weeping. Gobseck followed me; outside he flung at me one of those searching glances and said in his hoarse flute-like voice, “Do you take it upon yourself to judge me?”

We saw little of each other afterward. Gobseck let the Count’s mansion on lease, spent summers on the estates, repaired mills and roads, planted timber—he became a lord of the manor. I met him once in the Jardin des Tuileries.

“The Countess is behaving like a heroine,” I said. “She gives herself entirely to the children’s education. The oldest boy is a charming young fellow—”

“That is possible,” Gobseck answered.

“But ought you not to help Ernest?” I asked.

“Help him!” Gobseck cried. “Not I. Adversity is the greatest teacher; it teaches the value of money and the worth of men and women. Let him set sail on the seas of Paris; when he is a qualified pilot, we will give him a ship to steer.”

I left without trying to explain. M. de Restaud’s mother had prejudiced him against me; he would not take me as his adviser; still, I went to see Gobseck recently to urge him to honor his contract, since young Restaud was of age and wished to marry Mlle. Camille.

I found the old bill-discounter bedridden, worse than he suspected. He wanted to be alone, had taken rooms one by one as they fell vacant, and kept his own room exactly as always, as if preserved under glass. His faithful old portress and her pensioner cared for him; despite his feebleness he received clients in bed, taking money and simplifying affairs to avoid sending people on errands.

After the treaty by which France recognized the Haytian Republic, Gobseck had been on the commission to liquidate claims; his knowledge of old fortunes in San Domingo and planters’ claims led to his nomination. He devised an agency for discounting claims, selling the idea under names like Werbrust and Gigonnet and sharing spoils without disbursements, for his knowledge mattered instead of capital. Presents from those who sought his favor became a percentage on large sums: game, wax candles, rich plate, snuff-boxes—everything went in but nothing came out. “He swallows it all and is none the fatter for it; he is as thin and dried up as the cuckoo in the clock,” said the portress.

Last Monday Gobseck sent his pensioner for me. “Be quick,” he said. “The governor is going to hand in his checks; he has grown yellow as a lemon; the rattle is in his throat.”

I found the dying man kneeling before the grate, not cold but frantic. “I am going, I know not where, lad,” he said, looking at me with lightless eyes. He named people and bequests, half in delirium, half in lucidity, then, with the profile of a cast bronze, stretched out a bony hand and died in full consciousness. To those present he looked like an old Roman from a mourning picture.

“He was a good-plucked one, the old Lascar!” the pensioner remarked.

But his final enumeration of riches lingered in my ears. My eyes followed his glance to the ashes in the grate; the heap seemed large. I stirred the cinders and felt metal beneath—gold and silver coins, receipts tucked during illness. I ordered the justice of the peace to come and seal everything at once.

The rooms nearby explained his ravings: one was full of putrid pies, moldy fish, and shellfish among chests of tea, coffee, and packing-cases—an olfactory horror. Other rooms were crammed with furniture, silver-plate, lamps, vases, pictures, books, curios, and engravings—some presents, some pledges left unpaid. Jewel-cases bore ciphers and arms, fine table-linen and weapons of price. None of it was docketed. I found a thousand-franc note tucked in a misplaced book. The stench and confusion showed the depths of a miser’s eccentricity.

On his desk a pile of letters told of bargains left in suspense. Dealers to whom he had offered his presents would accept only at a loss, and Gobseck haggled for a few francs; while they argued the goods became unsaleable. He had refused free delivery of silver and would not guarantee coffee weights. Every article was the subject of a petty dispute—childish obstinacy born of age and a passion surviving the intellect.

“To whom will all these riches go?” I thought. Gobseck had given an address for a supposed heiress, a girl in the Rue Vivienne called “La Torpille.” I foresaw the duty—searching houses of ill-fame in Paris to pour out an immense fortune on some worthless jade. But first, Ernest de Restaud will soon inherit a fortune unquestionably his, enough to enable him to marry Mlle. Camille, after adequate provision has been made for his mother and siblings.
