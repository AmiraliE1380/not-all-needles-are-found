**GOBSECK**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated By Ellen Marriage*  

**DEDICATION**  
To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.  

In the winter of 1829-30, at one o'clock in the morning, the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu's salon was nearly empty. After a young man left, the Vicomtesse approached her daughter, Camille, who was distracted by sounds outside. Concerned about Camille's feelings for the Comte de Restaud, she warned her about his mother, Mlle. Goriot, whose questionable past could jeopardize Camille's future. Despite Restaud's admirable qualities, his family background raised concerns.

Derville, a family friend and attorney, overheard their conversation and decided to intervene. He had previously helped the Vicomtesse regain lost properties, improving her financial situation. His success earned him respect in high society, yet he remained humble and declined further career advancement.

As Derville recounted a story that might change Camille's view of Restaud, he noted her growing affection for him. At a recent ball, Camille expressed confidence in Restaud's potential, believing he would achieve wealth and status through his abilities. Derville pointed out that if Restaud were already wealthy, many girls would compete for his attention, hinting at Camille's feelings.

This realization shifted Camille's demeanor towards Derville, who had been more of a polite acquaintance than a friend. Her newfound attentiveness suggested a budding connection, despite the social obligations and gratitude complicating relationships across different classes.

Derville reflects on a significant romantic event involving a usurer named Gobseck, a sallow, impassive man with a meticulous lifestyle, living in a damp, dark house resembling a convent. A solitary figure, he rarely interacts with others except for Derville, who becomes his confidant over four years. Born Jean-Esther Van Gobseck, he is a wealthy usurer whose life has been filled with adventure yet remains emotionally detached.

Derville recounts a conversation where Gobseck emphasizes gold as the ultimate power and reality, dismissing societal norms and arguing that self-interest is the only constant in human nature. His cynical perspective suggests that true happiness lies in wealth and manipulation.

Gobseck shares his experiences collecting debts, revealing two bills of exchange: one from a beautiful Countess burdened by her extravagant lifestyle, who ultimately offers him a diamond instead of payment, and another from a struggling linen-draper named Fanny Malvaut. Derville is captivated by the Countess's beauty and the contrast between her life and Fanny's modest existence.

Visiting Fanny, who embodies innocence and sincerity, Derville feels a stark contrast to the Countess's world of luxury and moral decay. He ponders the differences between their lives, feeling pity for both women. Gobseck's insights into human nature and societal power dynamics leave Derville disturbed and reflective about wealth and desire.

As Derville grapples with his feelings for the Countess and admiration for Fanny, he realizes the profound impact of Gobseck's worldview. The usurer's life, driven by the pursuit of gold and power, starkly highlights the moral complexities and emotional voids accompanying such a lifestyle. Ultimately, Derville contemplates the true cost of wealth and the fleeting nature of beauty and happiness.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
“Will you take a glass of _eau sucree_?” asked the Vicomtesse, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it,” he replied.

Mme. de Grandlieu expressed skepticism about the conversation's relevance, prompting Derville to invoke Sardanapalus. He recounted how Mademoiselle Camille's happiness had once depended on Daddy Gobseck, who had recently passed away, leaving M. de Restaud a fortune. Derville then mentioned his wife, Fanny Malvaut.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” he affirmed, expressing his love for her.

Derville explained that after submitting his thesis, he became a licentiate in law and later an advocate. Gobseck, who had previously underestimated him, began consulting him on complex matters, showing newfound respect. Eventually, Derville became head-clerk and moved into his employer’s house, marking a significant career step.

When he bid farewell to Gobseck, the old man showed no emotion but later sought Derville’s advice on a challenging expropriation case. Derville’s principal, a man of pleasure, fell into financial trouble and decided to sell his practice for 150,000 francs. Lacking funds, Derville approached Gobseck for a loan, determined to maintain his self-respect.

Gobseck, after assessing Derville’s situation, agreed to lend him the money at a rate of thirteen percent per annum, a deal Derville accepted despite the high interest. They discussed guarantees, and Gobseck insisted on receiving bills made payable to a third party. Derville agreed to provide legal advice to Gobseck for free as part of their arrangement.

Three months later, Derville became an attorney and successfully recovered estates for Mme. de Grandlieu, gaining recognition in the legal field. He married Fanny, whose inheritance helped him pay off Gobseck within five years. Their shared struggles deepened their bond, leading to a happy life together.

A year after acquiring his practice, Derville attended a bachelor breakfast hosted by M. de Trailles, a well-known dandy. The event was marked by extravagant displays and chaotic revelry, where Derville found himself charmed by de Trailles, who persuaded him to introduce him to Gobseck.

The next day, Derville hesitated but ultimately agreed to accompany de Trailles to meet Gobseck. Upon arrival, de Trailles appeared anxious, revealing a woman in a cab nearby, which excited him. Derville introduced de Trailles to Gobseck, who remained impassive despite de Trailles’ attempts at charm.

Gobseck refused to lend money to de Trailles, citing his debts and lack of security. De Trailles, undeterred, left the room to fetch something he believed would satisfy Gobseck. Gobseck expressed concern about de Trailles’ intentions, revealing a rare moment of vulnerability.

When de Trailles returned with a woman, Derville recognized her as the Countess, one of Goriot’s daughters. The Countess, visibly distressed, sought to pawn her diamonds for cash, hoping to retain the right to repurchase them. Derville explained the transaction terms, which relieved her but darkened de Trailles’ mood.

Gobseck examined the diamonds with unsettling eagerness, revealing his obsession with wealth. His reaction was childlike, contrasting sharply with his usual stoicism. The scene underscored the complex interplay of desire, power, and desperation among the characters as they navigated the treacherous waters of social and financial relationships in Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The dialogue begins with a discussion about a set of diamonds, once highly valued before the Revolution, now losing worth due to market saturation from Brazilian stones. Gobseck, a moneylender, examines the diamonds with delight, noting their flaws and qualities. The Count seeks a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years, and Gobseck agrees but insists on weighing the diamonds first. The Countess appears distressed, hinting at her moral struggle.

As Gobseck weighs the diamonds, the narrator observes the Countess's turmoil and senses her remorse. When questioned about the ownership of the diamonds, she confirms they are hers, but he refuses to draft the loan agreement due to legal issues since she is married. Gobseck then offers a deal: eighty thousand francs down, with the diamonds as collateral. The Countess hesitates, and Maxime, a young man present, expresses his despair, suggesting he will end his troubles soon.

Ultimately, the Countess accepts Gobseck's offer, and he provides her with a check and cash. Tensions rise as Maxime insults Gobseck, who coolly threatens him with pistols. After the Countess leaves, Gobseck revels in acquiring the diamonds, showcasing his greed and delight in the transaction.

Days later, the Count visits the narrator, expressing confidence in him and discussing Gobseck's character. The Count reveals his intention to transfer his property to Gobseck, fearing for his health and future. The narrator warns the Count about disinheriting his other children, leading the Count to agree to include them in the counter-deed.

The narrative shifts to the Countess, who, despite her past, is determined to secure her children's future. She becomes increasingly desperate as her husband’s health declines. The Count, bedridden and resentful, forbids her from entering his room, prompting her to spy on him. She believes he is hiding money and documents that could secure their wealth.

As the Count nears death, he confides in his son Ernest, entrusting him with a secret letter for the narrator. The Countess, sensing her husband's intentions, tries to manipulate Ernest into revealing the secret. The Count confronts her, leading to a dramatic scene where he accuses her of treachery and expresses disdain for her actions.

After the Count's death, chaos ensues as the Countess searches for documents, revealing her desperation. The narrator discovers that the Count had hidden important papers, including the counter-deed, which the Countess inadvertently destroys in her panic. Gobseck, now the rightful owner of the property, contemplates the implications of the Countess's actions.

In the aftermath, the narrator reflects on the Countess's transformation and the consequences of her greed. Gobseck, despite his avarice, shows a peculiar understanding of human nature, believing that adversity teaches valuable lessons. The story concludes with the narrator contemplating the fate of the Countess and her children, as well as Gobseck's legacy.

The text explores themes of greed, morality, and the complexities of human relationships, illustrating the destructive power of ambition and the consequences of desperate choices.
