*Two Poets* is the first part of a trilogy centered on Lucien, his sister Eve, and their friend David in Angoulême. The narrative unfolds during the era of traditional printing, focusing on Jérôme-Nicolas Séchard, a former journeyman pressman who owns a small printing business acquired during the French Revolution.

Séchard evolves from a struggling pressman to a businessman driven by greed and the desire to secure a future for his son, David. After studying in Paris, David returns in 1819 to take over the family business, which faces competition from the Cointet Brothers. Tensions arise between father and son as Séchard's greed complicates their relationship. 

In a key scene, Séchard, intoxicated, overvalues the business, while David recognizes its true worth. Despite concerns, David agrees to terms that allow Séchard to retain a share, but soon faces financial strain as Séchard refuses to assist. 

David's friendship with Lucien deepens as they share dreams and innovative ideas, though their artistic pursuits distract them from practical issues. By May 1821, they seek escape from their oppressive environment, with Lucien's feelings for Mme. de Bargeton complicating his ambitions, setting the stage for his struggle against societal constraints while David remains a loyal friend.

M. de Bargeton, from a declining Bordeaux family, married Mlle. Marie-Louise-Anaïs de Nègrepelisse, a noblewoman with a strong sense of self-worth and disdain for others. After her father's death, she rebelled against constraints and married the older M. de Bargeton, hoping for freedom in Paris. However, she found her marriage limiting and longed for excitement. The rise of the Empire briefly rekindled her hopes, but the death of a young officer deepened her sorrow. 

M. du Châtelet, a charming man, disrupted her life, recognizing her social potential. He introduced her to Lucien, a young poet, leading to a deepening bond that attracted societal scrutiny. Mme. de Bargeton defended Lucien against gossip, hosting gatherings to showcase his talent, while Châtelet attempted to undermine him, highlighting the tension between aspirations and social hierarchy.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
As Lucien descended from Angoulême, he was troubled by his feelings for Mme. de Bargeton and the implications of his friend David's presence on her reputation. Aware of the social divide, he feared that David's lack of refinement would lead to ridicule. Lucien resolved to achieve success independently, inspired by Napoleon’s meritocracy, but felt discontented with his humble life as he prepared to meet Mme. de Bargeton.

He was excited to receive a letter from her, indicating her willingness to help David, though her biases against lower classes were evident. Lucien saw this as a victory, believing David would impress her. His sister Eve teased him about his happiness, sensing his romantic feelings.

David, in love with Eve, felt unworthy due to his background but was committed to supporting Lucien. As Lucien prepared for a public reading, David advised him to select a different poem, fearing his biblical work might not resonate. At the reading, Lucien was nervous, especially with M. de Bargeton present. He struggled to connect with the audience, and despite his passionate delivery, his poem was met with indifference, leaving him feeling isolated and rejected.

Meanwhile, Eve and David shared a tender bond, with David proposing marriage to help Lucien. Eve hesitated due to their social differences, but David reassured her of his commitment. Lucien, burdened by humiliation, confided in them about his determination to win over Mme. de Bargeton, unaware that their engagement could hinder his ambitions.

Lucien's status rose after receiving praise from the Bishop and Mme. de Bargeton, making him a local hero. He began to see himself as significant and enjoyed the luxuries of his newfound status, though rumors about his closeness with Louise circulated. Tensions escalated when Châtelet and Stanislas claimed to have seen Lucien with Louise, leading to a duel challenge.

The next day, news spread that Bargeton had shot Stanislas, turning public sentiment against Lucien and Mme. de Bargeton. As preparations for Eve’s wedding continued, Lucien felt torn between family obligations and his desire for Louise. Ultimately, his departure for Paris was bittersweet, filled with hope and uncertainty about the future.
