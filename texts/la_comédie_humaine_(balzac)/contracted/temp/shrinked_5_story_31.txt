Early in the autumn of 1826 the Abbé Birotteau, the chief figure of this tale, was caught in a downpour as he returned from an evening at a friend's. He hurried, as his corpulence and gout allowed, across the deserted square called the Cloister behind the chancel of Saint-Gatien at Tours. Small, apoplectic and about sixty, he loathed having his shoes soaked because damp always brought gout. Yet he bore the rain resignedly: he had won three francs ten sous at rubber with Madame de Listomère, had wrapped himself in a canon’s fur cape, and was elated by talk that he might be named canon — a post he had long coveted.

The Cloister contained, on the Grand’Rue side, a cluster of cathedral houses forming a Close that had become the narrow Rue de la Psalette. Opposite, a single house, its walls overshadowed by Saint-Gatien’s buttresses, had always been occupied by abbés and belonged to an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Time had covered the cathedral in dingy mantles, moss and lichens; the house too lay in shadow, a solitude only fit for those of absolute nullity or uncommon fortitude. Mademoiselle Gamard, devout and a careful housekeeper, took priests to board and kept the dwelling—bought by her father in the Terror—without objection from the restored Church.

Birotteau had lived two years in that house and had longed for its rooms as highly as he longed for a canonry. His two great desires—being Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and becoming a canon—were the ambitions of a provincial priest who sought decent lodging, good food, clean garments and the self-esteem of a title. The apartment he inhabited had been the cherished object of a passion begun when he first admired the rooms of his friend, the Abbé Chapeloud, and which grew into an obsession.

For years Chapeloud, who became canon when worship was restored under Napoleon, had lived in the south-facing apartment, gradually furnishing and renovating it with modest legacies and gifts: an oak bookcase from a demolished château, waxed floors, painted woodwork, a long ebony table, Boulle cabinets, folios and Fathers of the Church. The rooms had been transformed from shabby to dignified; an oratory and a salon with red damask and mahogany completed them. Birotteau, poor and the son of peasants like Chapeloud, admired these changes and secretly yearned to possess them. His desire increased until, when Chapeloud fell ill, thoughts he could not suppress ran, “If Chapeloud dies I can have this apartment.” Yet he never contemplated any means to influence his friend’s will.

Chapeloud, indulgent and knowing his friend, forgave his covetousness, and on his deathbed, as Birotteau read the Quotidienne, said, “This time you will certainly get the apartment.” Chapeloud bequeathed his library and furniture to Birotteau, who, though saddened, found consolation in the inheritance and in Mademoiselle Gamard’s cheerful agreement to take him as a lodger. He moved into the apartment and enjoyed comforts he had long imagined: meticulous linen, fires, well-chosen wood, fragrant orris-root, and the small attentions for which Chapeloud had praised the spinster. Mademoiselle Gamard’s household had been famous, Chapeloud had often declared, for its perfect order and attentive service.

Yet, having obtained his long-desired comforts, Birotteau soon replaced the earlier obsession with a new hunger: the canonry. Hopes fed at Madame de Listomère’s conversations persuaded him that promotion was near. Absorbed in these hopes, he did not notice until his return that he had left his umbrella and that some domestic attentions were missing. He found the outer door locked, Marianne the servant answering awkwardly that Mademoiselle had thought he was in; his candlestick had been moved to an ante-chamber; the fire in his room had not been burning; his slippers were not placed where they usually were. These trivial changes struck him as deliberate signs of displeasure. He concluded, by a sort of clerical logic, that Mademoiselle Gamard intended to chill and humiliate him. The small humiliations mortified him deeply.

Birotteau, kindly but simple-minded and ignorant of worldly calculations, lacked the frank self-knowledge to see what he himself had done to provoke dislike. He was a good man whose goodness verged on stupidity; his learning was shallowly acquired, and his life centered on mass and the confessional. He could not perceive how unconscious selfishness and provincial narrowness, common to many, had made him difficult. He was, in short, a clumsy egoist, unlike Chapeloud, who was a shrewd one.

Chapeloud had understood Mademoiselle Gamard: an old maid of about thirty-eight with pretensions turned into strong self-esteem. The canon had judged she needed constant, precise attentions if he would live comfortably under her roof. So he kept limited contact with her, avoided breakfast and supper, and came to dinner a few minutes early to pay polite compliments about the food and service. By these attentions, and by never creating points of contact beyond politeness, Chapeloud secured a peaceful coexistence and was often praised as easy to live with.

Troubert, the other lodger, was her creature: obedient, patient and entirely under her rule, judged often to have designs on her property because of the intimacy of their domestic life. When Chapeloud died the landlady had thought at first to offer the rooms to Troubert, but seeing Birotteau’s attachment to the apartment she accepted him as lodger for her own advantage and even improved the rooms: replacing brick with wooden floors and rebuilding the chimney.

Yet Birotteau, dazzled by his new happiness, did not adopt Chapeloud’s circumspection. He fell into Mademoiselle Gamard’s life with the embarrassment and timidity of a man who fears seeming rude and who stays too long in conversation. He began to spend his evenings there, and the spinster, whose real desire had long been to make her salon a weekly center of company, welcomed the chance to display more prominent visitors. Taking pride in entertaining, she boasted that thanks to Birotteau she could gather respectable company.

But the vicar soon deserted her salon for Madame de Listomère’s and Mademoiselle Salomon’s, preferring aristocratic circles to which the landlady did not belong. His withdrawal gravely wounded Mademoiselle Gamard; she had expected to use him to attract society. Her disappointment, joined to an old-maid’s craving for return on social investment and wounded pride, turned into resentment. The vicar, however, never understood this. He perceived, belatedly and clumsily, signs of slights—small omissions and unkindnesses—and concluded that ill will was directed at him though he could not see the reasons.

Mademoiselle Gamard, like many who never adapted their habits to changing lives, indulged a petty despotism over small things—where the basket of counters lay, the arrangement of cards, the order of household routine. Such trifles she magnified into tests of obedience. The vicar, unlike Chapeloud, could not accept being persistently cinched by such crochets without protest; he withdrew rather than submitting, irritating her more by his absence than by any direct opposition.

Her friends, defending her, called Chapeloud amiable and blamed Birotteau for turning away from the old maid; society at large often sided, covertly, with those who escaped a nightly round in the Cloister. Mademoiselle Gamard enjoyed the condolence she received, and Troubert explained the departure by saying the abbe sought fashion, luxury and witty talk—thus cleverly flattering the spinster by implying he had higher tastes.

In truth, the vicar’s abandonment was a clumsy desertion, and his naïveté and lack of social tact made him gradually intolerable to Mademoiselle Gamard. Eighteen months after she had taken him in, when he mistook her silence for contentment, she had in fact begun a deliberate, underhand persecution. The locked door, the forgotten slippers, the unlit fire and the moved candlestick were the first signs. Birotteau, too innocent to see his own faults toward his landlady, could not understand why she had turned against him.

This prologue, recounting the small domestic developments that precede the greater drama, requires many particulars. The little meannesses, jealousies and petty ambitions of bourgeois life inflict injuries as keen as those produced by grander interests, and their slow, minute growths needed this account to explain the coming catastrophe.

The next morning, on awaking, Birotteau thought so much of his prospective canonry that he forgot the four circumstances in which he had seen, the night before, such threatening prognostics of a future full of misery. The vicar did not rise without a fire. He rang to let Marianne know he was awake and that she must come; then he remained, as usual, absorbed in somnolent musings. The servant’s custom was to make the fire and gently draw him from his half sleep by the murmured sound of her movements—a sound he loved. Twenty minutes passed and Marianne had not appeared. Birotteau, now half a canon, was about to ring again when he let go the bell-pull, hearing a man’s step on the staircase. In a minute the Abbe Troubert, after discreetly knocking, obeyed Birotteau’s invitation and entered. This visit, which the two abbés usually paid each other once a month, was no surprise. The canon at once exclaimed when he saw that Marianne had not made the fire. He opened the window and called to her harshly, telling her to come at once to the abbé; then, turning to his ecclesiastical brother, he said, “If Mademoiselle knew that you had no fire she would scold Marianne.”

After this he inquired about Birotteau’s health, and asked gently if he had any news that gave him hopes of his canonry. The vicar explained the steps he had taken, and told, naively, the names of persons with whom Madame de Listomere was using her influence, quite unaware that Troubert had never forgiven that lady for not admitting him—the Abbe Troubert, twice proposed by the bishop as vicar-general—to her house.

It would be impossible to find two figures presenting so many contrasts as these two abbés. Troubert, tall and lean, was yellow and bilious, while the vicar was, familiarly, plump. Birotteau’s round ruddy face proclaimed a kindly nature barren of ideas, while Troubert’s long, deeply wrinkled features sometimes took an expression of sarcasm or contempt; but one had to watch him closely to detect those sentiments. The canon’s habitual condition was perfect calm; his eyelids usually lowered over orange-colored eyes that could, however, give clear, piercing glances. Reddish hair added to the gloomy effect of a countenance often obscured by the veil of deep meditation. Many at first thought him absorbed in high ambition; those who knew him better denied it, saying he was stupidly dull under Mademoiselle Gamard’s despotism or worn out by fasting. He seldom spoke and never laughed; when pleasantly moved, a feeble smile would flicker and lose itself in his wrinkles.

Birotteau, on the other hand, was all expansion and frankness; he loved good things and was amused by trifles with the simplicity of a man without spite or malice. Troubert at first sight roused an involuntary fear; the vicar’s presence brought a kind smile. When the tall canon marched with solemn step through Saint-Gatien, his head bowed, his eye stern, respect followed him; that bent face harmonized with the yellowing arches of the cathedral; the folds of his cassock fell in monumental lines. The good vicar, in contrast, perambulated with little gravity; he trotted and ambled and at times seemed to roll himself along. Yet in one point they resembled each other: Troubert’s ambitious air, which made him feared, probably helped keep him down to canon; similarly, Birotteau’s ways marked him out perpetually as vicar and nothing higher.

Yet the Abbe Troubert, now fifty, had, partly by circumspect conduct and apparent lack of ambition, and partly by a saintly life, removed the fears his suspected ability and powerful presence had roused in his superiors. His health had seriously failed during the last year, and it seemed probable he would soon be raised to vicar-general of the archbishopric. His competitors desired the appointment, that their own plans might have time to mature during the few remaining days that a now chronic malady might allow. Far from offering the same hopes to rivals, Birotteau’s triple chin showed to all who wanted his coveted canonry evidence of the soundest health; even his gout seemed to them, by the proverb, an assurance of longevity.

The Abbe Chapeloud, a man of great good sense, whose amiability made him sought in the diocese and among the best society in Tours, had steadily opposed, though secretly and with judgment, the elevation of Troubert. He had adroitly prevented Troubert’s access to the best salons. Nevertheless, during Chapeloud’s lifetime Troubert treated him with great respect and the utmost deference. This submission did not change Chapeloud’s opinion, who said to Birotteau during their last walk together: “Distrust that lean stick of a Troubert,—Sixtus the Fifth reduced to the limits of a bishopric!”

Such was the friend, the abiding guest of Mademoiselle Gamard, who now came, the morning after the old maid had, as it were, declared war against the poor vicar, to pay his brother a visit and show him marks of friendship.

“You must excuse Marianne,” said the canon as the woman entered. “I suppose she went first to my rooms. They are very damp, and I coughed all night. You are most healthily situated here,” he added, looking up at the cornice.

“Yes; I am lodged like a canon,” replied Birotteau.

“And I like a vicar,” said the other, humbly.

“But you will soon be settled in the archbishop’s palace,” said the kindly vicar, who wanted everybody to be happy.

“Yes, or in the cemetery, but God’s will be done!” Troubert raised his eyes to heaven resignedly. “I came,” he said, “to ask you to lend me the ‘Register of Bishops.’ You are the only man in Tours I know who has a copy.”

“Take it out of my library,” replied Birotteau, reminded by the canon’s words of the greatest happiness of his life.

The canon passed into the library and stayed while the vicar dressed. Presently the breakfast bell rang, and the gouty vicar reflected that if it had not been for Troubert’s visit he would have had no fire to dress by. “He’s a kind man,” thought he.

The two priests went downstairs together, each armed with a huge folio which they laid on one of the side tables in the dining-room.

“What’s all that?” asked Mademoiselle Gamard in a sharp voice, addressing Birotteau. “I hope you are not going to litter up my dining-room with your old books!”

“They are books I wanted,” replied the Abbe Troubert. “Monsieur Birotteau has been kind enough to lend them to me.”

“I might have guessed it,” she said with a contemptuous smile. “Monsieur Birotteau doesn’t often read books of that size.”

“How are you, mademoiselle?” said the vicar, in a mellifluous voice.

“Not very well,” she replied shortly. “You woke me up last night out of my first sleep, and I was wakeful for the rest of the night.” Then, sitting down, she added, “Gentlemen, the milk is getting cold.”

Stupefied at being so ill-naturedly received by his landlady, from whom he half expected an apology, and yet alarmed, like all timid people at the prospect of a discussion, especially if it relates to themselves, the poor vicar took his seat in silence. Observing Mademoiselle Gamard’s visible ill-humour, he was goaded into a struggle between reason, which told him not to submit to such discourtesy, and his natural character, which prompted him to avoid a quarrel.

Torn by this inward misery, Birotteau examined attentively the broad green lines painted on the oilcloth which, from custom immemorial, Mademoiselle Gamard left on the table at breakfast-time, without regard to the ragged edges or scars on its surface. The priests sat opposite each other in cane-seated arm-chairs on either side of the square table, the head of which was taken by the landlady, who seemed to dominate the whole from a high chair raised on casters, filled with cushions, standing very near the dining-room stove. This room and the salon were on the ground floor beneath Birotteau’s salon and bedroom.

When the vicar had received his cup of coffee, duly sugared, from Mademoiselle Gamard, he felt chilled to the bone by the grim silence in which he was forced to proceed with the usually gay function of breakfast. He dared not look at Troubert’s dried features nor at the old maid’s threatening visage; he therefore turned, to keep himself in countenance, to the plethoric pug lying on a cushion near the stove—a position that obese victim seldom quitted, having a little plate of dainties at his left and a bowl of fresh water at his right.

“Well, my pretty,” said the vicar, “are you waiting for your coffee?”

The personage thus addressed, one of the most important in the household though the least troublesome since he had ceased to bark and left talking to his mistress, turned his little eyes, sunk in rolls of fat, upon Birotteau. Then he closed them peevishly. To explain the poor vicar’s misery it should be said that, endowed by nature with an empty and sonorous loquacity like the resounding of a football, he asserted without medical reason that speech favored digestion. Mademoiselle Gamard, who believed in this hygienic doctrine, had not refrained, despite their coolness, from talking at meals; though for the last few mornings Birotteau had been forced to strain his mind to find beguiling topics to loosen her tongue. If the narrow limits of this history permitted us to report even one of the conversations which often brought a bitter, sarcastic smile to Troubert’s lips, it would offer a finished picture of provincial Boeotian life. The peculiar revelations of Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard on politics, religion, and literature would delight observing minds. It would be highly entertaining to transcribe the reasons on which they doubted Napoleon’s death in 1820, or conjectured the Dauphin living—rescued from the Temple in the hollow of a huge log. Who could help laughing to hear them assert and prove, by reasons evidently their own, that the King of France alone imposed the taxes, that the Chambers were convoked to destroy the clergy, that thirteen hundred thousand persons perished on the scaffold during the Revolution? They frequently discussed the press without either knowing what that modern engine really was. Monsieur Birotteau listened with acceptance to Mademoiselle Gamard when she told him that a man who ate an egg every morning would die in a year and that facts proved it; that a roll of light bread eaten without drinking for several days would cure sciatica; that all the workmen who assisted in pulling down the Abbey Saint-Martin had died in six months; that a certain prefect, under orders from Bonaparte, had tried to damage the towers of Saint-Gatien—with a hundred other absurd tales.

But on this occasion poor Birotteau felt tongue-tied, and he resigned himself to eat without engaging in conversation. After a while, however, the thought crossed him that silence was dangerous for his digestion, and he boldly remarked, “This coffee is excellent.”

That act of courage was wasted. Then, after looking at the scrap of sky visible above the garden between the two buttresses of Saint-Gatien, the vicar again summoned nerve to say, “It will be finer today than yesterday.”

At that remark Mademoiselle Gamard cast her most gracious look on Troubert and immediately turned her eyes with terrible severity on Birotteau, who fortunately by that time was looking at his plate.

No creature of the feminine gender was ever more capable of presenting to the mind the elegiac nature of an old maid than Mademoiselle Sophie Gamard. To describe a being whose character gives momentous interest to the petty events of this drama and to the anterior lives of the actors, it may be useful to summarize the ideas expressed in the being of an Old Maid—remembering always that habits form the soul, and the soul forms the physical presence.

Though all things in society as well as the universe are said to have a purpose, there do exist here below certain beings whose purpose and utility seem inexplicable. Moral philosophy and political economy condemn the individual who consumes without producing; who fills a place on earth but does not shed upon it either good or evil—for evil is sometimes good the meaning of which is not at once made manifest. It is seldom that old maids of their own motion enter the ranks of these unproductive beings. Now, if the consciousness of work done gives the workers a sense of satisfaction that helps them support life, the certainty of being a useless burden must produce a contrary effect and fill such fruitless beings with the same contempt for themselves which they inspire in others. This harsh social reprobation is one cause that helps fill old maids’ souls with the distress seen in their faces. Prejudice, in which there is truth, casts, throughout the world but especially in France, a great stigma on the woman with whom no man has been willing to share life’s blessings or ills. Now, all unmarried women reach a period when the world, right or wrong, condemns them for this rejection. If they are ugly, the goodness of character ought to have compensated their imperfections; if handsome, that argues some serious cause. It is impossible to say which class is more deserving of rejection. If, on the other hand, their celibacy is deliberate, proceeding from a desire for independence, neither men nor mothers will forgive their disloyalty to womanly devotion, evidenced in their refusal to feed those passions which render their sex affecting. To renounce the pangs of womanhood is to abjure its poetry and cease to merit the consolations to which mothers have inalienable rights.

Moreover, generous sentiments and exquisite qualities of a woman will not develop unless by constant exercise. By remaining unmarried, a woman becomes void of meaning; selfish and cold, she creates repulsion. This implacable judgment of the world is too just to leave old maids ignorant of its causes. Such ideas shoot up in their hearts as naturally as the effects of their saddened lives appear on their features. Consequently they wither, because the constant expression of happiness that blooms on other women’s faces and gives grace to their movements has never existed for them. They grow sharp and peevish because all human beings who miss their vocation are unhappy; they suffer, and suffering gives birth to bitterness. In fact, before an old maid blames herself for her isolation she blames others, and there is but one step between reproach and the desire for revenge.

But more than this, the ill grace and want of charm noticeable in these women result from their lives. Never having felt a desire to please, elegance and the refinements of taste are foreign to them. They see only themselves. This instinct brings them, unconsciously, to choose what is most convenient for themselves at the sacrifice of what might please others. Without accounting to their own minds for the difference between themselves and other women, they end by feeling that difference and suffering under it. Jealousy is indelible in the female breast. An old maid’s soul is jealous and yet void; for she knows but one side—the miserable side—of the only passion men will allow to women. Thus thwarted in all hopes, forced to deny the natural development of their natures, old maids endure an inward torment to which they never grow accustomed. It is hard at any age, especially for a woman, to see repulsion on others’ faces, when her true destiny is to move hearts to grace and love. One result of this inward trouble is that an old maid’s glance is always oblique, less from modesty than from fear and shame. Such beings never forgive society for their false position because they never forgive themselves for it.

Now it is impossible for a woman perpetually at war with herself and living in contradiction to her true life to leave others in peace or refrain from envying their happiness. The whole range of these sad truths could be read in the dulled gray eyes of Mademoiselle Gamard; the dark circles around them told of inward conflicts. All the wrinkles on her face were in straight lines. The structure of her forehead and cheeks was rigid and prominent. She allowed, with apparent indifference, certain scattered hairs, once brown, to grow upon her chin. Her thin lips scarcely covered teeth that were too long, though still quite white. Her complexion was dark, and her hair, originally black, had turned gray from frightful headaches—a misfortune that obliged her to wear a false front. Not knowing how to put it on to conceal the junction, there were often little gaps between the border of her cap and the black string fastening the semi-wig. Her gown, silk in summer, merino in winter, always brown, was invariably rather tight for her angular figure and thin arms. Her collar, limp and bent, exposed too much the red skin of a neck ribbed like an oak-leaf in winter seen in the light. Her origin explains to some extent these defects. She was the daughter of a wood-merchant, a peasant who had risen from the ranks. She might have been plump at eighteen, but no trace remained of the fair complexion and pretty color she boasted. The tones of her flesh had taken pallid tints often seen in “devotes.” Her aquiline nose chiefly proclaimed the despotism of her nature, and the flat forehead the narrowness of her mind. Her movements had an odd abruptness that precluded grace; the mere motion with which she twitched her handkerchief from her bag and blew her nose with a loud noise would have shown her character and habits. Being rather tall she held herself erect, and justified the remark of a naturalist who once explained old maids’ peculiar gait by declaring their joints were consolidating. When she walked her movements were not equally distributed over her person, as in other women, producing the graceful undulations so attractive. She moved, so to speak, in a single block, seeming to advance at each step like the statue of the Commendatore. When in good humour she was apt to tell of chances she had had to marry, and of her fortunate discovery in time of her lovers’ want of means—proving, unconsciously, that her worldly judgment was better than her heart.

This typical figure of the genus Old Maid was well framed by the grotesque designs representing Turkish landscapes on varnished paper that decorated the dining-room. Mademoiselle Gamard usually sat in this room, which boasted two pier tables and a barometer. Before each abbé’s chair was a little cushion covered with faded worsted work. The salon in which she received company was worthy of its mistress. It went by the name “the yellow salon.” The curtains were yellow, the furniture and walls yellow; on the mantelpiece, surmounted by a mirror in a gilt frame, the candlesticks and a clock all of crystal struck the eye with sharp brilliancy. As to Mademoiselle Gamard’s private apartment, no one had ever been permitted to look into it. Conjecture alone suggested it was full of odds and ends, worn-out furniture, and bits and pieces dear to the hearts of all old maids.

Such was the woman destined to exert a vast influence on the last years of Abbe Birotteau.

For want of exercising in nature’s way the activity bestowed upon women, and yet impelled to spend it, Mademoiselle Gamard had acquired the habit of using it in petty intrigues, provincial cabals, and self-seeking schemes which occupy sooner or later the lives of all old maids. Birotteau, unhappily, had developed in Sophie Gamard the only sentiments it was possible for that poor creature to feel—hatred; a passion hitherto latent under provincial calm but now to become more intense because spent on petty things in a narrow sphere. Birotteau was one of those beings predestined to suffer because, being unable to see things, they cannot avoid them; to them the worst happens.

“Yes, it will be a fine day,” replied the canon after a pause, apparently issuing from reverie and wishing to conform to politeness.

Birotteau, frightened at the length of time that had elapsed between question and answer—for he had, for the first time, taken his coffee without speaking—left the dining-room where his heart was squeezed as if in a vise. Feeling the coffee heavy on his stomach, he walked in a sad mood among the narrow, box-edged garden paths that outlined a star. As he turned after the first round, he saw Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert standing stock-still and silent on the threshold—he with arms folded and motionless like a statue on a tomb; she leaning against the blind door. Both seemed to be gazing at him and counting his steps. Nothing is so embarrassing to a timid creature as to feel itself the object of close examination, and if that is made by eyes of hatred, the suffering becomes intolerable martyrdom.

Presently Birotteau fancied he was preventing them from walking in the narrow path. That idea, inspired equally by fear and kindness, became so strong that he left the garden and went to the church, thinking no longer of his canonry, so absorbed was he by the disheartening tyranny of the old maid. Luckily he found much to do at Saint-Gatien—several funerals, a marriage, and two baptisms. Thus employed he forgot his griefs. When his stomach told him dinner was ready he drew out his watch and saw, with alarm, that it was some minutes after four. Being aware of Mademoiselle Gamard’s punctuality, he hurried back.

He saw at once on passing the kitchen door that the first course had been removed. When he reached the dining-room the old maid said, with a tone mixing sour rebuke and joy at being able to blame him:—

“It is half-past four, Monsieur Birotteau. You know we are not to wait for you.”

The vicar looked at the clock and saw at once, by the way the gauze protecting it had been moved, that his landlady had opened the face and set the hands ahead of the cathedral clock. He could make no remark. Had he expressed his suspicion it would only have caused one of those fierce and eloquent expositions to which Mademoiselle Gamard, like other women of her class, knew how to give vent in particular cases. The thousand and one annoyances a servant will sometimes make her master bear, or a woman her husband, were instinctively divined by Mademoiselle Gamard and used upon Birotteau. The way she delighted in plotting against the poor vicar’s domestic comfort bore marks of a profoundly malignant genius. Yet she so managed that she was never, as far as eye could see, in the wrong.

Eight days after the events that opened this history, changes in the household and the relations between the Abbe Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard made the former perceive a plot that had been growing for six months.

As long as the old maid’s vengeance was covert and the vicar could shut his eyes to it, the harm done him was slight. But after the candlestick and the altered clock, Birotteau could no longer doubt that hatred watched him. From that moment he fell into despair, seeing Mademoiselle Gamard’s skinny, clawlike fingers everywhere. She, delighting in a sentiment as pungent as revenge, hovered above him like a bird of prey preparing to pounce. She had long devised a plan which the simple priest could not imagine, and now unfolded it with the petty genius often shown by solitary persons who, barred from large feelings, expend themselves on the details of outward devotion.

These trifling torments prevented Birotteau, effusive and seeking pity, from confiding in friends—another cruel aggravation. His timidity made him dread seeming ridiculous by speaking of such pettiness. Yet these petty things composed his life—busy about nothing, finding happiness in the absence of strong feeling. His paradise altered to hell; his terror of confronting Mademoiselle Gamard increased, and the secret distress blighted his health. One morning, while putting on his mottled blue stockings, he noticed his calves had shrunk. Terrified by such a symptom, he resolved to ask the Abbe Troubert to intervene officially between him and the landlady.

In Troubert’s bare, cheerless room the vicar, ashamed to bother a man so gravely occupied, after many internal agonies, explained his case. The canon listened coldly, suppressing an occasional smile. He blamed Birotteau’s failure to notice the trouble on his preoccupation with elevated thoughts, remarked that in former days recluses thought little of food and lodging, and suggested that modern priests can retire into the solitude of their hearts. He told Birotteau that he might act as arbitrator because his friendship with Mademoiselle Gamard had never exceeded the limits imposed by the Church, but justice required hearing both sides. Troubert professed to see no change in Gamard, attributing slight temper fluctuations to pulmonary suffering and recommending patience; if Birotteau stayed with her longer he would come to understand and esteem her better.

Birotteau left confounded. Unable to consult anyone, he judged Mademoiselle Gamard alone and fancied that by leaving her house for a few days he might extinguish her dislike. He resolved to spend a week at the country house of Madame de Listomere, a practice he had often followed. Ironically, this was exactly what Gamard wished; only patient endurance could have foiled her plan. The vicar, incapable of divining this, was doomed to fall.

Madame de Listomere’s country-place, the Alouette, near Tours, combined town and country charms. About ten days after his arrival there, while breakfasting, Birotteau was told Monsieur Caron wished to speak with him. Caron was Mademoiselle Gamard’s lawyer. Birotteau, agitated and surprised, learned Caron had been sent to ask how long he intended to remain in the country. The original agreement had not foreseen a long absence and might lead to contest. Gamard, anxious to avoid dispute, had sent Caron to understand his plans. The abbe asked for time to take advice.

Back at table, Birotteau described his trouble. The company, bored with country-house monotony, eagerly took sides against the old maid. Madame de Listomere surmised Troubert wanted Birotteau’s apartment; Monsieur de Listomere urged the vicar to resist Gamard. An old landowner, Monsieur de Bourbonne, suggested something more serious lay behind the moves: that Gamard intended to turn Birotteau out, and those like her risked nothing in such games. De Bourbonne, thin, shrewd, a sort of provincial Fabius, was practised in weighing words and measuring actions; his reputation for cunning and decency made him a man to be feared in local affairs.

Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived from Tours with news that changed everything. She reported that the vicar-general was very ill and the archbishop had delegated his powers to Abbe Troubert provisionally, so the canonry would depend on him. At a recent gathering the Abbe Poirel had spoken of Birotteau’s alleged annoyances to Mademoiselle Gamard in a way that cast blame on the vicar. Madame de Listomere asked Birotteau whether he preferred to be made canon or to continue living with Gamard; in chorus they said canon. She suggested that by leaving Gamard’s house he might secure the canonry—“one good turn deserves another.” All applauded, except her nephew, the Baron, who joked about wishing to see a fight.

Despite their support, forces were unequal: the city’s best society had less power than Gamard backed by Troubert. By Madame de Listomere’s advice, a servant fetched Caron back. De Bourbonne counselled caution and delay, but passion prevailed. In a short conference Caron urged Birotteau to sign a paper declaring his relinquishment of domicile. Confused but overwhelmed, Birotteau read and mechanically signed it: he declared he left Gamard’s house of his own wish, that he had been properly fed and lodged. Caron asked where to send the abbe’s belongings; Birotteau said Madame de Listomere. De Bourbonne, suspicious, requested to see the paper and noted that there must be prior documents between Birotteau and Gamard. The fatal deed, the vicar said, was in his library.

The helplessness of the poor man, innocent of worldly craft, touched Madame de Listomere and Mademoiselle Salomon, who soothed him like mothers consoling a child. They promised him lodging and counsel, urged play to distract him, and spoke of asking Troubert for help. Birotteau, dazzled by the prospect of living with Madame de Listomere, temporarily forgot his ruined happiness. But that night the reality returned: the thought of moving, of his books and furniture scattered, of the end of long habits, overwhelmed him. He could not reconcile one sweet first year in Gamard’s house with the cruel second; the canonry seemed poor compensation. He clung to Mademoiselle Salomon’s friendship, but dared not fully trust new attachments.

The historian digresses to note that among spinsters there are heroic kinds—those who sacrifice themselves nobly. Mademoiselle Salomon belonged to this class: once beloved and young, she had tended a lover who went mad, refusing to call him insane; she had devoted herself for five years to his mechanical well-being, and after his death settled in Tours to do good. Simple, frank, and of regular features, she attached herself to the feeble, which made her warmly interested in Birotteau.

Mademoiselle de Villenoix returned Birotteau to Tours the next morning and left him at the quay of the cathedral to go to the Cloister, bent on preserving the canonry and overseeing removal of his furniture. At the house he found his old apartment occupied: Marianne told him Troubert was in his old rooms. The sight of Troubert, comfortably seated in Chapeloud’s library and armchair, confirmed the depth of the conspiracy. Birotteau grasped, with a sinking vision, that Troubert intended to possess Chapeloud’s and his spoils. Facing Troubert’s sardonic glance, the abbe felt as if struck dumb.

Troubert coolly told him that Mademoiselle Gamard had told him of Birotteau’s departure and that he had been installed out of necessity; if the furniture belonged to Birotteau, Gamard’s scrupulous honesty guaranteed its return; if Birotteau wished to return, Troubert would cede the apartment. The words were terrible. Birotteau ran to find Gamard and met her on the staircase. Bowing calmly in spite of her derisive smile, he protested that she should have waited to let him pack his books and furniture. She interrupted, asking if he had not read his deed, and then, with the triumphant clarity of hatred, reminded him of the clause: by agreement, if he left her house his furniture would indemnify her for the difference in price of board between what he paid and what the late Abbe Chapeloud had paid. With Poirel’s appointment as canon just made, Gamard exulted in the arrangement. At these words Birotteau faintly bowed and fled, fearing a complete breakdown should he stay.

He found at Madame de Listomere’s house his linen, clothing, and papers packed in a trunk, and sank, hiding his face in his hands. Poirel had been made canon; Birotteau had no home, means, or furniture. Mademoiselle Salomon drove by, the porter noticed the priest’s despair, and persuaded her to take him in her carriage. She, ignorant of the deed’s terms, believed his derangement due to the shock of Poirel’s nomination. Back at the Alouette, friends cared for him until evening, when he recounted the morning’s events.

De Bourbonne asked to see the deed. Birotteau produced it. De Bourbonne read the clause providing that, because of an eight-hundred-franc yearly difference between Chapeloud’s board and what Gamard charged, and because Birotteau was unable to pay full price, he engaged in consideration of sums lent by Gamard to leave her, as indemnity, all household property he might possess at death, or to transfer the same should he voluntarily give up the apartment. De Bourbonne cursed the agreement and declared Gamard armed with claws.

Birotteau had never imagined anything would separate him from the house where he hoped to live and die. He had not remembered the clause, which he had not discussed; in his eagerness to enter the house he would have signed any document. Madame de Listomere, in hot indignation, exclaimed she had made him sign and must restore his lost happiness. De Bourbonne advised law: the deed might constitute fraud. The Baron and others urged litigation. De Bourbonne privately advised a different path: that most of those now friendly would desert him soon, and if Birotteau wished peace he should resign the vicariat and leave Tours to a distant parish where Troubert could not pursue him. This prospect horrified the vicar—leaving Tours for him was like death; celibates replace feelings with habits, and Birotteau needed his routine: his walks, salons, and games.

Word quickly spread that Madame la Baronne de Listomere had invited Birotteau to stay. The baroness’s reception of him implicitly denied Gamard’s assertions and censured her conduct, so Gamard was deeply wounded. To understand how Gamard had resources, it’s necessary to see the network that served Troubert. Accompanied by Troubert, Gamard made evening rounds to several houses where circles of old maids, a few men swayed by servants’ gossip, and other busybodies met. These women, ever sifting words and scrutinizing neighbors, kept exact accounts of fortunes and actions, retailing scandal and controlling local opinion. Spread through town like capillary fibres, they absorbed household secrets and transmitted them mechanically to Troubert. They formed a kind of police—dumb but perpetually talking—whose gossip could have terrible effects when it touched important interests. The quarrel between Birotteau and Gamard became the absorbing passion of these salons; a class sentiment underlay the conflict—an old Roman people-vs-senate struggle reduced to a provincial molehill.

Yet these small passions engaged their participants with an intensity equal to the great affairs of life. Troubert’s hours, like those of a gambler or statesman, were heavy with anxious thought. The narrator asks, in passing, to consider how certain passions prompt virtues that widen a life beyond its selfish center.

Madame de Listomere returned to Tours and took Birotteau to her lawyer. He did not think the case easy. Friends who had believed justice certain had delayed bringing the suit until returning to town, and Gamard’s allies had taken the initiative, spreading the affair to Birotteau’s damage. The lawyer—whose practice lay among the devout—cautioned against litigation. According to the deed, Gamard had the law on her side; in equity, judges and respectable laymen might view Birotteau as having derogated from his formerly peaceable and mild character. Gamard had lent him money to pay Chapeloud’s legacy duties without a receipt; Birotteau, not of age or character to sign without understanding, left Gamard’s house after two years while Chapeloud and Troubert had been there twelve and fifteen years respectively; the suit would appear like ingratitude. The lawyer declined to undertake it and besought Madame de Listomere to avoid embroiling herself.

That evening Birotteau could not refrain from telling friends the lawyer’s refusal. De Bourbonne advised caution and mentioned only a radical lawyer might take the case. The Baron, heatedly, insisted: Troubert’s involvement made it a public matter; valuable pictures and furniture had been involved, and Birotteau could not have intended to give away such wealth for two years of board. He proposed getting Monsieur Salmon, an expert from the Museum, to appraise the paintings and furniture.

Two days later the suit began. Choosing the Radical lawyer harmed Birotteau’s cause: opponents of government and critics of religion seized on the case and the town buzzed. The Museum expert appraised the Virgin of Valentin and the Christ of Lebrun at eleven thousand francs; the bookshelves and Gothic furniture, increasingly fashionable in Paris, had immediate value of at least twelve thousand. The full appraisal exceeded thirty-six thousand francs. Clearly, Birotteau could not have intended to give Gamard such a sum under the deed; legally, he had equitable grounds to amend the agreement; if refused, Gamard’s conduct might constitute intentional fraud. The Radical lawyer served a harsh writ on Gamard, a document of skillful legal argument supported by precedent and code citations; it so clearly condemned the old maid that many copies were maliciously distributed through the town.

IV

A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, the Baron de Listomere, who expected promotion to captain of a corvette, received a letter warning that he might be put on the retired list. Astonished, he went at once to Paris and to the minister, who laughed at his fears; but the next day, despite the minister’s assurances, the baron made inquiries. By an indiscretion a secretary showed him a document confirming the news, awaiting only the director’s signature.

The baron hurried to an uncle, a deputy, who could see the minister at the Chamber, and begged him to find the minister’s intentions in a matter that threatened his future. Waiting anxiously in his uncle’s carriage, he was told on their way home: the minister had begun by accusing him of leading Radicals in Tours and having objectionable politics; but the real trouble, his uncle learned, was ecclesiastical. The baron had spoken slightingly of Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general, an influential man representing the Jesuits in the province. His uncle warned him to make peace with Troubert at once, saying such priests were men with whom one must live in harmony; Troubert was likely to be raised to a bishopric and could even harm the uncle’s own prospects.

These words explained to the baron the secret activities of Troubert that Birotteau had often incomprehensibly noted: “I can’t think what he does with himself—sitting up all night.” Troubert’s position and skill, and his female network in Tours, had made him the Congregation’s proconsul there; archbishop, general, prefect—all were under his covert influence. The Baron de Listomere decided at once on his course. “I shall take care not to get another round shot below my water-line,” he said to his uncle.

Three days later the baron, returning hurriedly, told his aunt that the family must abandon support of “that fool of a Birotteau.” He detained Monsieur de Bourbonne as the old man was leaving a rubber of whist; Bourbonne’s clear-sightedness seemed essential to understand the reefs the Listomeres faced. The baron’s return and disquiet hinted he had met a check in his campaign against Gamard and Troubert. He revealed Troubert’s hidden power.

“I knew that,” said Bourbonne.

“Then why did you not warn us?” the baroness cried.

“Forget that I knew it, and I will forget that you did,” Bourbonne said sharply. “If we do not keep this secret now we shall be thought his accomplices. Pretend to be duped; but watch your steps. I warned you, but you would not understand me.”

Abandoning Birotteau was taken for granted by the three. “To beat a retreat with the honors of war has always been the triumph of the ablest generals,” said Bourbonne. “Bow to Troubert; if his hatred is less than his vanity you may win him; if you bow too low he will walk over you. Make believe you intend to leave the service, and you’ll escape him. Send away Birotteau, madame, and you will set things right with Mademoiselle Gamard. Ask Troubert, when you meet him at the archbishop’s, if he plays whist; he will say yes. Invite him to your salon; you are a woman and can win a priest. When the baron is promoted and his uncle made a peer, and Troubert a bishop, you can make Birotteau a canon if you choose. Meantime yield—but yield gracefully, with a slight menace. Your family can give him as much as he can give you. As for you, sailor, keep your deep-sea line about you.”

“Poor Birotteau?” said the baroness.

“Get rid of him at once,” Bourbonne replied. “If some clever Radical gets that empty head, he may cause trouble. The court might give a verdict in his favor, and Troubert must fear that. I have said my say.”

The next morning the baroness took Birotteau aside and, not without embarrassment, asked him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and to leave her house. As she spoke he turned pale. She explained, briefly and without disguising the gravity, the wide ramifications of the affair, making him see the net woven by revenge and the capacity and power of Troubert, whose hatred of Chapeloud now vented itself in seizing Chapeloud’s property and persecuting his friend. Birotteau clasped his hands and wept, listening as one at the edge of a precipice. The baroness ended: family duties must be considered before friendship; yield, and she would ensure he lacked nothing, arranging matters through Monsieur de Bourbonne to save appearances. “Grant me the right to abandon you,” she begged.

The bewildered abbe cried, “Chapeloud was right when he said that if Troubert could drag him by the feet out of his grave he would do it!” Yet he yielded. “I trust myself to you—I am but the stubble of the streets,” he said, using a local word for a pathetic bit of straw. He asked only for Chapeloud’s portrait, painted for him, and the baroness promised to see Mademoiselle Gamard herself and to consult the archbishop.

Birotteau left dismayed; Troubert now seemed to him a pyramid whose hands were in Paris and elbows in the cloister. The news of Birotteau’s removal from the Listomere house seemed mysterious: the baroness told people the nephew meant to marry and leave the navy, and wanted the vicar’s apartment. The Listomeres’ apparent neutrality implied recognition of the Congregation’s occult power. But the lawsuit still awaited judgment; their opponents yielded while threatening.

Then Mademoiselle Gamard took cold leaving the cathedral and was soon dangerously ill. The town burst into commiseration: “Mademoiselle Gamard’s sensitive nature could not bear the scandal; in spite of the justice of her cause she was likely to die of grief—Birotteau has killed his benefactress.” Such talk spread through the female conclave and the town.

Madame de Listomere went to pay her promised visit but the old maid was too ill to see her. She asked to speak to the vicar-general. Troubert, gratified to receive in Chapeloud’s library the woman who had hitherto ignored him, kept her waiting before admitting her. No courtier could have put more dissimulation into negotiating personal interests than the baroness and the priest did now.

Bourbonne had coached the baroness: “Don’t forget your cue. You are a mediator, not an interested party. Troubert is a mediator. Weigh your words; if he strokes his chin you have him.” Their conversation was a duel of implied meanings. Madame de Listomere began by expressing regret at Birotteau’s lawsuit and her desire to settle it.

“The harm is done, madame,” Troubert said gravely. “The pious Mademoiselle Gamard is dying.” (He intended to place the guilt on the baroness.)

“On hearing of her illness I entreated Monsieur Birotteau to relinquish his claims; I have brought the document intending to give it to that excellent woman,” she replied. (She meant to avoid compromising herself.) After a tense interchange of civilities and veiled thrusts about religion, politics, and the paintings, she laid the deed of relinquishment on the table and said she had come to reconcile two Christians.

“Your nephew has been to Paris, I believe,” Troubert said. “He returns to-night; the minister does not want him to leave the service.” “He has done us the service of getting a proper estimate on the value of those paintings,” Troubert added, eyeing the pictures. Madame de Listomere offered frames for Saint-Gatien if they were to be given to the chapel. He replied they did not belong to him. She handed him the relinquishment, saying it ended all discussion and that she had some compassion for Birotteau; he had asked, as the price of renunciation, for Chapeloud’s portrait. Troubert looked fixedly at her and went down to ask Mademoiselle Gamard.

He soon returned. “Madame,” he said, “I bring you the words of a dying woman. ‘The Abbe Chapeloud was so true a friend...that I cannot consent to part with his picture.’ As for me, if it were mine I would not yield it. My feelings to my late friend were so faithful that I should feel my right above others’.”

“That’s no need to quarrel over a bad picture,” the baroness said, offering to have a copy made and courteously inviting him to play whist at her house. Troubert, stroked his chin—caught by vanity—and promised he would oblige her for her sake.

Madame de Listomere went home hoping the archbishop would complete the peace. But Mademoiselle Gamard died the next day. Her will left everything to Abbe Troubert; the fortune was appraised at three hundred thousand francs. He sent the Listomeres invitations to the services and funeral; the baroness insisted they go. “We must go,” she said. Bourbonne added that the baron must go to the cemetery; the lieutenant, unluckily, had not left Tours.

The services were performed with unusual ecclesiastical magnificence. Only Birotteau wept, kneeling in a side chapel; he believed himself guilty and sincerely prayed for the deceased, lamenting that he had not obtained her forgiveness. Troubert followed the body to the grave and delivered a discourse that, by eloquence, enlarged the old maid’s life to monumental proportions. In the peroration he spoke of her life devoted to God and of Providence withdrawing her from trials; his tone suggested that she rested innocent. After the sermon, in a gesture that betrayed more joy than grief, he sprinkled the coffin with holy water.

The next morning Mademoiselle Salomon came to tell Madame de Listomere that “our poor Abbe Birotteau has just received a frightful blow: he is appointed curate of Saint-Symphorien.” Saint-Symphorien lay beyond the bridge, a suburb three miles away—an exile in plain sight of the town. The parsonage was cold and damp, the parish too poor, and the removal seemed an infamous plot.

To end the history: five months later Troubert was made Bishop of Troyes; Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity of fifteen hundred francs to Abbe Birotteau. The day her will’s dispositions were announced, Monseigneur Hyacinthe, Bishop of Troyes, about to leave Tours for his diocese, delayed when he received the news. Furious at being foiled by the baroness—whom he had lately received while she had secretly aided a man he counted as an enemy—Troubert again threatened the baron’s career and jeopardized his uncle’s peerage. He made one of those soft, vengeful priestly speeches in the archbishop’s salon. The Baron de Listomere went to him the next day and must have submitted to hard conditions, for his later conduct showed entire submission to the Jesuit’s will.

The new bishop deeded Mademoiselle Gamard’s house to the cathedral chapter, gave Chapeloud’s books and bookcases to the seminary, and presented the two disputed pictures to the Chapel of the Virgin—but he kept Chapeloud’s portrait. No one could explain this near-total renunciation of Gamard’s bequest; Bourbonne supposed the bishop had secretly kept invested monies to support his rank in Paris. Only the night before Troubert’s departure did Bourbonne unearth the hidden reason: the baron had contested Madame de Listomere’s legacy to Birotteau on grounds of undue influence. The deathblow to the feeblest victim was complete.

A few days after the case was brought the baron was promoted to captain. As ecclesiastical discipline, the curate of Saint-Symphorien—Birotteau—was suspended; his superiors judged him guilty. The murderer of Sophie Gamard was also a swindler; had Troubert kept the property openly, it would have been harder to censure Birotteau.

When Monseigneur Hyacinthe drove along the quay Saint-Symphorien in a post-chaise on his way to Paris, poor Birotteau was placed in an armchair in the sun on a terrace above the road. Pale and haggard, smitten by the archbishop, he was the skeleton of the man who less than a year earlier had rolled contentedly along the cloister. The bishop cast one look of pity and contempt, then went his way.

There is no doubt Troubert might once have been a Hildebrand or an Alexander VI. In our era the Church is no longer the political power it once was; celibacy concentrates a man’s faculties on a single passion—egotism—which makes celibates either useless or mischievous. We live when governments tend to make Man for Society rather than Society for Man. There is a perpetual struggle between the Individual and a social system that uses him while he tries to use it. In earlier times man was first father, then lived for clan or city; later, for caste or religion, and his range of interests widened. Today his life attaches to a vast country; perhaps someday his family may be the whole universe.

Will such moral cosmopolitanism—Christian Rome’s hope of Brotherhood—prove only a sublime error? It is natural to believe in such a noble vision, but human nature lacks such divine proportions. Souls vast enough to hold a world of feeling belong to great exceptions. Physiologists who say as the brain enlarges the heart narrows are mistaken. The apparent egotism of those who bear a science, a nation, or a code is a highest passion; it is the maternity of the masses. To create peoples and ideas they must unite in their mighty brains the tenderness of woman and the force of God. The histories of Innocent III, Peter the Great, and other great leaders show the same vast thought of which Troubert was a provincial representative.
