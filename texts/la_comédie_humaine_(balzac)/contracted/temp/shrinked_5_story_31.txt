De Balzac dedicates "The Vicar of Tours" to sculptor David, contrasting the enduring nature of David's art with the fleeting permanence of his own writing.

In autumn 1826, Abbé Birotteau, a corpulent priest nearing sixty, is caught in the rain returning home, hopeful of a canon nomination at the cathedral of Saint-Gatien. He lives with Mademoiselle Gamard, an elderly landlady who boards priests, and envies the apartment of his deceased friend Abbé Chapeloud, from whom he inherited a library and furniture.

At first he enjoys Gamard's care, but soon senses neglect and suspects her ill will. Good-natured and naive, he cannot fathom her hostility and reflects that he may have taken her attentions for granted. Gamard, feeling slighted by his social engagements and ambitions, withdraws her kindness and creates tension between them.

Birotteau's longing for the canonry blinds him to the growing conflict; Gamard's resentment is rooted in his ambitions. The narrative examines envy, ambition, and the fragility of happiness, showing how misunderstandings can escalate into ruin.

The next morning, preoccupied with the canonry, Birotteau forgets the previous night's signs. When his servant Marianne fails to arrive, Abbé Troubert appears, outwardly concerned for Birotteau's health but harboring his own hopes for preferment—ambitions Birotteau fails to perceive. Tensions rise when Gamard scolds him for bringing books to breakfast, souring the atmosphere; oblivious to her malice, he keeps trying to please her. Returning late one evening, he finds Gamard has manipulated the clock so he misses dinner, a deliberate act of vindictiveness.

Eight days later he uncovers Gamard's plot, realizing he is under scrutiny. His peaceful life descends into despair as he interprets her actions as personal attacks. Isolated and ashamed, he seeks help from Troubert, who dismisses his concerns and deepens his helplessness. Planning a stay at Madame de Listomère's country house, Birotteau is warned by Gamard's lawyer that absence could invite legal trouble.

Despite friends urging resistance, his naivety leads him to sign a document relinquishing rights to his belongings, sealing his fate. Confronting Gamard, he finds Troubert has taken over his apartment and Gamard claims his furniture. Devastated, he takes refuge at Madame de Listomère's, where friends promise to help restore his dignity. As his health and spirits fail, Troubert's influence grows, exposing the corrosive effects of ecclesiastical politics in provincial France.
