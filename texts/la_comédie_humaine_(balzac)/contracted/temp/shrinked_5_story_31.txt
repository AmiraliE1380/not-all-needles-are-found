**DEDICATION**

To David, Sculptor:

The permanence of the work on which I inscribe your name—twice made illustrious in this century—is very problematical; whereas you have graven mine in bronze which survives nations—if only in their coins. The day may come when numismatists, discovering amid the ashes of Paris existences perpetuated by you, will wonder at the number of heads crowned in your atelier and endeavor to find in them new dynasties.

To you, this divine privilege; to me, gratitude.

De Balzac.

**THE VICAR OF TOURS**

I

In the autumn of 1826, Abbe Birotteau, the story’s protagonist, was caught in a rainstorm returning from a friend's house. He hurried across the deserted square called “The Cloister,” behind the cathedral of Saint-Gatien in Tours.

Short, corpulent, about sixty and subject to gout, Birotteau hated wet shoes. Yet he was in good spirits, having won money at cards with Madame de Listomere and buoyed by hope of finally being appointed a canon of the cathedral. This long-held ambition occupied his thoughts, making him oblivious to the rain and discomfort.

The Cloister had once housed Chapter dignitaries but, after ecclesiastical property confiscations, became the narrow Rue de la Psalette. On its left stood a single house shadowed by the cathedral, long occupied by priests and owned by an old maid, Mademoiselle Gamard. Devout and discreet, she retained the property through the Restoration by taking in priests as boarders.

Birotteau had lived there two years. To be Mademoiselle Gamard’s boarder and to secure a canonry were his two chief ambitions, representing the comforts and status he craved. His desire for the apartment had become a preoccupation, pursued with a mix of hope and impatience.

Gamard could take only two lodgers. For twelve years she had housed the Abbe Troubert and the late Abbe Chapeloud. When Chapeloud died, Birotteau took his place, having long admired the apartment’s furnishings during visits. Chapeloud had been a close friend; his death left Birotteau bereaved but also relieved to inherit the library and furniture he had coveted.

Chapeloud had gradually enriched the rooms with books and tasteful furniture, inciting Birotteau’s envy and longing. The vicar’s wish for that comfort grew until it bordered on obsession; he sometimes found himself wishing for Chapeloud’s passing to obtain the apartment, though he never schemed to influence his friend’s will. Chapeloud, aware of Birotteau’s desire, forgave such unspoken feelings and, just before dying, told him, “This time you will certainly get the apartment. I feel it is all over with me now.” After the funeral, Birotteau set to arranging his inherited possessions and felt contented despite being passed over for the canonry.

Mademoiselle Gamard welcomed him as boarder; he enjoyed the attentions and comforts he had long imagined. Yet his gratitude soon shifted to renewed longing for the canonry, which became an obsession that preoccupied him so much he left his umbrella in the rain. Arriving home, he found the door locked and had to ring several times. His servant Marianne said Gamard assumed he was already in. He noticed an absence of the usual attentions—no slippers, no fire—and felt slighted.

These small neglects pricked his vanity and made him suspect intentional disrespect. He reflected on possible causes and convinced himself he must have displeased her somehow. His innocence and lack of worldly experience left him vulnerable; he failed to perceive that Gamard’s coolness was growing into a subtle persecution. Where Chapeloud had managed the relationship shrewdly, Birotteau’s simplicity left him blind to mounting resentment.

Mademoiselle Gamard’s discontent had been building over the eighteen months of his tenancy. She prized social recognition and had hoped to create a salon, ambitions frustrated by Birotteau’s indifference to her expectations. As he resumed former social habits and spent evenings with friends who provided the engagement he craved, he further distanced himself from Gamard. She felt her plans slipping away and began to nurse a quiet vengeance.

The neglect he experienced—the locked door, the omission of small domestic attentions—manifested her growing animosity. Birotteau, naive and self-absorbed in his desire for preferment, remained oblivious to the calculations behind these omissions. He confronted only the symptoms, not their cause, and hoped for clarity in the morning, unaware the reasons for Gamard’s hostility would remain hidden to him.

The contrast between the two priests was clear: Chapeloud had navigated Gamard’s vanity with worldly tact; Birotteau’s simplicity made him an easy target for her petty reprisals. His lack of self-awareness and failure to reciprocate the social polish she sought intensified their estrangement.

As the vicar sank into discontent, he sought solace in outside company, further alienating Gamard who, thwarted in her ambitions, began to plot revenge. Thus small domestic grievances escalated into a deeper conflict, revealing how unnoticed slights and unacknowledged ambitions can shape human relationships in a provincial setting. The events of these nights are only a prologue to a fuller exploration of the intricacies of pride, naiveté, and social maneuvering that will follow.

The next morning Birotteau awoke preoccupied with thoughts of his potential canonry, momentarily forgetting the ominous signs he had noticed the night before. He rang for Marianne to light a fire, but when she did not appear he was about to ring again when Abbe Troubert entered after knocking. This monthly visit was expected; Troubert immediately noticed the absence of a fire, called for Marianne, then inquired about Birotteau’s health and any news of the canonry. Birotteau, unaware that Troubert resented Madame de Listomere for not inviting him to her home, shared details of his efforts to secure the position.

The two abbes presented a sharp contrast. Troubert was tall, thin, serious, his wrinkled face hinting at sarcasm and contempt; Birotteau was plump, with a round, cheerful face. Troubert’s calm exterior masked a dullness some attributed to his subservience to Mademoiselle Gamard, while Birotteau’s jovial nature made him well liked. Despite their differences, neither seemed likely to rise above his present station—Troubert because of his ambition and Birotteau because of his amiability.

At fifty, Troubert had assuaged fears about his ambitions by adopting a saintly life, and his declining health suggested he might soon be appointed vicar-general. Birotteau’s robust health made him appear likely to endure. Abbe Chapeloud, a respected diocesan figure, had quietly opposed Troubert’s elevation, yet Troubert treated him with respect despite Chapeloud’s skepticism.

During the visit Troubert said he needed to borrow the “Register of Bishops,” which Birotteau gladly supplied. They went downstairs together, each carrying a large book, and were met with Mademoiselle Gamard’s sharp disapproval. She scolded Birotteau for cluttering her dining room; he felt the sting of her ill temper, a jarring contrast to his usual cheerfulness.

Breakfast was tense. Mademoiselle Gamard complained about being disturbed the previous night, and Birotteau, weighed by her displeasure, struggled to engage. He attempted to break the silence with remarks about the coffee and the weather, but his efforts were met with disdain. Her demeanor reflected the bitterness often found in unmarried women who, feeling unfulfilled and rejected, harbor resentment toward others.

Such old maids frequently embody a mix of societal rejection and self-loathing. Lives without romantic fulfillment breed bitterness and a lack of charm. Mademoiselle Gamard’s appearance mirrored her inner turmoil—sharp features, abrupt movements, and an ungracious manner. Having never married, she bore the social stigma of failure, and that perception pressed upon her.

Feeling the oppressive atmosphere, Birotteau left the dining room with a heavy heart and wandered the garden, only to find Mademoiselle Gamard and Troubert watching him, which intensified his discomfort. Distracted by their scrutiny, he sought refuge in church activities and momentarily forgot his troubles. When he returned home he discovered he had missed dinner: Mademoiselle Gamard had set the clock ahead to avoid waiting for him. This manipulation exemplified her malicious nature—she took pleasure in undermining Birotteau’s comfort while maintaining an appearance of propriety.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Eight days after the events began, the relationship between Abbé Birotteau and Mademoiselle Gamard revealed a plot that had been brewing for six months. Birotteau had been oblivious to Gamard's malice, but after the incident with the candlestick and the clock he at last recognized her animosity. The realization plunged him into despair; her presence felt suffocating. Gamard, reveling in vengeance, carefully executed her plan against the unsuspecting priest, who was too preoccupied with trivial troubles to confide in friends.

Birotteau’s once-mundane life became a torment. His anxiety about confronting Gamard worsened his health, and noticing his decline he sought help from Abbé Troubert, hoping for intervention. He felt embarrassed discussing Gamard’s provocations with someone so serious. Troubert listened coolly, suppressing a smile, and suggested the vicar should try to understand Gamard better, attributing her behavior to health issues.

Desperate and confused, Birotteau thought a temporary departure from Gamard’s house might ease her hostility. He planned a stay at Madame de Listomère’s country house, unaware this would serve Gamard’s interests. After ten days at the Alouette, Monsieur Caron, Gamard’s lawyer, informed him she was concerned about his prolonged absence. Startled, Birotteau insisted he intended to return, but Caron said Gamard wanted clarity to avoid disputes.

Back in the dining room, Birotteau recounted his troubles to friends intrigued by the drama. Madame de Listomère suggested Troubert coveted Birotteau’s apartment. The group analyzed Gamard’s motives; an old landowner warned Birotteau was at the start of long troubles. Mademoiselle Salomon de Villenoix arrived with news that the vicar-general was ill and Troubert might be appointed in his place, giving Troubert power over appointments.

Madame de Listomère urged Birotteau to consider the canonry rather than endure Gamard. The group rallied, suggesting he let Gamard and Troubert have their way if it might advance him. But the struggle escalated, and friends summoned Caron again to clarify matters. Caron returned asking Birotteau to sign a document relinquishing his residence; pressed by his friends, he signed.

Birotteau’s despair deepened when he realized he had signed away his belongings, which Gamard claimed as compensation for board price differences. He confronted her; she coldly reminded him of the agreement. Devastated, he fled to Madame de Listomère, finding his possessions packed. Mademoiselle Salomon, concerned for his sanity, took him back to the Alouette, where he confided in friends about his misfortunes.

Monsieur de Bourbonne examined the deed and found it exploitative. Outraged, he suggested legal action. The group debated suing, while some favored compromise to avoid scandal. They resolved to support Birotteau against Gamard and Troubert, though Bourbonne warned many would abandon him when the struggle grew severe.

Madame de Listomère’s invitation to Birotteau to stay at her house sparked gossip and divided the town; Gamard, feeling slighted, sought to undermine his reputation. Meanwhile, Birotteau’s friends began gathering evidence for a lawsuit, complicated by political divisions and public sentiment against the clergy. An expert appraisal of Birotteau’s belongings revealed their true value, suggesting Gamard’s claims were fraudulent.

As the legal battle opened, Birotteau’s situation grew more precarious, with town gossip fueling the conflict. Stakes were high and the outcome uncertain as Birotteau faced the formidable alliance of Gamard and Troubert, determined to maintain their power and influence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
A few days after hostilities began between Birotteau and the old maid, Baron de Listomere, expecting promotion to captain of a corvette, received a letter warning of a possible retirement. Shocked, he rushed to Paris to see the minister of the Navy, who laughed off his concerns. Further inquiries, however, revealed a document confirming his impending retirement, pending the director's signature.

Desperate, the baron sought help from his uncle, a deputy, who learned the minister believed the baron had sided with the Radicals in Tours and had offended the influential Abbe Troubert, the vicar-general. His uncle advised reconciliation with Troubert, stressing the importance of good relations with the clergy for the baron’s career. Realizing the gravity, the baron resolved to avoid further conflict.

Three days later he told his aunt their family was precarious because of their support of Birotteau. They consulted Monsieur de Bourbonne, who advised retreating gracefully from the dispute with Troubert. The plan was to appease Troubert while distancing themselves from Birotteau, seen as a liability.

The baroness approached Birotteau, asking him to withdraw his lawsuit against Mademoiselle Gamard and leave her house. Shocked and distressed, Birotteau agreed, believing himself a burden. He requested the return of a portrait of Chapeloud that belonged to him; the baroness promised to negotiate with Gamard.

Soon Mademoiselle Gamard fell ill, and the town buzzed that Birotteau’s lawsuit had caused her distress. The baroness visited but could not see her, then sought an audience with Troubert. There they engaged in a duel of insinuations and civility, each trying to outmaneuver the other.

The baroness presented Birotteau’s relinquishment document; Troubert affected indifference but was secretly pleased. He later returned with Gamard’s refusal to part with Chapeloud’s portrait, which the baroness accepted to preserve peace. Gamard died shortly after, leaving her fortune to Troubert, further complicating matters for the Listomeres.

At Gamard’s funeral, Troubert delivered a grand eulogy that masked his true feelings. Birotteau, guilt-ridden over her death, mourned alone. The Listomeres struggled with the fallout and Troubert’s growing power.

Five months later Troubert was appointed Bishop of Troyes, while Madame de Listomere died, leaving an annuity to Birotteau. Troubert’s influence continued to threaten the baron’s career; he manipulated events to his advantage, ensuring Birotteau’s legacy would be contested.

In the end Birotteau was exiled to a distant parish, ill and grieving. Troubert’s ascent, marked by cunning and manipulation, reflected broader struggles within society and the Church. The narrative closes on a meditation about power, individualism, and the moral complexities of human relationships, suggesting the pursuit of personal ambition often exacts a heavy cost from others.
