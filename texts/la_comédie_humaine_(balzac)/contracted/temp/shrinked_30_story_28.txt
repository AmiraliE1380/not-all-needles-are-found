Have you felt unease and sadness in the crowded galleries since the 1830 revolution? Balzac argues the true Salon has been overwhelmed by quantity. Where careful selection once showcased the best and provoked debate—helping form reputations like Delacroix and Ingres—exhibitions now drown in numbers. Before 1817 only a few paintings were shown; today galleries overflow, individual works lose impact, shows close quickly, and the Salon’s role as arbiter of taste has become a tumultuous bazaar. Great artists are obscured by mediocrity, and talk of unrecognized genius increases when the Academy’s discriminating judgment is absent.

Into this swollen catalogue comes Pierre Grassou, nicknamed “Fougeres.” In 1832 he lives in a cramped rue de Navarin flat with a small studio overlooking Montmartre. A tidy, careful genre painter of modest means, he avoids grand historical subjects and produces small easel works. Dealer Elie Magus, who buys Grassou’s pictures cheaply, commissions portraits of the bourgeois Vervelle family—whose daughter’s dowry could secure Grassou’s future. Trained under Servin and Schinner but never mastering their art, ridiculed for mediocrity, his 1819 Salon entry was rejected and sold cheaply to Magus, launching a cautious, persistent career that typifies many of his contemporaries.

For days Pierre Fougeres waited near Elie Magus’s shop hoping his painting would attract notice; when it disappeared after a week, Elie said he had sold it and would buy more if Fougeres produced Flemish interiors, anatomy lessons, and landscapes. Overjoyed, Fougeres felt validated and ignored any ironic warning a more cynical artist might have offered.

He met his friend Joseph Bridau but, consumed with painting, could not enjoy the evening. Back home he sketched, bought supplies, hired models, and devoted himself to his craft. After two months he produced four paintings. Schinner and Bridau called them derivative and urged literature, but Fougeres retouched the canvases and sold them to Elie for twenty-five francs apiece, barely breaking even. He even noticed a strange mist over his works in the shop but dismissed it.

Over seven years he developed competent, if unremarkable, work. Elie sold the pieces and Fougeres earned a modest living. At the 1829 Exhibition, with friends’ help, he entered a striking piece—a young man in prison inspired by Gerard Dou—which drew attention even from Charles X. Madame bought the painting and commissioned more. Fougeres’s success rested more on imitation than innovation, yet his modesty, punctuality, and steadiness won supporters. He furnished a studio, completed commissions reliably, and by thirty-seven had produced around two hundred paintings that appealed to the bourgeoisie. Friends often preferred his character to his art, regretting his career choice, though he offered sound advice and appreciated beauty.

After the July Revolution he continued submitting to the Salon, lived frugally, and managed his finances with his notary Cardot. He dreamed of a comfortable artistic life and aspired to join the Institute. One day the wealthy Vervelle family came for portraits; the father admired Fougeres’ Legion of Honor cross, the family liked his work, Pierre was charmed by their daughter Virginie, and they began to see him as a suitable match, blending personal hopes with professional ambitions.

While painting Virginie, Grassou became part of the Vervelle household. Near the end of a sitting Bridau burst in, disheveled, begged five hundred francs to meet creditors, and criticized Grassou’s style, urging acceptance of nature’s imperfections. Bridau left; Madame Vervelle feared he had spoiled the portrait, but Grassou assured her he had improved it.

Flattered, Grassou accepted hospitality at Ville d’Avray and was shocked to find many canvases in their gallery were his earlier works he’d sold to Elie. Vervelle valued the collection at three hundred thousand francs; Grassou dryly noted his original low sale prices. Vervelle promised to double his daughter’s dowry if he could prove the claim. Grassou’s standing rose; he later gifted their completed portraits, securing their favor.

Today Pierre Grassou is a competent portrait painter, earning about twenty thousand francs a year, living on his wife’s six-thousand-franc dowry, painting bourgeois portraits for five hundred francs apiece, and even holding the Legion of Honor. Yet he remains insecure—sure other artists mock him and critics ignore him—still craving Academy recognition and secretly replacing celebrated paintings in the Ville d’Avray gallery with his weaker canvases. His mediocrity, tempered by kindness and generosity, renders him complex and oddly sympathetic.
