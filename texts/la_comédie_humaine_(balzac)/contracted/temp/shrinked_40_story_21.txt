Railroads threatened to make many contemporary industries obsolete and alter travel around Paris; elements of the Scene were already becoming relics. The once-common "coucous" of 1830 were fading by 1842 and survived mainly at suburban spectacles. In 1820 many districts still lacked regular stage-coach service, so short-route local vehicles remained vital.

The Touchards, father and son, held a near-monopoly on travel within about forty-five miles of their rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis base, but lively competition persisted from local coucous on shorter routes. Parisians’ appetite for the countryside let small, familiar enterprises thrive: proprietors—often innkeepers—knew the roads, undercut fares, bent rules, and earned local loyalty. These coucous could challenge the Touchards’ formal coaches until omnibuses later carried more passengers more efficiently.

One lucrative contested line ran from Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise; in 1822 multiple rivals operated despite Touchard efforts to cut fares and improve equipment because stops like Saint-Denis and Saint-Brice guaranteed demand. The Touchards extended to Chambly while rivals reached Beauvais. By contrast, the road to Isle-Adam, valued for its quarries and picturesque terminus, had only two coaches and discouraged speculation, giving Pierrotin and a colleague a virtual monopoly that locals often preferred to the crowded Beaumont coaches.

Pierrotin, in his forties, left the cavalry in 1815 to take over his father’s coucou, married an innkeeper’s daughter, enlarged the business, and combined intelligence with military precision. He treated passengers like cargo. Anticipating change, he ordered a larger coach while keeping an inherited yellow coucou—built for six but habitually overloaded—and a noisy four-wheeler seating seventeen though limited to fourteen. He paid taxes only on the coucou, using a special permit to skirt regulations.

On a Saturday morning in autumn 1822, outside the Lion d’Argent, Pierrotin worried over scarce passengers and a thousand-franc debt, even weighing a loan from Monsieur Moreau at Presles. Then a footman arrived seeking a discreet ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who had countermanded his carriage. Pierrotin seized the opportunity, hopeful the fare would ease his debts; the count’s secrecy heightened his sense of responsibility for a smooth, discreet journey.

Monsieur Huguet de Serizy descends from the eminent President Huguet, ennobled under François I; his family arms and motto link modesty to the estate’s name. His father was president of parliament before the Revolution. The count became a councillor of State at twenty-two, known for persuasive diplomatic memoranda. He stayed at Serizy during the Revolution, protected by his father’s stature, and after that father’s death in 1794 joined the Council of Five Hundred to distract himself from grief. After 18th Brumaire he won the First Consul’s favor, rejoined the Council of State, and was charged with reorganizing a chaotic department—work that shaped much of Napoleon’s administration. In 1806, at forty, he married the widow of a notable Republican general, improving his fortune. By 1814 years of labor and ill health compelled his resignation; though Napoleon treated him with some mistrust he retained rank. Louis XVIII later trusted him, appointing him a cabinet minister. During the Hundred Days he remained loyal to the Bourbons, staying at Serizy. After Napoleon’s final defeat he resumed duties as privy councillor and vice-president of the Council of State, influencing affairs without seeking acclaim. His life—early to rise, early to bed—reflected relentless, quiet duty; he received many honors, including the grand cross of the Legion of Honor, yet his devotion carried an unspoken sorrow.

He had long been passionately attached to his wife; despite strains their bond endured. Work was his refuge from heartache; he treated her with respect and allowed considerable freedom. Though she acknowledged some virtues, her ingratitude often wounded him; only occasionally did she soothe his pain.

A pressing business matter then occupied him: the Presles farm was encroaching on his estate. Leased by tenant Léger from the burgher Margueron, the lease was expiring and the property would be sold. The count intended to buy it to end disputes and instructed his notary and lawyer to negotiate rapidly.

His steward, Moreau, had served faithfully seventeen years—saved by the count’s father during the Revolution and later spared by the count himself. Moreau asked to manage Presles and at first was diligent, but after securing the post he grew lax and corrupt, taking bribes and arranging shady deals, already striking a bargain with Léger. His conduct drew the retired officer Monsieur de Reybert’s ire; Madame de Reybert brought the count detailed accusations. Initially skeptical of her motives, the count’s doubts grew and he ordered his notary to accompany her to Presles. What began as a straightforward purchase thus became an investigation fraught with intrigue and the threat of betrayal by trusted servants.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier he met Madame Clapart and her son Oscar, eighteen and anxious on his first trip alone. Ruined by a first husband’s suicide and a poor second marriage, Madame Clapart entrusted her overprotected, vain son to Pierrotin’s care. Moreau had proposed testing Oscar by placing him with the steward. Mocked and humiliated en route, Oscar’s struggle with identity simmers as the coach—carrying a cross-section of society and an incognito Comte de Serizy—sets off, preparing the stage for his trial.

Passengers traveling by public conveyance begin in silence until the most talkative breaks it. National types are sketched briefly: the English taciturn, Germans somber, Italians cautious, Spaniards rarely using public transport, Russians hindered by poor roads; France is lively, especially among the lower classes and bourgeois.

Georges, a clever, lively young man, quickly appraised his fellow passengers, judged himself superior, and resolved to amuse himself at their expense. As the coach descended from La Chapelle to Saint-Denis he toyed with identities, posing as a disguised Russian prince or a soldier returned from the East. When Mistigris complained about dust, Georges joked and steered talk toward the Levant, claiming service under Ali Pacha of Janina and inventing a plague scare that alarmed the count into stopping the coach. He also asserted he’d fought in 1813 and at Waterloo, where he’d been promoted to captain.

Pressed about decorations, Georges said he didn’t wear them while traveling; the count showed his, and Georges professed admiration for Napoleon, spinning tales of Egypt and a harem supposedly given him by Ali Tebelen. At Saint-Denis they stopped at a tavern famed for cheesecakes, where the count, growing suspicious, found a portfolio identifying Georges as Crottat’s second clerk. Despite this exposure, Georges continued to charm—especially Père Léger and Oscar—by paying for treats, ordering drinks, and telling colorful stories of Eastern oddities and a dramatic Dalmatian romance, keeping the party amused even as the count noted contradictions.

Pierrotin’s coach halted at Saint-Brice about half past one to rest the horses. The innkeeper greeted the passengers by name and they stepped down for a brief breakfast. Georges, ostentatious and boastful, demanded a hearty meal and twirled his cane; Père Léger, the stout farmer, agreed to “break a crust.” Georges produced a straw case, lit a pale cigar, and offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner. Oscar, flattered but clumsy, puffed ineptly and became the butt of teasing: Schinner smoked easily, Georges fumbled, and Mistigris mocked Oscar’s discomfort. Oscar watched Georges’s display with thinly veiled resentment, privately tallying the bills he suspected Georges was burning through.

Georges proposed a bottle of Bordeaux shared with Père Léger. The innkeeper, repeating gossip from François, conductor of Touchard’s coach, said Monsieur Margueron had been invited by the Comte de Serizy to dine at Presles that day. Père Léger laughed and boasted of a plan to preempt such patronage: he intended to buy Les Moulineaux cheaply by using newly minted coin, snapping up the farm before the count could act. The innkeeper calculated returns—Les Moulineaux produced six thousand francs—and mused about lending twenty thousand. Père Léger explained he’d made Moreau a partner and, by leveraging royal favor and the minting of new coin, secured the estate. The scheme sounded shrewd even if the count tried to intervene.

The count, listening nearby, grew uneasy about discretion. When Pierrotin stepped out to water the horses, the count drew him aside and reminded him of a bribe: ten louis to keep the matter secret. Then, raising the inducement dramatically, he offered a thousand francs the next day for a new coach if Pierrotin would conceal his name. He instructed him not to enter the inn, not to speak of the incident, and to stay with the horses. Pierrotin returned curt reassurance, hurried his customers on, and insisted he must deliver fish by three to a big dinner in Stors.

Père Léger suggested harnessing the horse Pierrotin meant to sell into the cabriolet so they could breakfast calmly and overtake Pierrotin. To everyone’s surprise, Pierrotin rebridled and set off; Schinner and Mistigris walked. Georges kept up talk about the hostess and the landscape, joking about spare cigars. Oscar, stung by being called “the little young man,” defended his dignity: he was not Mina’s aide-de-camp, had not been to the East, and his family intended him for diplomacy. This provoked ridicule: his companions mocked his pretensions, tutors, and “household” language—Oscar proudly noting his mother ran their household—and taunted his social standing and vocabulary.

Pressed to name his tutor, Oscar named the Abbé Loraux, vicar of Saint-Sulpice, and hinted at family influence that might aid the abbé’s ecclesiastical rise. He added that Abbé Frayssinous owed favors to his father. Then unexpectedly Oscar declared he was bound for the Château de Presles to visit the Comte de Serizy and claimed intimacy with the count’s son. His confident talk about the Serizys—detailing private matters—caught the count’s attention.

Seizing the chance, the count questioned Oscar, explaining he sought information on Monsieur de Serizy to support a claim he intended to press with the American government. Oscar advised approaching the count’s wife rather than the count himself: he said the count was madly in love with her, while she secretly despised him. He described the count’s disfiguring skin disease and humiliating remedies—sulphur and steam baths, even an iron box—portraying a man reduced and ashamed; in Oscar’s telling, such afflictions left the count vulnerable at home. He recommended instead seeking the Marquis d’Aiglemont, an old admirer of Madame de Serizy, for influence.

The young man’s ease in speaking of intimate matters shocked the company. Schinner scolded him for airing friends’ affairs in public; Mistigris professed indifference. Georges opined that one should know how to talk without saying anything in a public coach. When the conversation returned to the Comtesse de Serizy, the count interrupted sharply and forbade disparagement. Asserting his friendship with her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, he vowed that speaking ill of the countess would not go unpunished. The painter agreed: no man should mock women. The count hinted at influence—knowing ministers, blocking unworthy men from decorations, and even acquaintance with the architect of Presles—and asked Pierrotin to stop at the next inn so he could step out.

Once clear of the others, the count had Pierrotin show him aside in the inn-yard and asked about the talkative young man. Pierrotin explained the boy was the son of a poor woman in the rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, whom he often supplied with fruit and game from Presles. Pierrotin had driven the youngster only once and thought he might be the prince of Maffliers; the boy had told him to leave him near Maffliers, not to go on to Isle-Adam. The count was reassured by this account. When he rejoined the party, Père Léger and the others exchanged awkward glances, feeling exposed; the three young men kept a guilty silence. Mistigris quipped about suffering for loose talk, and Georges reminded Oscar that public conveyances required speaking artfully.

They rode on into the Carreau woods in heavy silence; the count noted their quiet as if led to the scaffold. Oscar, trying to assert himself, declared he was Oscar Husson and would be famous within ten years. The companions teased him further, and the count refined his name to Husson de la Cerisaie, lending Oscar’s modest boast a veneer of dignity. The coach descended toward the grand château of Presles.

Before disembarking, the count delivered formal, almost theatrical farewells: the colonel should make peace with the king; Schinner’s fame was assured; Oscar, the budding diplomat, needed no protection since he possessed “the secrets of statesmen;” Père Léger was urged to pluck the Comte de Serizy and to do so firmly. Asking Pierrotin to set and fetch him at the same spot the next day, the count left the coach to the others’ bewildered speculation about his identity. Père Léger guessed he was the count who’d leased Franconville; Schinner swore never to blague again in a public coach. Mistigris, unrepentant, shrugged with a proverb.

As they neared the château gate, Georges warned Oscar that if the man indeed had been the Comte de Serizy, Oscar should be wary—a chill reminder of his mother’s warnings. Pierrotin called out they had arrived at Presles. Some of the young men feigned familiarity, but Georges, not wishing to reveal his destination, pretended he would continue to Les Moulineaux. Père Léger then triumphantly disclosed that he was the farmer at Les Moulineaux, forcing Georges to admit it was so.

Oscar asked Pierrotin to leave his belongings at the steward’s; he intended to go straight to the château. He plunged forward along a narrow path until Père Léger corrected him: the little gate, not the forest path, led to the château. Guided thus, Oscar entered the courtyard. While Père Léger watched, Georges slipped away upon realizing Père Léger truly possessed Les Moulineaux. Pierrotin, having obeyed the count’s injunction to keep the matter secret for now, drove in with swagger, deposited the painter’s packages and Oscar’s bundles, and handed a small leather portmanteau to the concierge’s wife. Mistigris and the painter took rooms in the château itself—a discovery that stunned Oscar.

The scene closed on Pierrotin’s contentment: he had kept the count’s secret and now looked forward to the promised reward. With a sly peasant’s calculation, he anticipated receiving a thousand francs the next day and buying the splendid new coach he long desired. The coach rolled on, the passengers dispersed through the estate, and the social ambitions, pretensions, and private bargains that had surfaced in their talk settled back into the polite surface of conversation, moving unseen beneath it.

Oscar hid behind trees as Monsieur Moreau emerged from the guard-room in a blue overcoat, riding whip in hand. Moreau greeted the painters—Mistigris and Bridau, sent by architect Grindot—told the concierge to show them their rooms, invited them to dinner at five, and mentioned hunting on the estate. Leading Oscar through the park, he sent his son Jacques ahead to tell Madame Moreau of their arrival. Stern but kindly, he urged the inexperienced Oscar to learn to ride and hunt. Oscar’s mother had allowed only a two-week stay because Madame Moreau was present, yet Moreau seemed intent on extending it.

Their steward’s house, attached to the chateau and predating the Revolution, stood in a large garden and contained a salon, dining room, and a modern boudoir arranged to Madame Moreau’s taste. The family lived comfortably on estate provisions. Madame Moreau, once a maid, retained an air of elegance and worked to be treated as the chateau’s mistress, often patronizing villagers; an old slight from Madame de Reybert stung her. Eager to improve her standing, she hoped the visiting artist would flatter her.

On the painters’ arrival day Madame Moreau dressed elegantly, though the Paris coach brought only Mistigris’s packages. When Mistigris and Bridau reached the house she professed a love of art; the two artists quickly perceived her pretensions and humored her. Their talk was cut short when a maid announced the count’s unexpected arrival, and Madame Moreau grew flustered. Oscar, uneasy, was fetched by his younger brother and sharply rebuked for his appearance; a joking remark by Mistigris about Oscar’s future heightened the awkwardness. The maid then said the count wanted dinner promptly.

The count, angered to find Moreau absent and having overheard talk suggesting Moreau’s disloyalty, confronted him—outraged that private matters had been spoken of, especially in front of Oscar. Moreau’s explanations failed; he returned home distraught over having lost the stewardship at Presles because of Oscar’s careless words. Estelle, his wife, was furious and grasped the family’s peril. Moreau demanded an explanation from Oscar, who remained silent from shame and fear. In anger Moreau dragged him back to the count to apologize; the count dismissed Oscar and doubted his promise. Humiliated, Moreau took him home to face uncertain consequences.

Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, distraught that Oscar had revealed Madame de Serizy’s secrets to an incognito count, costing the family its position. At Clapart, Monsieur fretted about Oscar’s character while Madame defended him; when Brochon returned with Oscar and the letter, Madame Clapart fainted at the thought of her son’s prospects. The next day she urged Oscar—sullen and indifferent—to be discreet, hardworking, and prudent. As a last hope she appealed to Uncle Cardot, a modest self-made businessman who generously supported his children. Cardot praised Oscar’s promise and, moved by Madame Clapart, offered to fund his legal studies on condition of integrity, discipline, and effort.

Madame Clapart felt renewed hope. Moreau later visited, noting he had begun speculating in land and could place Oscar as a pupil in a law office; he and Madame Clapart insisted on disciplined study to repair Oscar’s reputation and secure the family’s future.

Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a twenty-six-year-old solicitor in the rue de Béthisy who had opened a roomy but inexpensive office. Raised in poverty, Desroches immediately saw Oscar’s situation: the boy’s gaunt look and curt manners intimidated him. “We work here day and night,” Desroches said, and put his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, in charge of training Oscar so he would not lose time on the way to law school and would master the Code. Oscar was lodged in a small attic beside Godeschal’s room; Desroches plainly intended to make him a capable clerk.

For two years Oscar lived at the office under exacting, petty work. Under Godeschal’s strict oversight he kept a monk-like schedule: rising at five to prepare for the day, running errands, studying the Code under rigorous tests, returning to office tasks after classes, and subsisting on plain meals. His social life was minimal — monthly breakfasts with his uncle Cardot and Sundays with his mother — interrupted only by occasional outings Moreau could arrange. His efforts at elegance met the disapproval of his superiors.

Godeschal counseled a modest wardrobe and frugal living, insisting a good clerk should not spend more than seven hundred francs a year; he himself lived by those principles. He embodied honest, unostentatious leadership: when a colleague failed his duties, Godeschal discreetly guided him to resign rather than face public dismissal, showing practical humanity.

By twenty, Oscar had become the third clerk, performing the second’s duties without pay but receiving room and board. His legal knowledge surpassed many licensed graduates, and he even began arguing cases in chambers. Yet he felt constrained by a life at odds with his desires: he envied well-dressed young men and yearned for freedom and pleasure, feelings he habitually suppressed. Godeschal, aware of these struggles, tried to shield him and sometimes covered the cost of outings to give him some enjoyment; Moreau supported him but could not connect as Godeschal did. Both feared youthful follies might imperil his future, though they hoped to steer him aright.

“How is he getting on?” Moreau asked. “Too much vanity,” Godeschal replied, noting Oscar’s taste for fine clothes and suggesting his talents might suit a barrister rather than a solicitor’s clerkship.

In November 1825 a new clerk, Frédéric Marest, arrived — well-to-do and aiming for the magistracy, though claiming a desire for hands-on practice. The clerks amused themselves with practical jokes: Godeschal kept a fictitious register of receptions for new clerks filled with absurd entries. When Oscar asked for it, Marest read, laughed, and revealed his inexperience.

The next day Georges Marest, now head-clerk to notary Hannequin, greeted Oscar and recalled past mischief that had led to his transfer. Georges invited the clerks to the Rocher de Cancale and an evening with “the Marquise,” promising high society. Desroches interrupted to assign Oscar the task of fetching a crucial legal document from the Palais and warned him to beware of Derville, who might obstruct the errand. Eager to prove himself, Oscar accepted.

Godeschal tried to learn more about the Marquise, but Frédéric maintained a ruse, calling her a wealthy widow from Mexico. In truth she was Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a celebrated danseuse at the Gaîté, supported by Père Cardot, who had taken her and her mother in and financed her career. Georges actively pursued Florentine. At the lavish breakfast that followed, the intoxicated clerks went to Florentine’s salon. Oscar, charmed, joined a game of vingt-et-un with Florentine and the actress Fanny Beaupré, quickly losing the money he’d been given for the legal document. Florentine offered him a thousand francs; he accepted but lost everything, fell into despair and stupor, and slept in the boudoir while the party continued into the early morning.

A loud noise wakes clerk Oscar Husson; his uncle Cardot scolds him for late revelry and, prompted by the danseuse Florentine who reveals Oscar is Cardot’s nephew, is urged to help. Oscar confesses he lost gambling money belonging to his employer, Monsieur Desroches; ashamed, he begs Cardot, who reluctantly lends 500 francs and warns it will be the last help. Mariette sends a note and money; Godeschal, another clerk, hurries to fetch documents for Desroches. Angered, Desroches resolves to dismiss Oscar despite Godeschal’s defense—Godeschal recalls past lapses and blames the influence of Georges Marest. At home Clapart taunts his wife over their son’s failures; Madame Clapart defends Oscar. Oscar confronts his father, asserting independence but is ignored.

Determined to take responsibility, Oscar faces conscription; Clapart thinks military service will reform him. He draws a number and, three days later through the Comte de Serizy’s influence, is sent to the cavalry. Madame Clapart turns to religion. Oscar applies himself in the army, rises to lieutenant-colonel, and years later, decorated, stands with his changed mother outside the Lion d’Argent. They encounter a ruined Georges Marest who fails to recognize him—military discipline, connections, and hard lessons having turned earlier errors into steps toward respect, security, and marriage to Georgette Pierrotin.
