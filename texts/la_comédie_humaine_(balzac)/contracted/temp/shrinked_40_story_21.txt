**CHAPTER I. THAT WHICH WAS LACKING TO PIERROTIN’S HAPPINESS**  

Railroads threaten to render certain transportation industries around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son duo, monopolized travel within a 45-mile radius but faced competition from local services, particularly the coucous, which thrived on shorter routes. Despite their efforts, the Touchards struggled against smaller competitors who enjoyed local goodwill.

In 1822, the Touchards contended with three rivals on the profitable Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise route. Although they extended their service to Chambly, competition remained fierce. Conversely, the road to Isle-Adam had less competition, with only two coaches operating in harmony. Pierrotin, a well-liked coachman, coordinated schedules with his rival to benefit customers from the “Lion d’Argent” inn.

On a Saturday morning in 1822, Pierrotin anxiously awaited passengers outside the inn. Financially strained after ordering a new coach, he hoped it would attract more customers. His inherited coucou was cramped and often overloaded, and he needed to raise a thousand francs to complete the new coach's payment. 

A footman approached, seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. Eager to please and hoping for a generous tip, Pierrotin agreed, despite the footman’s warning to keep the count’s identity secret. This chapter sets the stage for a narrative exploring ambition, social dynamics, and the evolving transportation landscape in early 19th-century France, highlighting Pierrotin's struggles and aspirations.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the notable president Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family coat of arms features a party per pale or and sable, with the motto “i, semper melius eris,” reflecting the modesty of burgher families. The current count, a councillor of State at just twenty-two, distinguished himself during the Revolution by remaining on his estate, Serizy, where he was protected by his father's reputation. After his father's death in 1794, he was elected to the Council of the Five Hundred and later became a prominent figure under Napoleon, who appointed him to the Council of State and made him a count and senator.

In 1806, he married the widow of a Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. By 1814, due to health issues, he resigned from his posts and returned to Paris, maintaining loyalty to the Bourbons and refusing Napoleon's peerage during the Hundred Days. After Napoleon's second fall, he resumed his political duties, becoming vice-president of the Council of State and managing foreign claims without seeking personal glory. His life was marked by hard work, rising early and retiring early, earning numerous honors for his service.

Despite his public success, the count suffered privately from unfulfilled love for his wife, who enjoyed considerable freedom and social engagement. He respected her autonomy, allowing her to manage her affairs while remaining devoted to her, even as his own health deteriorated. The count's dedication to work masked his emotional pain, stemming from jealousy and the complexities of their marriage.

The narrative shifts to a pressing matter involving a farm encroaching upon the count's estate, Presles. The lease on this farm, held by a farmer named Leger, was expiring, and the owner, Margueron, refused to renew it. The count sought to purchase the farm to eliminate disputes. He consulted his notary and lawyer, who advised inviting Margueron to dinner to finalize the sale. The count planned to bring part of the purchase money and secure a government position for Margueron's son as part of the deal.

Before this meeting, the count had initiated renovations at Presles, intending to surprise his wife. However, he decided to travel incognito to avoid drawing attention.

Moreau, the steward of Presles, had a complex background. His father had saved the Serizys during the Revolution, and Moreau had been granted a position by the count after escaping a death sentence. Initially loyal and diligent, Moreau's character changed after becoming comfortable in his role, leading him to accept bribes and engage in dishonest dealings. He aimed to profit from the sale of the farm, planning to pocket a significant sum while maintaining the appearance of integrity.

As the count prepared for his trip, Moreau conspired with Leger to inflate the farm's price, hoping to benefit from the transaction. Meanwhile, a retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, and his wife, feeling wronged by Moreau, sought to expose his dishonesty to the count. Madame de Reybert approached the count, claiming that Moreau was scheming to defraud him in the farm purchase.

Though initially skeptical, the count began to doubt Moreau's integrity after reading a letter from the steward that contradicted his assurances. He decided to investigate further, instructing his notary to accompany Madame de Reybert back to Presles and to delay sending his clerk as previously planned.

Thus, the count's incognito journey and the warning from Madame de Reybert set the stage for a confrontation that could jeopardize Moreau's position and the count's interests.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier, he spotted Madame Clapart and her son Oscar at the Lion d’Argent. The mother, in a worn black gown, anxiously searched for him, reflecting her devotion to Oscar, who was traveling alone for the first time. Oscar, dressed in a short olive-green coat and patched trousers, mirrored their modest means.

“Is this the conductor? Ah! Pierrotin, is it you?” she exclaimed, leaving her son to speak with him privately. She asked Pierrotin to take good care of Oscar, who was heading to Monsieur Moreau’s place. Pierrotin hesitated to voice his concerns about the challenges Oscar would face.

Pierrotin often transported goods for Moreau, who had a discreet arrangement with Madame Clapart, delivering baskets of food to her home, which puzzled him given their poverty. Madame Clapart lived in a modest apartment filled with old furniture but maintained a regal demeanor.

Monsieur Clapart, her husband, was timid yet tyrannical, and their life was marked by hardship. Despite their struggles, Madame Clapart’s unwavering love for Oscar blinded her to his shortcomings. Raised in past splendor, Oscar now faced harsh realities, compounded by his stepfather’s disdain.

Moreau, once close to Madame Clapart, had helped secure a scholarship for Oscar at a prestigious school. However, Oscar’s vanity and lack of self-awareness troubled Moreau, who wished to guide him toward a suitable career.

As they prepared for the journey, Madame Clapart fussed over Oscar, reminding him to avoid discussing servants and to be respectful. Oscar, embarrassed by his mother’s attentiveness, longed for independence and felt humiliated by two better-dressed young men nearby, who mocked him for his mother’s affection.

The young men, Georges and Amaury, engaged in playful banter, aggravating Oscar’s insecurities. He envied their confidence and style, feeling trapped by his circumstances. As the coach was readied, Pierrotin managed the logistics while the young men continued to tease Oscar, who tried to appear indifferent.

Eventually, the coach filled with passengers, including a stout farmer named Pere Leger and the Comte de Serizy, who arrived last, disguised as a bourgeois to maintain his incognito amidst the youthful mockery.

As the journey began, Georges expressed impatience with Pierrotin’s pace, while Mistigris, a young artist, made light of the situation. The count, overhearing their comments, remained composed, aware of the importance of keeping his identity hidden. The group’s dynamics shifted as they traveled, with Oscar grappling with feelings of inadequacy while others engaged in light-hearted exchanges, promising a mix of tension and camaraderie.

In public transport, passengers often start in silence, observing each other until conversation begins, typically led by the most talkative. This is particularly true in French stage-coaches, known for lively exchanges. Georges, a clever young man, assessed his fellow travelers, feeling superior and planning to amuse himself at their expense.

As the coach descended from La Chapelle, Georges decided to pose as a disguised Russian prince. He engaged in playful banter with Mistigris, spinning tales of his supposed adventures in the East, including serving under Ali, pacha of Janina. He embellished his past with stories of battles and decorations, claiming to be the grandson of Czerni-Georges, a historical figure who fought against the Ottoman Empire.

Upon arriving in Saint-Denis, they stopped at a tavern known for its cheese-cakes. Enjoying the attention, Georges paid for their treats and continued his elaborate tales. The count, intrigued yet skeptical, suspected Georges was not who he claimed to be and discovered a portfolio revealing him as Crottat’s second clerk.

As they resumed their journey, Georges shared more about his supposed military exploits, claiming crucial roles in battles. The count warned him about the risks of speaking too freely, but Georges entertained the group with tales of a dramatic encounter in Dalmatia involving a beautiful woman named Zena. Schinner, a painter in the group, added his own stories, creating a dynamic narrative as both vied for attention, exploring themes of identity and adventure.

Pierrotin's coach arrived in Saint-Brice around 1:30 PM for a routine stop. The innkeeper welcomed Pere Leger, who was eager for breakfast. Georges, showing off with his cane, impressed Oscar, but Oscar's admiration turned to jealousy when Georges lit a cigar. 

Georges offered cigars to Oscar and Schinner, who declined breakfast after eating earlier. Oscar envied Georges's carefree spending while calculating their trip costs. Mistigris joked about mismatched socks, amusing the count, who observed the young men with mixed feelings.

The innkeeper and Pere Leger discussed a land deal involving the Comte de Serizy, hinting at a scheme that intrigued the count, who suspected a conspiracy involving his steward, Moreau. Pierrotin promised to keep his identity secret for future payment and skipped breakfast due to a delivery.

Pere Leger suggested harnessing a horse to the cabriolet for breakfast, piquing the count's interest in potential profits. As Pierrotin drove away, Georges and the farmer admired the landscape, while Oscar, feeling belittled, tried to assert his future importance in diplomacy, drawing laughter from the others.

Oscar claimed friendship with the Comte de Serizy's son, surprising Pere Leger. The count, intrigued, asked for details, leading to shocking revelations about the countess. Defending her honor, the count revealed his connections before leaving the coach, leaving the young men confused. As they neared the chateau de Presles, Pierrotin planned to drop them off, while Oscar rushed ahead, unaware of his path.

Oscar, feeling shy, hid behind trees in the courtyard as Monsieur Moreau, dressed in a blue overcoat and carrying a riding whip, greeted him warmly. Moreau introduced himself to Mistigris and the painters sent by architect Monsieur Grindot, instructing the concierge to show them to their rooms and inviting them to dinner at five, mentioning hunting opportunities on the estate.

As they walked through the park, Moreau sent his son Jacques to inform Madame Moreau of Oscar's arrival. A stern yet kind man, Moreau encouraged Oscar to learn horseback riding and hunting, despite Oscar's reluctance, noting his two-week stay was due to Madame Moreau's presence.

The steward's house, built before the Revolution, was connected to the chateau and featured a salon, dining room, and a lavish boudoir for Madame Moreau, who enjoyed her status as a former maid and often dressed elegantly.

Tensions existed between the Moreaus and the Reyberts due to past reminders of Madame Moreau's previous station. The Moreaus cultivated relationships with local bourgeoisie, hosting gatherings.

Madame Moreau eagerly awaited the painters but was disappointed when only Mistigris's packages arrived. When the artists finally came, she tried to impress them with her art knowledge, though her past occasionally surfaced.

The atmosphere shifted when a maid announced the count's unexpected arrival. He confronted Moreau about gossip from Oscar, leading to Moreau's distress and a confrontation where he blamed Oscar. Overwhelmed, Oscar resisted, resulting in Moreau forcing him to apologize to the count, who dismissed Oscar, leaving Moreau to grapple with the fallout.

While preparing for a journey, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, expressing distress over Oscar's recent actions. Oscar had carelessly disclosed sensitive information about Madame de Serizy to a high-ranking official, leading to Moreau's dismissal as steward of Presles. Moreau decided to send Oscar home immediately, feeling the weight of his family's future and his own ambitions. He lamented Oscar's behavior and questioned his intelligence, wishing he had received better guidance.

That evening, Madame Clapart was knitting when her husband, Clapart, voiced concern that their friend Poiret might have visited while they were out. Clapart criticized Oscar, believing he would cause trouble at Presles. Madame Clapart defended her son, highlighting his good nature and potential, but Clapart dismissed her optimism, insisting Oscar was lazy and incapable.

Suddenly, a carriage arrived, bringing Oscar home. Brochon, the stable-man, informed them that Moreau had lost his position due to Oscar's actions. Distressed, Madame Clapart fainted upon reading Moreau's letter. When she regained consciousness, she urged Oscar to go to bed, while Clapart continued to berate him.

Despite the turmoil, Oscar slept soundly, unaware of the gravity of his situation. The next day, he felt hungry and surprised that life continued as normal. His mother, worried about their future without Moreau's support, explained the dire consequences of his actions. She emphasized the need for him to find a career, outlining limited options such as commerce, government work, or military service. She expressed fears about their financial instability and urged him to enlist in the army.

Oscar, still naive about life's challenges, listened to his mother but struggled to grasp their reality. Her tears moved him, and she implored him to promise to be more discreet in the future. They discussed seeking help from Uncle Cardot, who had prospered in business and might assist Oscar.

Uncle Cardot, a wealthy widower, lived modestly and had provided well for his children. Unaware of Madame Clapart and Oscar's struggles, he received them during their visit. She highlighted Oscar's achievements, hoping to gain his support. Cardot, while polite, was skeptical about Oscar's future and the family's needs.

Madame Clapart revealed their financial struggles, explaining her sacrifices for Oscar's education. Moved by her plight, Cardot offered to help Oscar pursue a legal career, promising to cover his educational costs if he showed promise. He advised Oscar to work hard and maintain integrity, emphasizing the importance of discretion in a big city like Paris.

After their visit, Madame Clapart felt hopeful for Oscar's future. Moreau later visited, having started a new venture in land speculation. He reassured her that Oscar could still have a successful career if he remained disciplined. Moreau proposed that Oscar work under a young lawyer named Desroches, who would provide the structure and guidance he needed. Madame Clapart was grateful for Moreau's continued support and urged Oscar to express his thanks. Moreau emphasized that the best way for Oscar to redeem himself was through hard work and proper conduct moving forward.

Ten days later, Oscar was introduced by Monsieur Moreau to Maitre Desroches, a young solicitor with a modest office on rue de Bethisy. Desroches, who had risen from poverty, took a keen interest in Oscar, recognizing his own past struggles, though his stern demeanor intimidated him.

Desroches informed his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, that Oscar would be living in the attic next to Godeschal’s room and would need strict supervision to excel in law school and learn the Code thoroughly. Godeschal, also from humble beginnings, was tasked with guiding Oscar, emphasizing hard work and integrity.

For two years, Oscar lived a monk-like existence in the office, waking at five each morning to work alongside Godeschal. He performed errands, prepared for law school, and faced rigorous examinations from both Godeschal and Desroches. His daily routine included a simple dinner and continued work until nightfall. Oscar occasionally visited his uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother, but he was often reprimanded for his attempts at elegance.

Godeschal advised Oscar on maintaining a modest wardrobe and living frugally, citing Desroches as an example of success through hard work. Eighteen months into his tenure, Oscar became the third clerk, performing the duties of the second clerk without a salary but receiving room and board. By the end of his second year, he had gained significant legal knowledge and was performing well at the Palais.

Despite his progress, Oscar felt trapped in a life that clashed with his desires. He envied the well-dressed young men he saw on the streets and longed for freedom and pleasure, which he suppressed under Godeschal's guidance. Godeschal, aware of Oscar's struggles, occasionally took him out for leisure to help him cope with the monotony of his life.

In November 1825, after passing his exams, Oscar received funds from Moreau to upgrade his wardrobe and began working as the second clerk with a salary of eight hundred francs. His uncle Cardot promised to help him find a practice if he continued to excel.

However, Oscar's internal conflict persisted. He often contemplated leaving his rigorous life, feeling more like a galley slave than a budding lawyer. Godeschal remained vigilant, ensuring Oscar was not exposed to temptations, as he often lacked money for excesses. Godeschal's occasional outings provided some relief from Oscar's otherwise dreary existence.

When Godeschal returned from a trip, he reported to Moreau that Oscar was becoming vain, dressing too elegantly and seeking social adventures. He worried that Oscar's aspirations might not align with a legal career, despite his talent for conversation.

Shortly after Oscar's promotion, a new clerk named Frederic Marest joined the office. Marest, a wealthy young man aspiring to become a magistrate, sought to learn the intricacies of legal practice. Godeschal announced Marest's arrival, hoping he would contribute to the office's camaraderie.

The clerks engaged in a tradition of welcoming new members with a humorous register filled with fictitious cases and exaggerated accounts of past receptions. This playful spirit helped the clerks bond and relieve the monotony of their work.

As Oscar settled into his role, he participated in the office's lighthearted pranks, including the ongoing joke surrounding the register. When Marest arrived, he quickly caught on to the humor, indicating he was not easily fooled.

In summary, Oscar's journey through the legal world was marked by rigorous discipline, internal conflict, and camaraderie among his fellow clerks, all while navigating the challenges of ambition and societal expectations.

The next day, Georges Marest, now head-clerk of notary Hannequin, invited Oscar and his colleagues to a lavish gathering at the Rocher de Cancale, promising elegance and excitement. Oscar's superior, Desroches, tasked him with retrieving a crucial legal document, warning him about Derville, who might hinder their efforts. Eager to prove himself, Oscar accepted the challenge.

After Georges left, Godeschal inquired about the marquise, and Frederic claimed she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. The clerks, excited about mingling with high society, prepared for the event. The Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse supported by her benefactor, Pere Cardot. Over the years, Florentine had risen in status, enjoying a luxurious lifestyle and courting Georges, who had recently inherited a fortune.

As the clerks anticipated the gathering, Oscar felt a mix of excitement and apprehension, particularly regarding Georges, who had previously caused him distress. Despite his reservations, he donned his new clothes and set out to enjoy the day. At the Rocher de Cancale, Oscar met various guests, including an old captain and a journalist. Georges charmed Oscar, downplaying past grievances. The feast was extravagant, and the clerks indulged in food and drink, leading to intoxication.

Afterward, they headed to Florentine's salon, greeted by a dazzling array of women and opulence. Oscar was captivated by Florentine's transformation into a sophisticated marquise. He soon found himself drawn into a game of vingt-et-un with Fanny Beaupre, an actress, and lost significant money, including the five hundred francs entrusted to him by Desroches. Overwhelmed by his losses, Oscar contemplated despair but was reassured by Florentine, who offered him a thousand francs to recover. He accepted, hoping to regain his standing, but lost even more as the night progressed.

By morning, Oscar was exhausted and fell asleep on a sofa, oblivious to the ongoing festivities. Florentine and her friends discussed the events, unaware of Oscar's gambling troubles. When Oscar awoke, his uncle Cardot scolded him for his late-night revelry and the state of the apartment, expressing disappointment that Oscar hadn’t informed him about the gathering. Florentine defended herself, claiming she had invited guests to please Cardot.

As tensions escalated, Florentine revealed Oscar's gambling losses, prompting Cardot to chastise him for his recklessness. Distraught, Oscar begged Cardot for help in explaining the situation to Desroches, fearing job loss. Florentine urged Cardot to assist Oscar, reminding him of their family ties. Reluctantly, Cardot gave Oscar five hundred francs, warning it would be the last time he would help him. Oscar left, uncertain of his next steps as he faced the consequences of his actions. Meanwhile, Mariette sent him a note and money to help recover his losses.

Desroches, upon receiving the note, was furious with Oscar for his irresponsibility. Godeschal defended him, suggesting that Oscar's vanity was leading him astray. Ultimately, Desroches decided to dismiss Oscar, believing he did not deserve further assistance. At home, Oscar's mother, Madame Clapart, learned of her son's troubles and was devastated. Clapart mocked her distress, but she remained hopeful for Oscar's future. When Oscar returned home, he confronted Clapart, asserting his independence and rejecting his father's authority.

Feeling the weight of his mistakes, Oscar resolved to change his ways. He drew a military number, which determined his fate. Three days later, Oscar drew number twenty-seven, leading to his enlistment in the cavalry. His fortunes changed when he was assigned to a noble regiment, thanks to the influence of the Comte de Serizy. Overwhelmed by her son's misfortunes, Madame Clapart turned to religion for solace.

Oscar excelled in the military, rising through the ranks and earning honors for bravery during a battle in Africa. Years later, Oscar, now a decorated officer, traveled with his mother to Beaumont, where they encountered Georges Marest, whose life had taken a downward turn. As they traveled, Oscar reflected on their contrasting circumstances. Oscar's military career flourished, and he eventually married Georgette Pierrotin, marking a new chapter in his life. Through his experiences, Oscar matured into a respectable man, earning a position as a collector and later a receiver-general, exemplifying the lessons learned from his earlier mistakes.
