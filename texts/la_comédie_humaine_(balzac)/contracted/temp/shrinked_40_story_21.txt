Whenever you have seriously looked at the exhibition of sculpture and painting since the revolution of 1830, have you not felt uneasiness and sadness at the sight of those long overcrowded galleries? Since 1830, the true Salon no longer exists. The Louvre has been taken again—this time by a populace of artists who have maintained themselves there.

Formerly, when the Salon showed only choicest works, exhibiting conferred the highest honor. Among two hundred selected paintings the public could still choose: an invisible hand crowned the masterpiece. Eager discussions—abuse and praise alike—marked success. Now neither the crowd nor criticism grows impassioned about the products of that bazaar. Forced to select for itself what a jury once chose, the public's attention soon tires, the exhibition drags on, and the Salon closes. Before 1817 the admitted pictures never went beyond the first two columns of the long gallery of old masters; in that year they filled the whole space. Historical, high-art, genre, easel pictures, landscapes, flowers, animals, and water-colors could scarcely offer more than twenty pictures a year worthy of attention; the public cannot attend to more. The more artists increase, the more careful the jury of admission should be.

The true character of the Salon was lost when its exhibitions spread along the galleries. It should have kept fixed, strict limits where each specialty could show its masterpieces. A decade's experience proved the excellence of the former institution. Now, instead of a tournament we have a mob; instead of a noble exhibition, a tumultuous bazaar; instead of selection, a chaotic mass. The result: a great artist is swamped. Decamps' "Turkish Cafe," "Children at a Fountain," "Joseph," and "The Torture" would have profited far more had four pictures been shown among the hundred good works of a year than his twenty among three thousand others jumbled in six galleries.

By a strange contradiction, ever since the doors opened to everyone there has been talk of unknown and unrecognized genius. When, twelve years earlier, Ingres' "Courtesan," Sigalon's, Gericault's "Medusa," Delacroix's "Massacre of Scio," Eugene Deveria's "Baptism of Henri IV" were admitted despite jealousy, the world saw young vigorous palettes existed and made no complaint. Now, when the veriest dauber can send his work, all the talk is of genius neglected! Where judgment no longer exists, there is nothing judged. But artists will return in time to examination and selection, which present their works to the crowd for whom they work. Without selection by the Academy there will be no Salon, and without the Salon art may perish.

Since the catalogue has grown into a book, many obscure names appear in it, attached to ten or a dozen pictures yet remaining in obscurity. Among these perhaps the most unknown to fame is Pierre Grassou, from Fougeres—called simply "Fougeres" among his brother-artists—who now holds a place, as they say, "in the sun," and whose case suggests the bitter reflections that introduce this sketch—reflections applicable to many other members of the tribe of artists.

In 1832 Fougeres lived in the rue de Navarin, on the fourth floor of one of those tall narrow houses resembling an obelisk, with an alley, a dark stairway, three windows on each floor, and within, a courtyard, or rather a square pit. Above the three or four rooms he occupied was his studio, looking toward Montmartre. The studio was painted brick-color for a background; the floor tinted brown and well frothed; each chair had a bit of carpet bound round the edges; the simple sofa was as clean as in some worthy bourgeoise's bedroom. All these things showed the tidy ways of a small mind and thrift of a poor man. A bureau held studio implements, a table for breakfast, a sideboard, a secretary; in short, all the articles necessary to a painter, neatly arranged and very clean. The stove added to this Dutch tidiness, made more evident by the northern light flooding the large apartment. Being merely a genre painter, Fougeres did not need the vast machinery that ruins historical painters; he never recognized sufficient faculty to attempt high art and therefore clung to easel painting.

At the beginning of December, a season when Parisian bourgeois fancy perpetuating their forms and figures already too bulky, Pierre Grassou rose early, prepared his palette, lighted his stove, ate a roll soaked in milk, and waited till the frost on his windows melted enough to let full light in. The weather was fine and dry. As he began to work in his tidy studio he heard and recognized the step of Elie Magus, a picture-dealer, a usurer in canvas. The next moment Elie entered.

"How are you, old rascal?" said the painter.

Fougeres had the cross of the Legion of Honor, and Elie Magus bought his pictures at two and three hundred francs apiece, so he gave himself the airs of a fine artist.

"Business is very bad," replied Elie. "You artists have such pretensions! You talk of two hundred francs when you haven't put six sous worth of color on a canvas. However, you are a good fellow; I'll say that. You are steady; and I've come to put a good bit of business in your way."

"Timeo Danaos et dona ferentes," said Fougeres.

"No," said Elie.

"Well, it means the Greeks never proposed good bits of business to the Trojans without getting their fair share. In the olden time they used to say, 'Take my horse.' Now we say, 'Take my bear.' Well, what do you want, Ulysses-Lagingeole-Elie Magus?"

This shows the mildness and wit with which Fougeres employed studio fun.

"Well, I don't deny that you are to paint me two pictures for nothing."

"Oh! oh!"

"I'll leave you to do it, or not; I don't ask it. But you're an honest man."

"Come, out with it!"

"Well, I'm prepared to bring you a father, mother, and only daughter."

"All for me?"

"Yes—they want their portraits taken. These bourgeois—they are crazy about art—have never dared to enter a studio. The girl has a dot of a hundred thousand francs. You can paint all three—perhaps they'll turn out family portraits."

Elie's uncanny laugh frightened the painter; he fancied Mephistopheles talking marriage.

"Portraits bring five hundred francs apiece," went on Elie; "so you can very well afford to paint me three pictures."

"True for you!" cried Fougeres, gleefully.

"And if you marry the girl, you won't forget me."

"Marry! I?" cried Pierre Grassou—"I, who have a habit of sleeping alone; who get up at cock-crow, and all my life arranged—"

"One hundred thousand francs," said Magus. "And a quiet girl, full of golden tones, as you call 'em, like a Titian."

"What class of people are they?"

"Retired merchants; just now in love with art; have a country-house at Ville d'Avray, and ten or twelve thousand francs a year."

"What business did they do?"

"Bottles."

"Now don't say that word; it makes me think of corks and sets my teeth on edge."

"Am I to bring them?"

"Three portraits—I could put them in the Salon; I might go in for portrait-painting. Well, yes!"

Old Elie descended the staircase to fetch the Vervelle family. To know how this proposition would affect the painter, and what effect the Sieur and Dame Vervelle, adorned by their only daughter, would produce, it is necessary to glance at the anterior life of Pierre Grassou of Fougeres.

As a pupil, Fougeres had studied drawing with Servin, a great draughtsman in academic circles. After that he went to Schinner's to learn the secrets of the powerful, magnificent color that distinguished that master. Master and scholars were discreet; at any rate Pierre discovered none of their secrets. From there he went to Sommervieux' atelier to acquire composition, but composition was shy and distant to him. Then he tried to snatch from Decamps and Granet the mystery of their interior effects. The two masters were not robbed. Finally Fougeres ended his education with Duval-Lecamus. During these studies Fougeres' habits and ways of life were tranquil and moral to a degree that furnished matter of jesting to the various ateliers where he sojourned; but everywhere he disarmed his comrades by his modesty and by the patience and gentleness of a lamblike nature. The masters, however, had no sympathy for the good lad; masters prefer bright fellows, eccentric spirits, droll or fiery, or else gloomy and reflective, which argue future talent. Everything about Pierre Grassou smacked of mediocrity. His nickname "Fougeres" (that of the painter in the play of "The Eglantine") was the source of much teasing; but, by force of circumstances, he accepted the name of the town where he had first seen light.

Grassou of Fougeres resembled his name. Plump and of medium height, he had a dull complexion, brown eyes, black hair, a turned-up nose, rather wide mouth, and long ears. His gentle, passive, resigned air gave a certain relief to these leading features of a physiognomy full of health but wanting in action. This young man, born to be a virtuous bourgeois, having left his native place and come to Paris to be a clerk with a color-merchant (formerly of Mayenne and a distant connection of the Orgemonts) made himself a painter simply by the obstinacy that constitutes the Breton character. What he suffered, the manner in which he lived during those years of study, God only knows. He suffered as much as great men suffer when hounded by poverty and hunted like wild beasts by the pack of commonplace minds and troops of vanities athirst for vengeance.

As soon as he thought himself able to fly on his own wings, Fougeres took a studio in the upper rue des Martyrs, where he began to work. He made his first appearance in 1819. The first picture he presented to the jury at the Louvre represented a village wedding laboriously copied from Greuze. It was rejected. When Fougeres heard of the decision, he did not fall into one of those fierce fits of self-love to which strong natures give themselves up; he quietly fetched his canvas, wrapped it in a handkerchief, and brought it home, vowing he would still make himself a great painter. He placed his picture on the easel and went to one of his former masters, Schinner—whose triumph at that year's Salon was complete—and asked him to criticize the rejected work. The great painter left everything and went at once. When Fougeres placed the work before him, Schinner, after a glance, pressed his hand.

"You are a fine fellow," he said; "you've a heart of gold, and I must not deceive you. Listen; you are fulfilling the promises you made in the studios. When you find such things at the tip of your brush, my good Fougeres, you had better leave colors with Brullon, and not take the canvas of others. Go home early, put on your cotton night-cap, and be in bed by nine o'clock. The next morning go to some government office, ask for a place, and give up art."

"My dear friend," said Fougeres, "my picture is already condemned; it is not a verdict I want of you, but the cause of that verdict."

"Well—you paint gray and sombre; you see nature as a crape veil; your drawing is heavy, pasty; your composition is a medley of Greuze, who only redeemed his defects by qualities you lack."

While detailing these faults Schinner saw on Fougeres' face so deep an expression of sadness that he carried him off to dinner and tried to console him. The next morning at seven Fougeres was at his easel, working over the rejected picture; he warmed the colors, made the corrections suggested by Schinner; he touched up his figures. Then, disgusted with such patching, he carried the picture to Elie Magus. Elie Magus, a sort of Dutch-Flemish-Belgian, had three reasons for being what he became—rich and avaricious. Coming last from Bordeaux, he had started in Paris selling old pictures and living on the boulevard Bonne-Nouvelle. Fougeres, who relied on his palette to go to the baker's, bravely ate bread and nuts, or bread and milk, or bread and cherries, or bread and cheese, or bread and cherries, according to the seasons. Elie Magus, to whom Pierre offered his first picture, eyed it for some time and then gave him fifteen francs.

"With fifteen francs a year coming in, and a thousand francs for expenses," said Fougeres, smiling, "a man will go fast and far."

Elie Magus made a gesture; he bit his thumbs, thinking he might have had that picture for five francs.

Several days Pierre walked down from the rue des Martyrs and stood at the corner of the boulevard opposite Elie Magus's shop, watching his picture receive no notice. After a week the picture disappeared. Fougeres went slowly to the dealer's; the Jew was at his door.

"Well, I see you have sold my picture."

"No, here it is," said Magus; "I've framed it, to show it to someone who fancies he knows about painting."

Fougeres had not the heart to return to the boulevard. He set about another picture and spent two months on it, eating meagrely and working like a galley-slave. One evening he went to the boulevard; his picture was not to be seen.

"I've sold your picture," said Elie Magus.

"For how much?"

"I got back what I gave and a small interest. Make me some Flemish interiors, a lesson of anatomy, landscapes, and such like, and I'll buy them of you," said Elie.

Touched to the core, Fougeres regarded Magus as a father. He went home joyful; the great painter Schinner had been mistaken—at least some hearts in Paris beat in unison with his. He was ingenuous and proud like a boy; another, more suspicious artist would have read the ironical twitch of Magus's moustache and the satisfaction of a dealer who had trapped a poor painter.

He walked the boulevard in a glow of joy and met Joseph Bridau. They went to the Opera, but Fougeres saw nothing; he was full of pictures. He bought colors, canvases, fed a pot, hired models; Magus lent him stuffs, and he "dug" at his work. After two months' seclusion he finished four pictures. Schinner and Bridau saw in three a servile imitation of Dutch interiors à la Metsu, and the fourth a copy of Rembrandt's "Lesson of Anatomy."

"Still imitating!" said Schinner. "Ah! Fougeres can't manage to be original."

Bridau advised him to do something else than painting. Humiliated, Fougeres accepted advice, retouched his pictures, and took them to Elie Magus, who paid twenty-five francs apiece. At that price he earned nothing, and he barely broke even thanks to his frugal living. He made excursions to the boulevard to see his works and underwent a strange hallucination: his neat, clean paintings, hard and shiny as porcelain, looked as if covered with a mist, as if old daubs. Magus was out; Pierre suspected his eyes.

Fougeres continued to paint. After seven years' toil he reached a passable level—about as good as any second-rate artist. Elie bought and sold all his paintings; the Breton earned laboriously about two thousand francs a year while he spent but twelve hundred. At the Exhibition of 1829, Leon de Lora, Schinner, and Bridau—leaders of the art movement—pitied his perseverance and had a picture of his admitted to the grand salon. The canvas, powerful in interest but derived in sentiment from Vigneron and in execution from Dubufe, represented a young man in prison having his hair cut at the nape; a priest, two weeping women, and a sheriff's clerk reading aloud formed the group; on a poor table a meal was untouched; light came through bars near the ceiling. Fougeres had been inspired by Gerard Dou: he turned the "Dropsical Woman" group toward the window, substituted a condemned man for the dying woman, replaced the Dutch doctor with a cold clerk, added an old woman to the young, and put on the group an executioner's simple, good-humored face. The plagiarism was cleverly disguised and unrecognized. The catalogue read:

510. Grassou de Fougeres (Pierre), rue de Navarin, 2.
Death-toilet of a Chouan, condemned to execution in 1809.

Though essentially second-rate, the picture had immense success because it recalled the "chauffeurs" affair of Mortagne. A crowd gathered every day before the fashionable canvas; even Charles X paused. "Madame," told of the painter's patient life, became enthusiastic. The Duc d'Orleans asked the price. Clergymen declared the subject morally edifying. Madame bought the picture for a thousand francs; the Dauphin ordered another like it. Charles X conferred the cross of the Legion of Honor on this son of a peasant who had once fought for the royal cause in 1799. The minister of the Interior ordered two church pictures from Fougeres.

This Salon of 1829 was Pierre Grassou's fortune, fame, future, and life. He learned that originality starved while imitation sold. This discovery—copy to live—was the gold mine that shaped his career; it is the fatal principle by which society elevates certain wretched mediocrities and excludes real talent. Yet Fougeres's modesty and genuine surprise disarmed envy. Pity and admiration for his perseverance did the rest: "Perseverance in the arts should be rewarded. Grassou hasn't stolen his successes; he has delved for ten years, the poor dear man!" Newspapers criticized, but the chevalier Fougeres accepted that like other counsel, with angelic patience.

Having by this time fifteen thousand francs laboriously earned, he furnished an apartment and studio in the rue de Navarin, painted the Dauphin's picture and the church commissions, and delivered them punctually—much to the exchequer's discomfort. By thirty-seven Fougeres had produced for Elie Magus about two hundred pictures, all unknown, and had attained that manner and point of execution before which true artists shrank and the bourgeoisie worshiped. He was loved for rectitude, steadiness, kindness, and loyalty, though his palette earned no esteem. His comrades called his "vice" the misfortune of painting.

Yet he gave sound counsel; unlike superficial critics, he felt beauty and judged justly. After the revolution of July, he sent about ten pictures a year to the Salon; the jury admitted four or five. He lived with rigid economy, mounted guard, went to reviews, visited friends, looked at works of art, took a few trips, and planned a journey to Switzerland for inspiration. An excellent citizen—he paid rent and bills—he had never had time to love. Poor and a bachelor, he had not sought to complicate his life.

Incapable of increasing his fortune by other means, he took his savings every three months to his notary Cardot, who invested them in first mortgages; the interest he drew and added to his quarterly receipts. He hoped the day would come when these savings would yield an income of two thousand francs, allowing him otium cum dignitate to paint true finished pictures and to enter the Institute, sit beside Schinner and Leon de Lora, reach the Academy before Bridau, and wear a rosette. Such modest, bourgeois dreams were his.

One day, hearing steps on the staircase, he tidied himself and admitted a family whose studios mockingly called "a melon on a pumpkin": Monsieur Vervelle, a bottle dealer wearing a three-thousand-franc diamond; his wife, plumply veneered and ostentatiously adorned; and their daughter Virginie, pale, with stringy hair and large innocent eyes, in a leghorn bonnet. A true painter might have dismissed such bourgeois clients, but Magus, who had brought them, stayed. Fougeres glanced at Magus and said, "There's fat in it!"—studio slang for a lucrative commission.

Monsieur Vervelle, hearing that the painter was decorated, quipped that a decorated artist was more suitable. The family were dazzled by Fougeres and assumed his honesty and thrift. During the sitting they hovered about the easel, marvelling and flattering; Virginie's modest bows and lowered eyes charmed the painter. He gave them sketches and drawings, small gifts that impressed them. Old Vervelle boasted of his country-house gallery—Rubens, Gerard Dou, Mieris, Terburg, Rembrandt, Titian, Paul Potter—and Madame Vervelle declared he had over one hundred thousand francs' worth of pictures. "I love Art," he said.

The family learned from the notary and the neighborhood that Fougeres had been prudent: Cardot, their notary as well, praised the painter—he had saved thirty-six thousand francs and now saved about ten thousand a year. This endorsement seemed decisive. As the Vervelles left, the father told Virginie to walk in front; she overheard a remark that would awaken her curiosity: "Decorated—thirty-seven years old—an artist who gets orders—puts his money with our notary. We'll consult Cardot. Madame de Fougeres! Not a bad name."

While the Vervelles discussed Pierre, he thought of them. He could not rest; he walked the boulevard, looking at red-haired women and making odd reasonings: gold was the handsomest metal, tawny yellow suggested it, the Romans favoured red hair—he turned Roman. After two years of marriage what man would care about hair color? Beauty fades, money steadies life. By night he had found Virginie Vervelle charming; the prospect of security warmed him.

On the second sitting the family returned. Fougeres had shaved, put on clean linen, arranged his hair, and chosen becoming garments. The mutual flatteries continued; Virginie's delicate bows and lowered eyes enchanted him. He praised the father as witty, sending the family into raptures; gifts of small drawings heightened their regard. Old Vervelle warned that pictures were money; yet Cardot's praise tipped the scale. Vervelle's friends now spoke only of the celebrated painter Fougeres, and marriage prospects were quietly under consideration.

The day Fougeres began Mademoiselle Virginie's portrait he was already virtually son‑in‑law to the Vervelle family. The three Vervelles had made his studio a second home, attracted by its neat, artistic air. Toward the end of the sitting the stairway shook and Joseph Bridau burst in like a whirlwind, hair flying and face haggard. He paced, fussed at Grassou’s coat and muttered about creditors. Then, seeing the portrait, he burst into blunt advice.

"Wood is dear," he said to Grassou. "The British are after me… Any shot in your locker? How much do you want?" He urged bolder color—“Put some vermilion on your palette, warm those cheeks; touch the little brown spots; butter it in”—taunting the painter for pretending to know more than Nature. Fougeres, embarrassed, sent Grassou to write a note while he obliged Bridau. The bottle‑dealer took the palette, accepted the IOU, and left without ceremony, plainly impatient with Virginie.

The Vervelles were startled by Bridau’s intrusion; the mother humbly preferred the decorous Grassou to any confusing genius. Timidly flattered by their deference, Vervelle invited Fougeres to his country house the next Sunday, promising wines and a picture collection that would compensate an artist for dining with merchants. This flattery particularly touched Pierre Grassou’s innocent vanity. He was an atrocious mediocrity with a heart of gold—loyal, poor at draughtsmanship, unambitious yet proud of small honors. Decorated with the Legion of Honor, he accepted and travelled modestly to Ville d'Avray, imagining marriage to Virginie and possession of the villa.

The Vervelles received him with enthusiastic bourgeois pomp. The day became a triumph: he was shown the park, led about the manicured grounds, feted with a magnificent dinner, and then taken to the theatrical climax—the picture gallery opened under carefully arranged lamps. Three neighbors, an old uncle, and other guests awaited his judgement of Pere Vervelle’s celebrated collection, which the bottle‑merchant loved to inflate.

Framed and labeled in gilt were names to dazzle any novice: Rubens—Dance of fauns and nymphs; Rembrandt—Interior of a dissecting room… One hundred and fifty varnished pictures shone; some were even veiled by green baize that prudence forbade drawing in ladies’ company. Pierre stood aghast as he recognized half his own works among the displayed "masters." He saw Rubens, Rembrandt, Mieris, Metzu, Paul Potter, Gerard Douw—twenty great names, yet many paintings were his.

Alarmed, a guest asked why his face had gone pale; he was led aside and told the host, ostentatiously, he should show him a Murillo. In whispers at the gallery, Pierre pressed Vervelle: had he bought these from Elie Magus? Yes, Vervelle said, all originals. Pierre asked what Magus had charged for some pieces; Vervelle named three thousand francs, though to the public he claimed forty thousand and boasted of a collection worth three hundred thousand. The painter then dropped the bombshell: he had painted those pictures and sold them to Elie Magus for a total under ten thousand francs. Magus, Pierre explained, had had them "aged" and retailed as old masters.

Pere Vervelle, far from being shamed, was delighted—if true, he said he would double his daughter's dowry, and the artist, now acclaimed as Rubens, Rembrandt, Titian, Gerard Douw, became a local celebrity. The Vervelles called him "Monsieur de Fougeres" and, happy with his inflated status, accepted the portraits he had painted of the family; when finished he presented them to his future father‑in‑law, mother‑in‑law, and wife without charge.

From then on, Pierre Grassou—who never missed the Salon—was regarded in bourgeois circles as a fine portraitist. He earned about twenty thousand francs a year while spoiling a thousand francs’ worth of canvas. His wife’s dowry had been six thousand francs; he lived with his father‑in‑law; the Vervelles and Grassous kept a carriage and were content. Grassou remained firmly within the bourgeois world: no family portrait between the barrier du Trone and the rue du Temple escaped him, none costing less than five hundred francs. The principal reason the bourgeoisie employed him was practical—he was prudent and "laid by twenty thousand francs a year with his notary."

Grassou’s civic partisanship—he took a creditable part in the May 12th riots—earned him a promotion to officer of the Legion of Honor and a major’s post in the National Guard. The Museum of Versailles commissioned a battle scene from such an exemplary citizen, and he paraded the commission as royal favor among his friends. At home, Madame de Fougeres adored her husband, who had given her two children and was a good father and spouse. Yet Grassou could not silence a gnawing thought: real artists scorned his work, studios used his name as an epithet, and critics ignored him in the feuilletons. Ambition for the Academy remained alive in him.

Bitter as that was, Grassou took a sly revenge: he bought works by struggling celebrated artists and substituted those true pieces for the daubs in the Ville d'Avray collection—an anonymous but pointed correction. Unlike many mediocre men who are aggressive and vindictive, Pierre remained beneficent, obliging and modestly malicious by stealth.
