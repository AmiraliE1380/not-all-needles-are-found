Doctor Monsieur Bianchon tells a chilling tale about La Grande Bretèche, a dilapidated house near Vendôme whose wild garden and decay suggest a cursed past. During his stay he sneaks into the grounds and meets the notary, Monsieur Regnault. Regnault explains the house belonged to the late Comtesse de Merret, whose will forbade entry or repairs for fifty years after her death.

Regnault recounts the Comtesse’s life: after the Count de Merret died she grew reclusive and the house fell into disrepair. On her deathbed she summoned Regnault to witness a will that left her estate to the Vendôme hospital with strict conditions about the property. Bianchon, engrossed in local gossip, learns from his landlady, Madame Lepas, of a young Spanish prisoner linked to the Comtesse’s fate.

A parallel account describes the mysterious disappearance of Count Férédia, a Spanish grandee. When he fails to return one night, his clothes are found by the river, suggesting escape. She suspects the Count’s involvement in Madame de Merret’s death and wrestles with keeping his money. Intrigued by Rosalie, the maid who knows key secrets, the narrator pursues the truth and uncovers a tragic confrontation between the Count and the Comtesse. The story closes on reflections about the moral complexities surrounding La Grande Bretèche.
