For souls given to effusiveness there is a delightful hour between day and night; twilight softens lights and reflections, favoring a dreamy mood wedded to the play of light and shade. The prevailing silence charms artists, who grow contemplative, step back from pictures they can no longer work on, and pass judgment, rapt by meanings that flash on the inner eye of genius. He who has never stood pensive by a friend in such a poetic hour can hardly grasp its soothingness. Favored by the clear-obscure, material skill used to produce illusion entirely disappears. If the work is a picture, the figures seem to speak and walk; shade is shadow, light is day; flesh breathes, eyes move, blood flows, and fabrics change sheen. Imagination heightens every realistic detail and sees only the work’s beauties. At that hour illusion reigns; it wakens a mind’s night, peopled by dreams, and bears the soul aloft to a world of fancies where the artist forgets the real world—yesterday, tomorrow, everything, miseries and goods alike.

At this magic hour a young, talented painter who saw in art nothing but Art itself was perched on a step-ladder working on a large, almost finished painting. Criticising and admiring himself, floating on the current of his thoughts, he lost himself in a meditative mood that elevated and soothed the soul. His reverie lasted long. Night fell. Whether he meant to come down or made some misjudged movement believing himself on the floor—the cause escaped him—he fell, struck his head on a footstool, lost consciousness, and lay motionless for an unknown time.

A sweet voice roused him. When he opened his eyes a bright light made him close them again; through the mist he heard two women whispering and felt two timid hands supporting his head. He soon recovered; by the light of an old-fashioned Argand lamp he saw the most charming face he had seen, a head that for an artist realized the ideal beauty he had formed for himself. Her features recalled the refined delicacy of Prudhon and the poetic sentiment of Girodet: fresh temples, regular eyebrows, pure outline, virginal innocence stamped on every feature. Her figure was slight and graceful. Her dress, simple and neat, revealed neither wealth nor penury.

As he came to, the painter expressed admiration and fumbling thanks. A handkerchief pressed to his forehead; amid the studio smell he recognized the strong odor of ether used to revive him. He saw an old woman, looking like a marquise of the old school, who held the lamp and advised the young girl.

“Monsieur,” said the younger woman in answer to a few questions the painter asked while still vague from the shock, “my mother and I heard the noise of your fall and fancied we heard a groan. The silence after the crash alarmed us, and we hurried up. Finding the key in the latch, we entered and found you lying motionless. My mother fetched what was needed to bathe your head and revive you. You have cut your forehead—there. Do you feel it?”

“Yes, I do now,” he replied.

“Oh, it will be nothing,” said the old mother. “Happily your head rested against this lay-figure.”

“I feel infinitely better,” replied the painter. “I need nothing further but a hackney cab to take me home. The porter’s wife will go for one.”

He tried to repeat his thanks, but the elder lady interrupted at every sentence: “Tomorrow, monsieur, pray be careful to put on leeches, or to be bled, and drink a few cups of something healing. A fall may be dangerous.”

The young girl stole glances at the painter and the pictures. Her expression revealed perfect propriety; her curiosity seemed rather an absence of mind, and her eyes spoke the natural interest women feel in suffering. The two strangers seemed to forget the painter’s works in his mishap. When he reassured them they left, looking at him with anxious kindness free from insistence or familiarity, without indiscreet questions or invitations. Their noble and simple manners made a quiet impression on the painter; later, recalling details of the incident, he was greatly struck by them.

On the landing the old lady gently observed, “Adelaide, you left the door open.”

“That was to hear me fall,” said the painter with a grateful smile.

“You came down just now, mother,” replied the young girl with a blush.

“Would you like us to accompany you downstairs? The stairs are dark.”

“No, thank you, madame; I am much better.”

“Hold tightly by the rail.”

The two women remained to light him, listening to his steps.

To appreciate the interest this scene aroused, the painter had only a few days earlier established his studio in the attics of this house in the darker, muddier part of the Rue de Suresnes, almost opposite the Church of the Madeleine and near his rooms in the Rue des Champs-Elysees. Fame had recently made him one of the artists most dear to his country; he was beginning to feel free from want, and, as he put it, was enjoying his last privations. Instead of working in cheaper studios near the city gates he had spared himself the long walk and the loss of time now more valuable than before.

No man inspired more interest than Hippolyte Schinner, though he did not lightly reveal the secrets of his life. He was the idol of a necessitous mother who had raised him amid severe privations. Mademoiselle Schinner, daughter of an Alsatian farmer, had never married. Her gentle soul had been cruelly crushed long ago by a rich man who showed no delicacy in his love affairs. The day she lost her illusions was an age of reflection, religious thought, and resignation. She refused the betrayer’s alms, renounced the world, and made a glory of her shame. She devoted herself to maternal love, sought in it her joys instead of social pleasures, worked, and saved a treasure for her son. In after years one day amply repaid her for those sacrifices.

At the last exhibition her son received the Cross of the Legion of Honor. Newspapers hailed an unknown genius, artists acknowledged Schinner as a master, and dealers covered his canvases with gold. At twenty-five Hippolyte, to whom his mother had transmitted her woman’s soul, understood his standing. Eager to restore to his mother the pleasures society had denied her, he lived for her, hoping that fame and fortune would one day make her happy, rich, respected, and surrounded by people of mark. Schinner chose friends among the honorable and distinguished; fastidious in selection, he desired to raise still further a position his talent had placed high. His solitary work from boyhood had left him with beautiful beliefs that grace early days. His adolescent soul was not closed to the shy emotions that make a young man an apart being, full of poetry and virginal hopes, puerile to the worldly but deep for their singleness.

He had gentle, polite manners that spoke to the soul and fascinated even those who did not understand them. His voice, from the heart, stirred noble sentiments and testified to his modesty by ingenuous tones. Those who saw him felt an attraction of moral nature they could not analyze; they might call it galvanism or attempt to express it in formulas.

These details help explain why, in the absence of the porter whom he had sent to the end of the Rue de la Madeleine for a coach, Hippolyte did not question the porter’s wife closely about the two women who had tended him. He replied to her natural inquiries about his accident and the fourth-floor tenants but could not stop her chatter, led by lodge policy and gossip.

“Ah,” she said, “they were, no doubt, Mademoiselle Leseigneur and her mother, who have lived here four years. We do not know exactly what these ladies do; in the morning, till noon, an old woman who is half deaf comes in to help them; in the evening, two or three old gentlemen with ribbon loops, like you, monsieur, come to see them and often stay very late. One comes in a carriage with servants; they say he has sixty thousand francs a year. However, they are very quiet tenants, as you are, monsieur; and economical! they live on nothing, and pay as soon as a letter is brought. It is queer, monsieur, the mother’s name is not the same as the daughter’s. Ah, but when they go for a walk in the Tuileries, mademoiselle is very smart, and she never goes out without being followed by a lot of young men; but she shuts the door in their face, and she is quite right. The proprietor would never allow—”

The coach came; Hippolyte heard no more and went home. His mother dressed his wound and would not allow him to return to the studio the next day. After advice and treatments, Hippolyte remained at home three days. During this enforced idleness his fancy recalled, bit by bit, the scene of his fainting fit. The young girl’s profile returned clearly against the darkness of his inward vision; he saw the mother’s faded features again, felt Adelaide’s touch. He remembered some gesture whose grace memory enhanced; then an attitude, a tone of voice rising like objects from deep waters to the surface.

When he could resume work he went early to his studio; really he hurried to visit his neighbors. When passion wakes, inexplicable pleasures are felt; he mounted the fourth-floor stairs slowly, and his heart throbbed as he neared the humble brown door of Mademoiselle Leseigneur. He fancied similarity between their positions, imagined misfortunes of birth akin to his own. He lingered at the studio to think of them, dined there, then about seven o’clock went to call.

No painter of manners has sufficiently told the curious privacy of certain Parisian existences: fresh elegant toilettes and brilliant women whose homes betray doubtful comfort. If my picture is sometimes bold, blame the truth; the appearance of his neighbors’ rooms greatly influenced Hippolyte Schinner’s feelings and hopes.

The house belonged to one of those proprietors who abhor repairs and decoration, who regard being a Paris homeowner as business. Between miser and usurer, such men are allergic to moving a cupboard or opening an air-hole; when chimneys fall they deprive themselves of theatre nights “on account of repairs.” Hippolyte, who had seen a comical scene about decorative repairs, was not surprised by dark greasy paint, oily stains, and other disagreeable woodwork signs. These stigmata of poverty are not without a sort of poetry to an artist.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur opened the door. Recognizing the young artist she bowed and, with Parisian tact and pride, turned to shut a glass partition through which Hippolyte might have seen linen hung over patent ironing stoves, an old camp-bed, charcoal and irons, a filter, household crockery—utensils of a small household. Muslin curtains screened this capharnaum, lighted by windows facing a neighboring yard.

With an artist’s quick eye Hippolyte saw the uses, furniture, and general condition of this cut-in-half first room. The more honorable half, serving as ante-room and dining-room, was hung with old salmon-rose paper with a flock border patched with wafers. Prints of the battles of Alexander, in gilding-rubbed frames, were arranged on the walls. In the middle stood a massive, old-fashioned mahogany table worn at the edges. A small stove with a thin pipe stood before the chimney, whose hearth was blocked by a cupboard. Chairs showed remains of former splendor—carved mahogany, red morocco seats, gilt nails—scarred like a veteran.

This room was a museum of objects that show both luxury and penury: a splendidly finished telescope hung over a discolored glass on the chimney. Between chimney and partition was a wretched painted sideboard pretending to be mahogany. Slippery red quarries, shabby rugs, and all the furniture shone with hard cleanliness that accentuated oldness. An indescribable odor mixed exhalations from the lumber-room, dining-room vapors, and stair smells, though the window was partly open. Street air fluttered dusty curtains, carefully drawn to hide a window bay where former tenants left ornamental additions—a sort of domestic fresco.

Adelaide hastened to open the inner room door and announced the painter with obvious pleasure. Hippolyte, who had earlier seen similar poverty in his mother’s home, noted these signs with an impression vivid as earliest memories and entered with sympathy rather than scorn.

“Well, monsieur, I hope you no longer feel the effects of your fall,” said the old lady, rising from an antique armchair and offering a seat.

“No, madame. I have come to thank you for the kind care you gave me, and above all mademoiselle, who heard me fall.”

As he spoke—words stamped with the exquisite awkwardness of first love—Hippolyte looked at the young girl. Adelaide lit the Argand lamp, replacing a tallow candle in a large copper candlestick, then placed the lamp on the chimney shelf and sat a little behind the painter to look at him at her ease while appearing interested in the lamp. The flame sputtered in the damp chimney. Seeing the large mirror on the chimney-piece, Hippolyte fixed his eyes on it to admire Adelaide; her stratagem only embarrassed them both.

While talking with Madame Leseigneur—Hippolyte called her so—he examined the room unobtrusively. Egyptian figures on iron fire-dogs were scarcely visible under cinders; two brands struggled before a sham log. An old Aubusson carpet, faded and mended, left the tiled floor cold. Walls were hung with reddish paper imitating figured silk. Opposite the windows he saw a crack and the paper outline of double doors closing a recess where Madame Leseigneur likely slept, poorly disguised by a sofa. Above a mahogany chest of drawers hung a portrait of an officer of rank, so dim he could not judge it well; from what he saw it must have been painted in China. Window-curtains of red silk were as faded as the furniture in red-and-yellow worsted work. On the chest’s marble top was a costly malachite tray with a dozen magnificently painted coffee cups, perhaps Sevres. On the chimney shelf stood an Empire clock—a warrior driving four horses on a chariot whose wheel bore the hours. Tall candlesticks held smoke-yellowed tapers; porcelain vases crowned with dusty artificial flowers stood in the corners.

In the middle of the room a card-table with new packs suggested a painful dilemma: either these two women were perfectly honest or they lived by intrigue and gambling. Yet in looking at Adelaide, so pure-minded, Schinner could not but believe in her innocence and attribute the furniture’s incoherence to honorable causes.

“My dear,” said the old lady to the young one, “I am cold; make a little fire, and give me my shawl.”

Adelaide fetched a cashmere shawl—once costly, now faded and darned—and wrapped her mother in it. She then brought a bundle of small wood from the lumber-room and gallantly threw it on the fire to revive it.

It is hard to reproduce the ensuing conversation. Guided by the tact born of early hardship, Hippolyte did not remark on his neighbors’ poverty; any direct question would be indiscreet. The simplest pity can offend, so his thoughts and words diverged. The two ladies began by talking of painting; women easily guess the embarrassment of a first call and supply devices to end it. Questioning him about his art emboldened him to talk, and the chat’s light nothings led Hippolyte to reflections that revealed his habits and mind. Trouble had prematurely faded the old lady’s face; only prominent features remained, an outline suggesting shrewdness with grace in the eyes—an expression of the old Court that can hint either at artifice or refined delicacy.

A woman’s face hides mystery: the line between frankness and duplicity, intrigue and heart, is inscrutable. A penetrating eye might discern differences; such appreciation lies in intuition alone. The old lady’s face, like her room, made it hard to tell whether the squalor covered vice or highest virtue. But at Schinner’s age the heart’s first impulse is to believe in goodness. As he studied Adelaide’s noble, almost haughty brow and soulful eyes, he breathed the modest fragrance of virtue. He seized an opportunity to discuss portraits and thus examine the faded pastel whose chalk had fallen away.

“You are attached to that picture for the sake of the likeness, no doubt, mesdames, for the drawing is dreadful?” he said, looking at Adelaide.

“It was done at Calcutta, in great haste,” replied the mother, her voice agitated.

She gazed at the formless sketch with the absorption memories of happiness bring; in her face were traces of perennial regret. The painter interpreted her attitude as the mark of a life once happy. He then sat down beside her.

“Madame,” he said, “in a very short time the colors of that pastel will disappear. The portrait will only survive in your memory. Will you allow me to reproduce the likeness on canvas? It will be more permanently recorded. Grant me, as a neighborly favor, the pleasure of doing you this service. There are times when an artist is glad of a respite from greater undertakings by doing work of less lofty pretension; it will be a recreation for me.”

The old lady flushed at the offer, and Adelaide shot a glance of deep feeling. Hippolyte wanted some tie to his neighbors, to gain a right to mingle in their life. His offer appealed to the heart’s affections, gratified his pride as an artist, and could not wound the ladies. Madame Leseigneur accepted, without eagerness or reluctance, with the self-possession of a noble soul, aware of the character of bonds formed by such an obligation.

“I fancy,” said the painter, “that the uniform is that of a naval officer.”

“Yes,” she said, “that of a captain in command. Monsieur de Rouville—my husband—died at Batavia from a wound received in a fight with an English ship off the Asiatic coast. He commanded a frigate of fifty-six guns; the Revenge carried ninety-six. The struggle was unequal, but he defended his ship bravely, held out till nightfall and got away. When I returned to France Bonaparte was not yet in power, and I was refused a pension. When I applied again, quite lately, I was sternly informed that if the Baron de Rouville had emigrated I should not have lost him; that by this time he would have been a rear-admiral; finally, his Excellency quoted degrees of forfeiture. I took this step, urged by friends, only for Adelaide’s sake. I have always hated holding out my hand as a beggar in the name of a grief that deprives a woman of voice and strength. I do not like this money valuation for blood irreparably spilt—”

“Dear mother, this subject always does you harm.”

The Baronne Leseigneur bowed and was silent.

“Monsieur,” said the young girl, “I had supposed a painter’s work was generally fairly quiet?”

Schinner colored, remembering the noise he had made. Adelaide rose at the sound of a carriage stopping at the door. She went into her own room and returned with a pair of tall gilt candlesticks with partly burnt wax candles, which she quickly lit. Without waiting for the bell she opened the outer door where she set the lamp down. The sound of a kiss given and received touched Hippolyte’s heart. His impatience to see the man who treated Adelaide familiarly was not immediately gratified; the newcomers conversed in undertone with the young girl.

At last Mademoiselle de Rouville returned, followed by two men whose costumes and countenances need a long tale. The first, about sixty, wore one of those coats fashioned for Louis XVIII.: neither civil nor military, it might pass for either. Fleurs-de-lis embroidered the lapels and gilt buttons bore the same. Shoulder straps begged for useless epaulettes; a buttonhole blossomed with many colored ribbons. He probably always carried his three-cornered hat with a gold cord, for his powdered hair showed no traces of pressure. He might be taken for fifty and seemed robust. His frank, loyal emigre expression hinted also at libertine habits, the reckless gallantry of the Musketeers. His gestures and manner showed no intention of renouncing royalism, religion, or love affairs.

A striking figure stood behind the portrait of “Louis XIV’s light infantry”—the Bonapartists’ nickname for these old Monarchy survivors. He ought to have been the picture’s principal subject, yet he was merely an accessory: a lean, dry man dressed like the first but seeming his reflection—older coat, less white powder, dimmer fleurs-de-lis, more hopeless shoulder-straps; his intellect feebler, his life nearer its end. He was, as Rivarol quipped about Champcenetz, “the moonlight” of the other. The painter found this speechless Chevalier mysterious. He spoke to no one and was spoken to by none. Friend, relation, servant, protector—his role was obscure. At the Baronne de Rouville’s he provoked curiosity without satisfying it. Who, after the Restoration, could recall an attachment that before the Revolution had bound him to the dead woman who had been his friend’s wife?

The leader of the group, least dilapidated, gallantly kissed Madame de Rouville’s hand and sat by her. The Chevalier bowed and stood a couple of chairs away. Adelaide stood behind the old gentleman’s armchair, leaning her elbows on the back, unconsciously recalling Guerin’s Dido’s sister.

Though the old gentleman’s familiarity was fatherly, it seemed to annoy the girl. “What, are you sulky with me?” he asked, then shot Schinner a shrewd, diplomatic glance that asked, “Is he one of us?”

“This is our neighbor,” said the Baronne, indicating Hippolyte. “Monsieur is a celebrated painter; despite your indifference to the arts, you must have heard his name.”

The old man saw the suppressed name and bowed. “Certainly. I heard much about his pictures at the last Salon. Talent has immense privileges,” he added, noting the artist’s red ribbon. “That distinction, which we must earn at the cost of blood and long service, you win in youth; but all glory is of the same kind,” he said, touching his Cross of Saint-Louis.

Hippolyte murmured thanks and fell silent, content to admire Adelaide. Her face gleamed in his mind; he forgot the extreme misery of the room. He answered briefly, inwardly divided between what he heard and what he felt—hope pouring out wild visions of happiness. He refused to analyze the evening’s small incidents.

After a while, the Baroness and daughter began to play cards with the old gentleman. The Chevalier, faithful to his shadow role, stood behind, watching the game and responding with nods and repeated gestures. The gentleman complained of bad luck; Adelaide sweetly offered advice. At last, the game ended. The old man threw two louis on the table in temper: “Forty francs—the exact sum. Deuce take it! It is eleven o’clock.”

“It is eleven o’clock,” repeated the silent figure, looking at the painter.

Hearing this, Hippolyte thought it time to go. He found a few commonplace remarks, took leave, and left wholly possessed by the first raptures of true love, without analyzing the evening.

The next day he longed to see Adelaide again. He might have come at six but waited until the afternoon. Blushing, he asked Mademoiselle Leseigneur for the Baron’s portrait. “But come in,” Adelaide said; she had heard him. Tongue-tied, he felt happiness dull his wit. To see Adelaide, to hear her skirt’s rustle after longing all morning, made trifles precious. The old lady was out. Adelaide brought a chair to reach the picture; finding she could not reach without stepping on a chest, she blushed and asked, “Will you get it down?”

He read her modesty, returned a look of mutual intelligence—love’s sweetest language. Adelaide cast down her eyes. Hippolyte, almost timid, took down the picture, examined it, and left saying only, “I will return it soon.” The brief contact set long ripples in both hearts.

Back in his studio he prepared a fresh canvas and, with ardor, worked until dinner. That evening he returned, stayed from nine till eleven, and, a little bolder, chatted with Adelaide. A week passed and their feelings slowly deepened into mutual understanding. Each day Adelaide’s greeting grew more intimate; they laughed and talked with the innocence of newly made friends. Schinner learned piquet poorly and lost often. Without a word of love they knew they were everything to each other: Hippolyte enjoyed his power over his gentle friend; Adelaide, timid and devoted, permitted many concessions. Familiarity with the old Count waned: Adelaide perceived the painter’s melancholy when the old man unceremoniously kissed her hand or throat.

Mademoiselle Leseigneur began to expect accounts of the painter’s visits and scolded him for absences; he ceased other friendships and lived chiefly in the small apartment that enchanted him. Adelaide’s jealousy flared when she heard he still visited Paris salons at eleven; she declared such life bad for his health and argued that a man who spent charms on several women could not love warmly. Driven by passion and Adelaide’s demands, the painter lived almost exclusively with them.

Never was love purer. Both were trusting and delicate; their passion grew without sacrifice. Two months after meeting, their lives were nearly one: Adelaide could tell by his footsteps that he was home; he always looked in when he returned to his mother and flew back at evening. He thought the ideal she dreamed of was realized.

The old gentleman came less often; Hippolyte, once jealous, had taken his place at the green table and shared his constant ill-luck. Yet sometimes, in happiness, Madame de Rouville’s desperate poverty haunted him. “Strange—twenty francs every evening?” he wondered, and dared not vocalize his odious suspicions.

He spent two months on Adelaide’s portrait; when finished, varnished, and framed, he considered it one of his best. Madame de Rouville had not mentioned it again. Was it indifference or pride? He would not analyze her silence. Joyfully plotting with Adelaide, he planned to hang it while the Baroness was out. One day, while her mother walked in the Tuileries, Adelaide came to his studio to see the portrait in its light. She stood speechless in rapt admiration. He leaned forward uneasily; she held out her hand, two tears fell, and he kissed it. They stood mute, longing to confess love but not daring. Overcome, they went downstairs and hung the picture. Hippolyte dined with the Baroness, who, in tears, embraced him.

That evening the old émigré, the Baron de Rouville’s comrade, visited to announce a promotion to vice-admiral; his land travels through Germany and Russia had been counted as naval campaigns. Seeing the portrait, he shook Hippolyte’s hand and exclaimed he would give five hundred pistoles to see himself as well depicted. The Baroness, grateful, looked at Hippolyte. He suspected the admiral meant to buy both portraits; his pride and jealousy were offended, and he replied sharply that if he were a portrait-painter he would not have done this one. The admiral bit his lip and sat down to cards.

Hippolyte stayed near Adelaide, who proposed a dozen hands of piquet. As he played he noticed an unexpected eagerness in Madame de Rouville to win and finger the old man’s gold. Evil suspicions troubled him: could the Baroness live by gambling? Was she losing to pay debt or rent? Why would so rich a man frequent a fourth-story room where he lost some forty francs each evening? Why had he been familiar with Adelaide and then given up any claim?

These reflections led him to watch the old man and the Baroness more closely. Their sidelong glances at Adelaide displeased him. “Am I being duped?” became his last, horrible thought. He resolved to stay after the two old men left to confirm or dispel his fears. He drew out his purse to pay and, carried away by thought, laid it on the table and fell into reverie; then, ashamed, he rose, answered a commonplace question, and went close to the Baroness to look at her as she talked.

He stepped out, racked by doubts. A few steps down, he turned back to fetch the forgotten purse. “I left my purse here!” he cried to Adelaide.

“No,” she answered, reddening.

“I thought it was there,” he said, pointing to the card-table. Not finding it, shamed for Adelaide and the Baroness, he felt in his waistcoat and said, “I must have made a mistake.”

The purse held fifteen louis d’or and change. The theft seemed flagrant and was denied; Hippolyte no longer doubted his neighbors’ morals. He stood on the stairs, legs shaking, dizzy, unable to walk, struggling with his destroyed hopes. He recalled trifling observations that now confirmed his suspicions. Had they waited until the portrait was given them? Perhaps the Baroness had used gambling to hide her need. He even remembered Adelaide’s curiosity about a worn silk netting—had she been watching his purse? He briefly fantasized other motives—maybe the admiral hoped to marry Adelaide—but rejected them: if she intended to marry him, they would not have robbed Hippolyte.

Clinging to illusion, he told himself the purse must have fallen, then searched his memory and saw it distinctly on the green cloth. Yet he excused Adelaide, thinking misfortune might explain a degrading action; perhaps some secret misfortune forced them. He refused to accept the proud face as a lie.

The next morning he had not slept. The loss of the future they might have shared made deep inroads. To lose a dreamed future is keener than losing real happiness. Thus Hippolyte wandered under the Tuileries’ shade, absorbed and forgetful.

There he met François Souchet, a schoolmate about to go to Italy. “Why, Hippolyte, what ails you?” the sculptor asked.

“I am most unhappy,” Hippolyte admitted.

“Only a love affair can make you so,” Souchet replied. Led into confidences, Hippolyte told him of the Rue de Suresnes and the young girl on the fourth floor. Souchet laughed and recalled what others knew: the mother’s appearance gave away her station. Jokes and mockery followed; Hippolyte’s reticence made him suffer as his secret was handled lightly. He pretended contradiction, but the jesting continued. “Come at four and study their walk,” Souchet advised.

Torn, Hippolyte left his friends, convinced Adelaide must be superior to such accusations and filled with remorse for suspecting her. His love was such that, despite the theft, he still worshiped her. He thought of playing the rescuer of a woman deserted by others—romantic and grand to his sensitive soul.

He sat in his studio, looking at the portrait without working, tears in his eyes. Night came; roused, he went downstairs, met the admiral, looked darkly, and fled. He had planned to see the ladies, but the admiral’s presence froze him. For the hundredth time he wondered what interested this prodigal, who had eighty thousand francs a year, in a fourth story room where he lost forty francs nightly; motives multiplied in his mind.

In the next days Hippolyte threw himself into work to distract himself; study consoled him though memories of tender hours persisted. One evening, leaving his studio, he saw the ladies’ door half open and Adelaide standing in the window recess. The door and staircase made it impossible not to be seen. He bowed coldly with a look of supreme indifference; yet thinking of the pain this coldness would cause, he shuddered. The week of disdain would have been terrible; perhaps the purse had been found and she had been seeking him. This hope filled him with remorse: perhaps Adelaide deserved an inquiry and at least justification. Ashamed of resisting his heart, he called that evening on Madame de Rouville.

All his suspicions vanished at the sight of Adelaide, pale and thin. “Good heavens! what is the matter?” he asked.

Adelaide gave him a look of deep melancholy that pained him. “You must have thought us indifferent?” said the Baroness.

“I was wrong,” he replied. “There are forms of pain we know not how to confide.”

“The sincerity and strength of friendship are not to be measured by time,” the Baroness observed sadly.

Hippolyte, blushing with shame, ascribed the loss to an accident. The evening was delicious; Adelaide could read his remorse, and though he could not confess his doubts, he came back more in love and seeking tacit forgiveness. Yet when the Baroness suggested a game—“Let us have our little game; my old friend Kergarouet will not let me off”—his fears revived. He sat at the table; Adelaide said he needed a partner.

Throughout the game the Baroness and her daughter exchanged looks that alarmed Hippolyte more because he was winning. At last a final hand left the lovers in the old lady’s debt. Reaching for money, he saw a purse Adelaide had slipped before him without his noticing; the poor child held the old one in her hand and, to keep her countenance, was looking into it for the money to pay her mother. The sight made blood rush to his heart; he was near fainting.

The substituted purse contained his fifteen gold louis. It was worked with gilt beads; rings and tassels showed Adelaide’s taste and the small hoard she had spent on it. It was an impossible, delicate way to show him that his gift could only be repaid by proof of affection.

Overcome, Hippolyte clasped Adelaide, pressed her to his heart, and kissed her; then, with an artist’s simple heartiness, he exclaimed, “I ask for her for my wife!” looking to the Baroness.

Adelaide looked half-wrothful; Madame de Rouville, somewhat astonished, considered her reply when a bell rang. The old vice-admiral entered, followed by his shadow and Madame Schinner. Alarmed by calumnies against Adelaide, Hippolyte’s mother had inquired and reported them to the admiral, who swore to punish the scoundrels.

Prompted by his wrath, the admiral explained his intentional losing at cards as his method to help the Baroness. After Madame Schinner paid her respects, the Baroness looked at Comte de Kergarouet, the Chevalier du Halga, Hippolyte, and Adelaide, and said warmly, “So we are a family party this evening.”
