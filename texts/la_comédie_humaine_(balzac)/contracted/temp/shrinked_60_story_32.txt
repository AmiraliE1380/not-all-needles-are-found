**The Two Brothers**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Monsieur Charles Nodier, a member of the French Academy. This book examines the hidden injustices of domestic life, where divine chance often replaces human justice, reflecting on the decline of paternal authority and the consequences of feminine weakness, urging a return to the moral foundations of marriage and family.

---

**Chapter I**  
In 1792, the townspeople of Issoudun viewed Dr. Rouget as malicious, believing he made his wife, Madame Rouget, unhappy. Their son, Jean-Jacques, was born first, followed by their daughter Agathe, whose arrival strained family dynamics. The Rougets were connected to the wealthy Descoings family, who profited from the wool trade. 

Descoings was imprisoned and executed during the Revolution, shocking the town. Madame Rouget’s health declined due to her husband’s neglect. Agathe was sent to Paris to live with her uncle, but he was unable to care for her. After inheriting the Descoings’ wealth, Dr. Rouget led a debauched life post his wife’s death in 1799, leaving Jean-Jacques unprepared for adulthood.

Agathe, devoted yet naive, faced misfortunes. Bridau, loyal to Napoleon, worked tirelessly until his death in 1808. Agathe received a pension but remained estranged from her family and did not contest her father’s will, losing her inheritance. 

Madame Descoings, now sixty-five, struggled with gambling addiction, worsening their financial situation. Agathe took drastic measures to pay off debts, selling possessions. The chapter highlights familial relationships, financial struggles, and the complexities of love and loyalty. In Paris, Madame Bridau lives modestly, consumed by grief and financial worries for her children, Philippe and Joseph, who face challenges reflecting their differing personalities.

In 1816, Joseph Bridau set up an art studio in his attic with support from his mother, Agathe, and financial backing from Madame Descoings. Both widows viewed painting as respectable. While Joseph studied under Gros and Schinner, political unrest in France led to conspiracies and riots. His brother Philippe became involved, was arrested, and later released, but his military career was jeopardized.

Agathe worried for Philippe's safety as he frequented disreputable cafes, while the Grecian sages advised sending him abroad. Philippe joined General Lallemand in the U.S., leading to the failed Champ d’Asile venture. Agathe invested ten thousand francs to support him, but upon his return in 1819, Philippe was hardened by his experiences and struggled with alcoholism.

Eager to help, Agathe bought him new clothes and indulged his drinking, despite her suspicions. Philippe's tales of woe masked his selfishness, alarming Agathe and Madame Descoings, who faced financial struggles. Philippe continued gambling and drinking, worsening their situation.

Philippe's social life revolved around old army comrades and gambling, becoming reckless. He pursued Mariette, a young dancer, while dreaming of political influence. Despite his failures, he sought to leverage his past for personal gain, planning to demand accountability for the funds raised for Champ d’Asile. His mother and Madame Descoings secured him a cashier position at a newspaper, but Philippe lived extravagantly without supporting them.

Bixiou informed Agathe and Madame Descoings of Philippe's infatuation with Mariette, shocking them as they viewed stage women as morally corrupt. Philippe proposed marriage, but she declined, seeking wealthier prospects. As Philippe distanced himself from his family, he indulged in a hedonistic lifestyle, leading to embezzlement from his job.

After Mariette left for London, Philippe returned home, grappling with despair over his financial situation. He confided in Joseph about his suicidal thoughts stemming from his embezzlement. Alarmed, Joseph urged him to reconsider, suggesting repayment could avert disgrace. Philippe fled, leaving his family distressed.

Philippe visited a gambling house, initially winning but ultimately losing everything. He returned home, feigning distress, and Agathe begged him not to harm himself. Agathe aged visibly from worry, realizing their dire financial situation. Madame Descoings suggested consulting a lawyer, Desroches, to manage the fallout from Philippe's actions.

Philippe resumed a life of leisure, while Joseph focused on his art, gaining recognition. Agathe remained preoccupied with Philippe, oscillating between hope and fear. Desroches assured them Philippe would soon be reinstated in the army, but warned that his gambling habits could jeopardize this opportunity. Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle, deepening their struggles.

Desperate for money, Philippe resorted to stealing from his family. Agathe recognized his thefts indicated a deeper moral failing. After a troubling dream where Philippe robbed her, she urged him to ask for money instead of stealing. However, within two weeks, Agathe was left with nothing and considered finding work.

As Agathe's health deteriorated from worry, Philippe continued his reckless lifestyle. On Christmas Eve, he planned to steal from Madame Descoings, taking twenty napoleons. He gambled and lost everything. When Philippe arrived home drunk for Christmas dinner, tensions escalated, leading to a confrontation with Agathe.

The next day, Madame Descoings learned her lottery ticket had won three million francs but was devastated to discover Philippe had stolen her money, leading to her stroke. As she lay ill, Philippe returned home, indifferent to the chaos he had caused. The confrontation escalated into violence, resulting in Philippe being restrained.

Madame Descoings passed away, leaving Agathe with a meager annuity. Philippe, now estranged from his family, continued his reckless lifestyle. By February 1822, Agathe had moved into a modest attic room, securing a job as an assistant in a lottery office. Despite her new routine, she found solace in stability.

Concerned for Philippe, Joseph visited him, but Philippe's behavior raised suspicions. One evening, Philippe stole a valuable painting from Joseph's studio. Months later, Agathe encountered Philippe on the street, giving him money despite knowing it would likely be squandered. Her health declined as she grappled with the pain of her son's choices.

When news broke of Philippe's involvement in a conspiracy, Agathe fainted. Joseph sought legal advice from Desroches, who urged them to secure their family's inheritance from Philippe's influence over their wealthy uncle, Jean-Jacques Rouget. Agathe wrote to her godmother for help but learned Rouget was unlikely to assist. Desroches advised Agathe to travel to Issoudun to protect her family's interests, prompting her to reflect on her estrangement from her brother.

Issoudun, with its rich history, faced social stagnation and decline due to resistance to change. The local bourgeoisie clung to medieval traditions, leading to a homogenous society with ineffective governance. Young men formed the "Knights of Idleness," engaging in pranks that terrorized the town, illustrating the interplay between social stagnation and youthful rebellion.

As Joseph and Madame Bridau traveled to Issoudun, the Knights of Idleness played a prank on Pere Fario, hoisting his cart onto an embankment. They returned to Mere Cognette’s for food and laughter, eager to see Fario's reaction.

Max, admired by Madame Hochon's grandsons Francois and Baruch, was warned about the arrival of Madame Bridau and her son, which could threaten his interests in Jean-Jacques Rouget's property, linked to Flore Brazier, Rouget's servant-mistress. Although Max initially dismissed the concern, he felt uneasy about Rouget's wealth being tied to Flore and the potential for legitimate heirs to disrupt his plans.

Francois speculated that if Rouget revoked his will in favor of Flore, it could impact Max's future. Max, confident in his relationship with Flore, sought the Knights' assistance in dealing with the Bridaus, aiming to secure his position without harming them. The Knights pledged loyalty, toasting to Flore and dismissing the Bridaus.

Max realized Flore had not informed him about her family, which could threaten their financial future. His affection for Flore was intertwined with her potential inheritance from Rouget, prompting him to act before the Bridaus could secure their claim.

Flore's backstory reveals she was taken in by Doctor Rouget as a child, receiving a better life than with her uncle. Rouget, nearing death, refused to leave her any inheritance, believing her beauty would ensure her future wealth. Jean-Jacques Rouget, socially awkward and timid, secretly loved Flore, but their relationship strained as she fell for Maxence Gilet.

When Madame Bridau returned to Issoudun, Rouget had lapsed into a semi-vegetative state, motivated solely by his affection for Flore. A commotion arose when Fario reported his cart missing, and Max feigned indifference while gathering a crowd.

As tensions rose, Francois informed Max of the Bridaus' imminent arrival. Max chose manipulation over open opposition, advising Rouget to welcome his family to avoid scandal. After breakfast, Max and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth against the Bridaus.

Agathe and Joseph arrived in Issoudun, where Agathe felt nostalgic. The Hochons scrutinized Joseph, whose appearance led to unflattering comments. The next morning, Joseph noted Flore's beauty despite her role in their family's troubles. A letter from Rouget invited Joseph to dinner, raising suspicions among the Hochons about Flore's manipulations.

At dinner, Joseph admired Flore, who encouraged Rouget to pay him for copies of significant paintings. News of Joseph's acquisition spread quickly, enhancing Flore's status. Meanwhile, Max and Flore strategized to keep the Bridaus at bay while benefiting from Rouget's wealth.

As the Bridaus struggled for Rouget's favor, Joseph received letters from painter Schinner and lawyer Desroches. Frustrated, he decided to return to Paris, asserting his independence by returning the paintings, which became gossip in Issoudun, leaving the Bridaus' future uncertain amid growing tension with Maxence and Flore.

################################################
Between midnight and one o’clock, the Knights of Idleness distributed food to the town's dogs, concluding their escapade around three in the morning. Max was attacked by Fario, who stabbed him before fleeing. Max cried out, prompting Lousteau-Prangin and Goddet to rush to his aid, but the townspeople, accustomed to false alarms, remained indifferent. After fainting, Max claimed to recognize Fario, leading Lousteau-Prangin to inform his father, the judge.

Max was taken home, where Goddet found the knife wound serious but not fatal. Seizing the moment, Max plotted to frame Joseph Bridau, the painter, for the attack. He instructed Flore to spread rumors of his impending death, hoping this would lead to Joseph's arrest and prompt his mother to flee to Paris.

Flore, feigning tears, accused Joseph of wanting Max dead to inherit from his uncle. The townspeople, stirred by her claims, began searching for Joseph, who had left Hochon’s house at dawn after a restless night. Alarmed by the crowd's anger, Hochon bolted his door, fearing for his gold. Meanwhile, Agathe, Joseph's mother, fainted upon hearing the news.

As the crowd grew, the police struggled to maintain order. Gendarmes arrived just in time to protect Joseph from the mob, who were convinced of his guilt. Despite his protests of innocence, Joseph was taken to the Palais for his safety. He recounted his whereabouts that morning, providing an alibi that could be verified by witnesses.

Initially skeptical, the magistrates agreed to investigate further but decided to detain Joseph for protection. He wrote a reassuring letter to his mother, calming her fears. Meanwhile, Max, recovering from his wound, plotted to keep Joseph imprisoned, believing it would rid them of the Parisians.

As the investigation progressed, Max's deception unraveled. Witnesses confirmed Joseph's alibi, and Max's own letter to the judge, detailing the assailant's shoes, further implicated him. Eventually, Joseph was released, warmly greeted by his relieved mother.

In the aftermath, the townspeople celebrated the departure of the Parisians as a victory. Joseph and Agathe returned to Paris, where Joseph's spirits lifted despite his brother Philippe's ongoing trial for actions during the political upheaval. Desroches, a lawyer, sought to help Philippe by arranging for his release and advising caution with Max, who was clever and dangerous. Ultimately, Philippe was released and prepared to confront Max and reclaim his family's legacy.

On November 2nd, Philippe Bridau returned to Issoudun, registering with the local police and lodging on rue l’Avenier. His return as a banished officer linked to a military conspiracy stirred excitement, especially since he was the brother of a wrongfully accused painter. Maxence Gilet, now recovered, had converted Pere Rouget’s mortgages into cash, raising concerns in town. Monsieur Hochon sought information from notary Monsieur Heron about Gilet's actions, worried for the Bridau family.

Gilet and Flore felt secure with Philippe's arrival, knowing they could manipulate old Rouget for his wealth. Philippe visited his uncle in worn clothing, reflecting his hardships, yet he maintained dignity, refusing financial help from Rouget due to past grievances. Flore, sensing Philippe's hostility, advised Rouget to avoid him.

Philippe later visited the Hochons, introducing himself and expressing his intent to live modestly on his limited income. He engaged in polite conversation, winning over Madame Hochon by discussing her nephew, journalist Lousteau. Aware of the town's gossip, Philippe suggested a private discussion with Hochon to avoid suspicion, aiming to connect with other officers of the old army who opposed Gilet.

Philippe soon acquired a new wardrobe on credit and befriended Mignonnet and Carpentier, fellow officers disapproving of Gilet. He shared insights about the failed conspiracy to overthrow the Bourbons, earning respect among the officers while plotting to undermine Gilet and secure his uncle's fortune.

As Philippe ingratiated himself with the local elite, he maintained a facade of respectability, contrasting with Gilet's dubious reputation. He frequently dined with Mignonnet and Carpentier, solidifying his community position. Meanwhile, Gilet viewed Philippe as a rival.

Philippe discovered that Gilet's associates, including Rouget's grandsons, were involved in dubious activities, which he reported to Hochon. This revelation heightened tensions, as Gilet and Flore plotted to secure Rouget's wealth. Philippe confronted his uncle about Flore's influence, insisting on a private discussion.

After a tense exchange, Philippe met Hochon, who informed him that Rouget had not signed any power of attorney, preventing Gilet from accessing his funds. Philippe planned to monitor his uncle and Gilet closely, determined to protect his family's interests. Alarmed by Philippe's boldness, Flore relayed the details to Gilet, who proposed a risky plan to manipulate Rouget into signing a power of attorney.

As Philippe walked with Rouget along the boulevard Baron, he contemplated the impending clash between Colonel Bridau and Captain Gilet, both vying for Rouget's affections and fortune. Philippe expressed disdain for Gilet, revealing his plan to eliminate him to ensure Rouget's happiness with Flore. Despite Rouget's infatuation with Flore, Philippe urged him not to sign a power of attorney that would grant Gilet access to his wealth.

Rouget, easily swayed by Flore's charms, proved difficult for Philippe to convince. Philippe insisted he would deal with Gilet if necessary, promising to make Flore obedient to Rouget. Their conversation shifted when they encountered Mignonnet and Carpentier, who were aware of Rouget's precarious situation. Philippe concluded that either Gilet must die or he would have to kill Gilet himself to save his uncle.

Meanwhile, Gilet recognized the growing animosity against him in Issoudun due to his reckless behavior. He met with Captain Potel, who urged him to confront Bridau to restore his honor. However, Gilet was preoccupied with securing Rouget's wealth for himself and Flore.

At Rouget's home, Kouski, under Gilet's orders, feigned sorrow to inform Rouget that Flore had left with Vedie. This devastated Rouget, who searched for Flore in vain. Gilet attempted to maintain his reputation while plotting to keep Rouget under his control.

As rumors of Gilet's misdeeds spread, the townspeople began to turn against him. Potel expressed dismay over the division within the Imperial Guard, now pitted against itself due to Gilet's actions. Feeling cornered, Gilet resolved to confront Bridau, believing he could outmaneuver him in a duel.

Philippe, anticipating Gilet's next move, prepared to counteract his plans, suspecting Gilet would further manipulate Flore and Rouget. When Rouget returned home, he began drafting a letter to Flore, threatening to revoke his will if she did not return immediately.

Seeing an opportunity, Philippe convinced Rouget to send the letter with Carpentier for personal delivery to Flore, believing this would bring her back and restore Rouget's authority.

As evening approached, Philippe strategized to ensure Flore's compliance and Rouget's happiness. He planned to take Gilet's place in Rouget's life, promising to keep Flore in line. When Flore returned, Philippe confronted her, asserting his dominance and warning her of the consequences if she did not comply with Rouget's needs.

The next day, gossip about the impending duel between Philippe and Gilet spread throughout Issoudun, with many speculating on the outcome. Flore, realizing the stakes, began to act more affectionately towards Rouget to secure her position. However, Gilet remained defiant, unwilling to back down from the challenge posed by Philippe. As tensions escalated, the townspeople rallied around Philippe, hoping for a resolution that would restore order and honor to the Imperial Guard, setting the stage for a dramatic confrontation that would determine their fates.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The day after the coronation banquet, officers at Lacroix's restaurant discussed Max Gilet's dismissal. Tensions escalated when Max arrived, leading to a duel with Colonel Philippe Bridau, who disarmed Max but both were seriously injured. Philippe delivered a fatal blow, ending Max's ambitions and the Order of Idleness.

As Philippe recovered, he plotted to marry Max's former lover, Flore Brazier, to secure his inheritance after she fell ill. With his mother Agathe's support, Flore agreed to marry Rouget, ensuring her a substantial dowry.

Philippe returned to Paris with Rouget, who soon died from indigestion. By October 1823, Philippe settled Rouget's estate, netting 1.6 million francs, which he invested. By March 1824, he married Madame Rouget, becoming Comte de Brambourg.

By January 1827, Philippe sought further honors, distancing himself from his past, while Joseph Bridau struggled as an artist. After Agathe's death, Joseph learned of Philippe's indifference and Flore's suffering. Philippe faced scrutiny during the July Revolution of 1830 and tragically died in battle in 1839. Joseph inherited Philippe's estate, finding solace in art and friendships, ultimately becoming Comte de Brambourg, an ironic title given his brother's legacy.
