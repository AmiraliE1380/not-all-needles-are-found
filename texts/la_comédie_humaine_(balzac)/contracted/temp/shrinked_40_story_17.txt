La Grenadiere is a quaint house on the right bank of the Loire, about a mile downstream from the bridge of Tours. The broad river, dotted with green islands, runs between cliffs crowned with white stone country houses, gardens and terraced vineyards warmed by southern exposure. A church spire marks Saint-Cyr; further on the Choisille joins the Loire.

Halfway up the hillside and a hundred paces from the church, La Grenadiere—an old Touraine house of two to three hundred years—is reached by a fissure in the rock providing steps down to the dike of the Paris–Nantes causeway. A gate opens onto a narrow footpath between terraces; trees partly conceal the house until the river view widens. A crumbling Gothic arch leads into a small garden hewn from rock on the highest terrace beside a wooden summer-house draped in jessamine and honeysuckle. Vine-clad steps lead to an arched doorway into cellars carved in the rock. Trellised vines and pomegranate trees—the house’s namesake—frame the yellowwashed front with two large windows, a rustic door, three dormers in a steep slate roof, and green shutters.

Inside, a crooked staircase rises to a lobby; to the right a large dining room with white tile floors, to the left a sitting room with saffron walls and exposed walnut rafters. The first floor has two whitewashed bedrooms with simple stone chimneypieces. Most openings face south; a later timber-framed kitchen with a pump and well suggests the house began as a vintner’s retreat.

The property includes two acres of vineyard, a steep back slope and a vinedresser’s cottage against the western gable. Stone walls and espaliers maximize the land while neglected corners fill with figs, wildflowers and strawberries. Modest yet dignified, La Grenadiere yields grapes, figs, peaches and melons; Spanish broom and Italian oleanders add exotic notes. The Loire flows below; evening breezes and shifting light reveal the south bank, Tours and its châteaux. Owned by a prosperous burgess of Tours, it remains a peaceful retreat for lovers and dreamers, where the river’s sounds invite quiet contemplation among perennial flowers and fruit.

If an Englishman rents La Grenadiere for the summer, he pays a thousand francs for six months; if he wants orchard fruit the rent doubles, and for the vintage it doubles again. With its stone staircase, triple terrace, two acres of vineyard, flowering roses, and well-head, and a view over three valleys of Touraine and cathedral towers, its health and beauty seem beyond price. Unsold since 1690 when it fetched forty thousand francs, it has remained a cherished family possession.

In a bright spring of the Restoration, Mme. Willemsens arrived in Tours with her housekeeper and two children, thirteen and eight, and chose La Grenadiere—perhaps for its distance from town. She turned the drawing-room into her bedroom, gave the children the two rooms above, and had the housekeeper sleep in a closet. The dining-room served as sitting and drawing room, simply furnished in plain walnut; the house’s charm lay in neatness and harmony rather than luxury.

Mme. Willemsens resisted easy classification: her plain dress invited assumptions, while her polished manners suggested higher rank. Tall and slender, with delicate features, a melancholy expression, dark eyes that sometimes revealed secret anguish and premature wrinkles, she always wore a long black gown with a silk ribbon and an English-style bonnet, signaling mourning. Her reserve provoked local curiosity; she visited Tours only twice—to inquire about teachers and to arrange lessons—and her rare appearances on the bridge drew attention. The owner said she signed the lease as Augusta Willemsens, Countess of Brandon, hinting at her husband’s name; the remark circulated in the business community.

To the local elite she remained a mystery: natural manners and a sweet voice charmed people, but her sadness and beauty seemed untouchable. Several young men fell in love yet kept away; she burned all letters, underscoring her wish for solitude.

The three tutors admired the close bond between Mme. Willemsens and her children. The boys resembled their mother and drew envy: Louis-Gaston, dark-haired and robust, and Marie-Gaston, almost golden-haired and delicate. Their contrasting looks matched temperaments—Louis protective of his brother. Well-behaved and attentive, they rose early, prayed, and kept a scrupulous routine. Their interactions with their mother were full of affection and intuitive understanding; she fostered a nurturing home, though Louis often noticed small signs of her declining strength and remained watchful.

Mme. Willemsens dressed during her children’s early breakfast and play to be a pleasing sight for them. She listened to their lessons from ten until three, breaking for a midday meal in the summer-house where they all ate together. After lunch the children played while she rested on a sofa, enjoying the Touraine landscape.

The children ranged through the orchard and garden, chasing lizards and studying plants and insects, often returning with eager questions. Mme. Willemsens embroidered during lessons but followed them attentively, encouraging her eldest, Louis, and asking little about little Marie. She walked tutors to the gate and frankly queried Louis’ progress; they reported his strengths and weaknesses honestly.

Their days balanced work and play, leaving little room for quarrels. Her steady love smoothed difficulties and taught her sons moderation and submission. She often wept for joy watching them, treasuring the laughter and voices in the garden-house. Thanks to their English nurse they spoke both English and French, and their mother guided their learning with engaging biographies of great figures, using those stories to teach ambition and success.

Little Marie often fell asleep on her mother’s knee. Afterwards Mme. Willemsens would grow pensive and tearful; one evening she confessed fears of leaving them alone and without resources. Louis, puzzled, promised to take care of Marie. She reassured him that love sustained her and hoped Touraine’s air might aid recovery.

As she spoke more of possible death, Louis adjusted the household to her melancholy. The lively home grew quieter; the old servant Annette watched with concern as Mme. Willemsens’ health declined. By August she remained beautiful but frail, preserving her appearance as the house grew somber. In autumn she spent nearly all her time with the children, while Louis prepared late for the École Polytechnique, listened to a retired naval officer’s tales at the bridge, and—encouraged by his mother—took walks, climbed trees and learned to swim, his sunburned face showing renewed purpose.

When October came, Mme. Willemsens could only rise at noon. The Loire’s warmth, likened to the Bay of Naples and recommended by the faculty, softened the days. At midday she sat in the shade with her two children, Louis and Marie, who never left her. After lessons they sought closeness: the elder child lying beside her while restless Marie gathered flowers. With tired eyes and languid movements, Mme. Willemsens smiled at them—a poignant scene tinged with autumnal melancholy.

The doctor soon ordered her confined to her room, brightened daily by her favorite flowers, her children at her side. One early November day she played the piano for the last time, fingers moving with feeling beneath a Swiss landscape painting; outside, her children played. When the doctor insisted she stay in bed, she asked Louis to take her to the terrace for a final view of her homeland. She looked skyward, absorbed by the clouds, then, anguished, clutched her children’s hands and fretted over their uncertain future and how they might judge her later. Seeing them kneel beside her, she returned to bed and never rose again.

Days blurred with Annette and Louis keeping vigil. On the fifth day the doctor banned flowers—an ominous sign. One quiet Sunday night she drew the curtain and solemnly told Louis tomorrow would be her last. She had him fetch important papers—birth certificates and a letter to Lord Brandon, her estranged husband—which she dictated, hesitating over her signature and finally choosing “Louis-Gaston.” She revealed twelve thousand francs saved and urged him to keep it safe, warning everything would be sealed after her death. Louis vowed to care for Marie, send her to school, and apprentice at sea, determined to return successful.

Her eyes brightened at his resolve. Growing weak, she requested a priest and, surrounded by loved ones, received the sacrament. As her breathing slowed the room fell silent; she died.

Annette and the vinedresser’s wife prepared the body. Only a few mourners attended; Marie, too young to grasp finality, cried as they lowered the coffin, marked by a simple cross. The children returned to La Grenadiere for a last look before leaving with Annette to her cousin’s house in Tours. Louis entrusted Annette with Marie’s birth certificate and the money, sent Marie to school, gave earnest advice, and departed to apprentice on a warship. A month later, watching the French coast fade, he embraced life at sea resolved to honor his mother’s memory, comforted by an old sailor’s words.
