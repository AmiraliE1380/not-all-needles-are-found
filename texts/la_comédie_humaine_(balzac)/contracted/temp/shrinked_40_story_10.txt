In Paris, balls often split into a formal round of polite small talk and a later, intimate salon where genuine conversation and wit thrive—hosted by women like Madame d’Espard and Mademoiselle des Touches. At one such evening, guests including the Minister de Marsay shared lively discourse.

De Marsay recounted a youthful, passionate affair with a woman six years older who publicly appeared indifferent but privately signaled affection with the flowers she carried. When illness forced him to cancel a meeting, jealousy led him to go anyway and discover her with another man. Her calm tenderness briefly soothed him, but the sight ignited a storm of feeling. Confronted with betrayal, de Marsay felt his innocence end, wrestled with revenge, and resolved that true love must transcend vengeance—prompting reflection on love, jealousy, social expectation, and fragile trust.

The narrator, freed from naïveté, began an affair with a charming heroine whose practiced beauty contrasted with his earlier deceptive angel. He avoided quarrels, suspecting refined hypocrisy could masquerade as virtue and that genuine love might not match the pleasures of her practiced deceit. Visiting a hair artisan in the Rue Boucher to commission a keepsake, he saw astonishing hair mementos and learned of passing trends. Suspicion flared when he recognized a handkerchief the artisan had worked on, confirming doubts about the authenticity of women’s affections. He left resolved to pursue pleasure but skeptical of love.

Two months later in the heroine’s boudoir he tested her. Charlotte professed exclusive devotion and claimed he was the only man in her world, but when he bluntly asked, “When are you to marry the Duke?” her startled reaction betrayed guilt. He suggested she should accept the Duke—rich, devout, proper. Kneeling, she swore before God she loved only him; he pressed her, revealing he'd seen her attend mass with the Duke. She dismissed his jealousy, alternately supplicating and vengeful, lamenting that had he spoken sooner they might have been happy. He insisted it was too late—that she had gone too far.

She feared the Duke’s suspicions; he advised showing devotion but also feigned the distressed lover until she asked his fate. Then he admitted making advances to another woman, surprising Charlotte, who accused him of unfaithfulness. He retorted that taking love seriously in their society was akin to romanticizing an actress. When she could not shake her bitterness even as a potential duchess, he felt he had successfully distanced himself—having learned how successful men withdraw.

Back in the salon, the women sympathized with Charlotte while de Marsay smiled. Conversation turned to the erosion of social distinctions: Lady Dudley observed that women often did not see marriages as inconstancy; de Marsay lamented the aristocracy’s decline—duchesses and marquises fading as the Civil Code leveled hierarchies and created a new class. The Comte de Vandenesse and Lord Dudley noted that anyone could claim nobility under the new order; unsettled property hastened the fall. Joseph Bridau lamented a decline in womanly beauty; Daniel d’Arthez argued society lost women who might have shaped it.

They debated the modern “perfect lady”: tasteful, graceful, poised but lacking the grandeur of past great ladies. Mademoiselle des Touches questioned whether this was progress; Madame de Nucingen argued a woman’s worth now lay more in character and presentation than lineage. Émile Blondet defined the perfect lady as effortlessly elegant and refined, commanding respect by bearing and demeanor. The dialogue captured modern femininity’s complexity—how charm, intellect, and presentation now often replace inherited splendor.

Blondet opens with a sharp caricature of the modern “perfect lady”: though married, she effectively conceals her husband. At a ball a middle-aged man with a decoration bows and departs; she orders her carriage and leaves, leaving an impression of delight rather than possession. At home she keeps strict hours, receives no one before afternoon, and arranges her house as a perpetual stage—warm staircases, fresh flowers (accepted only from favored suitors as symbols of promise), tasteful comforts, and fashionable trifles always renewed. She sits by the fire and lets visitors come to her; in public she dazzles, in private she calculates. Her salon is a tiny world where conversation clings to generalities because women are isolated and hungry for a sphere in which to shine.

Blondet argues that a woman’s intelligence in this milieu often consists in doubting everything, cultivating uncertainty as one of the few remaining graces. The townswoman is straightforwardly virtuous; the lady doubts and contends with virtue, discusses religion without practicing it, and is ready to use moral rhetoric to defend social institutions when convenient. Her discourse becomes a muddle of modern political and religious catchphrases—neo-Christian, neither fully Catholic nor Protestant, moralistic and contradictory. Women laugh at the satire, but Blondet presses on: a “perfect lady” feigns ignorance to extract confidences, arranges her passions like a musical score—careful pauses, modulations, and anonymous compromises—because she fears the ruin that could follow exposure. Name, position, and wealth no longer guarantee safety; the aristocracy offers little protection. Lacking the old grandeur that allowed earlier great ladies to triumph and crush, the modern woman must master subtleties and Jesuitical compromises, living between public propriety and private liberties. She is, Blondet says, never truly independent except in secrecy or theory; her chief task is to preserve her status.

Blondet imagines the plight of a woman repudiated by her husband: deprived of carriage, opera box, and social role, she is neither wife nor maid nor townswoman—adrift, vulnerable to calumny, with religious orders refusing her as scandalous. Thus the modern lady may provoke gossip, but rarely outright slander, because her cautious arrangements protect her more than frankness ever could. Blondet concludes that today’s combination of English hypocrisy and eighteenth-century frankness produces a degraded, transitional system: nothing that now appears truly resembles what has vanished. Great figures recede; distinctions become merely personal. He doubts that any woman, even of princely birth, can acquire before twenty-five the encyclopedic refinements—subtlety in manner, wit, innocence, mischief, silence, speech, diplomacy, and the artful balance—that made the perfect lady of the past.

Mademoiselle des Touches asks where the female author fits in this scheme. Blondet replies that an ungenial female author is precisely “a woman of whom there should not be one,” dryly echoing Napoleon. Canalis interrupts to defend Napoleon against Blondet’s epigram, pointing out Napoleon’s paradoxical genius: a phenomenal will who could create empires, laws, public works, and literature, yet was capricious, contradictory, and ultimately mortal in ordinary weakness. Canalis paints Napoleon as both brute force and subtle intellect—able to found codes and crowns, to take Europe by storm yet leave civilization unfixed—concluding with admiration for the man’s extraordinary, if inconsistent, greatness. De Marsay calls him “all despotism and all justice” at the proper moments, “the true king.”

Joseph Bridau observes that the conversational pleasures on offer are so rare and fleeting that one could not afford them if they were sold like music; every flash of wit is unique and unrepeatable. The Princesse de Cadignan pushes back gently at Blondet’s caricature: fashions and small forms do not mean the human heart has shrunk. Passions and sublime devotions exist still, though less publicized than in earlier eras; the lack of a grand public stage does not diminish depth. She asserts that contemporary women may rival historical figures—Madame Récamier can stand beside Mme. de Sévigné; Marquise d’Espard matches Madame Doublet or du Deffand; Taglioni equals Camargo; Malibran equals Saint-Huberti; poets of today match or surpass eighteenth-century ones. The Empire, she says, had its own stamp and splendor. Comte de Vandenesse amplifies this, praising Frenchwomen as enduringly significant and asserting that Blondet’s “perfect lady” may, when necessary, become heroic. He suggests that where feelings cross legal bounds, Providence often directs events, and he has seen women rise to greatness in ways that transcend their faults.

This praise prompts banter and compliments around the table, and a segue to General Montriveau’s story—a grim drama that illustrates the brutal realities behind honor, passion, and conscience. He recounts an episode from the 1812 campaign around the Beresina, when discipline collapsed and survivors wandered seeking shelter. Montriveau, hungry and cold, found a poor Polish farmhouse, its single room stowed with hay and a meager meal laid out. Inside were officers, a colonel, and a woman. The colonel is the vivid center of the tale: Italian by temperament, tall, powerful, with black curls and an aquiline nose, alternately tender-eyed and ferociously irascible, able to inspire both fear and devotion. His passions expressed themselves in violent, swift action: when another colonel’s battery struck their captain’s leg and badly wounded him, Montriveau watched the Italian colonel explode—foaming, roaring, and in two seconds split the other man’s skull with his sword. The soldiers were stunned.

Among those present was the wounded captain’s wife, Rosina, a Sicilian of striking dark beauty but ragged and exhausted. Her husband—an ironic, phlegmatic Piedmontese—had seemed oblivious to any scandal for years, but Montriveau sensed tension in him. At the farmhouse the colonel, after settling, called to Rosina with brusque possessive authority; she answered a gesture reflecting humiliation and perhaps foreboding. When the colonel summoned her again more brutally, she rose and went to him amid awkward silence; Montriveau laughed in discomfort, provoking a rebuke from the husband. That night they slept, and next morning each resumed the dreadful march. A short distance on, they saw the farmhouse in flames, barricaded, the air filled with smoke and cries. The captain returned to the caravan and, with a pointed gesture to his breast and a wave toward the burning house, said “Son’io” (I am the one). He walked on; they did not press him. Montriveau’s tale leaves the precise deed implied but not detailed—an act of vengeance or desperate protest—yet it shows how easily human dignity is crushed and how violence can follow humiliation. De Marsay comments bitterly that “there is nothing more terrible than the revolt of a sheep,” and the image haunts the company.

The conversation lightens briefly with laughter about English foibles and then turns to a softer closing vignette supplied by Doctor Bianchon. He recounts a moving bedside scene: a duchess dying of consumption, thin and luminous, having taken the sacrament. The Duke—an immense public man tired and complex—had fallen asleep at her bedside; when he awoke around dawn, despairing at his slip and how he might have missed the last moments, the dying duchess, in a tenderly altered voice, took his hand and said, “My poor dear, who is left to understand you now?” She died looking at him. Bianchon calls her end one of the most beautiful he has seen; the Duke’s sorrow and the serenity of the death emphasize a different facet of human nobility than any salon or scandal: the private, sacramental tenderness that outlasts display.

The assembled company reflects on such stories: Blondet’s satire, Canalis’s Napoleon, the Princesse’s defense of women, Montriveau’s brutal wartime anecdote, and Bianchon’s bedside charity together portray a society in which appearances, passions, public roles, and private heroics interweave. Women, though constrained by manners and the need to preserve rank, still possess capacity for deep feeling and sublimity; men, even those famed for will or genius, display paradoxical mixtures of grandeur and triviality. The dialogues and episodes thus sketch a varied portrait of contemporary Parisian life—its hypocrisies, its private loyalties, its sudden violences, its rare tendernesses.
