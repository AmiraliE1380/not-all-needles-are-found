DEDICATION

To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.

Among all the pupils of the Oratorian school at Vendome, I believe we are the only two who afterward met in mid-career in a life of letters. When we met, you were engaged on your noble works on German philosophy, and I on this study. So neither of us has missed his vocation; and you, when you see your name here, will feel as much pleasure as I do in inscribing my work to you. Your old schoolfellow, 1840 De Balzac.

GOBSECK

It was one o’clock in the morning in the winter of 1829–30, and in the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon two guests not of her family remained. A young man took his leave; as his carriage drove off, the Vicomtesse turned to her daughter Camille, who stood by the chimney-piece ostensibly looking at a fire-screen while listening to the courtyard, which stirred maternal anxiety.

“Camille,” the Vicomtesse said, “if you continue to treat young Comte de Restaud as you have this evening, I shall have to prevent his visiting. Trust my love and let me guide you. At seventeen one cannot judge the past or the future or social standings. M. de Restaud has a mother, Mlle. Goriot, who squandered her father’s fortune and is of no birth. Though he behaves admirably toward her and his siblings, any family would hesitate to entrust their daughter’s future to him while she lives.”

“I overheard part of your conversation with Mlle. de Grandlieu,” interposed Derville, a family friend. “I have won, M. le Comte,” he added to the departing man. “I shall assist your niece.”

“See what it is to have an attorney’s ears!” exclaimed the Vicomtesse. “How could you know what I was saying to Camille?”

“I knew it from your looks,” Derville replied, settling by the fire.

Camille’s uncle approached, and the Vicomtesse sat on a hearth-stool between them.

“It's time for a story that may change your view of Ernest de Restaud’s prospects,” Derville said.

“A story?” Camille cried. “Please begin!”

Derville’s look at the Vicomtesse made clear the tale was meant for her. The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu was a leading figure in the Faubourg Saint-Germain by birth and fortune; a Paris attorney’s intimacy with her would seem odd until one knew the facts.

After returning to France with the Royal family, the Vicomtesse had lived on a pension from Louis XVIII, a state she found intolerable. The Hotel de Grandlieu had been sold during the Revolution. Derville discovered defects in the title, pursued legal action, and reclaimed it. He also secured judgments restoring significant property, raising her income to about sixty thousand francs plus indemnities. Derville, a man of integrity and modesty, became a close family confidant.

His management of the Vicomtesse’s affairs gained him esteem in the Faubourg Saint-Germain and brought many aristocratic clients. Yet he did not exploit this popularity for personal advancement. The Vicomtesse urged him to accept a magistracy; he declined, preferring to keep his practice and social ties. His steadfast service revealed his talents, and since Ernest de Restaud’s arrival—especially as Camille showed interest—Derville had been visiting the Hotel de Grandlieu more often.

At a recent ball Derville had taunted Camille: “It’s a pity that young man doesn’t have two or three million francs, isn’t it?”

“Is it a pity? I don’t think so,” she answered. “M. de Restaud is capable and well educated. He will have as much money as he wishes when he comes into power.”

“But what if he were already rich?” Derville persisted.

“Rich already?” Camille blushed. “All the girls would be vying for him,” she said, watching the dancers.

“Then Mlle. de Grandlieu might not be the one he favors,” Derville teased. “That blush says it all! You like him, don’t you? Speak up.”

Camille suddenly rose to leave.

“She loves him,” Derville thought.

Since that evening Camille had been unusually attentive to Derville, who approved of her feelings for Ernest. Though she felt deep gratitude for his services to her family, she had previously shown respect rather than warmth, keeping a polite distance. Gratitude often weighs heavily on the next generation, which may seek to escape it.

“This adventure,” Derville began, “reminds me of the only romantic event in my life. You may find it odd for an attorney to speak of romance, but when I was twenty-five I knew some strange people. I must tell you about one you couldn’t possibly know—a usurer.

“Picture a sallow, wan face, hair iron-gray and sleek, features impassive like Talleyrand’s, little yellow eyes beneath a shabby cap, thin lips, a gimlet nose, a low suave voice, and perfect temper. He seemed either prematurely aged or unnaturally preserved. His room matched him: clean, threadbare, like an elderly spinster’s chamber. In winter the fire smoldered without a flame. He moved with pendulum regularity, economizing every movement and emotion, saving himself for inward pleasures.

“His life ran soundlessly, like sand through an hourglass. His victims sometimes erupted in rage and then fell silent. Toward evening, when business pleased him, his metals became a human heart; he would rub his hands and let his inward glee escape through the wrinkles of his face, a soundless laugh. Even in joy his words were monosyllables and his expression noncommittal.

“He was my neighbor in the Rue de Gres while I lived as a second clerk finishing my studies. The house was damp, dark, and convent-like, so gloomy that gaiety faded before reaching his door. We both lived sparingly. He and his house resembled one another; he communicated only with me after four years of acquaintance, borrowing a light or a book, and letting me visit in the evenings. Whether he had relatives or friends, whether rich or poor, no one could say. I never saw money in his room; his capital was likely in the Bank. He collected debts himself, darting across Paris on thin legs. Once gold fell from his pocket and another lodger returned it; he feigned surprise, saying if he were rich he wouldn’t live as he did. He made coffee on a cast-iron chafing-dish, bought dinner from a cookshop, and had our porter’s wife tidy his room. A whimsical chance named him Gobseck. Later I learned he was about seventy-six then—Jean-Esther Van Gobseck, born around 1740 near Antwerp to a Dutch father and Jewish mother. He once commented, without much feeling, that La belle Hollandaise, a murdered relative, was his grandniece. He shrugged at family ties—“the women never marry in our family”—and the idea of heirs repelled him; the thought of his wealth passing on was inconceivable.

“He had been ten when sent as a cabin boy to the Dutch Straits, spending twenty years at sea. The inscrutable lines on his forehead hid stories of horrible adventures, panic, luck, and lost fortunes. He had known notable men and pirates, searched for treasure in Buenos Ayres, and had lived through the American War of Independence. Yet when he spoke of those lands he seemed to regret it, as if some things were indiscreet. If humanity and sociability are religion, Gobseck was an infidel; his nature remained impenetrable. I never learned his religious views—he seemed indifferent.

“One evening I visited Daddy Gobseck. He sat motionless in his armchair, staring at the mantel, a lamp burning while his face looked paler. He barely spoke but motioned me to a chair. I wondered what such a being thought of God, feeling, women, happiness. I pitied him, like a diseased creature—he had millions and the world in idea, having explored, ransacked, weighed, appraised, and exploited it.

“‘Good day, Daddy Gobseck,’ I said.

“He turned, contracting his bushy brows in a look as communicative as a Southern smile. He mocked my gloom, reminding me of a bookseller’s bankruptcy that I had profited from. When I asked if he had bills to protest, he replied he was amusing himself. I asked if that amused him, and he retorted that not all poets print verses—poetry can exist in the head. He then spoke of life in his own terms. He bolted the door and drew a curtain, then began a monologue on his worldview.

“His creed was blunt: happiness lay in applying faculties to realities; belief and illusions were for the young. Moral rules change with latitude; conventions adapt to climate; the only constant is self-interest. After a life of traveling, he claimed, the only tangible reality worth caring for is GOLD—representing all human power. Gold realizes dreams and contains everything in embryo. Pleasure, power, vanity—these find their satisfaction in gold. Science, art, passion, calm—these might be higher ends, but for him insight into human motives replaced scientific curiosity. He wanted to show me his daily pleasures.

“He told of that morning: he had two amounts to collect—the rest he had let go—saving the small expense of collection. The first bill, a thousand francs, came from a dandy who presented a note bearing the signature of one of Paris’s prettiest women, a Countess. Why had a Countess signed a bill? Innocence, imprudence, love, charity—he wanted to discover which. The second bill, signed “Fanny Malvaut,” came from a linen-draper near bankruptcy; no one with credit comes to him—coming to Gobseck meant desperation. The Countess lived in Rue du Helder, Fanny in Rue Montmartre. Gobseck relished his hypotheses about what each woman’s bill meant.

“At the Countess’s: a maid said she had returned at three in the morning from a ball and could see him at noon. When the Countess appeared, she was magnificent yet disordered—an Indian shawl over bare shoulders, a loose ruffled gown, dark curls escaping a bright handkerchief, a bed and room strewn with signs of revelry: crushed pillows, satin slippers, open drawers, scattered jewels, a vague perfume. Beneath the luxury he perceived Misery: tired, coarsened beauty, circles around her eyes—a life of extravagance and remorse. He was struck; the scene awakened desire and memory in him.

“She asked him to wait. He answered that he could not protest until the next day. She pleaded, and as we spoke a man—her husband, the Count—entered. The Countess trembled; Gobseck, unmoved, revealed his inexorability by half-drawing the bill. She came and placed a diamond worth twelve hundred francs in his hands; he accepted and left. Outside, he noted flunkeys polishing boots and sumptuous carriages—this was what brought such people to him: they preserved appearances while their splendor depended on exploiting others.

“The young man who had brought the bill returned; Gobseck gave him two hundred francs to return to the Countess and told him he would keep the pledge for a week. The man smiled with cynical ease; Gobseck read the Countess’s future—ruin by a gay gambler, a portrait of Parisian salons wrecked by reckless appetites.

“He then visited Mlle. Fanny in Rue Montmartre, climbing steep stairs to a neat two-room apartment on the fifth floor. She was a young, quietly dressed seamstress with a fresh, delicate face, clear blue eyes, hair brushed in a modest style, surrounded by shaped linen. A crucifix and a sprig of box on her painted bedstead suggested faith and humble roots. She said the money had been left with the porter’s wife; she seldom left the room but worked hard and kept herself clean after long nights. Gobseck saw in her a life of honest toil, simplicity, and goodness—an innocent with faith. For a moment his generosity stirred; he considered offering lenient terms, even twelve percent instead of more. But suspicion tempered his impulse—perhaps she had kin who would exploit her signature. He restrained himself, aware that benevolence can ruin those it aims to help.

“Gobseck contrasted Fanny’s pure, obscure life with the Countess’s extravagance. He saw in his power a terrible insight into human hearts: every drama, every secret, every degradation and grandeur passed before him. He could read people as God reads them; the holder of purse-strings can bind or loose consciences and control ministers through their dependents, from clerks to mistresses. Power meant he could possess beauty and pleasure; gold was the spiritual basis of society. He described ten of them in Paris—silent, unknown kings—arbiters of destinies, meeting at the Cafe Themis, keeping a Black Book of important bills and secrets. They were casuists of the Bourse, analyzing the actions of the great, confident in their forecasts. One oversaw the judicial world, another the financial, another administrative affairs; Gobseck watched eldest sons, artists, society, and gamblers—the sensational side of Paris. Thwarted passion and mortified vanity were great babblers, feeding their knowledge. Thus they loved power and money for themselves.

“‘Here,’ he said, indicating his bare room, ‘the proudest man kneels with clasped hands. No merchant is so haughty elsewhere; no woman so vain; no soldier so bold—here they entreat me, with tears of rage or anguish. Famous artists, men of letters—here, inheritances and concerns of Paris are weighed. Behind this blank, impassive mask are delights of power.’

“I went back to my room stupefied. The little wizened man had become great in my eyes: the personification of gold’s power. I shrank from life. I asked myself if everything must be resolved into gold.

“That night I could not sleep, visions of gold surrounding me and the Countess’s beauty filling my thoughts. I confess, to my shame, that the Countess eclipsed the image of the quiet, innocent Fanny; yet in the morning Fanny’s sweet face rose before me, and I thought of nothing else.”

“Will you take a glass of eau sucrée?” asked the Vicomtesse, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it.”

“But I see nothing here that concerns us,” said Mme. de Grandlieu, ringing the bell.

“Sardanapalus!” cried Derville, invoking his favorite phrase. “Mademoiselle Camille’s happiness once depended on Daddy Gobseck; but with his death at ninety, M. de Restaud will soon inherit a fortune. This needs explanation. As for poor Fanny Malvaut, you know her; she is my wife.”

“Poor fellow, he would admit that, with his usual frankness, in front of a crowd!” said the Vicomtesse.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” said the attorney.

“Go on, drink your glass, my poor Derville. You will always be the happiest and best of men.”

“I left you in the Rue du Helder,” remarked the uncle, waking from a gentle doze. “You had gone to see a Countess; what became of her?”

“A few days after my conversation with the old Dutchman,” Derville continued, “I submitted my thesis, became a licentiate in law, and then an advocate. The old miser’s opinion of me improved. He consulted me (for free) on delicate matters which would have seemed risky to any ordinary practitioner. This man, who seemed immune to influence, listened to my advice with respect, and it often turned out well.

“Eventually, I became head-clerk in the office where I had worked for three years and then moved into my employer’s house. I had board and lodging and a salary of one hundred and fifty francs per month. It was a great day for me!

“When I went to bid the usurer goodbye, he showed no emotion, neither cordial nor regretful, and did not ask me to visit him again, only giving me a glance that seemed to reveal a second sight.

“A week later, my old neighbor came to see me with a thorny business matter, an expropriation, and continued to seek my advice as if I were still his clerk.

“My principal was a man of pleasure and expensive tastes; by the end of the second year (1818–1819), he found himself in difficulties and had to sell his practice. Back then, a professional connection didn’t fetch the exorbitant prices of today, and my principal asked for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. An active, competent man might hope to pay off the capital in ten years, living respectably in the meantime—if he could command confidence. But as the seventh child of a small tradesman from Noyon, I had no money and no connections except Daddy Gobseck. An ambitious idea and a glimmer of hope encouraged me. I went to Gobseck, my heart pounding as I knocked on his door in the gloomy house, recalling his warnings about the anguish awaiting those who crossed his threshold. Now, I was about to beg like so many others.

“‘Well, no, not that,’ I told myself; ‘an honest man must keep his self-respect. Success is not worth cringing for; let’s show him a front as determined as his own.’

“Daddy Gobseck had taken my room since I left, so as to have no neighbor; he had even added a grated window to his door and didn’t open until he had looked at me.

“‘So your principal is selling his practice?’ he said.

“How did you know that?” I asked, surprised.

“‘Nothing else would have brought you here,’ he replied dryly.

“‘Listen to me, M. Gobseck,’ I began, trying to maintain my composure. ‘I am a penniless clerk, and only you can understand my future. Let’s leave feelings aside. My principal’s practice is worth about twenty thousand francs per annum; in my hands, I believe it could bring in forty thousand. He is willing to sell it for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. If you would lend me the purchase money, I could pay it off in ten years.’

“‘Come, that is plain speaking,’ said Daddy Gobseck, shaking my hand. ‘Nobody has stated their motives more clearly. Guarantees?’ he asked, scanning me. ‘None to give,’ he added after a pause. ‘How old are you?’

“‘Twenty-five in ten days,’ I replied.

“‘Precisely. Well?’

“‘It is possible.’

“‘We must be quick about it, or I shall have someone buying over my head.’

“‘Bring your birth certificate tomorrow morning, and we will talk. I will think it over.’

“Next morning, at eight o’clock, I stood in the old man’s room. He took the document, put on his spectacles, coughed, and read it through. Then he said, ‘We will try to arrange this business.’

“I trembled.

“‘I make fifty percent on my capital,’ he continued, ‘sometimes I make a hundred, two hundred, five hundred percent.’

“I turned pale.

“‘But as we are acquaintances, I shall be satisfied to take twelve and a half percent per—well, yes, from you I would be content to take thirteen percent per annum. Will that suit you?’

“‘Yes,’ I answered.

“‘But if it is too much, stand up for yourself, Grotius!’ (a name he jokingly gave me). ‘When I ask for thirteen percent, it is all in the way of business; look into it, see if you can pay it; I don’t like a man to agree too easily. Is it too much?’

“‘No,’ I said, ‘I will make up for it by working a little harder.’

“‘Gad! your clients will pay for it!’ he said, looking at me wickedly.

“‘No, by all the devils in hell!’ I cried, ‘it shall be I who pays. I would sooner cut my hand off than flay people.’

“‘Good-night,’ said Daddy Gobseck.

“‘Fees are all according to scale,’ I added.

“‘Not for compromises and settlements out of court, and cases where litigants come to terms,’ he replied. ‘You can send in a bill for thousands of francs, six thousand even at a swoop, depending on the case’s importance. A man must learn to look out for business of this kind. I will recommend you as a competent attorney. I will send you so much work that your colleagues will be fit to burst with envy. Werbrust, Palma, and Gigonnet, my cronies, shall hand over their expropriations to you; they have plenty of them! So you will have two practices—the one you are buying and the other I will build up for you. You ought almost to pay me fifteen percent on my loan.’

“‘So be it, but no more,’ I said firmly.

“Daddy Gobseck’s face relaxed; he looked pleased.

“‘I shall pay the money over to your principal myself, to establish a lien on the purchase and caution-money.’

“‘Oh, anything you like in the way of guarantees.’

“‘And you will give me bills for the amount made payable to a third party (name left blank), fifteen bills of ten thousand francs each.’

“‘Well, so long as it is acknowledged in writing that this is a double—’

“‘No!’ Gobseck interrupted. ‘Why should I trust you any more than you trust me?’

“I kept silent.

“‘Furthermore,’ he continued, ‘you will give me your advice without charging fees as long as I live, will you not?’

“‘So be it; as long as there is no outlay.’

“‘Precisely,’ he said. ‘Ah, by the by, you will allow me to visit you?’ (The old man found it hard to assume an air of good humor.)

“‘I shall always be glad.’

“‘Ah! yes, but it would be difficult to arrange in the morning. You will have your affairs to attend to, and I have mine.’

“‘Then come in the evening.’

“‘Oh, no!’ he replied briskly, ‘you ought to go into society and see your clients, and I have my friends at my café.’

“‘His friends!’ I thought. ‘Very well,’ I said, ‘why not come at dinner-time?’

“‘That is the time,’ said Gobseck, ‘after “Change,” at five o’clock. Good, you will see me Wednesdays and Saturdays. We will talk over business like friends. Aha! I am gay sometimes. Just give me the wing of a partridge and a glass of champagne, and we will chat. I know many things that can be told now at this distance of time; I will teach you to know men, and what is more—women!’

“‘Oh! a partridge and a glass of champagne if you like.’

“‘Don’t do anything foolish, or I shall lose my faith in you. And don’t set up housekeeping grandly. Just one old general servant. I will come and see that you keep your health. I have capital invested in your head, he! he! so I must look after you. There, come round in the evening and bring your principal with you!’

“‘Would you mind telling me, if there is no harm in asking, what was the good of my birth certificate in this business?’ I asked as we stood on the doorstep.

“Jean-Esther Van Gobseck shrugged his shoulders, smiled maliciously, and said, ‘What blockheads youngsters are! Learn, master attorney (for learn you must if you don’t want to be taken in), that integrity and brains in a man under thirty are commodities that can be mortgaged. After that age, there is no counting on a man.’

“And with that, he shut the door.

“Three months later, I was an attorney. Soon after, madame, I had the good fortune to undertake the suit for the recovery of your estates. I won, and my name became known. Despite the exorbitant interest, I paid off Gobseck in less than five years. I married Fanny Malvaut, whom I loved deeply. Our parallel lives, our hard work and luck, strengthened our bond. One of her uncles, a wealthy farmer, died and left her seventy thousand francs, which helped clear the loan. Since then, my life has been nothing but happiness and prosperity. Nothing is more utterly uninteresting than a happy man, so let’s return to the other characters.

“About a year after purchasing the practice, I was dragged into a bachelor breakfast-party given by one of our number who had lost a bet to a young man greatly in vogue in fashionable circles. M. de Trailles, the epitome of dandyism, enjoyed a prodigious reputation.”

“But he is still enjoying it,” interjected the Comte de Born. “No one wears clothes with a finer air, nor drives a tandem with better grace. Maxime can gamble, eat, and drink more gracefully than anyone. He is a judge of horses, hats, and pictures. All the women lose their heads over him. He spends about a hundred thousand francs a year, and no one can discover that he has an acre of land or a single dividend warrant. The typical knight-errant of our salons, our boudoirs, our boulevards—Maxime de Trailles is a singular being, fit for anything, and good for nothing, capable of both noble and base acts, more often bespattered with mire than blood, knowing more anxiety than remorse, more concerned with his digestion than any mental process, shamming passion, feeling nothing. Maxime de Trailles is a brilliant link between the hulks and the best society; he belongs to the eminently intelligent class from which a Mirabeau, or a Pitt, or a Richelieu springs at times, though it more often produces Counts of Horn, Fouquier-Tinvilles, and Coignards.”

“Well,” Derville continued, “I had heard much about this individual from poor old Goriot, a client of mine, and I had already taken pains to avoid the dangerous honor of his acquaintance, as I sometimes encountered him in society. Still, my friend was so insistent about this breakfast-party that I could hardly refuse without earning a reputation for squeamishness. Madame, you could hardly imagine what a bachelor’s breakfast-party is like. It means superb display and a studied refinement seldom seen; the luxury of a miser when vanity leads him to be sumptuous for a day.

“You are surprised as you enter the room at the neatness of the table, dazzling with silver, crystal, and damask linen. Life is in full bloom; the young men are graceful, smiling, and speaking in low, demure voices like brides; everything about them looks girlish. Two hours later, you might mistake the room for a battlefield after a fight. Broken glasses, crumpled serviettes lie strewn among the nauseous remnants of food. There is an uproar of jesting toasts, a fire of witticisms and bad jokes; faces are flushed, eyes inflamed and expressionless, unintentional confidences reveal the truth. Bottles are smashed, songs are sung amidst a diabolical racket; men call each other out, hang on each other’s necks, or fall to fisticuffs; the room is filled with a horrid scent made up of a hundred odors, and noise enough for a hundred voices. No one knows what he is eating, drinking, or saying. Some are depressed, others babble, one turns monomaniac, repeating the same word like a bell; another tries to keep the tumult in check; the steadiest proposes an orgy. If a sober person were to enter, he would think he had interrupted a Bacchanalian rite.

“It was in the midst of such chaos that M. de Trailles tried to ingratiate himself with me. My head was clear, and I was on guard. He pretended to be drunk, but was perfectly cool and knew what he was about. Somehow, by the end of the evening, I had promised to introduce him to our Papa Gobseck. The words ‘honor,’ ‘virtue,’ ‘countess,’ ‘honest woman,’ and ‘ill-luck’ were mingled in his discourse with a magical potency, thanks to his golden tongue.

“When I awoke the next morning, trying to recollect the previous day, I struggled to piece together my impressions. Eventually, it seemed that the daughter of one of my clients was in danger of losing her reputation and her husband’s love unless she could gather fifty thousand francs that morning. There had been gaming debts and other accounts. My charming companion had impressed upon me that she was rich enough to cover these reverses with a few years of economy. Only then did I begin to grasp the urgency of his request. I confess, to my shame, that I had no doubt it was crucial for Daddy Gobseck to reconcile with this dandy. I was dressing when the young gentleman appeared.

“‘M. le Comte,’ I said after the usual greetings, ‘I fail to see why you need me to introduce you to Van Gobseck, the most civil and smooth-spoken of capitalists. Money will be forthcoming if he has any, or rather, if you can provide adequate security.’

“‘Monsieur,’ he replied, ‘I do not wish to force you to do me a service, even though you have given your word.’

“‘Sardanapalus!’ I thought, ‘am I going to let him think I won’t keep my word?’

“‘I told you yesterday,’ he said, ‘that I had fallen out with Daddy Gobseck most inopportunely; and as there is scarcely another man in Paris who can provide a hundred thousand francs at the end of the month, I begged you to make my peace with him. But let us say no more about it—’

“M. de Trailles looked at me with a civil insult, making as if to leave.

“‘I am ready to go with you,’ I said.

“When we reached the Rue de Gres, my dandy looked around with a circumspection and unease that puzzled me. His face changed color, and by the time we reached Gobseck’s door, sweat stood in drops on his forehead. Just as we were getting out of the cabriolet, a hackney cab turned into the street. His hawk eye detected a woman inside. His face lit up with a gleam of almost savage joy; he called to a little boy passing by and gave him his horse to hold. Then we approached the old bill discounter.

“‘M. Gobseck,’ I said, ‘I have brought one of my closest friends to see you (whom I trust as I would trust the Devil,’ I added for the old man’s private ear). ‘To oblige me, you will do your best for him (at the ordinary rate), and pull him out of his difficulty (if it suits your convenience).’

“M. de Trailles made his bow to Gobseck, took a seat, and listened with a courtier-like attitude; its charming humility would have touched your heart. But Gobseck sat impassively by the fireside, resembling the statue of Voltaire under the peristyle of the Théâtre-Français. He had partly risen as if to bow, and the skull cap covering his head, along with the narrow strip of sallow forehead, completed his likeness to the man of marble.

“‘I have no money to spare except for my own clients,’ he said.

“‘So you are cross because I may have tried in other quarters to ruin myself?’ laughed the Count.

“‘Ruin yourself!’ Gobseck repeated ironically.

“‘Were you about to say that it is impossible to ruin a man who has nothing?’ the dandy asked. ‘I defy you to find a better stock in Paris!’ he cried, turning on his heels.

“This half-earnest buffoonery produced no effect on Gobseck.

“‘Am I not on intimate terms with the Ronquerolles, the Marsays, the Franchessinis, the two Vandenesses, the Ajuda-Pintos—all the most fashionable young men in Paris? A prince and an ambassador (you know them both) are my partners at play. I draw my revenues from London and Carlsbad and Baden and Bath. Is not this the most brilliant of all industries!’

“‘True.’

“‘You make a sponge of me, begad! You encourage me to swell myself in society, so you can squeeze me when I am hard up; but you yourselves are sponges, just as I am, and death will squeeze you someday.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘If there were no spendthrifts, what would become of you? The pair of us are like soul and body.’

“‘Precisely so.’

“‘Come, now, give us your hand, Grandaddy Gobseck, and be magnanimous if this is “true” and “possible” and “precisely so.”’

“‘You come to me,’ the usurer answered coldly, ‘because Girard, Palma, Werbrust, and Gigonnet are full up of your paper; they are offering it at a loss of fifty percent; and as they likely only gave you half the figure on the face of the bills, they are not worth twenty-five percent of their supposed value. I am your most obedient! Can I in common decency lend a stiver to a man who owes thirty thousand francs and has not one farthing?’ Gobseck continued. ‘The day before yesterday, you lost ten thousand francs at a ball at the Baron de Nucingen’s.’

“‘Sir,’ said the Count, with rare impudence, ‘my affairs are no concern of yours,’ and he scrutinized the old man. ‘A man has no debts until payment is due.’

“‘True.’

“‘My bills will be duly met.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘And at this moment, the question between you and me is simply whether the security I am going to offer is sufficient for the sum I have come to borrow.’

“‘Precisely.’

“A cab stopped at the door, and the sound of wheels filled the room.

“‘I will bring something directly which perhaps will satisfy you,’ cried the young man, and he left the room.

“‘Oh! my son,’ exclaimed Gobseck, rising and stretching out his arms to me, ‘if he has good security, you have saved my life. It would be the death of me. Werbrust and Gigonnet thought they were going to trick me; and now, thanks to you, I shall have a good laugh at their expense tonight.’

There was something frightful about the old man’s ecstasy. It was the one occasion he opened his heart to me; that flash of joy, swift though it was, will never be effaced from my memory.

“‘Favor me by staying here,’ he added. ‘I am armed and a sure shot. I have gone tiger-hunting and fought on deck when there was nothing for it but to win or die; but I don’t care to trust yonder elegant scoundrel.’

He sat down again in his armchair before his bureau, and his face grew pale and impassive as before.

“‘Ah!’ he continued, turning to me, ‘you will see that lovely creature I once told you about; I can hear a fine lady’s step in the corridor; it is she, no doubt;’ and indeed, the young man returned with a woman on his arm. I recognized the Countess, one of old Goriot’s two daughters.

The Countess did not see me at first; I stayed in the window bay, my face against the pane; but I saw her give Maxime a suspicious glance as she entered the money-lender’s damp, dark room. She was so beautiful that, despite her faults, I felt sorry for her. There was a storm of anguish in her heart; her proud features were drawn with pain she strove to disguise. The young man had become her evil genius. I admired Gobseck, whose perspicacity had foreseen their future four years ago at the first bill she endorsed.

“‘Probably,’ I thought, ‘this monster with the angel face controls every possible spring of action in her: rules her through vanity, jealousy, pleasure, and the current of life in the world.’”

The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu interrupted the story.

“Why, the woman’s very virtues have been turned against her,” she exclaimed. “He has made her shed tears of devotion, then abused her kindness and made her pay dearly for unhallowed bliss.”

Derville did not understand the signs Mme. de Grandlieu made to him.

“I confess,” he said, “that I had no inclination to shed tears over the lot of this unhappy creature, so brilliant in society, so repulsive to eyes that could read her heart; I shuddered rather at the sight of her murderer, a young angel with a clear brow, red lips, white teeth, and a winning smile. There they stood before their judge, he scrutinizing them much as a fifteenth-century Dominican inquisitor might have peered into the dungeons of the Holy Office while the torture was administered to two Moors.

“The Countess spoke tremulously. ‘Sir,’ she said, ‘is there any way of obtaining the value of these diamonds, and of keeping the right of repurchase?’ She held out a jewel-case.

“‘Yes, madame,’ I interjected, stepping forward.

“She looked at me, and a shudder ran through her as she recognized me, giving me the glance that means, ‘Say nothing of this,’ all over the world.

“‘This,’ I said, ‘constitutes a sale with faculty of redemption, a formal agreement to transfer and deliver a piece of property, either real estate or personalty, for a given time, after which the previous owner recovers their title upon payment of a stipulated sum.’

“She breathed more freely. The Count looked black; he doubted whether Gobseck would lend much on the diamonds after such a fall in their value. Gobseck, impassive as ever, took up his magnifying glass and scrutinized the jewels. If I were to live for a hundred years, I would never forget the sight of his face at that moment. There was a flush in his pale cheeks; his eyes seemed to catch the sparkle of the stones, for there was an unnatural glitter in them. He rose and went to the light, holding the diamonds close to his toothless mouth, as if he meant to devour them; mumbling vague words over them, holding up bracelets, sprays, necklaces, and tiaras one after another, to judge their quality; taking them out of the jewel-case and putting them back, letting the light bring out all their fires. He was more like a child than an old man; or rather, childhood and dotage seemed to meet in him.

“Fine stones! This set would have fetched three hundred thousand francs before the Revolution. What water! Genuine Asiatic diamonds from Golconda or Visapur... No one in Paris but Gobseck can appreciate them.” Gobseck examined the jewels with greedy delight, alternating rapture at their perfection and a merciless, calculating austerity. His gaunt face, animated only by the reflected light of the gems, revealed nothing human beneath the glitter.

The Count had come to Gobseck seeking a loan of one hundred thousand francs for three years, offering the Countess’s diamond set as security. Gobseck weighed and judged the stones, then, after a glance at the Countess and counsel from the narrator—an attorney who intervened to protect legal formality—rejected making out a deed in the Countess’s name because married women’s agreements could be voidable by their husbands. He insisted instead on immediate possession. Negotiations hardened: Gobseck initially offered eighty thousand francs, then fifty thousand in cash and thirty thousand in bills—paper likely protested and worthless as coin. Maxime, the Countess’s dissolute lover, bristled; tempers flared and Gobseck, unshaken, even produced pistols to underscore the point. The Countess accepted; the jewels were Gobseck’s. His small brutal triumph at possessing the stones made the narrator uneasy.

Shortly after, the Count confronted Gobseck in fury, insisting the diamonds were family property illegally taken by his wife. Gobseck answered coolly that if the jewels had been sold in separate pieces there would have been no way for him to know whose they were, and that a married woman able to put her name to a bill could appear to buy and sell in her own right. The narrator, acting as mediator, urged compromise: a legal dispute would force the Count to expose his wife and would be precarious because the goods were not easily identifiable. He advised an instrument allowing the husband to redeem the stones later. Gobseck, shrewd and merciless, negotiated the Count’s acceptance of a settlement—an instrument acknowledging receipt of eighty-five thousand francs in consideration of Gobseck’s promise to return the diamonds—while extracting terms to their advantage.

After the Count left, the narrator learned of a more elaborate plan the Count had proposed to him confidentially: to “transfer” his estate to Gobseck by simulated sale so that Gobseck would hold the property in trust and deliver it to the Count’s eldest son when he came of age. The Count feared the boy’s affection for his mother and would not entrust him with the papers. The narrator, reluctant to harm the other children, demanded that any counter-deed provide for them too; the Count, moved and unwell, agreed and signed. The narrator left thinking the deed would be safely held in trust, intending to protect the heirs if necessary.

Illness and mutual mistrust between the Count and Countess grew. As the Count weakened, he became aversive to his wife and forbade her and the two youngest children from his room. The Countess, terrified at the prospect of destitution for her children, watched the dying man closely, feigning devotion and keeping near his bedside to overhear every word. She suspected the papers that would secure their future were hidden somewhere and guarded the house with a mother’s vigilance and cunning. The Count’s peculiar, ascetic disorderliness in his sickroom—the damp, broken furniture and a general state of neglect—was part of his medical state and, perhaps, of his scorn.

The narrator feared for the counter-deed signed but not delivered to him: if the Countess found or destroyed it, a legal tangle between her and Gobseck would ensue—one the usurer would not surrender. To forestall such a catastrophe, the narrator sued the Count in a contrived action nominally to secure a claim for Gobseck, procured a judgment, and bribed a servant to inform him when the Count was at death’s door so he could take legal steps to seize any papers or documents the Count might leave. Money and the fear of losing it drove the Countess’s vigilance; she studied the law with the dying man’s moans in her ears, seeking ways to keep the family’s patrimony intact.

When the Count lay dying, he summoned his young son Ernest and, with solemnity, entrusted him with a sealed packet belonging to the narrator, charging the child to post it so that the narrator would have notice of what the Count intended. The Count pressed Ernest to secrecy, promising that in years his son would understand and be rewarded. Ernest obeyed; on leaving the room he encountered his mother, who questioned him sharply. The Count, hearing the exchange, accused her of trying to pervert their son and corrupt him, and their confrontation was violent and passionate. The Countess fell at his feet, imploring mercy and offering penance to secure her children’s future; the dying man reproached her and called her unfaithful, then lapsed into unconsciousness. Priests administered the sacraments. The Count died at midnight.

When the narrator and Gobseck arrived at the house after the death, the scene was chaotic: the room was in disarray, drawers flung open, papers and boxes scattered, evidence that the Countess had rifled the place in the frantic hope of finding the documents that would restore her children’s rights. The pillow lay on the floor; a paper with the Count’s seal had been ripped and was burning in the grate. The narrator snatched an unburned fragment addressed to him and realized with horror that the Countess had been destroying the very papers that might have secured her children’s property—mistaking them, perhaps in ignorance and frantic guilt, for a will that disinherited them. Gobseck, who had come to claim the estate, coldly revealed his intention to assert rightful ownership of the property sold to him by the late Count. He showed neither pity nor moral qualms; the usurer was adamant about enforcing the terms of the sale—possession, he insisted, being tantamount to title.

When confronted, Gobseck asked, with suave malevolence, whether the narrator intended to judge him. The narrator left the Countess alone by her husband’s bed, weeping and paralyzed by the discovery. Gobseck took immediate control: he let out the house, assumed the role of landlord and manager, and, over time, settled the estate into practical order—repairing mills, planting timber, reaping income. He transformed himself, paradoxically, into a feudal steward, severe and efficient.

The narrator’s later attempts to move Gobseck to act generously toward Ernest—who, it appeared, might yet claim a sufficient fortune to marry and establish himself—met the usurer’s hard moral: adversity would teach the boy the value of money; let him learn his lesson. Gobseck refused to be a patron or a sentimental trustee; he believed in a harsh natural selection by poverty.

Years passed. The narrator visited Gobseck, now bedridden, kept for the most part as he always had been: letting rooms be taken as they became vacant, preserving one private room untouched, receiving clients even from bed, and closely managing affairs. His old portress and a pensioner cared for him. Despite failing health he remained alert to deals, discounts, and profits. During the liquidation of claims after France recognized the Haitian Republic, Gobseck’s knowledge of old colonial fortunes and claims had gained him a place on a commission; he devised agencies and systems to profit from buying or speculating on debts and claims, and those who sought his help rewarded him in kind—presents of game, silver plate, snuffboxes. He accepted gifts and bargains but meted them out in stingy, petty barter; nothing positive flowed from his hoard.

Called by Gobseck’s urgency as he neared death, the narrator found the usurer in the posture of a man reconciled to fate: naming bequests, talking of relatives, half-delirious, then stretched out and died in full consciousness. His body looked like a bronze portrait of old Rome—brittle and lean. Stirring the cinders of Gobseck’s grate, the narrator found metal and coins among the ashes—signs of secret hoards. The rooms disclosed the habits of a miser: putrid pies and decaying provisions in one room, the stench of neglect among stacked chests and crates, while other rooms were crammed with unrecorded treasures—silver-plate, furniture, jewelry, weapons, pictures—many items pledged and taken in but not sold; none catalogued. A thousand-franc note slipped from a misplaced book. The squalor and the petty disputes recorded in his letters showed the miser’s paradox: infinite appetite for possession but niggardly to the point of losing the value of what he hoarded through his parsimony.

The narrator wondered who would inherit such scattered riches. Gobseck had left, among other instructions, an address pointing to a reputed heiress, a girl known as “La Torpille” in the Rue Vivienne—a hint that the usurer’s fortune might pass to someone morally unworthy. Yet before any such fate could be settled, the narrator reflected on Ernest de Restaud: though the Countess’s frantic search and destruction of papers may have cost the children, Ernest still stood to inherit an estate large enough to allow him to marry Mlle. Camille, so long as proper provision was made for his mother and siblings.

Thus the narrative closes on the uneasy mixture of justice and irony: the usurer’s triumph at legalities and possession, the Countess’s ruinous panic and maternal devotion distorted into theft and destruction, the narrator’s legal maneuvers to protect heirs becoming entangled with moral compromise, and the final image of Gobseck dying amid the very hoard and petty quarrels that defined his life. The tale leaves the question of the family’s future and the distribution of Gobseck’s wealth unsettled, foreshadowing further complications in the lives of the Restaud family and those around them.
