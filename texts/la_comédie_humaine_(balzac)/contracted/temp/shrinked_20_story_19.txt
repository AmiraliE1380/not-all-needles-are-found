GOBSECK

By Honore De Balzac

Translated By Ellen Marriage

DEDICATION

To M. le Baron Barchou de Penhoen.

Among all the pupils of the Oratorian school at Vendome, we are, I think, the only two who have afterwards met in mid-career of a life of letters. When we met, you were engaged upon your noble works on German philosophy, and I on this study. So neither of us has missed his vocation; and you, when you see your name here, will feel as much pleasure as I do in inscribing my work to you. Your old schoolfellow, 1840 De Balzac.

GOBSECK

It was one o’clock in the morning during the winter of 1829-30, but in the Vicomtesse de Grandlieu’s salon, two guests remained who were not part of her family. A young man took his leave, and as the sound of a departing carriage faded, the Vicomtesse turned to her daughter, Camille, who was by the chimney-piece, seemingly examining a fire-screen but listening intently to the courtyard sounds, which raised maternal concerns.

“Camille,” the Vicomtesse said, “if you continue to treat young Comte de Restaud as you have this evening, I will have to prevent him from visiting. Trust my love and let me guide you. At seventeen, one cannot judge the past or future or social standings. M. de Restaud has a mother, Mlle. Goriot, who squandered her father’s fortune and is of no birth. Despite his admirable behavior towards her and his siblings, any family would hesitate to entrust their daughter’s future to him while she lives.”

“I overheard part of your conversation with Mlle. de Grandlieu,” interjected Derville, a family friend. “I have won, M. le Comte,” he added, turning to his opponent. “I shall assist your niece.”

“See what it is to have an attorney’s ears!” exclaimed the Vicomtesse. “How could you know what I was saying to Camille?”

“I knew it from your looks,” Derville replied, settling into a chair by the fire.

Camille’s uncle approached her, and the Vicomtesse took a seat on a hearth stool between them.

“It’s time for a story that may change your view of Ernest de Restaud’s prospects,” Derville said.

“A story?” Camille exclaimed. “Please begin!”

Derville’s glance at the Vicomtesse indicated the tale was for her. The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu was a prominent figure in Faubourg Saint-Germain due to her wealth and lineage. Although it may seem unusual for a Paris attorney to be so familiar with her, it was easily explained.

Upon returning to France with the Royal family, the Vicomtesse lived on a pension from Louis XVIII, a situation she found intolerable. The Hotel de Grandlieu had been sold during the Republic. Derville discovered flaws in the title and successfully initiated proceedings to reclaim it for her. He also won lawsuits that restored significant property, increasing her income to around sixty thousand francs, along with substantial indemnities. Derville, a man of high character and modesty, became a close family friend.

His management of the Vicomtesse’s affairs earned him respect in Faubourg Saint-Germain, and he counted many elite families among his clients. However, he did not exploit his popularity for personal gain. The Vicomtesse encouraged him to pursue a magistracy, but he consistently declined, preferring to maintain his practice and connections. His dedication to the Vicomtesse had brought his talents to light, and he had been visiting the Hotel de Grandlieu more frequently since Ernest de Restaud’s arrival, especially as Camille showed interest in him.

At a recent ball, Derville had remarked to Camille, “It’s a pity that young man doesn’t have two or three million francs, isn’t it?”

“Is it a pity? I don’t think so,” she replied. “M. de Restaud is capable and well-educated. He will have as much money as he wishes when he comes into power.”

“But what if he were already rich?” Derville countered.

“Rich already?” Camille blushed. “All the girls would be vying for him,” she said, glancing at the dancers.

“Then Mlle. de Grandlieu might not be the one he favors,” Derville teased. “That blush says it all! You like him, don’t you? Speak up.”

Suddenly, Camille stood to leave.

“She loves him,” Derville thought.

Since that evening, Camille had been unusually attentive to Derville, who approved of her feelings for Ernest. Although she recognized her family’s debt to him, she had previously felt more respect than warmth towards him, maintaining a polite distance. Gratitude often weighs heavily on the next generation, who may wish to distance themselves from it.

“This adventure,” Derville began, “brings to mind the one romantic event in my life. You may find it ridiculous that an attorney speaks of romance, but once I was twenty-five and had seen some strange things. I should start by telling you about someone you could not possibly know—a usurer.

“Can you picture his sallow, wan face? I wish the _Academie_ would allow me to call such faces the _lunar_ type. His hair was iron-gray, sleek, and carefully combed; his features were as impassive as Talleyrand’s. His little yellow eyes, devoid of eyelashes, peered from under a shabby cap, as if afraid of the light. He had thin lips reminiscent of Rembrandt’s portraits of alchemists and a sharp nose that resembled a gimlet. His voice was low and suave; he never lost his temper. It was hard to tell his age; he seemed to have either aged prematurely or conserved his youth.

“His room was as clean and threadbare as that of an elderly spinster. In winter, the fire smoldered in a bank of ashes, never producing a flame. He moved through his day with the regularity of a pendulum, stopping mid-sentence when a cart passed by to save his voice. He was thrifty with his pulse, concentrating all human sensibility within himself.

“His life flowed soundlessly, like the sands of an hourglass. His victims sometimes erupted in rage, only to fall silent afterward, much like a kitchen after a fowl’s neck has been wrung.

“Toward evening, this bill of exchange incarnate would take on a human shape, and his metals would transform into a human heart. When satisfied with his day’s business, he would rub his hands, and his inward glee would escape through the wrinkles of his face, akin to the soundless laughter of _Leather Stocking_. Even in joy, his conversation was limited to monosyllables, and he maintained a non-committal expression.

“This was the neighbor Chance found for me in the Rue de Gres, where I lived as a second clerk finishing my studies. The house was damp and dark, with all windows facing the street, resembling an old convent. It was so gloomy that the gaiety of eldest sons vanished before they reached my neighbor’s door. He and his house were alike; even so does the oyster resemble its rock.

“I was the only person he communicated with socially; he would come to borrow a light or a book, and in the evenings, he would allow me to visit him. These gestures of confidence resulted from four years of neighborly interaction and my sober conduct. I lived much like he did due to my lack of funds. Did he have relatives or friends? Was he rich or poor? No one could answer these questions. I never saw money in his room; his capital was likely secured in the Bank. He collected his debts himself, running all over Paris on skinny legs. Once, when he had gold in his pocket, a double napoleon fell out, and another lodger returned it to him.

“That isn’t mine!” he exclaimed, surprised. “If I were rich, would I live as I do?”

“He made his coffee every morning on a cast-iron chafing dish in the corner of the grate; his dinner came from a cookshop, and our old porter’s wife tidied his room at the prescribed hour. A whimsical chance, which Sterne would have seen as predestination, named him Gobseck. When I later did business for him, I learned he was about seventy-six when we met. Born around 1740 in an outlying suburb of Antwerp to a Dutch father and a Jewish mother, his name was Jean-Esther Van Gobseck. You may recall the murder case of _La belle Hollandaise_? I mentioned it to him, and he replied without interest, ‘She is my grandniece.’

“That was the only remark he made about the death of his sole surviving relative. Reports indicated that _La belle Hollandaise_ was named Sara Van Gobseck. When I asked how his grandniece bore his surname, he smiled, saying, ‘The women never marry in our family.’

“He had never cared to find out about any relatives over four generations on the female side. The thought of heirs was abhorrent to him; the idea of his wealth passing to others after his death was inconceivable.

“He was ten when his mother sent him as a cabin boy to the Dutch Straits Settlements, where he spent twenty years. The inscrutable lines on his sallow forehead concealed secrets of horrible adventures, sudden panic, unanticipated luck, and lost fortunes. He had known notable figures like Admiral Simeuse, M. de Lally, and Tippoo Sahib. His long stay in St. Thomas brought him into contact with notorious pirates. He had left no stone unturned in his quest for fortune, even attempting to discover treasure from a famous tribe in Buenos Ayres. He had personal knowledge of the American War of Independence. However, when he spoke of the Indies or America, he seemed to regard it as indiscretion and would repent afterward. If humanity and sociability are a religion, Gobseck might be considered an infidel; despite my efforts to understand him, his true nature remained impenetrable.

“Did he adhere to his mother’s religion? Did he view Gentiles as legitimate prey? Had he converted to Roman Catholicism, Lutheranism, or something else? I never learned his religious opinions; he appeared indifferent rather than incredulous.

“One evening, I visited this man who had turned himself to gold, the usurer whom his clients called Daddy Gobseck, perhaps ironically. He sat motionless in his armchair, staring at the mantel-shelf, seemingly reading his statements. A lamp burned in the room, but his face appeared paler against the background. He gestured to a chair for me but said nothing.

“What thoughts could this being have?” I wondered. “Does he know God exists? Does he know about feeling, women, happiness?” I felt pity for him, akin to pitying a diseased creature. Yet, I knew that while he had millions at his command, he possessed the world in idea—having explored, ransacked, weighed, appraised, and exploited it.

“‘Good day, Daddy Gobseck,’ I began.

“He turned his face toward me, slightly contracting his bushy black eyebrows, a characteristic expression that conveyed as much as a jubilant smile on a Southern face.

“‘You look as gloomy as you did when you heard about the failure of that bookseller you admired, though you were one of his victims.’

“‘One of his victims?’ he repeated, astonished.

“‘Yes. You refused to accept composition at the creditors’ meeting until he promised to pay you in full, and he gave you bills accepted by the bankrupt firm. When he resumed business, he paid you the dividend on those bills.’

“‘He was sharp, but I got it out of him.’

“‘Do you have bills to protest? Today is the 30th, I believe.’

“It was the first time I mentioned money to him. He looked at me ironically, then replied in his bland voice, ‘I am amusing myself.’

“‘So you amuse yourself now and again?’

“‘Do you think the only poets are those who print their verses?’ he asked, pityingly.

“‘Poetry in that head!’ I thought, knowing little of his life.

“‘What life could be as glorious as mine?’ he continued, his eyes lighting up. ‘You are young; your visions are colored by youthful blood. You see women’s faces in the fire, while I see only coals. You have beliefs; I have none. Keep your illusions if you can. I will show you life with the discount taken off. You can go wherever you like or stay at home with your wife, but eventually, you settle into a groove. Happiness consists in applying your faculties to realities. Anything more is false. My principles have varied among different men; I had to change them with every latitude. What we admire in Europe is punishable in Asia, and a vice in Paris becomes a necessity elsewhere. There are no hard-and-fast rules, only conventions adapted to the climate. Throw a man into various social melting pots, and convictions and moral systems become meaningless. The only constant is self-interest. If you lived as long as I have, you would know that the only concrete reality worth caring about is GOLD. Gold represents every form of human power. I have traveled and found either hills or plains everywhere; the plains are monotonous, the hills wearisome; thus, place is irrelevant. Manners are universal; the battle between the poor and the rich is inevitable everywhere. It is better to exploit than to be exploited. Everywhere, you find the laboring man and the idle man; pleasures are the same, and when all sensations are exhausted, only Vanity remains. Vanity is satisfied only by gold. Our dreams require time and means to realize. Gold contains everything in embryo; it realizes all things for us.

“‘Only fools and invalids find pleasure in shuffling cards for a few pence. Only dupes think they are useful by laying down political rules amid unpredictable events. Only simpletons delight in discussing stage players and repeating their sayings. What is all this but Parisian life summed up? Let us seek a higher outlook on life. Happiness consists in strong emotions that drain our vitality or in methodical occupation that makes existence like clockwork. A higher happiness consists in noble curiosity, a wish to learn Nature’s secrets, or to imitate Nature. In two words, that is Science and Art, or passion and calm. Every human passion, heightened in the struggle for existence, parades before me while I live in calm. As for scientific curiosity, I replace it with insight into the springs of action in man and woman. In summary, the world is mine without effort, and it has no hold on me. Listen,’ he continued, ‘I will tell you about my morning, and you will understand my pleasures.’

“He got up, bolted the door, drew a tapestry curtain across it, and sat down again.

“‘This morning,’ he said, ‘I had only two amounts to collect; the rest of the bills due I gave away instead of cash to my customers yesterday. So much saved, you see, for when I discount a bill, I always deduct two francs for a hired brougham—collection expenses. It would be absurd for my clients to make me trudge all over Paris for a few francs of discount when no man is my master, and I only pay seven francs in taxes.

“‘The first bill for a thousand francs was presented by a young fellow, a dandy with a spangled waistcoat, eyeglass, tilbury, and English horse. The bill bore the signature of one of the prettiest women in Paris, married to a Count, a great landowner. How did that Countess come to sign a bill of exchange, legally worthless but practically good business? These women, poor things, fear the scandal of a protested bill and would rather pay than fail. I wanted to find out what that bill truly represented—stupidity, imprudence, love, or charity?

“‘The second bill, signed “Fanny Malvaut,” came from a linen-draper on the brink of bankruptcy. No one with bank credit comes to _me_. The first step to my door means desperation; the news of his failure will soon emerge, and he has been everywhere else first. The stag is always at bay when I see him, and a pack of creditors is on his tail. The Countess lived in the Rue du Helder, and my Fanny in the Rue Montmartre. I made many conjectures as I set out this morning. If these two women could not pay, they would show me more respect than their fathers. What tricks would the Countess try for a thousand francs? She would be so nice to me, speaking in that ingratiating tone of endorsers, pouring out coaxing words, while I...’ (here the old man turned his pale eyes on me)—‘and I would remain unmoved, inexorable! So much for hypotheses. I reached the house.

“‘“Madame la Comtesse is asleep,” said the maid.

“‘“When can I see her?”

“‘“At twelve o’clock.”

“‘“Is Madame la Comtesse ill?”

“‘“No, sir, but she returned home at three o’clock this morning from a ball.”

“‘“My name is Gobseck; tell her I will call again at twelve,” and I left, leaving muddy footprints on the carpet. I enjoy leaving mud on a rich man’s carpet; it’s not spite, but a reminder of Necessity. In the Rue Montmartre, I entered a poor-looking house and looked into a dark courtyard where sunlight never shone. The porter’s lodge was grimy, the window like a shabby gown—greasy, dirty, and full of holes.

“‘“Mlle. Fanny Malvaut?”

“‘“She has gone out; but if you’ve come about a bill, the money is waiting for you.”

“‘“I will look in again,” I said.

“‘As soon as I knew the porter had the money, I wanted to know what the girl was like; I pictured her as pretty. The rest of the morning, I spent looking at prints in shop windows along the boulevard; then, just as it struck twelve, I entered the Countess’ antechamber.

“‘“Madame has just rung for me,” said the maid; “I don’t think she can see you yet.”

“‘“I will wait,” I said, sitting in an easy chair.

“‘Venetian shutters opened, and the maid hurried back.

“‘“Come in, sir.”

“‘From the sweet tone of the girl’s voice, I knew the mistress was not ready to pay. What a handsome woman I saw! She had thrown an Indian shawl over her bare shoulders, covering herself while revealing the outlines beneath. She wore a loose gown trimmed with ruffles, indicating her laundress’ bills amounted to about two thousand francs a year. Her dark curls escaped from a bright handkerchief knotted carelessly around her head. The bed lay in disorder, suggesting broken slumber. A painter would have paid to see the scene. Under luxurious draperies, the pillow, crushed into the depths of an eider-down quilt, bore a vague impress that sparked the imagination. Satin slippers gleamed from the bear-skin rug at the bed-foot, where she had flung them off in weariness after the ball. A crumpled gown hung over a chair, stockings twisted around an easy chair leg, and ribbon garters sprawled over a settee. A half-open fan glittered on the chimney-piece. Drawers stood open; flowers, diamonds, gloves, a bouquet, a girdle were scattered about. The room was filled with vague sweet perfume. And beneath all the luxury and disorder, I saw Misery lurking, ready for her or her lover, rearing its head, for the Countess felt the edge of those fangs. Her tired face epitomized the room strewn with relics of past revelry. The scattered gewgaws, pitiable this morning, had turned heads the night before.

“‘What efforts to drink from the Tantalus cup of bliss I could read in these traces of love struck by remorse—in this visible representation of a life of luxury, extravagance, and riot. There were faint red marks on her young face, signs of fine skin; her features were coarsened, and dark circles surrounded her eyes. Yet Nature was vigorous in her; these traces of past folly did not mar her beauty. Her eyes glittered. She resembled a da Vinci _Herodias_, magnificently full of life and energy; nothing was starved or stinted in feature or outline; she awakened desire; it seemed there was a passion in her stronger than love. I was taken with her. It had been long since my heart throbbed; I was paid then and there—for I would give a thousand francs for a sensation that brought back memories of youth.

“‘“Monsieur,” she said, finding a chair for me, “will you be so good as to wait?”

“‘“Until this time tomorrow, madame,” I replied, folding the bill again. “I cannot legally protest this bill any sooner.” And within myself, I thought—“Pay the price of your luxury, your name, your ease, the monopoly you enjoy! The rich have invented judges and courts to secure their goods, and the guillotine—where many lie in silk, under silken coverlets, there is remorse and grinding of teeth beneath a smile, and those fantastical lions’ jaws are gaping to sink their fangs into your heart.”

“‘“Protest the bill! Can you mean it?” she cried, her eyes on me; “could you have so little consideration for me?”

“‘“If the King himself owed me money and did not pay, I would summon him even sooner than any other debtor.”

“‘While we spoke, someone tapped gently at the door.

“‘“I cannot see anyone,” she cried imperiously.

“‘“But, Anastasie, I particularly wish to speak to you.”

“‘“Not just now, dear,” she answered in a milder tone, but with no sign of relenting.

“‘“What nonsense! You are talking to someone,” said the voice, and in came a man who could only be the Count.

“‘The Countess glanced at me. I saw how it was; she was thoroughly in my power. There was a time when I was young and might have been foolish enough not to protest the bill. At Pondicherry in 1763, I let a woman off, and she paid me back nicely. I deserved it; what reason had I to trust her?

“‘“What does this gentleman want?” asked the Count.

“‘I could see the Countess trembling; the white satin skin of her throat was rough, “turned to goose flesh.” I laughed inwardly without moving a muscle.

“‘“This gentleman is one of my tradesmen,” she said.

“‘The Count turned his back on me; I drew the bill halfway out of my pocket. After that inexorable movement, she came over and placed a diamond in my hands. “Take it,” she said, “and be gone.”

“‘We exchanged values, and I bowed and left. The diamond was worth twelve hundred francs to me. Outside, I saw a swarm of flunkeys brushing their liveries, waxing their boots, and cleaning sumptuous equipages.

“‘“This is what brings these people to me!” I thought. “They steal millions with all due formalities to maintain this lifestyle. The great lord and the little man who apes him bathe in mud to save themselves a splash or two when they walk through the streets.”

“‘Just then, the great gates opened to admit a cabriolet. It was the same young fellow who had brought the bill to me.

“‘“Sir,” I said as he alighted, “here are two hundred francs, which I beg you to return to Mme. la Comtesse, and please tell her I hold the pledge she deposited with me this morning for a week.”

“‘He took the two hundred francs, and an ironical smile crossed his face, as if to say, “Aha! So she has paid, has she? ... Faith, so much the better!” I read the Countess’ future in his face. That good-looking, fair-haired young man is a heartless gambler; he will ruin himself, her, her husband, and the children, devouring their portions and wreaking more havoc in Parisian salons than a battery of howitzers.

“‘I returned to see Mlle. Fanny in the Rue Montmartre, climbed a steep, narrow staircase, and reached a two-room dwelling on the fifth floor. Everything was as neat as a new ducat. I saw no dust on the furniture in the first room, where Mlle. Fanny sat. She was a young Parisian girl, quietly dressed, with a delicate fresh face and a winning look. The arrangement of her neatly brushed chestnut hair in a double curve on her forehead lent a refined expression to her blue eyes, clear as crystal. The daylight streaming through the short curtains softened the light on her girlish face. A pile of shaped linen indicated she was a sempstress. She looked like a spirit of solitude. When I held out the bill, I noted she had not been home when I called in the morning.

“‘“But the money was left with the porter’s wife,” she said.

“‘I pretended not to understand.

“‘“You go out early, mademoiselle, it seems.”

“‘“I very seldom leave my room; but when you work all night, you must take a bath sometimes.”

“‘I looked at her. A glance revealed her life. Here was a girl condemned by misfortune to toil, from honest farmer folk, with a freckle or two indicating her country birth. There was an indefinable atmosphere of goodness about her; I felt as if I were breathing sincerity and frank innocence. It was refreshing. Poor innocent child, she had faith in something; a crucifix and a sprig of green box adorned her little painted wooden bedstead. I felt touched, inclined to offer to charge no more than twelve percent, helping her establish a good business.

“‘But maybe she has a cousin who would raise money on her signature and sponge on her,’ I thought.

“‘So I left, keeping my generous impulses in check; I have often observed that when benevolence harms no one who gives it, it ruins those who take it. When you came in, I was thinking that Fanny Malvaut would make a nice little wife; I was considering the contrast between her pure, lonely life and the Countess’s life—she has already sunk as low as a bill of exchange; she will sink to the lowest depths of degradation before she is done!’ I scrutinized him during the deep silence that followed, but he spoke again. ‘Well,’ he said, ‘do you think it is nothing to have this power of insight into the deepest recesses of the human heart, to embrace so many lives, to see the naked truth underlying it all? There are no two dramas alike: hideous sores, deadly chagrins, love scenes, misery that will soon lie under the Seine, young men’s joys leading to the scaffold, laughter of despair, and sumptuous banquets. Yesterday was a tragedy. A father drowned himself because he could not support his family. Tomorrow will be a comedy; some young man will try to rehearse the scene of M. Dimanche, brought up to date. You have heard people extol the eloquence of our latter-day preachers; I have wasted time listening to them; they changed my opinions, but never my conduct. Well, well; these good priests and your Mirabeaus and Vergniauds are mere stammering beginners compared to my orators.

“‘Often it is a girl in love, a gray-headed merchant on the verge of bankruptcy, a mother concealing her son’s wrongdoing, a starving artist, or a great man whose influence wanes, who pleads for me. Their power of pleading makes me shudder. Sublime actors play for me, for an audience of one, and they cannot deceive me. I can see their inmost thoughts, read them as God reads them. Nothing is hidden from me. The holder of the purse-strings can loose and bind. I am rich enough to buy the consciences of those who control ministers, from their office boys to their mistresses. Is that not power? I can possess the fairest women, receive their softest caresses; is that not Pleasure? And is not your whole social economy summed up in terms of Power and Pleasure?

“‘There are ten of us in Paris, silent, unknown kings, arbiters of your destinies. What is life but a machine set in motion by money? Know this for certain—methods are always confounded with results; you will never separate the soul from the senses, spirit from matter. Gold is the spiritual basis of existing society. The ten of us are bound by common interest; we meet certain days at the Cafe Themis near the Pont Neuf, revealing the mysteries of finance. No fortune can deceive us; we possess family secrets in all directions. We keep a kind of Black Book, noting the most important bills issued, drafts on public credit, or on banks, or given and taken in business. We are the Casuists of the Paris Bourse, a kind of Inquisition weighing and analyzing every man of fortune’s actions, and our forecasts are infallible. One of us oversees the judicial world, another the financial, another the administrative, and yet another the business world. I keep an eye on eldest sons, artists, people in high society, and gamblers—on the most sensational side of Paris. Everyone who comes to us reveals their neighbor’s secrets. Thwarted passion and mortified vanity are great babblers. Vice, disappointment, and vindictiveness are the best detectives. My colleagues and I have enjoyed all things, are sated with all things, and have reached the point where power and money are loved for their own sake.

“‘Here,’ he said, indicating his bare, chilly room, ‘the most high-mettled gallant, who chafes at a word and draws swords for a syllable elsewhere, will entreat with clasped hands. No city merchant is so proud, no woman so vain of her beauty, no soldier so bold, but they all entreat me here, with tears of rage or anguish in their eyes. Here they kneel—the famous artist, the man of letters whose name will go down to posterity. Here, in short, all inheritances and concerns of all Paris are weighed in the balance. Are you still of the opinion that there are no delights behind the blank mask that has often amazed you with its impassiveness?’ he asked, stretching out that livid face that reeked of money.

“I returned to my room, feeling stupefied. The little, wizened old man had grown great. He had transformed into a strange visionary symbol; he had become the power of gold personified. I shrank from life and my kind.

“‘Is it really so?’ I thought; ‘must everything be resolved into gold?’

“I remember it was long before I slept that night. I envisioned heaps of gold surrounding me. My thoughts were filled with the lovely Countess; I confess, to my shame, that the vision eclipsed another quiet, innocent figure—the woman who had entered a life of toil and obscurity. But the next morning, through the clouds of slumber, Fanny’s sweet face rose before me in all its beauty, and I thought of nothing else.”

“Will you take a glass of _eau sucree_?” asked the Vicomtesse, interrupting Derville.

“I should be glad of it.”

“But I see nothing here that concerns us,” said Mme. de Grandlieu, ringing the bell.

“Sardanapalus!” cried Derville, invoking his favorite phrase. “Mademoiselle Camille’s happiness once depended on Daddy Gobseck; but with his death at ninety, M. de Restaud will soon inherit a fortune. This needs explanation. As for poor Fanny Malvaut, you know her; she is my wife.”

“Poor fellow, he would admit that, with his usual frankness, in front of a crowd!” said the Vicomtesse.

“I would proclaim it to the universe,” said the attorney.

“Go on, drink your glass, my poor Derville. You will always be the happiest and best of men.”

“I left you in the Rue du Helder,” remarked the uncle, waking from a gentle doze. “You had gone to see a Countess; what became of her?”

“A few days after my conversation with the old Dutchman,” Derville continued, “I submitted my thesis, became a licentiate in law, and then an advocate. The old miser’s opinion of me improved. He consulted me (for free) on tricky business, which would have seemed risky to any ordinary practitioner. This man, who appeared immune to influence, listened to my advice with respect, and it often turned out well.

“Eventually, I became head-clerk in the office where I had worked for three years and then moved into my employer’s house. I had board and lodging and a salary of one hundred and fifty francs per month. It was a great day for me!

“When I went to bid the usurer goodbye, he showed no emotion, neither cordial nor regretful, and did not ask me to visit him again, only giving me a glance that seemed to reveal a second sight.

“A week later, my old neighbor came to see me with a thorny business matter, an expropriation, and continued to seek my advice as if I were still his clerk.

“My principal was a man of pleasure and expensive tastes; by the end of the second year (1818-1819), he found himself in difficulties and had to sell his practice. Back then, a professional connection didn’t fetch the exorbitant prices of today, and my principal asked for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. An active, competent man might hope to pay off the capital in ten years, living respectably in the meantime—if he could command confidence. But as the seventh child of a small tradesman from Noyon, I had no money and no connections except Daddy Gobseck. An ambitious idea and a glimmer of hope encouraged me. I went to Gobseck, my heart pounding as I knocked on his door in the gloomy house, recalling his warnings about the anguish awaiting those who crossed his threshold. Now, I was about to beg like so many others.

“‘Well, no, not _that_,’ I told myself; ‘an honest man must keep his self-respect. Success is not worth cringing for; let’s show him a front as determined as his own.’

“Daddy Gobseck had taken my room since I left, so as to have no neighbor; he had even added a grated window to his door and didn’t open until he had looked at me.

“‘So your principal is selling his practice?’ he said.

“How did you know that?” I asked, surprised.

“‘Nothing else would have brought you here,’ he replied dryly.

“‘Listen to me, M. Gobseck,’ I began, trying to maintain my composure. ‘I am a penniless clerk, and only you can understand my future. Let’s leave feelings aside. My principal’s practice is worth about twenty thousand francs per annum; in my hands, I believe it could bring in forty thousand. He is willing to sell it for one hundred and fifty thousand francs. If you would lend me the purchase money, I could pay it off in ten years.’

“‘Come, that is plain speaking,’ said Daddy Gobseck, shaking my hand. ‘Nobody has stated their motives more clearly. Guarantees?’ he asked, scanning me. ‘None to give,’ he added after a pause. ‘How old are you?’

“‘Twenty-five in ten days,’ I replied.

“‘Precisely. Well?’

“‘It is possible.’

“‘We must be quick about it, or I shall have someone buying over my head.’

“‘Bring your birth certificate tomorrow morning, and we will talk. I will think it over.’

“Next morning, at eight o’clock, I stood in the old man’s room. He took the document, put on his spectacles, coughed, and read it through. Then he said, ‘We will try to arrange this business.’

“I trembled.

“‘I make fifty percent on my capital,’ he continued, ‘sometimes I make a hundred, two hundred, five hundred percent.’

“I turned pale.

“‘But as we are acquaintances, I shall be satisfied to take twelve and a half percent per—well, yes, from you I would be content to take thirteen percent per annum. Will that suit you?’

“‘Yes,’ I answered.

“‘But if it is too much, stand up for yourself, Grotius!’ (a name he jokingly gave me). ‘When I ask for thirteen percent, it is all in the way of business; look into it, see if you can pay it; I don’t like a man to agree too easily. Is it too much?’

“‘No,’ I said, ‘I will make up for it by working a little harder.’

“‘Gad! your clients will pay for it!’ he said, looking at me wickedly.

“‘No, by all the devils in hell!’ I cried, ‘it shall be I who pays. I would sooner cut my hand off than flay people.’

“‘Good-night,’ said Daddy Gobseck.

“‘Fees are all according to scale,’ I added.

“‘Not for compromises and settlements out of court, and cases where litigants come to terms,’ he replied. ‘You can send in a bill for thousands of francs, six thousand even at a swoop, depending on the case’s importance. A man must learn to look out for business of this kind. I will recommend you as a competent attorney. I will send you so much work that your colleagues will be fit to burst with envy. Werbrust, Palma, and Gigonnet, my cronies, shall hand over their expropriations to you; they have plenty of them! So you will have two practices—the one you are buying and the other I will build up for you. You ought almost to pay me fifteen percent on my loan.’

“‘So be it, but no more,’ I said firmly.

“Daddy Gobseck’s face relaxed; he looked pleased.

“‘I shall pay the money over to your principal myself, to establish a lien on the purchase and caution-money.’

“‘Oh, anything you like in the way of guarantees.’

“‘And you will give me bills for the amount made payable to a third party (name left blank), fifteen bills of ten thousand francs each.’

“‘Well, so long as it is acknowledged in writing that this is a double----’

“‘No!’ Gobseck interrupted. ‘Why should I trust you any more than you trust me?’

“I kept silent.

“‘Furthermore,’ he continued, ‘you will give me your advice without charging fees as long as I live, will you not?’

“‘So be it; as long as there is no outlay.’

“‘Precisely,’ he said. ‘Ah, by the by, you will allow me to visit you?’ (The old man found it hard to assume an air of good humor.)

“‘I shall always be glad.’

“‘Ah! yes, but it would be difficult to arrange in the morning. You will have your affairs to attend to, and I have mine.’

“‘Then come in the evening.’

“‘Oh, no!’ he replied briskly, ‘you ought to go into society and see your clients, and I have my friends at my café.’

“‘His friends!’ I thought. ‘Very well,’ I said, ‘why not come at dinner-time?’

“‘That is the time,’ said Gobseck, ‘after ‘Change, at five o’clock. Good, you will see me Wednesdays and Saturdays. We will talk over business like friends. Aha! I am gay sometimes. Just give me the wing of a partridge and a glass of champagne, and we will chat. I know many things that can be told now at this distance of time; I will teach you to know men, and what is more—women!’

“‘Oh! a partridge and a glass of champagne if you like.’

“‘Don’t do anything foolish, or I shall lose my faith in you. And don’t set up housekeeping grandly. Just one old general servant. I will come and see that you keep your health. I have capital invested in your head, he! he! so I must look after you. There, come round in the evening and bring your principal with you!’

“‘Would you mind telling me, if there is no harm in asking, what was the good of my birth certificate in this business?’ I asked as we stood on the doorstep.

“Jean-Esther Van Gobseck shrugged his shoulders, smiled maliciously, and said, ‘What blockheads youngsters are! Learn, master attorney (for learn you must if you don’t want to be taken in), that integrity and brains in a man under thirty are commodities that can be mortgaged. After that age, there is no counting on a man.’

“And with that, he shut the door.

“Three months later, I was an attorney. Soon after, madame, I had the good fortune to undertake the suit for the recovery of your estates. I won, and my name became known. Despite the exorbitant interest, I paid off Gobseck in less than five years. I married Fanny Malvaut, whom I loved deeply. Our parallel lives, our hard work and luck, strengthened our bond. One of her uncles, a wealthy farmer, died and left her seventy thousand francs, which helped clear the loan. Since then, my life has been nothing but happiness and prosperity. Nothing is more utterly uninteresting than a happy man, so let’s return to the other characters.

“About a year after purchasing the practice, I was dragged into a bachelor breakfast-party given by one of our number who had lost a bet to a young man greatly in vogue in fashionable circles. M. de Trailles, the epitome of dandyism, enjoyed a prodigious reputation.”

“But he is still enjoying it,” interjected the Comte de Born. “No one wears clothes with a finer air, nor drives a tandem with better grace. Maxime can gamble, eat, and drink more gracefully than anyone. He is a judge of horses, hats, and pictures. All the women lose their heads over him. He spends about a hundred thousand francs a year, and no one can discover that he has an acre of land or a single dividend warrant. The typical knight-errant of our salons, our boudoirs, our boulevards—Maxime de Trailles is a singular being, fit for anything, and good for nothing, capable of both noble and base acts, more often bespattered with mire than blood, knowing more anxiety than remorse, more concerned with his digestion than any mental process, shamming passion, feeling nothing. Maxime de Trailles is a brilliant link between the hulks and the best society; he belongs to the eminently intelligent class from which a Mirabeau, or a Pitt, or a Richelieu springs at times, though it more often produces Counts of Horn, Fouquier-Tinvilles, and Coignards.”

“Well,” Derville continued, “I had heard much about this individual from poor old Goriot, a client of mine, and I had already taken pains to avoid the dangerous honor of his acquaintance, as I sometimes encountered him in society. Still, my friend was so insistent about this breakfast-party that I could hardly refuse without earning a reputation for squeamishness. Madame, you could hardly imagine what a bachelor’s breakfast-party is like. It means superb display and a studied refinement seldom seen; the luxury of a miser when vanity leads him to be sumptuous for a day.

“You are surprised as you enter the room at the neatness of the table, dazzling with silver, crystal, and damask linen. Life is in full bloom; the young men are graceful, smiling, and speaking in low, demure voices like brides; everything about them looks girlish. Two hours later, you might mistake the room for a battlefield after a fight. Broken glasses, crumpled serviettes lie strewn among the nauseous remnants of food. There is an uproar of jesting toasts, a fire of witticisms and bad jokes; faces are flushed, eyes inflamed and expressionless, unintentional confidences reveal the truth. Bottles are smashed, songs are sung amidst a diabolical racket; men call each other out, hang on each other’s necks, or fall to fisticuffs; the room is filled with a horrid scent made up of a hundred odors, and noise enough for a hundred voices. No one knows what he is eating, drinking, or saying. Some are depressed, others babble, one turns monomaniac, repeating the same word like a bell; another tries to keep the tumult in check; the steadiest proposes an orgy. If a sober person were to enter, he would think he had interrupted a Bacchanalian rite.

“It was in the midst of such chaos that M. de Trailles tried to ingratiate himself with me. My head was clear, and I was on guard. He pretended to be drunk, but was perfectly cool and knew what he was about. Somehow, by the end of the evening, I had promised to introduce him to our Papa Gobseck. The words ‘honor,’ ‘virtue,’ ‘countess,’ ‘honest woman,’ and ‘ill-luck’ were mingled in his discourse with a magical potency, thanks to his golden tongue.

“When I awoke the next morning, trying to recollect the previous day, I struggled to piece together my impressions. Eventually, it seemed that the daughter of one of my clients was in danger of losing her reputation and her husband’s love unless she could gather fifty thousand francs that morning. There had been gaming debts and other accounts. My charming companion had impressed upon me that she was rich enough to cover these reverses with a few years of economy. Only then did I begin to grasp the urgency of his request. I confess, to my shame, that I had no doubt it was crucial for Daddy Gobseck to reconcile with this dandy. I was dressing when the young gentleman appeared.

“‘M. le Comte,’ I said after the usual greetings, ‘I fail to see why you need me to introduce you to Van Gobseck, the most civil and smooth-spoken of capitalists. Money will be forthcoming if he has any, or rather, if you can provide adequate security.’

“‘Monsieur,’ he replied, ‘I do not wish to force you to do me a service, even though you have given your word.’

“‘Sardanapalus!’ I thought, ‘am I going to let him think I won’t keep my word?’

“‘I told you yesterday,’ he said, ‘that I had fallen out with Daddy Gobseck most inopportunely; and as there is scarcely another man in Paris who can provide a hundred thousand francs at the end of the month, I begged you to make my peace with him. But let us say no more about it----’

“M. de Trailles looked at me with a civil insult, making as if to leave.

“‘I am ready to go with you,’ I said.

“When we reached the Rue de Gres, my dandy looked around with a circumspection and unease that puzzled me. His face changed color, and by the time we reached Gobseck’s door, sweat stood in drops on his forehead. Just as we were getting out of the cabriolet, a hackney cab turned into the street. His hawk eye detected a woman inside. His face lit up with a gleam of almost savage joy; he called to a little boy passing by and gave him his horse to hold. Then we approached the old bill discounter.

“‘M. Gobseck,’ I said, ‘I have brought one of my closest friends to see you (whom I trust as I would trust the Devil,’ I added for the old man’s private ear). ‘To oblige me, you will do your best for him (at the ordinary rate), and pull him out of his difficulty (if it suits your convenience).’

“M. de Trailles made his bow to Gobseck, took a seat, and listened with a courtier-like attitude; its charming humility would have touched your heart. But Gobseck sat impassively by the fireside, resembling the statue of Voltaire under the peristyle of the Theatre-Francais. He had partly risen as if to bow, and the skull cap covering his head, along with the narrow strip of sallow forehead, completed his likeness to the man of marble.

“‘I have no money to spare except for my own clients,’ he said.

“‘So you are cross because I may have tried in other quarters to ruin myself?’ laughed the Count.

“‘Ruin yourself!’ Gobseck repeated ironically.

“‘Were you about to say that it is impossible to ruin a man who has nothing?’ the dandy asked. ‘I defy you to find a better _stock_ in Paris!’ he cried, turning on his heels.

“This half-earnest buffoonery produced no effect on Gobseck.

“‘Am I not on intimate terms with the Ronquerolles, the Marsays, the Franchessinis, the two Vandenesses, the Ajuda-Pintos—all the most fashionable young men in Paris? A prince and an ambassador (you know them both) are my partners at play. I draw my revenues from London and Carlsbad and Baden and Bath. Is not this the most brilliant of all industries!’

“‘True.’

“‘You make a sponge of me, begad! You encourage me to swell myself in society, so you can squeeze me when I am hard up; but you yourselves are sponges, just as I am, and death will squeeze you someday.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘If there were no spendthrifts, what would become of you? The pair of us are like soul and body.’

“‘Precisely so.’

“‘Come, now, give us your hand, Grandaddy Gobseck, and be magnanimous if this is “true” and “possible” and “precisely so.”’

“‘You come to me,’ the usurer answered coldly, ‘because Girard, Palma, Werbrust, and Gigonnet are full up of your paper; they are offering it at a loss of fifty percent; and as they likely only gave you half the figure on the face of the bills, they are not worth twenty-five percent of their supposed value. I am your most obedient! Can I in common decency lend a stiver to a man who owes thirty thousand francs and has not one farthing?’ Gobseck continued. ‘The day before yesterday, you lost ten thousand francs at a ball at the Baron de Nucingen’s.’

“‘Sir,’ said the Count, with rare impudence, ‘my affairs are no concern of yours,’ and he scrutinized the old man. ‘A man has no debts until payment is due.’

“‘True.’

“‘My bills will be duly met.’

“‘That is possible.’

“‘And at this moment, the question between you and me is simply whether the security I am going to offer is sufficient for the sum I have come to borrow.’

“‘Precisely.’

“A cab stopped at the door, and the sound of wheels filled the room.

“‘I will bring something directly which perhaps will satisfy you,’ cried the young man, and he left the room.

“‘Oh! my son,’ exclaimed Gobseck, rising and stretching out his arms to me, ‘if he has good security, you have saved my life. It would be the death of me. Werbrust and Gigonnet thought they were going to trick me; and now, thanks to you, I shall have a good laugh at their expense tonight.’

“There was something frightful about the old man’s ecstasy. It was the one occasion he opened his heart to me; that flash of joy, swift though it was, will never be effaced from my memory.

“‘Favor me by staying here,’ he added. ‘I am armed and a sure shot. I have gone tiger-hunting and fought on deck when there was nothing for it but to win or die; but I don’t care to trust yonder elegant scoundrel.’

“He sat down again in his armchair before his bureau, and his face grew pale and impassive as before.

“‘Ah!’ he continued, turning to me, ‘you will see that lovely creature I once told you about; I can hear a fine lady’s step in the corridor; it is she, no doubt;’ and indeed, the young man returned with a woman on his arm. I recognized the Countess, one of old Goriot’s two daughters.

“The Countess did not see me at first; I stayed in the window bay, my face against the pane; but I saw her give Maxime a suspicious glance as she entered the money-lender’s damp, dark room. She was so beautiful that, despite her faults, I felt sorry for her. There was a storm of anguish in her heart; her proud features were drawn with pain she strove to disguise. The young man had become her evil genius. I admired Gobseck, whose perspicacity had foreseen their future four years ago at the first bill she endorsed.

“‘Probably,’ I thought, ‘this monster with the angel face controls every possible spring of action in her: rules her through vanity, jealousy, pleasure, and the current of life in the world.’”

The Vicomtesse de Grandlieu interrupted the story.

“Why, the woman’s very virtues have been turned against her,” she exclaimed. “He has made her shed tears of devotion, then abused her kindness and made her pay dearly for unhallowed bliss.”

Derville did not understand the signs Mme. de Grandlieu made to him.

“I confess,” he said, “that I had no inclination to shed tears over the lot of this unhappy creature, so brilliant in society, so repulsive to eyes that could read her heart; I shuddered rather at the sight of her murderer, a young angel with a clear brow, red lips, white teeth, and a winning smile. There they stood before their judge, he scrutinizing them much as a fifteenth-century Dominican inquisitor might have peered into the dungeons of the Holy Office while the torture was administered to two Moors.

“The Countess spoke tremulously. ‘Sir,’ she said, ‘is there any way of obtaining the value of these diamonds, and of keeping the right of repurchase?’ She held out a jewel-case.

“‘Yes, madame,’ I interjected, stepping forward.

“She looked at me, and a shudder ran through her as she recognized me, giving me the glance that means, ‘Say nothing of this,’ all over the world.

“‘This,’ I said, ‘constitutes a sale with faculty of redemption, a formal agreement to transfer and deliver a piece of property, either real estate or personalty, for a given time, after which the previous owner recovers their title upon payment of a stipulated sum.’

“She breathed more freely. The Count looked black; he doubted whether Gobseck would lend much on the diamonds after such a fall in their value. Gobseck, impassive as ever, took up his magnifying glass and scrutinized the jewels. If I were to live for a hundred years, I would never forget the sight of his face at that moment. There was a flush in his pale cheeks; his eyes seemed to catch the sparkle of the stones, for there was an unnatural glitter in them. He rose and went to the light, holding the diamonds close to his toothless mouth, as if he meant to devour them; mumbling vague words over them, holding up bracelets, sprays, necklaces, and tiaras one after another, to judge their quality; taking them out of the jewel-case and putting them back, letting the light bring out all their fires. He was more like a child than an old man; or rather, childhood and dotage seemed to meet in him.

“‘Fine stones! This set would have fetched three hundred thousand francs before the Revolution. What water! Genuine Asiatic diamonds from Golconda or Visapur. Do you know their worth? No one in Paris but Gobseck can appreciate them. In the Empire, such a set would have cost another two hundred thousand francs!’

He shrugged in disgust and added, ‘But now diamonds are losing value daily. The Brazilians have flooded the market since the Peace; Indian stones are a better color. Others wear them now besides court ladies. Does madame go to court?’

As he spoke, he examined each stone with indescribable delight. ‘Flawless!’ he exclaimed. ‘Here is a speck!... here is a flaw!... A fine stone that!’

His haggard face lit up by the sparkling jewels reminded me of a dingy old mirror in a country inn, reflecting every luminous image without revealing the light, leaving a traveler to see a man in an apoplectic fit.

‘Well?’ asked the Count, clapping Gobseck on the shoulder.

The old man trembled, set down his playthings, and resumed his role as a money-lender—hard, cold, and polished as marble.

‘How much do you want?’

‘One hundred thousand francs for three years,’ said the Count.

‘That is possible,’ replied Gobseck, pulling out a pair of scales from a mahogany box. He weighed the diamonds, calculating their value while delight and severity struggled on his face. The Countess seemed in a stupor, perhaps contemplating the abyss into which she had fallen. I sensed remorse still lingered in her soul. Perhaps a hand held out in charity might save her. I would try.

‘Are the diamonds your personal property, madame?’ I asked clearly.

‘Yes, monsieur,’ she replied, meeting my gaze with pride.

‘Make out the deed of purchase with power of redemption, chatterbox,’ Gobseck instructed me, relinquishing his chair at the bureau.

‘Madame is undoubtedly a married woman?’ I pressed.

She nodded abruptly.

‘Then I will not draw up the deed,’ I stated.

‘And why not?’ asked Gobseck.

‘Why not?’ I echoed, drawing Gobseck aside to speak privately. ‘This woman is under her husband’s control; the agreement would be void in law. You could not possibly ignore a fact recorded on the document itself. You would be compelled to return the diamonds according to the weight, value, and cutting described.’

Gobseck nodded and turned to the guilty couple. ‘He is right! That changes everything. Eighty thousand francs down, and you leave the diamonds with me,’ he added in a husky voice. ‘In property, possession is as good as title.’

‘But----’ the young man objected.

‘You can take it or leave it,’ Gobseck replied, returning the jewel-case to the lady.

‘Too many risks to run,’ I whispered to her.

Gobseck likely understood my words from my lips’ movement. He shot me a cool glance. The Count’s face turned livid, and the Countess wavered visibly. Maxime approached her, whispering, ‘Adieu, dear Anastasie, may you be happy! As for me, by tomorrow my troubles will be over.’

‘Sir!’ cried the lady, turning to Gobseck. ‘I accept your offer.’

‘Come now,’ Gobseck replied. ‘You have taken long enough to come to it, my fair lady.’

He wrote a cheque for fifty thousand francs on the Bank of France and handed it to the Countess. ‘Now,’ he continued with a smile reminiscent of M. Voltaire, ‘I will give you the rest in bills, thirty thousand francs’ worth of paper as good as bullion. This gentleman here has just said, “My bills will be met when they are due,”’ he added, producing drafts bearing the Count’s signature, all protested the day before at the request of some confraternity, likely transferred to him at a reduced figure.

The young man muttered something, including ‘Old scoundrel!’ Gobseck remained unfazed, pulling out a pair of pistols from a pigeonhole. ‘As the insulted man, I fire first.’

‘Maxime, you owe this gentleman an explanation,’ the trembling Countess urged in a low voice.

‘I had no intention of giving offense,’ Maxime stammered.

‘Quite sure of that,’ Gobseck replied calmly; ‘you had no intention of meeting your bills, that was all.’

The Countess rose, bowed, and left, dread gnawing at her. M. de Trailles followed but managed to say, ‘If either of you gentlemen forget yourselves, I will have his blood, or he will have mine.’

‘Amen!’ Gobseck called as he put his pistols away. ‘But a man must have blood in his veins before he can risk it, my son, and you have nothing but mud in yours.’

Once the door closed and the vehicles departed, Gobseck began to prance about. ‘I have the diamonds! I have the diamonds!’ he cried repeatedly, ‘the beautiful diamonds! such diamonds! and tolerably cheaply. Aha! aha! Werbrust and Gigonnet, you thought you had old Papa Gobseck! _Ego sum papa_! I am master of the lot of you! Paid! paid, principal and interest! How silly they will look tonight when I come out with this story between two games of dominoes!’

His dark glee and savage ferocity at possessing a few water-white pebbles left me shuddering. I was dumbfounded.

‘Aha! There you are, my boy!’ he said. ‘We will dine together. We will have some fun at your place, for I haven’t a home of my own, and these restaurants, with their broths, sauces, and wines, would poison the Devil himself.’

Something in my face suddenly returned his usual cold, impassive expression.

‘You don’t understand it,’ he said, sitting by the hearth and placing a tin saucepan full of milk on the brazier. ‘Will you breakfast with me?’ he continued. ‘Perhaps there will be enough here for two.’

‘Thanks,’ I replied, ‘I do not breakfast till noon.’

Before I finished speaking, hurried footsteps sounded from the passage. A stranger knocked at Gobseck’s door, his knock suggesting a man consumed with rage. Gobseck peered through the grating, then opened the door to a man of about thirty-five, seemingly harmless despite his anger. The newcomer resembled the late Duc de Richelieu; he was the Countess’ husband, a man with the aristocratic figure typical of statesmen from your faubourg.

‘Sir,’ he addressed Gobseck, who had regained his composure, ‘did my wife just leave this house?’

‘That is possible.’

‘Well, sir? Do you not understand my meaning?’

‘I have not the honor of knowing your lady wife,’ Gobseck replied. ‘I have had many visitors this morning, women and men, and mannish young ladies, and young gentlemen who look like young ladies. I would find it hard to say----’

‘A truce to jesting, sir! I mean the woman who just left you.’

‘How can I know whether she is your wife or not? I never had the pleasure of seeing you before.’

‘You are mistaken, M. Gobseck,’ the Count said with profound irony. ‘We have met before, one morning in my wife’s bedroom. You came to demand payment for a bill—no bill of hers.’

‘It was no business of mine to inquire what value she had received for it,’ Gobseck replied, casting a malignant look at the Count. ‘I had come by the bill in the way of business. At the same time, monsieur,’ he continued, quietly pouring coffee into his bowl of milk, ‘you will permit me to observe that your right to enter my house and expostulate with me is far from proven. I came of age in the sixty-first year of the preceding century.’

‘Sir,’ the Count said, ‘you have just bought family diamonds that do not belong to my wife for a mere trifle.’

‘Without feeling it incumbent upon me to tell you my private affairs, I will say this much, M. le Comte—if Mme. la Comtesse has taken your diamonds, you should have sent a circular to all jewelers, warning them not to buy them; she might have sold them separately.’

‘You know my wife, sir!’ the Count roared.

‘True.’

‘She is under her husband’s power.’

‘That is possible.’

‘She had no right to dispose of those diamonds----’

‘Precisely.’

‘Very well, sir?’

‘Very well, sir. I knew your wife, and she is in her husband’s power; I am quite willing; she is in the power of many people; but—I—do—not—know—your diamonds. If Mme. la Comtesse can put her name to a bill, she can go into business, of course, and buy and sell diamonds on her own account. The thing is plain on the face of it!’

‘Good day, sir!’ the Count cried, now white with rage. ‘There are courts of justice.’

‘Quite so.’

‘This gentleman here,’ he added, indicating me, ‘was a witness to the sale.’

‘That is possible.’

The Count turned to leave. Sensing the gravity of the situation, I interjected between the two. ‘M. le Comte,’ I said, ‘you are right, and M. Gobseck is by no means in the wrong. You could not prosecute the purchaser without bringing your wife into court, and the whole odium would not fall on her. I am an attorney, and I owe it to myself, and still more to my professional position, to declare that the diamonds of which you speak were purchased by M. Gobseck in my presence; but, in my opinion, it would be unwise to dispute the legality of the sale, especially as the goods are not readily recognizable. In equity, our contention would lie; in law, it would collapse. M. Gobseck is too honest a man to deny that the sale was a profitable transaction, especially as my conscience, no less than my duty, compels me to make the admission. But once bring the case into a court of law, M. le Comte, the issue would be doubtful. My advice to you is to come to terms with M. Gobseck, who can plead that he bought the diamonds in good faith; you would be bound in any case to return the purchase money. Consent to an arrangement, with power to redeem at the end of seven or eight months, or a year even, or any convenient lapse of time, for the repayment of the sum borrowed by Mme. la Comtesse, unless you would prefer to repurchase them outright and give security for repayment.’

Gobseck dipped his bread into the bowl of coffee and ate with perfect indifference; but at the words ‘come to terms,’ he looked at me as if to say, ‘A fine fellow that! He has learned something from my lessons!’ I, for my part, responded with a glance he understood well. The business was dubious and shady; there was pressing need to come to terms. Gobseck could not deny all knowledge of it, for I would appear as a witness. The Count thanked me with a smile of goodwill.

In the ensuing debate, Gobseck displayed enough greed and skill to baffle a congress of diplomats; ultimately, I drafted an instrument in which the Count acknowledged receipt of eighty-five thousand francs, interest included, in consideration of which Gobseck undertook to return the diamonds to the Count.

‘What waste!’ he exclaimed as he signed the agreement. ‘How is it possible to bridge such a gulf?’

‘Have you many children, sir?’ Gobseck asked gravely.

The Count winced at the question; it was as if the old money-lender, like an experienced physician, had pinpointed the sore spot. The Comtesse’s husband did not reply.

‘Well,’ Gobseck said, taking the pained silence for an answer, ‘I know your story by heart. The woman is a fiend, but perhaps you love her still; I can well believe it; she made an impression on me. Perhaps, too, you would rather save your fortune for one or two of your children? Well, fling yourself into the whirlpool of society, lose that fortune at play, come to Gobseck pretty often. The world will say that I am a Jew, a Tartar, a usurer, a pirate, will say that I have ruined you! I snap my fingers at them! If anybody insults me, I lay my man out; nobody is a surer shot nor handles a rapier better than your servant. And everyone knows it. Then, have a friend—if you can find one—and make over your property to him by a fictitious sale. You call that a _fidei commissum_, don’t you?’ he asked, turning to me.

The Count seemed entirely absorbed in his thoughts.

‘You shall have your money tomorrow,’ he said, ‘have the diamonds in readiness,’ and he left.

‘There goes one who looks to me to be as stupid as an honest man,’ Gobseck said coolly when the Count had gone.

‘Say rather stupid as a man of passionate nature.’

‘The Count owes you your fee for drawing up the agreement!’ Gobseck called after me as I took my leave.

A few days after the scene that initiated me into the depths of a woman of fashion’s life, the Count came into my private office.

‘I have come to consult you on a matter of grave moment,’ he said, ‘and I begin by telling you that I have perfect confidence in you, as I hope to prove. Your behavior to Mme. de Grandlieu is above all praise,’ the Count continued. (You see, madame, that you have paid me a thousand times over for a very simple matter.)

I bowed respectfully and replied that I had done nothing but my duty as an honest man.

‘Well,’ the Count continued, ‘I have made many inquiries about the singular personage to whom you owe your position. From all I can learn, Gobseck is a philosopher of the Cynic school. What do you think of his probity?’

‘M. le Comte,’ I began, ‘Gobseck is my benefactor—at fifteen percent,’ I added, laughing. ‘But his avarice does not authorize me to paint him to the life for a stranger’s benefit.’

‘Speak out, sir. Your frankness cannot injure Gobseck or yourself. I do not expect to find an angel in a pawnbroker.’

‘Daddy Gobseck,’ I began, ‘is intimately convinced of the truth of the principle he takes for a rule of life. In his opinion, money is a commodity that you may sell cheap or dear, according to circumstances, with a clear conscience. A capitalist, by charging a high rate of interest, becomes in his eyes a secured partner by anticipation. Apart from his peculiar philosophical views of human nature and financial principles, which enable him to behave like a usurer, I am fully persuaded that, out of his business, he is the most loyal and upright soul in Paris. There are two men in him; he is petty and great—a miser and a philosopher. If I were to die and leave a family behind me, he would be the guardian I would appoint. This is how I came to see Gobseck in this light, monsieur. I know nothing of his past life. He may have been a pirate, may, for anything I know, trafficked in diamonds, or men, or women, or State secrets; but I affirm this of him—never has a human soul been more thoroughly tempered and tried. When I paid off my loan, I asked him, with a little circumlocution, of course, how it was that he had made me pay such an exorbitant rate of interest; and why, seeing that I was a friend, and he meant to do me a kindness, he should not have yielded to the wish and made it complete. “My son,” he said, “I released you from all need to feel any gratitude by giving you ground for the belief that you owed me nothing.” So we are the best friends in the world. That answer, monsieur, gives you the man better than any amount of description.’

‘I have made up my mind once and for all,’ said the Count. ‘Draw up the necessary papers; I am going to transfer my property to Gobseck. I have no one but you to trust in the draft of the counter-deed, which will declare that this transfer is a simulated sale, and that Gobseck as trustee will administer my estate (as he knows how to administer), and undertakes to make over my fortune to my eldest son when he comes of age. Now, sir, this I must tell you: I should be afraid to have that precious document in my own keeping. My boy is so fond of his mother that I cannot trust him with it. So dare I beg of you to keep it for me? In case of death, Gobseck would make you legatee of my property. Every contingency is provided for.’

The Count paused, appearing greatly agitated.

‘A thousand pardons,’ he said at length; ‘I am in great pain and have grave misgivings as to my health. Recent troubles have disturbed me painfully and forced me to take this great step.’

‘Allow me first to thank you, monsieur,’ I said, ‘for the trust you place in me. But I must deserve it by pointing out that you are disinheriting your other children. They bear your name. Merely as the children of a once-loved wife, now fallen from her position, they have a claim to an assured existence. I tell you plainly that I cannot accept the trust you propose to honor me with unless their future is secured.’

The Count trembled violently at my words, and tears came to his eyes as he grasped my hand, saying, ‘I did not know my man thoroughly. You have made me both glad and sorry. We will make provision for the children in the counter-deed.’

I accompanied him to the door; it seemed there was a glow of satisfaction on his face at the thought of this act of justice.

Now, Camille, this is how a young wife takes the first step to the brink of a precipice. A quadrille, a ballad, a picnic party is sometimes cause enough for frightful evils. You are hurried on by the presumptuous voice of vanity and pride, on the faith of a smile, or through giddiness and folly! Shame, misery, and remorse are three Furies awaiting every woman the moment she oversteps the limits.

“Poor Camille can hardly keep awake,” the Vicomtesse hastily interrupted. “Go to bed, child; you have no need of appalling pictures to keep you pure in heart and conduct.”

Camille de Grandlieu took the hint and left.

“You were going rather too far, dear M. Derville,” said the Vicomtesse. “An attorney is not a mother of daughters nor yet a preacher.”

“But any newspaper is a thousand times----”

“Poor Derville!” exclaimed the Vicomtesse. “What has come over you? Do you really imagine that I allow a daughter of mine to read the newspapers?—Go on,” she added after a pause.

“Three months after everything was signed and sealed between the Count and Gobseck----”

“You can call him the Comte de Restaud now that Camille is not here,” said the Vicomtesse.

“So be it! Well, time went by, and I saw nothing of the counter-deed, which by rights should have been in my hands. An attorney in Paris lives in such a whirl of business that, with certain exceptions we make for ourselves, we do not have the time to give each individual client the amount of interest he takes in his affairs. Still, one day when Gobseck came to dine with me, I asked him as we left the table if he knew why I had heard no more of M. de Restaud.

“There are excellent reasons for that,’ he said; ‘the noble Count is at death’s door. He is one of those soft men who cannot learn to put an end to chagrin, allowing it to wear them out instead. Life is a craft, a profession; every man must take the trouble to learn that business. When he has learned what life is through painful experiences, his fiber toughens, acquiring a certain elasticity, so that he has his sensibilities under control; he disciplines himself until his nerves are like steel springs, which always bend but never break; given a sound digestion, a man in such training ought to live as long as the cedars of Lebanon, and famous trees they are.’

‘Then is the Count actually dying?’ I asked.

‘That is possible,’ said Gobseck; ‘the winding up of his estate will be a juicy bit of business for you.’

I looked at him, trying to gauge his thoughts. ‘Just explain to me how it is that we, the Count and I, are the only two in whom you take an interest?’

‘Because you are the only two who have trusted me without finessing,’ he replied.

Although this answer warranted my belief that Gobseck would act fairly even if the counter-deed were lost, I resolved to visit the Count. I pleaded a business engagement, and we parted.

I went straight to the Rue du Helder, where I was shown into a room where the Countess sat playing with her children. Upon hearing my name, she sprang up to meet me, then sat down and pointed silently to a chair by the fire. Her face wore the inscrutable mask women of the world use to conceal their most vehement emotions. Trouble had already withered that face. Nothing of its beauty remained, save the marvelous outlines that had once been its principal charm.

“It is essential, madame, that I speak to M. le Comte----”

“If so, you would be more favored than I am,” she interrupted. “M. de Restaud will see no one. He will hardly allow his doctor to come, and will not be nursed even by me. When people are ill, they have such strange fancies! They are like children, they do not know what they want.”

“Perhaps, like children, they know very well what they want.”

The Countess reddened. I almost regretted my thrust worthy of Gobseck. To change the subject, I added, “But M. de Restaud cannot possibly lie there alone all day, madame.”

“His oldest boy is with him,” she replied.

It was useless to gaze at the Countess; she did not blush this time, and it seemed she was resolved more firmly than ever that I should not penetrate her secrets.

“You must understand, madame, that my proceeding is no way indiscreet. It is strongly to his interest—” I bit my lips, realizing I had gone the wrong way. The Countess seized the opportunity.

“My interests are in no way separate from my husband’s, sir,” she said. “There is nothing to prevent your addressing yourself to me----”

“The business which brings me here concerns no one but M. le Comte,” I stated firmly.

“I will let him know of your wish to see him.”

The civil tone and expression she assumed did not fool me; I sensed she would never allow me to see her husband. I chatted about indifferent matters for a while to study her; but like all women who have begun to plot for themselves, she could dissimulate with rare perfection, which in your sex means the last degree of perfidy. If I may dare to say it, I expected anything from her, even a crime. She produced this feeling in me because it was evident from her manner and everything she did or said, down to the very inflections of her voice, that she had an eye to the future. I left.

Now, I will pass on to the final scenes of this adventure, adding a few circumstances revealed by time and some details guessed by Gobseck’s perspicacity or my own.

When the Comte de Restaud apparently plunged into the vortex of dissipation, something passed between the husband and wife, something that remains an impenetrable secret, but the wife sank even lower in the husband’s eyes. As soon as he became so ill that he had to take to his bed, he manifested his aversion for the Countess and the two youngest children. He forbade them to enter his room, and any attempt to disobey brought on such dangerous attacks that the doctor implored the Countess to submit to her husband’s wishes.

Mme. de Restaud had seen the family estates and property, even the very mansion in which she lived, pass into Gobseck’s hands, who appeared to play the fantastic ogre regarding their wealth. She partially understood what her husband was doing, no doubt. M. de Trailles was traveling in England (his creditors had been a little too pressing of late), and no one else was in a position to enlighten the lady and explain that her husband was taking precautions against her at Gobseck’s suggestion. It is said she held out for a long while before she signed the required document for the sale of the property; nevertheless, the Count gained his point. The Countess was convinced that her husband was realizing his fortune, and that somewhere there would be a little bunch of notes representing the amount; they had been deposited with a notary, or perhaps at the bank, or in some safe hiding-place. Following this train of thought, it was evident that M. de Restaud must have some kind of document in his possession by which any remaining property could be recovered and handed over to his son.

So she resolved to keep the strictest watch over the sick-room. She ruled despotically in the house, and everything in it was submitted to her feminine espionage. All day she sat in the salon adjoining her husband’s room, so she could hear every syllable he uttered, every movement he made. She had a bed put there for her at night, but she did not sleep much. The doctor was entirely in her interests. Such wifely devotion seemed praiseworthy enough. With the natural subtlety of perfidy, she disguised M. de Restaud’s repugnance for her and feigned distress so perfectly that she gained a sort of celebrity. Strait-laced women even said she had expiated her sins. Always before her eyes was a vision of the destitution to follow on the Count’s death if her presence of mind failed her; thus, the wife, repulsed from the bed of pain on which her husband lay and groaned, drew a charmed circle around it. So near, yet kept at a distance; all-powerful, but in disgrace, the apparently devoted wife lay in wait for death and opportunity, crouching like the ant-lion at the bottom of his spiral pit, ever on the watch for the prey that cannot escape, listening to the fall of every grain of sand.

The strictest censor could not but recognize that the Countess pushed maternal sentiment to the last degree. Her father’s death had been a lesson to her, people said. She worshiped her children. They were so young that she could hide the disorders of her life from their eyes and win their love; she had given them the best and most brilliant education. I confess I cannot help admiring her and feeling sorry for her. Gobseck used to joke me about it. Just about that time, she discovered Maxime’s baseness and was expiating the sins of the past in tears of blood. I was sure of it. Hateful as her measures to regain control of her husband’s money were, were they not the result of a mother’s love and a desire to repair the wrongs she had done her children? And again, it may be, like many a woman who has experienced the storm of lawless love, she felt a longing to lead a virtuous life again. Perhaps she only learned the worth of that life when she came to reap the woeful harvest sown by her errors.

Every time little Ernest came out of his father’s room, she put him through a searching examination about all his father had done or said. The boy willingly complied with his mother’s wishes, telling her even more than she asked in his anxious affection.

My visit was a ray of light for the Countess. She was determined to see in me the instrument of the Count’s vengeance and resolved that I should not be allowed to go near the dying man. I augured ill of all this and earnestly wished for an interview, for I was uneasy about the fate of the counter-deed. If it fell into the Countess’ hands, she might turn it to her own account, leading to a series of interminable lawsuits between her and Gobseck. I knew the usurer well enough to feel convinced he would never give up the property to her; there was room for plenty of legal quibbling over a series of transfers, and I alone knew all the ins and outs of the matter. I was determined to prevent such a misfortune, so I went to the Countess a second time.

“I have noticed, madame,” I said, turning to the Vicomtesse and speaking confidentially, “certain moral phenomena to which we do not pay enough attention. I am naturally an observer of human nature, and instinctively I bring a spirit of analysis to the business I transact in the interest of others when human passions are called into play. Now, I have often noticed, and always with new wonder, that two antagonists almost always divine each other’s inmost thoughts and ideas. Two enemies sometimes possess a power of clear insight into mental processes, reading each other’s minds as two lovers read in either soul. So when the Countess and I came together, I understood at once the reason for her antipathy toward me, disguised though it was by gracious forms of politeness and civility. I had been forced to be her confidant, and a woman cannot help but hate the man before whom she is compelled to blush. And she, on her side, knew that if I was the man in whom her husband placed confidence, that husband had not yet given up his fortune.

“I will spare you the conversation, but it abides in my memory as one of the most dangerous encounters in my career. Nature had bestowed on her all the qualities which, combined, are irresistibly fascinating; she could be pliant and proud by turns, and confiding and coaxing in her manner; she even tried to subjugate me. It was a failure. As I took my leave, I caught a gleam of hate and rage in her eyes that made me shudder. We parted enemies. She would fain have crushed me out of existence; and for my part, I felt pity for her, and for some natures, pity is the deadliest of insults. This feeling pervaded the last representations I put before her; and when I left, I think I left dread in the depths of her soul, declaring that, turn which way she would, ruin lay inevitably before her.

‘If I were to see M. le Comte, your children’s property at any rate would----’

‘I should be at your mercy,’ she interrupted, disgust evident in her gesture.

Now that we had spoken frankly, I resolved to save the family from impending destitution. I decided to strain the law as needed to gain my ends, and this is what I did. I sued the Comte de Restaud for a sum of money ostensibly due to Gobseck and gained judgment. The Countess, of course, did not allow him to know of this, but I had gained my point; I had the right to affix seals to everything upon the Count’s death. I bribed one of the servants in the house—the man undertook to inform me at any hour of the day or night if his master should be at death’s door, so I could intervene at once, scare the Countess with a threat of affixing seals, and secure the counter-deed.

I later learned that the woman was studying the Code, with her husband’s dying moans in her ears. If we could picture the thoughts of those who stand about a deathbed, what fearful sights should we not see? Money is always the motive spring of the schemes elaborated, of all the plans made and the plots woven around it! Let us leave these details, nauseating in nature; perhaps they may give you insight into all that this husband and wife endured; perhaps they may unveil much that is passing in secret in other houses.

For two months, the Comte de Restaud lay on his bed, alone and resigned to his fate. Mortal disease slowly sapped his strength of mind and body. Unaccountable and grotesque sick fancies preyed upon him; he would not allow them to set his room in order, no one could nurse him, he would not even permit his wife to attend him. All his surroundings bore the marks of this last degree of apathy; the furniture was out of place, the daintiest trifles covered with dust and cobwebs. In health, he had been a man of refined and expensive tastes; now he delighted in the comfortless look of the room. Rows of medicine bottles, empty and full, most dirty, crumpled linen, and broken plates littered the writing-table, chairs, and chimney-piece. An open warming-pan lay on the floor before the grate; a bath, still full of mineral water, had not been removed. The sense of coming dissolution pervaded all the details of an unsightly chaos. Signs of death appeared in inanimate things before the Destroyer came to the body on the bed. The Comte de Restaud could not bear daylight; the Venetian shutters were closed, darkness deepening the gloom in the dismal chamber. The sick man himself had wasted greatly. All the life in him seemed to have taken refuge in his still brilliant eyes. The livid whiteness of his face was horrible to see, enhanced by the long dank locks of hair that straggled along his cheeks, for he would never allow them to cut it. He looked like a religious fanatic in the desert. Mental suffering extinguished all human instincts in this man, scarcely fifty years of age, whom all Paris had known as so brilliant and successful.

One morning at the beginning of December 1824, he looked up at Ernest, who sat at the foot of his bed gazing at his father with wistful eyes.

‘Are you in pain?’ the little Vicomte asked.

‘No,’ said the Count, with a ghastly smile, ‘it all lies _here and about my heart_!’

He pointed to his forehead, then laid his wasted fingers on his hollow chest. Ernest began to cry at the sight.

‘How is it that M. Derville does not come to me?’ the Count asked his servant (he thought Maurice was really attached to him, but the man was entirely in the Countess’ interest). ‘What! Maurice!’ The dying man suddenly sat upright in his bed, seeming to recover all his presence of mind. ‘I have sent for my attorney seven or eight times during the last fortnight, and he does not come!’ he cried. ‘Do you imagine that I am to be trifled with? Go for him at once, this very instant, and bring him back with you. If you do not carry out my orders, I shall get up and go myself.’

‘Madame,’ the man said as he entered the salon, ‘you heard M. le Comte; what ought I to do?’

‘Pretend to go to the attorney, and when you come back, tell your master that his man of business is forty leagues away from Paris on an important lawsuit. Say that he is expected back at the end of the week. Sick people never know how ill they are,’ thought the Countess; ‘he will wait until the man comes home.’

The doctor had said the previous evening that the Count could scarcely live through the day. When the servant returned two hours later with that hopeless answer, the dying man seemed greatly agitated.

‘Oh God!’ he cried again and again, ‘I put my trust in none but Thee.’

For a long while, he lay gazing at his son, and finally spoke in a feeble voice.

‘Ernest, my boy, you are very young; but you have a good heart; you can understand, no doubt, that a promise given to a dying man is sacred; a promise to a father... Do you feel that you can be trusted with a secret, and keep it so well and so closely that even your mother shall not know you have a secret to keep? There is no one else in this house whom I can trust today. You will not betray my trust, will you?’

‘No, father.’

‘Very well, then, Ernest, in a minute or two I will give you a sealed packet that belongs to M. Derville; you must take such care of it that no one can know you have it; then you must slip out of the house and put the letter into the post-box at the corner.’

‘Yes, father.’

‘Can I depend upon you?’

‘Yes, father.’

‘Come and kiss me. You have made death less bitter to me, dear boy. In six or seven years’ time, you will understand the importance of this secret, and you will be well rewarded then for your quickness and obedience; you will know how much I love you. Leave me alone for a minute, and let no one—no matter whom—come in meanwhile.’

Ernest went out and saw his mother standing in the next room.

‘Ernest,’ she said, ‘come here.’

She sat down, drew her son to her knees, and clasped him in her arms, holding him tightly to her heart.

‘Ernest, your father said something to you just now.’

‘Yes, mamma.’

‘What did he say?’

‘I cannot repeat it, mamma.’

‘Oh, my dear child!’ cried the Countess, kissing him in rapture. ‘You have kept your secret; how glad that makes me! Never tell a lie; never fail to keep your word—those are two principles which should never be forgotten.’

‘Oh! mamma, how beautiful you are! _You_ have never told a lie, I am quite sure.’

‘Once or twice, Ernest dear, I have lied. Yes, and I have not kept my word under circumstances that speak louder than all precepts. Listen, my Ernest, you are big enough and intelligent enough to see that your father drives me away and will not allow me to nurse him, and this is not natural, for you know how much I love him.’

‘Yes, mamma.’

The Countess began to cry. ‘Poor child!’ she said. ‘This misfortune is the result of treacherous insinuations. Wicked people have tried to separate me from your father to satisfy their greed. They mean to take all our money from us and keep it for themselves. If your father were well, the division between us would soon be over; he would listen to me; he is loving and kind; he would see his mistake. But now his mind is affected, and his prejudices against me have become a fixed idea, a sort of mania with him. It is one result of his illness. Your father’s fondness for you is another proof that his mind is deranged. Until he fell ill, you never noticed that he loved you more than Pauline and Georges. It is all caprice with him now. In his affection for you, he might take it into his head to tell you to do things for him. If you do not want to ruin us all, my darling, and to see your mother begging her bread like a pauper, you must tell her everything----’

‘Ah!’ cried the Count. He had opened the door and stood there, a sudden, half-naked apparition, almost as thin and fleshless as a skeleton.

His smothered cry produced a terrible effect on the Countess; she sat motionless, as if a sudden stupor had seized her. Her husband was as white and wasted as if he had risen from his grave.

‘You have filled my life to the full with trouble, and now you are trying to vex my deathbed, to warp my boy’s mind, and make a depraved man of him!’ he cried hoarsely.

The Countess flung herself at his feet. His face, working with the last emotions of life, was almost hideous to see.

‘Mercy! mercy!’ she cried aloud, shedding a torrent of tears.

‘Have you shown me any pity?’ he asked. ‘I allowed you to squander your own money, and now do you mean to squander my fortune too and ruin my son?’

‘Ah! well, yes, have no pity for me, be merciless to me!’ she cried. ‘But the children? Condemn your widow to live in a convent; I will obey you; I will do anything, anything that you bid me, to expiate the wrong I have done you, if that so the children may be happy! The children! Oh, the children!’

‘I have only one child,’ said the Count, stretching out a wasted arm in despair toward his son.

‘Pardon a penitent woman, a penitent woman!...’ wailed the Countess, her arms around her husband’s damp feet. She could not speak for sobbing; vague, incoherent sounds broke from her parched throat.

‘You dare to talk of penitence after all you said to Ernest!’ the dying man exclaimed, shaking off the Countess, who lay groveling over his feet. ‘You turn me to ice!’ he added, and there was something appalling in the indifference with which he uttered the words. ‘You have been a bad daughter; you have been a bad wife; you will be a bad mother.’

The wretched woman fainted away. The dying man returned to his bed and lay down again, sinking into unconsciousness. The priests came and administered the sacraments.

At midnight, he died; the scene that morning had exhausted his remaining strength, and on the stroke of midnight, I arrived with Daddy Gobseck. The house was in confusion, and under cover of it, we walked into the little salon adjoining the death-chamber. The three children were there in tears, with two priests who had come to watch with the dead. Ernest approached me and said that his mother desired to be alone in the Count’s room.

‘Do not go in,’ he said, and I admired the child for his tone and gesture. ‘She is praying there.’

Gobseck began to laugh that soundless laugh of his, but I felt too touched by the emotion in Ernest’s little face to join in the miser’s sardonic amusement. When Ernest saw we were moving toward the door, he planted himself in front of it, crying out, ‘Mamma, here are some gentlemen in black who want to see you!’

Gobseck lifted Ernest out of the way as if the child were a feather and opened the door.

What a scene met our eyes! The room was in frightful disorder; clothes, papers, and rags lay tossed about in a horrible confusion in the presence of Death; and there, in the midst, stood the Countess in disheveled despair, unable to utter a word, her eyes glittering. The Count had scarcely breathed his last before his wife came in and forced open the drawers and the desk; the carpet was strewn with litter, some furniture and boxes were broken, and signs of violence were everywhere. If her search had initially proved fruitless, there was something in her excitement and attitude that led me to believe she had found the mysterious documents at last. I glanced at the bed, and my professional instinct told me all that had happened. The mattress had been flung contemptuously down by the bedside, and across it, face downwards, lay the body of the Count, like one of the paper envelopes strewn on the carpet—he too was nothing now but an envelope. There was something grotesquely horrible in the attitude of his stiffening limbs.

The dying man must have hidden the counter-deed under his pillow to keep it safe as long as life lasted; his wife must have guessed his thought; indeed, it might be read plainly in his last dying gesture, in the convulsive clutch of his claw-like hands. The pillow had been flung to the floor at the foot of the bed; I could see the print of her heel upon it. At her feet lay a paper with the Count’s arms on the seals; I snatched it up and saw it was addressed to me. I looked steadily at the Countess with the pitiless clear-sightedness of an examining magistrate confronting a guilty creature. The contents were blazing in the grate; she had flung them on the fire at the sound of our approach, imagining, from a first hasty glance at the provisions I had suggested for her children, that she was destroying a will that disinherited them. A tormented conscience and involuntary horror of the deed she had done had taken away all power of reflection. She had been caught in the act, and possibly the scaffold was rising before her eyes, and she already felt the felon’s branding iron.

There she stood gasping for breath, waiting for us to speak, staring at us with haggard eyes.

I went across to the grate and pulled out an unburned fragment. ‘Ah, madame!’ I exclaimed, ‘you have ruined your children! Those papers were their titles to their property.’

Her mouth twitched; she looked as if threatened by a paralytic seizure.

‘Eh! eh!’ cried Gobseck; the harsh, shrill tone grated upon our ears like the sound of a brass candlestick scratching a marble surface.

There was a pause, then the old man turned to me and said quietly, ‘Do you intend Mme. la Comtesse to suppose that I am not the rightful owner of the property sold to me by her late husband? This house belongs to me now.’

A sudden blow on the head from a bludgeon would have given me less pain and astonishment. The Countess saw the look of hesitation in my face.

‘Monsieur,’ she cried, ‘Monsieur!’ She could find no other words.

‘You are a trustee, are you not?’ I asked.

‘That is possible.’

‘Then do you mean to take advantage of this crime of hers?’

‘Precisely.’

I left that scene, leaving the Countess sitting by her husband’s bedside, shedding hot tears. Gobseck followed me. Outside in the street, I separated from him, but he came after me, flung me one of those searching glances with which he probed men’s minds, and said in husky flute-tones, pitched in a shriller key, ‘Do you take it upon yourself to judge me?’

From that time forward, we saw little of each other. Gobseck let the Count’s mansion on lease; he spent summers on the country estates. He was a lord of the manor in earnest, putting up farm buildings, repairing mills and roadways, and planting timber. I came across him one day while walking in the Jardin des Tuileries.

‘The Countess is behaving like a heroine,’ I said; ‘she gives herself entirely to the children’s education; she is giving them a perfect upbringing. The oldest boy is a charming young fellow----’

‘That is possible.’

‘But ought you not to help Ernest?’ I suggested.

‘Help him!’ Gobseck cried. ‘Not I. Adversity is the greatest of all teachers; it teaches us to know the value of money and the worth of men and women. Let him set sail on the seas of Paris; when he is a qualified pilot, we will give him a ship to steer.’

I left him without seeking to explain the meaning of his words.

M. de Restaud’s mother has prejudiced him against me, and he is very far from taking me as his legal adviser; still, I went to see Gobseck last week to tell him about Ernest’s love for Mlle. Camille and pressed him to carry out his contract, since that young Restaud is just of age.

I found the old bill-discounter had been bedridden for a long time with the complaint of which he was to die. He put me off, saying he would give the matter his attention when he could get up again and see after his business; his idea being no doubt that he would not give up any of his possessions as long as breath remained in him; no other reason could be found for his shuffling answer. He seemed much worse than he suspected. I stayed long enough to discern the progress of a passion that age had converted into a sort of craze. He wanted to be alone in the house and had taken the rooms one by one as they fell vacant. In his own room, he had changed nothing; the furniture I knew so well sixteen years ago looked the same as ever; it might have been kept under a glass case. Gobseck’s faithful old portress, with her husband, a pensioner who sat in the entry while she was upstairs, was still his housekeeper and charwoman, and now in addition, his sick-nurse. Despite his feebleness, Gobseck saw his clients himself as before, receiving sums of money; his affairs had simplified so that he only needed to send his pensioner out now and again on an errand and could carry on business in his bed.

After the treaty by which France recognized the Haytian Republic, Gobseck was one of the members of the commission appointed to liquidate claims and assess repayments due by Hayti; his special knowledge of old fortunes in San Domingo and the planters and their heirs and assigns to whom the indemnities were due had led to his nomination. Gobseck’s peculiar genius devised an agency for discounting the planters’ claims on the government. The business was carried on under the names of Werbrust and Gigonnet, with whom he shared the spoil without disbursements, for his knowledge was accepted instead of capital. The agency was a sort of distillery, extracting money from dubious claims and those who knew no better or had no confidence in the government. As a liquidator, Gobseck could make terms with the large landed proprietors; these, either to gain a higher percentage of their claims or to ensure prompt settlements, would send him presents in proportion to their means. In this way, presents became a kind of percentage upon sums too large to pass through his control, while the agency bought up cheaply the small and dubious claims, or the claims of those who preferred a little ready money to a deferred and somewhat hazy repayment by the Republic. Gobseck was the insatiable boa constrictor of the great business. Every morning he received his tribute, eyeing it like a Nabob’s prime minister considering whether to sign a pardon. Gobseck would take anything, from the present of game sent by some poor devil or the pound’s weight of wax candles from devout folk, to the rich man’s plate and the speculator’s gold snuff-box. Nobody knew what became of the presents sent to the old money-lender. Everything went in, but nothing came out.

‘On the word of an honest woman,’ said the portress, an old acquaintance of mine, ‘I believe he swallows it all and is none the fatter for it; he is as thin and dried up as the cuckoo in the clock.’

At length, last Monday, Gobseck sent his pensioner for me. The man came up to my private office.

‘Be quick and come, M. Derville,’ he said. ‘The governor is just going to hand in his checks; he has grown as yellow as a lemon; he is fidgeting to speak with you; death has fair hold of him; the rattle is working in his throat.’

When I entered Gobseck’s room, I found the dying man kneeling before the grate. If there was no fire on the hearth, there was at least a monstrous heap of ashes. He had dragged himself out of bed, but his strength had failed him, and he could neither go back nor find the voice to complain.

‘You felt cold, old friend,’ I said, helping him back to bed. ‘How can you do without a fire?’

‘I am not cold at all,’ he replied. ‘No fire here! No fire! I am going, I know not where, lad,’ he went on, glancing at me with blank, lightless eyes. ‘But I am going away from this. I have _carpology_,’ said he (the use of the technical term showing how clear and accurate his mental processes were even now). ‘I thought the room was full of live gold, and I got up to catch some of it. To whom will all mine go, I wonder? Not to the crown; I have left a will, look for it, Grotius. _La belle Hollandaise_ had a daughter; I once saw the girl somewhere in the Rue Vivienne, one evening. They call her “_La Torpille_,” I believe; she is as pretty as pretty can be; look her up, Grotius. You are my executor; take what you like; help yourself. There are Strasburg pies, bags of coffee, and sugar, and gold spoons. Give the Odiot service to your wife. But who is to have the diamonds? Are you going to take them, lad? There is snuff too—sell it at Hamburg; tobaccos are worth half as much again at Hamburg. All sorts of things I have in fact, and now I must go and leave them all. Come, Papa Gobseck, no weakness, be yourself!’

He raised himself in bed, the lines of his face standing out sharply against the pillow as if the profile had been cast in bronze; he stretched out a lean arm and bony hand along the coverlet and clutched it, as if to keep his hold on life, then gazed hard at the grate, cold as his own metallic eyes, and died in full consciousness of death. To us—the portress, the old pensioner, and myself—he looked like one of the old Romans standing behind the Consuls in Lethiere’s picture of the _Death of the Sons of Brutus_.

‘He was a good-plucked one, the old Lascar!’ said the pensioner in his soldierly fashion.

But as for me, the dying man’s fantastical enumeration of his riches still sounded in my ears, and my eyes, following the direction of his, rested on that heap of ashes. It struck me that it was very large. I took the tongs, and as soon as I stirred the cinders, I felt the metal underneath—a mass of gold and silver coins, receipts taken during his illness, doubtless, after he grew too feeble to lock the money up, and could trust no one to take it to the bank for him.

‘Run for the justice of the peace,’ I said, turning to the old pensioner, ‘so that everything can be sealed here at once.’

Gobseck’s last words and the old portress’ remarks had struck me. I took the keys of the rooms on the first and second floors to make a visitation. The first door I opened revealed the meaning of the phrases I took for mad ravings; I saw the lengths to which covetousness goes when it survives only as an illogical instinct, the last stage of greed found in many misers in country towns.

In the room next to the one in which Gobseck had died, a quantity of eatables of all kinds were stored—putrid pies, moldy fish, even shellfish; the stench almost choked me. Maggots and insects swarmed. These comparatively recent presents were put down, haphazard, among chests of tea, bags of coffee, and packing-cases of every shape. A silver soup tureen on the chimney-piece was full of advices of the arrival of goods consigned to his order at Havre—bales of cotton, hogsheads of sugar, barrels of rum, coffees, indigo, tobaccos; a perfect bazaar of colonial produce. The room itself was crammed with furniture, silver-plate, lamps, vases, pictures; there were books, curiosities, and fine engravings lying rolled up, unframed. Perhaps these were not all presents, and some part of this vast quantity of stuff had been deposited with him as pledges, left on his hands in default of payment. I noticed jewel-cases with ciphers and armorial bearings stamped upon them, and sets of fine table-linen and weapons of price; but none of the things were docketed. I opened a book that seemed misplaced and found a thousand-franc note in it. I promised myself I would go through everything thoroughly; I would try the ceilings, floors, walls, and cornices to discover all the gold hoarded with such passionate greed by a Dutch miser worthy of a Rembrandt’s brush. In all my professional career, I have never seen such impressive signs of the eccentricity of avarice.

I returned to his room and found an explanation for this chaos and accumulation of riches in a pile of letters lying under the paper-weights on his desk—Gobseck’s correspondence with the various dealers to whom he usually sold his presents. These persons had perhaps fallen victim to Gobseck’s cleverness, or he may have wanted fancy prices for his goods; at any rate, every bargain hung in suspense. He had not disposed of the eatables to Chevet because Chevet would only take them at a loss of thirty percent. Gobseck haggled for a few francs between the prices, and while they wrangled, the goods became unsalable. Again, Gobseck had refused free delivery of his silver-plate and declined to guarantee the weights of his coffees. There had been a dispute over each article, the first indication in Gobseck of the childishness and incomprehensible obstinacy of age, a condition of mind reached at last by all men in whom a strong passion survives the intellect.

I said to myself, as he had said, ‘To whom will all these riches go?’ ... And then I think of the grotesque information he gave me as to the present address of his heiress; I foresee that it will be my duty to search all the houses of ill-fame in Paris to pour out an immense fortune on some worthless jade. But, in the first place, know this—that in a few days, Ernest de Restaud will come into a fortune to which his title is unquestionable, a fortune that will put him in a position to marry Mlle. Camille, even after adequate provision has been made for his mother, the Comtesse de Restaud, and his sister and brother.”
