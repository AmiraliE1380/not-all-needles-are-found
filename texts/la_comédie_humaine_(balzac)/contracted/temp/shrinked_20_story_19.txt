Bianchon, a physician who contributed notable theoretical physiology and early won a name at the Paris medical school, practised surgery before taking up medicine. His earliest studies were guided by one of France’s greatest surgeons, the illustrious Desplein, who shone across science like a meteor. By common consent he carried an incommunicable method. Like many geniuses, he left no heirs; his talent made no permanent trace. The glory of surgeons resembles that of actors: both are heroes of a moment, living only while they live.

Desplein shows how such transient genius fades outside its field. His name, once famous, will survive chiefly within his specialty. To reach broader historical stature requires circumstances that lift a professor from scientific history into the general human narrative. Did Desplein possess the universal command that makes a man the living word, the great figure of an age? He had a godlike eye; by intuition he discerned the sufferer and the malady, determining the precise time to operate, accounting for atmospheric conditions and temperament. Whether he deduced this from studying assimilation of atmospheric elements or by analogy like Cuvier, he seemed to grasp the human frame in past and future while emphasizing the present.

But he did not epitomize all science as Hippocrates, Galen, or Aristotle did, nor did he guide a whole school to new worlds. Though a persistent observer of human chemistry, acquainted with elements in fusion and causes of life, everything in him was personal. Isolated by egoism in life, that trait now mars his glory; on his tomb there is no proclaiming statue to imprint his discoveries on posterity.

Perhaps Desplein’s genius accounted for his beliefs, and thus his mortality. He saw the atmosphere as a generative envelope and the earth as an egg; unable to decide which came first, he rejected both the antecedent animal and a surviving spirit. He had no doubts; he was positive. His bold atheism resembled that of many scientific men—good men yet invincible atheists, the kind religious people deem impossible. Accustomed from youth to dissect humans before, during, and after life, he hunted through organs without finding the individual soul essential to religious theory. Finding cerebral and nervous centres and a centre for aerating the blood—the first two so complementary that he later thought hearing and sight could be supplied by the solar plexus—he inferred two "souls" in man and embraced atheism, though that does not disprove God. He died impenitent, as many noble geniuses do.

Desplein’s greatness was marred by many smaller contradictions, which enemies labeled meannesses. Envious fools, ignorant of the motives of superior spirits, seize superficial inconsistencies to condemn. If actions succeed, showing correlation between preliminaries and results, some charges still cling. In our day, Napoleon was condemned for rashness; only later events explained earlier moves.

Because Desplein’s glory and science were invulnerable, critics attacked his odd moods and temper; in truth he was eccentric. Sometimes handsomely dressed, sometimes indifferent; sometimes in a carriage, sometimes on foot; by turns rough and kind—harsh and covetous outwardly yet capable of offering his whole fortune to exiled patrons—he provoked contradictory judgments. Though he might drop a prayer-book at Court to win a black ribbon, in his heart he mocked everything; after studying humanity from every angle, he held a deep contempt for men, seeing their genuine expression in both solemn and mean acts.

The qualities of a great man are often federative. Even if one has more talent than wit, his wit surpasses that of someone merely called "witty." Genius presupposes moral insight; he who can see a flower can see the sun. A man who, on hearing a diplomat he saved ask, "How is the Emperor?" replies, "The courtier is alive; the man will follow!" shows not only medical skill but prodigious wit. Hence a student of human nature will accept Desplein’s exorbitant pretensions and believe—as Desplein believed—that he might have been as great a minister as a surgeon.

Among the riddles of Desplein’s life we choose one whose answer appears at the end and will vindicate him against foolish charges.

Of all Desplein’s students, Horace Bianchon was among those the surgeon most warmly attached to. Before becoming a house surgeon at the Hôtel-Dieu, Bianchon had been a medical student lodging in the squalid Maison Vauquer in the Quartier Latin. He knew the gnawing of poverty, a crucible from which great talents emerge as pure as diamonds and able to endure shocks. In the fire of unbridled passions they acquire impeccable honesty and the habit of constant work to meet life’s battles.

Horace was upright, incapable of tergiversation on honor, direct, ready to pledge his cloak for a friend and give his time or nights. He expected nothing in return yet trusted that in time he would receive more than he gave. His friends respected him for unostentatious virtue and feared his censure. He made no pedantic display; neither puritan nor preacher, he could swear with grace while giving advice, and was always ready for a jollification. A jolly companion, frank and open as an honest man with nothing to hide, he walked with head erect and a content mind. In a word, Horace was the Pylades of more than one Orestes—creditors being the modern Furies.

He bore poverty cheerfully, an attribute of courage, and because he had nothing he incurred few debts. Sober as a camel, active as a stag, he was steadfast in ideas and conduct.

His fortunate turn began when Desplein noticed both his qualities and faults, which made Bianchon dear to friends. When a leading clinician takes a young man to his bosom, the young man gets his foot in the stirrup. Desplein did not fail to take Bianchon as assistant to wealthy houses where complimentary fees reached the student’s pocket and where Parisian life’s mysteries unfolded. He kept him at consultations, gave him occupation, and sometimes sent him with rich patients to watering-places; he was effectively building a practice for him. Over time the tyrant of surgery had a devoted ally. The two men—one at the summit of honor and fortune, the other a humble Omega without fortune or fame—became intimate friends.

Desplein confided in his house surgeon. Bianchon knew who had sat near the master or on the famous couch in Desplein’s surgery, where he sometimes slept. He knew the temperament, a mix of lion and bull, that swelled beyond measure and eventually caused the man’s death by heart degeneration. He studied the eccentricities of that busy life, the schemes of sordid avarice, and the politician who lurked behind the man of science; he could foresee mortifications awaiting the solitary sentiment hidden within a steeled heart.

Once Bianchon spoke to Desplein of a poor water-carrier of Saint-Jacques who had a horrible disease from fatigue and want; the man had eaten only potatoes during the winter of 1821. Desplein left his rounds and, at the risk of killing his horse, rushed off followed by Bianchon to remove the man to a sick house founded by Dubois in the Faubourg Saint-Denis. After curing him, Desplein gave him money to buy a horse and a water-barrel. The Auvergnat later took a sick friend to Desplein, saying, "I could not have borne to let him go to any one else!" Desplein grasped the man’s hand and said, "Bring them all to me."

Bianchon had noticed his chief’s predilection for Auvergnats and water-carriers, but because Desplein prided himself on cures at the Hôtel-Dieu, the pupil found nothing strange.

One morning as he crossed the Place Saint-Sulpice, Bianchon saw his master enter the church at about nine, slipping in by the door in Rue du Petit-Lion as if into a suspect house. Curious and a devoted Cabaniste himself, he stole into the church and was astonished to see the great Desplein—the atheist who had no mercy on angels—kneeling humbly in the Lady Chapel, staying through mass, giving alms toward the service and the poor, looking as serious as a man supervising an operation.

"He has certainly not come here to clear up the question of the Virgin's delivery," Bianchon thought. "If he'd been holding a canopy rope on Corpus Christi it would be laughable; but alone at this hour—it's a marvel!"

Not wanting to seem a spy, he left. Desplein asked him that day to dine at a restaurant. At dessert Bianchon steered conversation to the mass, calling it a farce.

"A farce," Desplein said, "which has cost Christendom more blood than all Napoleon’s battles and all Broussais’ leeches. The mass is a papal invention, not older than the sixth century, based on the Hoc est corpus. What floods of blood were shed to establish the Fête-Dieu, the Festival of Corpus Christi—Rome’s triumph in the question of the Real Presence, a schism which rent the Church for three centuries! The wars of the Count of Toulouse against the Albigenses were the tail end of that dispute. The Vaudois and the Albigenses refused this innovation."

Desplein thus delighted in atheistical vein, pouring out Voltairean satire or its poor imitation.

"Hallo! where is my worshiper of this morning?" Bianchon thought.

He said nothing; he doubted whether he had really seen Desplein at Saint-Sulpice. Desplein would not have lied; they had discussed systems de natura rerum before, probing them with the knife and scalpel of incredulity.

Three months passed. Bianchon did not pursue the matter though it remained in his memory. One day a physician of the Hôtel-Dieu, in Bianchon’s presence, asked Desplein bluntly, "What were you doing at Saint-Sulpice, my dear master?"

"I went to see a priest who has a diseased knee-bone, and to whom the Duchesse d'Angoulême did me the honor to recommend me," Desplein answered.

The questioner took this for an answer; not so Bianchon. "Oh, he goes to see damaged knees in church!—He went to mass," he thought.

Bianchon resolved to watch Desplein. He remembered the day and hour he had seen him entering Saint-Sulpice and resolved to be there the following year, same day and hour, to see if the phenomenon recurred. In such a man there should be no direct antagonism of thought and action.

Next year, at that hour, Bianchon—by then no longer Desplein’s house surgeon—saw the surgeon’s cab at the corner of Rue de Tournon and Rue du Petit-Lion; Desplein crept along the wall of Saint-Sulpice and once more attended mass at the Virgin’s altar. The regularity complicated the mystery. After Desplein left, Bianchon went to the sacristan and asked whether the gentleman was a regular worshiper.

"For twenty years that I have been here," the man replied, "M. Desplein has come four times a year to attend this mass. He founded it."

"A mass founded by him!" Bianchon said as he left. "This is as great a mystery as the Immaculate Conception—an article enough to make a physician an unbeliever."

Some time elapsed before Doctor Bianchon, though an intimate friend, found an opportunity to speak to Desplein of this incident of his life. Though they met in consultation or society, it was difficult to find a confidential hour when two men could tell each other their secrets. At last, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, when the mob invaded the Archbishop's residence and Republican agitators spurred them on to destroy the gilt crosses flashing like streaks of lightning in the immensity of the city, Bianchon once more detected Desplein going into Saint-Sulpice. The doctor followed him, and knelt down by him without any notice or demonstration of surprise. They both attended the mass he had founded.

"Will you tell me, my dear fellow," said Bianchon as they left the church, "the reason for your fit of monkishness? I have caught you three times going to mass—You! You must account to me for this mystery; explain such a flagrant disagreement between your opinions and your conduct. You do not believe in God, and yet you attend mass? My dear master, you are bound to give me an answer."

"I am like a great many devout people—men who on the surface are deeply religious, but quite as much atheists as you or I can be."

He then poured out a torrent of epigrams on certain political personages, of whom the best known gives us, in this century, a new edition of Moliere's Tartufe.

"All that has nothing to do with my question," retorted Bianchon. "I want to know why you have just been doing that, and why you founded this mass."

"Faith! my dear boy," said Desplein, "I am on the verge of the tomb; I may safely tell you about the beginning of my life."

At this moment Bianchon and the great man were in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, one of the worst streets in Paris. Desplein pointed to the sixth floor of one of the houses looking like obelisks, its narrow door opening into a passage with a winding staircase at the end, with windows appropriately termed 'borrowed lights'—jours de souffrance. It was a greenish structure; the ground floor occupied by a furniture-dealer, while each floor seemed to shelter a different and independent form of misery. Throwing up his arm with a vehement gesture, Desplein exclaimed:

"I lived up there for two years."

"I know; Arthez lived there; I went up there almost every day during my first youth; we used to call it then the pickle-jar of great men! What then?"

"The mass I have just attended is connected with events which took place when I lived in the garret where you say Arthez lived—the one with the window where the clothes-line hangs with linen over a pot of flowers. My early life was so hard, my dear Bianchon, that I may dispute the palm of Paris suffering with any man living. I have endured everything: hunger and thirst, want of clothes, of shoes, of linen—every cruelty that penury can inflict. I have blown on my frozen fingers in that pickle-jar of great men, which I should like to see again with you. I worked through a whole winter, seeing my head steam and perceiving the atmosphere of my own moisture as we see that of horses on a frosty day. I do not know where a man finds the fulcrum that enables him to hold out against such a life.

"I was alone, with no one to help me, no money to buy books or to pay the expenses of my medical training; I had not a friend; my irascible, touchy temper was against me. No one understood that this irritability was the distress and toil of a man at the bottom of the social scale struggling to reach the surface. Still, I had, if I may say so to you, a ground-bed of good feeling and keen sensitiveness which must always be the birthright of any man strong enough to climb to any height after long tramping in the bogs of poverty. I could obtain nothing from my family beyond an inadequate allowance. At that time I breakfasted on a roll the baker sold me cheap because it was left over, which I crumbled into milk; thus my morning meal cost me but two sous. I dined only every other day in a boarding-house where the meal cost sixteen sous. You know what care I took of my clothes and shoes. I hardly know whether in later life we feel grief so deep on detecting the mocking smile of a gaping seam in a shoe, or hearing the armhole of a coat split. I drank nothing but water; a café seemed a promised land where none but the Lucullus of the pays Latin had a right of entry. 'Shall I ever take a cup of coffee there with milk in it?' said I to myself, 'or play a game of dominoes?'

"I threw into my work the fury I felt at my misery. I tried to master positive knowledge so as to acquire the greatest personal value and merit the position I should hold as soon as I could escape from nothingness. I consumed more oil than bread; the light I burned during those endless nights cost me more than food. It was a long duel, obstinate, with no consolation. I found no sympathy anywhere. To have friends, must we not form connections with young men, have a few sous to go tippling with them, and meet where students congregate? And I had nothing! And no one in Paris can understand that nothing means nothing. When I even thought of revealing my beggary, I had that nervous contraction of the throat which makes a sick man believe that a ball rises from the oesophagus into the larynx.

"In later life I have met people born to wealth who, never having wanted for anything, had never even heard this problem in the rule of three: A young man is to crime as a five-franc piece is to X.—These gilded idiots say to me, 'Why did you get into debt? Why involve yourself in such onerous obligations?' They remind me of the princess who, on hearing that the people lacked bread, said, 'Why do not they buy cakes?' I should like to see one of these rich men, who complain that I charge too much for an operation—yes, I should like to see him alone in Paris without a sou, without a friend, without credit, and forced to work with his five fingers to live at all! What would he do? Where would he go to satisfy his hunger?

"Bianchon, if you have sometimes seen me hard and bitter, it was because I added my early sufferings to the insensibility and selfishness I have seen thousands of times in the highest circles; or perhaps I was thinking of the obstacles which hatred, envy, jealousy, and calumny raised up between me and success. In Paris, when certain people see you ready to set your foot in the stirrup, some pull your coat-tails, others loosen the buckle of the strap that you may fall and crack your skull; one wrenches off your horse's shoes, another steals your whip, and the least treacherous of them all is the man whom you see coming to fire his pistol at you point-blank.

"You yourself, my dear boy, will become acquainted with the odious and incessant warfare waged by mediocrity against the superior man. If you should drop twenty-five louis one day, you will be accused of gambling on the next, and your best friends will report that you have lost twenty-five thousand. If you have a headache, you will be considered mad. If you are a little hasty, no one can live with you. If, to make a stand against this armament of pigmies, you collect your best powers, your best friends will cry out that you want everything, that you aim at domineering and tyranny. In short, your good points become your faults, your faults vices, and your virtues crime.

"If you save a man, you will be said to have killed him; if he reappears, it will be positive that you secured the present at the cost of the future. Stumble, and you fall! Invent anything and claim your rights, you will be called crotchety, cunning, ill-disposed to rising younger men.

"So, you see, if I do not believe in God, I believe still less in man. But do you not know in me another Desplein, altogether different from the Desplein whom everyone abuses?—However, we will not stir that mud-heap.

"Well, I was living in that house, working hard to pass my first examination, and I had no money at all. I had come to one of those moments of extremity when a man says, 'I will enlist.' I had one hope. I expected from my home a box full of linen, a present from one of those old aunts who, knowing nothing of Paris, think of your shirts while imagining that their nephew with thirty francs a month is eating ortolans. The box arrived while I was at the schools; it had cost forty francs for carriage. The porter, a German shoemaker living in a loft, had paid the money and kept the box. I walked up and down the Rue des Fosses-Saint-Germain-des-Pres and the Rue de l'Ecole de Medecine without hitting on any scheme to release my trunk without payment of the forty francs, which of course I could pay as soon as I should have sold the linen. My stupidity proved to me that surgery was my only vocation. Refined souls, whose powers move in a lofty atmosphere, have none of that spirit of intrigue that is fertile in resource and device; their good genius is chance; they do not invent, things come to them.

"At night I went home just as my fellow lodger also came in—a water-carrier named Bourgeat, a native of Saint-Flour. We knew each other as two lodgers do who have rooms off the same landing, and who hear each other sleeping, coughing, dressing, and so at last become used to one another. My neighbor informed me that the landlord, to whom I owed three quarters' rent, had turned me out; I must clear out next morning. He himself was also turned out on account of his occupation. I spent the most miserable night of my life. Where was I to get a messenger to carry my few chattels and my books? How could I pay him and the porter? Where would I go? I repeated these unanswerable questions in tears. I fell asleep; poverty has for its friends heavenly slumbers full of beautiful dreams.

"Next morning, just as I was swallowing my little bowl of bread soaked in milk, Bourgeat came in and said in his vile Auvergne accent:

"'Mouchieur l'Etudiant, I am a poor man, a foundling from the hospital at Saint-Flour, without either father or mother, and not rich enough to marry. You are not fertile in relations either, nor well supplied with the ready? Listen, I have a hand-cart downstairs which I have hired for two sous an hour; it will hold all our goods; if you like, we will try to find lodgings together, since we are both turned out of this. It is not the earthly paradise, when all is said and done.'

"'I know that, my good Bourgeat,' said I. 'But I am in a great fix. I have a trunk downstairs with a hundred francs' worth of linen in it, out of which I could pay the landlord and the porter, and I have not a hundred sous.'

"'Pooh! I have a few dibs,' replied Bourgeat joyfully, and he pulled out a greasy old leather purse. 'Keep your linen.'

"Bourgeat paid our arrears and settled with the porter. Then he put our furniture and my box of linen in his cart, and pulled it along the street, stopping in front of every house where there was a notice board. I went up to see whether the rooms to let would suit us. At midday we were still wandering about the neighborhood without having found anything. The price was the great difficulty. Bourgeat proposed that we should eat at a wine-shop, leaving the cart at the door. Towards evening I discovered, in the Cour de Rohan, Passage du Commerce, at the very top of a house next the roof, two rooms with a staircase between them. Each of us was to pay sixty francs a year. So there we were housed, my humble friend and I. We dined together. Bourgeat, who earned about fifty sous a day, had saved a hundred crowns or so; he would soon be able to gratify his ambition by buying a barrel and a horse. On learning of my situation—for he extracted my secrets with a quiet craftiness and good nature, of which the remembrance touches my heart to this day—he gave up for a time the ambition of his whole life; for twenty-two years he had been carrying water in the street, and he now devoted his hundred crowns to my future prospects."

Desplein at these words clutched Bianchon's arm tightly. "He gave me the money for my examination fees! That man, my friend, understood that I had a mission, that the needs of my intellect were greater than his. He looked after me, he called me his boy, he lent me money to buy books, he would come in softly sometimes to watch me at work, and took a mother's care in seeing that I had wholesome and abundant food instead of the bad and insufficient nourishment I had been condemned to. Bourgeat, a man of about forty, had a homely, mediaeval type of face, a prominent forehead, a head a painter might have chosen as Lycurgus'. The poor man's heart was big with affections seeking an object; he had never been loved but by a poodle that had died some time since, of which he would talk to me, asking whether I thought the Church would allow masses for the repose of its soul. His dog, he said, had been a good Christian, who for twelve years had accompanied him to church, never barking, listening to the organ without opening his mouth, crouching beside him in a way that made it seem as though he were praying too.

"This man centered all his affections in me; he looked upon me as a forlorn and suffering creature, and he became to me the most thoughtful mother and considerate benefactor, the ideal of the virtue that rejoices in its own work. When I met him in the street, he would throw me a glance of intelligence full of unutterable dignity; he would affect to walk as though he carried no weight and seemed happy in seeing me in good health and well dressed. It was, in fact, the devoted affection of the lower classes, the love of a girl of the people transferred to a loftier level. Bourgeat did all my errands, woke me at night at any fixed hour, trimmed my lamp, cleaned our landing; as good a servant as he was a father, and as clean as an English girl. He did all the housework. Like Philopoemen, he sawed our wood, and gave to all he did the grace of simplicity while preserving his dignity, for he seemed to understand that the end ennobles every act.

"When I left this good fellow to be house surgeon at the Hotel-Dieu, I felt an indescribable dull pain, knowing that he could no longer live with me; but he comforted himself with the prospect of saving enough for me to take my degree, and he made me promise to go to see him whenever I had a day out: Bourgeat was proud of me. He loved me for my own sake and for his own. If you look up my thesis, you will see that I dedicated it to him.

"During the last year of my residence as house surgeon I earned enough to repay all I owed to this worthy Auvergnat by buying him a barrel and a horse. He was furious with rage at learning that I had deprived myself of spending my money, and yet delighted to see his wishes fulfilled; he laughed and scolded, looked at his barrel and his horse, and wiped away a tear, saying, 'It is too bad. What a splendid barrel! You really ought not. Why, that horse is as strong as an Auvergnat!'

"I never saw a more touching scene. Bourgeat insisted on buying for me the case of instruments mounted in silver which you have seen in my room and which is to me the most precious thing there. Though enchanted with my first success, never did the least sign escape him implying, 'This man owes all to me!' Yet, but for him, I should have died of want; he had eaten bread rubbed with garlic that I might have coffee to enable me to sit up at night.

"He fell ill. As you may suppose, I passed my nights by his bedside, and the first time I pulled him through; but two years after he had a relapse; in spite of the utmost care and exertions of science, he succumbed. No king was ever nursed as he was. Yes, Bianchon, to snatch that man from death I tried unheard-of things. I wanted him to live long enough to see his work accomplished, to realize his hopes, to give expression to the only gratitude that ever filled my heart, to quench a fire that burns in me to this day.

"Bourgeat, my second father, died in my arms," Desplein went on after a pause, visibly moved. "He left me everything he possessed by a will he had had made by a public scrivener, dating from the year when we had gone to live in the Cour de Rohan.

"This man's faith was perfect; he loved the Holy Virgin as he might have loved his wife. He was an ardent Catholic, but never said a word to me about my want of religion. When he was dying he entreated me to spare no expense that he might have every possible benefit of clergy. I had a mass said for him every day. Often, in the night, he would tell me of his fears as to his future fate; he feared his life had not been saintly enough. Poor man! he was at work from morning till night. For whom, then, is Paradise—if there be a Paradise? He received the last sacrament like the saint that he was, and his death was worthy of his life.

"I alone followed him to the grave. When I had laid my only benefactor to rest, I looked about to see how I could pay my debt to him; I found he had neither family nor friends, neither wife nor child. But he believed. He had a religious conviction; had I any right to dispute it? He had spoken to me timidly of masses said for the repose of the dead; he would not impress it on me as a duty, thinking it a form of repayment for his services. As soon as I had money enough I paid to Saint-Sulpice the requisite sum for four masses every year. As the only thing I can do for Bourgeat is thus to satisfy his pious wishes, on the days when that mass is said at the beginning of each season of the year, I go for his sake and say the required prayers; and I say with the good faith of a sceptic—'Great God, if there is a sphere which Thou hast appointed after death for those who have been perfect, remember good Bourgeat; and if he should have anything to suffer, let me suffer it for him, that he may enter all the sooner into what is called Paradise.'

"That, my dear fellow, is as much as a man who holds my opinions can allow himself. But God must be a good fellow; He cannot owe me any grudge. I swear to you, I would give my whole fortune if faith such as Bourgeat's could enter my brain."

Bianchon, who was with Desplein all through his last illness, dares not affirm to this day that the great surgeon died an atheist. Will not those who believe like to fancy that the humble Auvergnat came to open the gate of Heaven to his friend, as he did that of the earthly temple on whose pediment are the words—"A grateful country to its great men."

PARIS, January 1836.
