To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,—If these lines endure in literature, they will be a small token of gratitude for your efforts. You, Hozier, Cherin, the King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life, have enriched the “Human Comedy” with your heraldic designs. Your manual of French Heraldry, showing deep knowledge of the feudal spirit and learned symbolism, has greatly influenced me.

Your old friend,  
DE BALZAC.

On the skirts of Le Berry lies Sancerre, a town perched on a hill above the Loire flats, set on fertile alluvium though some tracts suffer drifting sand. Its distance from the river let the nearby port of Saint-Thibault prosper, shipping wine and goods; by this story’s opening suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault have replaced ferries—improvements dating from the upheaval of 1830 when the House of Orleans funded local works.

Sancerre’s steep, narrow streets within the Great Ramparts form its principal ways. Wine is the chief industry, producing vintages worthy of Burgundy and supplying Paris; lower villages like Fontenoy and Saint-Satur recall their vineyard beauty. Once militarily important, the town declined as trade moved to the Loire’s right bank. Its population of about 3,500 is now eclipsed by Cosne’s 6,000, a reversal of fifty years, yet Sancerre retains historic charm, broad views, pure air, and a civic, watchful community, predominantly Calvinist without Puritan severity.

Notable natives include Horace Bianchon in medicine and Etienne Lousteau in journalism. Politically, a few large landowners dominate municipal life and skew elections. A local plot to break their hold collapsed through vanity and jealousy; the proposed remedy is to unite behind one candidate for the next election—one of Sancerre’s two Paris representatives—a progressive provincial scheme championed by the borough’s Superior Woman, whose interests and past explain her ambitions.

Around 1836 Sancerre revealed a locally notable figure—the Superior Woman—made visible by Parisian influences like Etienne Lousteau, Bianchon, and George Sand’s sentimental “Sandism,” which brought previously hidden superior women into view.

The Superior Woman of Sancerre lived at La Baudraye, a town-and-country house near Saint-Satur belonging to a diminished branch of the Milaud family. The family’s fortunes shifted over generations: a Milaud sheriff converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes and was rewarded under Louis XIV, later suffered reprisals, and under Louis XV secured a military commission whose son died in battle, leaving a child later favored by Louis XVI. In 1800 an ex–farmer-general returned from exile and bought La Baudraye. His only son, Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, was frail and devotedly raised by his noble mother; on his father’s death he inherited the fief, some money, and a collection of autographs. He lived frugally, enlarged the vineyards (1802–1815), hesitated to press Parisian debts during the Restoration, tried and failed to monetize the autograph collection, then returned home.

In 1823, at forty-three, Polydore sought an heir and chose Dinah Piedefer: beautiful, educated, from a once-Calvinist family now poor, who converted to Catholicism to improve her prospects. The Archbishop of Bourges saw promise in her, but families feared her beauty and intellect. Polydore’s bluntness offended ministers when he sought ecclesiastical support; nevertheless they married, provoking local speculation.

Polydore’s Paris dealings—pressing debtors, forgiving debts in exchange for a tax-collector post, briefly holding office, securing lucrative appointments, rising to Royal Commissioner, and buying Anzy in 1827—funded the household. Dinah transformed La Baudraye—gardens, interiors, refined company—while keeping her husband’s financial maneuvers private. Her beauty, intellect, Paris correspondence, and demand for conversation set her apart: admired by local men, resented and isolated by provincial women. Madame Piedefer rejoiced, and Polydore proudly had his Superior Woman—an example of how exceptional qualities can inspire both admiration and resentment, ultimately isolating those who exceed local norms.

By the end of 1825 Dinah de la Baudraye faced gossip that she socialized only with men and became a target of criticism. In a rash reply to a false friend she declared, “I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!” The remark backfired; childless after five years of marriage, she invited further ridicule.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was a frail, eccentric man who rarely contradicted Dinah and allowed her to dominate conversation. Admired for beauty and intelligence, she attracted gentlemen from nearby towns, relishing attention and delivering prepared speeches drawn from wide reading. Her charm created a protective aura that shielded her from much censure; Monsieur often excused himself from her long expositions to walk or attend to business.

Dinah championed the romantic arts, collected antiques, and filled their home with Renaissance treasures. Her husband tacitly supported these pursuits but focused chiefly on his land. Over the years her notable collection impressed visitors and provided material for talks on art and history. She tried to found a literary circle in Sancerre to elevate local culture, but it degenerated into social gatherings—an evident disappointment. Admirers, including magistrates and a priest, were often diverted from intellectual aims; one young man who yawned during a lecture fell from her favor, revealing her strict expectations for cultural devotion.

When Dinah turned twenty in 1826 suitors still courted her. Sheltered by her mother and the visible presence of admirers, she kept slander at bay while Monsieur used the marriage to secure favors. Local women speculated, and a slow domestic drama—fed by the era’s appetite for gossip—shaped her seemingly virtuous early married life.

Though Monsieur appeared generous at first—letting her use five acres and financing house improvements—his apparent liberality proved an illusion. After a trip to Paris he imposed a strict monthly allowance, paid a yearly sum to her mother, and warned of uncertain winegrower income. Dinah’s attempts to provoke jealousy produced only indifference; he remained steady and unyielding.

Frustration grew as she felt trapped in La Baudraye and Sancerre, longing for a more ambitious life. She briefly hoped for change when he bought the estate of Anzy and applied for a patent of entail, but the short reconciliation exposed deeper contrasts: Dinah’s energy and potential clashed with her husband’s lack of ambition and rigid routine. Over six years she became increasingly provincial, losing individuality and spirit—wardrobe reduced, sewing her own clothes—and a meeting with her former schoolfriend Anna, now a sophisticated Parisian, left her despondent.

She turned to poetry; her "Paquita la Sevillane" drew provincial attention and revealed talent but provoked anxiety about exposure. After the Revolution of 1830, George Sand’s fame encouraged towns to celebrate local female talent. Monsieur de Clagny published a collection by "Jan Diaz," a supposedly young dead poet—two hundred copies were printed. It emerged that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym for Madame de la Baudraye. Many copies sold in the departments where the poet had lived; those sent to Paris were largely lost in editorial offices. Dinah quickly became celebrated and was cast by some provincials as a rival to George Sand. Local, often polarized praise elevated work that might otherwise have been overlooked.

Dinah longed for recognition to fill unmet ambitions. The late Abbé Duret had warned her about her husband’s character; she resisted until she discerned his incapacity for love and vengefulness. As her reputation grew, domestic life felt ever more constraining and public sympathy intensified her despair. After a confrontation she vowed to stop writing and withdrew into desolation at Sancerre.

She had hoped to support Monsieur de Clagny’s political ambitions, but his electoral defeat left her disillusioned. At the château d’Anzy she contemplated her future and sought contact with influential figures. She admired Lousteau, a charming journalist, and tried to attract him and Bianchon to Sancerre; their visits and autographs became tangible links to the literary world and the recognition she craved. Dinah’s story reveals the tension between ambition and domesticity and a woman’s struggle for identity and fulfillment in a restrictive provincial environment.

At five the next morning the Chateau d’Anzy stirred for a day's sport arranged by the young Monsieur de la Baudraye, a scheme by Parisians to reclaim local land. Gatien Boirouge asked Monsieur Gravier why the Public Prosecutor, Monsieur de Clagny, was absent; Gravier gave court duty, but Gatien suspected Clagny spent time with Madame de la Baudraye (Dinah). Lousteau and Bianchon teased Gatien’s provincial anxieties, treating the matter as vanity and gossip.

At a copse Gravier left the men with a keeper while Bianchon and Lousteau lingered. Gatien fretted that Gravier might already be dining with Dinah and Clagny and that, if together, they would watch for opportunites. He feared Clagny’s persuasive eloquence and Dinah’s Parisian ambitions; Lousteau and Bianchon dismissed such intrigues. Lousteau proposed a prank: at dinner the Parisians would tell lurid tales of betrayed wives and vengeful husbands to watch Dinah and Clagny for reactions. Gatien favored entrapment, recalling a Parisian editor’s wife who frightened her husband with stories of lovers boiled alive. They resolved to be diplomatic by day and provocative by night.

At dinner Lousteau excelled. He produced an album of lachrymose verses purportedly by himself—an elegy that won Dinah’s sympathy. Clagny, seeing her expression, recoiled into backgammon. Dinah, stitching for her husband’s charity box, replied to Lousteau’s mockery with gentle retorts; wry jests about adultery and morality followed. The Public Prosecutor attacked Parisian writers’ treatment of illicit love; Lousteau defended literature with classical references, provoking Clagny.

Conversation widened into paradoxes on justice and innovation: history and stern courts seemed to condemn pioneers—Socrates, Jesus—and institutions persecute what they cannot assimilate. Lousteau teased that religion and state are fragile before new ideas; Clagny called this blasphemy. Gravier half-jokingly said he’d side with a betrayed husband if he were a writer; Lousteau mused on the sublimity of trust versus jealousy. Dinah’s quiet sympathy for victims tortured into crime suggested human justice poorly judges private tragedies.

Lousteau then told a Nodier tale about a Chevalier who feigned escape from a jealous commandant—harmless and bemusing—eliciting no reaction from Clagny or Dinah. Bianchon followed with the Spanish La Grande Bretèche—of a grandee walled into a cupboard to starve by a jealous husband—stunning the guests. Gravier added a Spanish adventure of illicit deliveries, betrayals, and a murdered surgeon-major. The cumulative gruesomeness chilled the table; Clagny reminded them the Gazette des Tribunaux often reported horrors rivaling romance.

The Parisians watched Dinah and Clagny closely, betting that one guilty of passion would react. After dinner the bachelors conferred: Gravier briefed them on probing Dinah’s virtue; Lousteau warned that serenity can mask cunning. Gravier suggested a country trick—pressing hairs into wax on the doors of Madame de la Baudraye and the Public Prosecutor to detect nocturnal visits. That night the doctor, Lousteau, the tax receiver, and Gatien crept barefoot to press hairs into wax seals and agreed to examine them at five.

At dawn the seals were unbroken—same wax, same hairs. Lousteau declared they had chased a will-o’-the-wisp and admitted the matter was more complex. Puzzled, Gravier and Gatien faced an unexpected result. Lousteau privately told Bianchon that if Dinah were truly virtuous she deserved the effort; his mockery softened into admiration and he sought Dinah in the garden.

Their conversation was partly contrived: she had asked him to study provincial life; he charged that Gatien had misrepresented him as adoring her and that her silence at dinner had been an unguarded sign. He confessed engineering the evening’s stories to rouse emotion and test both her and Clagny, and said her innocence seemed proved—if she had yielded to Clagny he would lose respect for her. He professed a taste for a certain perfection and disdain for cold, taciturn men. Dinah replied that Lousteau sought pleasure but not love, and that true love often comes late; she showed sympathy for his vulnerability.

Lousteau’s candor delighted Dinah. He and Bianchon spent the day entertaining her with anecdotes and wit—an intellectual feast she savored—while Clagny, Gravier, and Gatien noted her warmer bearing. By sundown sixteen people had gathered at Anzy; Dinah, proud hostess, prepared the drawing-room. Lousteau found a tattered romance used as packing—“Olympia, or Roman Revenge”—and turned its brigands, a Duchess’s glove, and Cardinal Borborigano into comic spectacle. Bianchon joined, amusing the assembled grandees and young women. The evening showcased Parisian cleverness in the provinces, while the mystery of the sealed doors remained unresolved for the men who had come to spy on hearts and reputations.

For three days Dinah’s imagination was stirred by her Parisian visitors. Lousteau watched her, eager to captivate; Bianchon, blunt and professorial, prodded her. Lousteau bantered and sat close while Bianchon mocked the local men and interrogated the unhappy circumstances of her life, putting Dinah on the defensive.

Crossing the Loire, Bianchon told her frankly she had shut herself against love but now needed it; Dinah, startled, asked if he prescribed love. He warned that her habits would make her unattractive. Lousteau half‑jokingly defended him as offering sound counsel. At Cosne, when a crowd gathered by an old carriage, Dinah insisted they disembark and walked quickly with Bianchon along the Loire. There Bianchon suggested Lousteau loved her but was frivolous and hard to keep; he advised making a friend of him, since Lousteau could bring beauty and happiness. Under Bianchon’s scrutiny Dinah felt vulnerable and unsure how she might compete with Lousteau’s past lovers.

Their coachman announced Gatien’s return. Bianchon blessed them as they entered town. Lousteau immediately took Dinah’s hand and declared his love; she resisted, asking whether she should give herself to a man who treated her as a passing pleasure. Gatien’s arrival complicated matters. Lousteau whispered that she should feign resistance to preserve his honor and, in a reckless instant, crushed her muslin dress, leaving her disordered and humiliated. Gatien, oblivious, rode up while Lousteau tried to distract him; Dinah sent Gatien to fetch Bianchon’s papers, and he complied.

Back at La Baudraye, Madame Piedefer appeared anxious; hearing Gatien’s horse, Dinah fled to her room. Outside, Lousteau and Gatien confronted one another—Gatien accused trickery, Lousteau shrugged it off as Dinah’s mockery. Dinah changed into a velvet gown; when she rejoined them, conversation skirted Bianchon while she remained silent, weighed down by recent events.

Intent on winning her, Lousteau declared he would stay in Sancerre to be near her and swore he had renounced Parisian vices. On the road back to Anzy his charm began to dissolve Dinah’s reserve; she laughed, a sign her feelings were shifting. After a stolen intimacy in the gardens and a pledge exchanged before his return to Paris, Dinah accompanied him to Cosne, openly flouting propriety. At La Baudraye she praised him for showing what true love felt like. Her three admirers, including Monsieur de Clagny, were left to reckon with their feelings; Dinah took Clagny as a confidant, lavishing affection while he remained devoted despite her new happiness.

In Paris, however, Lousteau soon forgot the happy days at the Chateau d’Anzy. He lived by his pen in an age when writing—after the rise of the bourgeoisie—had become near servitude: constant production or the appearance of it. He wrote daily for a newspaper and several literary magazines without artistic pretensions; deprived of advancement, he had become a journalist. Still, his life was not unpleasant. He mixed with blue‑stockings, aspiring actresses, publishers, and authors, keeping theatre boxes and a social life that required little beyond paying rent.

Financially he remained constrained. Lousteau rented well‑furnished ground‑floor rooms in the Rue des Martyrs for twelve hundred francs; despite earning seven to eight hundred francs a month from writing and reviews, he spent heavily on cabs, meals, and gifts for lovers and was perpetually broke. He joked with friends about marriage and romance. He had a mistress, Madame Schontz, proud to have a writer as her lover; she lived nearby and enjoyed teasing friends. This bohemian existence would later affect Dinah: he could laugh about his entanglements, including her, whom he had almost forgotten.

When Bixiou asked about Sancerre, Lousteau recounted three timid provincial men who’d failed to pursue a local beauty. He received a long, affectionate letter from Dinah, which he read and burned, dismissing her feelings; another arrived a week later and he discarded it unread. Madame Schontz noticed the scented paper, opened hampers Dinah had sent—game and flowers—and proposed a dinner. Lousteau sent a clever but insincere letter he thought would charm Dinah; by December her letters lay unread in a drawer.

Then Madame Schontz told him of a marriage opportunity: a young woman’s previous lover had died, and her father, a notary, sought a respectable man to marry his daughter quickly, restoring her position with a substantial dowry. Hesitant at first, Lousteau agreed to meet them. He flattered the notary, recalled his own father, and presented himself as ambitious. At his first dinner he noticed Félicie, the shy grateful daughter; Madame Cardot regarded him as a man of promise. Buoyed by this reception, Lousteau felt a new purpose—stability, respectability, and the financial security the marriage promised.

Madame Cardot, having borrowed an illustrated Gil Blas to inspect prints, unexpectedly took her daughter Félicie to Lousteau’s bachelor rooms to judge a prospective son‑in‑law. Madame Camusot, uneasy about the match from her husband the Councillor’s reports, had already mistrusted Lousteau. On a January day in 1837 they hired a cab; the porter’s daughter handed over the key after Madame Cardot slipped her a gold coin.

Around noon Lousteau, returning from breakfast, was startled when Dinah, his former lover, arrived by coach and revealed she was a mother. Overwhelmed, he tried to dismiss her, but she insisted on staying. The child, Pamela, sensing the awkwardness, announced that his mother‑in‑law was inside; Lousteau discovered Madame Cardot and Félicie hidden. Dinah professed her love; Lousteau, fearing the arranged marriage he faced, began to imagine using her to escape it.

Madame Cardot openly disapproved and left with Félicie, while Dinah, trusting Lousteau’s reassurances and high‑minded air, remained. She admired the apparent elegance of his rooms, not their poverty. Pamela had borrowed dinnerware from Madame Schontz, who learned of Lousteau’s predicament and warned him Cardot would not pursue the match. Lousteau, however, focused on his intimacy with Dinah and the chance to avoid a wealthy marriage.

When his friend Bixiou challenged him—arguing provincial women often failed to adapt to Paris and that marriage should secure legitimacy—Lousteau dismissed him. Later Madame Schontz told him Cardot had arranged Félicie’s marriage to another, extinguishing his respectable prospects. Having lost a stable future, Lousteau grew more entangled with Dinah; their domestic life, sustained by his entertainments, slipped into the provincial romance he once mocked, leaving both trapped in dependency and illusion.

That Paris winter did for Dinah what autumn in Sancerre had before. Etienne (Lousteau) eager to introduce her to Parisian life, took her to the theater, fashionable dressmakers, and society, stripping away her provincial modesty. Dinah adapted quickly—immersing herself in the city’s culture and proving intelligent and well read, able to hold her own among sophisticated women. Lousteau came to regard her as both muse and partner, even as he privately entertained deceits when his fancy waned.

A newspaper item about Monsieur le Baron de Clagny’s promotion recalled Sancerre to Dinah and prompted teasing. Their passion remained intense, but Lousteau’s motives were increasingly self‑interested. When Dinah became pregnant she withdrew from public life; at a premiere she was humiliated when Lousteau abandoned her and Clagny warned the liaison threatened her reputation.

After the child’s birth Dinah devoted herself to motherhood and the household, pawning jewelry to help. Lousteau failed in letters, accumulated debts, grew indifferent, and sought solace elsewhere. A second child increased Dinah’s burdens. The turning point came when her estranged husband, Monsieur de la Baudraye, reappeared seeking power of attorney over an inheritance. Clagny—now Attorney‑General—helped Dinah secure an allowance in exchange for cooperation and protected her interests. Confronted with Lousteau’s betrayal and lack of ambition, Dinah resolved to reclaim her identity and seek a future based on mutual respect and commitment rather than mere passion.

From that day Etienne lived in luxury and Dinah could stand among Paris’s best-dressed women. Lousteau, however, played the ruined lover in public—blaming Madame de la Baudraye and feigning helplessness to exploit Dinah’s devotion—while remaining unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, disturbed by Dinah’s disgrace, warned her of betrayal; she answered, “I know it.” When he asked if she still loved Lousteau, she cried, “I would lose my soul for you!”—leaving him speechless and moved.

Slowly Dinah perceived Lousteau’s true nature. She described him to her mother as an idle poet, pleasure-seeking and impossible to hate because she felt responsible and believed she alone supported him. Her mother deplored the hell Dinah endured; Dinah replied, “I will be a mother to him!” Accepting humiliations to keep him, she compromised her dignity, hoping by emulating Madame de Pompadour to win him back. His theatrical devotion eroded her self-respect, yet she clung to the warped consolation of being his plaything.

She rehearsed dramatic separations and even contemplated suicide in despair, but every word or gesture from Lousteau pulled her back. Influenced by the novel Adolphe, she vowed not to display pain in reproaches or tears.

Meanwhile Monsieur de la Baudraye schemed to restore his fortune by manipulating Dinah’s inheritance—underreporting its value, pocketing the difference, and investing in land—planning to defer obligations to Dinah while pursuing status: a noble life in Sancerre, a title, and higher standing.

By early 1842 Dinah felt reduced to Lousteau’s convenience, sacrificing herself for his comfort. She dressed in mourning as penitence; her mother sought help. Clagny and Madame Piedefer urged her to live apart; Clagny negotiated, assuring support for a respectable return to society.

Unbeknownst to Dinah, Monsieur de la Baudraye had invested in a grand Paris residence to polish his image and pursue a peerage. He invited her to live with him in Paris and promised financial support for their children’s education.

Now a Countess, Dinah bore the weight of title and expectation, preparing for social prominence that contrasted with earlier struggles. In May 1842 she paid off her debts and invited Lousteau to dinner—an event marking a decisive change. He arrived struck by her transformation. Dinah announced their separation: she still cared but demanded a calm parting. Lousteau accused her of ruining his life; she refused to be blamed. Their last exchange mixed sorrow and defiance. Dinah left both liberated and heartbroken.

In her new life she made her home a model of comfort, spending beyond her husband’s expectations. Charming and fashionable, she missed past passion and struggled to bond with other women. Clagny introduced her to influential figures, yet learning Lousteau lived with another provoked conflicting emotions and pushed her deeper into society. In March 1843 her appointment to collect charity donations restored her public image. Summers in Sancerre found her a doting mother and proud Countess, still torn between love and ambition—an unresolved tension that defines her journey.
