To Monsieur le Comte Ferdinand de Gramont.

MY DEAR FERDINAND,—If these lines survive, accept this token of gratitude. You—Hozier, Cherin, King-at-Arms of these Studies of Life—have enriched the "Human Comedy" with your heraldic designs. Your complete manual of French Heraldry, learned in feudal spirit and symbolism, has greatly influenced me.

Your old friend,  
DE BALZAC.

On the skirts of Le Berry lies Sancerre, a hilltop town above the Loire flats, fed by fertile alluvium though troubled by drifting sand. Its port, Saint-Thibault, ships wine and goods. By this story suspension bridges at Cosne and Saint-Thibault have replaced ferries, improvements from the House of Orleans after the 1830 upheaval.

Within the Great Ramparts steep, narrow streets center a wine trade rivaling Burgundy and supplying Paris; nearby villages such as Fontenoy and Saint-Satur recall the vineyards. Once militarily significant, Sancerre declined as trade moved to the Loire’s right bank: its 3,500 inhabitants now trail Cosne’s 6,000, reversing fifty years. It still offers fine views, pure air, and a civic, mostly Calvinist populace not given to Puritanism.

Notables include Horace Bianchon and journalist Etienne Lousteau. Municipal life is dominated by a few large landowners who manipulate elections; a locally hatched plot to break them failed through jealousy. A proposed remedy—to unite behind a single champion for the next election, one of Sancerre’s two deputies in Paris—was championed by the borough’s Superior Woman, whose ambitions reflect her interests and past.

Around 1836 Sancerre had a locally famous figure called the Superior Woman, long overlooked but now attracting notice. Two Sancerrois prospered in Paris then—Etienne Lousteau and the physician Bianchon—and the influence of George Sand fostered a sentimental “Sandism” that revealed many previously hidden superior women.

The Superior Woman lived at La Baudraye, a town-and-country house near Saint-Satur belonging to a diminished branch of the Milaud family. The family’s fortunes rose and fell: an ancestor converted at the Revocation of the Edict of Nantes and was rewarded under Louis XIV; later generations suffered reverses, though one member obtained a military commission and left descendants favored by Louis XVI.

In 1800 an ex–farmer-general returning from exile bought La Baudraye. His son, Jean Athanase Polydore Milaud de la Baudraye, frail and blamed on his father’s excesses, was raised by his noble mother. On inheriting the fief and a collection of autographs, Polydore lived frugally, enlarged vineyards (1802–1815), hesitated to press Parisian debts during the Restoration, tried and failed to monetize the collection, and returned home.

In 1823, at forty-three, he sought an heir and married Dinah Piedefer, a beautiful, educated woman from a reduced ex-Calvinist family who converted to Catholicism. Though the Archbishop of Bourges saw promise and tried to arrange matches, families feared her beauty and intelligence; Polydore’s bluntness offended the Cardinal but he married her.

Polydore pressed debtors in Paris, made a bargain with the Duc de Navarreins—forgiving debts for a tax-collector post—then obtained lucrative appointments and became Royal Commissioner, buying Anzy in 1827. Dinah transformed La Baudraye—gardens, interiors, refined company—while keeping her husband’s financial dealings private. Her beauty, intellect, Paris correspondence, and demand for conversation set her apart; provincial women resented and isolated her even as men admired her. Their story shows how exceptional qualities attract both admiration and exclusion.

By the end of 1825 Dinah de la Baudraye was criticized for socializing chiefly with men. In defending herself she made a rash remark to a false friend—“I would rather have my bowl empty than with anything in it!”—which only backfired. Childless after five years of marriage, she invited further ridicule.

Monsieur de la Baudraye was a frail, gently yielding man with thin legs who sometimes wore false calves and favoured eccentric dress. He seldom contradicted Dinah and allowed her to dominate conversation. Admired for beauty and intelligence, she drew gentlemen from nearby towns, relishing attention and delivering prepared speeches from wide reading.

Her charm protected her from much censure; her husband often excused himself from long expositions to walk or mind business. She championed the romantic arts, collected antiques, and filled their home with Renaissance treasures, which impressed visitors and supplied material for her lectures. Her attempt to found a literary circle in Sancerre degenerated into idle socializing—an evident disappointment—while admirers, including magistrates and a priest, proved easily diverted. When Dinah turned twenty in 1826 suitors still courted her; sheltered by her mother and admirers, she maintained a seemingly virtuous but gossipy domestic life, which Monsieur used to secure favors.

Dinah Piedefer, proud and academically accomplished, married the seemingly frail Monsieur de la Baudraye at seventeen, wondering why and what she would gain. At first he surprised her: he let her use five acres for pleasure and financed house improvements so she could redecorate. She struggled to reconcile his stingy reputation with this generosity, but after his second trip to Paris the illusion vanished; he imposed a strict monthly allowance, paid her mother a yearly sum, and insisted on caution because their winegrower income was uncertain.

Her attempts to provoke jealousy met only steadiness; he remained unyielding. Frustrated, she felt trapped in La Baudraye and Sancerre, craving a more ambitious life. His purchase of the Anzy estate and application for an entail briefly revived hope, but their short reconciliation only highlighted the clash between Dinah’s energy and his rigid routine.

Over six years she grew provincial, losing individuality—wardrobe simplified, elegance gave way to conformity. A meeting with Anna, now a sophisticated Parisian, left her despondent. Turning to poetry, her "Paquita la Sevillane" gained local notice and revealed talent, yet provoked anxiety about exposure. Despite literary effort, Dinah remained caught between dreams of fame and a suffocating routine that bred lasting resentment.

After the Revolution of 1830 George Sand’s fame prompted many towns to celebrate local women writers. Monsieur de Clagny exploited this trend by publishing two hundred copies of poems by "Jan Diaz," a young deceased poet; some sold regionally while others were lost en route to Paris. It soon emerged that Jan Diaz was a pseudonym for Madame de la Baudraye—Dinah—who was quickly hailed by provincials as a rival to George Sand. Local, often partisan praise magnified work that might otherwise have been overlooked.

Long feeling unrecognized, Dinah sought fame to fill unmet ambitions. The late Abbé Duret had warned her about her husband’s coldness and vindictiveness; she resisted until discovering his incapacity for love. As her reputation grew, domestic life felt more constraining and public sympathy deepened her despair. After a confrontation she vowed to stop writing and withdrew to Sancerre, where her absence was keenly felt.

Jealous local rivals and Clagny’s electoral defeat added to her disillusionment, masking a deeper longing for freedom from marriage. At the Château d’Anzy she hoped to meet influential figures. When Lousteau and Bianchon arrived she hosted them, frankly denouncing provincial monotony, the stifling of creativity, and Paris-centered culture. Her intelligence, wit, charm, and vulnerability captivated Lousteau; she collected their autographs as tangible links to the literary world. Dinah’s story exposes the tension between ambition and domesticity and a woman’s struggle for identity in a restrictive provincial setting.

At five the next morning the household at the Chateau d’Anzy woke for a day’s shoot arranged by the young Monsieur de la Baudraye, an ostentatious plan to reclaim twelve hundred acres. The Parisians in his company—Gatien Boirouge, Lousteau, Bianchon, and Monsieur Gravier—were up and gossiping. Gatien asked why the Public Prosecutor, Monsieur de Clagny, had not joined them; Gravier said court duty, but Gatien suspected Clagny had taken a place on the bench and might be with Madame de la Baudraye (Dinah). The talk slipped into mockery: Lousteau and Bianchon derided provincial life and Gatien’s anxiety, calling it vanity and gossip.

At a copse Gravier left to pick a ravine path; the others lingered debating whether to wait. Gatien fretted that if Dinah and Clagny were together they might watch one another for a chance to act—he feared Clagny’s seductive eloquence and Dinah’s Parisian ambitions. Lousteau and Bianchon treated such intrigues with amused disdain, but Gatien insisted he could read a small town’s women—secrets did not survive there.

Lousteau proposed a prank: at dinner they would tell lurid tales of betrayed wives and vengeful husbands to watch Dinah and Clagny for reaction. Bianchon agreed that theatrical stories might reveal expressions; Gatien favored entrapment, recalling a Parisian editor’s wife who frightened her husband with tales of lovers boiled alive. They resolved to be diplomatic by day and provocative by night.

At dinner Lousteau shone. He produced an album of melancholic verses signed by himself—an elegy that won Dinah’s sympathy. Clagny, offended, took refuge in backgammon. Dinah stitched for a supposed charity and replied wryly when taunted about needlework and marriage. Jests about adultery and morality flew: Clagny criticized Parisian writers’ treatment of illicit love; Lousteau defended literature with poets and symbols, provoking the prosecutor.

Conversation widened into paradoxes: history and courts often condemn pioneers—Socrates, Jesus—and institutions persecute what they cannot assimilate. Lousteau teased that religion and state are fragile before new ideas; Clagny called it blasphemy. The company touched nerves about justice, passion, and revenge. Gravier half-joked he’d side with a betrayed husband; Lousteau mused on the sublimity of trusting husbands and the balance of trust and jealousy. Dinah’s quiet sympathy for victims tormented into crime stirred unease: human justice poorly judges private tragedies.

Lousteau recounted a Nodier anecdote about a Chevalier feigning escape from a jealous commandant; no one reacted. Bianchon followed with La Grande Bretèche—the Spanish tale of a grandee walled into a cupboard to starve by a jealous husband—stunning the guests. Gravier added a Spanish adventure of illicit deliveries, betrayals, and murder. Clagny reminded them the Gazette des Tribunaux frequently reported horrors rivaling romance.

The Parisians watched Dinah and Clagny closely, hoping grim tales would elicit a telltale emotion. That night Gravier proposed a country trick: affixing hairs in wax on Dinah’s and Clagny’s door locks to detect nocturnal comings and goings. The doctor, Lousteau, the tax receiver, and Gatien crept barefoot to press hairs into wax seals and agreed to inspect them at five.

At dawn the seals were unbroken—same wax, same hairs. Lousteau declared they had been chasing a will-o’-the-wisp and admitted the matter was more complex. He told Bianchon that if Dinah were truly virtuous she deserved the effort; the joke turned to admiration. Lousteau sought Dinah in the garden and confessed he had plotted the evening’s stories to rouse emotion and test both her and Clagny. He reassured her innocence was proved, criticized cold men, and revealed a softened taste for a certain perfection. Dinah, seeing through him, replied that he sought pleasure not love, but showed sympathy for his vulnerability. For a moment Lousteau’s bravado softened.

He and Bianchon spent the day entertaining Dinah with anecdotes and wit. By sundown sixteen guests had arrived; Dinah prepared the drawing-room proudly. When a footman brought letters and a packet of proofs, Lousteau found a shabby romance used as packing and turned its brigands and absurdities into a mock-critical performance. The improvised critique delighted the assembled provincials; Dinah enjoyed the attention and wit. Thus the evening became a stage where Parisian cleverness dazzled provincial complacency, while the sealed-door mystery remained unresolved.

For three days Dinah’s imagination was stirred by her two Parisian visitors. Lousteau watched her distraction, eager to captivate her. A ruse about Bianchon’s papers in Anzy sent Gatien off to fetch them, leaving Dinah alone with Lousteau and Bianchon. They mocked the locals and debated her unhappy circumstances, making Dinah defensive.

Crossing the Loire, Bianchon told her she had shut herself to love but now needed it, warning that her current life would render her unattractive; Lousteau half-jokingly defended him. At Cosne Dinah, insisting they disembark, walked briskly with Bianchon, who suggested Lousteau loved her though he was frivolous; he counseled cultivating Lousteau, who could bring beauty and happiness. Under Bianchon’s scrutiny Dinah felt vulnerable.

When Gatien returned, Bianchon blessed the party. Lousteau then declared his love; Dinah resisted, asking if she was merely a passing pleasure. Gatien’s arrival complicated matters: Lousteau urged her to feign resistance to save his honor and, recklessly, crushed her muslin dress, leaving her disheveled. Dinah, humiliated, sent Gatien for the papers and retreated indoors; Lousteau followed, begging forgiveness.

After a tense confrontation between Lousteau and Gatien, and Dinah’s silent change into a velvet gown, Lousteau vowed to stay near her and renounce Parisian vices. His charm began to breach her reserve; she laughed, their intimacy deepened in the gardens, and they exchanged a pledge before he returned to Paris. Dinah accompanied him to Cosne and later praised him at La Baudraye for showing her true love. Her other admirers, including Monsieur de Clagny, were left to reckon with their feelings; Dinah made Clagny a confidant, lavishing affection while he remained devoted.

Back in Paris, Lousteau soon forgot the happy days at the Chateau d’Anzy. He lived by his pen in an age when writing—after the rise of the bourgeoisie—had become almost servitude: constant production, or the appearance of it. Writing daily for a newspaper and several literary magazines, he lacked artistic pretensions and ranked among Paris’s literary hacks. Deprived of advancement, he had become a journalist.

His life, nonetheless, was not unpleasant. He socialized with blue‑stockings, aspiring actresses, publishers, and authors, kept theatre boxes, and relied on review fees to cover minor expenses. After ten years he still believed a monthly income of five hundred francs would make him rich, though he actually earned seven to eight hundred a month and rented well‑furnished ground‑floor rooms in the Rue des Martyrs for twelve hundred francs. His finances peaked and dipped—January, April, July, and October were especially tight—because he spent freely on cabs, meals, and gifts for lovers, leaving him perpetually broke.

Weary at times, he joked about marriage with friends like Nathan and Bixiou. He had a mistress, Madame Schontz, proud of having a writer as a lover; she liked teasing friends about relationships. Dinah lingered at the edges of his life: he read and burned one affectionate letter, discarded another unread, and by December her letters piled in a drawer. Madame Schontz opened hampers Dinah had sent and suggested a dinner; Lousteau sent a clever but insincere note, convinced it would charm her.

Then Madame Schontz hinted at a marriagable opportunity: a young woman whose lover had died, a notary father eager to restore her position with a quick marriage and substantial dowry. Pressed by promises of comfort—and warned about a strict mother‑in‑law—Lousteau met the notary, flattered him, and was introduced to the Cardot family. At his first dinner he noticed Félicie, shy and grateful, and saw Madame Cardot regard him as a man of promise. Buoyed by this reception, Lousteau felt a new purpose: stability, respectability, and financial security.

Madame Cardot, having borrowed the illustrated Gil Blas to inspect the prints, went with her daughter Félicie to Lousteau’s bachelor rooms to judge her future son‑in‑law. Her sister‑in‑law Madame Camusot had already been uneasy. In January 1837 they took a cab; the porter was away, but his daughter handed them the key after Madame Cardot slipped her a gold coin.

Around noon Lousteau, returning from breakfast, was startled when Dinah, his former lover, arrived by coach with a child, Pamela, and revealed she was a mother. He tried to dismiss her, but she stayed; Pamela announced that his mother‑in‑law was inside, and Lousteau discovered Madame Cardot and Félicie hiding. Dinah declared her love; Lousteau, fearing the arranged marriage he faced, began to imagine using her to escape it.

Madame Cardot openly disapproved and left. Dinah trusted Lousteau and admired the apparent elegance of his rooms, oblivious to his poverty. Pamela borrowed dinnerware from Madame Schontz, who warned Cardot would not pursue the match. Bixiou argued provincial women often fail in Paris and marriage secures legitimacy, but Lousteau insisted he preferred Dinah to any fortune. When Madame Schontz later learned Cardot had married Félicie to another, Lousteau’s respectable prospects vanished.

Having lost a stable future, Lousteau and Dinah grew entangled; their domestic life slid into the provincial romance he once mocked, leaving both trapped in dependency and illusion, unequipped for hardship.

That winter in Paris transformed Madame de la Baudraye as October had in Sancerre. Etienne introduced Dinah to city life: theater, dressmakers, society. Lousteau, vain and ambitious, stripped away her provincial modesty and she quickly adapted, becoming intelligent, well read, and able to hold her own among sophisticated women. Their passion was intense, and Dinah became both muse and partner, though Lousteau sometimes saw her as a useful asset and toyed privately with the idea of deceiving her.

When Dinah became pregnant she withdrew from public life but first attended a Nathan premiere, where Lousteau’s abandoning her to mingle left her exposed and humiliated beside an old acquaintance, Anna Grossetete. Clagny’s warning that the liaison threatened her reputation stirred nostalgia and regret despite her resolve.

After the child’s birth Dinah devoted herself to motherhood and household management, pawning jewelry to support Lousteau’s career. His financial irresponsibility and laziness soon surfaced: he failed to write regularly, accumulated debts, grew indifferent, and sought comfort elsewhere. A second child increased Dinah’s burdens and his detachment.

The turning point came when her estranged husband, Monsieur de la Baudraye, reappeared seeking a power of attorney for an inheritance. Clagny—now Attorney-General—negotiated an allowance for Dinah and protected her interests. Confronted with betrayal and loss, Dinah realized her sacrifices were unrewarded and resolved to reclaim her identity and seek a future based on mutual respect and commitment rather than mere passion.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
From that day on Etienne lived in luxury and Dinah could hold her own among Paris’s best-dressed women. Lousteau, however, pretended ruin to friends, blamed Madame de la Baudraye, and painted himself as helpless before Dinah’s desperate love—while remaining unfaithful. Monsieur de Clagny, distressed by Dinah’s disgrace, warned her of betrayal; she acknowledged it and, when asked if she still loved Lousteau, cried that she would lose her soul for him, leaving Clagny speechless.

Gradually Dinah saw Lousteau’s true nature. She described him to her mother as a lazy, pleasure-seeking poet she could not hate because she felt responsible—convinced she was his only support. Her mother deplored her suffering; Dinah replied, “I will be a mother to him,” accepting compromises to live with shame. Hoping to retain his affection, she resolved to emulate Madame de Pompadour. His insincere, theatrical devotion eroded her dignity, yet she clung to the hope his love might return and found a bitter consolation in being his plaything.

She often rehearsed the tragedy Le Dernier Jour d’un condamné, picturing their separation, and even contemplated suicide in her garden, but remained devoted. Influenced by the novel Adolphe, she vowed not to become like Ellenore—refusing public reproach or tears.

Meanwhile Monsieur de la Baudraye schemed to restore his fortune by manipulating his wife’s inheritance—reducing its apparent value, pocketing the difference, and investing in land. He convinced himself he owed Dinah nothing for now and imagined a noble life in Sancerre, seeking title and social prominence.

By early 1842 Dinah felt reduced to Lousteau’s reserve, dressing in mourning as penance; her mother worried and sought intervention. With Clagny’s help Madame Piedefer urged Dinah to consider leaving Lousteau; Clagny negotiated, assuring that her husband might allow her to keep the children and return respectably to Paris. Unknown to Dinah, Monsieur de la Baudraye was buying a luxurious Paris residence to polish his image, inviting her to live with him and promising support for their children’s education.

As a Countess preparing for social prominence, Dinah paid off debts in May 1842 and invited Lousteau to dinner. She announced their separation calmly: she still cared but insisted on parting. Lousteau accused her of ruining him; she refused to accept blame and left both liberated and heartbroken.

In her new life she made her home a model of comfort, spending beyond her husband’s expectations. He admired her efforts but was unaware of her inner turmoil. Charming and fashionable, she nevertheless missed past passion and struggled to connect with other women. When Clagny revealed Lousteau was living with another woman, Dinah threw herself into society seeking recognition. Her appointment in March 1843 to collect donations for charity restored her public image. Summers in Sancerre found her mothered by a husband proud to present their children; by June 1844 the family’s status was on display.

Throughout these changes Dinah maintained composure, balancing roles as mother, Countess, and woman shaped by sacrifice—yet torn between love and ambition, public success and private longing, an unresolved tension that defines her journey.
