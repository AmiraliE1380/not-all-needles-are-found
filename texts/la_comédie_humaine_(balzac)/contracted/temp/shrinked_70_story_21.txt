Railroads threaten to render certain transportation services around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son team, faced competition on the Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise route in 1822, despite extending their service to Chambly. Meanwhile, Pierrotin, a popular coachman on the Isle-Adam route, coordinated schedules with a rival to benefit customers. On a Saturday morning, he awaited passengers while needing funds for a new coach. The Comte de Serizy sought a ride incognito, dealing with personal issues and conspiracies against him, while Pierrotin met Madame Clapart and her son Oscar, who struggled with feelings of inadequacy before their journey.

In public transport, conversations often begin with the most talkative passengers. Georges, a clever young man, pretends to be a Russian prince on a French stage-coach, captivating fellow travelers with his stories. The count discovers Georges is actually Crottat’s second clerk, yet Georges continues to share tales despite the count's skepticism, exploring themes of identity and storytelling.

Oscar, feeling shy, is introduced to Mistigris and painters by Monsieur Moreau, who encourages him to learn horseback riding. Tensions arise between the Moreaus and Reyberts due to Madame Moreau's past. When the painters arrive, Madame Moreau tries to impress but is confronted by the count about gossip overheard during Oscar's journey, leading to Moreau's dismissal.

Distraught, Moreau blames Oscar, who is forced to apologize and is ultimately dismissed. Madame Clapart urges Oscar to find a career, and he begins preparing for law school. After passing his exams, he works as a clerk, facing societal expectations.

At a lavish gathering, Oscar loses money gambling and faces consequences from his uncle and Desroches. Realizing his mistakes, he resolves to change, enlists in the cavalry, excels, and matures, eventually marrying Georgette Pierrotin and reflecting on his past.
