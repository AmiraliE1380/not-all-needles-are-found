To Baron Von Hammer-Purgstall, Member of the Aulic Council, Author of the History of the Ottoman Empire.

Dear Baron, your encouragement of my “History of French Manners in the Nineteenth Century” led me to associate your name with my work. As a representative of studious Germany, your approval lends credibility to my efforts. Your dedication to research sustained me in my labor on modern civilization. I hope this token of respect finds you well in Dobling, and reminds you of your sincere admirer and friend.

THE COLLECTION OF ANTIQUITIES

In a small French town stands the house called the Hotel d’Esgrignon, home of the old Marquis Charles Marie Victor Ange Carol. What began as a jest in naming became earnest over time. The d’Esgrignon family, descended from powerful Northmen, preserved a proud lineage and provincial nobility, refusing titles that would diminish their status and never bowing to kings or commoners. Revered locally, they maintained dignity despite long neglect by the court.

During the Revolution the d’Esgrignons were imperiled. The Marquis, respected in the countryside, avoided emigration but went into hiding. Family lands were sold; yet Mlle. d’Esgrignon managed to save some property through the family steward, Chesnel. After the Terror the Marquis returned to a dilapidated castle, mourning lost heritage. The old Hotel d’Esgrignon had been demolished; Chesnel used the last of the Marquis’s money to buy a new home for the family, wryly named the Hotel d’Esgrignon.

Around 1800 émigrés returned, including the Baron de Nouastre and his daughter, whom the Marquis married. She died in childbirth, leaving a son to carry the family name. Grief compounded the Marquis’s sense of diminished circumstances. Later, when a local contractor proposed to Mlle. d’Esgrignon, she and her brother found the offer insulting; this incident drove a wedge between them and Chesnel, who had acted on the contractor’s behalf.

Chesnel, a respected notary, was far more than a servant: confidant, manager, and protector of family interests. The altered dynamics troubled him; the Marquis regarded Chesnel as a loyal companion, while Chesnel felt the strain of fidelity to a declining house. Politically the era shifted—some nobles aligned with Napoleon while others remained monarchist—and the d’Esgrignons held fast to their principles.

By 1804 many émigrés had returned, yet the d’Esgrignons’ fortunes did not recover. The Marquis became a symbol of loyalty and old nobility, a leader among remaining royalists. His salon, nicknamed the “Collection of Antiquities,” became a gathering for those clinging to the past; what was once a playful sobriquet took on grave meaning as political tides turned.

The family faced challenges from the rising bourgeoisie, epitomized by du Croisier, who sought to undermine their standing. Rivalry intensified as his faction gained strength while the d’Esgrignons remained a bulwark of old-world values. By 1822 their situation remained precarious: the Marquis, now elderly, was uncertain of his role in a changing society; the Restoration had not restored prosperity, and income barely met needs. The Collection of Antiquities stood as a testament to a bygone era and the d’Esgrignons’ enduring legacy.

The Marquis neglected estate management, and most found the idea that the d’Esgrignons could regain their former glory absurd. Hopes centered on Victurnien, the handsome heir, marrying into nobility to restore prestige. In truth the family lacked the political connections and finances to reclaim status; their continuity had been broken over the preceding forty years.

Only Chesnel, the notary, grasped the futility of such hopes. He recognized the social changes of the Revolution and the altered political landscape. Loyal to the family, he nevertheless urged adaptation; the old Marquis, however, remained convinced that fate or divine intervention would restore their fortunes.

Victurnien, adored by his father and aunt, had been a spoiled child who never learned opposition. That indulgence made him selfish and reckless. The Chevalier, a relic of the old order, fed his extravagance with romantic tales of past grandeur, inflating his sense of superiority. Surrounded by flatterers and exempt from ordinary constraints, Victurnien came to regard himself as above the common people, deepening both moral and practical flaws.

When he entered society, the gap between his upbringing and modern realities became clear. His behavior alienated townspeople; escapades produced legal troubles Chesnel discreetly managed. Encouraged by companions who enjoyed his company, his arrogance hardened into excess, assuming lineage would shield him from consequences.

Du Croisier, a rival, sought to exploit Victurnien’s weaknesses and hasten the d’Esgrignons’ ruin. Meanwhile Victurnien’s vanity and appetite for pleasure pushed him into gambling and frivolity, sustained by Chesnel’s financial sacrifices, which he accepted as his due. Sent to Paris with high expectations, he soon discovered the court far less welcoming than imagined; the nobility he met were largely self-interested and indifferent to reviving the d’Esgrignon name.

Undeterred, he tried to secure a place by adopting the extravagant lifestyle of Parisian elites. His charm and looks opened doors; he became a fixture in high society, most captivated by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Enchanted by her beauty and the glitter of her world, he fell intensely in love, blind to her debts and convinced their passion was pure.

As he immersed himself in Parisian pleasures his debts mounted beyond his means. Companions who enjoyed his company also recognized his precarious state and egged his spending, entertained by the prospect of his fall. Chesnel, ever loyal, continued to prop him up financially but grew increasingly alarmed. Despite warnings, Victurnien remained absorbed in fantasy, oblivious to reality.

Victurnien’s passage through Parisian high society exposes the dangers of vanity, excess, and the illusion of nobility. His entanglement with the Duchesse and the accumulating deceit and financial ruin underscore the fragility of status and the consequences of living beyond one’s means, offering a cautionary tale about pride and the need to recognize limits in a changing world.

By the end of 1823 the Kellers had advanced Victurnien two hundred thousand francs, without Chesnel or Mlle. Armande knowing; Chesnel had also paid two thousand crowns to conceal the source. Victurnien wrote deceitful letters to his father and aunt, who remained unaware of the disaster looming over their house. Only du Croisier knew, and he took pleasure not only in ruining the d’Esgrignons but in dishonoring them; he savored his approaching revenge as Victurnien’s debts mounted.

Du Croisier’s first move targeted Chesnel, who lived modestly in the Rue du Bercail. One afternoon, after dinner, Chesnel’s housekeeper announced du Croisier. The old lawyer’s heart sank as du Croisier presented a bill for two hundred and twenty-seven thousand francs, demanding payment or threatening to expose Victurnien’s obligations. Overwhelmed, Chesnel realized he could raise the money only by mortgaging his lands. Du Croisier seized the chance, proposed to buy Chesnel’s property—and did so within two days. Stripped of his lands, Chesnel was left with only his practice and his house, consumed by worry for Victurnien’s future.

Chesnel dreaded telling Mlle. Armande about the family’s financial ruin. She had just received a charming, hypocritical letter from Victurnien describing his travels in Italy with the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse and remained enchanted and oblivious. Chesnel’s arrival shattered her reverie: all was lost, and Victurnien risked bringing dishonor on their house. Devastated, she realized they had given Victurnien too much freedom.

The Marquis, having read Victurnien’s letter, remained unaware of the crisis. Mlle. Armande and Chesnel shared a somber understanding of their concern for the young Count. They discussed intervening to save him from his reckless lifestyle, fearing he would be left destitute. Mlle. Armande resolved to go to Paris to rescue Victurnien, masking the trip as a medical consultation to avoid gossip.

Chesnel gave her his last bag of louis, and she set off, determined to confront the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse. Du Croisier, meanwhile, had arranged his revenge to ensure Victurnien would be cut off from further support. He wrote to Victurnien, informing him that the Kellers would advance no more money without security, timing the letter to coincide with Victurnien’s increasing desperation.

Victurnien, until then living in luxury, suddenly faced the reality of his debts. Despite earlier gambling wins, he found himself unable to procure funds and felt the weight of ruin. In despair he briefly considered suicide but rejected it. Instead, he contemplated fleeing with the Duchess and forging a bill of exchange to finance their escape, though he hesitated, wanting the Duchess’s agreement first.

When he confessed his financial troubles to the Duchess, she assured him money could be found and agreed to escape with him. Buoyed by her promise, Victurnien forged a bill for three hundred thousand francs and presented it to the Kellers, who advanced the money, unaware of the deceit. Du Croisier, learning of the transaction, realized his plan for revenge was bearing fruit.

When Victurnien received the money he gave it to Mme. de Maufrigneuse, who locked it away and suggested one last visit to the Opera. He considered confessing everything to Chesnel but, despite doubts, lingered at the Duchess's behest; her enchanting glance kept him longer than he intended.

The next day he returned to the Hotel de Maufrigneuse intending to plan an escape, only to find the Duchess resolved to stay. She believed it would be more remarkable to face the storm than to flee, and Victurnien, torn between admiration and despair, realized how deeply he loved her.

After their argument Diane's coldness left Victurnien distraught. He begged forgiveness, but she remained unyielding—an angelic indifference that intensified his anguish. As time passed his despair deepened; he felt lost and unable to think clearly. Meanwhile the Count had taken Diane's hand but found it lifeless, a moment that intensified the household's distress.

Victurnien’s personal turmoil was compounded by the discovery that his financial misdeeds had put him in serious trouble. When Josephin informed him that a warrant for his arrest had been issued, he panicked, convinced he was ruined. Josephin urged him to flee to the Hotel du Bon la Fontaine, where Mlle. Armande waited. Clinging to his distraught but determined aunt, Victurnien discussed hiding and the possibility of slipping into Chesnel’s house unnoticed. In executing the plan he unwittingly walked into a trap.

Chesnel, meanwhile, was selling his practice to a rival notary, M. Lepressoir, who hoped to profit from Victurnien's misfortune. Learning of the young man’s plight devastated Chesnel; fearing for Victurnien’s future, he even contemplated taking the blame for the forgery himself. Resolving to act, Chesnel decided to confront du Croisier, Victurnien's creditor, and negotiate a settlement.

Du Croisier, however, reveled in his power over the d’Esgrignon family. He saw the affair as an opportunity to assert dominance and settle scores with the aristocracy. At a gathering at his place, news of Victurnien's arrest spread among guests who were both shocked and intrigued; the atmosphere grew tense as allies discussed the implications and the potential trial.

Realizing the gravity of the situation, Chesnel sought to influence the examining magistrate, M. Camusot, by appealing to his ambitions and connections, hoping this would mitigate damage to Victurnien's reputation. As legal proceedings unfolded, Chesnel intensified his efforts and turned to Mme. de Maufrigneuse. Initially unaware of the forgery’s full extent, she felt compelled to help once she understood Victurnien's love and the consequences he faced.

Negotiations with du Croisier proved difficult: he demanded not only financial compensation but political favors and social recognition—requests Chesnel could scarcely satisfy. In desperation Chesnel offered a substantial sum to have the charges withdrawn, but du Croisier’s pride and ambition made him resistant. Both sides were unwilling to yield, and the conflict escalated as the trial approached.

Chesnel’s determination drove him toward morally questionable options, including lying under oath, as he realized the d’Esgrignon family’s honor hung in the balance. The legal battle thus became emblematic of broader tensions between aristocracy and bourgeoisie: its outcome would decide Victurnien's fate and signal shifting power dynamics. Throughout, Chesnel’s unwavering loyalty and willingness to navigate provincial politics highlighted the complexities of love, honor, and ambition in a world where social status could dictate destiny.

Blondet, a competent judge, was eclipsed by his passion for gardening—especially pelargoniums—which consumed his time and ambition. His marriage to a much younger woman, who had an affair with the local prefect, further clouded his career. Though he had a legitimate son, Joseph, Blondet preferred his illegitimate son Emile, whom he ultimately sent to Paris with scant support after his wife’s death.

His modest brick house and neat garden reflected his horticultural devotion. Despite his favoritism, Blondet planned to leave his estate to Joseph and had disinherited Emile, whom he regarded with contempt. Emile, however, thrived in Paris thanks to help from his mother’s family, notably Mlle. de Troisville, who promised patronage and aided his literary recognition—successes Blondet never learned of, absorbed as he was in law and gardening. Blondet’s life was defined by frugality, enforced by a maid who ran the household.

Joseph was being groomed for the bench and spent time with the Blandureau family, while Michu, a younger lawyer, moved through social circles and often outshone the older judges. Meanwhile the public prosecutor, a shrewd politician, became entangled in a scheme against the d’Esgrignons, producing a forgery charge against the young noble Victurnien d’Esgrignon.

The President of the Tribunal, seeking advantage for du Croisier—a rival of the d’Esgrignons—manipulated events. Influenced by the President’s wife, the prosecutor pursued the case against Victurnien despite potential political costs.

As the trial approached, the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse intervened, providing funds to secure Victurnien’s release. With Chesnel, the family notary, supporting the defense and restoring the family’s standing, the trial exposed the truth: Victurnien was exonerated and du Croisier’s intrigues laid bare.

Afterward the d’Esgrignons endured social scrutiny but retained their honor. Victurnien’s future remained uncertain as he confronted his past and lineage’s expectations. The narrative probes ambition, familial loyalty, and shifting social ranks in a changing France, with characters like Chesnel embodying fading feudal devotion.
