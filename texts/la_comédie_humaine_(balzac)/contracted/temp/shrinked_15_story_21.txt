Railroads threaten to make certain transportation services around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son team, previously dominated travel within a 45-mile radius but faced stiff competition from local services, particularly the coucous, which excelled on shorter routes. In 1822, they contended with three rivals on the lucrative Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise route, despite extending their service to Chambly.

The Isle-Adam route had less competition, with only two coaches operating smoothly. Pierrotin, a popular coachman, coordinated schedules with his rival to benefit customers from the “Lion d’Argent” inn, where the innkeeper’s wife managed bookings.

On a Saturday morning in 1822, Pierrotin awaited passengers outside the inn, anxious about his financial situation after ordering a new coach. His inherited coucou was cramped, and he needed to raise a thousand francs for a more luxurious vehicle.

A footman approached, seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. The footman hinted at intrigue surrounding the count's canceled carriage. Eager for a generous tip, Pierrotin agreed, reflecting on the count’s steward, Monsieur Moreau, a respected figure who often employed him. This chapter introduces themes of ambition, social dynamics, and the evolving transportation landscape in early 19th-century France, focusing on Pierrotin's struggles and aspirations.

Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from a notable family, tracing back to President Huguet, ennobled under François I. The family coat of arms features a party per pale or and sable, with the motto “i, semper melius eris.” The current count, a State councillor at twenty-two, distinguished himself during the Revolution by remaining on his estate, protected by his father's reputation. After his father's death in 1794, he became a prominent figure under Napoleon, who appointed him to the Council of State and made him a count and senator.

In 1806, he married the widow of a Republican general, significantly increasing his wealth. By 1814, due to health issues, he resigned from his posts and returned to Paris, remaining loyal to the Bourbons. After Napoleon's second fall, he resumed political duties, becoming vice-president of the Council of State and managing foreign claims without seeking personal glory. His life was marked by hard work, earning numerous honors for his service.

Despite public success, the count suffered from unfulfilled love for his wife, who enjoyed considerable freedom. He respected her autonomy while remaining devoted, even as his health deteriorated. His dedication to work masked emotional pain stemming from jealousy and the complexities of their marriage.

The narrative shifts to a pressing matter involving a farm encroaching upon the count's estate, Presles. The lease on this farm, held by farmer Leger, was expiring, and owner Margueron refused to renew it. The count sought to purchase the farm to eliminate disputes and consulted his notary and lawyer, who advised inviting Margueron to dinner to finalize the sale.

Before this meeting, the count initiated renovations at Presles to surprise his wife but decided to travel incognito. Moreau, the steward of Presles, had a complex background; initially loyal, he later accepted bribes and engaged in dishonest dealings, aiming to profit from the sale of the farm.

As the count prepared for his trip, Moreau conspired with Leger to inflate the farm's price. Meanwhile, retired officer Monsieur de Reybert and his wife sought to expose Moreau's dishonesty. Madame de Reybert approached the count, claiming Moreau was scheming to defraud him. Initially skeptical, the count began to doubt Moreau's integrity after reading a contradictory letter from the steward and decided to investigate further.

As Pierrotin left the Café de l’Echiquier, he spotted Madame Clapart and her son Oscar at the Lion d’Argent. The mother, in a worn black gown, anxiously searched for him, reflecting her devotion to Oscar, who was traveling alone for the first time. Pierrotin hesitated to voice concerns about the challenges Oscar would face.

Pierrotin often transported goods for Moreau, who had a discreet arrangement with Madame Clapart, delivering baskets of food to her home. Despite their struggles, Madame Clapart’s unwavering love for Oscar blinded her to his shortcomings. Moreau had helped secure a scholarship for Oscar at a prestigious school, but Oscar’s vanity troubled him.

As they prepared for the journey, Madame Clapart fussed over Oscar, reminding him to be respectful. Oscar, embarrassed by his mother’s attentiveness, longed for independence. The young men nearby mocked him, aggravating his insecurities. Eventually, the coach filled with passengers, including a stout farmer named Pere Leger and the Comte de Serizy, who arrived last, disguised as a bourgeois.

As the journey began, Georges expressed impatience with Pierrotin’s pace, while Mistigris, a young artist, made light of the situation. The count, overhearing their comments, remained composed, aware of the importance of keeping his identity hidden. The group’s dynamics shifted as they traveled, with Oscar grappling with feelings of inadequacy while others engaged in light-hearted exchanges.

In public transport, passengers often begin in silence, observing one another until conversation starts, usually led by the most talkative. This is especially true in French stage-coaches, known for their lively banter. Georges, a clever young man, assessed his fellow travelers, feeling superior and planning to entertain himself.

As the coach descended from La Chapelle, Georges decided to pose as a disguised Russian prince, engaging in playful banter with Mistigris and spinning tales of his supposed adventures in the East. He embellished his past with stories of battles and claimed to be the grandson of Czerni-Georges, a historical figure who fought against the Ottoman Empire.

Upon arriving in Saint-Denis, they stopped at a tavern where Georges enjoyed the attention of his fellow travelers, treating them while continuing his elaborate tales. The count, intrigued yet skeptical, suspected Georges was not who he claimed to be and discovered a portfolio revealing him as Crottat’s second clerk.

As they resumed their journey, Georges shared more about his military exploits, claiming crucial roles in battles and lavish gifts from Ali Tebelen. The count warned him about the dangers of boasting, but Georges remained undeterred, entertaining the group with tales of a dramatic encounter involving a beautiful woman named Zena.

Meanwhile, Pierrotin's coach arrived in Saint-Brice for a routine stop. Georges impressed Oscar but sparked jealousy when he lit a cigar. As they waited, Mistigris joked about mismatched socks, amusing the count. Discussions about a land deal involving the Comte de Serizy caught the count's attention, hinting at a conspiracy involving his steward, Moreau.

As they drove away, Georges and the farmer admired the landscape, while Oscar, feeling belittled, boasted about his future in diplomacy. The conversation shifted to the countess, prompting the count to defend her honor before leaving the coach, leaving the young men confused. As they neared the chateau de Presles, Pierrotin planned to drop them off, while Oscar rushed ahead, unaware of his path.

Oscar, feeling shy, hid behind trees as Monsieur Moreau, in a blue overcoat, greeted him and introduced him to Mistigris and the painters sent by architect Monsieur Grindot. Moreau invited them to dinner and mentioned hunting opportunities on the estate, sending his son Jacques to inform Madame Moreau of Oscar's arrival. He encouraged Oscar to learn horseback riding and hunting, despite his reluctance, as his stay was limited to two weeks due to Madame Moreau's presence.

The steward's house, connected to the chateau, had a salon and boudoir for Madame Moreau, who enjoyed her elevated status as a former maid. Tensions existed between the Moreaus and the Reyberts due to reminders of Madame Moreau's past. The Moreaus cultivated relationships with local bourgeoisie, hosting gatherings and playing cards with the rector and mayor.

Madame Moreau eagerly awaited the painters, hoping to impress them, but was disappointed when only Mistigris's packages arrived. When the artists finally came, she tried to showcase her art knowledge, though her past occasionally surfaced. Just as the atmosphere relaxed, a maid announced the count's unexpected arrival, shocking Madame Moreau.

The count confronted Moreau about gossip overheard during Oscar's journey, leading to Moreau's dismissal while allowing him to keep his earnings. As the count prepared for dinner, tensions rose with various guests arriving. Mistigris made light-hearted comments, while clerk Georges Marest faced trouble for his antics.

Distraught over losing his position, Moreau confronted Oscar, blaming him for the situation and demanding an apology to the count. Overwhelmed, Oscar resisted, leading to a confrontation where Moreau forced him to apologize. The count dismissed Oscar, leaving Moreau to contemplate the fallout from the evening's events.

While preparing for a journey, Moreau wrote to Madame Clapart, expressing distress over Oscar's indiscretion, which led to his dismissal as steward. He decided to send Oscar home, feeling the weight of his family's future and lamenting Oscar's behavior.

That evening, Madame Clapart was knitting when her husband, Clapart, expressed concern about their friend Poiret possibly visiting. Clapart criticized Oscar, believing he would cause trouble at Presles, while Madame Clapart defended her son. Suddenly, a carriage arrived with Oscar, and Brochon informed them of Moreau's dismissal. Distressed, Madame Clapart fainted upon reading Moreau's letter, and when she regained consciousness, she urged Oscar to go to bed as Clapart continued to berate him.

Despite the turmoil, Oscar slept soundly, unaware of the gravity of his situation. The next day, he felt hungry and surprised that life continued as normal. His mother explained the dire consequences of his actions and emphasized the need for him to find a career, outlining limited options like commerce, government work, or military service. She expressed fears about their financial instability and urged him to enlist in the army.

Oscar, still naive, listened but struggled to grasp their reality. Her tears moved him, and she implored him to promise to be more discreet. They discussed seeking help from Uncle Cardot, who might assist Oscar.

Uncle Cardot, a wealthy widower, received them during their visit. Madame Clapart highlighted Oscar's achievements, hoping to gain his support. Cardot, while polite, was skeptical about Oscar's future. Moved by her plight, he offered to help Oscar pursue a legal career, promising to cover his educational costs if he showed promise. He advised Oscar to work hard and maintain integrity.

After their visit, Madame Clapart felt hopeful for Oscar's future. Moreau later reassured her that Oscar could still have a successful career if disciplined. He proposed that Oscar work under a young lawyer named Desroches, who would provide the structure he needed. Madame Clapart was grateful for Moreau's support and urged Oscar to express his thanks.

Ten days later, Oscar was introduced to Maitre Desroches, who took an interest in him. Desroches informed his head clerk, Monsieur Godeschal, that Oscar would need strict supervision to excel in law school. Godeschal, also from humble beginnings, was tasked with guiding Oscar, emphasizing hard work and integrity.

For two years, Oscar lived a monk-like existence, waking early to work alongside Godeschal. He performed errands, prepared for law school, and faced rigorous examinations. His routine included simple meals and continued work until nightfall. He occasionally visited Uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother, but he was often reprimanded for attempts at elegance.

Godeschal advised Oscar on maintaining a modest wardrobe and living frugally. Eighteen months in, Oscar became the third clerk, performing duties without a salary but receiving room and board. By the end of his second year, he had gained significant legal knowledge and was performing well at the Palais.

Despite his progress, Oscar felt trapped, envying the well-dressed young men he saw and longing for freedom. Godeschal occasionally took him out for leisure to help him cope with monotony.

In November 1825, after passing his exams, Oscar received funds from Moreau to upgrade his wardrobe and began working as the second clerk with a salary. However, his internal conflict persisted; he often contemplated leaving his rigorous life. Godeschal remained vigilant, ensuring Oscar was not exposed to temptations.

When Godeschal returned from a trip, he reported to Moreau that Oscar was becoming vain and seeking social adventures. Shortly after Oscar's promotion, a new clerk named Frederic Marest joined the office, seeking to learn legal practice. The clerks engaged in a tradition of welcoming new members with humor, helping them bond and relieve monotony.

Oscar's superior, Desroches, tasked him with retrieving a crucial legal document and warned him about Derville, who might hinder their efforts. Eager to prove himself, Oscar accepted the challenge.

After Georges left, Godeschal inquired about the marquise, and Frederic claimed she was a wealthy widow from Mexico. The clerks, excited about mingling with high society, prepared for the event. The Marquise was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse supported by her benefactor, Pere Cardot. Florentine had risen in status and was courting Georges, who had recently inherited a fortune.

As the clerks anticipated the gathering, Oscar felt excitement and apprehension, particularly regarding Georges, who had previously caused him distress. Despite his reservations, he donned new clothes and set out. At the Rocher de Cancale, Oscar met various guests, including an old captain and a journalist. Georges charmed Oscar, downplaying past grievances. The feast was extravagant, leading to intoxication among the clerks.

Afterward, they headed to Florentine's salon, where Oscar was captivated by her transformation into a sophisticated marquise. He soon lost significant money in a game of vingt-et-un, including five hundred francs entrusted to him by Desroches. Overwhelmed, Oscar contemplated despair but was reassured by Florentine, who offered him a thousand francs to recover. He accepted but lost even more.

By morning, Oscar was exhausted and fell asleep on a sofa. He was awakened by his uncle Cardot, who scolded him for his late-night revelry. Tensions escalated when Florentine revealed Oscar's gambling losses, prompting Cardot to chastise him. Distraught, Oscar begged Cardot for help, and reluctantly, Cardot gave him five hundred francs, warning it would be the last time.

Desroches was furious with Oscar for his irresponsibility and decided to dismiss him. At home, Oscar's mother, Madame Clapart, learned of his troubles and was devastated. When Oscar confronted her, he asserted his independence, deepening her sorrow.

Feeling the weight of his mistakes, Oscar resolved to change. He drew a military number, leading to his enlistment in the cavalry. His fortunes changed when he was assigned to a noble regiment, thanks to the Comte de Serizy. Overwhelmed by her son's misfortunes, Madame Clapart turned to religion for solace.

Oscar excelled in the military, earning honors for bravery during a battle in Africa, saving the Vicomte de Serizy. Years later, Oscar, now a decorated officer, traveled with his mother to Beaumont, where they encountered Georges Marest, whose life had taken a downward turn. Oscar reflected on their contrasting circumstances, having risen from past mistakes while Georges had fallen into obscurity.

Oscar's military career flourished, and he eventually married Georgette Pierrotin, marking a new chapter in his life. Through his experiences, Oscar matured into a respectable man, earning a position as a collector and later a receiver-general, exemplifying the lessons learned from his earlier mistakes.
