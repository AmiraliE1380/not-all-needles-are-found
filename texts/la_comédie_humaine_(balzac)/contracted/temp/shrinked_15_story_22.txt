**Modeste Mignon: Summary**

**Dedication**  
The book is dedicated to a Polish lady, celebrating her roles as a daughter, angel, witch, and poet.

**Chapter I: The Chalet**  
In October 1829, notary Simon Babylas Latournelle walks with his family and clerk, Jean Butscha, to the Chalet in Ingouville. He instructs his son Exupere to engage in a secret plan involving Mademoiselle Modeste Mignon, hinting at her romantic inclinations. Madame Latournelle, proud of her lineage, is portrayed comically, while Exupere prepares to leave for law studies. The Chalet reflects the Mignon family's decline from wealth.

**Chapter II: A Portrait from Life**  
The Latournelles visit the Mignons and meet banker Gobenheim. Modeste, embroidering beside her blind mother, becomes the focus, while tension arises as Dumay, the cashier, prepares for a confrontation regarding Modeste's suitor.

**Chapter III: Preliminaries**  
Dumay's loyalty to the Mignons stems from his service under Colonel Mignon during the Napoleonic Wars, and he vows to protect Modeste's honor.

**Chapter IV: A Simple Story**  
The Mignon family's downfall, including the tragic death of Bettina, Modeste's sister, is explored alongside societal gossip and their efforts to maintain dignity.

**Chapter V: The Problem Still Unsolved**  
Madame Mignon worries about Modeste's emotional state, leading to humorous attempts to uncover her feelings.

**Chapter VI: A Maiden’s First Romance**  
Modeste's romantic fantasies reveal her desire for idealized love, influenced by her readings.

**Chapter VII: A Poet of the Angelic School**  
Modeste writes to poet Canalis, who responds with flattery and caution, leaving her disillusioned. Themes of love, societal expectations, and the contrast between reality and idealism permeate Modeste's journey.

To Monsieur de Canalis:

Monsieur, you are a great poet and an honest man. After your candidness with a young girl in despair, can you answer me truthfully? Would your response to my letter have been the same if you had known that Mademoiselle O. d’Este has six million francs and seeks a fool for a husband?

I admire your talent and integrity, which compel me to remain your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

---

Ernest de La Briere contemplated this letter as he walked the boulevards, feeling conflicted. He recognized that he would not have responded to a wealthy heiress with the same sincerity.

Six days later, Modeste received a letter from him:

Mademoiselle, you are not a D’Este. Your name is a disguise. Do I owe revelations to someone who is untruthful about herself? Answer me sincerely, and I will respond to your question.

---

La Briere acknowledged that had he known of her wealth, he might have acted differently. He valued honesty and wished to maintain his self-respect, admitting he was drawn to Modeste’s beauty.

---

Buoyed by La Briere’s response, Modeste felt joy and confusion, devising a plan to keep their correspondence secret. She expressed her feelings openly in her next letter, revealing her noble lineage and wealth, longing for a connection free from societal constraints.

---

La Briere wrestled with his feelings and resolved to meet Modeste’s father to confess his love. Meanwhile, Modeste’s family dynamics complicate matters, with her blind mother and wealthy merchant father, alongside the arrival of the Duc d’Herouville, a potential suitor.

---

As Modeste prepares to meet her suitors, including La Briere and Canalis, she navigates the complexities of love, ambition, and familial duty, leading to a climax that will determine her fate.
