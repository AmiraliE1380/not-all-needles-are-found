Many tales, rich in plot, can be rendered simply without losing charm. But some human experiences require a delicate touch: certain details—anatomical, one might say—demand a nuanced rendering of thought. Portraits need a soul; some things can only be articulated through secret harmonies born of time, circumstance, or an inner moral inclination.

Such revelations are essential to tell this simple story, intended for those who are grave and reflective, who draw sustenance from tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon at a dying friend’s side, feels reverence for the subject, should not the reader share that feeling? It is easy to resonate with the vague sadness that surrounds us, a gentle suffering oddly pleasing. If the reader has mourned a dear one, is alone, or the day is waning, they may continue; if not, they should set this book aside. Those who have never buried a beloved relative will not grasp these pages, which may seem fragrant to some and colorless to others. The reader must have known the luxury of tears and the silent pangs of memory, evoking regret for what is lost and smiles for past joys.

The writer assures this narrative is a true story, worthy of the reader's emotional investment, if they possess any.

In contemporary France, language mirrors as many idioms and idiosyncrasies as there are individuals. It is entertaining to hear differing accounts of the same person from Parisians. Ask a Practical man about Madame Firmiani and he will describe wealth and standing: a fine house in the rue du Bac, well-furnished salons, an income of a hundred thousand francs, and a husband once a receiver-general—solid people, nothing to criticize.

A Lounger knows her as a hostess: respectable house, excellent tea, gatherings of the best society. An Egotist dismisses her salons as dull, lamenting that possessing a mind should exempt one from trivial conversation. A Student calls her enchanting and wishes to introduce you. A Fop notes faded beauty but admits a lingering charm.

A Connoisseur praises her collection of paintings, while a Spiteful woman warns against visiting her, labeling her a dangerous siren who arouses jealousy. An Attache remembers her beauty a decade ago. A Distinguishing type remarks on elite connections despite modest living. A Duke pretends not to know her, recognizing only those presented at court. A Ninny mistakenly claims she used to sing at the Opera. Two old ladies gossip about her lineage, revealing ties to the noble Cadignan family.

An Original declares her salon safe since any scoundrels present are of high quality. An Observer describes a beautiful woman who rarely rises to greet guests, suggesting she is a mystery married to a mythical husband; he notes her charm and conversational skill, hinting at many admirers but asserting none favored. A Contradictor will claim she was once Murat’s mistress, while an Envious person dismisses her wealth, calling her a coquette who ruined a young man’s prospects—an oft-seen, puzzling spite.

Thus Madame Firmiani emerges through many lenses—each account revealing more about the observer than the observed. These varied testimonies, shaped by personal bias and experience, invite readers to explore interpretations while reflecting on the deeper emotional truths that connect them all.

In January 1824, opinions about Madame Firmiani varied so widely that no clear portrait emerged. She might be a widow or a wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, beautiful or plain. This range of interpretations showed how society distorted a reputation, like lithographic blocks producing different images from slight variations.

That winter Monsieur de Bourbonne, an elderly country magnate from Touraine, grew intrigued. A provincial planter used to managing an estate and tenants, he was shrewd and accustomed to practical affairs. He came to Paris to learn more, but gossip only increased his confusion. A man of honor, he had a beloved nephew, Octave de Camps, in whom he had high hopes. Octave had recently sold an estate without consulting him, which angered the old man. A distant relative then claimed Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and was now teaching mathematics to survive—a revelation that deeply troubled Monsieur de Bourbonne.

Refusing to accept the worst, he went to Paris to investigate Octave’s situation. Using his connections in the faubourg Saint-Germain, he learned more about Madame Firmiani and resolved to meet her under the name de Rouxellay, hoping to discern the truth of her relationship with Octave. He chose a time when he knew Octave would be occupied, and soon confirmed that the rumors of his nephew’s financial distress were true.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was no typical provincial uncle; once a member of the King’s guard, he understood society’s manners and politics. Entering Madame Firmiani’s salon with the Marquise de Listomère, he expressed pity for Octave, wondering how a woman of such luxury could be linked to someone in such straits. Yet on seeing Madame Firmiani his anger melted. Her beauty, heightened by the salon’s atmosphere, captivated him and made him reconsider his initial judgments.

Madame Firmiani, said to be twenty-five but closer to twenty-eight, had married the invisible Firmiani at sixteen. At her height she combined beauty, wealth, musical talent, intelligence, and wit. Admired in high society and respected by rivals, her popularity made her a target for malicious gossip; many envied her apparent happiness and propriety. Though the rumors were unfounded, they persisted, revealing the dangers of societal suspicion.

This account hints at Madame Firmiani’s true nature: a complex blend of qualities—beauty, grace, charm—that inspired admiration and intrigue.

The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had left; Madame Firmiani found him still seated before her as the clock struck two in the morning.

“Madame,” he began as she rose, signaling he should go, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat back down, visibly moved. The old man, Monsieur de Bourbonne, could not tell whether her pallor was shame or pleasure—provincial men often misread reserved women. She gave him a sharp look that puzzled him.

“Someone in my province tells me my nephew has ruined himself for you, living in a garret while you enjoy wealth,” he said bluntly. “Forgive my rustic honesty; you should know these rumors.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interrupted. “I know of them. You are too polite to press and too gallant to suspect me. It would be absurd to defend myself. Believe me when I say I scorn money; I married without fortune. Your nephew’s wealth or poverty matters not; I welcome him as a friend. All my friends respect one another; I cannot accept those I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip and dishonesty.”

“Madame,” Monsieur de Bourbonne said, trembling, “I am old, almost a father to Octave. Pardon me for one question. I swear as a gentleman your answer shall remain here.” He placed his hand on his heart. “Do you love Octave?”

“To any other man I would answer with a look,” she replied. “To you I ask what you would think of a woman who answered such a question. To confess love is the lover’s reward. To tell another—” She left the sentence unfinished, rose, bowed, and withdrew to her private rooms.

He left thinking her either sly or angelic.

The next morning about eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne found Octave in his garret. The old man berated him for squandering the advantages of a comfortable uncle who meant to leave him an estate in Touraine. “Have you been gambling? Lost at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie, I’ll return to my youthful ways.”

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” he went on, approvingly. “Have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle,” Octave admitted.

“I guessed as much. In my day women were cleverer at ruining men—this one is a piece of the last century,” the old man said.

“You are mistaken, uncle. Madame Firmiani deserves esteem and adoration,” Octave said tenderly. He produced a letter from her, given in a portfolio she had handed him. “Read this; it explains almost everything.”

Octave began to read aloud: “‘My beloved—’”

“Then you are still intimate with her?” his uncle interrupted.

“Of course,” Octave said. “We are married.”

“Heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Then why do you live in a garret?”

“Let me continue.” Octave read, sometimes pausing with feeling: “‘My beloved Husband, you ask why I am sad. I cannot deceive you; a wife should be gay and caressing. I long to love you forever; a woman’s glory is in the man she loves. Esteem, consideration, honor—must they not belong to him who receives our all? Well, my angel has fallen. The tale you told me has tarnished my past joys. I feel humiliated in you—you whom I thought the most honorable of men. I must have deep confidence to make this avowal, which costs me much. How can you, knowing your father unjustly deprived others of their property, keep it? You told me of this in the room where our love began; you are a gentleman, you think yourself noble, and I am yours. I find some excuses in your youth and thoughtlessness, but I cannot respect a man who knowingly is smirched for a sum of money. Five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand won by a legal trick equally dishonor. Love, to a woman, means boundless confidence and reverence. If you come to me poor, my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. I do not wish you to make restitution because I urge it; consult your conscience. A man who loves a woman sincerely respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself. I am your wife, not your mistress. I blame myself for preaching, but scold me if you must—only a little. Dear, between us the power is yours—you alone must perceive your faults.’”

There was silence. Octave wiped his eyes. “There’s more,” he said, but the rest he smiled away as a lover’s secret.

“You teach mathematics, and you have your uncle’s capital?” Monsieur de Bourbonne asked. Octave explained that the letter had sparked a revolution in him. Haunted by conscience—every pleasure felt like theft—he rushed to Madame Firmiani. Together they calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs, whom Octave’s father had wronged. “I condemned myself to pay three percent interest,” he said, though his means did not cover the full amount. Madame Firmiani, whose position had made her careful, offered her savings and he accepted.

“You mean that adorable woman actually saves money?” the uncle exclaimed.

“Do not laugh at her,” Octave said. “Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died three years later. Legal proof of his death and his will, leaving everything to her, were lost or stolen during the troubles there. Unsure whether she might lose her fortune, she lived prudently. I wanted to acquire property of my own to provide for her if she lost hers.” He added that he gave lessons to avoid living at anyone’s expense and that the act of restitution had brought him joys beyond millions. He found the Bourgneufs living miserably: an old father agent, daughters keeping books and house, a sick mother. To bring them relief felt like being Providence; words could not convey the happiness of making amends. He called Madame Firmiani “my dear conscience”—she inspired a delicacy of feeling he thought he had lacked—and he hoped to become rich by his own industry.

Touched, the old man said, “Ah, my boy, you have your mother’s soul.” Then, from the street, the sound of a carriage announced Madame Firmiani. She entered, slightly annoyed to find Monsieur de Bourbonne there but smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune,” she said. “The Austrian Embassy has sent me a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet kept safe for me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” She then laid her head on his breast in unrestrained happiness.

Monsieur de Bourbonne concluded, “In my day we made love; in yours, you love. Women are all that is best in humanity; they are not even guilty of their faults, for they come through us.”
