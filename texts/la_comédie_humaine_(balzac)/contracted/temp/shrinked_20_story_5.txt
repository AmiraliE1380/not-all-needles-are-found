MADAME FIRMIANI

Many tales, whether eventful or accidental, possess their own setting and can be told without loss of charm. But some human incidents need the heart to give them life; certain delicate, almost anatomical touches demand an equally delicate rendering of thought. Some portraits require the infusion of a soul; they mean nothing unless the subtlest expression of a speaking countenance is given. There are things we cannot say or do without the help of secret harmonies produced by a day, an hour, a fortunate conjunction, or an inward tendency.

Such mysterious revelations are needed to tell this simple history, which will interest the naturally grave and reflective who feed on tender emotions. If the writer, like a surgeon beside his dying friend, feels a species of reverence for his subject, should not the reader share that inexplicable feeling? Is it difficult to be in unison with the vague, nervous sadness that casts gray tints about us—a semi-illness whose gentle sufferings are often pleasing? If the reader sometimes thinks of lost dear ones, if he is alone, if the day wanes or the night has come, let him read on; otherwise lay aside this book. If he has never buried a good old relative, infirm and poor, he will not understand these pages, which to some will seem redolent of musk, to others colorless and virtuous as Florian. In short, the reader must have known the luxury of tears, felt the silent pangs of passing memory, the vision of a dear yet far-off Shade—memories that bring regret for all earth has swallowed up, with smiles for vanished joys.

Believe that the writer would not, for the wealth of England, steal from poesy a single lie to embellish this narrative. The following is a true history, on which you may safely spend the treasures of your sensibility—if you have any.

In these days the French language has as many idioms and expresses as many idiosyncrasies as there are varieties of men in the great family of France. It is curious and amusing to hear the different interpretations of the same thing by the various species that compose the genus Parisian—“Parisian” used merely to generalize our remark.

Ask a Practical man, “Do you know Madame Firmiani?” and he will inventory: “Fine house in the rue du Bac, salons handsomely furnished, good pictures, one hundred thousand francs a year, husband formerly receiver-general of the department of Montenotte.” The Practical man—rotund, usually in black—will nod to signify solidity: “Solid people, those; nothing to be said against them.” For him, everything is settled by figures, incomes, or acres.

Ask a Lounger. “Madame Firmiani? Yes, I go to her parties; she receives Wednesdays; highly creditable house.” Madame Firmiani becomes a house—an inn, in fact, where the Lounger, with his agreeable smile and acquired cleverness, whispers that Monsieur Firmiani is never seen, that Madame is a Frenchwoman who spends like a Parisian, that she keeps excellent tea and admits only the best society. He goes there, but won’t present you.

An Egotist will answer with disdain: “Her parties are as dull as the Court itself. What is the good of having a mind unless to avoid such salons, where stupid talk and feeble ballads reign?” Egotists would keep the universe locked and let nothing be done without their permission; they forgive nothing but vices and favor protégés.

A Student, bold among men and timid in the boudoir, exclaims: “Oh, Madame Firmiani, my dear fellow! she is one of those adorable women who serve Nature’s excuse for all the ugly ones she creates. She is enchanting and so kind! I wish I were in power and had millions that I might—” (here a whisper). “Shall I present you?”

A Fop will say brusquely: “A woman between thirty and thirty-five; faded complexion, handsome eyes, flat figure, contralto voice worn out, much dressed, rather rouged, charming manners; in short, the remains of a pretty woman still worth a passion.” Fops are pitiless at breakfast-time.

A Connoisseur: “Magnificent collection of pictures in her house; go and see them; nothing finer.” To him, Madame Firmiani is a gallery.

A Spiteful Woman warns: “I don’t wish you to visit her.” She means: dangerous woman! a siren! dresses well, has taste; gives other women sleepless nights.

An Attache to an embassy: “Isn’t she from Antwerp? I saw her ten years ago in Rome; she was very handsome then.” Attaches speak as if polishing diplomatic names—Spain, Vienna, Italy—producing refined, sometimes imperceptible wit.

A person eager for distinction asks: “That Madame Firmiani sees much of the faubourg Saint-Germain, doesn’t she?” She gives the “de” to everybody and strives to do “the right thing,” though she may live in the Marais and have a husband who is a lawyer.

A Duke: “Madame Firmiani? I do not know her.” He recognizes only women presented at court; pray excuse him, he is one of Napoleon’s creations.

A Ninny will blurt: “She used to sing at the Opera-house.” Ninnies have an answer for everything and will tell lies sooner than silence.

Two old ladies, wives of former magistrates, discuss her maiden name. The first asks sharply; the second, gentle, replies: “She was a Cadignan, niece of the old Prince de Cadignan, consequently cousin to the present Duc de Maufrigneuse.” Madame Firmiani is a Cadignan; that prejudice never dies.

An Original: “I’ve seen no galoshes in her antechamber; you can visit without compromising yourself, and play cards there without fear; if scoundrels come, they are people of quality and arrive in carriages; such persons never quarrel.”

The Observer counsels: “If you call on Madame Firmiani, you will find a beautiful woman at ease by her fireplace. She will scarcely rise—only for women, ambassadors, dukes, and great persons. She is gracious, converses well on many topics, and shows signs of a passionate nature; but she has so many adorers that she cannot have a favorite. Suspicion might rest on two or three intimates, but it does not. The lady is a mystery. She is married, though none of us have seen her husband. Monsieur Firmiani is altogether mythical; he is like that third post-horse for which we pay though we never behold it. Madame has the finest contralto voice in Europe, so say the judges, but she has sung in Paris only two or three times since she came. She receives much company, but goes nowhere.”

The Observer speaks as an Oracle. His anecdotes and quotations must be accepted as truths, under pain of being reckoned socially ill-bred and of provoking his slanders in twenty salons where he is indispensable. The Observer is forty, never dines at home, declares himself no longer dangerous to women, wears a maroon coat, and has boxes at the “Bouffons.” He is sometimes mistaken for the Parasite, but he has filled enough real functions not to be thought a sponger; he owns a small estate in some department whose name he never utters.

A Contradictor says, “Why, she was Murat’s former mistress.” Contradictors correct memoirs, rectify dates, bet a hundred to one, and are certain about everything. They are often “chevaliers” of the Legion of honor; they talk loudly, have retreating foreheads, and play high.

An Envious man scoffs at the story of a hundred thousand francs: “Nonsense! Some people dower their heroines with millions. Madame Firmiani is simply a coquette who ruined a young man and now prevents his fine marriage. If she were not so handsome she wouldn’t have a penny.” Envy needs no description; its perennial vigor baffles one.

Thus, the same woman is a ledger, an inn, a siren, a gallery, a devotion, a scandal, a mystery, a name, or a falsehood—according to the species that speaks. Each type interprets Madame Firmiani through its own prejudice and vocabulary, making her a mirror in which Paris reads its own faces.

Persons in society, literary men, honest folk—in short, individuals of all species—were promulgating in January 1824 so many different opinions about Madame Firmiani that it would be tedious to set them all down. A man wishing to understand her, yet unwilling or unable to go to her house, would have found in hearsay as many versions of her as there are sects in Catholicism: widow or wife, silly or wise, virtuous or the reverse, rich or poor, soulless or full of feeling, handsome or plain. Frightful reflection! We are like lithographic blocks from which critics draw various proofs—the likenesses differing by such slight shadings that reputation depends, aside from friends’ calumnies and newspapers’ witticisms, on a balance struck between Truth that limps and Falsehood to which Parisian wit gives wings.

Madame Firmiani, like other noble women who keep their hearts a sanctuary and disdain the world, was liable to misjudgment by Monsieur de Bourbonne, an old country magnate who had reason to think of her that winter. He belonged to the provincial planters—men living on their estates, keeping close accounts, bargaining with the peasantry. Thus employed, a man becomes sagacious by routine. The old gentleman had come to Paris from Touraine to satisfy his curiosity about Madame Firmiani. He was a man of honor and breeding. His sole heir was a nephew he greatly loved, in whose interests he planted his poplars; watching trees flourish for an heir made affection grow with every blow of the spade. Though uncommon, this feeling exists in Touraine.

This cherished nephew, Octave de Camps, descended from the famous Abbé de Camps—known to bibliophiles and learned men. Provincials have the bad habit of reprobating any young man who sells inherited estates; this antiquated prejudice has clashed with the stock-jobbing encouraged by the present government. Without consulting his uncle, Octave had lately sold an estate to the Bande Noire, a mysterious association of speculators that bought and parceled landed estates. The château de Villaines would have been pulled down but for the remonstrances of the old uncle to the “Pickaxe company.” To increase the old man’s wrath, a distant relative—one of those poor but astute cousins about whom provincials say, “No lawsuits for me with him!”—had come to visit Monsieur de Bourbonne and casually informed him of his nephew’s ruin.

“Monsieur Octave de Camps,” he said as the old man sat digesting a provincial dinner, “having wasted his means on a certain Madame Firmiani, is now reduced to teaching mathematics for a living while awaiting your death, not daring to let you know of his dissipations.”

But heirs cannot always be shorn of uncles. Thanks to his obstinacy, Monsieur de Bourbonne refused to believe the story; his digestion survived the shock. Like a true Saint Thomas, he came to Paris, unknown to Octave, resolved to make full inquiries into his nephew’s insolvency. He had many acquaintances in the faubourg Saint-Germain—among the Listomères, the Lenoncourts, and the Vandenesses—and heard so much gossip, so many facts and falsities about Madame Firmiani, that he resolved to be presented to her under the name of de Rouxellay, that of his estate. The astute old gentleman chose an evening when he knew Octave would be engaged finishing a lucrative piece of work—for this supposed lover still frequented her house; as to Octave’s ruin, that, he discovered, was no fable.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was not at all like the provincial uncle at the Gymnase. Formerly in the King’s guard, a man of the world and a favorite with women, he knew how to present himself with the courteous manners of the olden time; he could make graceful speeches and understand the Charter, or most of it. Though he loved the Bourbons with frankness, believed in God as a gentleman should, and read nothing but the Quotidienne, he was not as ridiculous as the liberals of his department pretended. He could hold his own in courtly circles, provided no one spoke of “Moses in Egypt,” nor of the drama, romanticism, nor of railways. He had never got beyond Voltaire, Buffon, Payronnet, and the Chevalier Gluck.

“Madame,” he said to the Marquise de Listomère, who was on his arm as they entered Madame Firmiani’s salon, “if this woman is my nephew’s mistress, I pity him. How can she live in this luxury, and know he is in a garret? Hasn’t she any soul? Octave is a fool to have given up such an estate as Villaines for a—”

Monsieur de Bourbonne belonged to the species Fossil, and used the language of yore.

“But suppose he had lost it at play?” the Marquise asked.

“Then, madame, he would at least have had the pleasure of gambling.”

“And do you think he has had no pleasure here? See! look at Madame Firmiani.”

The old man’s sternest memories faded at the sight of his nephew’s supposed mistress. His anger died away at a gracious exclamation as he looked at her. By one of those fortunate accidents that favor pretty women, it was a moment when all her beauties shone with peculiar lustre: perhaps owing to the wax-lights, the charming simplicity of her dress, and the ineffable atmosphere of elegance about her. One must study salon evenings to appreciate the imperceptible lights and shades that color a woman’s face. There comes a moment when, content with her toilet, pleased with her wit, delighted to be admired, and feeling herself queen of a salon full of remarkable men who smile to her, the Parisian woman reaches a full consciousness of her grace; her beauty is enhanced by the looks gathered in, a mute homage she transfers with subtle glances to the man she loves. At such a moment she becomes a magician, involuntarily inspiring the love that fills her own bosom; her smiles and glances fascinate. If this inward condition can give attraction even to a plain woman, with what radiance must it invest one of natural elegance, distinguished bearing, fair, fresh, with sparkling eyes and a tasteful dress that wrings approval from artists and rivals.

Have you ever, for your happiness, met a woman whose harmonious voice gives to her speech the same charm that emanates from her manners? A woman who knows how to speak and to be silent, whose words are happily chosen, whose language is pure, and who concerns herself in your interests with delicacy? Her raillery caresses, her criticism never wounds; she neither discourses nor argues, but enjoys leading a discussion and stopping it at the right moment. Her manner is affable and smiling; her politeness never forced, her readiness to serve never servile; she reduces the respect she claims to a soft shadow; she never wearies you, and you leave her satisfied with her and with yourself. Her charming grace extends to everything about her. In her presence one breathes, as it were, native air. This woman is natural. There is no effort about her; she aims at no effect; her feelings are shown simply because they are true. Frank herself, she does not wound the vanity of others; she accepts men as God made them; pitying the vicious, forgiving defects and absurdities, comprehending all ages, and vexed by nothing, because she has had the sense and tact to foresee all. Tender and gay, she gratifies before she consoles. You love her so well that if this angel did wrong you would be ready to excuse her. If, for your happiness, you have met such a woman, you know Madame Firmiani.

After Monsieur de Bourbonne had talked with her for ten minutes, sitting beside her, his nephew was forgiven. He perceived that whatever the actual truth might be, the relation between Madame Firmiani and Octave covered some mystery. Returning to the illusions that gild youth, and judging her by her beauty, the old gentleman became convinced that a woman so innately conscious of her dignity could not be guilty of a bad action. Her dark eyes told of inward peace; the lines of her face were noble, the profile pure, and the passion he had come to investigate seemed little to oppress her heart. Noting all the promises of love and virtue given by that adorable countenance, he told himself, while noting all the promises of love and virtue given by that adorable countenance, “My nephew is committing some folly.”

Madame Firmiani acknowledged to twenty-five. Practical persons pointed out that, having married the invisible Firmiani (then a respectable man in his forties) in 1813 at the age of sixteen, she must be at least twenty-eight in 1825. Still, these same persons also asserted that she had never been so desirable or so completely a woman as at present. She was at an age when women are most prone to conceive passion in their pensive hours. She possessed all that earth sells, lends, and gives. The Attaches declared there was nothing of which she was ignorant; the Contradictors said there was much she ought to learn; the Observers remarked that her hands were white, her feet small, her movements a trifle too undulating. Nevertheless, individuals of all species envied or disputed Octave’s happiness, agreeing, for once, that Madame Firmiani was the most aristocratically beautiful woman in Paris.

Young, rich, a perfect musician, intelligent, witty, refined, and received—like a Cadignan—by the Princesse de Blamont-Chauvry, that oracle of the noble faubourg; loved by rivals such as the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, her cousin the Marquise d’Espard, and Madame de Macumer, Madame Firmiani gratified all vanities that feed or excite love. She was therefore sought by too many men not to fall a victim to Parisian malice and its charming calumnies, whispered behind a fan or in a safe aside. It was necessary to quote those remarks given at this history’s outset to bring out the true Firmiani in contradistinction to the Firmiani of society. If some women forgave her happiness, others did not forgive her propriety. Now nothing is so dangerous in Paris as unfounded suspicions—for it is impossible to destroy them.

This sketch of a woman admirably natural gives only a faint idea of her. It would need the pencil of an Ingres to render the pride of that brow with its wealth of hair, the dignity of that glance, and the thoughts betrayed by the changing colors of her cheeks. In her were all things: poets could have found an Agnes Sorel and a Joan of Arc, also the woman unknown, the Soul within that form, the soul of Eve, the knowledge of the treasures of good and the riches of evil, error and resignation, crime and devotion, the Donna Julia and the Haïdee of Lord Byron.

The former guardsman remained boldly after the other guests had gone; Madame Firmiani found him quietly seated in an armchair, stubborn as a fly. The clock marked two in the morning.

“Madame,” said the old gentleman as she rose, hoping to hint she wished him to go, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani immediately sat again, visibly moved. Monsieur de Bourbonne could not tell whether her pallor came from shame or pleasure; delicate women conceal pleasing emotions. He credited her less generously—provincials are suspicious.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked with a glance that questioned more than it revealed.

“Well, madame,” he replied, “someone in my province told me that my nephew has ruined himself for you and lives in a garret while you wear silk and gold. Forgive my rustic frankness; perhaps useful to you, these calumnies.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she said, imperiously; “I know all that. You are too polite to press this if I ask you not to, and too gallant—in the old sense—not to agree you have no right to question me. It would be ridiculous to defend myself. I trust you will have a good opinion of my character and believe the profound contempt I feel for money, though I married a man of immense wealth without fortune. It is nothing to me whether your nephew be rich or poor; I received him because I esteem him. My friends respect each other; I have not the philosophy to admit into my house those I do not esteem; my guardian angel keeps me from tattle and dishonesty.”

Her voice rose with the first part; the last words had the ease and self-possession of Celimene bantering the Misanthrope.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, moved, “I am an old man; I am almost Octave’s father, and I beg your pardon for the question I shall venture to ask, giving you my word of honor that your answer will die here”—laying his hand over his heart—“Are these rumors true; do you love Octave?”

“To any other man I should have answered by a look,” she replied; “but to you, since you are almost his father, I answer by asking what you would think of a woman if she answered plainly? To confess our love, when he loves us—ah! that may be. But even when we are assured of being loved forever, it is an effort for us, and a reward to him. To say to another—” She did not finish, rose, bowed, and withdrew into her private rooms; the doors closing told him all he needed to hear.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought; “what a woman! she is either sly or an angel,” and he left in his hired coach while the coachman cursed the late hour.

The next morning, about eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed the stairs to Octave’s lodging in the rue de l’Observance. The young professor, who had been up all night, was astonished to see his uncle.

“You rascal!” said the old man, sitting. “Since when is it fashion to laugh at uncles who have twenty-six thousand francs a year from solid acres? In my day we stood in awe of such uncles. Haven’t I been the most accommodating uncle? You write, or you don’t—no matter. I’m making you the prettiest estate in Touraine. But what do you do?—sell your property and live like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I have a right to your confidence. Have you been gambling, lost money on the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you tell me any lies greater than those I told at your age I’ll sell my property and go back to the evil ways of my youth—if I can.”

“I saw Madame Firmiani yesterday,” he added, kissing his fingers. “She is charming. You have my consent, if that will do you any good. Have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! the jade! I’d have wagered it. In my time the women of the court ruined men better than the courtesans of to-day; but this one—she is a bit of the last century.”

“You are mistaken,” Octave said tenderly. “Madame Firmiani deserves esteem and all adoration.”

“Youth, youth! Well, speak. But mind—my experience in gallantry is long.”

“My dear uncle, here is a letter which will tell you nearly all,” said Octave, taking it from an elegant portfolio.

“I haven’t my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began to read:

“My beloved—You ask why I am sad. I cannot deceive you; this may be a misfortune, for a wife should be gay and caressing. How much gratitude there is in my love. I long to love you forever; a woman’s glory is in the man she loves. But a tale you told me has tarnished my joy. Since then I have felt humiliated in you—you whom I thought the most honorable of men. How can you, knowing your father unjustly deprived others of their property, keep it?

“You told me of this criminal act in the room that witnessed our love; and you are a gentleman, and you think yourself noble. I try to excuse you—youth and thoughtlessness—but I cannot respect a man who knowingly is smirched for money. Five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand gained by a legal trick are equally dishonoring. Love, to a woman, means boundless confidence joined to reverence. I have thought of love as a fire that purifies noble feelings.

“I have but one thing to say: come to me poor and my love will be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Should I see you no more, I shall know what it means.

“I do not wish to force restitution because I urge it. Consult your conscience. An act of justice ought not to be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife, not your mistress; it is less a question of pleasing me than of inspiring true respect. If I am mistaken about your father, if you still think your claim equitable, consider and act from your conscience. A man who loves a woman as you love me respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself.

“I blame myself for this sermon; a word might have sufficed. Scold me; I wish to be scolded—but not much. Between us the power is yours—you alone should perceive your faults.”

Octave paused, tears in his eyes. “There’s more,” his uncle said. “Finish it.”

“The rest is to be read by a lover,” Octave smiled.

“I too have loved,” the uncle conceded softly. “But I don’t understand why you give lessons in mathematics.”

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that like saying I had dipped into my inheritance? After I read this letter a revolution took place in me. I felt ashamed of every comfort; when I drove I thought the horse was not mine; when I ate I felt I had stolen my food. The fresher my honesty, the more intense. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. That day we made out the account of what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself, against her advice, to pay three per cent interest. But all I had did not cover the amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer—and for me to accept—her savings.”

“What! besides other virtues does that adorable woman save money?” cried the uncle.

“Don’t laugh at her; her position obliged prudence. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died there three years later. It has been impossible to get legal proof of his death or the will; papers were lost or stolen in the troubles. Uncertain, she lived with the utmost economy. For my part, I wished to acquire property of my own to provide for my wife if she lost hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me? I love you enough to pay your debts,” said the uncle. “I’ll play the traditional uncle now, and revenge myself!”

“Ah! uncle, I know your vengeance! But let me grow rich by my own industry. If you want to do me a real service, make me an allowance till I can undertake a scheme for which I need capital. At this moment I am so happy that all I desire is the means of living. I give lessons so I may not be a burden. If you had seen the happiness I felt making restitution! I found the Bourgneufs living miserably: the father a lottery agent, the daughters keeping the books and caring for the house, the mother always ill. I entered their home the accomplice in a crime; I left an honest man who had purged his father’s memory. Lawyers can make injustice legal; laws have apologies for conscience. My visit was a drama. To be Providence—actually to supply what they had only mocked at—no words suffice. If there is a Paradise, my father is happy there now. As for me, I am loved as no man ever was. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she has inspired a delicacy of feeling I lacked. I call her my dear conscience. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich by my industry. I have an industrial scheme that may bring millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, moved.

At that moment they heard a carriage outside. “There she is!” cried Octave. Madame Firmiani entered.

“Ah!” she exclaimed, slightly annoyed at seeing Monsieur de Bourbonne. Smiling, she added, “Our uncle is not in the way; I came to beg my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has sent a document proving the death of Monsieur Firmiani and the will, which his valet kept safely to give me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here”—she laid her hand on his heart—“to which none but God can add.” Overcome, she laid her head on his breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. Women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults—they come through us.”
