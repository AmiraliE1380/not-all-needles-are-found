In January 1824, opinions about Madame Firmiani varied widely, making it difficult for anyone to form a clear understanding of her character. She could be perceived as a widow or a wife, wise or foolish, virtuous or otherwise, rich or poor, and beautiful or plain. This multitude of interpretations reflects how society can distort an individual's reputation, much like lithographic blocks that produce different images based on subtle variations.

Monsieur de Bourbonne, an old country magnate, became intrigued by Madame Firmiani during that winter. As a provincial planter, he was accustomed to managing his estate and dealing with the peasantry, which made him astute. He traveled to Paris from Touraine to learn more about her, but the gossip he encountered only deepened his confusion. He was a man of honor, with a beloved nephew, Octave de Camps, for whom he had high hopes. Octave, however, had recently sold an estate without consulting his uncle, which angered Monsieur de Bourbonne. A distant relative informed him that Octave had squandered his fortune on Madame Firmiani and was now teaching mathematics to survive, a revelation that troubled the old man.

Despite the cousin's claims, Monsieur de Bourbonne refused to believe the worst about his nephew. He traveled to Paris, determined to investigate Octave's situation. With connections in the faubourg Saint-Germain, he learned much about Madame Firmiani and decided to meet her under the name de Rouxellay, hoping to understand the truth behind her relationship with Octave. He chose a time when he knew Octave would be busy, and soon discovered that the rumors about his nephew's financial troubles were indeed true.

Monsieur de Rouxellay was not the typical provincial uncle; he was a former member of the King’s guard, well-versed in society's manners and politics. As he entered Madame Firmiani’s salon with the Marquise de Listomere, he expressed his pity for Octave, questioning how a woman of such luxury could be involved with someone in dire straits. ############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The former guardsman lingered after the other guests had departed, and Madame Firmiani found him seated before her, seemingly determined to stay. The clock struck two in the morning.

“Madame,” he began as she rose, signaling her desire for him to leave, “I am the uncle of Monsieur Octave de Camps.”

Madame Firmiani sat back down, visibly affected. The old Planter, despite his insight, could not discern whether her pallor stemmed from shame or pleasure. Women often conceal their joys, and the more delicate they are, the more they hide their emotions. Monsieur de Bourbonne misinterpreted her agitation, as provincials often do.

“Well, monsieur?” she asked, giving him a piercing look that left men perplexed.

“Well, madame,” he replied, “someone informed me in my province that my nephew has ruined himself for you, living in a garret while you enjoy wealth. Forgive my rustic honesty; you should know of these rumors.”

“Stop, monsieur,” she interjected, raising her hand. “I am aware of all that. You are too polite to continue if I ask you to leave this topic, and too gallant not to agree that you have no right to question me. It would be absurd for me to defend myself. I trust you will believe in my profound contempt for money, despite marrying a wealthy man without a fortune. Your nephew’s wealth or poverty is irrelevant; I welcome him because I consider him a friend. All my friends respect one another; I cannot admit those I do not esteem. My guardian angel has kept me from gossip and dishonesty.”

Her voice rose slightly at first, but she concluded with the composure of Celimene teasing the Misanthrope.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Bourbonne said, his voice trembling, “I am an old man, almost a father to Octave, and I humbly ask your pardon for the question I am about to pose. I swear as a gentleman that your answer shall remain here.” He placed his hand on his heart, an old-fashioned gesture. “Do you love Octave?”

“To any other man, I would answer with a look,” she replied. “But to you, I ask what you would think of a woman who answered such a question. To confess our love is an effort, even when we are certain of being loved. It is a reward for him. To say it to another—”

She did not finish, rising and bowing before retreating to her private quarters, the doors closing behind her.

“Ah! the mischief!” he thought. “What a woman! She is either sly or an angel.” He left in his hired coach, the horses stamping impatiently.

The next morning, around eight, Monsieur de Bourbonne climbed the stairs to Octave de Camps’ residence. The young professor was astonished to see his uncle, having stayed up all night.

“You rascal!” said Monsieur de Bourbonne, taking a seat. “Since when is it fashionable to mock uncles with twenty-six thousand francs a year? What fault do you find with me? Have I not been a good uncle? Did I ever demand respect or refuse you money? You write or don’t write; it matters not. I’m making you a beautiful estate in Touraine, though I won’t give it to you until the last moment. And what do you do? Sell your property and live like a lackey!”

“Uncle—”

“I’m not talking about uncles; I’m talking about nephews. Come, confess; it’s easier. Have you been gambling? Lost money at the Bourse? Say, ‘Uncle, I’m a wretch,’ and I’ll hug you. But if you lie, I’ll sell my property and return to my youthful ways.”

“Uncle—”

“I saw your Madame Firmiani yesterday,” he continued, kissing his fingers. “She is charming. You have my approval if that helps. As for the Church’s sanction, I suppose that’s useless. Come, have you ruined yourself for her?”

“Yes, uncle.”

“Ha! I’d have wagered it. In my day, women were cleverer at ruining men than today’s courtesans, but this one—I recognized her! A piece of the last century.”

“Uncle,” Octave said, tenderly, “you are mistaken. Madame Firmiani deserves your esteem and all the adoration the world gives her.”

“Youth, youth! Always the same!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Go on; tell me the same old story. But remember, my experience in gallantry is not from yesterday.”

“My dear uncle, here is a letter that will explain nearly everything,” Octave said, retrieving it from an elegant portfolio, her gift. “When you’ve read it, I’ll tell you the rest, and you’ll know a Madame Firmiani unknown to the world.”

“I don’t have my spectacles; read it aloud.”

Octave began: “‘My beloved—’”

“Hey, then you are still intimate with her?” his uncle interrupted.

“Of course.”

“You haven’t parted from her?”

“Parted!” Octave repeated. “We are married.”

“Heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne. “Then why do you live in a garret?”

“Let me continue.”

“True—I’m listening.”

Octave resumed, but struggled to read some passages due to his emotion: “‘My beloved Husband, you ask why I am sad. Has it passed from my soul to my face, or have you guessed it? I cannot deceive you; this may be a misfortune, for a wife should be gay and caressing. I cannot deceive you, even if my happiness depended on it. Ah! how much gratitude there is in my love. I long to love you forever, without limit; I desire to be proud of you. A woman’s glory is in the man she loves. Esteem, consideration, honor—must they not belong to him who receives our all? Well, my angel has fallen. Yes, dear, the tale you told me has tarnished my past joys. Since then, I have felt humiliated in you—you whom I thought the most honorable of men. I must have deep confidence in your heart to make this avowal, which costs me much. How is it that you, knowing your father unjustly deprived others of their property, can keep it? You told me of this in a room filled with the mute witnesses of our love; you are a gentleman, and you think yourself noble, and I am yours! I try to find excuses for you; I do find them in your youth and thoughtlessness. Perhaps you have never thought seriously about fortune and integrity. Oh! how your laugh wounded me. Reflect on that ruined family, always in distress; poor young girls who have reason to curse you daily; an old father saying to himself each night: ‘We might not be starving if that man’s father had been honest.’”

“Good heavens!” cried Monsieur de Bourbonne, interrupting, “surely you didn’t tell that woman about your father’s affair with the Bourgneufs? Women know more about wasting a fortune than making one.”

“They know about integrity. But let me read on: ‘Octave, no power on earth can change the principles of honor. Look into your conscience and ask it by what name you call the action by which you hold your property.’”

The nephew looked at his uncle, who lowered his head.

“‘I will not tell you all the thoughts that assail me; they can be reduced to one: I cannot respect the man who knowingly is smirched for a sum of money, whatever the amount. Five francs stolen at play or five times a hundred thousand gained by a legal trick are equally dishonoring. I feel degraded by the very love which has hitherto been my joy. There rises in my soul a voice my tenderness cannot stifle. I have wept to feel that I have more conscience than love. Were you to commit a crime, I would hide you from justice, but my devotion could go no farther. Love, to a woman, means boundless confidence, united to a need of reverencing the being to whom she belongs. I have never conceived of love otherwise than as a fire in which all noble feelings are purified. I have one thing to say: come to me poor, and my love shall be redoubled. If not, renounce it. Should I see you no more, I shall know what it means. But I do not wish you to make restitution because I urge it. Consult your conscience. An act of justice ought not to be a sacrifice made to love. I am your wife, not your mistress, and it is less a question of pleasing me than of inspiring in my soul true respect. If I am mistaken, if you have ill-explained your father’s action, if you still think your right to the property equitable, consider and decide by listening to your conscience; act solely from yourself. A man who loves a woman sincerely respects the sanctity of her trust too deeply to dishonor himself. I blame myself for what I have written; a word might have sufficed, and I have preached to you! Scold me; I wish to be scolded—but not much, only a little. Dear, between us, the power is yours—you alone should perceive your own faults.’”

“Well, uncle?” Octave asked, tears in his eyes.

“There’s more in the letter; finish it.”

“Oh, the rest is for a lover’s eyes only,” Octave replied, smiling.

“Yes, right, my boy,” the old man said gently. “I have had many affairs, but I beg you to believe that I too have loved. But I don’t understand why you teach mathematics.”

“My dear uncle, I am your nephew; isn’t that as good as saying I dipped into my father’s capital? After reading this letter, a revolution occurred within me. I paid my entire debt of remorse in one day. I cannot describe my state. As I drove in the Bois, a voice called to me, ‘That horse is not yours’; when I ate, it said, ‘You have stolen this food.’ I was ashamed. The fresher my honesty, the more intense it was. I rushed to Madame Firmiani. Uncle! that day I experienced pleasures of the heart far beyond millions. Together, we calculated what was due to the Bourgneufs, and I condemned myself to pay three percent interest. But all I had did not suffice to cover the full amount. We were lovers enough for her to offer her savings, and I accepted.”

“What! Besides her other virtues, does that adorable woman save money?” his uncle exclaimed.

“Don’t laugh at her, uncle; her position has forced her to be careful. Her husband went to Greece in 1820 and died three years later. It has been impossible to obtain legal proof of his death or the will he made leaving everything to her. Those papers were lost or stolen during the troubles in Greece, where records are not kept as in France. Uncertain whether she might lose her fortune, she has lived prudently. I wish to acquire property that is mine to provide for my wife if she loses hers.”

“But why didn’t you tell me all this? My dear nephew, you might have known I love you enough to pay your good debts. I’ll play the traditional uncle now and take revenge!”

“Ah! uncle, I want to get rich by my own industry. If you want to help me, make me an allowance of two or three thousand francs a year until I find a venture needing capital. Right now, I am so happy that all I desire is just the means to live. I give lessons so I do not live at anyone’s expense. If you only knew the happiness I had in making that restitution! I found the Bourgneufs living miserably and in need. The old father was a lottery agent; the daughters kept his books and cared for the house; the mother was always ill. The daughters are charming but have learned that beauty without money is undervalued. My visit was a drama. To be Providence itself; to fulfill that futile wish, ‘If heaven were to send us twenty thousand francs a year’—to bring wealth to a family sitting by a single lamp over a poor fire! Words cannot describe it. My extreme justice seemed unjust to them. If there is a Paradise, my father is happy there now. As for me, I am loved as no man has ever been. Madame Firmiani gives me more than happiness; she inspires a delicacy of feeling I think I lacked. I call her my dear conscience, a love-word expressing secret harmonies within our hearts. I find honesty profitable; I shall get rich in time by myself. I have an industrial scheme in mind, and if it succeeds, I shall earn millions.”

“Ah! my boy, you have your mother’s soul,” said the old man, his eyes filling with memories of his sister.

Just then, despite the distance from Octave’s garret to the street, he heard a carriage.

“There she is!” he exclaimed. “I know her horses by the way they pull up.”

Moments later, Madame Firmiani entered.

“Ah!” she said, annoyed to see Monsieur de Bourbonne. “But our uncle is not in the way,” she added quickly, smiling. “I came to humbly entreat my husband to accept my fortune. The Austrian Embassy has sent me a document proving Monsieur Firmiani’s death and the will, which his valet kept safe for me. Octave, you can accept it all; you are richer than I, for you have treasures here” (laying her hand on his heart) “to which none but God can add.” Unable to contain her happiness, she laid her head on her husband’s breast.

“My dear niece,” said the old man, “in my day we made love; in yours, you love. You women are all that is best in humanity; you are not even guilty of your faults, for they come through us.”
