PIERRETTE

By Honore De Balzac

Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

DEDICATION

To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

Dear Child,--You, the joy of the household, whose pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of Wierzschovnia, how can I dedicate to _you_ a story full of melancholy? Yet should not sorrows be spoken of to a young girl like you, since the day may come when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is difficult to find a subject worthy of your eyes, so I have no choice; perhaps you will feel fortunate when you read this story sent to you by your old friend, De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

On an October morning in 1827, a sixteen-year-old boy in modest, proletarian clothes stood in a small square of Lower Provins. He observed the houses, noting mills along the river already at work, their sounds echoing in the crisp air. The square, bordered by linden trees, featured simple bourgeois architecture, few signs of commerce, and only a couple of porte-cochères, one belonging to Monsieur Martener, a physician.

The young man recognized the house he sought: a newly restored white stone structure with gray blinds and yellow-painted iron balconies. The front door was reached by three stone steps; its fresh appearance contrasted with the timeworn exteriors of its neighbors, reflecting the self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

As he admired it, a hint of sadness crossed his face. The rising sun illuminated a calico curtain at one garret window, and he sang a Breton ditty traditionally sung to newlyweds, evoking memories of Brittany’s customs and life’s bittersweet nature.

A girl appeared at the window and signaled him. He revealed a yellow broom-flower, and they exchanged words. She was Pierrette; he was Brigaut, her childhood companion. He expressed his desire to settle near her, but she urged him to flee as a woman below opened a window, revealing an old maid roused by his song.

The old maid, a grotesque and unflattering figure, searched for the music’s source but saw nothing. Known for keen observations that often spawned grave suppositions and domestic dramas, she heightened the moment’s tension.

Pierrette, overwhelmed by Brigaut’s visit, reflected on her past, recalling childhood memories and the joy they brought. She sat in a shabby armchair, lost in thought, her delicate appearance marred by illness. Her mother, a Demoiselle Auffray, had a complicated family history: her father married twice and left a tangled inheritance.

Pierrette’s mother married a Breton officer, Major Lorrain, who died in battle, leaving her with little means. The Lorrains, Pierrette’s grandparents, struggled financially, and after a series of misfortunes they could not care for her. Pierrette was eventually sent to live with her uncle and aunt, the Rogrons, in Provins, who became the unexpected masters of her fate.

Unaware of the full complexities of Pierrette’s past, the Rogrons would soon play a significant role in her life, setting the stage for the unfolding drama of her existence.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Pere Rogron, an innkeeper in Provins, was a short, fat man with a ruddy face and a taste for indulgence. He and his equally unattractive wife were selfish and brutish, eking out a modest living from their land and the sale of their inn. After selling it they moved into a dilapidated house inherited from old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandmother. Their two children, Sylvie and Jerome-Denis, were as neglected and ill-bred as their parents, their features coarsened and their voices harsh. Rogron sent them to Paris to work in shops, subscribing to a doctrine of tough love and minimal support.

Sylvie became an apprentice on rue Saint-Denis, eventually gaining independence and rising to forewoman. Jerome followed a similar path and became a head-clerk. They met only occasionally, content with modest outings, and pooled their savings to buy a retail shop called the “Family Sister.” Though they saw some success, they faced fierce competition and became notorious for their harshness and lack of empathy in business.

Sylvie, not conventionally attractive, was shrewd and ran the business effectively; Jerome lacked the intellect to grasp broader trade concerns. Their lives revolved around the shop, leaving them oblivious to the world beyond it. Their stinginess and poor treatment of employees produced a high turnover of clerks.

Sylvie cultivated a dual persona: a pleasing saleswoman to customers and a sour spinster in private. While mourning their father and weighing their future, the siblings received a letter from the Lorrains about their cousin Pierrette. Inheriting property and significant assets, they contemplated selling the business and returning to Provins for a comfortable life.

They viewed Pierrette as a possible burden and debated the implications of taking her in, ultimately deciding against it to preserve their wealth for themselves and future generations. Sylvie intended to write a refusal but was sidetracked by a business opportunity. The siblings left for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival—an absence and a sequence of choices that would prove as consequential for Pierrette as their earlier life in Paris.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
When a petty shopkeeper returns to his provincial town from Paris, he brings back a few ideas that soon fade as he immerses himself in provincial life. The transition from shopkeeper to bourgeois feels like an illness: the vibrancy of Parisian life gives way to provincial stillness. With newfound wealth, these individuals indulge long-held desires—travel, politics, and hobbies like hunting or fishing. The Rogrons, a brother and sister duo, exemplify this as they remodel their old house into a charming new one, driven to emulate the wealthier bourgeois of Provins.

Their renovations express these aspirations: the contractor consults them on every detail to align choices with local elite tastes. The Rogrons’ first year back is consumed by talk of their home and attempts to integrate into the town’s social fabric. They seek acceptance among established families—the Julliards, Guepins, and Guenées—who dominate local society.

Madame Tiphaine, the town belle, is ambitious for her husband and maneuvers to elevate him to deputy. She navigates social dynamics skillfully, hosting gatherings that cement her influence. The Rogrons, however, struggle. Sylvie tries to learn card games to engage socially; Rogron remains awkward and silent. Their efforts to impress expose their lack of refinement and understanding of social norms.

Despite hosting lavish dinners to display their wealth, the Rogrons grow increasingly isolated. Invitations are declined; they become objects of ridicule. Sylvie’s belief that wealth guarantees acceptance crumbles. She turns to frugality, dismissing extravagant dinners and opting for a simpler life. The siblings slide into ennui, reminiscing about their business and feeling the weight of isolation. Attempts to engage the community yield little satisfaction, breeding resentment toward the social elite.

Seeking to regain standing, Sylvie invites their cousin Pierrette Lorrain to live with them, hoping to use her as a bridge to the upper class. The plan backfires when townspeople learn of Pierrette’s neglect in their household, bringing further ostracism. Bitter and sidelined, the Rogrons begin aligning with the lower bourgeoisie, aiming to establish a new social order.

Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet, both marginalized in Provins, see opportunity in the Rogrons’ discontent. They plan to leverage the siblings’ wealth and social ignorance to further their own political ambitions. As the Rogrons sink into resentment, they unwittingly position themselves as a potential center for the town’s liberal opposition.

Pierrette’s arrival becomes a catalyst for the Rogrons’ ambitions and frustrations: they hope to use her to regain favor, but the town’s power dynamics are complex. Rivalries and carefully maintained hierarchies show that wealth alone won’t secure acceptance or influence. The Rogrons must navigate a treacherous landscape of social rivalries and ambitions, their journey illustrating the challenges of mobility and the harsh realities of provincial life, where status and subtle codes often outweigh money.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette, burdened by living on charity, felt relieved to learn of wealthy relatives. Brigaut, her childhood friend and the son of her mother’s friend, offered sixty francs for her trip to Paris, which she accepted without thanks, reflecting their close bond. Brigaut often visited to console her during hard times.

In October 1824, after Pierrette turned eleven, she was entrusted to the diligence conductor for the journey to Paris. Brigaut ran after the coach, tears in his eyes, until he could no longer keep up. On arrival Pierrette had no money; the conductor paid for her hotel and arranged a transfer to Provins, treating her like a package. Four days later a kinder conductor brought her to Mademoiselle Rogron’s home, where her cousins received her with astonishment.

She arrived with only a small bundle of clothes, looking charming in her Breton attire. Sylvie and Rogron were initially taken aback and questioned her, but Pierrette, shy and overwhelmed, said little. Sylvie and the maid, Adèle, showed her a modest room that seemed luxurious to Pierrette. Sylvie immediately worried about her lack of clothing and resolved to buy her a new wardrobe.

In Brittany Pierrette had known affection and freedom; with the Rogrons she found the opposite. The warmth she had known was replaced by coldness, leaving her lost and confused. The next morning she explored the house and garden, only to be reprimanded by Rogron for dirty shoes.

As she settled in, Pierrette faced constant criticism from cousins more concerned with possessions than her well-being. Sylvie and Rogron’s harshness contrasted sharply with the love she had received in Brittany. Pierrette’s innocence made her acutely aware of their disdain, and she began to suppress her natural joy.

The Rogrons fixated on Pierrette’s clothing, seeing her as a burden rather than kin. Their fault-finding and harsh reprimands stifled her spirit. Once lively and carefree, she learned to be passive around them, fearing their scorn. When she accidentally tore a dress while playing, she was devastated, anticipating stern rebukes.

Over time life in Provins grew harder. Sylvie forbade visits to friends, extinguishing whatever small pleasures remained. The Rogrons’ obsession with appearances and conditional affection confined Pierrette to a cycle of fear and repression; they treated her as a means to fill their lives with purpose, their care rooted in self-interest rather than genuine love.

The arrival of Vinet and the colonel altered household dynamics further. They encouraged Sylvie to oppose the local social elite, drawing her into ambition and social positioning that deepened Pierrette’s isolation. As her cousins became consumed by these schemes, Pierrette was increasingly restricted to the house and subjected to constant scrutiny. The once vibrant child was transformed into a shadow of her former self, struggling to endure the harsh realities of her new life.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One evening, marking a new phase in Pierrette’s life with her wealthy cousins, she went to bid goodnight. Sylvie turned her cheek away coldly, making Pierrette cry. When asked, she said her rich cousin was not as kind as her poor grandmother. Sylvie dismissed her feelings, observing that the grandmother had taken money while her cousin would leave her some; Pierrette, feeling unloved, wished for affection over wealth.

Sylvie accused Pierrette of mischief, claiming she had broken a watch and ignored instructions. Ashamed, Pierrette left the room. Rogron wanted to punish her, Madame Vinet suggested sending her to school, and the colonel thought teaching might keep her quiet. Vinet proposed hiring a schoolmaster, calling Pierrette a rough diamond needing polishing.

A writing master was appointed, and his lessons brought chaos to the Rogron household: ink stains, scattered materials, and blame placed on Pierrette. Harsh words followed—she must earn her keep. Rogron’s strictness and Sylvie’s indifference intensified Pierrette’s isolation. Her curiosity and spirit were stifled; she began to feel ill, often complaining of pains her cousins dismissed.

Only Adèle, the servant, showed kindness, secretly warming Pierrette’s bed. Sylvie believed in hardening children; Pierrette’s innocent affection was seen as manipulation. The colonel and Vinet backed the Rogrons’ harsh treatment, attributing Pierrette’s behavior to a stubborn Breton nature. Vinet and Rogron invested in a local newspaper, raising their social standing while Pierrette’s situation worsened.

She thought of escaping to her grandparents in Brittany, but two events prevented it: a death and Rogron’s appointment as her guardian. Vinet hinted at Pierrette’s possible inheritance, prompting Rogron to secure from her grandmother a promise of eight thousand francs upon her death. These developments affected Pierrette deeply, particularly as preparations for her first communion changed household dynamics.

Sylvie insisted Pierrette be instructed by the zealous priest Monsieur Habert, who profoundly influenced her. Pierrette found solace in faith, abandoning escape plans as she embraced the Church’s teachings. Sylvie, noticing Pierrette’s transformation, grew curious about the priest’s methods and adopted a new piety herself.

Monsieur Habert’s sister, Mademoiselle Habert, befriended Sylvie; the two grew close. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette into her school, but the Rogrons refused, saying they would miss her. The colonel and Vinet, uneasy about the priest’s influence, feared he might press for a convent education for Pierrette.

As political and matrimonial intrigues unfolded, Vinet sought to strengthen his position by bringing in Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, impoverished but socially advantageous. Their arrival bolstered the liberal faction and alarmed Provins’ aristocrats. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, beautiful and charming, quickly attracted Rogron’s attention, while Sylvie began to see Pierrette as an obstacle to her own marriage prospects.

Sylvie decided Pierrette should learn domestic skills and effectively made her a servant. Pierrette, losing her only protector Adèle, accepted the role. Treated with less regard than a servant and scolded for trifles, she strove to please but received no praise, only criticism, deepening her despair.

Over the years Pierrette’s health deteriorated under relentless pressure and lack of affection. She suffered severe internal pains and stunted growth. The once vibrant child became a shadow of her former self, enduring in silence while her cousins remained oblivious.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before recounting the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut's arrival would incite in the Rogron family, it is necessary to explain how he came to Provins, since he plays a significant yet passive role in the events.

Brigaut fled the Rogron house, shaken by Pierrette’s distressing change. He scarcely recognized her; her once vibrant spirit had given way to illness and sorrow. From a high point in the Upper town he looked mournfully at Pierrette’s home, consumed by questions about her well-being and the depth of his feelings. Their bond, rare between children of opposite sexes, was profound. Brigaut had resolved to improve his prospects for her sake, learning a trade and moving to Paris with dreams of success to support her. But after a fortnight in the city he returned to Provins, quickly finding work as a journeyman cabinetmaker so he could stay near Pierrette.

He buried his feelings while trying to learn more about the Rogrons from Madame Frappier, who disclosed their dubious acquisition of Pierrette’s grandfather’s property. He often saw Pierrette at the market, where her frailty distressed him. Sundays at church brought them close, though she signaled him to remain hidden, sensing danger. Determined to discover the cause of her pallor and weakness, he feared for her health.

Pierrette, meanwhile, fretted about the changes since Brigaut’s arrival but was unaware of the internal strife among the Rogrons, particularly between Sylvie and Mademoiselle Habert (Céleste), both angling for marriage. Sylvie, forty-two, felt her age as a threat and dreaded dying alone, while Céleste, ten years younger, was encouraged by her brother, a priest, to pursue marriage. The political shift with the rise of the Martignac ministry emboldened Liberals in Provins and intensified competition for social standing.

Sylvie’s anxiety drove her to consult a doctor about the risks of marrying at her age; the discouraging prognosis heightened her fear. Céleste, seeing an opening, urged Sylvie to delay, hoping to remove a rival. Sylvie’s inner turmoil deepened, and jealousy flared when she noticed the colonel’s growing interest in Pierrette. Vinet, a lawyer with designs on Bathilde, recognized Sylvie’s vulnerability and schemed to exploit it, intending to wedge the colonel away from Pierrette and secure the Rogron fortune for Bathilde.

Vinet approached the colonel, urging him to court Pierrette rather than Sylvie. He painted a rosy picture of happiness with Pierrette, manipulating the colonel’s feelings while secretly plotting to advance Bathilde’s claim. The colonel, who had shown Pierrette an uneasy mix of affection and reserve, began to seek her out more often, which inflamed Sylvie’s jealousy.

Sylvie’s suspicions sharpened when she overheard Brigaut serenading Pierrette, and she concluded the colonel was involved with the girl. Her jealousy clouded her judgment and made her increasingly paranoid about losing the colonel. This obsession consumed her, warping her perception and driving her to confront Pierrette.

On a tense morning Sylvie accused Pierrette of harboring a secret admirer, mocking her innocence and demanding answers. Pierrette, frightened and bewildered, remained silent, which only intensified Sylvie’s suspicions. Sylvie’s jealousy turned into a desire for control; she threatened Pierrette with severe consequences if she did not confess.

As Pierrette endured the oppressive atmosphere of the Rogron household, her health continued to decline, though she remained unaware of its full severity. Her innocence and naivety sheltered her from some harsh realities, but emotional strain and heavy labor eroded her strength. Despite her suffering, Brigaut’s presence offered brief comfort and hope that eased her pain.

Sylvie’s relentless scrutiny and bitterness intensified the household’s cruelty, leaving Pierrette isolated and desperate. The old maid’s suspicion escalated into open hostility and confrontation, further undermining Pierrette’s well-being. As tensions peaked, Pierrette’s condition worsened and she longed for escape from her torment.

Amid this turmoil, love, jealousy, and ambition became entangled, setting the stage for a tragic sequence that would affect everyone involved. The struggle for power, affection, and security within the Rogron household threatened devastating consequences for Pierrette, whose innocence and vulnerability made her an easy target in a world rife with envy and desire.

For a month Rogron had stopped delivering the Constitutionnel to Gouraud, who then came for it himself, often lingering to gossip and walk with Sylvie. Eager to see the colonel, Sylvie dressed in her finest. When Gouraud arrived, Rogron excused himself, leaving Sylvie alone with the colonel.

Noting her solemnity, Gouraud asked about her cousin's health; she said the cousin was unwell. The colonel, uneasy about Vinet's influence over the Rogrons, felt marginalised. Vinet seemed to dominate their political plans, while Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, feared being sidelined. He was also aware of Rogron's growing infatuation with Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, which threatened his prospects.

Gouraud mentioned an interest in Bathilde, making Rogron pale at the idea of competition. The colonel suspected Vinet might be manoeuvring him into abandoning Sylvie for Pierrette as a ploy to benefit Vinet and Bathilde.

Left alone with Sylvie, Gouraud observed her unease and suspected Vinet's meddling. Sylvie, testing his feelings, raised Pierrette; the colonel dismissed any affection for her, saying he preferred a more mature partner. Relieved, Sylvie concealed her intentions and suggested her brother had considered Pierrette as a match.

Sensing Sylvie's interest, Gouraud declared his intention to marry her, delighting Rogron. Sylvie began planning their future home but remained wary of Pierrette's interactions with the colonel.

As dinner neared, Sylvie scolded Pierrette over a stain on her dress, blaming her for the mishap. Despite her harshness she ordered Pierrette to dress nicely for the evening, suspecting collusion with Gouraud.

The Chargeboeuf ladies arrived; Bathilde was spectacularly dressed. Under Vinet's guidance, Bathilde planned to use a marriage to Rogron as a stepping stone to power, treating him as a means rather than a partner. Vinet had instilled in her a hunger for independence and influence, urging her to exploit beauty and connections.

The evening grew tense. Bathilde's condescension toward Pierrette was obvious, and Vinet's remarks heightened the rivalry. They played cards; Sylvie tried to manipulate the game, while Pierrette's innocent comments provoked Sylvie's ire.

When Pierrette left the room in distress, Sylvie's anger flared and she resolved to make her pay for the perceived slight. The evening ended with Sylvie frustrated and feeling betrayed, reflecting on men's duplicity and her precarious position.

The next day Pierrette suffered a painful fall, which Sylvie blamed on disobedience, showing no sympathy. Rogron suggested caring for Pierrette, but Sylvie dismissed the idea, revealing her coldness and the widening rift between the cousins.

################################################
The week ended in relentless torment for Pierrette, who endured Sylvie's growing cruelty. She kept silent about Brigaut's arrival to avoid further anger, but his memory was her solace; she longed for him and feared for his safety. Their connection felt vital, and even one glimpse was a lifeline.

Determined to communicate, Brigaut arranged to exchange letters with Pierrette. One morning he slipped a note to her while pretending to buy fruit. The exchange unleashed deep feelings in both, exceeding ordinary affection. Sylvie remained unaware, and Pierrette returned home with the note.

That night Pierrette read Brigaut’s heartfelt letter: he expressed longing, a wish to escape their oppressive circumstances, and asked her to lower a string from her window so they could pass notes each night. Moved, Pierrette resolved to reply despite the difficulty of obtaining writing materials without waking Sylvie. She poured out her heart, confessing her love and describing the suffering inflicted by her relatives.

At midnight she sent her letter down to Brigaut, who awaited it eagerly. Their correspondence continued—full of hope and plans—despite Sylvie’s latent jealousy. Observing Pierrette’s newfound joy, Sylvie grew watchful, suspecting she had seen someone.

Under Sylvie’s oppressive scrutiny Pierrette’s health declined and one evening she fainted, drawing concern from the colonel and others. Sylvie dismissed the illness as a ruse, but Pierrette’s condition worsened. In desperation Pierrette cried for help, triggering a violent confrontation as Sylvie tried to seize her letters. At the height of Sylvie’s fury, Pierrette’s grandmother arrived, witnessed the struggle, and rushed to her granddaughter’s aid.

Her arrival brought hope after days on the brink of despair. Sylvie, confronted, seemed confused and frightened. The grandmother, determined to protect Pierrette, sought medical help fearing for her life.

Meanwhile Brigaut wrote to Madame Lorrain urging her to come and rescue Pierrette. His letters stressed the urgency—he feared for her health and safety. Upon receiving Brigaut’s appeals, the grandmother felt both joy and dread and immediately traveled to Provins.

She found Pierrette distressed and traumatized, bearing physical and emotional wounds. The sight of her injured hand—resulting from Sylvie’s violent attempts to control her—filled the grandmother with anguish. She vowed to secure justice and ensure Pierrette’s safety.

As Pierrette recovered, her bond with her grandmother deepened, giving her strength to face what lay ahead. The arrival of medical attention and the promise of a better life with her grandmother and Brigaut offered a glimmer of hope amid recent darkness. The struggle for freedom and love continued, with Pierrette determined to reclaim her life from the clutches of oppressive relatives.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine o’clock that morning, Monsieur Martener visited Monsieur Tiphaine to report the mistreatment Pierrette had suffered at the Rogrons’ hands. Tiphaine summoned Auffray, a notary and Pierrette’s relative. Tensions between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions were rising, fueled by damaging truths about Madame Tiphaine’s family, which intensified animosity and linked personal grievances to local political strife.

Tiphaine saw an opportunity to undermine the Rogrons, influential opponents of the monarchy. After consulting the public prosecutor, he, Auffray, and Martener arranged to petition the courts for Auffray to become Pierrette’s guardian, enabling a Family Council—backed by medical testimony—to remove her from the Rogrons’ control.

By midday the town buzzed: neighbors had heard Pierrette’s cries the night before, and speculation spread. Doctor Bianchon and Brigaut examined her injuries and confirmed the Rogrons’ abuse. Vinet, the Rogrons’ lawyer, rushed to warn of potential criminal charges. Sylvie, initially evasive, eventually confessed to her jealousy and the truth of the incident, grasping the severity of their situation.

Exploiting their fear, Vinet suggested a marriage announcement might quiet the scandal. Pressured and manipulated, the Rogrons agreed to a marriage contract that served their political interests. Vinet then filed a petition alleging Brigaut and the widow Lorrain had abducted Pierrette, attempting to cast the Rogrons as victims.

The court hearing was postponed, fueling public excitement. The Tiphaines rallied support for Pierrette, who remained unaware of the turmoil. She found care with her grandmother and Brigaut, devoted to her recovery. The doctors, appalled by her condition, unanimously condemned the Rogrons’ treatment.

When the court reconvened, Auffray presented the physicians’ report arguing for Pierrette’s protection. Vinet countered, but the judge ordered that Pierrette be kept away from the Rogrons until a Family Council could convene—a significant public rebuke.

The Family Council, composed of Pierrette’s relatives and allies, voted to remove Rogron as guardian and appoint Auffray in his place. Despite Vinet’s lengthy defense, the evidence against the Rogrons was overwhelming, prompting a civil suit for neglect and abuse.

As the legal battle continued, Pierrette’s health worsened. Despite medical efforts, she faced a grim prognosis. Martener and the Auffray family grew fond of her; her grandmother’s steadfast care and Brigaut’s visits provided comfort.

In March 1828 a critical operation was performed, but recovery was unlikely. As her condition declined, Pierrette expressed a desire to forgive her cousins, showing grace amid suffering. She died soon after. The funeral drew the town, while the Rogrons, fearing public outrage, stayed away.

Grief-stricken, Brigaut made her coffin, pouring his sorrow into the task. The Rogrons tried to exploit the situation legally, but community support for Pierrette blocked their efforts. Vinet, attempting to defend his clients, faced backlash as the truth of the Rogrons’ actions became more widely known.

Afterward, Brigaut left Provins to seek a new life in the military. Vinet and the Rogrons, however, continued to prosper politically; despite the scandal their reputations remained largely intact. The story of Pierrette’s fate became distorted, with many in town believing the Rogrons had acted nobly. Only a few—Martener, Brigaut, and kindred spirits—remembered the reality of her suffering and the injustices she endured.

The account closes with a reflection on justice and the societal forces that allowed such tragedies: the need for accountability and the lasting impact of Pierrette’s life and death on those who truly understood her plight.
