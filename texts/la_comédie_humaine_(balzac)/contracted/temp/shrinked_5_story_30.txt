Honoré de Balzac dedicates "Pierrette" to Mademoiselle Anna Hanska, noting the story's melancholy and the need to confront sorrows for loved ones.

In October 1827, sixteen-year-old Jacques Brigaut admires Pierrette Lorrain’s renovated house in Lower Provins and sings a Breton wedding song, expressing a wish to settle nearby. Pierrette, alarmed by a noise, urges him to flee.

Alone, Pierrette recalls three years of sorrow since her father, Major Lorrain, died. Her mother’s second marriage to a physician brought financial ruin. Pierrette was sent to live with the Rogrons in Provins, appointed guardians after a letter from her grandparents, fixing her dependent future.

The narrative examines social class, familial duty, and how hardship distorts relationships. Pierrette’s delicate health and emotional turmoil reflect broader pressures on vulnerable women and foreshadow the self-interest she will meet from the Rogrons.

Père Rogron, an unattractive, selfish innkeeper, and his equally unappealing wife live on about two thousand francs a year. After selling their inn, they move into a dilapidated house inherited from Pierrette’s grandfather. Their poorly raised children, Sylvie and Jérôme-Denis, become miserly yet save enough to buy a retail establishment called the “Family Sister.”

Sylvie runs the business efficiently while Jérôme struggles with trade. Their lives center on the shop, leaving them oblivious to the wider world. When the Lorrains try to place Pierrette with them, the siblings refuse, fearing she would threaten their inheritance.

A provincial shopkeeper returned from Paris briefly brings new ideas but soon lapses into provincial habits, shifting from shopkeeper to bourgeois. The Rogrons remodel their old house to imitate wealthier neighbors, spending a year obsessed with home improvements and social integration. Despite efforts, they are seen as vulgar and become socially ostracized.

To revive their standing, Sylvie invites Pierrette to live with them, hoping a respectable niece will confer respectability. Yet their past misdeeds over family property and Pierrette’s precarious circumstances complicate the plan. After her grandparents were hospitalized, Pierrette felt burdened by charity until discovering wealthier relatives. Her childhood friend Brigaut, an apprentice cabinet-maker, gave her money for her trip to Paris, which she accepted.

At her cousins’ home Pierrette meets indifference and criticism for her clothing. Her cousins prioritize financial appearance over her well-being, scrutinizing her dress instead of offering warmth. The arrivals of Vinet and Colonel Gouraud, who seek to manipulate the Rogrons, deepen the household’s self-interest and neglect.

As Pierrette’s life in Provins progresses, she becomes increasingly confined and controlled, losing early joys. During a family gathering she is coldly dismissed by Sylvie, who contrasts with Pierrette’s longing for affection. The Rogrons impose strict educational demands; despite a writing master, Pierrette struggles and is punished for perceived failures.

Over time her health declines amid this oppressive atmosphere. Relentless criticism and lack of affection hasten her deterioration. The narrative contrasts Pierrette’s innocence with her relatives’ coldness, exposing the harsh realities for those dependent on wealthier kin. Approaching her first communion, Pierrette finds solace in faith, but the Rogrons remain indifferent to her spiritual growth, valuing ambition over her welfare.

To understand the Rogron family turmoil after Jacques Brigaut's arrival, consider his journey to Provins. Troubled by Pierrette's decline from a lively child to a sickly shadow, Brigaut fled her home in distress, watched her house from a vantage point, and recognized his deep feelings for her.

At sixteen he returned from Paris determined to improve himself for Pierrette, becoming a journeyman with Monsieur Frappier, the town's best cabinet-maker. He worked diligently, earning a modest living while watching Pierrette from a distance. He learned the Rogrons had questionably acquired her grandfather’s property and that her living situation was oppressive. He often saw her in the market, burdened and unhappy, and though he longed to approach her, he refrained, wary of her distress.

Pierrette faced her cousin Sylvie's escalating jealousy after Brigaut’s arrival. Fixated on marrying Colonel Gouraud, Sylvie grew envious of any perceived connection between Pierrette and the colonel. Her insecurities led her to consult a doctor about marriage risks, complicating her feelings. Within the household Sylvie accused Pierrette of having a secret admirer; Pierrette, innocent and fearful of reprisal, kept silent. Sylvie’s scrutiny worsened Pierrette’s fragile health.

Sylvie’s jealousy turned vindictive. Tensions reached a peak during a breakfast scene where accusations left Pierrette isolated and ill. Colonel Gouraud stopped delivering the “Constitutionnel” to the Rogrons for a month, limiting himself to brief visits. Sylvie, eager for his attention, dressed attractively and awaited him. Gouraud felt uneasy about his partnership with Vinet, who seemed to dominate their political efforts, and provoked Rogron’s jealousy by mentioning interest in Bathilde.

As Pierrette’s beauty became more noticeable, Sylvie plotted against her cousin. She ordered Pierrette to dress nicely for dinner, suspecting the colonel’s interest. During an evening card game domestic tensions erupted: Sylvie believed Pierrette had helped Gouraud win, confronted her, and Pierrette fled, injuring her head. Sylvie cruelly labeled the injury divine punishment for disobedience, exposing her harsh control.

Pierrette endured a week of torment, physical and emotional, silently keeping Brigaut’s arrival secret for his safety. Thoughts of him were her solace, like an exile longing for home. Brigaut, aware of her suffering, arranged clandestine communication: at midnight Pierrette lowered a letter from her window. Their mutual longing allowed secret exchanges; at a market Brigaut slipped her a note declaring his love and urging escape.

Pierrette replied, describing her suffering and desire for freedom. Sylvie, suspicious of her newfound happiness, began watching her closely. When Pierrette fainted at a gathering, Sylvie dismissed the concerned colonel. Tensions escalated when Sylvie discovered Pierrette’s letters and confronted her. The struggle turned violent, leaving Pierrette injured. Brigaut had alerted Pierrette’s grandmother, who arrived and removed her from the oppressive household.

Her grandmother vowed to seek medical help and act against the Rogrons; Brigaut sought a doctor for Pierrette’s recovery. As she recuperated, her grandmother promised a better life, underscoring themes of love and resilience. Monsieur Martener visited Monsieur Tiphaine to denounce Pierrette’s mistreatment, intensifying the feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions. They plotted legal means to remove Pierrette from the Rogrons’ guardianship, stirring public outrage.

Vinet manipulated the situation, coercing the Rogrons into a marriage contract to distract from possible criminal charges. Pierrette was moved to Auffray’s care, where doctors confirmed her injuries resulted from abuse. A court ruling barred her return to the Rogrons, but her health nevertheless declined. Tragically, she died; her funeral drew public attention the Rogrons avoided.

Brigaut, devastated, vowed to seek justice. Vinet exploited the tragedy for political gain, using the affair to criticize societal indifference. The episode leaves a portrait of love thwarted by jealousy and cruelty, legal and political maneuvering, and a community forced to confront the consequences of its neglect.
