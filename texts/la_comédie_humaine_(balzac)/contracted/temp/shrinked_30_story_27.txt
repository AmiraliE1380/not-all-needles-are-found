In 1828, Dr. Horace Bianchon and Baron de Rastignac walked from Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré to the boulevard, discussing Rastignac's admiration for the Marquise d’Espard. Bianchon cautioned him against pursuing her, comparing her to a “one-eyed horse” and suggesting she was not as youthful as Rastignac thought.

Rastignac argued that the Marquise’s wealth and social status were appealing, especially since he faced financial difficulties after marrying off his sisters. Bianchon criticized fashionable women for their lack of genuine character and warned that the Marquise might be in debt despite her charm. He asserted that true virtue is often absent in high society. Rastignac countered that a fashionable partner could significantly benefit an ambitious man, viewing her as a means to success. Despite Bianchon’s warnings, Rastignac remained determined to pursue the Marquise, prioritizing her connections over the virtues of a more devoted partner.

Bianchon agreed to introduce Rastignac to his uncle, Judge Jean-Jules Popinot, known for his integrity but overlooked due to his modesty. As Bianchon approached Popinot’s home, he contemplated the challenges of persuading the judge to assist Rastignac, reflecting on ambition and societal expectations. The narrative critiques the superficiality of fashionable society while exploring themes of ambition and moral dilemmas.

Twelve years earlier, in 1816, Popinot was appointed President of the Commission Extraordinary to distribute food during a severe scarcity. Initially reluctant to stay in the Rue du Fouarre, he became dedicated to alleviating local suffering. Over five years, he transformed from a judge focused on legal outcomes to a compassionate figure devoted to helping the poor, akin to Saint Vincent de Paul. His charitable work drained his resources, yet he found fulfillment as a protector and benefactor.

By the end of his second year, Popinot had converted his storeroom into a parlor for the needy, meticulously recording donations and requests for assistance. His aide, Lavienne, helped manage the influx of desperate individuals seeking aid. Despite the stifling atmosphere, the poor revered Popinot, who tirelessly provided food, shelter, and employment.

Living frugally on a modest inherited fortune, Popinot was known for his absent-mindedness regarding personal appearance, often linked to his intellectual pursuits. He remained unrecognized by his peers, having not received the Legion of Honor.

Popinot's disheveled attire reflects his preoccupation with charity as he engages with petitioners. Bianchon expresses concern about the Marquis, and Popinot reads the petition detailing the Marquis's alleged mental decline and the influence of a woman named Jeanrenaud.

While skeptical, Popinot acknowledges the need for further investigation and reflects on the Marquise's potential ulterior motives in seeking the Commission in Lunacy for her husband. The following evening, Bianchon finds Popinot absorbed in a complex judgment, appearing disheveled due to a tailor's delay. They visit Madame d’Espard, a notable figure in Paris since her separation from her husband in 1815. At thirty-three, she maintained a youthful appearance and had re-entered society in 1820, hosting gatherings and forming ties with influential figures.

In the Marquise’s boudoir, she recounts her version of events leading to their separation, portraying herself as a victim. Popinot questions her motives and financial implications, noting her luxurious lifestyle contradicts her claims of distress. Tension escalates as he presses for clarity, hinting that her request might be driven by self-interest rather than genuine concern for her children.

After their meeting, Popinot resolves to investigate further, planning to summon Madame Jeanrenaud, linked to M. d’Espard. The next day, Madame Jeanrenaud arrives, presenting herself as unassuming and expressing concern for M. d’Espard, intriguing Popinot about the true dynamics at play. The case promises to reveal not only the truth about M. d’Espard’s mental state but also the lengths individuals will go to protect their interests in Parisian society.

The text explores the life of M. le Marquis d’Espard, a nobleman residing in a dilapidated Paris mansion, illustrating how minor events can lead to significant consequences. A minor illness delayed an important meeting with a judge, emphasizing the impact of small incidents.

D’Espard's once-grand home symbolizes the decline of French nobility. Chosen for its garden to benefit his children's health, the mansion, despite its state, provided a peaceful atmosphere with modest decor. His private family life was supported by a few loyal servants. D’Espard’s choice to isolate his children led to misunderstandings with neighbors, who viewed him as eccentric. Gossip portrayed him as mad, fueled by his frugal lifestyle and perceived financial ruin, exacerbated by tenants’ jealousy of his noble status.

D’Espard and his wife embodied true aristocratic values, instilling dignity and pride in their children, Comte Clement and Vicomte Camille. At around fifty, the Marquis appeared refined but was perceived as cold. The narrative highlights the complexities of social status, isolation, and societal perceptions of declining nobility in a changing France.

On a Thursday holiday, Popinot planned to question M. d’Espard. Early that morning, his sons played in the garden, waking their father. Upon Popinot's arrival, the portress informed him of the Marquis' supposed madness, claiming he encouraged his children to fight. In a shabby office, the Marquis learned of a petition for a Commission in Lunacy filed by his wife. Distressed over allegations of financial impropriety involving Madame Jeanrenaud, he revealed a family secret: the Jeanrenaud fortune had been wrongfully acquired, and he felt compelled to repay them, straining his finances.

Moved by the Marquis' noble intentions, Popinot listened intently. Madame Jeanrenaud soon entered, wanting to return the money given to the Marquis. Popinot reassured her that the payments were legally justified. Afterward, he reflected on the encounter and the nobility of the Marquis. The next day, he was removed from the case due to a potential conflict of interest, ultimately accepting the decision to maintain the Court's integrity. The narrative explores themes of honor, familial duty, and the complexities of social standing as the Marquis grapples with his family's dark history while striving to restore their honor.
