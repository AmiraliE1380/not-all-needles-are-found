During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon regularly attended Baronne de Watteville’s drawing-room because of her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After a quiet life in La Franche‑Comté he married Mademoiselle de Rupt, an heiress; the match was arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the Second Restoration. After her grandparents’ deaths Madame de Watteville grew more pious and exerted influence in Besançon’s circles.

Thin and plain, he worked as a turner and collected geological specimens. Many admired his noble soul: he accepted he would not dominate his wife and devoted himself to work and hobbies. The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings, fine cuisine—and he often acted as his own butler. Madame’s fortune was considerable; his estate modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 Abbé de Grancey announced a favorable lawsuit verdict. The Vicar‑General, calling it unexpected in the political climate, credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, whose obscure origins intrigued the guests. Amedée de Soulas, present that evening, would later prove significant.

France and England have long exchanged cultural influences—especially in language and fashion—so that what is "English" in Paris becomes "French" in London. Examples include the English national anthem "God Save the King," sometimes attributed to Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts that, after initial ridicule, spread across Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, only for fashions to shift again.

More recently England supplied French with terms like "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset, sparked a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that reached the provinces. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee‑Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent ill at ease in the dull military town. He kept a spare apartment, lived modestly, yet cultivated an ostentatious appearance and maintained a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger," dressing him fashionably while subsisting on scant income.

Seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was clever at economizing—often dining out to avoid household costs. His grooming and clothes were a façade meant to impress the local aristocracy and win Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His Parisian polish and travel stories masked financial strain.

Besançon’s conservative "Colony" maintained strict hierarchies; Amedee’s quirks, like trouser‑straps, set him apart and allowed nobles to seem modern to visiting Parisians. Rosalie, raised strictly, was modest at eighteen but resolute; her mother fiercely guarded her honor, complicating Amedee’s courtship. His life thus illustrates social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraint as he strives to win Rosalie’s heart.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas began as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took rooms on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie as ‘Albert Savaron’ and left his card with the local officials.”

“The Savaron name is well known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville observed.

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amédée de Soulas replied. “If he claims the Savaron arms he must add a bar-sinister. The only surviving Brabant is Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister denotes a bastard,” Rosalie chimed in, “but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble.”

“Enough,” the Baroness cut in.

“You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville said, and the Baroness urged Monsieur de Soulas to continue.

“In a town like Besançon, where everything is catalogued, Albert Savaron was quickly accepted by the local lawyers. They wondered who had sent him and what he intended—he sent his card to judges rather than calling. Three days later he was forgotten. He hired Jérôme, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. No one saw him much.”

“Does he go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight, breakfasts, works again, walks a little in his garden, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven,” Monsieur de Soulas said.

“How do you know all this?” she asked.

“I live on Rue Neuve and overlook his house; my servant speaks with Jérôme.” Babylas and riding supplied the rest of the gossip.

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man’s innocence, exposed the real culprits, and won their arrest. His defense astonished the Court. A merchant later gave him a difficult case, which he won. Monsieur Berryer advised we employ Savaron for our matter. Upon meeting him, I felt confident—and was right.”

“Is he extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” Abbé de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room lined with law books, simply furnished: an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, a green carpet. He was younger than I expected. He wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” exclaimed Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbé continued, “but he has a magnificent head. Black hair streaked with gray, thick and curly; a round white throat; a splendid forehead furrowed by deep lines of thought; an olive complexion; a square nose; fiery eyes. He has passion but calm, and a sweet voice that can thunder when needed. Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his shabby bedroom. The third time, I found him having a frugal breakfast in his library. He is no ordinary man; there’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why leave Paris for Besançon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. The Abbé, having studied his head and heard him argue against a prominent Parisian lawyer, thought Savaron would make a significant impact.

The Vicar-General’s portrait enthralled Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the mundane Count who had courted her. She replayed the Abbé’s words and fixated on his description—“What a fine forehead!” she thought—comparing him in her mind to the men at the table. When her mother asked why she wasn’t eating, Rosalie answered she had no appetite, lost in thought about the Abbé’s prelate-like hands and the window she had glimpsed from her bed, now realizing it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

At dinner she asked whether the Chapter’s lawsuit was settled; the Abbé answered. Her mother laughed; Rosalie admitted the Abbé’s talk had engrossed her. That night she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse his windows. She felt a thrill watching candlelight through the trees and saw him writing, imagining the color of his furniture. “While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls poses delicate questions: how much should they know? Too much enlightenment forces premature maturity; too little produces stunted minds. Rosalie’s crisis was triggered by the Abbé’s portrait of Savaron—a sudden inward stirring that made the ordinary extraordinary.

The next morning she saw Albert walking in the garden next to the Hôtel de Rupt. Having seen him, Rosalie resolved to enter his house, uncover his mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes. Throughout the day she embroidered with the concentration of someone who appears idle but is deeply reflective. She decided to go to confession—arranging to confess at half-past seven, just before the eight o’clock service—hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop and suggested he turn larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. He delighted in the idea but she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing a scolding. Rosalie imagined a charming observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled; the cross-court walk quickened her heart. She arrived at church before Mass, prayed briefly, and checked if Abbé Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered. No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression: solemn, burdened, his deep gaze conveying intense thought—those eagle-like eyes revealed a hidden ardor. In a bold moment she stood in his path to exchange glances, igniting a rush of warmth within her.

After Albert took a seat, Rosalie found a place to observe him during her confession. Time in the confessional seemed fleeting; by the time she emerged, Mass had ended and Albert had gone. “The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besançon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie concentrated on her embroidery, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother. From that Sunday, after their glance, she eagerly supported the plan for the belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. Rosalie mentioned a cool room in the grotto where they could take coffee; her mother approved and inspected the columns.

She approved a site in the garden not visible from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but commanding a view into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier. Rumors spread in Besançon about the Wattevilles’ new garden feature.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—” people said.

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior, and it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” an Abbé added; the young man corrected him—“No, he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.” No one suspected that the girl thought a witless goose had devised the plan to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings and thereby increasing the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the belvedere, Savaron was launching another enterprise. Through quiet connections among local merchants he was starting a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, who believed in promoting Besançon as a trade hub. To compete with Strasbourg, Besançon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants eager to promote their own enterprise.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed across regions, and with a low subscription price it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers. To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while letting him believe he was gaining. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, stirring comment in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though higher circles of Besançon dismissed it for liberal views. Rosalie, hearing about the Review, persuaded her father to subscribe.

“Papa,” she said, “there’s a Review published in Besançon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie who had shown him such affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first story she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact on her was profound. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages she became convinced it reflected him; others noted that Albert drew from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men set out from Lucerne to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, Rodolphe was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay while his companion Leopold continued the tour.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together. Leopold was bound for a notary’s post in Paris; Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a harder past: his mother had sacrificed everything for his education, hoping to secure his future. Rodolphe was passionate and impulsive, in contrast to Leopold’s calm temperament.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple and learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. Of modest means, Fanny supported her father by translating and writing; Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day Rodolphe obtained permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens—the family that rented to the Lovelaces. After hesitation he was allowed to explore and noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who hinted at an unusual backstory. Eager to meet Fanny, he found the Bergmanns protective and resistant.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met her and Gina, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded Rodolphe. Fanny and Gina aided him and got him to safety.

While he recovered Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments; she later revealed her husband was not as old as he had seemed and that she had married him under duress. She encouraged Rodolphe’s ambitions.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, arrived. Though they dreamed of freedom together, Rodolphe’s ambition, Fanny’s ties to her husband, and social constraints forced them to weigh love against duty and sacrifice, the landscape mirroring their intense emotions.

For twenty days Rodolphe stayed with the Bergmanns, observing Francesca with fascination and puzzlement. He saw youthful imprudence and a resolute, sometimes resistant character. The old man treated her like a father; her gratitude revealed an inherent nobility that deepened the enigma she presented.

Those days combined secret joys and melancholy, quarrels mingled with harmony. Rodolphe grew increasingly captivated by Francesca’s constant tenderness and jealous of trifles. One evening, when she said she wished to leave Gersau for its lack of luxuries, he asked if she valued luxury. She answered passionately, equating a love of luxury with an appreciation for art and beauty, while insisting she had borne hardship without complaint. Her playful claim that ordinary people aspired to greatness revealed her charm.

Francesca then insisted Rodolphe not accompany them to Geneva, citing town gossip and the need to protect her husband’s reputation. She professed love for Rodolphe but demanded secrecy, kissed him, and left him astonished.

The next day, after the Bergmanns’ lodgers departed, Rodolphe went to Vevay and then Geneva. To avoid local discomforts he rented a room in Eaux-Vives and asked after Italian refugees, hoping for a bookseller named Lamporani, but learned only the Colonna and Gandolphini couples had recently arrived. On his first walk to Villa Diodati he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy. Learning she was at the Colonnas’ lifted the veil: the intimacy he’d felt collided with the reality of her noble rank, and he feared he had been a mere whim.

He left his card with Prince Gandolphini and met a false Lamporani who invited him to a concert; though curious, Rodolphe did not impose. That night his passion intensified; he felt driven to raise his social standing. At the concert Francesca was radiant; their eyes met in charged recognition, and her beauty convinced him she had remained true despite her rank.

Afterward he was introduced to the Colonnas and Francesca, who teasingly called herself “Princess Rodolphini,” reviving memories of Gersau. She acknowledged his deep, faithful affection; he confessed an ambition to achieve greatness so she could be proud.

Francesca’s life was marked by contradiction: born for the cloister, family tragedies forced her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve status, yet she preferred independence, liberal ideas, and sympathized with revolutionary sentiments. Winter in Geneva flew by in her intelligent, beautiful company, but with spring Rodolphe grew restless; her calm contrasted with his need for more demonstrative feeling.

One day on a terrace he bitterly lamented others’ happiness, especially that of a noblewoman. Francesca, wounded, withdrew; he found her in tears and kissed her. She rebuffed him, asserted her dignity, and ordered him to leave—declaring they would not meet again until Naples. He, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

In Paris, Rodolphe found a portrait of Princess Gandolphini by Schinner. He thought himself unmoved, yet Francesca persuaded Schinner to paint originals for Rodolphe and Emilio, announcing their correspondence.

Driven by love and ambition, he invested everything in a deceitful venture that consumed three years. When Villele's ministry fell he was ruined; after three months at Villa Gandolphini in Naples, where they recovered, he turned to politics.

He tried to rebuild: his talents were acknowledged and a high post promised, but the July 1830 upheaval thwarted him. Only Francesca and God saw his brave efforts—a noble heart unfavored by fortune. Sustained by love, he faced trials. Lovers! Pray for him!

When Mademoiselle de Watteville finished her tale, Rosalie flushed and wept. The novel—an early example of a man narrating his own love—was her first encounter with that voice and convinced her Francesca Colonna was Albert’s beloved.

Curiosity hardened into resolve: despite insecurity about her looks and lack of musical talent, Rosalie was determined either to win Albert or to learn the truth. Her sheltered imagination spawned schemes—sneaking into his garden, writing to him, visiting her father’s land to force a meeting.

From Albert’s window she saw Jerome, his servant, helped over the wall by Mariette, the maid. Mariette’s plainness and relative security explained Jerome’s interest, and the scene taught Rosalie a dangerous lesson—servants could circumvent her mother’s restrictions.

The next morning Rosalie accompanied Mariette to Mass and seized the chance to question her about Jerome. Confronting Mariette about secret meetings, Rosalie unexpectedly offered help in exchange for access to letters Albert gave Jerome. Relieved by Rosalie’s discretion, Mariette agreed.

Rosalie’s obsession deepened when she read a letter Albert had written to his friend Leopold. It revealed his noble lineage, past struggles, ambition, and steadfast devotion to Francesca. Absorbed by his character, Rosalie read more correspondence and grew painfully aware of her own inadequacies and the moral wrong of invading his privacy. Still, she took the letters as proof of love and punished herself with penances and self-imposed suffering.

One evening Mariette brought a letter meant for Francesca. Torn, Rosalie read it; it confirmed Albert’s ongoing love and hopes for a shared future. Struck by their fidelity and by her unrequited longing, she resolved to step back—recognizing the nobility of their bond and yearning for the selfless love she had witnessed.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil; by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Alone, that struggle fueled her passion and dark plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; this is presented as a cautionary tale.

That winter Albert de Savarus advanced his standing in Besançon, eager for the Chamber’s dissolution. Backed by a wealthy contractor allied with the moderates, he revived the old Arcier aqueduct issue. Though drawing from the Doubs made restoring Arcier seem absurd, Savarus insisted the river water was unfit and promoted “Roman” water; the local Review echoed him and townspeople across parties accepted the idea. The controversy, driven by hidden interests, dismissed opponents as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the previously stagnant town, gaining influence without broad popularity.

He also won lawsuits for local priests; ambition consumed him. His apparent disinterest in personal gain masked another kind of moral usury: he expected a larger reward. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with Léopold Hannequin’s funds, a move that also qualified him for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who discerned Savarus’s designs, called him remarkable and suggested he had taken a wrong path.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, at first content merely to observe him. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year but was poorly managed by the inattentive Baron, left to an old steward, Modinier. The family often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot improved by the famous Watteville, who had built a dam and lake. Watteville’s heirs nonetheless pressed a dubious claim over the village of Riceys, whose inhabitants had long-held rights to the lands. The Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation his descendants continued to assert.

Rosalie’s maneuvers were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, enchanted by the lake and associating it with love. On a boat trip her father confided legal troubles with Riceys over land rights; Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate. She argued a lawsuit would add excitement and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey, clearly aiming to fix her future there and create gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing alignment would compromise his neutrality; the Abbé pressed political advantages, especially potential votes. Rosalie, determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election, wrote to the préfet revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but was ignored. She understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it.

As the election approached, influential men unexpectedly nominated Savarus. Surprised, he had been unaware. The Abbé warned him of potential betrayals and advised transparency about his past. Savarus’s passionate speech captivated his audience, yet the préfet worked to undermine him, eroding support. The first vote disappointed Savarus; he returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and an uncertain future. Then the Abbé brought hope: he had secured behind-the-scenes support, promised the Rouxey case would be resolved in time, and suggested marriage to Sidonie de Chavoncourt might consolidate votes. Savarus, initially resistant, recognized the advantage and prepared to navigate the coming choices of love and politics.

By nine the next evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy to discuss the surprise nomination of a candidate—the daughter of the Rupts, a former Master of Appeals and secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch. Madame de Chavoncourt arrived with her younger daughter Sidonie; her elder sister, secure in her relationship, stayed away, a choice much remarked upon. Abbé de Grancey circulated, steering talk and probing an elderly statesman about whom the Elder Branch might field; they feared Berryer, noted the Duc de Fitz-James’s Toulouse nomination, and worried that backing Monsieur Savarus might drive Republicans toward them rather than the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not come; Madame de Watteville took his lateness as an insult, while Madame de Chavoncourt urged patience and consultation. Rosalie, watching hostess and mother, noted the leniency shown Albert and hinted at a possible match between him and Sidonie. By ten impatience grew and some gentlemen began playing cards. The Abbé, uneasy, went to the window where Rosalie had withdrawn and suddenly cried, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went to Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent; the servant Jérôme said Albert had left in a post-chaise. The Abbé exclaimed ambiguously, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!”—and Rosalie’s face betrayed a joy the Vicar-General affected not to notice.

Back in the drawing-room Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven about fifteen guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt and her daughters, the Abbés de Godenars and de Grancey, Monsieur de Vauchelles, Rosalie, Amédée de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert. Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose pallor had turned to agitation.

When Madame de Chavoncourt asked what had occurred, a servant brought a letter for the Abbé on a silver tray. He read it; Rosalie grew pale and he recognized the handwriting. After a meaning glance he folded the letter into his pocket and declared, “She is in love with Albert Savarus!” As he moved to leave, Rosalie stopped him: the letter, she confessed passionately, was from Albert, and she begged him to remain silent when he announced Albert’s withdrawal—knowing such a withdrawal would destroy any hope of her sister’s marriage to him.

On her way to Mass Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette that Albert had suddenly vanished. An elderly Parisian had visited Monsieur Savaron; Savaron turned pale at the card, met him for over an hour, left with a large package, then hurried to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage with post horses, quit his post, and obtained a passport. His abrupt departure inflamed local prejudice against outsiders. Most soon forgot Albert—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness pressed Rosalie to marry; the Baron promised the family estate, les Rouxey, though his wife fretted over finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged; Rosalie refused, insisting she was content. Tensions rose during a fraught summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jérôme married, the Baron invested in the estate—fuel for the Baroness’s resentment. The Vicar-General tried to mediate.

Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo, by intercepting their letters, causing their separation. Horrified by the moral weight, the Vicar-General urged her to seek forgiveness. Her guilt deepened when a Parisian clerk, acting for Albert, came to sell his belongings and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received Albert’s letter: he forgave Rosalie and wished to remain in solitude.

The Vicar-General urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept de Soulas; she refused. After his death, Madame de Watteville married de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support. Now of age and financially independent, Rosalie went to Paris to confront the Duchess, publicly admitting at a ball her part in Albert’s downfall. She returned to Besançon having exposed the truth, but at heavy emotional cost—leaving a tale of love, betrayal, and the lasting consequences of ambition.

Mademoiselle de Watteville, noted in the Eastern provinces, lived alone at Les Rouxey—riding, hunting, improving her estate. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, looks younger than her years; Monsieur de Soulas has aged and told Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, one must, unfortunately, marry her!” Known for eccentricities, she visited Grande Chartreuse each year, perhaps recalling a grand‑uncle who once broke monastery walls to gain his freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon ostensibly to marry; she returned after a Loire steamboat boiler explosion cost her right arm and left leg and scarred her face. With failing health and constant pain she devoted herself to religion at the Chartreuse of Les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
