One of the few drawing-rooms where the Archbishop of Besançon was seen during the Restoration was that of the Baronne de Watteville, closely associated with him because of her devout nature.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming compared to his ancestor. After living quietly in La Franche-Comté, he married the heiress of the Rupt family, Mademoiselle de Rupt, who brought a substantial income. Their union, arranged in 1802, was formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following the death of her grandparents, Madame de Watteville grew more devout, leading a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur de Watteville, thin and unremarkable, spent his days as a turner and collector of local geological specimens. Some observed a noble soul in him: he recognized early that he would never dominate his wife, so he immersed himself in work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence was grand, reflecting both families’ status. It featured elegant furnishings and exquisite food, with Monsieur de Watteville often acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s wealth was considerable, while her husband’s estate was modest. Their close ties with the Archbishop attracted several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests eagerly welcomed Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit. The Vicar-General explained that the legal victory was unexpected given the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for its success.

Curious about Savaron, Madame de Watteville inquired into his background. The Vicar-General said Savaron lived nearby but his origins were unknown, heightening the guests’ interest. Amedee de Soulas, present at the gathering, would later prove significant to the unfolding story.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, particularly in language and fashion, often bypassing customs. The term "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual influence. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, once ridiculed, became dominant in Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, but French styles soon evolved.

In recent years England contributed new terms to French, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset’s poetry, inspired a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that spread to provincial towns. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt out of place in the dull military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained a lavish appearance, employing a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and spent extravagantly on his wardrobe while surviving on a limited income.

Though perceived as a spendthrift, Amedee was adept at budgeting, often dining out to save on household costs. His expensive grooming and clothes were a façade designed to impress the local aristocracy and win the favor of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and knowledge of Parisian culture earned him a reputation as well-informed despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy—referred to as "the Colony"—maintained strict social hierarchies. Amedee’s distinctive fashion choices, like trouser-straps, set him apart and reinforced his status as a "lion." The town’s aristocrats were eager to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee provided that touch of cosmopolitanism.

Amedee’s ambitions included marrying Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict religious household with little exposure to the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye, with domestic skills emphasized over social graces and sheltered from contemporary influences.

Despite a passive demeanor, Rosalie possessed a strong spirit from her family lineage. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This dynamic produced tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: the Baroness’s strictness caused Rosalie to suppress any feelings, creating a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and her own uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies, aiming for a favorable marriage while preserving his reputation. The town’s elite found him charming, and talk of an engagement circulated among the nobility. Yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts, since she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her.

Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus exemplifies the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints, as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville amid rigid social structures.

He registered as a resident at the Mairie and had his name, ‘ALBERT SAVARON,’ entered on the list of barristers at the Court, leaving his card with local officials.”

“The name Savaron is well-known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville interjected, “as one of the oldest and richest families in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he wishes to claim the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining member of the Brabant family is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister indicates a bastard, but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble,” Rosalie added.

“Enough, that will do, mademoiselle!” the Baroness interjected.

“You insisted on her learning heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville remarked, “and she knows it well.”

“Please continue, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In a town like Besancon, where everything is classified and known, Albert Savaron was received without hesitation by the local lawyers. They wondered who had sent him and what he hoped to achieve, especially since he sent his card to the judges instead of calling in person. Three days later, he was forgotten. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron was overlooked because no one had seen him.”

“Does he not go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight, has breakfast, then continues working. He walks in his garden, circling fifty or sixty times, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt inquired.

“I live on Rue Neuve, overlooking his house, and my servant communicates with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?”

“What else is there to do while riding?”

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked, directing the conversation to the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man’s innocence and exposed the real culprits, leading to their arrest. His defense impressed the Court and jury. A merchant subsequently entrusted him with a difficult case, which he won. Given our situation, Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ Savaron, predicting our success. Upon meeting him, I felt confident in him, and I was right.”

“Is he truly extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in his outer room, which he had decorated like old oak and lined with law books. The room was simply furnished with an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, and a green carpet. As I waited, I did not expect my advocate to be a young man. The setting matched his appearance; he wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” exclaimed Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. His black hair is streaked with gray, thick and curly, with a round white throat, a splendid forehead marked by deep lines of thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and fiery eyes. Despite his passionate nature, he has a calm demeanor and a sweet voice that can be thunderous when needed.”

“Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his bedroom, which was quite shabby. The third time, he was having a frugal breakfast in his library, and I observed him closely. He is no ordinary man; there’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besancon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little chance a stranger has here?”

“After studying his fine head,” the Abbe replied, “and hearing him respond to a prominent Parisian lawyer, I believe he will eventually make a significant impact.”

“Why discuss him? You’ve won your case and paid him,” Madame de Watteville remarked, noting her daughter’s rapt attention.

The conversation shifted, and Albert Savaron was no longer mentioned. The Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron captivated Rosalie, who found herself drawn to the romance of his character. For the first time, she encountered someone exceptional, contrasting sharply with the mundane Count who had previously courted her. Albert Savaron presented many enigmas to unravel.

“Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, longing to see him. She replayed the Abbe’s words in her mind, fixating on his description. “What a fine forehead!” she thought, scrutinizing the men at the table, finding none that compared.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she replied, lost in thought about the prelate’s hands.

As she pondered, she recalled a lighted window she had seen from her bed, realizing it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

“Monsieur de Grancey, is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” Rosalie asked during a lull in conversation.

“What does that matter to you?” her mother replied, sensing her daughter’s unusual interest.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” the Abbe answered.

“I never thought Rosalie would think about a lawsuit during dinner,” Madame de Watteville remarked.

“Nor I,” Rosalie said dreamily, prompting laughter. “But Monsieur de Grancey was so engrossed that I became interested.”

After dinner, Rosalie listened for any mention of Albert Savaron, but aside from congratulations to the Abbe for winning his suit, he was not discussed. Eager for bedtime, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse Albert’s windows. When the hour arrived, she felt a thrill watching the light from his candles through the trees. She saw him writing and imagined the color of his furniture.

“While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls poses serious challenges, as the future of a nation rests on mothers. The University of France has long avoided this issue: Should girls be informed about everything? Should their minds be restrained? The religious system often imposes such restraint. Enlightening them too much can lead to premature maturity, while suppressing their thoughts can result in explosive outbursts, as seen in Moliere’s character Agnes. This crisis was triggered in Rosalie by the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron.

The next morning, while dressing, Rosalie looked out to see Albert walking in the garden next to the Hotel de Rupt. “What if he lived elsewhere? At least I can see him here. What is he thinking?” she wondered.

Having seen this extraordinary man, Rosalie was determined to enter his house, uncover the mystery surrounding him, hear his eloquent voice, and catch a glimpse of his eyes. She resolved to achieve this.

Throughout the day, she embroidered with the concentration of a girl who appears to think of nothing but is deeply reflective. She decided to go to confession. The next morning, after Mass, she arranged to confess at half-past seven, just before the eight o’clock service, hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop, suggesting he turn larger pieces and construct a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. As he delighted in the idea, she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing a scolding.

“Don’t worry!” he replied, sharing her frustration with his wife.

Rosalie envisioned a charming observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

The eagerly awaited Sunday arrived, and Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled.

“It’s the first time I’ve seen you so fidgety,” Mariette remarked.

“Perhaps you too are more particular on certain days,” Rosalie replied, blushing.

As she crossed the courtyard, her heart raced with anticipation. She had never walked the streets like this before, feeling as if her mother could read her thoughts and would forbid her confession. She felt invigorated, as if walking on fire. She arrived at church before Mass, praying briefly before checking if the Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered.

No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression on her. His solemn demeanor suggested he bore a heavy burden, and his deep gaze conveyed intense thought. Rosalie understood the Vicar-General’s words fully now. Those eagle-like eyes revealed a hidden ardor. In a moment of boldness, she stood in his path to exchange glances, igniting a rush of warmth within her.

After Albert took a seat, Rosalie found a spot to observe him during her confession. When Mariette announced the arrival of Monsieur Giroud, it felt as if her time in the confessional had been fleeting. By the time she emerged, Mass had ended, and Albert had left.

“The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besancon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie focused intently on her embroidery, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother.

From that Sunday, after their glance, Rosalie eagerly supported the plan for the Belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a Belvedere he wants to build in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. “Why should I object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he’ll see us. Perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie, thinking you know more about life and propriety than we do?”

“I’ll say no more, mamma. Besides, my father mentioned a cool room in the grotto where we can take coffee.”

“Your father has had an excellent idea,” Madame de Watteville said, going to inspect the columns.

She fully approved of the design, choosing a location in the garden that could not be seen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but would allow them to see into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior was to be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor, while Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier.

“The Wattevilles are creating something lovely in their garden,” rumors spread in Besancon.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—”

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior, and it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” said an Abbe.

“No,” young Monsieur de Soulas corrected, “he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.”

“You know the least about what happens in that house,” Madame de Chavoncourt remarked sourly, glancing at her daughter, who had been waiting to marry for a year.

Mademoiselle de Watteville felt a flush of pride at the success of her Belvedere, sensing her superiority over others. No one suspected that a girl thought to be a witless goose had simply devised a plan to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten, as envy among other lawyers grew. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, which increased the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger in Besancon. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal acumen.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was also establishing a new venture. Through connections he had quietly formed among local merchants, he was launching a fortnightly paper called the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, who were convinced of the need to promote Besancon’s interests as a trade hub.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon, bringing light to Eastern France. His ideas resonated with the merchants, who believed they were their own.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, allowing his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, to handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed to various regions, and with a low subscription price, it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side of the Review. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while allowing him to believe he was gaining from their association. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among all French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, which sparked discussion in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, despite the higher circles of Besancon dismissing it for its liberal views.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besancon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie, who had shown him much affection, went to subscribe to the Eastern Review and lent her the four issues already published. That night, Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact of this work on her cannot be judged by ordinary standards. She had hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages, she became convinced that it reflected his experiences, as critics noted that Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, intending to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking mountain, river, and meadow views. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was destined to be a notary in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled background. His mother had sacrificed everything to secure his education, hoping to improve his prospects. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often led by desire, a contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple who took him in. He learned the woman at the window was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were modestly off, and Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day he sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens; the Bergmanns rented land to the Lovelaces and were initially hesitant but allowed him to explore. There he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who appeared to have a distinctive past. Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina in person, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety.

While recovering, Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny disclosed that her husband was not as old as he appeared and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his love for her and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, particularly when news arrived from Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past. Tito’s appearance provoked rivalry in Rodolphe, who feared losing her affection.

Despite these challenges, Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition and Fanny’s loyalty to her husband—created persistent tension. As they navigated their feelings, Rodolphe wrestled with the demands of love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, yet the realities of their lives threatened to overshadow their dreams.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil, but by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude, that imagined struggle fueled her passion and dark thoughts, leading her to devise numerous plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist, and this story serves as a cautionary tale.

During that winter, Albert de Savarus made significant strides in Besançon, eagerly anticipating the Chamber’s dissolution. He’d gained the backing of a wealthy contractor influential in the moderate party. The Romans had invested heavily in town water supplies, and Besançon drew water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to drink the Romans’ water seemed absurd in a town already served by the Doubs, but convincing citizens would yield substantial expenses benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit to drink and promoted Arcier water. Articles in the local Review echoed this, and townspeople, across political lines, agreed on the desirability of drinking like the Romans. The issue became a focal point, driven by hidden interests. Opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned, while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town and gained influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests, and his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to financial gain was really a form of moral usury: he anticipated a far greater reward.

In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also gave him a qualification for election. The Abbé de Grancey, who understood Savarus’s ambitions, remarked on his remarkable nature, suggesting he’d taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile, Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hôtel de Rupt, initially content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year, but its management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant named Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income, and they often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot created by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had constructed a dam and lake, enhancing the area’s beauty, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron’s death left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s efforts were complex and subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, where the lake’s beauty captivated her and she associated it with love. She suggested a boat trip to explore the lake, during which her father confided about legal troubles with Riceys over land rights. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as their lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for the estate.

She argued that a lawsuit would enliven their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Her determination to secure her future there was evident: she envisioned creating beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing such an alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to consider the political advantages of supporting the Wattevilles, especially given potential votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, was determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election. She wrote to the préfet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besançon decided to nominate Savarus as a candidate, much to his surprise. He was initially unaware of the political machinations at play. The Abbé, who had been watching, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. However, he faced challenges as the préfet worked to undermine his support, and his voter base began to decline. Despite his efforts, the political landscape shifted against him, and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting the results were disappointing for Savarus, who felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as hope faded, the Abbé de Grancey arrived with encouraging news. The priest had been working behind the scenes to secure support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes and encouraged Savarus to pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify his political standing. Savarus was initially resistant but realized the potential benefits of such a union.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable, and Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, understanding that his future depended on choices he would have to make in the coming days.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By nine o’clock the following evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy, gathered over the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, was to be presented that evening. Madame de Chavoncourt attended with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder sister, confident in her relationship, had made no effort with her appearance—an omission noted in small towns. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and offering incisive comments that shaped the talk.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman replied that Berryer, isolated on his bench, was uncertain and might disrupt the Government if supported. They discussed the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus could align Republicans with them instead of the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not arrived, and Madame de Watteville found his lateness disrespectful. Madame de Chavoncourt counseled her not to let trifles affect serious issues, suggesting Albert might be delayed by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother and Madame de Chavoncourt, noted her leniency toward Albert, hinting at a possible marriage between Sidonie and him.

At ten the absent Albert had provoked growing frustration; some gentlemen began playing cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant. When he confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s expression betrayed joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended to ignore, wondering about her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election, and others. The Abbe de Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose usually pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered aloud. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did he noticed Rosalie turn pale and recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, having exchanged glances with Rosalie that hinted at her feeling.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried. When he asked how she recognized the writing, her passionate reply revealed her feelings: “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, stunned, begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended so too would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In the morning, on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette the circumstances of Albert’s sudden disappearance. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National and called on Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer turned pale at the gentleman’s card; they met over an hour. Afterwards the gentleman left with a large package, and Savaron was distraught. He went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, and told his servant to find other employment.

The next day Savaron’s disappearance caused a stir in Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The affair fed local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most — except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness fretted over the finances. She and the Baron arranged a marriage with Monsieur de Soulas, which Rosalie firmly rejected; she insisted she was content as she was.

The conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and a summer at les Rouxey made Rosalie’s refusal public. After Mariette and Jerome married, the Baron made improvements to the estate, which the Baroness resented. As tensions rose, the Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified, stressing the moral gravity of her actions. He urged her to seek forgiveness and to grasp the consequences she had set in motion.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when she learned of Albert’s fate. A Paris clerk representing Albert came to sell his effects, revealing that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this with Rosalie he urged reconciliation with her mother and acceptance of Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now legally of age, Rosalie encouraged the marriage and thus secured her independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society intent on confronting the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon, having exposed the truth, yet the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, and improving the land, which made her well known in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, who has two children, appears younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has grown old. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville was famed for her eccentric “vagaries.” Each year she visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps following the example of a grand-uncle who had broken through monastery walls to seek freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, said to be for marriage, though the true reason was unclear. She returned after a tragic accident on a Loire steamboat: a boiler explosion so grievously injured her that she lost her right arm and left leg and was left with disfiguring facial scars. Her health declined, she suffered constant pain, and she now leads a life given to religious observance at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
