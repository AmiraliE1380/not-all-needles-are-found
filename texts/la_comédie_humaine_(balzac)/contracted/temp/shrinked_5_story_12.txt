During the Restoration one of the few drawing-rooms frequented by the Archbishop of Besançon was Baronne de Watteville’s, owing to her devoutness.

Monsieur de Watteville, a descendant of the infamous Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After a quiet life in La Franche-Comté, he married Mademoiselle de Rupt, heiress of the Rupt family, whose substantial income enriched their household. The match was arranged in 1802 and formalized in 1815 after the second Restoration. Following the death of her grandparents, Madame de Watteville became more devout, adopting a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Monsieur de Watteville, thin and unremarkable in appearance, occupied himself as a turner and collector of local geological specimens. Many observed a certain nobility of soul in him: realizing early he would not dominate his wife, he immersed himself in work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence reflected both families’ standing—grand rooms, elegant furnishings, and fine cuisine—with Monsieur sometimes acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s fortune was considerable, while her husband’s estate was modest. Their intimacy with the Archbishop drew several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834 guests warmly received Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit. The Vicar-General explained that the legal success had been unexpected given the political climate and credited a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron, for his decisive role.

Curious about Savaron, Madame de Watteville asked about his background. The Vicar-General said Savaron lived nearby but that his origins were unknown, which piqued the guests’ curiosity. Amedee de Soulas, present at the gathering, would later prove significant to the story that followed.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often skirting customs. What is "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual borrowing. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, first derided, became a dominant European fashion for decades. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, yet French styles soon shifted in response.

More recently England supplied French with terms like "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset's poetry, sparked a trend of stylish young men in Paris that spread to the provinces. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt ill at ease in the dull, military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained an ostentatious appearance, keeping a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and lavished expense on his own wardrobe while subsisting on scant income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was adept at economizing, often dining out to save on household meals. His costly grooming and clothes were a façade aimed at impressing the local aristocracy and winning Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and Parisian knowledge helped him cultivate a reputation as a well-informed young man despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy, called "the Colony," kept strict social hierarchies. Amedee’s unusual fashions—like wearing trouser-straps—set him apart and bolstered his status as a "lion." The town’s nobles wished to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee enabled that image.

Amedee’s chief ambition was to marry Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict, religious household with little contact with the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye and instructed more in domestic skills than social polish. Sheltered from modern influences, she lacked awareness of the social expectations surrounding courtship.

Despite a passive exterior, Rosalie had a resolute spirit from her lineage. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This dynamic created tension, especially as Amedee courted Rosalie. The Baroness’s strictness led Rosalie to suppress any feelings for Amedee, producing a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and Rosalie’s uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie, he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies to secure a favorable match while protecting his reputation. The town’s elite regarded him as charming, and gossip about a possible engagement circulated among the nobility. Yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts; she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her.

Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus illustrates the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville’s heart amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” said Monsieur de Soulas to the Baroness, as he waited for his soup to cool, “one fine morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and chose to stay on the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie and had his name—‘ALBERT SAVARON’—entered on the list of barristers at the Court, leaving his card with local officials.”

“The name Savaron is well-known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville interjected, “as one of the oldest and richest families in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he wishes to claim the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining member of the Brabant family is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister indicates a bastard,” Rosalie added, “but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble.”

“Enough, that will do, mademoiselle!” the Baroness interjected.

“You insisted on her learning heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville remarked, “and she knows it well.”

“Please continue, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In a town like Besancon, where everything is classified and known, Albert Savaron was accepted without fuss by the local lawyers. They wondered who had sent him and what he hoped to achieve, especially since he sent his card to the judges instead of calling. Three days later, he was forgotten. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron remained overlooked because no one saw him.”

“Does he not go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works until eight, breakfasts, continues working, walks in his garden in circles, dines, and goes to bed between six and seven.”

“How do you know all this?” Madame de Chavoncourt inquired.

“I live on Rue Neuve, overlooking his house, and my servant communicates with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?”

“What else is there to do while riding?”

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked, turning to the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man’s innocence and exposed the real culprits, leading to their arrest. His defense impressed the Court and jury. A merchant subsequently entrusted him with a difficult case, which he won. Given our situation, Monsieur Berryer advised us to employ Savaron, predicting our success. Upon meeting him, I felt confident— and I was right.”

“Is he truly extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in his outer room, decorated like old oak and lined with law books. The room was simply furnished with an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, and a green carpet. I did not expect my advocate to be young. The setting matched his appearance; he wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” exclaimed Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head. His black hair is streaked with gray, thick and curly; he has a round white throat, a splendid forehead marked by deep lines of thought, an olive complexion, a square nose, and fiery eyes. Despite his passion, he has a calm demeanor and a sweet voice that can thunder when needed.”

“Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his shabby bedroom. The third time, he was having a frugal breakfast in his library; I observed him closely. He is no ordinary man; there’s much behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besancon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “Doesn’t he know how little chance a stranger has here?”

“After studying his fine head,” the Abbe replied, “and hearing him respond to a prominent Parisian lawyer, I believe he will eventually make a significant impact.”

“Why discuss him? You’ve won your case and paid him,” Madame de Watteville remarked, noting her daughter’s rapt attention.

The conversation shifted, and Albert Savaron was no longer mentioned. The Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron captivated Rosalie, who found the romance of his character intoxicating. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the mundane Count who had courted her. Albert Savaron presented many enigmas.

“Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, longing to see him. She replayed the Abbe’s words, fixating on his description. “What a fine forehead!” she thought, scrutinizing the men at the table, finding none that compared.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she replied, lost in thought about the prelate’s hands.

She recalled a lighted window she had seen from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

“Monsieur de Grancey, is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” Rosalie asked during a lull.

“What does that matter to you?” her mother replied, sensing her daughter’s unusual interest.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” the Abbe answered.

“I never thought Rosalie would think about a lawsuit during dinner,” Madame de Watteville remarked.

“Nor I,” Rosalie said dreamily, prompting laughter. “But Monsieur de Grancey was so engrossed that I became interested.”

After dinner, Rosalie listened for any mention of Albert Savaron, but aside from congratulations to the Abbe for winning his suit, he was not discussed. Eager for bedtime, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse Albert’s windows. When the hour arrived, she felt a thrill watching the light from his candles through the trees. She saw him writing and imagined the color of his furniture.

“While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

Enlightening them too much can lead to premature maturity, while suppressing their thoughts can produce explosive outbursts, as in Moliere’s Agnes. She visited her father in his workshop, suggesting he turn larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. As he delighted in the idea, she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing a scolding.

“Don’t worry!” he replied, sharing her frustration with his wife.

Rosalie envisioned a charming observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

The eagerly awaited Sunday arrived, and Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled.

As she crossed the courtyard, her heart raced with anticipation. She had never walked the streets like this before, feeling as if her mother could read her thoughts and would forbid her confession. She felt invigorated, as if walking on fire. She arrived at church before Mass, praying briefly before checking if the Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered.

No man could seem unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression. His solemn demeanor suggested a heavy burden, and his deep gaze conveyed intense thought. Rosalie now fully understood the Vicar-General’s words. Those eagle-like eyes revealed a hidden ardor. In a bold moment she stood in his path to exchange glances, igniting a rush of warmth within her.

After Albert took a seat, Rosalie found a place to observe him during her confession. When Mariette announced Monsieur Giroud’s arrival, the time in the confessional felt fleeting. By the time she emerged, Mass had ended, and Albert had gone.

“The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besancon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie focused intently on her embroidery, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother.

From that Sunday, after their glance, Rosalie eagerly supported the plan for the Belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has a unique idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. “Why should I object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he’ll see us—perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie, thinking you know more about life and propriety than we do?”

“Your father has had an excellent idea,” Madame de Watteville said, going to inspect the columns.

She approved the design, choosing a spot in the garden that could not be seen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but would allow them to see into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined with periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior was to be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and a table of inlaid bark. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor, while Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier.

“The Wattevilles are creating something lovely in their garden,” rumors spread in Besancon.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—”

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris is coming to rusticate the interior, and it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” added an Abbe.

“No,” the young man corrected, “he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.”

“You know the least about what happens in that house,” Madame de Chavoncourt remarked sourly, glancing at her daughter, who had been waiting to marry for a year.

Mademoiselle de Watteville felt a flush of pride at the Belvedere’s success, sensing superiority over others. No one suspected that the girl thought to be a witless goose had simply devised the plan to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten amid envy from other lawyers. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, increasing the likelihood of being overlooked as a stranger in Besancon. Nevertheless, he successfully argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, gaining four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal skill.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was also launching a new venture. Through quiet connections among local merchants, he was starting a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients, convinced of the need to promote Besancon as a trade hub.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of enlightenment and commerce. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and bring light to Eastern France. His ideas resonated with the merchants, who liked to believe in their own enterprise.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of the profits. The Review appealed to various regions, and with a low subscription price it quickly garnered five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Boucher family’s eager son, Alfred, to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred’s contributions while letting him think he was gaining. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, only had to pay a fraction of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would yield dividends for shareholders, while the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue, the Review gained recognition among French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, sparking discussion in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though higher circles of Besancon dismissed it for liberal views.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besancon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie who had shown him so much affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already published. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited experience, the impact on her cannot be judged by ordinary standards. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert. From the first pages, she became convinced it reflected him, as critics noted Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men set out from Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, planning to tour the lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking views of mountains, rivers, and meadows. As they passed the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window. He decided to stay in Gersau while his friend Leopold continued the tour.

Rodolphe and Leopold were close, having studied law together. Leopold was destined to be a notary in Paris; Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more troubled background. His mother had sacrificed everything for his education, hoping to secure his future. Rodolphe was passionate and sensitive, often driven by his desires, in contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple willing to take him in. He learned the woman was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were of modest means; Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day Rodolphe sought permission to visit the gardens of the Bergmanns, who rented to the Lovelaces. After initial hesitation he was allowed to explore. He noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, who seemed to have an unusual background. Eager to meet Fanny, he met resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening Rodolphe heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later encountered her and Gina, but their conversation was cut short when Rodolphe was attacked and wounded by an unknown assailant. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken back to safety.

While he recovered Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny later revealed that her husband was not as old as he had seemed and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his feelings and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing he had great potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past, sent news and eventually arrived. Tito’s presence stirred rivalry and fear in Rodolphe of losing Fanny’s affection.

Despite these challenges Rodolphe and Fanny’s connection remained strong. They shared dreams of freedom and a future together, but the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition, Fanny’s loyalty to her husband, social constraints—loomed over them. As they navigated their feelings Rodolphe grappled with the tensions between love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, even as the realities of their lives threatened to overshadow their dreams.

He recognized in her the youthful imprudence and unbroken character of a woman, sometimes resisting love and at other times yielding. The old man treated her like a father, and Francesca’s gratitude stirred her innate nobility. Rodolphe found both the situation and Francesca an intriguing enigma.

Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from asking to see the Princess, returning to Eaux-Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety.

When he finally saw her at the concert she was radiant, and their eyes met in an electric moment. After the concert Rodolphe was introduced to the Colonnas and Francesca, who playfully called herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of their time in Gersau; he was overwhelmed by her voice and the intimacy of their proximity.

Francesca, with her blend of gaiety and tenderness, was remarkable. Born destined for a cloister, family tragedies forced her into marriage with Prince Gandolphini to preserve their status. Despite her noble background she had a taste for independence and liberal ideas and sympathized with revolutionary sentiments.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva, where time flew in the company of the intelligent, beautiful Francesca. Yet as spring neared he felt inner turmoil: Francesca’s calm contrasted with his emotional unrest, and he longed for more demonstrative feeling, believing Italian women to be more expressive.

One day on the terrace, Rodolphe voiced bitterness about others’ happiness, particularly that of a noblewoman. Francesca, wounded, drew back; Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she rebuffed him and asserted her dignity.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834-35 in turmoil, but by April, at nineteen, she fantasized about triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude, that struggle fueled her passion and dark thoughts, prompting numerous plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist, and this story serves as a cautionary tale for them.

During that winter, Albert de Savarus made strides in Besancon, eagerly anticipating the dissolution of the Chamber. He had secured support from a wealthy contractor influential in the moderate party. The Romans had long invested in water supplies for their towns, and Besancon sourced water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to drink the same water as the Romans seemed absurd in a town already served by the Doubs River. Yet if convinced, the citizens would authorize large expenditures that would benefit the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit for drinking and promoted Arcier water. Articles in the local Review echoed this, and townspeople across the political spectrum agreed on drinking the Romans’ water. The issue became a focal point in Besancon, driven by hidden interests. Opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned, while Savarus’s projects dominated discussion. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town, gaining influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests, and ambition consumed him. His apparent disinterest in personal gain masked a form of moral usury, as he anticipated a much greater reward.

In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Leopold Hannequin, which also qualified him for election. The Abbe de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s ambitions, remarked on his remarkable nature and suggested he had taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus to the society of the Hotel de Rupt, initially content to observe him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, was worth ten thousand francs a year, but its management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant, Modinier. The Baron's indifference stemmed from his wife's substantial income, and they often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot created by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had constructed a dam and lake, enhancing the area’s beauty, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron's death left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s efforts were complex and subtle. In May 1835 she left Besancon with her father for les Rouxey, captivated by the lake’s beauty and associating it with love. She suggested a boat trip, during which her father confided about legal troubles with the village of Riceys over land rights. Rosalie encouraged him to hire Savarus as a lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for their estate.

She argued that a lawsuit would add excitement to their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Rosalie’s determination to secure her future there was clear; she envisioned creating beautiful gardens.

Four days later the Abbe de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing alignment would compromise his neutrality. The Abbe urged him to consider the political advantages, especially the potential for votes.

Rosalie was determined to keep Savarus in Besancon and prevent his election. She wrote a letter to the Prefet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the Prefet disregarded it. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men in Besancon decided to nominate Savarus, much to his surprise. He was initially unaware of the political machinations. The Abbe de Grancey, observing the situation, warned Savarus of potential betrayals and advised him to be transparent about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. However, he faced challenges as the Prefet worked to undermine him, leading to a decline in his voter base. Despite his efforts, the political tides shifted against him, and he struggled to maintain his position.

On the day of the first voting the results disappointed Savarus, who felt the weight of his ambitions and the pressure of the election. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as he neared hopelessness, the Abbe de Grancey arrived with news of hope. The priest had been working behind the scenes to secure support, urging Savarus to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbe revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes, and he encouraged Savarus to pursue a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to further solidify his political standing. Savarus was initially resistant but recognized the potential benefits.

The Abbe’s guidance proved invaluable, and Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of both love and politics, aware that his future depended on choices he would make in the coming days.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By nine o’clock the following evening, Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with the aristocracy of Besançon, assembled to discuss the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. It was known that the former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, would be presented. Madame de Chavoncourt attended with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed, while her elder sister, secure in her relationship, had not made an effort with her appearance. In small towns, such details are noted. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and offering incisive comments that steered the conversation.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman said Berryer, alone on his bench, was uncertain and could disrupt the Government if he rallied support. They discussed the nomination of the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus might align Republicans with them instead of the Moderates.

By nine-thirty, Albert had still not arrived, and Madame de Watteville took his tardiness as disrespect. Madame de Chavoncourt advised her not to let small matters affect serious issues, suggesting Albert might be detained by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother’s exchange with Madame de Chavoncourt, noted her leniency toward Albert, hinting at a possible match between Sidonie and him.

At ten o’clock Albert was still absent and frustration grew. Some gentlemen began to play cards, while the Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to the window where Rosalie was hidden, exclaiming, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville, and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant below. When he confirmed that Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe de Grancey exclaimed, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s expression betrayed joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended not to. He wondered about her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced the shocking news of Albert’s departure without explanation. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained, including Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, and a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election. The Abbe de Grancey placed himself near the Baroness to watch Rosalie, whose normally pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. At that moment, a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did, he noticed Rosalie turn pale, recognizing the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, having exchanged glances with Rosalie that hinted at her feelings.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie stopped him, saying, “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” When he questioned how she knew the writing, her passionate reply revealed her: “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, stunned, begged him to keep the information secret, realizing that if the election ended, so would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In the morning on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette about the circumstances of Albert’s sudden disappearance. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National and visited Monsieur Savaron. The lawyer turned pale on seeing the visitor’s card; they met more than an hour. Afterwards the gentleman left with a large package, Savaron appeared distressed, went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other work.

The next day Savaron’s disappearance stirred Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The episode fueled local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most—except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was still no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness worried about finances. A match with Monsieur de Soulas was arranged, but Rosalie rejected it, insisting she was content as she was.

Conflict between Rosalie and her mother escalated, and they spent a tense summer at les Rouxey. After Mariette and Jerome wed, the Baron improved the estate—actions the Baroness viewed with resentment. As tensions mounted, the Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had sabotaged Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters, causing their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified at the moral gravity of her deed, urging her to seek forgiveness and to grasp the consequences.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when she learned of Albert’s fate. A clerk from Paris, acting for Albert, came to sell his belongings and revealed that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and a wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this, he urged Rosalie to reconcile with her mother and accept Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to ruin her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now of age, Rosalie encouraged her mother to marry, securing her own financial independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society, resolved to confront the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her part in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon having exposed the truth, yet the emotional cost weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.

Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, and improving the land, making her notable in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, mother of two, looks younger, while her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has aged considerably. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville is known for her eccentric “vagaries.” Each year she visits the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps inspired by a grand-uncle who once broke monastery walls to gain freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, reportedly to marry, though the true reason remains unclear. She returned after a steamboat boiler explosion on the Loire that cost her her right arm and left leg and left disfiguring facial scars. Her health deteriorated; in constant pain, she now devotes herself to religious practices at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
