During the Restoration the Archbishop of Besançon was one of the few to frequent the drawing-room of the devout Baronne de Watteville.

Monsieur de Watteville, descended from the notorious Watteville, was gentle and unassuming. After living quietly in La Franche-Comté he married the Rupt heiress—an arrangement of 1802 formalized in 1815—bringing her substantial income into the family. After her grandparents' death, Madame de Watteville grew markedly more devout, leading a pious life that influenced Besançon’s upper circles.

Thin and unremarkable, Monsieur de Watteville spent his days as a turner and a collector of local geological specimens, especially fossils. Some saw a noble character in him: accepting he would never dominate his wife, he immersed himself in work and hobbies.

The Rupt residence reflected both families’ status: elegant furnishings and ceremonious meals, with Monsieur often acting as his own butler. Madame de Watteville’s fortune was considerable, his estate modest. Their close ties to the Archbishop drew several notable abbés to their home.

At a ceremonial dinner in September 1834, guests warmly received Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a favorable verdict in a lawsuit over family property. The Vicar-General described the victory as unexpected given the political climate and attributed it to a young lawyer, Monsieur Savaron.

Curious about Savaron, Madame de Watteville asked his background. The Vicar-General said he lived nearby but his exact origins were unknown, which heightened the guests’ interest. Among those present was Amedee de Soulas, who would later prove significant to the unfolding story.

In all ages France and England have exchanged cultural influences, especially in language and fashion, often bypassing customs. The label "English" in Paris is "French" in London, reflecting mutual influence. For example, the English national anthem, "God Save the King," was composed by Lulli for a French play, and hoop skirts, once ridiculed, became dominant in Europe. By 1815 English long waists were mocked in Paris, but French styles soon evolved.

England contributed terms to French usage, such as "dandy" and "lion." The latter, popularized by Alfred de Musset’s poetry, inspired a vogue for stylish young men in Paris that spread to provincial towns. In 1834 Besançon had its own "lion" in Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas, a young man of Spanish descent who felt out of place in the dull military town. He lived modestly in a sparsely furnished apartment but maintained a lavish appearance, employing a young servant, Babylas, as his "tiger." Amedee dressed Babylas fashionably and spent extravagantly on his wardrobe while surviving on limited income.

Though seen as a spendthrift, Amedee was skilled at budgeting, often dining out to save on household costs. His expensive grooming and clothes were a façade to impress the local aristocracy and win the favor of Mademoiselle Rosalie de Watteville, a wealthy heiress. His travels and knowledge of Parisian culture earned him a reputation as well-informed despite financial strain.

Besançon was conservative and resistant to change; the local aristocracy—called "the Colony"—maintained strict hierarchies. Amedee’s distinctive fashion, like trouser-straps, set him apart and reinforced his status as a "lion." The town’s aristocrats were eager to appear modern to visiting Parisians, and Amedee provided that cosmopolitan touch.

Amedee’s ambitions included marrying Rosalie, who had been raised in a strict religious household with little exposure to the wider world. At eighteen she was modest and unassuming, educated under her mother’s watchful eye, trained in domestic skills rather than social graces, and sheltered from contemporary influences.

Despite a passive demeanor, Rosalie possessed a strong spirit inherited from her family. Her mother, Madame de Watteville, was fiercely protective and controlling, convinced her daughter’s honor was at stake. This dynamic produced tension as Amedee courted Rosalie: the Baroness’s strictness caused Rosalie to suppress feelings, creating a complex relationship marked by maternal jealousy and the daughter’s uncertainty.

As Amedee pursued Rosalie he navigated Besançon’s social intricacies, aiming for a favorable marriage while preserving his reputation. The town’s elite found him charming, and talk of an engagement circulated among the nobility. Yet Rosalie’s upbringing and her mother’s influence complicated his efforts, since she remained largely unaware of the romantic expectations around her.

Amedee de Soulas’s life in Besançon thus exemplifies the interplay of social ambition, cultural exchange, and provincial constraints as he strives to win Rosalie de Watteville amid rigid social structures.

“Madame,” Monsieur de Soulas said to the Baroness as he waited for his soup to cool, “one morning a gentleman from Paris arrived at the Hotel National and took the first floor of Mademoiselle Galard’s house on Rue du Perron. He registered at the Mairie and had his name, ‘ALBERT SAVARON,’ entered on the list of barristers at the Court, leaving his card with local officials.”

“The name Savaron is well-known,” Mademoiselle de Watteville interjected, “one of the oldest and richest families in Belgium.”

“He’s a Frenchman and no man’s son,” Amedee de Soulas replied. “If he intends to claim the Savaron arms, he must add a bar-sinister. The only remaining Brabant family member is a rich heiress, Mademoiselle de Savarus.”

“The bar-sinister marks a bastard, but a bastard of a Comte de Savarus is still noble,” Rosalie added.

“Enough, that will do, mademoiselle!” the Baroness cut in.

“You insisted she learn heraldry,” Monsieur de Watteville remarked, “and she knows it well.”

“Please continue, Monsieur de Soulas.”

“In a town like Besancon, where everything’s cataloged, Albert Savaron was taken in by the local lawyers without fuss. They wondered who sent him and what he hoped to do, especially since he sent his card to the judges rather than calling. Three days later, he was forgotten. He hired Jerome, the late Monsieur Galard’s servant, who could cook a little. Savaron was overlooked because no one noticed him.”

“Does he not go to mass?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“He attends the early service at Saint-Pierre on Sundays. He rises between one and two in the morning, works till eight, has breakfast, then continues. He walks in his garden in long circuits, dines, and goes to bed by six or seven.”

“How do you know all this?” she pressed.

“I live on Rue Neuve, overlooking his house, and my servant speaks with Jerome.”

“And you gossip with Babylas?”

“What else is there to do while riding?”

“How did you come to hire a stranger for your defense?” the Baroness asked the Vicar-General.

“The President of the Court assigned him to defend a half-witted peasant accused of forgery. Savaron proved the man innocent and exposed the real culprits, leading to their arrest. His defense impressed Court and jury. A merchant then gave him a difficult case, which he won. Given our situation, Monsieur Berryer advised we hire Savaron, predicting success. Upon meeting him, I felt confident—and I was right.”

“Is he truly extraordinary?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked.

“Certainly,” the Vicar-General replied.

“Tell us more,” urged Madame de Watteville.

“The first time I met him,” the Abbe de Grancey recounted, “he received me in an outer room paneled like old oak and lined with law books. It was simply furnished—an old writing table, six armchairs, gray curtains, and a green carpet. I didn’t expect my advocate to be young. The setting matched his appearance; he wore a black dressing gown with a red cord, red slippers, a red waistcoat, and a red smoking cap.”

“The devil’s colors!” cried Madame de Watteville.

“Yes,” the Abbe continued, “but he has a magnificent head: black hair streaked with gray, thick and curly; a round white throat; a splendid forehead furrowed with thought; an olive complexion; a square nose; and fiery eyes. Despite passion, he’s calm with a sweet voice that can thunder when needed.”

“Monsieur Albert Savaron is of average height, neither stout nor thin, with hands like a prelate.”

“The second time I visited, he received me in his shabby bedroom. The third time he breakfasted frugally in his library, and I watched him closely. He is no ordinary man; much lies behind that terrible yet gentle face. He seems to carry a heavy burden.”

“Why would such an eloquent man leave Paris for Besancon?” Madame de Chavoncourt asked. “A stranger has little chance here.”

“After studying his head and hearing him answer a prominent Parisian lawyer, I believe he will make a significant impact,” the Abbe said.

“Why discuss him? You’ve won your case and paid him,” Madame de Watteville observed, noting her daughter’s rapt attention.

Conversation moved on, but the Vicar-General’s portrait of Savaron had captivated Rosalie. For the first time she encountered someone exceptional, unlike the mundane Count who had courted her. Albert Savaron presented enigmas she longed to unravel.

“Albert Savaron de Savarus,” she mused, wanting to see him. She replayed the Abbe’s words in her mind, fixating on his description. “What a fine forehead!” she thought, scanning the men at the table and finding none that compared.

“Rosalie, why aren’t you eating?” her mother asked.

“I’m not hungry, mamma,” she answered, lost in thought about the prelate’s hands.

She recalled a lighted window she’d seen from her bed and realized it belonged to him. “I must see him!” she decided.

“Monsieur de Grancey, is the Chapter’s lawsuit settled?” Rosalie asked in a lull.

“What does that matter to you?” her mother retorted.

“It might go to the Court of Appeal, but our adversaries will hesitate,” the Abbe replied.

“I never thought Rosalie would think about a lawsuit at dinner,” Madame de Watteville said.

“Nor I,” Rosalie murmured dreamily, prompting laughter. “But Monsieur de Grancey was so engrossing I became interested.”

After dinner Rosalie listened for any mention of Savaron, but aside from the Abbe’s congratulations, he was not discussed. Eager for bed, she planned to wake between two and three to glimpse Albert’s windows. When the hour came, she thrilled at the candlelight seen through the trees. She watched him writing and imagined the color of his furniture.

“While the world sleeps, he is awake—like God!” she thought.

The education of girls raises difficult questions, since mothers shape a nation’s future. The University of France has long avoided them: should girls be fully informed or restrained? Religious systems often impose restraint. Enlightening them too much can force premature maturity; suppressing their thoughts can cause sudden outbursts, as Moliere’s Agnes shows. For Rosalie, the Abbe’s portrait of Savaron triggered such a crisis.

Next morning, while dressing, Rosalie saw Albert walking in the garden beside the Hotel de Rupt. “What if he lived elsewhere? At least I can see him here. What is he thinking?” she wondered.

Having seen this extraordinary man, Rosalie resolved to enter his house, uncover the mystery, hear his voice, and glimpse his eyes.

She embroidered with the absorbed air of one appearing idle but deeply reflective. She decided to go to confession. The next morning, after Mass, she arranged to confess at half-past seven, just before the eight o’clock service, hoping to encounter Albert.

She visited her father in his workshop, suggesting he turn larger pieces and build a grotto in the garden to showcase his work. As he delighted in the idea, she cautioned him not to tell her mother, fearing scolding.

“Don’t worry!” he replied, sharing her frustration with his wife.

Rosalie envisioned a small observatory from which she could see into Albert’s private room. Young girls often go to great lengths for the sake of a man, even if he remains unaware.

Sunday arrived. Rosalie dressed with such care that her maid Mariette smiled.

“It’s the first time I’ve seen you so fidgety,” Mariette remarked.

“Perhaps you too are particular on certain days,” Rosalie replied, blushing.

Crossing the courtyard, her heart raced. She had never walked the streets like this, feeling as if her mother could read her thoughts and would forbid confession. She felt invigorated as if walking on fire. She arrived at church before Mass, prayed briefly, and checked whether the Abbe Giroud was in his confessional, positioning herself to see Albert as he entered.

No man seemed unattractive to Rosalie in her curious state. Albert Savaron, strikingly handsome, made a profound impression. His solemnity suggested a heavy burden; his deep gaze conveyed intense thought. She now understood the Vicar-General’s words. Those eagle-like eyes revealed ardor. Boldly, she stood in his path to exchange glances, and warmth rushed through her.

After Albert sat, Rosalie found a place to watch him during her confession. When Mariette announced Monsieur Giroud’s arrival, her time in the confessional felt fleeting. By the time she emerged, Mass had ended and Albert had left.

“The Vicar-General was right,” she thought. “He is unhappy. Why has this eagle come to Besancon? I must know everything! But how?”

With this new desire, Rosalie embroidered, masking her thoughts with an innocent demeanor to deceive her mother.

From that Sunday, after their glance, Rosalie eagerly supported the plan for the Belvedere.

“Mamma,” she said one day, “my father has an idea for a Belvedere in the garden. Do you approve?”

“I approve of everything your father does,” Madame de Watteville replied dryly. “Why should I object to something that amuses him?”

“Because from there we’ll see into Monsieur de Soulas’ rooms, and he’ll see us. Perhaps remarks will be made—”

“Do you presume to guide your parents, Rosalie, thinking you know more about life and propriety than we do?”

“I’ll say no more, mamma. Besides, my father mentioned a cool grotto room where we can take coffee.”

“Your father has had an excellent idea,” Madame de Watteville said, going to inspect the columns.

She approved the design, choosing a garden spot that could not be seen from Monsieur de Soulas’ windows but would allow them to see into Albert Savaron’s rooms. A builder was commissioned to construct a grotto with a path lined in periwinkles, clematis, ivy, honeysuckle, and Virginia creeper. The interior would be rustic, with a mirror, an ottoman, and an inlaid bark table. Monsieur de Soulas suggested an asphalt floor; Rosalie proposed a rustic chandelier.

“The Wattevilles are creating something lovely in their garden,” rumors spread in Besancon.

“They’re wealthy and can afford such whims—”

“A thousand crowns!” Madame de Chavoncourt exclaimed.

“Yes, a thousand crowns,” young Monsieur de Soulas replied. “A man from Paris will rusticate the interior; it will be very pretty. Monsieur de Watteville is carving the chandelier himself.”

“Berquet is making a cellar beneath it,” an Abbe said.

“No,” young Monsieur de Soulas corrected, “he’s raising the kiosk on a concrete foundation to prevent dampness.”

“You know the least about what happens in that house,” Madame de Chavoncourt remarked sourly, glancing at her daughter, who’d been waiting to marry for a year.

Mademoiselle de Watteville felt a flush of pride at the Belvedere’s success, sensing superiority over others. No one suspected that the supposed witless girl had devised the plan simply to get a closer look at Albert Savaron’s private study.

Albert Savaron’s brilliant defense of the Cathedral Chapter was soon forgotten as envy among other lawyers grew. He remained secluded, avoiding social gatherings, which made him more easily overlooked as a stranger. Nevertheless, he argued three complex cases at the Commercial Tribunal, winning four prominent merchants as clients who recognized his legal acumen.

On the day the Watteville family inaugurated the Belvedere, Savaron was launching a new venture. Through connections he'd quietly formed among local merchants, he was founding a fortnightly paper called the Eastern Review, funded by forty shares of five hundred francs each from his first ten clients. They believed in promoting Besancon as a trade hub.

To compete with Strasbourg, Besancon needed to become a center of commerce and enlightenment. Albert’s vision was to challenge the literary dominance of Strasbourg and Dijon and to bring light to Eastern France. His ideas appealed to merchants who fancied themselves their town’s champions.

Savaron wisely kept his name out of the spotlight, letting his chief client, Monsieur Boucher, handle finances while he retained editorial control and a share of profits. The Review appealed to various regions, and at a low subscription price it quickly gathered five hundred subscribers.

To avoid offending local pride, Albert invited the Bouchers’ eager son Alfred to manage the literary side. Albert maintained control, subtly guiding Alfred while letting him think he was gaining from the association. The commercial community, pleased with the Review’s success, paid only parts of their shares. With two hundred more subscribers, the publication would pay dividends for shareholders, though the editor remained unpaid.

After the third issue the Review gained notice among French publications, which Albert began reading at home. The third number featured a tale attributed to him, which sparked talk in Madame de Chavoncourt’s drawing room, though Besancon’s higher circles dismissed it for its liberal views.

“Papa,” Rosalie said, “there’s a Review published in Besancon; you should subscribe and keep it in your room, as mamma wouldn’t let me read it, but you can lend it to me.”

Monsieur de Watteville, eager to please Rosalie, who had shown him much affection, subscribed and lent her the four issues already out. That night Rosalie devoured the tale—the first she had ever read. Given her limited experience, its effect on her exceeded ordinary judgment. She hoped to find in it the sentiments and perhaps the life of Albert, and from the first pages she felt convinced it reflected his experiences; critics noted Albert had drawn from his own life in this confession.

In 1823 two young men left Lucerne for a holiday in Switzerland, intending to tour the Lake of the Four Cantons. The journey offered striking mountain, river, and meadow views. Passing the hamlet of Gersau, one friend, Rodolphe, was captivated by a newly built house and a woman at the window and decided to stay; his friend Leopold continued the trip.

Rodolphe and Leopold had studied law together: Leopold was bound for a notary’s post in Paris, while Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, had a more unsettled background. His mother had sacrificed everything to secure his education, hoping to improve his prospects. Passionate and sensitive, Rodolphe was often led by desire, a contrast to Leopold’s calm demeanor.

In Gersau Rodolphe lodged with a couple who took him in. He learned the woman at the window was Fanny Lovelace, who had moved there with her elderly father for his health. They were modestly off, and Fanny supported her father by translating and writing. Rodolphe was struck by her beauty and musical talent.

The next day he sought permission to visit the Bergmanns’ gardens; the Bergmanns rented land to the Lovelaces and were initially hesitant but allowed him to explore. There he noticed a deaf-mute girl, Gina, whose presence hinted at a difficult past. Eager to meet Fanny, Rodolphe encountered resistance from the protective Bergmanns.

That evening he heard Fanny singing and was enchanted. He later met Fanny and Gina in person, but their conversation was cut short when an unknown assailant attacked and wounded him. Fanny and Gina aided him and he was taken to safety.

While recovering, Rodolphe received devastating news: his mother had died. The loss plunged him into deep depression, but he found solace in Fanny’s company. They bonded over shared interests, and Rodolphe confessed his feelings, only to learn Fanny was married to an older man, Emilio. Despite this, Fanny’s affection for Rodolphe grew and they shared intimate moments.

Fanny disclosed that her husband was not as old as he appeared and that she had married him under duress. Rodolphe was torn between his love for her and the reality of her marriage. Fanny encouraged him to pursue his ambitions, believing in his potential.

Their relationship deepened, yet Rodolphe struggled with jealousy and insecurity, especially when news arrived from Tito, a young man from Fanny’s past. Tito’s appearance provoked rivalry in Rodolphe, who feared losing her affection.

Though their connection remained strong and they shared dreams of freedom and a future together, the complexities of their circumstances—Rodolphe’s ambition and Fanny’s loyalty to her husband—created persistent tension. As they navigated their feelings, Rodolphe wrestled with the demands of love, ambition, and the sacrifices required for happiness. The beauty of their surroundings mirrored the intensity of their emotions, yet the realities of their lives threatened to overshadow their dreams.

The old man treated her like a daughter; her gratitude aroused a nobility in her that intrigued Rodolphe and made her an enigma.

One evening, when Francesca said she wanted to leave Gersau because it lacked luxury, Rodolphe asked if she cared for luxury. She then insisted Rodolphe not come with them to Geneva, citing town gossip and her wish to protect her husband’s reputation. She affirmed her love for Rodolphe but demanded it remain concealed. She kissed him and left him astonished.

To avoid the town’s discomforts he rented a room in Eaux-Vives. His first inquiry was after Italian refugees, especially a bookseller named Lamporani, but the landlord said the only new arrivals were Prince and Princess Colonna and Prince and Princess Gandolphini.

Rodolphe’s first walk took him to the Villa Diodati, whose beauty struck him. Approaching, he saw Francesca in an elegant carriage with an old lady and felt dizzy with surprise. Learning they were at the Colonnas’ residence, a veil seemed to lift: the implications of their past interactions became clear.

He feared he had been a mere whim for Francesca, struggling with the reality of her noble standing. He sent his card to Prince Gandolphini and was received by the false Lamporani, who invited him to a concert that evening. Though curious, Rodolphe refrained from requesting an audience with the Princess and returned to Eaux-Vives with mingled anticipation and anxiety.

As evening approached his passion deepened and he felt compelled to elevate his social position to match hers. At the concert she was dazzling; their eyes met with immediate recognition. Francesca’s beauty and grace captivated him and he felt a profound connection, convinced she had remained faithful to their past despite her rank.

After the concert Rodolphe was introduced to the Colonnas and to Francesca, who playfully styled herself “Princess Rodolphini.” The jest revived memories of their time in Gersau; he was overwhelmed by the beauty of her voice and the intimacy of their proximity.

In the ensuing days Francesca perceived Rodolphe’s deep love, which for her felt natural. She said their affection should be faithful and enduring, whatever the distance. Rodolphe, however, was driven by ambition; he wanted to achieve greatness so Francesca could be proud of him.

Francesca proved a remarkable blend of gaiety and tenderness. Once destined for a cloister, family misfortunes had compelled her to marry Prince Gandolphini to preserve her family’s standing. Yet despite her noble birth she had acquired a taste for independence and liberal ideas, inclining her toward revolutionary sentiments.

Rodolphe spent the winter in Geneva where time flew in Francesca’s intelligent and charming company. With spring came inner turmoil: Francesca’s calmness contrasted with his emotional unrest; he wished she would show more feeling, believing Italian women to be more expressive.

One day on the terrace he voiced a bitter complaint about the happiness of others, singling out a woman of noble lineage; his words wounded Francesca and she withdrew. Rodolphe followed and found her in tears. In a moment of passion he kissed her, but she repelled him and asserted her dignity.

Francesca then ordered him to leave, declaring they would not meet again until Naples. Her command was firm; Rodolphe, recognizing the strength of her will, complied.

Rosalie spent the winter of 1834–35 in turmoil, but by April, at nineteen, she imagined triumphing over the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. In solitude that struggle fed a dark passion and led her to devise many plans. Many young women like Rosalie exist; her story is a cautionary tale.

During that winter Albert de Savarus made significant strides in Besançon, eagerly awaiting the Chamber’s dissolution. He’d gained the backing of a wealthy contractor influential within the moderate party. The Romans had once invested in local water supplies, and Besançon drew water from Arcier, a distant hill. Restoring an aqueduct to bring Arcier water into town seemed absurd where the Doubs already flowed, but persuading citizens would produce large expenses benefiting the contractor.

Savarus argued the river water was unfit and promoted Arcier water. Articles in the local Review echoed this, and townspeople across political lines found the idea of drinking like the Romans appealing. The issue became a focal point, driven by hidden interests; opponents were dismissed as old-fashioned while Savarus’s projects dominated conversation. After eighteen months he had stirred the stagnant town and acquired influence without popularity.

Throughout the winter he won several lawsuits for local priests; his ambition consumed him. His apparent indifference to money disguised a kind of moral usury: he expected a far greater reward. In October 1834 he helped a struggling merchant with funds from Léopold Hannequin, which also gave him an electoral qualification. The Abbé de Grancey, who saw Savarus’s designs, remarked on his remarkable nature and suggested he’d taken a wrong turn in life.

Meanwhile Rosalie resolved to introduce Savarus into the Hôtel de Rupt’s society, content initially to watch him from afar. The Wattevilles’ estate, les Rouxey, yielded ten thousand francs a year, but its management was neglected by the Baron, who left it to an old servant named Modinier. The Baron’s indifference sprang from his wife’s substantial income; the couple often retreated to les Rouxey, a picturesque spot created by the famous Watteville.

Watteville had built a dam and lake that enhanced the area’s beauty, but his heirs maintained a questionable claim over the village of Riceys, which had long enjoyed rights to the land. The Baron’s death left a legacy of usurpation, and his descendants continued to assert control over the village.

Rosalie’s efforts were subtle. In May 1835 she left Besançon with her father for les Rouxey, where the lake captivated her and she associated its beauty with love. She suggested a boat trip and during it her father confided legal troubles with Riceys over land. Rosalie urged him to hire Savarus as their lawyer, stressing the importance of securing the Dent de Vilard for the estate.

She argued a lawsuit would enliven their lives and insisted on bringing Savarus to les Rouxey. Her determination to secure her future there was clear: she envisioned creating gardens and a lasting home.

Four days later the Abbé de Grancey visited Savarus to persuade him to represent the Wattevilles. Savarus hesitated, fearing that such an alliance would compromise his neutrality. The Abbé urged him to weigh the political advantages of supporting the Wattevilles, especially given potential votes.

Rosalie, meanwhile, was determined to keep Savarus in Besançon and prevent his election. She wrote to the préfet, revealing Savarus’s ambitions, but the préfet disregarded her. Rosalie understood the political landscape and sought to manipulate it to her advantage.

As the election approached, a meeting of influential men decided to nominate Savarus, much to his surprise. He had been initially unaware of the political machinations surrounding him. The Abbé, who’d been watching, warned Savarus of possible betrayals and advised him to be candid about his past.

Savarus delivered a passionate speech outlining his vision and ambitions, captivating his audience. However, he faced obstacles as the préfet worked to undermine him and his voter base began to shrink. Despite his efforts, the political tide shifted against him and he struggled to hold his ground.

On the day of the first voting the results were disappointing for Savarus; he felt the weight of ambition and the pressure of the contest. He returned home in despair, reflecting on his love for Francesca and the uncertainty of his future.

Just as hope faded, the Abbé de Grancey arrived with encouraging news. The priest had been working behind the scenes to marshal support for Savarus, urging him to visit the Wattevilles and promising their case would be resolved in time for the election.

The Abbé revealed that winning the Rouxey lawsuit could secure crucial votes and urged Savarus to consider a marriage with Sidonie de Chavoncourt to solidify his political standing. Savarus resisted at first but soon recognized the potential advantages of such an alliance.

The Abbé’s guidance proved invaluable. Savarus prepared to navigate the complexities of love and politics, aware that his future would depend on choices he would have to make in the coming days.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
By nine o’clock the following evening Madame la Baronne de Watteville’s rooms were filled with Besançon’s aristocracy, gathered over the unusual decision to go to the polls for the daughter of the Rupts. The former Master of Appeals, a secretary to a loyal minister of the Elder Branch, was to be presented. Madame de Chavoncourt attended with her younger daughter Sidonie, elegantly dressed; her elder sister, confident in her relationship, had made no effort with her appearance—an omission noted in small towns. The Abbe de Grancey moved among the groups, listening and offering incisive comments that shaped the talk.

“If the Elder Branch returns,” he asked an elderly statesman, “what politicians would they find?” The statesman replied that Berryer, isolated on his bench, was uncertain and might disrupt the Government if supported. They discussed the Duc de Fitz-James in Toulouse and how voting for Monsieur Savarus could align Republicans with them instead of the Moderates.

By nine-thirty Albert had not arrived, and Madame de Watteville found his lateness disrespectful. Madame de Chavoncourt counseled her not to let trifles affect serious issues, suggesting Albert might be delayed by a consultation. Rosalie, watching her mother and Madame de Chavoncourt, noted her leniency toward Albert, hinting at a possible marriage between Sidonie and him.

At ten the absent Albert had provoked growing frustration; some gentlemen began playing cards. The Abbe de Grancey, perplexed, went to a window where Rosalie stood and exclaimed, “He must be dead!” The Vicar-General, Monsieur de Watteville and Rosalie went into the garden to check Albert’s rooms, which were dark and silent.

“Jerome!” Rosalie called to the servant. When he confirmed Albert had left in a post-chaise, the Abbe cried, “He is ruined! Or he is happy!” Rosalie’s expression betrayed joy, which the Vicar-General noticed but pretended to ignore, wondering about her involvement.

Back in the drawing-room, Monsieur de Watteville announced Albert’s unexplained departure. By half-past eleven only fifteen guests remained: Madame de Chavoncourt, the Abbe de Godenars, the two Chavoncourt girls, Monsieur de Vauchelles, the Abbe de Grancey, Rosalie, Amedee de Soulas, a retired magistrate who had supported Albert’s election, and others. The Abbe de Grancey stationed himself near the Baroness to observe Rosalie, whose usually pale face now showed agitation.

“What could have happened to Monsieur de Savarus?” Madame de Chavoncourt wondered aloud. At that moment a servant brought a letter for the Abbe de Grancey on a silver tray. The Baroness urged him to read it. As he did he noticed Rosalie turn pale and recognized the handwriting. He folded the letter and discreetly pocketed it, having exchanged glances with Rosalie that hinted at her feeling.

“She is in love with Albert Savarus!” he concluded. As he prepared to leave, Rosalie intercepted him. “Monsieur de Grancey, it was from Albert!” she cried. When he asked how she recognized the writing, her passionate reply revealed her feelings: “I love him! What is the matter?” He told her that Albert was withdrawing from the election. Rosalie, stunned, begged him to keep the news secret, realizing that if the election ended so too would the prospect of her sister’s marriage to Albert.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
One morning, on her way to Mass, Mademoiselle de Watteville learned from Mariette of Albert’s sudden disappearance. An elderly gentleman from Paris had arrived at the Hotel National and called on Monsieur Savaron. Savaron turned pale at the gentleman’s card; they met for over an hour. The gentleman left with a large package; Savaron was distraught. He went to the Prefecture, bought an old carriage, ordered post-horses, left his keys, and told his servant to seek other employment.

Savaron’s flight the next day caused a stir in Besançon; the Prefect quickly granted him a passport. The affair fueled local prejudice against outsiders, and Albert was soon forgotten by most — except Girardet, the Vicar-General, and Rosalie, who alone knew the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death.

By April 1836 there was no news of Albert. The Baroness de Watteville urged Rosalie to marry, noting how much she had changed. The Baron agreed to give Rosalie the family estate, les Rouxey, but the Baroness fretted over finances. She and the Baron arranged a match with Monsieur de Soulas, which Rosalie firmly rejected; she insisted she was content as she was.

The conflict escalated, and a summer at les Rouxey made Rosalie’s refusal public. After Mariette and Jerome married, the Baron improved the estate, provoking the Baroness’s resentment. As tensions rose, the Vicar-General tried to mediate.

One day Rosalie confessed to the Vicar-General that she had interfered in Albert’s relationship with Francesca, the Duchess d’Argaiolo: she had intercepted letters and caused their separation. The Vicar-General was horrified, stressing the moral gravity of her deception. He urged her to seek forgiveness and recognize the consequences she had set in motion.

Rosalie’s guilt deepened when she learned more of Albert’s fate. A Paris clerk representing him came to sell his effects, revealing that Albert had renounced the world and entered a monastery. The Vicar-General wrote to the monastery and received a letter from Albert expressing complete forgiveness for Rosalie and his wish to remain in solitude.

When the Vicar-General shared this, he urged reconciliation with her mother and acceptance of Monsieur de Soulas. Rosalie remained defiant, unwilling to sacrifice her mother’s happiness. The Vicar-General’s death left a void; soon after, Madame de Watteville married Monsieur de Soulas, believing Rosalie’s support.

Now legally of age, Rosalie encouraged the marriage and thus secured her independence. With a substantial fortune she entered Parisian society intent on confronting the Duchess d’Argaiolo. At a ball she publicly revealed her role in Albert’s downfall, leaving the Duchess shaken.

Rosalie returned to Besançon, having exposed the truth, yet the emotional toll of her actions weighed heavily. The tale of love, betrayal, and the consequences of ambition unfolded, leaving a lasting impact on all involved.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mademoiselle de Watteville lived alone on her estate of les Rouxey, riding, hunting, and improving the land, making her well known in the Eastern provinces. Madame de Soulas, who has two children, appears younger; her husband, Monsieur de Soulas, has grown old. He remarked to young Chavoncourt, “My fortune has cost me dear. To know a bigot, it is unfortunately necessary to marry her!”

Mademoiselle de Watteville was famed for her eccentric vagaries. Each year she visited the walls of the Grande Chartreuse, perhaps following a grand-uncle who once burst through monastery walls seeking freedom. In 1841 she left Besançon, supposedly to marry, though the true reason was unclear. She returned after a tragic Loire steamboat accident: a boiler explosion so grievously injured her that she lost her right arm and left leg and suffered disfiguring facial scars. Her health declined; in constant pain she now leads a life of religious observance at the Chartreuse of les Rouxey.

PARIS, May 1842.
