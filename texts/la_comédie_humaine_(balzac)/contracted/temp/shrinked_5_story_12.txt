In Baronne de Watteville's drawing-room, a center of Besançon society, the Archbishop was a frequent guest because of her devoutness. Monsieur de Watteville, from a notorious lineage, married the wealthy Mademoiselle de Rupt in 1815, which deepened his wife's piety. Their home, furnished with tasteful refinement, displayed their status and attracted notable clergy.

At a dinner in September 1834, guests welcomed Monsieur l’Abbé de Grancey, who announced a legal triumph by the young lawyer Albert Savaron, arousing interest—particularly Madame de Watteville’s. The Vicar-General mentioned Savaron lived nearby but his origins were obscure, stirring curiosity about his character.

France and England had long influenced one another culturally, especially in language and fashion. England recently imported terms like "dandy" and "lion," the latter for a flamboyant man, which affected towns like Besançon, where Amedee-Sylvain de Soulas became a local "lion." Of Spanish descent, Amedee sought to raise his standing in a conservative town.

Living modestly yet projecting luxury with the help of his stylish servant Babylas, Amedee maintained an extravagant appearance despite limited means. His social aim was to impress the local aristocracy, particularly Rosalie de Watteville, a sheltered heiress. Amedee coveted Rosalie’s dowry, but Madame de Watteville scrutinized his motives. Rosalie remained indifferent, making his pursuit an example of aspiration and social expectation.

Monsieur de Soulas recounted Savaron’s arrival from Paris: he rented an apartment, registered as a barrister, and kept a low profile. Mademoiselle de Watteville suspected noble lineage, while Amedee claimed he was a commoner who had adopted a false aristocratic mark. Initially ignored by the provincial bar, Savaron worked steadily and lived quietly, remaining obscure until he successfully defended a mentally challenged man accused of forgery. His performance impressed the court and drew clients; the Vicar-General remarked on his striking appearance and eloquence.

Rosalie, increasingly intrigued, found Savaron more attractive than her superficial suitors. She planned to meet him at church and felt a connection during mass. Her fascination grew until she persuaded her father to erect a belvedere in their garden to observe Savaron’s private life. Meanwhile Savaron launched a fortnightly paper, the Eastern Review, promoting Besançon’s interests; Rosalie’s father subscribed, which intensified her infatuation.

In July 1823, friends Rodolphe and Leopold traveled to Switzerland. Rodolphe became captivated by Fanny Lovelace in Gersau and stayed behind while Leopold continued on. Rodolphe, the illegitimate son of a nobleman, was passionate and sensitive; Leopold was bound for a notary’s career.

Rodolphe lodged with the Stopfers and learned Fanny lived simply as a translator and writer. Eager to meet her, he gained access to her garden and suspected her mute servant, Gina, had a hidden past. One evening, overhearing Fanny speak Italian with Gina, Rodolphe grew convinced they were refugees. A misunderstanding led Gina to attack him; Fanny and Gina then aided his recovery, deepening his attachment.

Soon after, news of his mother’s death plunged Rodolphe into despair. Leopold returned to France, leaving Rodolphe in Gersau, where Fanny’s company brought solace. Their bond deepened through shared sorrows and dreams. Fanny admitted she was married to an older man, Emilio, but held hope for a future with Rodolphe. She encouraged his ambitions, and their conversations revealed a mutual passion for freedom and liberal ideals. When Fanny learned her family would return to Italy, she rejoiced; Rodolphe, jealous of her past, vowed to wait. Their love blossomed amid the Swiss landscape, symbolizing hopes sustained through political turmoil and personal sacrifice.

For twenty days Rodolphe was enchanted by Francesca, who embodied youthful recklessness and the complexities of womanhood. He observed her affectionate, playful relationship with her husband Emilio, which both intrigued and frustrated him. One evening she admitted a taste for luxury, likening it to her love of art, and refused to accompany Rodolphe to Geneva to protect Emilio’s reputation; though she professed love for Rodolphe, she insisted on discretion.

After the Bergmanns left, Rodolphe went to Geneva, settled in Eaux-Vives, and sought information about Italian refugees, including a bookseller named Lamporani. At Villa Diodati he encountered Francesca in an elegant carriage and learned of her aristocratic Colonna ties. Feeling inadequate loving a princess disguised as a commoner, he sought an audience with her husband, Prince Gandolphini, who received him cordially. Despite wanting to see Francesca, Rodolphe returned to his lodgings, preoccupied with their uncertain future.

When they next met at a gathering, their bond deepened. Francesca urged Rodolphe to pursue his ambitions without losing his youth. Her background as a well-educated heiress—originally destined for a cloistered life—contrasted with a revolutionary spirit that aligned with Rodolphe’s aims.

As spring approached, Rodolphe wrestled with longing and anxiety, doubting Francesca’s feelings. Tension flared when he carelessly compared her to a French noblewoman, wounding her pride; she told him to leave until they could meet again in Naples, marking a turning point in their relationship.

Back in Paris, Rodolphe found a portrait of Princess Gandolphini inspired by Francesca. Driven by love and ambition he invested in a venture that failed, costing him three years. After the Villele ministry fell he sought refuge at Villa Gandolphini in Naples. Just as success seemed near, the July 1830 revolution erupted, but Rodolphe persevered, sustained by love.

Meanwhile Mademoiselle de Watteville was consumed by jealousy over Francesca, convinced she loved Albert; this spurred her desire to win his affection and to confront Mariette about Jerome. Rosalie learned of Albert’s noble lineage and ambitions, which intensified her infatuation. As his career advanced, her feelings hardened into obsession; she read another letter outlining his hopes for the Duchesse.

Despite her turmoil, Rosalie resolved to withdraw, sacrificing her desires for Albert and the Duchesse’s happiness. Her inner conflict underscores the complexities of love, jealousy, and the sacrifices people make for affection.

In the spring of 1835 nineteen-year-old Rosalie, driven by solitude, longed to overcome the Duchesse d’Argaiolo. Albert de Savarus, meanwhile, gained political traction in Besançon, securing influential support for an aqueduct restoration, including backing from a wealthy contractor.

Savarus, who had represented local priests in lawsuits, concealed his ambitions behind apparent indifference. In October 1834 he funded a merchant's election. The Abbe de Grancey saw his potential but noted his reluctance to ally with the Wattevilles, fearing it would compromise his neutrality.

Rosalie visited her family estate, les Rouxey, learned of her father's legal troubles, and urged hiring Savarus as their lawyer to protect her inheritance. To keep him in Besançon she tried to hinder his political rise by alerting the Prefect to his Royalist leanings. Despite her efforts, Savarus was nominated and his eloquence won town support.

As the election neared, the Prefect and allies worked to weaken Savarus's base. Facing unrequited love for Francesca, Savarus was urged by the Abbe to reach out to the Wattevilles—especially Madame de Chavoncourt, whose daughter Sidonie might provide crucial backing. He began to consider a marriage alliance with Sidonie as a balance of love and ambition.

At a gathering hosted by Madame la Baronne de Watteville concern grew over Savarus's absence; Madame de Chavoncourt dismissed it, but Rosalie suspected otherwise. They learned he had left in a post-chaise, prompting mixed reactions.

The next morning Mademoiselle de Watteville discovered Savarus's abrupt disappearance after a visit from an old gentleman; Monsieur de Chavoncourt was elected in his stead. Time passed and most forgot Albert—except Rosalie, who wrestled with the truth about the Duc d’Argaiolo’s death and refused her mother’s plan to marry her to Monsieur de Soulas.

Tension at les Rouxey escalated and ended in tragedy when the Baron drowned. Grief-stricken, Rosalie considered confessing her part in Albert's fate. The Vicar-General urged her to seek redemption and revealed Albert had entered a monastery. Years later, after her mother married Monsieur de Soulas, Rosalie confronted the Duchess about Albert's letters, leaving the Duchess shaken. Now living alone at les Rouxey after a severe steamboat accident, Rosalie devotes herself to religious practices.
