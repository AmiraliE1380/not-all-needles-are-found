**Summary of "Albert Savarus" by Honoré de Balzac**

**Dedication**
The novel is dedicated to Madame Emile Girardin.

**Setting and Characters**
The story unfolds in Besançon during the Restoration, primarily in the drawing-room of Baronne de Watteville, a devout woman married to the gentle Baron de Watteville. Their daughter, Rosalie, is the sole heir to their wealth, which includes a significant dowry. The Baron is a turner by trade, while Madame de Watteville is deeply involved in religious activities and social gatherings.

**Introduction of Albert Savaron**
Albert Savaron, a mysterious lawyer, enters the scene after winning a significant lawsuit for the Archbishop of Besançon. His reputation grows as he defeats a renowned Parisian lawyer, leading to curiosity about his background. He is described as a man of great intellect and ambition, yet his origins remain obscure.

**Amedee de Soulas**
Amedee de Soulas, a young dandy from a Spanish family, aspires to marry Rosalie. He is portrayed as a man of style and charm, but his financial situation is precarious. He seeks to impress Rosalie and her mother, hoping to secure a favorable marriage.

**Rosalie's Education and Character**
Rosalie has been raised in a strict, religious environment, leading to her innocence and naivety. She is described as physically unremarkable but possesses a hidden strength and potential for passion, inherited from her ancestors. Her upbringing has left her unprepared for the complexities of love and desire.

**The Love Triangle**
As Amedee pursues Rosalie, she becomes intrigued by Albert Savaron, whose intellect and mystery captivate her. Rosalie's feelings for Albert grow, leading to jealousy and inner turmoil as she grapples with her emotions. Meanwhile, Albert is haunted by his past love for Francesca Soderini, a noblewoman who marries another man, the Duc d'Argaiolo.

**Albert's Ambitions**
Albert's ambition drives him to seek a political career, aiming to become a deputy. He believes that marrying into the Watteville family could elevate his status. However, he struggles with the weight of his past and the fear of failure.

**Conflict and Resolution**
The narrative builds tension as Rosalie's feelings for Albert clash with her mother's expectations for her to marry Amedee. The Vicar-General, Abbe de Grancey, becomes a confidant for both Albert and Rosalie, attempting to navigate the complexities of their relationships. 

Ultimately, Albert's ambitions lead him to a crossroads where he must choose between love and political success. His decision to leave Besançon in search of a new life signifies a turning point, leaving Rosalie heartbroken and determined to reclaim her agency.

**Conclusion**
The story concludes with Rosalie's realization of her feelings for Albert and her resolve to pursue him, despite the obstacles in her path. The themes of ambition, love, and the struggle for identity resonate throughout the narrative, highlighting the complexities of human relationships and societal expectations.

**Addendum**
The novel connects to other works in Balzac's "Human Comedy," featuring characters like the Vicomtesse de Beauseant and Leopold Hannequin, illustrating the interconnectedness of Balzac's literary universe.
