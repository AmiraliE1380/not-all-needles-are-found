**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work expresses my gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. I often reflect on our cherished moments in Milan and hope this book serves as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse are distressed by their strict upbringing under Comte and Comtesse de Granville, leading to loveless marriages. Madame du Tillet is trapped in a marriage with banker Ferdinand du Tillet, while Madame de Vandenesse feels unfulfilled despite affection from her husband, Comte Felix de Vandenesse. Their strict religious education left them innocent yet naive, yearning for deeper fulfillment.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her unhappiness to her sister, expressing the emptiness of her luxurious life and societal constraints. Despite her own struggles, Madame du Tillet tries to comfort her, but both feel powerless against their circumstances, lamenting their lost innocence and lack of genuine love.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix marries Madame de Vandenesse with hopes of providing her a loving home, but she soon feels monotony and becomes aware of her desires, complicating her feelings for her husband, who fails to recognize her emotional changes.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, captures Madame de Vandenesse's attention. Their interactions are charged with unspoken attraction, while Nathan struggles with his feelings for her and his obligations to Florine, an actress who has supported him.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine relies on Nathan for her success, leading to a possessive and tense relationship. As Nathan becomes more involved with Madame de Vandenesse, Florine grapples with jealousy and fear of losing him.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's relationship deepens with passion and longing. The countess is captivated by Nathan's artistic spirit, while he is drawn to her innocence. Their secret love flourishes amid societal pressures, causing both joy and anxiety.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's struggles with debt lead him to contemplate suicide. Unaware of his despair, the countess nurtures her feelings for him. Upon learning of his plight, she rushes to save him, demonstrating her love.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess seeks financial assistance for Nathan, facing societal judgment. Despite her efforts, Nathan's precarious situation threatens to pull them apart, testing her love as she confronts the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
The countess and Nathan face new challenges as the count, aware of their bond, seeks to protect his family's honor. The countess must navigate her feelings for Nathan alongside her responsibilities to her husband, culminating in difficult choices that will shape their futures.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, illustrating the interconnectedness of his literary universe. Each character's journey reflects the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
