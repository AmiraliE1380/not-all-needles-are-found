**A Daughter of Eve**  
*By Honoré De Balzac*  
*Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley*  

**Dedication**  
To Madame la Comtesse Bolognini, nee Vimercati. This work expresses my gratitude for the joy your conversation brought to a traveler reminiscing about Paris. Your daughter Eugenie embodies the gifts of womanhood, unlike the Eugenie in these pages, who faced a rigid upbringing. I often reflect on our cherished moments in Milan and hope this book serves as a sign of my friendship.

---

**Chapter I: The Two Maries**  
In a luxurious Parisian boudoir, sisters Madame du Tillet and Madame Felix de Vandenesse are distressed. Raised by strict parents, they entered loveless marriages without understanding love. Madame du Tillet feels trapped in her marriage to banker Ferdinand du Tillet, while Madame de Vandenesse enjoys a more affectionate relationship with Comte Felix de Vandenesse but still feels unfulfilled. Their upbringing left them innocent yet naive, leading to a sense of loss and yearning for deeper fulfillment.

---

**Chapter II: A Confidence Between Sisters**  
Madame de Vandenesse confides her unhappiness to her sister, expressing the emptiness of her luxurious life and feeling trapped by societal expectations. Despite her own struggles, Madame du Tillet tries to comfort her sister, but both lament the lack of genuine love in their marriages.

---

**Chapter III: The History of a Fortunate Woman**  
Comte Felix de Vandenesse marries Madame de Vandenesse, hoping to provide her with a loving home. Initially happy, she soon feels monotony in her life and becomes aware of her desires, complicating her feelings for her husband, who fails to recognize her changing heart.

---

**Chapter IV: A Celebrated Man**  
Raoul Nathan, a rising literary figure, captures Madame de Vandenesse's attention. Their interactions are filled with tension and attraction, but Nathan struggles with his ambitions and his relationship with Florine, an actress who has supported him.

---

**Chapter V: Florine**  
Florine, a talented actress, relies on Nathan for success while feeling possessive. Their relationship becomes strained as Nathan's ambitions grow, leading to jealousy and tension, especially as he becomes more involved with Madame de Vandenesse.

---

**Chapter VI: Romantic Love**  
Madame de Vandenesse and Nathan's relationship deepens, filled with passion and longing. The countess is captivated by Nathan's artistic spirit, while he is drawn to her innocence. Their love blossoms in secret, but societal pressures complicate their feelings.

---

**Chapter VII: Suicide**  
Nathan's struggles with debt lead him to contemplate suicide, believing it to be his only escape. Unaware of his despair, the countess continues to nurture her feelings for him. When she learns of his plight, she rushes to save him, demonstrating her love and commitment.

---

**Chapter VIII: A Lover Saved and Lost**  
The countess seeks financial assistance for Nathan, facing societal judgment and personal turmoil. Despite her efforts, Nathan's situation remains precarious, testing the countess's love as she grapples with the consequences of their relationship.

---

**Chapter IX: The Husband’s Triumph**  
The count, aware of the bond between his wife and Nathan, takes steps to protect his family's honor while seeking to understand their connection. The countess confronts her feelings for Nathan, navigating her responsibilities to her husband and societal expectations, leading to difficult choices that will shape their futures.

---

**Addendum**  
The characters in "A Daughter of Eve" appear in various other works by Balzac, illustrating the interconnectedness of his literary universe. Each character's journey reflects the complexities of love, ambition, and societal expectations in 19th-century France.
