Railroads were beginning to transform travel to and from Paris, making many small, traditional enterprises seem antiquarian. The picturesque "coucous" that had run from the Place de la Concorde and the Cours-la-Reine a century earlier were becoming scarce by 1842; in 1820 not all environs of Paris enjoyed regular stage-coach service. The Touchards had built a prosperous messagerie from the rue du Faubourg-Saint-Denis, but short-run coucous still competed on nearby routes. Parisian demand for country trips sustained local operators—often innkeepers along the road—who retained custom by familiarity, lower charges, and accommodating practices the Touchards’ formal system could not match. Travelers preferred a known coachman and would wait for a familiar old vehicle rather than take a stranger.

One much-contested line was Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise, a lucrative route that in 1822 supported competing enterprises. Touchard tried to monopolize it by lowering fares and improving coaches, but short-run lines serving towns like Saint-Denis and Pierrefitte remained productive. On the main road toward England, a turning at a place called La Cave leads to the delightful Oise valley and the little town of Isle-Adam. Isle-Adam, near Nogent and Parmain—villages famed for quarries whose stone built many Paris structures—had fine sites and châteaux but was reached only by a detour: from La Cave the paved road made by the Princes of Conti turned six miles off the main line and ended at Isle-Adam, a divergence a speculator would not take. Thus, in 1822 the Conti highway was the only road to Isle-Adam, and two local proprietors reigned on the route, beloved by the countryfolk and often preferred even by those on the main road as far as La Cave.

These two proprietors maintained a friendly rivalry; when one left Isle-Adam the other returned from Paris. In the capital they economized by sharing the same yard, hotel, stable, coach-house, office, and clerk. Their hotel, at the corner of the rue d’Enghien—the Lion d’Argent—was long accustomed to lodging coachmen and vehicles. Although their lines advertised fixed hours, both proprietors were indulgent—a practice winning peasant gratitude and sometimes embarrassing strangers. The afternoon trip listed for four might not go until half-past; the morning at eight seldom left before nine. In summer the rules relaxed further for country customers; this elasticity sometimes let a driver take two fares for one place if a countryman arrived early and sat in a seat booked by a late traveler. They excused such practices by citing hard times, winter losses, the need for new coaches, or adherence to printed tariffs—documents displayed only when a traveler demanded them.

Pierrotin, about forty and a father, had been discharged from the cavalry at the great demobilization of 1815 and succeeded his father, who had long driven a coucou between Paris and Isle-Adam. He married an inn-keeper’s daughter and expanded the business into a regular service noted for intelligence and a certain military precision. Active and decisive, Pierrotin’s weather-worn face betrayed sly shrewdness; he had learned the art of speech from life and could soften his rough voice for bourgeois passengers. His military schooling left him respect for social superiority and obedience to the upper classes; he mingled little with the lesser bourgeoisie and always respected women. By "trundling the world," he came to regard his human passengers as parcels requiring less attention than cargo—the essential object of a coaching business.

Alert to postwar changes, Pierrotin refused to be outclassed. Since the previous summer he had talked of a large coach ordered from Farry, Breilmann, and Company—renowned makers who had adopted square English springs—an addition prompted by an influx of travelers. Pierrotin’s establishment consisted of two vehicles. One, used in winter and the only one he reported to the tax-gatherer, was the coucou inherited from his father. Its rounded body provided two hard metallic seats that, by stretching the law, might hold six; a removable wooden bar Pierrotin called "a back" divided the seats and annoyed passengers. Though each seat was legally for three, it was not uncommon to see eight persons jammed onto the two seats. Pierrotin maintained travelers were more comfortable in a compact mass; when fewer occupied a seat they bumped and risked hitting their hats against the roof on jolting roads.

In front was a wooden bench for the driver, where three travelers—called "rabbits"—could perch; Pierrotin sometimes placed four rabbits on the bench and sat on a box beneath the coach-body, which he kept full of straw and parcels. The coucou was painted yellow with a barber’s-blue band and bore in silver "Isle-Adam, Paris" and "Line to Isle-Adam." On great occasions it could carry three more passengers in a square compartment under an awning—allowed only for regulars and avoided near gendarmerie posts where overloads might be reported. Thus on busy nights Pierrotin’s coucou could "trundle" fifteen travelers, pairing Rougeot, his stout old horse, with Bichette, a small mare of which he was fond.

The other vehicle was a comical four-wheel coach holding seventeen though legally allowed fourteen. It rattled so that Isle-Adam folk would say, "Here comes Pierrotin!" when he had barely emerged from the forest. It had an "interior" for six, a front cabriolet called the "coupe" closed with awkward sashes, and an "imperial" holding six under leather curtains. Pierrotin sat on a nearly hidden seat below the coupe. He paid tax only on the coucou, rated for six, taking out special permits when he ran the four-wheeler—common practice, since taxes on vehicles had been timidly enforced and proprietors often cheated. Often in summer the two coaches ran together, carrying thirty-two passengers though Pierrotin paid tax on six. On lucky days the convoy left Faubourg Saint-Denis at half-past four and reached Isle-Adam by ten at night, proclaiming "We went at a fine pace!" To do the twenty-seven miles in five hours he sometimes omitted stops at smaller villages like Saint-Brice, Moisselles, and La Cave.

The Lion d’Argent’s deep plot extended back through a long courtyard to the stables; its passage under the second floor could shelter several coaches. In 1822 the offices of all lines starting there were kept by the innkeeper’s wife, who received fares, booked passengers, and stowed packages in her vast kitchen—a patriarchal system travelers accepted. If early they waited beneath the kitchen chimney, in the passage, or in the nearby Café de l’Echiquier.

On an early autumn Saturday in 1822 Pierrotin stood with hands in his blouse beneath the Lion d’Argent porte-cochere, watching the courtyard and stables. Daumartin’s diligence had just gone, and it was past eight. Pierrotin fretted: only four passengers were booked on a fine Saturday. He told his hostler to harness anyway, lamenting the hard fate of coachmen. The hostler noted there was scarcely room left but the cabriolet; Pierrotin reminded him of the new coach arriving the next day and insisted he needed at least eighteen passengers for it. He boasted it would make Touchard envious—painted red and gold and drawing three horses; he had bought a mate for Rougeot and planned a showy team.

Yet Pierrotin’s buoyant talk disguised anxiety. The coach-builders required a two-thousand-franc preliminary payment; Pierrotin had exhausted resources and credit—his wife, father-in-law, and friends had bled for him. He had already paid two thousand and still owed another thousand plus five hundred for Rougeot’s mate and three hundred for new harnesses on three months’ credit. Driven by despair and vanity, he had publicly announced the coach would start the next day, hoping the builders would accept fifteen hundred instead of the two thousand five hundred still due. He considered asking Monsieur Moreau, steward at Presles, to take his note for six months.

At that moment a footman in livery, carrying a leather portmanteau and returning from the Touchard establishment where he had gone too late for places, asked whether Pierrotin would wait a quarter-hour to take his master. Pierrotin agreed and received the trunk—well buckled, with a brass plate bearing a coat of arms. He wrapped it in hay and put it in the boot, then invited the valet into the Café de l’Echiquier for two absinthes. The valet explained that his master came only once a year to the district and preferred to travel in his own carriage; this time he was going to Presles to see Monsieur Moreau. Pierrotin recognized the name: Monsieur le Comte de Serizy.

"But listen!" said the footman. "There’s a special order. If you have any of the country neighbors in your coach you are not to call him Monsieur le comte; he wants to travel 'en cognito,' and will pay a handsome pourboire if not recognized."

Pierrotin wondered whether the secret journey related to recent local affairs. The valet mentioned a countermanded order: the Daumont carriage had been ordered to leave at seven for Presles, but Monsieur le Comte had changed his mind when a lady called the previous night and again that morning—perhaps from the country. Pierrotin speculated whether the lady had said anything against Monsieur Moreau, whom Pierrotin greatly admired as an honest and capable steward; he had done errands for the Moreaus, received fees, and even fetched and returned their children, and every time he carried someone to the château he was allowed to drive up to the gate—a benefit he valued.

The valet, however, was more cynical. They said Monsieur Moreau had little when Monsieur le Comte made him steward of Presles; the valet hinted that masters were annoying and advised Pierrotin, "Every man for himself. Do what Monsieur le Comte asks, and you will be paid well." He stretched his arm to indicate a generous tip. This prudent counsel cooled Pierrotin’s ardor for borrowing from Moreau. The footman’s presence and his mention of the Comte and his steward introduced the social actors central to the drama about to unfold in Pierrotin’s vehicle, and a rapid glance at the lives of the Comte de Serizy and his steward, Moreau, is necessary to understand what is to follow.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Monsieur Huguet de Serisy descends from the celebrated président Huguet, ennobled under François I; the family arms and motto—“i, semper melius eris,” a pun yielding Serisy—reflect the restrained pride of bourgeois magistrates who rose into the nobility.

The present Comte de Serizy’s father was a parlement president before the Revolution. At twenty-two, in 1787, the count became councillor of State at the Grand Council and wrote important memoranda on diplomatic questions. He did not emigrate during the Revolution, remaining on the Serizy estate near Arpajon where his father’s rank protected them. After nursing his dying father in 1794, he was elected to the Council of Five Hundred and took legislative duties to distract his grief. After 18th Brumaire, like many old parliamentary families, he accepted Napoleon’s overtures: appointed to the Council of State, he reformed a disordered department and became useful to the regime.

Soon he was called to a ministry, raised to the rank of count and senator by the Emperor, and served as proconsul to two kingdoms. In 1806, at forty, he married the sister of the former Marquis de Ronquerolles, a young widow of General Gaubert who had left her property. The marriage suited his rank, increased his fortune, and linked him to the former Marquis de Rouvre, later a count and chamberlain under the Emperor.

Weary and ill in 1814, the Comte de Serizy resigned and came to Paris; Napoleon at first suspected him of defection but accepted his illness as cause. Though never disgraced, Serizy felt wronged by Napoleon. When the Bourbons returned, Louis XVIII—whom the count regarded as his rightful sovereign—trusted him with private affairs and made him a cabinet minister. On March 20 he refused to go to Ghent; he told Napoleon he remained faithful to the Bourbons, would not accept a peerage during the Hundred Days, and stayed at his estate. After Napoleon’s final fall he resumed office as privy councillor, became vice-president of the Council of State, and acted as liquidator of claims and indemnities demanded by foreign powers.

Unambitious personally but influential, little of importance occurred without his counsel; he avoided court and salons. A life of incessant work had formed his habit: rising at four to toil until midday, attending duties in the afternoon, and sleeping by nine. He held the grand cross of the Legion of Honor and several foreign orders; public honors mattered little to him—his devotion to duty had a deeper, private motive.

He had loved his wife before marrying her; that passion endured through the private unhappiness of wedding a composed widow who continued to live with great liberty while he treated her with indulgent deference. His constant toil shielded him from pain he kept secret with a diplomat’s care. He knew jealousy would seem ridiculous in a society that would not credit an old statesman’s conjugal passion. One painful fact was that his labor had caused a skin malady that repelled his wife. Kind and considerate, he let her be mistress of herself and their home. She received all Paris, passed long hours in the country, and returned as if still a widow. He managed her fortune and supplied her luxury like a steward. The countess admired his mind and could soothe him by approving his endeavors; an hour of talk in his study made him pliable. Like old seigneurs, he protected her so loyally that any disrespect toward her would have seemed an unforgivable injury. The world admired him and his wife benefited; she was often ungrateful, yet sometimes mingled charm and soft kindness that patched his wounds.

This private humiliation and that devotion explain why a minister of State would undertake a sudden incognito journey.

A dispute about land near Presles precipitated the journey. A rich farmer of Beaumont-sur-Oise named Lég­er leased and cultivated the Moulineaux farm, whose ninety-six parcels projected into and troubled the Comte de Serizy’s Presles estate. The farm belonged to a bourgeois, Margueron, and the lease of Lég­er—made in 1799—was about to expire; Margueron refused Lég­er’s offers to renew. Monsieur de Serizy wished to buy the farm to remove the annoyance of enclosed fields. He was told Margueron’s chief ambition was to have his only son—then merely a tax-gatherer—appointed special collector at Beaumont. Lég­er, knowing he could resell parcels to the count at a large profit, planned to buy and resell.

Two days before the narrative, anxious to settle the matter, Monsieur de Serizy summoned his notary Alexandre Crottat and his lawyer Derville to examine the situation. Though Derville and Crottat distrusted the count’s steward Moreau—prompted by a disturbing letter—the count defended Moreau, who had served him faithfully for seventeen years.

Derville advised direct action: go to Presles himself and invite Margueron to dinner; Crottat would prepare a deed with blanks for the property description and titles; bring part of the purchase money as a Bank of France check; and secure the appointment of the son to the collectorship. If not settled immediately, the farm would slip away. Crottat agreed, and at his valet Pierrotin’s suggestion the count adopted the plan. The preceding evening he had sent Moreau a line by diligence to Beaumont, telling him to invite Margueron to dinner so they might conclude the purchase.

Before this affair, the count had ordered the château of Presles restored and refurnished; for a year Grindot, a fashionable architect, had visited weekly. The count intended, while concluding the purchase, to examine restorations and new furniture as a surprise for his wife when he brought her to Presles; he had put personal pride into the work. Why the count, who planned to drive there in his own carriage in the evening, set out early the next morning incognito in Pierrotin’s coucou merits explanation.

Some words on the steward Moreau are necessary. Moreau was the son of a provincial attorney who became syndic-attorney at Versailles during the Revolution; his efforts almost saved the Serizys’ lives and property. The elder Moreau belonged to Danton’s party, was pursued by Robespierre, and was executed at Versailles. The son, inheriting his father’s connections and doctrines, was implicated in a conspiracy against the First Consul. Monsieur de Serizy, wishing to repay past services, enabled Moreau—then under sentence of death—to escape; in 1804 he sought and obtained his pardon, offered him a government post, and finally made him private secretary.

Some time after the count’s marriage Moreau fell in love with the countess’s waiting-woman and married her. To avoid the annoyances of court life, he asked to manage the Presles estate, where his wife could act as lady and neither would suffer wounded pride. The count wanted a trustworthy man at Presles because his wife preferred Serizy, an estate only fifteen miles from Paris. For three or four years Moreau thus held the key of the count’s affairs; he was intelligent, had studied law in his father’s office, and the count granted his request.

“You can never advance in life,” the count told Moreau, “for you have broken your neck; but you can be happy, and I will take care that you are so.”

He gave Moreau a salary of three thousand francs, a lodge near the château, all wood needed from estate timber, oats, hay, and straw for two horses, and a right to the garden’s produce. A sub-prefect was not as well provided.

For the first eight years Moreau managed the estate conscientiously and took interest in it. The count, visiting now and then, admired Moreau’s loyalty and rewarded him liberally. But after the birth of Moreau’s third child he felt secure and ceased to see his advantages as gifts. About 1816 the steward, who had until then taken only what he needed, accepted twenty-five thousand francs from a wood-merchant as inducement to lease timber cutting for twelve years. Moreau argued he had no pension, was father of a family, and the count owed him that sum as a gift after ten years’ management; with sixty thousand francs saved, this would let him buy a farm worth 125,000 francs in Champagne, above Isle-Adam on the Oise. Political events prevented the count and neighbors from noting this investment, made in Madame Moreau’s name as if she had inherited from an aunt.

Once he tasted possession, Moreau—while keeping an appearance of integrity—seized opportunities to increase his fortune clandestinely; the interests of his three children served as a poultice to his conscience. By local custom he shared with the count in profits due to his management, but this swelled his resources. Presles returned a net revenue of seventy thousand francs; locally people said, “Monsieur de Serizy has a second self in Moreau.” Prudent, Moreau invested yearly after 1817, piling up savings secretly. He often played the poor man so well that the count sent his sons to the school Henri IV. At present Moreau held 120,000 francs invested in Consolidated thirds, paying five percent and quoted at eighty francs. These hidden 120,000 and his Champagne farm, enlarged by purchases, amounted to about 280,000 francs, giving an income of some 16,000 francs.

Such was the steward’s position when the Comte de Serizy wished to buy the Moulineaux farm—ownership indispensable to his comfort. Père Lég­er wished to buy only to resell it to the count at a handsome advance. To secure that sum he sought an understanding with Moreau. Three days before the crucial Sunday Lég­er talked with the steward and showed that Moreau could persuade the count to invest at two and a half percent and thus pocket forty thousand francs off Lég­er’s offer.

“I tell you what,” Moreau said to his wife, “if I make fifty thousand francs out of the Moulineaux affair—and I certainly shall, for the count will give me ten thousand as a fee—we’ll retire to Isle-Adam and live in the Pavillon de Nogent.”

“That will suit me,” his wife replied. “The Dutchman there has put it in order and now returns to India; he might let us have it for thirty thousand francs.”

“We shall be close to Champagne,” said Moreau. “I hope to buy the farm and mill of Mours for a hundred thousand francs. That would give us ten thousand a year. Nogent is one of the most delightful residences in the valley; and we should still have an income of ten thousand from the Grand-Livre.”

“But why not ask for the post of juge-de-paix at Isle-Adam? That would give influence and fifteen hundred a year.”

“Well, I did think of it.”

With such plans, Moreau—hearing the count was coming to Presles and had asked him to invite Margueron to dinner—sent an express to the count’s head-valet enclosing a letter to his master, which the messenger failed to deliver before the count retired. Augustin, however, placed it on his master’s desk in the morning. In the letter Moreau begged the count not to come, and to trust him entirely; he added that Margueron was no longer willing to sell the whole at once and spoke of cutting the farm into lots. It was necessary to circumvent this plan, perhaps by employing a third party to make the purchase.

Everybody has enemies. Moreau and his wife had offended a retired officer, Monsieur de Reybert, and his wife, who lived near Presles. From pin-prick speeches matters had advanced to dagger thrusts. Monsieur de Reybert sought vengeance; he aimed to make Moreau lose his situation and gain it himself. Reybert had spied on the steward for two years. The same conveyance that took Moreau’s letter to the count carried Madame de Reybert to Paris, whom her husband had despatched. There she so earnestly asked to see the count that, though sent away at nine, she was ushered into his study the next morning at seven.

“Monsieur,” she said, “we cannot write anonymous letters, so I have come in person. I am Madame de Reybert, née de Corroy. My husband is a retired officer with a pension of six hundred francs, and we live at Presles, where your steward has offered us insult after insult, though we are persons of good station. Monsieur de Reybert, who retired in 1816 after twenty years, has watched your steward for three years, aware of his dishonesty and intending to have him dismissed. Moreau plans to get an extra hundred thousand francs out of you, to be divided between the notary, the farmer Lég­er, and Moreau. You have written Moreau to invite Margueron, and you are going to Presles today; but Margueron will be ill, and Lég­er is so certain of buying he is now in Paris with the money. If we have enlightened you, and you want an upright steward, take my husband; though noble, he will serve you as he has served the State. Your steward has made two hundred and fifty thousand francs from his place; he is not to be pitied.”

The count thanked Madame de Reybert coldly, for he despised backbiting; but he remembered Derville’s doubts and felt shaken. He then saw Moreau’s letter and read it. In its assurances of devotion and its implied blame of the count for distrust, he read the truth.

“Corruption has come to him with fortune—as it always does!” he said.

The count questioned Madame de Reybert, partly for information but more to observe her, and wrote a short note to his notary telling him not to send his head-clerk to Presles as requested, but to come himself in time for dinner.

“Though Monsieur le comte,” Madame de Reybert concluded, “may have judged me unfavorably for acting without my husband, he ought to be convinced we obtained this information in a natural and honorable manner; the most sensitive conscience cannot take exception to it.”

Madame de Reybert stood erect: a face seamed with smallpox, a flat spare figure, eager eyes, fair hair plastered on an anxious forehead, and a plain dress. The count recognized the wife of some poor, half-pay captain: a puritan, earnest in virtue but mindful of comfort and eager for a good situation. He had thought of entrusting estate management to some retired officer, about whom he could obtain exact information from the minister of war.

“Madame,” he resumed, ringing for his valet, “return to Presles this afternoon with my notary, who is going down for dinner and to whom I have recommended you. Here is his address. I am going myself secretly to Presles, and will send for Monsieur de Reybert to come and speak to me.”

Thus Monsieur de Serizy’s journey by public conveyance and his valet’s injunction to conceal his name and rank had reason: a minister intent on confronting a steward’s possible treachery and protecting his estate and honor—both public and painfully private.

################################################
As Pierrotin emerged from the Café de l’Échiquier after tending the valet, he noticed in the gateway of the Lion d’Argent a lady and a young man he recognized as patrons. The lady, neck stretched and anxious, searched for him; she wore a black-dyed silk gown, a brown bonnet, an old cashmere shawl, raw-silk stockings, low shoes and carried a straw bag. Once beautiful and about forty, her blue eyes had lost the fire of happiness and bore the look of long renunciation. Her dress and manner betrayed a mother wholly devoted to home and son: the bonnet’s faded strings and the shawl fastened with a broken needle revealed thrift. She waited impatiently to entrust her son—no doubt travelling alone for the first time—to Pierrotin’s care.

Her son completed the picture: an olive-green coat with sleeves too short, blue patched trousers, and a bright patch visible when the coat-tails parted. “Don’t rub your gloves that way, you’ll spoil them,” she admonished as Pierrotin appeared. “Is this the conductor? Ah! Pierrotin, is it you?” she cried, leaving the boy to speak with the coachman. Pierrotin greeted her with a mixture of respect and familiarity. She asked him to take good care of Oscar—he was travelling alone to Monsieur Moreau. Pierrotin, curious, probed whether Madame Moreau was willing to receive the boy; Madame Clapart answered, with resignation, that life would not be all roses for Oscar but his future required the journey. This reply struck Pierrotin: he feared for the steward, felt some unease, but did not voice it to Madame Clapart, who feared to ask too much lest she harm her son.

As they talked lightly of weather and stopping places, Pierrotin’s relationship with the Claparts required explanation. Several times a month he met the steward Moreau near La Cave. When the carriage arrived, Moreau signalled a gardener who, with Pierrotin’s help, loaded baskets of fruit, vegetables, poultry, eggs, butter and game. The steward paid fares and added money for tolls if the baskets contained dutiable goods. These parcels were never addressed; the first time, Moreau had given Madame Clapart’s address verbally to the discreet Pierrotin, requesting secrecy. Expecting to find a pretty intrigue, Pierrotin had gone to number 7 rue de la Cerisaie and found Madame Clapart instead.

Public drivers see many homes and learn many secrets; social accident often places them in intimate proximity. Pierrotin, not a close observer, remained puzzled for months about Moreau and Madame Clapart’s exact relation. Although lodgings were not dear in the Arsenal quarter, Madame Clapart lived on the third floor at the end of a courtyard in a house once belonging to a great family. Her flat, panelled with ancient carvings, had three rooms—dining-room, salon, and bedroom; above were the kitchen and Oscar’s attic room. Opposite the entrance on the landing was a door into a rough stone tower and staircase; that back room was where Moreau slept when in Paris.

The room where Pierrotin deposited the hampers contained six wooden chairs with straw seats, a table, and a sideboard; the curtains were discolored. The salon held shabby Empire furniture; the bedroom matched the salon and dining-room. Painted woodwork blurred the mouldings; floors, never waxed, had the gray tone of boarding schools. Pierrotin noticed the china and glass betrayed poverty, yet forks and spoons were silver—some relic of a vanished past. Monsieur Clapart, in a shabby surtout and broken slippers, always wore green spectacles and revealed a pointed skull with a few dirty threads of hair. Timorous yet tyrannical, he looked wan. In that north-facing apartment, with only a vine and a well to look on, Madame Clapart bore herself like a queen and moved like a woman unaccustomed to walking. Often she slipped Pierrotin a twelve-sous piece with charming thanks. Pierrotin had never seen Oscar at home because the boy was always at school when his business took him there.

The sad story Pierrotin could not learn from the portress—who only knew the Claparts paid 250 francs a year and had no servant but a charwoman—was as follows. There seldom exists a criminal wholly criminal, nor a dishonest nature entirely dishonest; Moreau, if guilty in some ways, was persistently kind to a woman whose favours he once claimed and in whose house he had hidden when condemned to death in 1797. This woman, famed under the Directory for a liaison with a powerful protector, had married through that protection a purveyor named Husson, who made millions from government contracts but whom Napoleon ruined in 1802. Husson went insane and threw himself into the Seine, leaving Madame Husson pregnant. Moreau, allied with her, had been condemned to death and could not marry her; forced to leave France, he nevertheless watched over her.

Madame Husson, at twenty-two, later married a clerk named Clapart, aged twenty-seven, reputed to be promising. Clapart, coarse and handsome but lacking intelligence, expected wealth but found none; his Treasury post provided only a modest salary. When Moreau returned as secretary to the Comte de Serizy he learned of Madame Husson’s plight and obtained, before his own marriage, an appointment for her as head waiting-woman to Madame Mère, the Emperor’s mother. Still, Clapart was never promoted. Ruined in 1815 by the fall of the Empire, the once brilliant Aspasia of the Directory had no resources beyond Clapart’s meagre clerk’s salary and the small help Moreau could discretely provide. Moreau, the only protector of a woman he had once known in opulence, obtained a half-scholarship for her son, Oscar Husson, at the collège Henri IV, and sent her regularly, via Pierrotin, supplies from his estate at Presles as he could decently offer to a household in distress.

Oscar was his mother’s whole life and future. The poor woman’s only fault was exaggerated tenderness for her boy—the bête-noire of his stepfather. Oscar unfortunately had a foolish, overweening conceit his mother did not perceive despite her husband’s sarcasm. This conceit worried Moreau so much that he begged Madame Clapart to send Oscar to him for a month so he might judge the lad’s character and find a suitable career; Moreau even thought of proposing Oscar as his successor to the count. The sources of Oscar’s vanity were evident: he had been born in Madame Mère’s household and had childhood impressions of imperial splendour. His imagination retained those images, and maternal tales of a golden past nurtured his boastfulness. As a scholarship pupil among paying students he had borne humiliations that hardened the conceit unless a scholar could command respect by physical superiority. This mixture of departed splendour, faded beauty, blind maternal love, and heroic suffering made Madame Clapart a pathetic figure in Paris.

Pierrotin, incapable of understanding Moreau’s real attachment to her and of the past intimacy that bound her to him, did not communicate his sense of danger to the steward. He preferred to think of his own livelihood and obey the “chefs de file”—men of rank. His routine, deferential “Very good, madame,” hid a desire to escape long instructions.

“You will be sure to place the packages so they can’t get wet,” Madame Clapart said.

“I’ve a hood,” Pierrotin replied. “Besides, see with what care they are placed.”

“Oscar, don’t stay more than two weeks, no matter how much they may ask you,” she continued. “You can’t please Madame Moreau; besides, be home by the end of September. We go to Belleville to your uncle Cardot.”

“Yes, mamma,” Oscar answered, evidently annoyed at being lectured on the threshold of the Lion d’Argent. The mother, forgetful of the street, embraced Oscar and took a little roll from her basket. “Tiens! you were forgetting your roll and the chocolate! My child, don’t take anything at the inns; they’ll charge you ten times the value.”

Two well-dressed young men a few years older—without a mother clinging to them—witnessed the scene and mocked the word “mamma,” as if infantilizing Oscar. Oscar cried “Good-bye, mother!” in a tone of impatience. Madame Clapart, perhaps lacking tact because all absorbing sentiments contain egoism, spoke too loudly and seemed anxious to display her tenderness. “What is the matter with you, Oscar?” she asked. “Listen—do not talk and show off; keep your tongue in check. You are not advanced enough to judge persons; there is nothing more dangerous than speaking in public conveyances. In a diligence well-bred persons always keep silence.”

The two young men had walked to the other end of the establishment and returned with tapping heels. To rid himself of his mother, Oscar took a measure that revealed how vanity could stimulate cunning. “Mamma,” he said, “you are standing in a draught, and you may take cold. Besides, I am going to get into the coach.” The lad touched a tender spot; his mother caught him to her bosom, kissed him as if he were starting on a long voyage, and went with him to the vehicle with tears. “Don’t forget to give five francs to the servants when you come away,” she said; “write me three times during the fifteen days; behave properly, and remember all I have told you. You have linen enough; don’t send any to the wash. Above all, remember Monsieur Moreau’s kindness; mind him as you would a father, and follow his advice.”

As Oscar got into the coach his blue woollen stockings showed as his trousers pulled up, and the new patch showed through his parted coat-tails. The two young men’s smiles, noticing these signs of respectable indigence, wounded his vanity. “The first place was engaged for Oscar,” said his mother to Pierrotin. “Take the back seat,” she told the boy with a fond smile.

Oscar regretted that poverty had destroyed his mother’s beauty and subdued her dress. One young man, wearing top-boots and spurs, nudged the other to notice Oscar’s mother; the other twirled his moustache as if to say, “Rather a pretty figure!” Oscar wished they would leave. The two young men—one named Amaury—began to chatter and taunt. “He said mamma!” one cried, and their half-toned remarks cut him. Oscar compared his companion’s fine clothes with his own and felt that his mother’s shabby toilet counted for much. He fancied the other’s pockets full of francs when he noticed a gold chain and watch.

Living in rue de la Cerisaie since 1815, brought to and from school by his stepfather and seeing only his mother’s poverty, Oscar had little comparison. Brought up on Moreau’s advice, he seldom went to the theatre except to the Ambigu-Comique. His stepfather still wore his watch with a heavy gold chain and old finery Oscar called the “ne plus ultra.” He was bewildered by the revelation of superior, negligent elegance: spotless gloves and a gold-headed switch cane twirled with grace. Oscar had reached the sensitive quarter of adolescence when little things cause immense joys and miseries—a period when youth prefers appearance and torments itself about dress. A nineteen-year-old only child adored at home envying a twenty-two-year-old in a frogged surtout is a peccadillo common to many.

But Oscar’s peccadillo deepened into ill feeling. He felt humiliated, angry with the youth he envied, and secretly desired to prove himself as good. The two young fellows paced the gate and stables, looking at Oscar curled in his corner. He pretended indifference and began to hum a liberal chorus, provoking Amaury to sneer that perhaps Oscar was one of the Opera chorus. This exasperated Oscar, who burst up and called to Pierrotin, “When do we start?”

“Presently,” Pierrotin answered, whip in hand, looking toward rue d’Enghien. At that moment a young man arrived with a true gamin followed by a porter with a hand-cart. The cart’s contents—trunks, buckets, brushes, oddly shaped boxes and innumerable packages—were stowed so quickly Oscar, smiling across the street at his mother, noticed none of the paraphernalia that might reveal his new companion’s trade.

The gamin, about sixteen, wore a gray blouse buckled with a leather belt, a jaunty cap, curly brown hair, a black-silk cravat around a very white neck, bright gray eyes and a vivacious, mischievous face—Figaro-like. His gestures and mocking eye betrayed a lively intellect born of early vocation. He called himself “in style.” The companion, a slight pale young man with thick black hair worn fantastically and an enormous forehead suggesting precocious intellect, looked like an artist: a shabby American-green surtout, a black waistcoat buttoned to the throat, a scarlet neckerchief peeking, black trousers and muddy boots. His face seemed harassed and hollowed as if by chronic sickness or grief. With a rapid look he took in the Lion d’Argent scene and glanced at Mistigris’s satirical stare.

“Charming!” said Mistigris.

“Yes, very,” replied the master.

“We seem to have got here too early,” Mistigris continued. “Couldn’t we get a mouthful somewhere? My stomach, like Nature, abhors a vacuum.”

“Have we time for a cup of coffee?” the artist asked Pierrotin.

“Yes, but don’t be long,” Pierrotin replied. “Good; that means a quarter of an hour,” said Mistigris, and they disappeared. Nine o’clock chimed in the hotel kitchen. Georges, seated inside handling a portfolio under the seat cushion, complained about the wheels and the horse. Pierrotin explained they were going to harness Bichette while the gentlemen took coffee. Georges worried about places; he and Oscar had claimed seats.

As they prepared, Père Léger arrived—a stout man of perhaps two hundred and fifty pounds, square-backed with a protuberant stomach, powdered pigtail, little blue linen coat, white gaiters above the knee, velveteen breeches with silver buckles, and hob-nailed shoes. He carried a polished stick with a large knob. “At your service,” he said with a face like Louis XVIII, fat rubicund cheeks and a large nose. He was good-humored and only travelling to La Cave.

Pierrotin still looked rue d’Enghien up and down, whip in hand, reluctant to take his hard seat where Mistigris fretted. “If you expect someone else, I am not the last,” said the count, as Monsieur de Serizy—whom Pierrotin had fetched—arrived another way. “Père Léger,” Pierrotin suggested, “will you give your place to Monsieur le comte? That will balance the carriage better.” Georges protested the habit of moving for late comers. Mistigris and his master bantered about respect and seats; Mistigris quipped and sprang about like a frog. The Comte de Serizy, taken for a bourgeois named Lecomte by the coach’s company, answered little and assumed a good-natured air. He sat beside Pierrotin in front.

The count had a burning red face with inflamed blotches set against white hair that harmed his noble air; his green-gray eyes revealed shrewdness and wisdom. His flat face and depressed nose, hat hiding his forehead, made him odd. He wore a long blue overcoat buttoned to the throat, a white cravat, cotton wool in his ears, and a high shirt-collar. No decoration adorned him; nothing betrayed him as a peer and a useful statesman. Père Léger had never seen the count; the count knew the farmer only by name. When the count entered the carriage he glanced about, affronting Georges and Oscar but truly looking for the head-clerk of his notary, in case he, too, had been forced into Pierrotin’s vehicle. Feeling reassured by Oscar, Père Léger assumed his note had reached his notary Alexandre Crottat in time to keep the clerk from leaving.

When they reached the steep faubourg Saint-Denis at rue de la Fidélité, Pierrotin suggested the farmer might disembark. “Ten louis for you if you keep the secret of my being here as I told you before,” the count whispered to Pierrotin, who winked, thinking of the thousand francs he might earn. Oscar and Georges stayed in the coach. Georges threatened to pay his fare and take a post-horse if Pierrotin did not go faster. Père Léger reassured him they would arrive by mid-day. Pierrotin promised punctuality, and Mistigris mocked the count’s supposed preferences. Thus, amid joking, impatience and the shuffle of passengers, the diligence prepared to set off with its motley company: Oscar inwardly wounded, his mother left behind with last admonitions, Pierrotin balancing duty and profit, a sly artist with his rapin, a complacent farmer and the discreet Comte de Serizy hidden among them.

Those who travel in public conveyances know strangers do not at once begin to talk; there is first a period of mutual examination. Once each thinks he has sized up his companions, the most talkative starts and the conversation livens—especially in French stage-coaches, where everyone is eager to laugh and show wit.

A twenty-two-year-old, Georges, was tempted to display his cleverness. He had sized up his fellow passengers: the count as a second-rate manufacturer or chandler; the shabby young man with Mistigris as insignificant; Oscar as a ninny; Pere Léger as an excellent dupe. He resolved to amuse himself at their expense and, as the coucou descended from La Chapelle toward Saint-Denis, ran over possible personae—Etienne, Béranger, a Carbonaro, the son of Marshal Ney, a disguised Russian prince, Cousin, Lord Byron—finally choosing a swaggering, Eastern-adventurer role. Mistigris’s banter and Pierrotin’s exclamations opened the talk.

Assuming the air of someone just returned from the Levant, Georges said he had been ill three months and had only just left his bed after a “germ” of suppressed plague. This produced alarm and gibes. He launched into tales of Egypt and Greece: service under Ali, pacha of Janina, a quarrel with him, and Oriental living that had disordered his liver.

When Pere Léger asked if he had been a soldier and how old he was, Georges answered twenty-nine and sketched a patched military history: enlisted in 1813, present at Hanau, promoted to sergeant-major, made sub-lieutenant at Montereau and decorated by the Emperor; he hinted at service at Mont-Saint-Jean in 1815, expressed disgust with France, then said he went abroad with comrades to serve a pacha in Egypt. He declared he wouldn’t renounce his religion for Islam and told of receiving a thousand talari from the pacha.

The passengers were captivated. Georges boasted of being grandson of the famous Czerni-Georges, who had fought the Porte; his father had died in Paris leaving his mother pregnant. Their property had been seized; his mother had remarried a purveyor, Monsieur Yung, now dead; she had left Georges estranged from his stepfather—supposed reasons for his enlisting as a private in 1813. Back in Greece, Ali Tebelen welcomed him as descendant of Czerni-Georges; Georges claimed Ali gave him a harem—five wives and ten slaves—but the women were stupid and the harem little better than a herd of goats; he preferred French grisettes.

They were entering Saint-Denis and stopped at a tavern famed for its cheese-cakes. The count, suspecting the youth had left something in the coach, returned and found beneath the cushion a portfolio labeled “Maitre Crottat, notary.” He took out the deed of sale for the farm at Moulineaux, pocketed it, and went into the inn, concluding Georges was Crottat’s second clerk sent as head-clerk. Georges meanwhile had paid for cheese-cakes and wine for Pere Léger and Oscar, basking in their admiration, and tried to pick up Mistigris and his master.

When Pierrotin returned, Georges piled on: he had been made commander of Ali’s cavalry and had distinguished himself against Chosrew pacha, cutting his line and receiving Ali’s gratitude and costly gifts. Back at Janina Ali proposed degrading acts—drowning a wife, renouncing his religion—but Georges refused and fled. He claimed Chosrew later captured Ali by sea with English help; Chosrew had become a naval commander and, when he recognized Georges at Smyrna, nearly executed him. Monsieur de Rivière, the French ambassador, intervened, saving him and recovering two thousand talari, though most treasures were taken by the admiral.

Georges said he had witnessed the execution of Smyrna’s governor and then gone to Spain, becoming aide-de-camp to Mina with the rank of colonel for the constitutional cause. The count reacted sharply, warning that the Court of Peers was then investigating conspiracies and punishing French soldiers who bore arms against France or engaged in foreign intrigues. He added that if he were a magistrate he would have ordered the gendarmes at Pierrefitte to arrest the supposed aide-de-camp and summon the coach’s occupants to testify. The remark silenced Georges as they passed the gendarmerie guard-house.

Conversation turned to cultivation in Eastern lands. Georges, with his habitual flippancy, described them as too busy cutting each other’s heads off to farm like the French: onions, rice, tobacco, dates, rugs at Smyrna, Latakia tobacco, opium from poppies—customs that left the fellahs barely enough after taxes. Pere Léger, puzzled, asked about sheep and fields; Georges offered evasive explanations about crops growing with little cultivation. He mocked bureaucracy and contrasted despotic plunder with French stamped papers. The count, drawing from this talk, advised landowners to sell, warning taxes in Italy were heavy too.

Mistigris and Schinner—the supposed painter—joined in. Schinner claimed to be a humble painter returned from Rome after winning the grand prix and sent to Italy at the government’s expense. He insisted he’d go merely as a decorator to a château. The count indulged this mock-importance of artists, arguing that great ceilings and the love that inspired them were worth sacrifices. Oscar, jealous and awkward, listened, eager to be somebody.

Pierrotin, the driver, joked about carrying sovereigns and the pourboires he expected, and fretted about a debt for a new coach. The passengers exchanged banter about ceilings, Venice, Rome, and a near-murder Schinner claimed to have endured there. Mistigris hinted Lord Byron had been involved; Schinner demurred, not wishing to speak of Byron.

Schinner then related a long account of Dalmatia, Zara, and a Greek beauty named Zena. He had stayed with an apothecary, seen Zena on a neighboring balcony, and, despite an ugly duenna and a jealous seventy-year-old husband—an Uscoque pirate with millions, a missing ear and eye—had exchanged looks and stolen walks with her. He detailed the dangerous delicacy of their flirtation: ramparts, the duenna who wouldn’t leave, and growing tension. He finally entered the pirate’s house and found Zena; they walked the ramparts together. One day he returned to find the street full of people; he was seized, garroted, gagged, and dragged before magistrates accused of murdering the pirate. He was stoned and nearly killed by a maddened populace.

He learned that Zena had poisoned the pirate with opium to free herself for walks with him; the duenna had overdone the dose, implicating him. He insisted he knew nothing of the murder. The mayor and Austrian commissary of police released him with orders to return to Rome, while Zena, who had let the heirs take most of the Uscoque’s fortune, was sentenced to two years’ seclusion in a convent; Schinner hoped to paint her portrait someday. Mistigris complained that Schinner had left him penniless in Venice, forcing him to paint cheap portraits to reach Rome.

Georges chimed in with comments on Dalmatian maraschino and the follies of youth. Pere Léger repeatedly asked about farming and crops, receiving brief, often evasive replies. The count maintained a guarded attitude, sometimes smiling at fashionable braggadocio and sometimes revealing surprising knowledge—for example, when Georges mentioned names tied to Smyrna that the count recognized, making the youth seem less sure of his invented recollections.

Throughout the journey the characters parried between bluster and information: Georges’s ostentatious Eastern adventures mixed fact and fabrication; the count, discreet but observant, suspected a clerk posing as a colonel; Schinner’s romance showed the dangers beneath exotic talk; Mistigris supplied cynical comments; Pierrotin kept an eye on profit; Pere Léger asked homely questions; Oscar gaped and yearned to be more. Conversation, prompted by the coach’s enforced intimacy and the French habit of talk, ran on—at times diverting, at times revealing—until they moved on toward Pierrefitte and beyond.

Pierrotin’s coach stopped at Saint-Brice about half-past one for the usual rest. At the innkeeper’s cry—“Here’s Pere Leger”—the travelers prepared for breakfast. Georges, twirling his cane, produced a straw case of cigars and lit one on the threshold, provoking poor Oscar, who coughed and tried to look jaunty. Schinner and Georges showed him how to smoke; the painter and Georges mocked his awkwardness. Mistigris seized Oscar’s cigar and smoked with relish; when Oscar fumed at Georges’s expenditures—Alicante, cheesecakes, cigars—he felt his shabby clothes and patched stockings betrayed him. Mistigris, sharing the embarrassment, revealed his patched socks; the count, standing apart beneath the porte-cochere, smiled at the youngsters’ folly while envying their easy gaiety.

In the kitchen the innkeeper and Pere Leger spoke low about Les Moulineaux. The innkeeper guessed Pere Leger had arranged to buy it and offered to lend money. Pere Leger calmly said he had done it for the count and explained his plan: the King had given him forty thousand “pictures on coin” to present to Moreau; he would buy Les Moulineaux outright for 260,000 francs and then sell it to the Count de Serizy for 360,000, making a neat profit while preserving appearances by letting the count propose him as tenant. The farm currently yielded 6,000 francs; Pere Leger would take it at 7,500 for eighteen years and show it as a sound investment. The innkeeper approved; the count, listening unseen, believed he had found incriminating evidence but wondered whether steward Moreau was implicated or if this was mere talk.

When Pierrotin came out to water the horses, the count, fearing indiscretion, whispered to him to keep his name secret in Saint-Brice and Isle-Adam and promised Pierrotin a thousand francs next morning if he did. Pierrotin, flattered and pale with happiness, agreed. Then, pretending he had a fish to deliver, he refused the innkeeper’s invitation to breakfast and urged Pere Leger to come on; Pere Leger accepted, planning to harness a horse the innkeeper wished to sell and overtake Pierrotin.

Surprisingly, Pierrotin rebridled, and Schinner and Mistigris walked on. When Pierrotin caught up, Pere Leger and Georges appeared, Pere Leger boasting he was the farmer of Les Moulineaux. Conversation resumed: Georges puffed cigars and asked about careers; Oscar, indignant at being called “little young man,” declared himself destined for diplomacy. The others laughed; Schinner mock-coached Oscar in courtly manners. Oscar swelled with pretension, boasting of his family, his private tutor Abbe Loraux, and acquaintance with the Comte de Serizy and his son, whom he rode with—claims that surprised Pere Leger and the others.

The count, interested, asked Oscar about Serizy. Oscar repeated local gossip: the count supposedly suffered a hideous skin disease; his wife, who could not endure him, was the real power; the count loved her but was pitiable as Arnolphe. He advised seeking the marquis rather than the count. The companions laughed at Oscar’s intimate knowledge; the count, shocked by gossip about the Comtesse de Serizy, interposed sharply: he was a friend of her brother, the Marquis de Ronquerolles, and would not tolerate disparagement of the countess. He warned them against speaking ill of people and declared he knew many influential persons. Then, asking Pierrotin to stop at the next inn, he left the coach and took a path through the woods toward what everyone perceived as the chateau.

Pierrotin drove through Moisselles; the little group fell silent. In the inn-yard the count drew Pierrotin aside and asked about Oscar. Pierrotin explained the youth was the son of a poor woman from rue de la Cerisaie, Madame Husson, who often received fruit and game from Presles. Pere Leger teased that Pierrotin fancied Oscar noble, and the three young men were mortified, realizing their boastful talk might have misled the count.

As they neared Presles, boasting and rebuke resumed. Georges warned Oscar that if the man was really the Count de Serizy, he had cause to be ashamed. Oscar, recalling his mother’s counsel and sensing trouble, tried to keep composure. The chateau rose splendid in the valley. The count made light farewells, urged reconciliations and prudence, and told Pierrotin to set him down and fetch him the same time next day, then slipped along a wood-path, leaving his companions embarrassed.

Pere Leger, suddenly recognizing the path to Les Moulineaux, confronted Georges—who quickly slipped away. Pierrotin pulled into the iron gates and discharged the painter’s implements and Oscar’s effects, including a small leather portmanteau, which the concierge’s wife took in charge. Oscar, uncertain and impulsive, flung himself onto a narrow path into the grand courtyard. Pierrotin drove out through the forest road to Isle-Adam, his face aglow at the thought of the thousand francs he expected and the profit due to his secrecy. 

Thus youthful boastfulness, the farmer’s scheme, the count’s discreet surveillance, Pierrotin’s opportunism, and the travelers’ pretensions intersected: Pere Leger’s plotted purchase of Les Moulineaux, Oscar’s bluster, and Pierrotin’s double gain all came to light at Presles, leaving the coach’s occupants in varying states of triumph, confusion, and shame.

Oscar, abashed, skulked behind some trees in the courtyard as Monsieur Moreau appeared on the guard-room portico. The steward, in a long blue overcoat and top-boots, greeted the two painters with a brisk courtesy, whistled for the concierge, and ordered rooms 14 and 15 prepared. He told them Madame Moreau would give the keys, directed the concierge to show them the way and make fires if needed, and extended an invitation to dine at five “as in Paris,” adding that they could enjoy hunting over twelve thousand acres of forest. Oscar and the two artists exchanged looks; Mistigris quipped in Latin, and Oscar followed Moreau through the park.

Moreau, about fifty, dark-complexioned, abrupt but kindly in manner, exercised a decisive authority that always humbled Oscar. He now bore his authority with the steadiness of one used to commanding and, in Oscar’s cultivated timidity, inspired a new unease: Oscar feared Moreau might harm him. Calling to his child to tell his wife that “little Husson” had arrived and that he must go to Les Moulineaux, Moreau sent the boy to fetch Madame Moreau and himself took a short path to the gamekeeper’s house.

The steward’s pavilion, a neat Louis XV freestone house attached to the estate, contained a country-salon, bedrooms, a dining-room and billiard-room; two suites divided by a staircase and peristyle and a boudoir Mrs. Moreau had added. Furnished from the chateau’s old furniture and dressed in blue-and-white damask, oriental rugs and a tented boudoir, it suggested rustic luxury. Madame Moreau had a garden opening into the park and an iron gateway at the entrance.

The Moreaus cleverly hid their dependence on the count by living as comfortable, independent managers of a friend’s property. The count and countess rarely inspected Presles. Perquisites from Monsieur de Serizy—milk, eggs, poultry, game, fruits, forage, vegetables, wood—kept horses and servants; they bought only butcher’s meat, wine, and colonial supplies. Moreau ran the farm with small appropriations that seemed plausible: he paid his butcher with farm pigs after reserving some for himself, stacked far more hay than recorded, raised poultry and cattle at the estate’s expense, and used stable manure in the chateau gardens. These petty embezzlements, and services arranged through his influence, let the couple live in comfort and secure favor in the neighborhood.

Estelle, Madame Moreau, was a pretty, affected woman of about thirty-six. Although allowed only five hundred francs a year for her toilette, she spent it to appear like a princess, resented being called “the steward’s wife at Presles,” and coveted the position of a chateau mistress. She patronized villagers with the haughty tone of a great lady, depending on her husband to manage stewardship and fortune. Afraid of returning to servitude, she urged Moreau to secure the Moulineaux property with Léger so they could retire to Isle-Adam. A rancorous enmity with the Reyberts had grown from a slight when Estelle presumed precedence on arrival at Presles; the Reyberts brooded on revenge.

When Grindot the architect told them two painters would come to finish the chateau decorations—Schinner having done the principal paintings and gained renown—Madame Moreau decided to receive and praise the artist as if he were Schinner’s equal, to regain prestige. For two days she prepared, reserving a fine dress for Saturday and arranging a choice dinner. She stood near a flower-bed in the court with a rose-colored parasol, ready to be seen as mistress upon the arrival of the Beaumont coach; when the coach from Pierrotin left only Mistigris’s packages with the concierge, she withdrew disappointed, feeling too fine to wait idly in her salon. The Beaumont coach in fact left Paris an hour after Pierrotin.

The two artists were sent to their rooms to dress, then, piloted by Jacques Moreau, went to the steward’s lodge. Madame Moreau rose to display her graces as they were announced. She received them with the flattering airs of one who believes herself an arbiter of taste and claimed Schinner had always asked her opinion. The painters, Joseph Bridau and his pupil Leon de Lora (Mistigris), responded with polite sarcasm: Bridau, urbane and amused, and Mistigris sly and theatrical. Bridau praised Madame Moreau’s beauty and suggested Leon paint her portrait; Leon, forward, took her hand and cooed for secret sittings. Bridau proposed painting her children “in arabesques,” and they both played the agreeable flatterers, while Madame Moreau, indulging the role, offered a carriage drive through the woods.

At that moment Rosalie announced that the count was at the chateau, wanting his trunk and the key of his apartment. Estelle, unsettled though she pretended composure, hid her worry. Jacques brought in Oscar Husson, who blushed to see the artists in evening dress. Estelle stiffly told him to go dress. Mistigris jested that “two coats are better than none,” and Oscar, bewildered, had tears in his eyes; he had attempted to be amusing in the coach and now felt exposed.

The count, who had left his coach at the top of the hill and taken a wood-path to the gamekeeper’s house, had stopped there earlier. He instructed the keeper to ride to Beaumont with a short sealed note and told the keeper’s wife to deny Moreau’s demand for his horse if Moreau came. Entering the lodge, he descended through the park and into the chateau by the iron gates. The count, worn by public life yet composed in evening dress and ornaments, felt wounded by the thought that Moreau had deceived him. Thinking of Père Léger’s revelations and local gossip, he judged Moreau less a conspirator than a traitor who had betrayed intimate confidences.

When Moreau arrived to fetch his horse, the keeper’s wife told him the count had taken it. Alarmed, he ran to the chateau and found the count dressing and giving orders for dinner. He overheard that seven persons were invited: the count had summoned Monsieur Schinner, Mistigris, the notary Crottat and others. Before Moreau could deny anything, the count accused him sharply: he had spoken of the count’s infirmities and private attachment to Madame de Serizy in company—laughed at him with Madame Clapart, revealed plans for Les Moulineaux, and allowed a boy in the coach (Oscar) to blunder about the comtesse and other matters. The count said that such gossip hurt him far more than any pecuniary theft. He could forgive small embezzlement, he said, but to make his heart the subject of ridicule was unpardonable.

Moreau protested, pleading his long service and the backing his family had given to the count’s. He admitted some faults but denied talking with his wife about certain matters. The count, cold and dignified, declared the trust irrevocably broken. He would leave Moreau what he had gained and forget him; they would part decently. Moreau must explain stewardship duties to Monsieur de Reybert, who would succeed him, and behave with decorum. “As for that miserable boy,” the count added, “I will not have him sleep at Presles; send him to the inn.” The count, wounded in pride and feeling betrayed, made clear he would not tolerate the boy who had revealed family scandals.

While the sale negotiation for Les Moulineaux proceeded in the salon—Margueron, his notary Crottat, Monsieur de Reybert and others present—the Beaumont coach deposited Maître Crottat and Georges Marest, Crottat’s second clerk, who had earlier amused himself on the road by pretending to be a colonel and joking with the travelers in the diligence. Georges now realized the man he had teased might be the count; he feared he had insulted him. The artists, awkwardly conscious, tried to appear at ease. Mistigris made some saucy remark, which provoked the count to catch the rapin by the ear and jest with him about decoration, while Bridau avowed his parentage—he was the son of a devoted imperial official—and received the count’s friendly attention.

The count, seeing the documents meant for the Moulineaux sale, intervened. He chided Georges for carelessness: a notary’s clerk should not leave important deeds at the mercy of travelers, spend twenty francs on a small trip, or expose himself to such ridicule. Georges, ashamed, tried to explain he had only joked; Crottat, embarrassed between client and clerk, urged him to be silent. The count took the deed from Georges’ portfolio and handed it to Crottat, who turned it over in astonishment to find it was the document he had thought the clerk had left behind. The count’s intervention saved the transaction from an inadvertent leak: if Père Léger had seen the deed, he might have guessed the count’s purpose. The count insisted they conclude the business before dinner.

Back at the steward’s lodge, a bewildered Moreau returned, pale. He had been publicly rebuked and dismissed from trust, and he felt the world collapse. He told his wife of the count’s sentence in torn words: their stewardship was over. Her anger at Oscar's earlier indiscretion flared: it was Oscar who had “spoken ill of the comtesse” in the coach and revealed the count’s maladies and attachments. As Moreau explained what had happened, the household realized that Oscar’s ill-timed chatter, combined with gossip from Père Léger and others, had exposed the count’s private affairs and the Moulineaux negotiations. Moreau, frantic and humiliated, seized Oscar, dragged him to a window, and demanded to know what he had said to the count. Oscar, bewildered and terrified, was mute.

Moreau ordered Oscar to go to the chateau and beg the count’s pardon. Estelle scorned the boy; the household bustled. Unable to manage Oscar’s collapse, Moreau seized him by the coat and dragged him through the yard, Oscar’s outcries echoing, and flung him, raging and rigid, into the salon at the very moment the count, having completed the purchase, was about to leave for the dining-room with his guests. Moreau thrust the boy to his knees before the assembled visitors and commanded him to ask pardon of the man who had endowed his scholarship. Oscar, prostrate, foamed with shame and said nothing. The assembly recoiled.

The count, who had already said he would not have the boy sleep at Presles, looked on; after a pause he pronounced his judgment on the lad. “This young man is a mere lump of vanity,” he said. “A proud man humbles himself because he sees grandeur in a certain self-abasement. I fear you will never make much of that lad.” With that cold assessment he passed on to the dining-room, leaving Moreau white with rage and mortification.

Thus disowned in dignity rather than fortune, Moreau was commanded to arrange his affairs, to be allowed to remain in the pavilion until October, and to behave with the decorum befitting a man who had lost his master’s confidence. Monsieur and Madame de Reybert would occupy the chateau in the meantime; Moreau was to maintain the outward appearance of civility. Moreau carried the humiliation home; he returned to the lodge, ordered the horses to be hitched to his wife’s caleche, and, choked with fury, took Oscar away from the chateau’s protection—sending him, as the count had declared, away from Presles.

My dear,—Oscar has ruined me. During his journey in Pierrotin’s coach he told the Count travelling incognito about Madame de Serizy’s behavior and betrayed the secret of his terrible malady. After dismissing me from my stewardship, his Excellency ordered that Oscar should not sleep at Presles but be sent away at once. The horses are being harnessed now to my wife’s carriage, and Brochon, my stableman, will take the miserable child to you to-night.

My wife and I are in a distress you may imagine but I cannot describe. I shall see you in a few days, for I must take another course. I have three children and must think of their future. I own about two hundred and fifty thousand francs; I mean to raise myself to a fortune which may one day make me the equal of his Excellency. This bitter humiliation has awakened in me the power to move mountains. Whose blood has Oscar in his veins? He has behaved like a blockhead; up to this moment he has not answered a word nor made a sign to any question we put to him. Will he become an idiot, or is he one already? Dear friend, why did you not instruct him how to behave before you sent him? How many troubles you would have spared me if you had brought him yourself as I begged you. But it is done.

Adieu; I shall see you soon.

Your devoted servant and friend,
Moreau

That evening Madame Clapart, just returned from a walk with her husband, sat by candlelight knitting winter socks for Oscar while Monsieur Clapart, who prided himself on avoiding cafés despite their small means, expected Poiret to play dominoes. They argued in the petty, familiar way of long-married people. Clapart blamed Oscar for conceit and laziness; Madame Clapart defended her son’s good heart and reminded her husband that Oscar had stood second in philosophy at school — an answer that silenced him briefly.

Their quarrel was cut short by the crack of a postilion’s whip and a carriage stopping before the house. Clapart looked out and cried that they had sent Oscar back in a post-chaise. Madame Clapart trembled. Brochon entered, followed by Oscar and Poiret.

“I don’t know,” said the stableman, “but Monsieur Moreau is no longer steward of Presles, and they say your son has caused it. His Excellency ordered that he should be sent home to you. Here’s a letter from poor Monsieur Moreau, madame, which will tell you all. You never saw a man so changed in a single day.”

Madame Clapart snatched the letter and read. She reeled toward her son’s bed. “Oscar,” she cried, “do you want to kill your mother? After all the cautions I gave you this morning—” and she fainted. When she recovered, Monsieur Clapart was shaking Oscar by the arm demanding an answer. Oscar, who had slipped to bed at once after receiving the order, slept through the tumult. Young, tired, and startled, he slept the deep, restorative sleep of youth; the next day the crises of the night were already dimmer and physical hunger had replaced despair.

Madame Clapart sent her husband out so she could be alone with Oscar. She was woeful in appearance—eyes red and hollow, her voice feeble. She reminded her son of all they owed to Monsieur Moreau: six years of delicate charity, Monsieur Clapart’s salary and Oscar’s demi-bourse scholarship procured through the Count de Serizy. Most of those benefits would now cease. Clapart had no claim to a pension; when he could no longer keep his post, what would become of them?

“For myself,” she said, “I could nurse the sick or be a housekeeper and support Monsieur Clapart; but you, Oscar, what could you do? You must earn your living. There are four careers—commerce, government employment, the professions, or the military. Commerce needs capital, and we have none. Public employment requires influence and a long probation—you have alienated our only protector. The professions demand years and fees, and money for your maintenance while you learn. You are eighteen and strong; enlist in the army; it seems the only sure way to earn your bread.”

Oscar, inexperienced and unversed in making his way, listened submissively. The single word “army,” the sight of his mother’s tears, and her pleading produced in him the tears she needed to see. She softened, and, as mothers do, added hope. “Promise me you will be more discreet—repress your silly vanity,” she urged. Oscar promised, and she kissed and consoled him.

“We will see your uncle Cardot,” she added. “He owed much to your father, who gave his sister an enormous dowry and helped him make his fortune. Perhaps he might place you with Monsieur Camusot, his successor and son-in-law. He scarcely knows of our existence, but tomorrow we will go and see him; he is our last hope.”

Jean-Jérôme-Séverin Cardot was a shrewd, small, neat man who had bought the ancient Cocon d’Or in 1793 with his wife’s dowry and made a fortune. To secure his children he had converted his capital into annuities and divided much among them; he now lived on a thirty-thousand-franc annuity in a modest first-floor Belleville apartment with a garden, served by an old cook and a former maid. He kept his late wife’s furniture and habits, dined in Paris five nights a week, returned in a hackney-coach, and cultivated a polite, decorous appearance while enjoying pleasures in private. His children believed he had lived frugally for their sake, which prompted them to be dutiful. Only his son-in-law Camusot knew the truth of his income.

Madame Clapart had long held on to Cardot as a hope and took Oscar to see him during vacations. Cardot had occasionally given Oscar a dinner, a visit to the Gaîté, a school suit, a silver cup and fork—small kindnesses that showed his benignity without binding him deeply. When Madame Clapart and Oscar called, Cardot said gently that if his late wife had been living she would have wished to do something for her brother’s child. He confessed that his fortune was left to his children and that he had only an annuity for himself, but he knew the way the boy ought to be started.

“Let him study law,” Cardot said. “I’ll pay the costs. Put him in a lawyer’s office and let him learn the business; if he does well, and if I am still alive, each of my children shall lend him a quarter of the cost of a practice; I will be security. You will only have to feed and clothe him. Discretion, honesty, and work—live at home, go straight to the law-school, from there to a lawyer’s office; drudge night and day, and study at home. Become, by twenty-two, a second clerk; by twenty-four, head-clerk; be steady, and you will win all. If you don’t like the profession you might enter my son the notary’s office.”

Madame Clapart, seized with hope, blessed him and Cardot led the boy to breakfast. On parting he advised her to send Oscar to him now and then so he could form him. The visit steadied the poor mother. For the next fortnight she took Oscar walking daily and watched him closely.

By the end of October, as the household breakfasted on a humble meal, Moreau—the ex-steward—entered and surprised them. He explained he no longer lived at Presles because of the incident, but reassured them with news of his own fortunes. He had gone into partnership with Père Légér and Père Margueron of Beaumont, speculating in land. They had purchased the estate of Persan; the concern had a capital of a million, part of which he had secured on his own name. He was the head of the enterprise, doing most of the work and receiving half the profits; for that reason he would be constantly on the road. His wife lived plainly in the Faubourg du Roule. If the business paid off and if Oscar behaved, they might perhaps employ the boy.

Moreau’s tone combined wounded pride and ambition. He told Madame Clapart he had been buried at Presles and that the catastrophe might spur him to a brilliant fortune. He repeated his resolve to make the Count aware of what seventeen years of his life were worth. He spoke of his capital, of the energy this outrage had roused, and of how it had matured his plans.

He also offered a practical plan for Oscar. He had heard of a young lawyer who had just bought a “titre nu,” a practice without clients: Desroches, a hard, iron-willed man eager for work. Moreau proposed offering Desroches their business on condition that he take Oscar as a pupil and lodge him in his house for nine hundred francs a year. Moreau would pay three hundred, leaving the family to provide six hundred francs’ worth of maintenance without living costs—on the understanding that Oscar must be harshly disciplined and apply himself. Moreau insisted the boy mustn’t leave the track; he must go straight through with law or notary work, and only under strict discipline would he become a man.

“Come, Oscar; thank our kind Monsieur Moreau, and don’t stand there like a stone post,” said Madame Clapart. Moreau pressed Oscar’s hand and added, “The best way to make your peace with me is to work now with steady application, and to conduct yourself in future properly.”

Thus, from Moreau’s humiliation and the household’s panic, a plan emerged: Cardot’s offer of legal apprenticeship and Moreau’s placement with Desroches would combine to provide training, discipline, and a path out of dependence—but only if Oscar could abandon vanity and folly for steady work. The three adults—detached in motive though united in purpose—resolved to hold him to that discipline, each in his own way: the uncle by means and advice, the ex-steward by employment and severity, and the mother by love and exhortation.

Ten days later Monsieur Moreau took Oscar to Maître Desroches, a twenty-six-year-old solicitor newly established in the rue de Béthisy in a large, inexpensive apartment at the end of a narrow courtyard. Desroches, the son of poor parents and brought up strictly, knew of Oscar’s situation and felt some interest in him, though his outward severity limited the aid he could show. Oscar—gaunt, mud-streaked, hair cropped like a brush, speaking curtly, with a piercing eye and a restless gloom—was intimidated by his new master.

“We work here day and night,” Desroches announced from his armchair amid piles of papers. “Monsieur Moreau, we won’t kill him; but he’ll have to go at our pace.” Though it was Sunday, the head-clerk appeared, pen in hand.

“Monsieur Godeschal, here’s the pupil I told you of. Monsieur Moreau takes the liveliest interest in him. He will dine with us and sleep in the small attic next to your chamber. You will allot the exact time it takes to go to the law-school and back, so he does not lose five minutes on the way. See that he learns the Code and is proficient; after his work here you will give him authors to read. He will be under your immediate direction, and I shall keep an eye on him. They want to make him what you have made yourself, a capable head-clerk, so he can take such a place himself. Go with Monsieur Godeschal, my young friend; he’ll show you your lodging, and you can settle down.”

Desroches praised his clerks—young men relying only on their ten fingers—working with the zeal of a dozen ordinary fellows. He had taken Godeschal from maître Derville when the latter was a mere second clerk, had paid him a thousand francs a year with board, and admired his indefatigable honesty. “For the slightest fault of that kind a clerk leaves my office,” he declared. Moreau thought Oscar was in a good school.

For two years Oscar lived in the rue de Béthisy, in a petty-law den governed by strict, capable supervision. His hours were rigid; his Paris life resembled that of a monk. At five each morning, in all weather, Godeschal woke and took Oscar to the office, where their master was already at work. Oscar ran errands and prepared law-school lessons; Godeschal and often Desroches pointed out authors and difficulties. He was not allowed to leave a single section of the Code until he had mastered it; Godeschal gave him preliminary examinations more searching and longer than those of the law-school. After brief classes he returned to the office; sometimes he was sent to the Palais, but always under Godeschal’s thumb, until dinner. Dinner was modest—one dish of meat, one of vegetables, a salad; dessert a piece of Gruyère cheese. After dinner Godeschal and Oscar returned to work until night. Once a month Oscar breakfasted with his uncle Cardot and spent Sundays with his mother. Occasionally Moreau, when in Paris, took Oscar to dine at the Palais-Royal or to the theater. Having been scolded for attempts at elegance, Oscar soon ceased to think about his clothes.

“A good clerk,” Godeschal told him, “should have two black coats, one new, one old, a pair of black trousers, black stockings, and shoes. Boots cost too much; you can’t have boots till you are called to the bar. A clerk should never spend more than seven hundred francs a year. Good stout shirts of strong linen are what you want. Ha! when a man starts from nothing to reach fortune, he has to keep down to bare necessities. Look at Monsieur Desroches; he did what we are doing, and see where he is now.”

Godeschal exemplified strict principles of honor, discretion, and honesty without ostentation; such conduct came naturally to him. Eighteen months after Oscar’s installation the second clerk was, for the second time, slightly wrong in his accounts. Godeschal said to him before the others:

“My dear Gaudet, go away of your own free will, that it may not be said that Monsieur Desroches has dismissed you. You have been careless or absent-minded, and neither of those defects can pass here. The master shall know nothing about the matter; that is all that I can do for a comrade.”

At twenty Oscar became third clerk in the office. Though he earned no salary, he was lodged and fed while doing the work of the second clerk. Desroches employed two chief clerks, and the second’s work was unremitting toil. By the end of his second year in the law-school Oscar knew more than most licensed graduates; he did work at the Palais intelligently and argued some cases in chambers. Godeschal and Desroches were satisfied. Yet Oscar still showed occasional hankerings after pleasure and a suppressed desire to shine.

Moreau, satisfied with Oscar’s progress, relaxed his vigilance; in July 1825, when Oscar passed his examinations with a spotless record, the land-agent gave him money to dress elegantly and Madame Clapart prepared his outfit splendidly.

In November, when the courts reopened, Oscar occupied the chamber of the second clerk and did that work wholly, with a salary of eight hundred francs plus board and lodging. Uncle Cardot, who had privately inquired at Desroches about his nephew, promised Madame Clapart to look out for a practice for Oscar if he continued to do well.

Despite these virtuous appearances, Oscar endured great inward strife. At times he thought of quitting a life so alien to his tastes; he fancied galley-slaves happier. Galled by the collar of this iron system, wild desires seized him when he compared himself in the street with well-dressed young men. Sometimes he was driven toward women; then he resigned himself, only to sink into deeper disgust for life. Impelled by Godeschal’s example, he was forced rather than led into the rugged way.

Godeschal watched Oscar and, by principle, kept him from temptation. Generally the young clerk had so little money he could not give way to excess. During the last year Godeschal had made five or six pleasure parties with Oscar, defraying expenses, feeling the rope by which he tethered the young kid must be slackened. These “pranks,” as he called them, helped Oscar endure existence, for there was little amusement in breakfasting with Uncle Cardot or seeing his mother, who lived even more penuriously than Desroches. Moreau could not be as familiar with Oscar as Godeschal; perhaps Godeschal, sincere friend to young Husson, had been ahead of Moreau in careful attempts to initiate the poor youth into life’s mysteries. Oscar, grown prudent, came to see the extent and character of the fault committed on that luckless journey which had brought him low; but his repressed fancies and youthful follies might still get the better of him. Nevertheless, the more knowledge he acquired of the world and its laws, the better his mind would form itself, and, provided Godeschal never lost sight of him, Moreau flattered himself that between them they could bring Madame Clapart’s son through in safety.

“How is he getting on?” asked Moreau of Godeschal on his return from a journey.

“Always too much vanity,” replied Godeschal. “You give him fine clothes and fine linen, he wears the shirt-fronts of a stockbroker, and so my dainty coxcomb spends his Sundays in the Tuileries, looking out for adventures. What else can you expect? That’s youth. He torments me to present him to my sister, where he would see actresses, ballet-dancers, elegant young fops, spendthrifts wasting fortunes! His mind, I’m afraid, is not fitted for law. He can talk well, though; and if we could make him a barrister he might plead cases that were carefully prepared for him.”

In November 1825, soon after Oscar had taken his new clerkship and was about to pass his licentiate examination, a new clerk arrived to fill the place vacated by Oscar’s promotion.

This fourth clerk, Frédéric Marest, intended to enter the magistracy and was in his third year at the law school. A fine young man of twenty-three, he inherited about twelve thousand francs a year from a bachelor uncle, and was the son of Madame Marest, widow of a wealthy wood-merchant. Desiring to understand procedure and train to be a head-clerk in two years, he had placed himself in Desroches’ office to study; by thirty he hoped to be “procureur du roi.” Though Frédéric was cousin-german to Georges Marest, Oscar did not connect him with that family.

“Messieurs,” said Godeschal at breakfast, “I announce the arrival of a new jurisconsult; and as he is rich, rishissime, we will, I hope, make him pay a glorious entrance-fee.”

“Forward, the book!” cried Oscar, nodding to the youngest clerk, “and pray let us be serious.”

The youngest clerk climbed to the top shelf and produced a register covered with dust. This perennial joke of legal offices supplied rare amusement in a life of toil: the gravest clerks, craving diversion, seized on any hoax. When Desroches bought a practice without clients he began a new dynasty, breaking custom by assembling clerks from various offices. Godeschal, who had apprenticed under maître Derville, would not lose the precious tradition of the “welcome,” a breakfast every neophyte gives to the ancients of the office.

About the time Oscar arrived, during the first six months of Desroches’ installation, on a winter evening when work had been finished early and the clerks warmed themselves before the fire, Godeschal conceived and composed a Register “architriclino-basochien” of great antiquity, contrived to appear genuine and saved from the Revolution’s flames. The clerks searched for old paper, bound a book in parchment stamped like an official order, left it to gather mold and wear, and forged entries. The first sixty pages pretended to report receptions in an eighteenth-century legal style, as though the Basoche guild had been continuous since 1525.

The Register opened with mock solemnity: an eighteenth-century invocation to Sainte-Geneviève, an assertion that since 1525 the clerks of this Practice had existed under her protection, and a declaration of continuity. Names and dates—Malin, Grévin, Athanase Féret, Jacques Héret, Regnault de Saint-Jean-d’Angély, Bedeau—were appended, with an account of a breakfast at Courtille lasting until seven the next morning. Expertly engrossed, it contained twenty-seven reports of receptions; after a blank it resumed in 1806 with Bordin’s appointment, proclaiming the reconstitution of the kingdom of Basoche and crediting Sainte-Geneviève and maître Bordin for preserving the archives. The entries mixed legal formality with rollicking conviviality—detailed praise for dinners, wines, and the master’s generosity—creating the illusion of a continuous clerical tradition.

Under date June 1822, when Desroches took the oath, Godeschal’s declaration boasted of recovering the famous archives architriclino-basochien, seeking out a document dated 1786 connected with proofs going back to 1525, and celebrating the “worship” of the Goddess Bottle and good living. Godeschal recorded a breakfast at the “Cheval Rouge,” recounting twelve bottles of twelve different wines, melons, and dishes, thanking his sister Mariette, a celebrated dancer, for orchestra seats. The page bore wine, pâté, and candle-grease stains.

Oscar’s own mock reception was recorded with extravagant praise: radishes, gherkins, anchovies, butter and olives; “succulent soup of rice” credited to maternal solicitude; fine stews; tongue of beef with tomatoes; a compote of pigeons; a timbale of macaroni with chocolate custards; and eleven dessert dishes, all washed down with sixteen bottles of choicest wines, Roussillon and Rhône outdoing Champagne and Burgundy. The statutes of their Order were parodied too: candidates must limit the magnificence of their welcome to the length of their purse; Madame Clapart’s liberality merited hurrahs. The entry purported to be signed by all the clerks.

Three clerks had already been deceived by the Book, and three real “receptions of welcome” were falsely recorded in it. After each arrival the little sub-clerk placed the Archives Architriclino-Basochiennes on the new-comer’s desk; the clerks enjoyed the stunned look as he studied its facetious pages. Inter pocula each candidate learned the secret of the farce, and the revelation inspired him to hoax his successor.

Hence Oscar, now a participator in the hoax, cried, “Forward, the book!”

Ten minutes later a handsome young man with a fine figure and pleasant face presented himself, asked for Monsieur Desroches, and gave his name without hesitation.

“I am Frédéric Marest,” he said, “and I come to take the place of third clerk.”

“Monsieur Husson,” said Godeschal, “show monsieur his seat and tell him about the customs of the office.”

The next day the new clerk found the register on his desk. He read a few pages, began to laugh, said nothing to the assembled clerks, and laid the book down again.

“Messieurs,” he said at five o’clock, “I have a cousin who is head-clerk of the notary Maître Léopold Hannequin; I will ask his advice as to what I ought to do for my welcome.”

“That looks ill,” cried Godeschal when Frédéric had gone, “he hasn’t the cut of a novice, that fellow!”

“We’ll get some fun out of him yet,” said Oscar.

The next day at two a young man entered the office whom Oscar recognized as Georges Marest, now head-clerk to the notary Hannequin.

“Ha! here’s the friend of Ali Pacha!” cried Oscar.

“Hey! you here, Monsieur l’ambassadeur!” answered Georges, recognizing him.

Godeschal asked if they knew one another.

“We got into a scrape together about two years ago,” Georges said. “I had to leave Crottat and go to Hannequin.”

“What was it?” asked Godeschal.

“Oh, nothing,” Georges replied at Oscar’s signal. “We tried to hoax a peer of France and he bowled us over. So you want to jockey my cousin, do you?”

“We jockey no one,” said Oscar with dignity, producing the famous register and pointing to a sentence of banishment passed on a refractory in 1788.

Georges laughed, leafed through the archives, and then announced a fête. “Tomorrow (Sunday) you are bidden to the Rocher de Cancale at two. Afterwards I’ll take you to spend the evening with Madame la Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos, where we’ll play cards and you’ll see the elite of the women of fashion. Therefore, gentlemen of the lower courts,” he added with notarial airs, “you will have to behave yourselves, and carry your wine like the seigneurs of the Regency.”

The office cheered. Desroches emerged from his private room.

“What’s all this about?” he asked. Seeing Georges, he suspected a plan to demoralize his clerks. He opened his cash-box and gave Oscar five hundred francs. “Go to the Palais and get from the registrar a copy of the decision in Vandernesse against Vandernesse; it must be served to-night if possible. I promised Simon twenty francs. Wait if the copy is not ready. Don’t let Derville stick a spoke in our wheel; Count Félix de Vandernesse is more powerful than his brother, our client, the ambassador. Keep your eyes open; if there’s the slightest hitch come straight back.”

Oscar set off resolved to distinguish himself in his first entrusted affair as second clerk.

After Georges and Oscar left, Godeschal probed the new clerk about the supposed marquise. Frédéric, cool and grave like a king’s attorney, sustained his cousin’s hoax. By his manner he convinced the office that the marquise might indeed be a Mexican-born widow of a Spanish grandee, courted by rich Georges. “She loves to laugh, sing, drink like me,” he whispered, quoting Béranger. “Georges is very rich: he inherited eighteen thousand francs a year and with twelve thousand from an uncle he has thirty thousand. He pays his debts and gives up the law. He hopes to be Marquis de las Florentinas; the young widow can give her title to her husband.”

Though some clerks remained skeptical, the promise of a Rocher de Cancale breakfast and a fashionable evening put them in high spirits.

In truth the Marquise de las Florentinas y Cabirolos was simply Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, first danseuse at the Gaîté, whom uncle Cardot had taken up. After Madame Cardot’s death Cardot encountered Florentine leaving Coulon’s dancing-class when she was about thirteen. Charmed, he followed her home, learned she was a portress’s daughter, and soon installed mother and daughter in a modest apartment in rue de Crussol with mahogany furniture, carpets, a proper kitchen, and a monthly pension. Cardot, the generous patron, sent her to Vestris and watched her debut at sixteen in a melodrama’s ballet. He raised her stipend to five hundred francs; though no longer adored as an angel, he remained a “friend for life,” a second father.

From 1820 to 1823 she moved among celebrated dancers—Mariette, Tullia, Florine, and Coralie. Cardot, now older, indulged a semi-paternal devotion; he kept her close so he could still sing “Mère Godichon.” Over the years he saved eighty thousand francs. Expecting that Florentine would soon need the luxuries of a theatrical star, he had recently spent forty-five thousand francs fitting up the former apartment of the late Coralie for her. The dance star’s lodgings on rue de Vendôme were magnificently furnished; she began to give dinners, spent three hundred francs a month on dress, kept a carriage, and had servants. An engagement at the Opera was in prospect. Cardot, who had been master of his household, now found himself under a capricious rule, but he loved his charge and intended to leave her a hundred thousand francs.

Georges Marest, with thirty thousand francs a year and a handsome face, courted Florentine. A danseuse always wants a young man to take her out—dinners, boxes, drives, wines; the passion for such a star costs trifles to the favored mortal. Georges, newly wealthy after his uncle’s death, stopped thinking of becoming a notary. Frédéric, by contrast, persisted in pursuing public office.

The clerks, sons of poor parents unused to the great world, dressed up to attend Georges’ breakfast and the promise of meeting the Mexican marquise. Oscar felt a mix of resentment toward Georges for a prior slight—he still recalled Moreau having flung him at the count’s feet—but he eagerly prepared to display his new clothes. “What luck,” he told Godeschal, “my mother had just made me a new coat, trousers, waistcoat; I have six frilled shirts!” Madame Clapart appeared with cravats and praised Godeschal as a model. Godeschal warned Oscar not to blunder as he had the previous night; Desroches had been furious that the judgment was not obtained. Godeschal had gone to the Palais himself at six that morning and secured a promise the copy would be ready by seven.

Madame Clapart, proud of her son’s appointment and savings, had given him a gold watch and told him Cardot promised money for a conscript substitute. She also brought a pocket-book from their friend Moreau. Oscar insisted on carrying the five hundred francs Desroches gave him to fetch the judgment; his mother, alarmed, urged leaving it with Godeschal, but the latter had already gone.

Oscar lounged on the boulevards until Georges’ breakfast. Proud in his new clothes—blue waistcoat with palm pattern, black cashmere trousers, black coat, and a saved gilt-top cane—he enjoyed prospects of the gay world. He tried to keep his aversion for Georges in check—an instinct to avoid those who had once injured him—but when at half-past two he entered the Rocher de Cancale salon he forgot it. Present were an old captain of dragoons named Giroudeau, Finot the journalist who might secure Florentine’s Opera engagement, and du Bruel the author and friend of Tullia. Georges made himself charming.

“You’ve taken to private diplomacy,” he said. “What difference between a lawyer and an ambassador? Ambassadors are attorneys of peoples. If I can ever be useful to you, let me know.”

Oscar confessed Georges had once done him great harm. “Pooh!” said Georges. “It was Monsieur de Sérizy who behaved badly. His wife—I wouldn’t have her; and the count, Minister of State and peer of France, has a small mind. I don’t care a fig for him.” Oscar welcomed such slurs, which diminished his own fault in his eyes.

The feast stretched long; courses took hours, dessert did not appear until eight. The wines were excellent. The clerks ate and drank like men newly freed from privation. Godeschal, after signing the report of the meal, left them to the wines and liqueurs. By half-past ten Oscar was so intoxicated Georges packed him into a coach and sent him home, but ten others, also drunk, determined to walk to Florentine’s house. Only Georges, Giroudeau, du Bruel, and Finot could keep pace; the rest were carried by wines and carriages Georges sent for, and after an hour’s drive round the boulevards they returned by Bercy and the quays to rue de Vendôme.

Florentine’s salon sparkled with stage princesses who, knowing of Frédéric’s joke, imitated the women of good society as they ate ices. Wax candles burned in candelabra; footmen in full livery served dainties on silver; Lyonnaise hangings and carpets dazzled the newcomers. Through Georges’ artful presentation and their own intoxication, the clerks believed in the Marquise de las Florentinas. Gold glittered on card-tables in the bed-chamber; in the salon women played vingt-et-un, kept by the celebrated Nathan.

Presented to Florentine by Georges, Oscar at first did not recognize the dancer he had seen at the Gaîté in this aristocratically décolletée and lace-swaddled figure. She received him with gracious manners, then took him to a vingt-et-un table and introduced him to the pretty Fanny Beaupré, who had just succeeded at the Porte-Saint-Martin.

“My dear,” said Florentine, “allow me to present a charming youth whom you can take as a partner in the game.”

“Ah! that will be delightful,” smiled the actress. “I am losing. Shall we go shares, monsieur?”

Oscar sat, ashamed to mix his small silver coins with the actress’s gold. She put up five gold pieces; he produced his hundred francs in silver. In ten throws the actress lost the two hundred francs. She became banker; Florentine lent her five hundred francs obtained from Georges. Nathan had won twelve hundred francs, and the actress whispered to Oscar to bring them luck.

Tempted by the opportunity and the atmosphere, Oscar took out the five hundred francs Desroches had given him. The first throws brought gains—two thousand francs—but a few turns later the gains vanished. Oscar wanted to feign illness and escape, but “honor” held him. Three more throws lost their stake. He drank iced punch; then, in a fit of desperation and hope, he accepted Florentine’s offer when she lent him a thousand francs to win back what he’d lost, advising him not to risk more than five hundred. She gave him the money to help him, yet the brigands Finot and Giroudeau won it from him. The company laughed over the “poor innocent.”

By dawn, after “character dancing,” Florentine, tired, went to bed, oblivious to Oscar still asleep in the boudoir.

About eleven the next morning a tremendous noise woke the unfortunate clerk. Hearing his uncle Cardot’s voice, he feigned sleep and hid in the yellow velvet cushions where he had passed the night.

Cardot reproached Florentine for her excesses: she had danced at the Gaieté, passed the night in revelry, and inaugurated her new apartment without consulting him. Florentine replied defiantly: she had entertained a charming company—Tullia, du Bruel, Mariette, the Duc de Maufrigneuse, Florine, Nathan—and meant to dance a “pas de Zéphire” for Cardot. The old man bewailed the disorder—stained carpets, broken glasses—and was about to retire, when both he and Florentine noticed a man in a black coat through the open boudoir door.

“Ah, Mademoiselle Cabirolle!” Cardot said; Florentine’s laughter betrayed her: the man was Oscar Husson. Cardot and Oscar were ashamed; Florentine teased them, and Oscar tried to escape.

Florentine then confessed she had lent Oscar money—he had played with his master Desroches’ funds and lost them, then borrowed a thousand francs from her to recover and lost that too. She asked Cardot to help. Oscar fell to his knees, distraught, admitting that Desroches had entrusted him with money to obtain a paper at the Palais in the Vandernesse case and that he had failed. He begged his uncle to go with him to Desroches to beg forgiveness and find an excuse. The scene—his sobs, his appeal to be saved for the sake of his father and aunt—moved Florentine. Cardot, angry at his nephew’s folly and the loss (fifteen hundred francs in all), at last agreed to restore five hundred and to repay Florentine’s thousand, but vowed no further aid. Oscar fled, overwhelmed.

Outside, Oscar wandered uncertain. Chance intervened. Mariette, who cared for the youth Godeschal favored, had that night written to Desroches enclosing a five-hundred-franc banknote to repair Oscar’s loss and sent her maid to deliver it before seven. Godeschal, rising early and finding Oscar absent, guessed the situation; he took his own five-hundred-franc note to the Palais, procured a copy of the judgment, and delivered it to Desroches by eight.

Desroches, an early riser, had been in his office since seven. Mariette’s maid, finding no one at home, presented the letter and banknote; seeing the note, Desroches retired to his private room, furious at his second clerk. Half an hour later Godeschal entered with the document; Desroches questioned him.

“Did Oscar Husson fetch the paper this morning from Simon?” he demanded.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“Who gave him the money?”

“You did, on Saturday.”

Bitter at repeated folly, Desroches, who had opened Mariette’s letter disguised as business to read the banknote, resolved to dismiss Oscar. His anger mixed moral exasperation with a professed hatred for “idiots”—those who squandered opportunities despite kind oversight. Godeschal lamented the trouble the boy caused, blamed Georges Marest as an evil influence, and urged that Oscar avoid him. He related earlier misadventures at Presles that reminded Desroches of favors later granted by Monsieur de Sérizy related to Joseph Bridau’s brother. When Moreau, concerned with the Vandernesse case, arrived, Desroches half-consoled himself by suggesting Oscar might become a barrister, letting his vanity fuel eloquence; this offered faint comfort beside dismissal.

Meanwhile at the rue de Béthisy, Clapart’s household presented a different scene. Clapart, ill and peevish, was nursed by his wife, who boasted of Oscar at small successes; Clapart took malicious pleasure in rousing her anxiety. In their meager apartment they bickered about the son they shared. Clapart, relishing his wife’s distress, urged that extravagant young men like Oscar were liable to debt and ruin.

Madame Clapart defended her son: Oscar had cost them nothing; he held the second-clerk’s post, his uncle Cardot and Monsieur Moreau paid his expenses, and he earned eight hundred francs. If the family kept bread on the table in old age, it was thanks to him. Clapart sneered, predicting debt and shame. The bell rang; Madame Clapart opened to find Moreau, who had come to soften the blow of Oscar’s folly.

When she learned he had gambled with office money, she burst into tears. Clapart taunted; Moreau, practical and blunt, advised that if Oscar were his own son he might let fate take its course—draw for conscription and perhaps secure discipline through military service. Six years in the army, Moreau argued, would sober him; it might delay a bar career but teach subordination and harden his character.

Madame Clapart recoiled at the thought of Oscar as a common soldier; she could not bear it. Moreau reasoned there was no alternative: Oscar’s imprudence had closed the path to the bar; he lacked funds to set up in practice or to begin a trade; he was unfit for manual labor. The mother had given him education and a career; now he had abandoned it. The army would preserve him from dissipation and provide steadiness. It was better than dishonor or ruin.

At that moment Oscar arrived, with all his few possessions. Clapart greeted him with derision; Oscar kissed his mother and offered his hand to Moreau, which the latter coldly refused. Forced into sudden maturity, Oscar rebuked his stepfather sharply, asserting his coming majority in a few months and claiming adult rights: he had never asked Clapart for anything, cost him nothing, and demanded to be left alone. Clapart, silenced, retreated.

Oscar apologized for his fault as a “peccadillo,” blamed a momentary temptation and a debauch in which even Godeschal had been half-seas over, vowed he was cured, and begged Moreau to help; he promised to keep the remaining six years of clerkship without excess. Moreau refused to promise. Madame Clapart urged Oscar to remember Cardot; Oscar said Cardot had renounced him. Overwhelmed, Madame Clapart fainted and Moreau tended her. Oscar sat crushed.

When Moreau returned he gave a blunt verdict: Oscar’s only recourse was the army. Clapart, who seemed unable to live long, would soon be gone, leaving the mother without means; it was prudent to reserve money for her, and military discipline would do more for Oscar than uncertain counsel. Oscar accepted; he would draw his lot for conscription soon and decide his future in the interim.

Three days later Oscar drew number twenty-seven. The former steward of Presles, moved by pity, went to the Comte de Sérizy to ask help obtaining a cavalry commission. Fortune intervened: the count’s son, a poor student at the École Polytechnique, had been appointed sub-lieutenant in a cavalry regiment commanded by the Duc de Maufrigneuse as a favor. Through the Comte’s intercession, Oscar was drafted into that regiment with the promise of promotion to quartermaster within a year. Chance thus placed the ex-clerk under the count’s son’s command—and under conditions that would improve his prospects.

Madame Clapart, after days of anguish, sank into remorse. She blamed her second marriage’s miseries and her son’s troubles on divine retribution for youthful pleasures. For the first time in forty years she went to confess to the Abbé Gaudron, vicar of Saint-Paul’s, and embraced devotion and pious works—less from character change than from desperate hope for Oscar’s welfare. Her piety was simple and sincere; when Monsieur Clapart’s life was prolonged, she took his continued tyranny as a trial from a loving God.

Oscar meanwhile behaved steadily. By 1830 he had risen to first sergeant of the Vicomte de Sérizy’s company, a post equivalent to sub-lieutenant of the line, and was twenty-five. The Royal Guard, often near Paris, allowed him to visit his mother and recount difficulties. He recognized social obstacles to promotion—cavalry commissions were monopolized by noble sons and men without titles advanced slowly. His only ambition was to leave the Guards for a line regiment as sub-lieutenant.

In February 1830 Madame Clapart, leveraging the Abbé Gaudron’s influence and Madame la Dauphine’s favor, obtained the promotion her son sought. Though Oscar professed fidelity to the Bourbons outwardly, his sympathies were liberal; when the Revolution of July 1830 erupted, he sided with the people. That choice brought public notice: during August’s triumph he was promoted to lieutenant, awarded the Legion of Honor, and attached as aide-de-camp to La Fayette, who made him captain in 1832. When La Fayette was removed from command of the National Guard, Oscar’s zeal for the new regime remained; he was appointed major of a regiment sent to Africa during the Prince-Royal’s first expedition, in which the Vicomte de Sérizy was lieutenant-colonel.

In the field Oscar distinguished himself. At the affair of the Makta, when French troops had to give ground and the vicomte lay wounded under a dead horse, Oscar rallied the squadron—“Messieurs, it is going to death, but we cannot abandon our colonel”—and led a furious countercharge. His men followed; the Arabs, taken by surprise, allowed Oscar to seize the vicomte and fling him across his horse, carrying him off amid the rescue, while Oscar received two slashes on his left arm from yataghans.

For this conduct he received the officer’s cross of the Legion of Honor and promotion to lieutenant-colonel. The vicomte, however, succumbed to his wounds and died. Oscar was so seriously wounded his left arm was amputated under the care of surgeons summoned by the Comtesse. The Comte de Sérizy, moved not only by gratitude for Oscar’s devotion but by the proof of character since Presles, felt compelled to forgive the young man. Oscar’s services—and his life—created an obligation of honor. The man who had once resented Oscar’s rashness now repaid him in the world’s language: acknowledgment, patronage, and remembrance toward a son who had died under his command.

Years after the Makta affair, on a May morning beneath the porte-cochere of the Lion d’Argent in the rue de Faubourg Saint-Denis, an old widow in black leaned on a bronzed, one-armed man of about thirty-four wearing a Legion of Honor rosette, waiting for Pierrotin’s diligence. Pierrotin, master of the Oise line, would scarcely have recognized in this soldier the Oscar Husson he once escorted to Presles. Madame Husson, now a pious widow, was hardly recognizable either: Clapart, the idle lounger whose presence had once brought disaster, was dead; his death had, in a way, served his wife, who now received a pension of fifteen hundred francs a year.

The coach, capable of nineteen passengers, was in good order; Pierrotin, still brisk near fifty-six, supervised the porters. Oscar told Pierrotin that two places in the interieur were taken under his servant Bellejambe’s name; Pierrotin guessed Oscar was the new collector at Beaumont, replacing Monsieur Margueron’s nephew. Oscar, wishing to remain unknown for the moment, pressed his mother’s arm to keep her from speaking.

A voice from the street made Oscar start: Georges Marest hailed, asking for a seat. Pierrotin rebuked him for his familiarity. Once elegant, Georges now showed almost baldness, tufts of hair, a pear-shaped belly, blotched skin and dull, vinous eyes; his shabby finery and battered cane suggested a fallen man still trying to display former rank.

Georges asked about seats; Pierrotin said the coupe was taken by a peer of France, the Baron de Canalis, his wife and mother-in-law, leaving only one interior place. Georges, recalling past adventures with the Comte de Serizy, accepted it. He glanced at Oscar and his mother but did not at first recognize them: Oscar’s African bronzing, full moustache and whiskers, strict dress, missing arm and rosette concealed his identity; Madame Clapart’s religious austerity hid the Aspasia of 1797.

Père Leger, an enormous, plainly dressed but evidently wealthy old man, arrived and received Pierrotin’s respectful greeting. Georges jeered at his own past follies; Leger corrected his address and they traded remarks about change and fortune. An old man named Reybert joined, followed by Joseph Bridau, the celebrated painter, whose fame and manner were immediately noticeable though neither Georges nor Oscar recognized him at first.

A clerk read the passenger list: the Canalis party in the coupe; Bellejambe with two interior places; de Reybert with three; Georges gave his name in a humbled voice. The rotunda filled with nurses, country folk and shopkeepers; four young men were sent to the imperiale. The coach moved off, climbing the faubourg Saint-Denis toward Saint-Denis, then into the valley of Montmorency.

At the turn into Montmorency, Georges broke the silence with jokes about speed and a silver watch. Leger mocked his title; Georges boasted he had “made the revolution of July,” admitting it ruined him. Bridau teased about new campaigns; the talk turned to familiar names and fortunes. Leger noted Pierrotin had prospered, becoming a principal entrepreneur of Oise coaches, owning a hotel where diligences stopped, helped by a good wife and daughter.

They spoke of the old circle: Mistigris, now famed as Leon de Lora; the youth who had once offended the count; the fragile Comte de Serizy—Bridau feared the count’s last journey would be to Bridau’s marriage at Isle-Adam. Reybert reported the count’s wife visited once a month and had recently had her niece marry a rich Pole, the Comte Laginski. Madame Clapart asked who would inherit Monsieur de Serizy’s property; Georges said the count’s wife would take it and bury him. Leger teased Georges about illusions; Georges defended the countess’s elegance.

Conversation turned to Moreau, the steward the count had dismissed, now deputy from the Oise and owner of the Pointel estate between Presles and Beaumont—an advancement Georges called “bad taste.” Reybert warned Georges to speak softly: Madame Moreau and her daughter, the Baronne de Canalis, were in the coupe. Georges speculated on what dowry could have induced the great orator to marry Moreau’s daughter; Leger guessed “something like two millions.” Georges sneered that the orator always liked millions.

Oscar interrupted to reproach Georges for speaking ill in a public conveyance. Bridau glanced at Oscar and then recognized his rosette.

“Monsieur is not an ambassador, but his rosette tells us he has made his way nobly,” Bridau observed, noting that he and General Giroudeau had named Oscar in their reports. Georges then recognized Oscar.

“If it hadn’t been for your voice I should never have known you,” Georges exclaimed. Reybert recalled that Oscar had rescued Vicomte Jules de Serizy from the Arabs and that the count had secured for him the collectorship at Beaumont as a step toward Pontoise. Oscar confirmed this. Bridau invited Oscar to his marriage at Isle-Adam; Oscar accepted and learned Bridau would marry Mademoiselle Leger, Père Leger’s granddaughter—a union arranged by the count to secure the painter’s future.

Eager for assistance, Georges announced he worked for a newly formed company called “l’Espérance,” whose statutes were approved by royal ordinance. He explained it provided dowries to girls after ten years, annuities to old men, education for children—handling fortunes and appointing agents across France. Leger wryly likened it to an insurance company; Georges called himself the inspector-general tasked with finding honest correspondents and agents—a delicate, difficult job. Pierrotin and Oscar joked; Georges replied curtly when Oscar asked how he had lost his thirty thousand a year: “As you lost your arm,” to which Oscar returned a bitter, sarcastic remark. Georges admitted he had too many shares he could not sell.

They halted at Saint-Leu-Taverny to change horses and allow passengers to disembark briefly. Oscar admired Pierrotin’s agility as he unhooked the traces. Oscar introduced his mother to Pierrotin, who confessed he wouldn’t have known them and remarked that Africa darkens the skin. Madame Moreau de l’Oise, alighting from the coupe, heard the name and glanced disdainfully at Oscar and his mother.

Oscar’s attempt to be gracious to Pierrotin would prove one of his last vanities: two months after settling in Beaumont-sur-Oise, he began courting Pierrotin’s daughter, Georgette, whose dowry was one hundred and fifty thousand francs, and married her in late winter 1838.

The narrative notes that the Presles journey had taught Oscar discretion; his disaster at Florentine’s card-party had strengthened his honesty; military hardships taught him social restraint and acceptance of his lot. He became prudent and capable, content enough. Thanks to the Comte de Serizy, who before his death obtained him the collectorship at Pontoise, and with Moreau de l’Oise’s influence and support from the Comtesse de Serizy and the Baron de Canalis, Oscar later obtained a receiver-generalship and was recognized by the Camusot family as a relation. He emerges as a commonplace, gentle, modest man who keeps to a middle course like his government, exciting neither envy nor contempt—the modern bourgeois.

ADDENDUM

The Human Comedy repeatedly reuses characters; many figures mentioned here recur across Balzac’s works. Representative examples include:

- Joseph Bridau — appears notably in The Purse and Modeste Mignon.
- Georges Marest and Frédéric Marest — recur (see The Peasantry; The Member for Arcis).
- Baron de Canalis — recurs in Letters of Two Brides; Modeste Mignon; The Member for Arcis.
- The Serizy family (Comte, Comtesse, Vicomte) — appear in multiple pieces (A Bachelor’s Establishment; Modeste Mignon; The Imaginary Mistress; Scenes from a Courtesan’s Life).
- Leon de Lora (Mistigris), Pierre Grassou, Cesar Birotteau, and other provincial or artistic figures — recur across novels such as The Unconscious Humorists; Pierre Grassou; Cesar Birotteau.
- Officials and bureaucrats like Finot, Desroches, Schinner, and Poiret appear in government- or society-focused tales (The Government Clerks; The Middle Classes; A Distinguished Provincial at Paris).
- Nobles and recurring romantic leads—Vandenesse, Gondreville, Maufrigneuse, the Duc de Maufrigneuse—feature in The Lily of the Valley; The Secrets of a Princess; The Gondreville Mystery.
- Additional recurring names: Beaupré (Fanny), Bruel, Cabirolle, Crottat, Derville, Godeschal, Grindot, Grevin, Loraux, Lupin, Maufrigneuse, Poiret, Rouvre, Schinner, and many others who populate Balzac’s interconnected panorama.

These cross-references underscore Balzac’s technique of a shared universe, where characters move between novels to create a comprehensive social tableau.
