LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES

By Honore de Balzac

Translated by R. S. Scott

DEDICATION

To George Sand

Your name, dear George, adds a reflected radiance to my book, not from self-interest or diffidence, but to witness our enduring friendship, which has triumphed over the world's malice. This bond is unlikely to change. The names attached to my works bring me pleasure amidst the vexation of their increasing number. Each new book brings fresh annoyance, as if my pen could rival the world from which I draw my models! Would it not be wonderful if future antiquarians found only great names and generous hearts among my friends? I take pride in this possession rather than in unstable popularity. For those who know you well, it is happiness to sign myself, as I do here,

Your friend,  
DE BALZAC.  
PARIS, June 1840.

LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES

FIRST PART

I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.

Sweetheart, I too am free! I am the first to write, unless you have already sent a letter to Blois.

Raise those great black eyes of yours, fixed on my opening sentence, and save your excitement for the letter that will tell you of my first love. By the way, why always "first?" Is there a second love?

Don't run on like this, you will say, but tell me how you escaped from the convent where you were to take your vows. My own deliverance was ordinary. The cries of an alarmed conscience triumphed over stern policy. The melancholy that seized me after you left hastened my aunt's decision; she did not want to see me die of decline, and my mother, whose cure for my malady was a novitiate, relented.

So I am in Paris, thanks to you, my love! If you had seen me the day I parted from you, you would have gloried in the deep impression you made on my youthful heart. We had lived so closely together, sharing dreams and letting our fancies roam, that I believe our souls became welded together, like those two Hungarian girls whose tragic story we heard from M. Beauvisage—poor man! Never was anyone better suited to the role of convent physician!

Tell me, did you not droop and sicken without your darling?

In my gloomy depression, I could only count the ties that bind us. It seemed distance had loosened them; I wearied of life, like a turtle-dove widowed of her mate. Death smiled sweetly on me, and I was quietly proceeding to die. To be at Blois, at the Carmelites, consumed by dread of taking my vows there, a Mlle. de la Valliere without her prelude, and without you! How could I not be sick—sick unto death?

How different it used to be! That monotonous existence, where every hour brings its duty, prayer, and task, with such desperate regularity that you could tell what a Carmelite sister was doing at any hour, had become for us a world of life and movement. Imagination opened her fairy realms, and our spirits roamed freely, each serving as a magic steed to the other. The world from which our bodies were shut out became our playground, where we reveled in frolicsome adventure. Even the Lives of the Saints helped us understand what was left unsaid! But the day I lost your sweet company, I became a true Carmelite, like a modern Danaid, drawing an empty pitcher from Heaven knows where, thinking to find it full.

My aunt knew nothing of this inner life. How could she, who has made a paradise for herself within the two acres of her convent, understand my revolt against life? A religious life, if embraced by girls of our age, demands either extreme simplicity of soul, which we do not possess, or an ardor for self-sacrifice like my aunt's noble character. But she sacrificed herself for a devoted brother; to do the same for an unknown person or an idea is surely more than can be asked of mortals.

For the last fortnight, I have been swallowing reckless words, burying reflections in my bosom, and accumulating a store of things to tell you, fit for your ear alone. I would have suffocated without the resource of letter-writing as a poor substitute for our beloved talks. How hungry one's heart gets! I am beginning my journal this morning, picturing that yours is already started, and that soon I shall be at home in your beautiful Gemenos valley, which I know only through your descriptions, just as you will live that Paris life revealed to you only in our dreams.

Well, sweet child, know that on a certain morning—a red-letter day in my life—a lady companion and Philippe, the last of my grandmother's valets, arrived from Paris to carry me off. When my aunt summoned me to her room and told me the news, I could not speak for joy, only gazed at her stupidly.

"My child," she said, "I can see you leave me without regret, but this farewell is not the last; we shall meet again. God has placed on your forehead the sign of the elect. You have the pride that leads to heaven or hell, but your nature is too noble to choose the downward path. I know you better than you know yourself; with you, passion will be very different from what it is with most women."

She drew me gently to her and kissed my forehead. The kiss burned with the consuming fire that has turned her life to black, softened the lines around her eyes, and cast a sallow tinge over her beautiful face.

Before replying, I kissed her hands.

"Dear aunt," I said, "I shall never forget your kindness; and if it has not made your nunnery all that it ought to be for my health of body and soul, you may be sure nothing short of a broken heart will bring me back again—and that you would not wish for me. You will not see me here again till my royal lover has deserted me, and I warn you that if I catch him, death alone shall tear him from me. I fear no Montespan."

She smiled and said:

"Go, madcap, and take your idle fancies with you. There is certainly more of the bold Montespan in you than of the gentle la Valliere."

I threw my arms around her. The poor lady could not refrain from escorting me to the carriage. There her tender gaze was divided between me and the armorial bearings.

At Beaugency, night overtook me, still sunk in a stupor of mind produced by these strange parting words. What awaits me in this world for which I have so hungered?

To begin with, I found no one to receive me; my heart had been schooled in vain. My mother was at the Bois de Boulogne, my father at the Council; my brother, the Duc de Rhetore, never comes in until it is time to dress for dinner. Miss Griffith (she resembles a griffin) and Philippe took me to my rooms.

The suite belonged to my beloved grandmother, the Princess de Vauremont, to whom I owe some fortune no one has ever mentioned. As you read this, you will understand the sadness that came over me as I entered a place sacred to so many memories, finding the rooms just as she had left them! I was to sleep in the bed where she died.

Sitting on the edge of the sofa, I burst into tears, forgetting I was not alone, remembering how often I had stood there by her knees to hear her words. I had gazed upon her face, buried in brown lace, worn by age and the pangs of approaching death. The room seemed still warm with her presence. How is it that Armande-Louise-Marie de Chaulieu must be like a peasant girl, who sleeps in her mother's bed the very morrow of her death? For me, it was as though the Princess, who died in 1817, had passed away but yesterday.

I saw many things in the room that ought to have been removed. Their presence showed the carelessness with which busy people treat their own affairs, and the little thought given since her death to this grand old lady, a striking figure of the eighteenth century. Philippe seemed to sense my tears. He told me that the Princess's furniture had been left to me in her will, and that my father had allowed the larger suites to remain dismantled, as the Revolution had left them. Upon hearing this, I rose, and Philippe opened the door to the small drawing-room leading into the reception-rooms.

In these, I found all the well-remembered wreckage; the panels above the doors, which had contained valuable pictures, bare of all but empty frames; broken marbles, mirrors carried off. In old days, I was afraid to go up the state staircase and cross these vast, deserted rooms; I used to get to the Princess's rooms by a small staircase that runs under the arch of the larger one, leading to the secret door of her dressing-room.

My suite, consisting of a drawing-room, bedroom, and pretty morning-room in scarlet and gold, lies in the wing on the side of the Invalides. The house is separated from the boulevard by a wall covered with creepers and a splendid avenue of trees, mingling their foliage with that of the young elms on the sidewalk. But for the blue-and-gold dome of the Invalides and its gray stone mass, you might be in a wood.

The style of decoration in these rooms, along with their situation, indicates they were the old show suite of the duchesses, while the dukes must have had theirs in the opposite wing. The two suites are decorously separated by the two main blocks, as well as by the central one, which contained those vast, gloomy, resounding halls shown to me by Philippe, all despoiled of their splendor, as in my childhood.

Philippe grew quite confidential when he saw my surprise. In this home of diplomacy, even the servants have a reserved and mysterious air. He told me that a law would soon be passed restoring the value of property to the fugitives of the Revolution, and that my father is waiting to renovate the house until this restitution is made, the king's architect having estimated the damage at three hundred thousand livres.

This news flung me back despairing on my drawing-room sofa. Could it be that my father, instead of spending this money arranging a marriage for me, would have left me to die in the convent? This was the first thought to greet me on the threshold of my home.

Ah! Renee, what would I have given then to rest my head upon your shoulder, or to return to the days when my grandmother filled these rooms with life? You two in all the world have loved me—one away at Maucombe, and the other who survives only in my heart, the dear old lady, whose still youthful eyes opened from sleep at my call. How well we understood each other!

These memories suddenly changed my mood. What had seemed profanation now breathed of holy association. It was sweet to inhale the faint odor of the powder she loved still lingering in the room; sweet to sleep beneath the shelter of those yellow damask curtains, which must have retained something of her spirit. I told Philippe to polish the old furniture and make the rooms look lived in; I explained how I wanted everything arranged, and where to place each piece. In this way, I entered into possession, showing how an air of youth might be given to the dear old things.

The bedroom is white, a little dulled with time, harmonizing well with the faded colors of the Savonnerie tapestry, presented to my grandmother by Louis XV. The timepiece was a gift from the Marechal de Saxe, and the china ornaments on the mantelpiece came from the Marechal de Richelieu. My grandmother's portrait, painted at twenty-five, hangs in an oval frame opposite that of the King. The Prince, her husband, is conspicuous by his absence. I like this frank negligence, untinged by hypocrisy—a characteristic touch summing up her charming personality. Once, when my grandmother was seriously ill, her confessor urged that the Prince, waiting in the drawing-room, should be admitted.

"He can come in with the doctor and his drugs," was her reply.

The bed has a canopy and well-stuffed back, with curtains looped up with fine wide bands. The furniture is gilded wood, upholstered in the same yellow damask with white flowers that drapes the windows, lined with white silk that looks watered. The panels over the doors have been painted, representing a sunrise and a moonlight scene.

The fireplace is a very interesting feature. Life in the last century centered around the hearth, where great events were enacted. The copper gilt grate is a marvel of workmanship, and the mantelpiece is delicately finished; the fire-irons are beautifully chased; the bellows are a perfect gem. The tapestry of the screen comes from the Gobelins and is exquisitely mounted; charming figures run all over the frame, on the feet, the supporting bar, and the wings; the whole thing is wrought like a fan.

Dearly would I like to know who was the giver of this dainty work of art, which was such a favorite with her. How often have I seen the old lady, her feet upon the bar, reclining in the easy-chair, toying with the snuff-box, which lay upon the ledge between her box of pastilles and her silk mitts. What a coquette she was! To the day of her death, she took as much care with her appearance as though the beautiful portrait had been painted only yesterday, and she were waiting to receive the throng of exquisites from the Court! How the armchair recalls the inimitable sweep of her skirts as she sank back in it!

These women of a past generation carried off secrets typical of their age. The Princess had a certain turn of the head, a way of dropping her glance and remarks, a choice of words, which I look for in vain, even in my mother. There was subtlety and good nature; the points were made without affectation. Her talk was lengthy yet concise; she told a good story and could express her meaning in three words. Above all, she was extremely free-thinking, which has undoubtedly influenced my perspective.

From seven to ten, I never left her side; it pleased her to attract me as much as it pleased me to go. This preference caused more than one passage at arms between her and my mother, and nothing intensifies feeling like the icy breath of persecution. How charming was her greeting, "Here you are, little rogue!" when curiosity taught me to glide stealthily to her room. She felt that I loved her, and this childish affection was welcome as a ray of sunshine in the winter of her life.

I don't know what went on in her rooms at night, but she had many visitors; when I tiptoed in the morning to see if she were awake, I would find the drawing-room furniture disarranged, card-tables set out, and patches of snuff scattered about.

This drawing-room is furnished in the same style as the bedroom. The chairs and tables are oddly shaped, with claw feet and hollow moldings. Rich garlands of flowers, beautifully designed and carved, wind over the mirrors and hang down in festoons. On the consoles are fine china vases. The ground colors are scarlet and white. My grandmother was a high-spirited, striking brunette, as might be inferred from her choice of colors. I have found in the drawing-room a writing-table I remember well; the figures on it used to fascinate me; it is plaited in graven silver, a present from one of the Genoese Lomellini. Each side of the table represents the occupations of a different season, with hundreds of figures in relief.

I remained alone for two hours, while old memories rose before me, one after another, in this spot hallowed by the death of a remarkable woman among the witty and beautiful Court ladies of Louis XV.'s day.

You know how abruptly I was parted from her, at a day's notice, in 1816.

"Go and bid good-bye to your grandmother," said my mother.

The Princess received me as usual, without display of feeling, expressing no surprise at my departure.

"You are going to the convent, dear," she said, "and will see your aunt there, who is an excellent woman. I shall take care, though, that they don't make a victim of you; you shall be independent and able to marry whom you please."

Six months later, she died. Her will was given into the keeping of the Prince de Talleyrand, her most devoted friend. He contrived, while visiting Mlle. de Chargeboeuf, to intimate through her that my grandmother forbade me to take the vows. I hope to meet the Prince soon, and then I shall learn more from him.

Thus, sweetheart, if I have found no one in flesh and blood to meet me, I have comforted myself with the shade of the dear Princess and prepared to carry out one of our pledges—to keep each other informed of the smallest details in our homes and occupations. It makes such a difference to know where and how the life of one we love is passed. Send me a faithful picture of the veriest trifles around you, omitting nothing, not even the sunset lights among the tall trees.

October 19th.

It was three in the afternoon when I arrived. About half-past five, Rose came and told me that my mother had returned, so I went downstairs to pay my respects to her.

My mother lives in a suite on the ground floor, exactly corresponding to mine, in the same block. I am just over her head, and the same secret staircase serves for both. My father's rooms are in the opposite block, larger by the space occupied by the grand staircase on our side. These ancestral mansions are so spacious that my parents continue to occupy the ground-floor rooms, despite the social duties that have returned with the Bourbons, and can even receive guests there.

I found my mother, dressed for the evening, in her drawing-room, where nothing had changed. I came slowly down the stairs, speculating how I would be met by this mother who had shown so little maternal affection, from whom I had heard nothing beyond the two letters you know. Judging it unworthy to simulate an affection I could not feel, I put on the air of a pious imbecile and entered the room with many inward qualms, which soon disappeared. My mother’s tact was equal to the occasion. She made no pretense of emotion; she neither held me at arm's length nor hugged me to her bosom like a beloved daughter, but greeted me as though we had parted the evening before. Her manner was that of the kindliest and most sincere friend, addressing me like a grown person, first kissing me on the forehead.

"My dear little one," she said, "if you were to die at the convent, it is much better to live with your family. You frustrate your father's plans and mine; but the age of blind obedience to parents is past. M. de Chaulieu's intention, and in this I am quite at one with him, is to lose no opportunity of making your life pleasant and letting you see the world. At your age, I would have thought as you do, therefore I am not vexed with you; it is impossible you should understand what we expected from you. You will not find any absurd severity in me; and if you have ever thought me heartless, you will soon find out your mistake. Still, though I wish you to feel perfectly free, I think that, to begin with, you would do well to follow the counsels of a mother who wishes to be a sister to you."

I was charmed by the Duchess, who spoke gently, straightening my convent tippet as she did. At thirty-eight, she is still exquisitely beautiful. She has dark-blue eyes, silken lashes, a smooth forehead, and a complexion so pink and white that you might think she paints. Her bust and shoulders are marvelous, and her waist is as slender as yours. Her hand is milk-white and extraordinarily beautiful; the nails catch the light in their perfect polish, the thumb is like ivory, the little finger stands just a little apart from the rest, and the foot matches the hand; it is the Spanish foot of Mlle. de Vandenesse. If she is like this at forty, she will still be beautiful at sixty.

I replied, sweetheart, like a good little girl. I was as nice to her as she was to me, nay, nicer. Her beauty completely vanquished me; it seemed only natural that such a woman should be absorbed in her regal part. I told her this as simply as if I were talking to you. I daresay it surprised her to hear words of affection from her daughter's mouth, and the unfeigned homage of my admiration evidently touched her deeply. Her manner changed and became even more engaging; she dropped all formality as she said:

"I am much pleased with you, and I hope we shall remain good friends."

The words struck me as charmingly naive, but I did not let this appear, for I saw at once that the prudent course was to allow her to believe herself much deeper and cleverer than her daughter. So I only stared vacantly, and she was delighted. I kissed her hands repeatedly, telling her how happy it made me to be treated so and to feel at ease with her. I even confided my previous tremors. She smiled, put her arm around my neck, and drawing me towards her, kissed me on the forehead most affectionately.

"Dear child," she said, "we have people coming to dinner today. Perhaps you will agree with me that it is better for you not to make your first appearance in society until you have been in the dressmaker's hands; so, after you have seen your father and brother, you can go upstairs again."

I assented most heartily. My mother's exquisite dress was the first revelation to me of the world our dreams had pictured; but I did not feel the slightest desire to rival her.

My father now entered, and the Duchess presented me to him.

He became all at once most affectionate, playing the father's part so well that I could not but believe his heart was in it. Taking my two hands in his and kissing them, with more of the lover than the father in his manner, he said:

"So this is my rebel daughter!"

He drew me towards him, with his arm passed tenderly around my waist, kissing me on the cheeks and forehead.

"The pleasure with which we shall watch your success in society will atone for the disappointment we felt at your change of vocation," he said. Then, turning to my mother, "Do you know that she is going to turn out very pretty, and you will be proud of her some day?—Here is your brother, Rhetore." He introduced a fine young man who came in, "Alphonse, here is your convent-bred sister, who threatens to send her nun's frock to the deuce."

My brother came up leisurely and took my hand, which he pressed.

"Come, come, you may kiss her," said my father.

And he kissed me on both cheeks.

"I am delighted to see you," he said, "and I take your side against my father."

I thanked him, but could not help thinking he might have come to Blois when he was at Orleans visiting our Marquis brother in his quarters.

Fearing the arrival of strangers, I now withdrew. I tidied up my rooms and laid out on the scarlet velvet of my lovely table all the materials necessary for writing to you, meditating all the while on my new situation.

This, my fair sweetheart, is a true and veracious account of the return of a girl of eighteen, after an absence of nine years, to the bosom of one of the noblest families in the kingdom. I was tired from the journey and all the emotions I had been through, so I went to bed in convent fashion, at eight o'clock after supper. They have preserved even a little Saxe service which the dear Princess used when she had a fancy for taking her meals alone.

II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.

The next day, I found my rooms done out and dusted, and even flowers put in the vases by old Philippe. I began to feel at home. Only it didn't occur to anyone that a Carmelite schoolgirl has an early appetite, and Rose had no end of trouble getting breakfast for me.

"Mlle. goes to bed at dinner-time," she said, "and gets up when the Duke is just returning home."

I began to write. About one o'clock, my father knocked at the door of the small drawing-room and asked if he might come in. I opened the door; he came in and found me writing to you.

"My dear," he began, "you will have to get yourself clothes and make these rooms comfortable. In this purse, you will find twelve thousand francs, which is the yearly income I purpose allowing you for your expenses. You will make arrangements with your mother for a governess you may like, in case Miss Griffith doesn't please you, for Mme. de Chaulieu will not have time to go out with you in the mornings. A carriage and man-servant shall be at your disposal."

"Let me keep Philippe," I said.

"So be it," he replied. "But don't be uneasy; you have money enough of your own to be no burden either to your mother or me."

"May I ask how much I have?"

"Certainly, my child," he said. "Your grandmother left you five hundred thousand francs; this was the amount of her savings, for she would not alienate a foot of land from the family. This sum has been placed in Government stock, and, with the accumulated interest, now brings in about forty thousand francs a year. With this, I had purposed making an independence for your second brother, and it is here that you have upset my plans. Later, however, it is possible that you may fall in with them. It shall rest with yourself, for I have confidence in your good sense far more than I had expected.

"I do not need to tell you how a daughter of the Chaulieus ought to behave. The pride so plainly written in your features is my best guarantee. Safeguards, such as common folk surround their daughters with, would be an insult in our family. A slander reflecting on your name might cost the life of the man bold enough to utter it, or the life of one of your brothers, if by chance the right should not prevail. No more on this subject. Good-bye, little one."

He kissed me on the forehead and went out. I cannot understand the relinquishment of this plan after nine years' persistence in it. My father's frankness is what I like. There is no ambiguity about his words. My money ought to belong to his Marquis son. Who, then, has had bowels of mercy? My mother? My father? Or could it be my brother?

I remained sitting on my grandmother's sofa, staring at the purse my father had left on the mantelpiece, pleased and vexed that I could not withdraw my mind from the money. Further speculation was useless. My doubts had been cleared up, and there was something fine in the way my pride was spared.

Philippe has spent the morning rushing about among the various shops and workpeople who are to undertake the task of my metamorphosis. A famous dressmaker, by name Victorine, has come, as well as a woman for underclothing, and a shoemaker. I am as impatient as a child to know what I shall be like when I emerge from the sack that constituted the conventual uniform; but all these tradespeople take a long time; the corset-maker requires a whole week if my figure is not to be spoiled. You see, I have a figure, dear; this becomes serious. Janssen, the Operatic shoemaker, solemnly assures me that I have my mother's foot. The whole morning has gone in these weighty occupations. Even a glovemaker has come to take the measure of my hand. The underclothing woman has got my orders.

At the meal I call dinner, and the others lunch, my mother told me that we were going together to the milliner's to see some hats, so that my taste should be formed, and I might be in a position to order my own.

This burst of independence dazzles me. I am like a blind man who has just recovered his sight. Now I begin to understand the vast interval separating a Carmelite sister from a girl in society. Of ourselves, we could never have conceived it.

During this lunch, my father seemed absent-minded, and we left him to his thoughts; he is deep in the King's confidence. I was entirely forgotten; but from what I have seen, I have no doubt he will remember me when he has need of me. He is a very attractive man in spite of his fifty years. His figure is youthful; he is well made, fair, and extremely graceful in his movements. He has a diplomatic face, at once dumb and expressive; his nose is long and slender, and he has brown eyes.

What a handsome pair! Strange thoughts assail me as it becomes plain to me that these two, so perfectly matched in birth, wealth, and mental superiority, live entirely apart, having nothing in common but their name. The show of unity is only for the world.

The cream of the Court and diplomatic circles were here last night. Very soon, I am going to a ball given by the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, and I shall be presented to the society I am so eager to know. A dancing-master is coming every morning to give me lessons, for I must be able to dance in a month, or I can't go to the ball.

Before dinner, my mother came to talk about the governess with me. I have decided to keep Miss Griffith, who was recommended by the English ambassador. Miss Griffith is the daughter of a clergyman; her mother was of good family, and she is perfectly well bred. She is thirty-six and will teach me English. The good soul is quite handsome enough to have ambitions; she is Scotch—poor and proud—and will act as my chaperon. She is to sleep in Rose's room. Rose will be under her orders. I saw at a glance that my governess would be governed by me. In the six days we have been together, she has made sure that I am the only person likely to take an interest in her; while, for my part, I have ascertained that, for all her statuesque features, she will prove accommodating. She seems to me a kindly soul, but cautious. I have not been able to extract a word of what passed between her and my mother.

Another trifling piece of news! My father has this morning refused the appointment as Minister of State which was offered him. This accounts for his preoccupied manner last night. He says he would prefer an embassy to the worries of public debate.

This news was told to me at lunch, the one moment of the day when my father, mother, and brother see each other in an easy way. The servants then only come when they are rung for. The rest of the day, my brother, as well as my father, spends out of the house. My mother has her toilet to make; between two and four, she is never visible; at four o'clock, she goes out for an hour's drive; when she is not dining out, she receives from six to seven, and the evening is given to entertainments of various kinds—theatres, balls, concerts, at homes. In short, her life is so full that I don't believe she ever has a quarter of an hour to herself. She must spend considerable time dressing in the morning; for at lunch, which takes place between eleven and twelve, she is exquisite. The meaning of the things said about her is dawning on me. She begins the day with a bath barely warmed and a cup of cold coffee with cream; then she dresses. She is never, except on some great emergency, called before nine o'clock. In summer, there are morning rides, and at two o'clock, she receives a young man whom I have never yet contrived to see.

Behold our family life! We meet at lunch and dinner, though often I am alone with my mother at this latter meal, and I foresee that still oftener I shall take it in my own rooms (following the example of my grandmother) with only Miss Griffith for company, for my mother frequently dines out. I have ceased to wonder at the indifference my family has shown to me. In Paris, my dear, it is a miracle of virtue to love the people who live with you, for you see little enough of them; as for the absent—they do not exist!

Knowing as this may sound, I have not yet set foot in the streets, and am deplorably ignorant. I must wait until I am less of the country cousin and have brought my dress and deportment into keeping with the society I am about to enter, the whirl of which amazes me even here, where only distant murmurs reach my ear. So far, I have not gone beyond the garden; but the Italian opera opens in a few days, and my mother has a box there. I am crazy with delight at the thought of hearing Italian music and seeing French acting.

Already I begin to drop convent habits for those of society. I spend the evening writing to you until the moment for going to bed arrives. This has been postponed to ten o'clock, the hour at which my mother goes out, if she is not at the theatre. There are twelve theatres in Paris.

I am grossly ignorant and I read a lot, but quite indiscriminately, one book leading to another. I find the names of fresh books on the cover of the one I am reading; but as I have no one to direct me, I light on some that are fearfully dull. What modern literature I have read all turns upon love, the subject that used to bulk so largely in our thoughts, because it seemed our fate was determined by man and for man. But how inferior are these authors to two little girls, known as Sweetheart and Darling—otherwise Renee and Louise. Ah! my love, what wretched plots, what ridiculous situations, and what poverty of sentiment! Two books, however, have given me wonderful pleasure—Corinne and Adolphe. Apropos of this, I asked my father one day whether it would be possible for me to see Mme. de Stael. My father, mother, and Alphonse all burst out laughing, and Alphonse said:

"Where in the world has she sprung from?"

To which my father replied:

"What fools we are! She springs from the Carmelites."

"My child, Mme. de Stael is dead," said my mother gently.

When I finished Adolphe, I asked Miss Griffith how a woman could be betrayed.

"Why, of course, when she loves," was her reply.

Renee, tell me, do you think we could be betrayed by a man?

Miss Griffith has at last discerned that I am not an utter ignoramus, that I have somewhere a hidden vein of knowledge, the knowledge we learned from each other in our random arguments. She sees that it is only superficial facts of which I am ignorant. The poor thing has opened her heart to me. Her curt reply to my question, when I compare it with all the sorrows I can imagine, makes me feel quite creepy. Once more, she urged me not to be dazzled by the glitter of society, to be always on my guard, especially against what most attracts me. This is the sum-total of her wisdom, and I can get nothing more out of her. Her lectures, therefore, become a trifle monotonous, and she might be compared in this respect to the bird which has only one cry.

III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.

My Darling,—Here I am ready to make my bow to the world. By way of preparation, I have been trying to commit all the follies I could think of before sobering down for my entry. This morning, I have seen myself, after many rehearsals, well and duly equipped—stays, shoes, curls, dress, ornaments—all in order. Following the example of duelists before a meeting, I tried my arms in the privacy of my chamber. I wanted to see how I would look and had no difficulty discovering a certain air of victory and triumph, bound to carry all before it. I mustered all my forces, in accordance with that splendid maxim of antiquity, "Know thyself!" and boundless was my delight in thus making my own acquaintance. Griffith was the sole spectator of this doll's play, in which I was at once doll and child. You think you know me? You are hugely mistaken.

Here is a portrait, then, Renee, of your sister, formerly disguised as a Carmelite, now brought to life again as a frivolous society girl. She is one of the greatest beauties in France—Provence, of course, excepted. I don't see how I can give a more accurate summary of this interesting topic.

True, I have my weak points; but were I a man, I should adore them. They arise from what is most promising in me. When you have spent a fortnight admiring the exquisite curves of your mother's arms, and that mother the Duchesse de Chaulieu, it is impossible, my dear, not to deplore your own angular elbows. Yet there is consolation in observing the fineness of the wrist and a certain grace of line in those hollows, which will yet fill out and show plump, round, and well-modeled under the satiny skin. The somewhat crude outline of the arms is seen again in the shoulders. Strictly speaking, indeed, I have no shoulders, but only two bony blades, standing out in harsh relief. My figure also lacks pliancy; there is a stiffness about the side lines.

Poof! There's the worst out. But then the contours are bold and delicate, the bright, pure flame of health bites into the vigorous lines, a flood of life and blue blood pulses under the transparent skin, and the fairest daughter of Eve would seem a Negress beside me! I have the foot of a gazelle! My joints are finely turned, my features of Greek correctness. It is true, madame, that the flesh tints do not melt into each other; but at least, they stand out clear and bright. In short, I am a very pretty green fruit, with all the charm of unripeness. I see a great likeness to the face in my aunt's old missal, which rises out of a violet lily.

There is no silly weakness in the blue of my insolent eyes; the white is pure mother-of-pearl, prettily marked with tiny veins, and the thick, long lashes fall like a silken fringe. My forehead sparkles, and the hair grows deliciously; it ripples into waves of pale gold, growing browner towards the center, whence escape little rebel locks, which alone would tell that my fairness is not of the insipid and hysterical type. I am a tropical blonde, with plenty of blood in my veins, a blonde more apt to strike than to turn the cheek. What do you think the hairdresser proposed? He wanted, if you please, to smooth my hair into two bands and place over my forehead a pearl, kept in place by a gold chain! He said it would recall the Middle Ages.

I told him I was not aged enough to have reached the middle or to need an ornament to freshen me up!

The nose is slender, and the well-cut nostrils are separated by a sweet little pink partition—an imperious, mocking nose, with a tip too sensitive ever to grow fat or red. Sweetheart, if this won't find a husband for a dowerless maiden, I'm a donkey. The ears are daintily curled; a pearl hanging from either lobe would show yellow. The neck is long, with an undulating motion full of dignity. In the shade, the white ripens to a golden tinge. Perhaps the mouth is a little large. But how expressive! what a color on the lips! how prettily the teeth laugh!

Then, dear, there is a harmony running through all. What a gait! what a voice! We have not forgotten how our grandmother's skirts fell into place without a touch. In a word, I am lovely and charming. When the mood comes, I can laugh one of our good old laughs, and no one will think the less of me; the dimples, impressed by Comedy's light fingers on my fair cheeks, will command respect. Or I can let my eyes fall and my heart freeze under my snowy brows. I can pose as a Madonna with melancholy, swan-like neck, and the painters' virgins will be nowhere; my place in heaven would be far above them. A man would be forced to chant when he spoke to me.

So, you see, my panoply is complete, and I can run the whole gamut of coquetry from deepest bass to shrillest treble. It is a huge advantage not to be all of one piece. Now, my mother is neither playful nor virginal. Her only attitude is an imposing one; when she ceases to be majestic, she is ferocious. It is difficult for her to heal the wounds she makes, whereas I can wound and heal together. We are absolutely unlike, and therefore there could not possibly be rivalry between us, unless indeed we quarreled over the greater or lesser perfection of our extremities, which are similar. I take after my father, who is shrewd and subtle. I have the manner of my grandmother and her charming voice, which becomes falsetto when forced, but is a sweet-toned chest voice at the ordinary pitch of a quiet talk.

I feel as if I had left the convent today for the first time. For society, I do not yet exist; I am unknown to it. What a ravishing moment! I still belong only to myself, like a flower just blown, unseen yet by mortal eye.

In spite of this, my sweet, as I paced the drawing-room during my self-inspection, and saw the poor cast-off school-clothes, a queer feeling came over me. Regret for the past, anxiety about the future, fear of society, a long farewell to the pale daisies we used to pick and strip of their petals in light-hearted innocence—there was something of all that; but strange, fantastic visions also rose, which I crushed back into the inner depths, whence they had sprung, and whither I dared not follow them.

My Renee, I have a regular trousseau! It is all beautifully laid away and perfumed in the cedar-wood drawers with lacquered front of my charming dressing-table. There are ribbons, shoes, gloves, all in lavish abundance. My father has kindly presented me with the pretty gewgaws a girl loves—a dressing-case, toilet service, scent-box, fan, sunshade, prayer-book, gold chain, cashmere shawl. He has also promised to give me riding lessons. And I can dance! Tomorrow, yes, tomorrow evening, I come out!

My dress is white muslin, and on my head, I wear a garland of white roses in Greek style. I shall put on my Madonna face; I mean to play the simpleton and have all the women on my side. My mother is miles away from any idea of what I write to you. She believes me quite destitute of mind and would be dumfounded if she read my letter. My brother honors me with profound contempt and is uniformly and politely indifferent.

He is a handsome young fellow, but melancholy and given to moods. I have divined his secret, though neither the Duke nor Duchess has an inkling of it. In spite of his youth and title, he is jealous of his father. He has no position in the State, no post at Court; he never has to say, "I am going to the Chamber." I alone in the house have sixteen hours for meditation. My father is absorbed in public business and his own amusements; my mother, too, is never at leisure; no member of the household practices self-examination; they are constantly in company and have hardly time to live.

I should immensely like to know what potent charm society wields to keep people prisoner from nine every evening until two or three in the morning, forcing them to lavish strength and money. When I longed for it, I had no idea of the separations it brought about or its overmastering spell. But then, I forget, it is Paris that does it all.

It is possible, it seems, for members of one family to live side by side and know absolutely nothing of each other. A half-fledged nun arrives, and in a couple of weeks has grasped domestic details of which the master diplomatist at the head of the house is quite ignorant. Or perhaps he does see and shuts his eyes deliberately, as part of the father's role. There is a mystery here which I must plumb.

Yesterday at two o'clock I drove through the Champs-Elysées and the Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day that reminded me of the Loire. I finally experienced Paris! The Place Louis XV is impressive, a testament to human artistry.

I was impeccably dressed, pensive, and poised, yet not a single smile was aimed at me. The carriage moved slowly, matching my demeanor. Only one person, a duke—my father—acknowledged me with satisfaction; my mother sent a butterfly kiss. Griffith, ever observant, scanned indiscriminately. A young woman should know where to look.

I was furious. One man even inspected my carriage without seeing me—probably a carriage-maker. I had misjudged my appeal; beauty, it seems, is common in Paris. I saw hats tipped to less deserving people while my mother received admiration. I am determined to understand this.

The men were generally unattractive, with a few poor imitations among them. Their fashion lacks the elegance, color, and comfort of previous generations. Hats, like truncated columns, sit awkwardly on the head. It seems easier to incite a revolution than to create a stylish hat. Despite their fickle reputation, Frenchmen cling to these ridiculous styles.

Overall the men felt unappealing, their worn faces reflecting ambition thwarted and vanity bruised. A fine forehead was rare. "And these are the products of Paris!" I said to Miss Griffith. "Most cultivated and pleasant men," she replied. I was silent; a thirty-six-year-old spinster's heart is a well of tolerance.

In the evening I went to a ball, staying close to my mother, who received all attention. I was merely a pretext for compliments aimed at her. She found me partners who all noted the heat and beauty of the ball, as if I were freezing and blind. Each man claimed it was his first time seeing me.

My dazzling dress paled beside the extravagant attire of married women, each surrounded by admirers and watching one another closely. A few shone with actual beauty, my mother among them. At a ball a girl is merely a dancing machine, insignificant.

The men impressed me no more at the ball than on the Champs-Elysées: exhausted, bland, and uniform. The proud, vigorous men of our ancestors seem vanished. One man, however, stood out for his beauty yet failed to stir the expected feeling. I did not know his works, and he lacked noble birth. No matter his genius, a commoner could not touch me. He seemed absorbed in himself, suggesting intellectuals view us as objects. When they love, they should abandon their writing; otherwise their love is not genuine. I learned he is a professor and author, driven by ambition.

I resolved not to quarrel with society for failing to recognize my merits and instead embraced the simple joy of dancing, which I thoroughly enjoyed. I overheard much trivial gossip about people I did not know; perhaps my ignorance kept me from enjoying it, though many seemed to relish such talk. Society is a web of intrigue, and I am perceptive.

Returning home pleasantly tired, I began sharing my experiences with my mother. She interrupted, advising me to confide only in her. "My dear child," she said, "it requires as much tact to know when to be silent as when to speak."

Her advice revealed feelings that should remain concealed, even from a mother. I realized the vast realm of feminine duplicity. In our innocence we would seem astute gossipers; a mere gesture or glance can teach profound lessons. Suddenly I felt overwhelming shyness. Must I never again express my joy in dancing? What of deeper emotions?

I went to bed sorrowful, reeling from the clash between my candid, joyful nature and society's harsh rules. Already the hedges of my life are flecked with white wool! Farewell, beloved.

How deeply your letter moved me, especially when I reflect on our divergent paths! You are entering a brilliant world; I will conclude my modest life in a peaceful retreat.

In the Castle of Maucombe my room remains as I left it, now with a splendid view of the Gemenos valley that I once saw without understanding. Shortly after my arrival my parents took me to dine with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy old gentleman who prospered by frugality.

M. de l'Estorade lost his only son to Bonaparte's conscription in 1813. After evading the draft, the son joined the Emperor's bodyguard but was not heard from after the Battle of Leipzig. The Baron clung to hope that his son lived and hoarded his income for him. Eventually I realized the son's unexpected return explained my own: who could have imagined my future husband was making his way back from Russia, Poland, and Germany? His fortune turned in Berlin, where the French Minister facilitated his return. The Baron, a small landowner in Provence with an income of about ten thousand livres, has not become famed enough to draw attention to his son's adventures.

The combined income from the late Mme. de l'Estorade's estate and the father's savings amounts to around two hundred and fifty thousand livres, excluding property—quite a fortune in Provence. The Baron recently bought a poorly managed estate, intending to plant ten thousand mulberry trees. Now that he has found his son, his sole desire is to marry him to a girl of good family.

My parents supported the idea once they learned that Renée de Maucombe would be acceptable without a dowry and that her future inheritance would be recognized in the marriage contract. Similarly, my younger brother Jean de Maucombe signed a document acknowledging an advance on his inheritance. This is how the nobles of Provence navigate the restrictive Civil Code of Bonaparte, which sends many girls into convents rather than into marriage.

The dinner was the first meeting between your sweetheart and the exile. My father-in-law-to-be has no castle, only a simple country house at the entrance of a beautiful valley. The house is unpretentious, with four pebble walls and a red-tiled roof. The garden is typically Provençal, enclosed by low walls of round pebbles and showing neglect.

For all its simplicity, the house of l'Estorade did its best for the dinner, serving us on old silver. The exile, my dear, is like the railing—emaciated and pale, marked by suffering. At thirty-seven he looks much older, with streaks of white in his once-beautiful hair. He is a little deaf, like the Knight of the Sorrowful Countenance.

Despite this, I have graciously agreed to become Mme. de l'Estorade with a dowry of two hundred and fifty thousand livres, on the condition that I can transform the grange into a park. I have requested a water grant from my father to help this. In a month I will be Mme. de l'Estorade, having made a good impression. After enduring Siberia, Louis de l'Estorade seems pleased to marry the fair Renée de Maucombe.

While you prepare to enjoy Paris's vibrant life, I, Renée, descend from our shared dreams into the humble realities of country life. I am determined to bring joy to this young man, who has known little youth, having gone straight from his mother's arms to war and the harshness of Siberia.

I will find pleasure in the quiet country, seeking to enhance the oasis around our home with fine trees and greenery. I envision a park on the hillside, perhaps with a kiosk from which I can glimpse the Mediterranean. I will cultivate orange and lemon trees and embrace the poetry of the surrounding nature. My father-in-law and the Chevalier share my religious sentiments.

My life unfolds like a smooth highway, free from the turmoil of another Bonaparte. If I have children, they will be mine to nurture and guide. If you remain true to your destiny, your children will have a devoted protector in me.

Thus I bid farewell to the romances and adventures we once imagined. My life’s milestones will now revolve around the care of the young Masters de l'Estorade and the mischief they bring. I will embroider their caps and be cherished by a frail man in the Gemenos valley. Perhaps one day I may visit Marseilles, but the dangers of provincial life are minimal. We will take an interest in the silkworms whose mulberry leaves we sell, and we will share in the ordinary ups and downs of Provence. Quarrels will be unlikely, for M. de l'Estorade has promised to leave household matters to me, and I will ensure he keeps that promise.

You, dear Louise, will provide the romance in my life. Share all your adventures, your balls and parties, your attire, and the men you meet. Your other self will always be with you, listening and feeling the excitement. If only I could enjoy some Parisian fun while you managed the household at La Crampade, the name of our grange. Poor M. de l'Estorade thinks he is marrying one woman—will he discover there are two?

I am rambling, and since I can only be foolish through you, I should conclude. One kiss on each cheek—my lips remain untouched, as he has only taken my hand. Our propriety is quite amusing, I assure you. I must go now. Goodbye, dear.

P.S. I have just opened your third letter. I have about one thousand livres to spend; please buy me pretty things we cannot find here or even in Marseilles. While you pursue your affairs, think of the recluse at La Crampade. Remember, neither family has anyone with taste in Paris to make purchases. I will answer your letter later.

VI. DON FELIPE HENAREZ TO DON FERNAND PARIS, September.

The heading of this letter shows the head of your house is safe. The massacre of our ancestors in the Court of Lions forced us into a double identity, but it also gave us Arab cunning, which may have saved my life.

Ferdinand’s fear made his acting convincing enough for Valdez to believe him. Without my intervention, the Admiral would have perished. The Liberals still fail to understand kingship. I know this Bourbon well; his repeated assurances of protection only confirm my suspicions. A true Spaniard does not need to repeat promises; excess words often signal deceit.

Valdez left on an English ship. As soon as I saw the cause of my beloved Spain falter in Andalusia, I set my escape in motion. I instructed my steward, sent coral fishers to meet me at a coast, and while Ferdinand sought to seize me, I was already in my barony of Macumer, among brigands who defy authority.

The last Hispano-Moorish family of Granada has found refuge in an African desert, and even a Saracen horse on an estate inherited from Saracens. The brigands, who once feared my authority, now shield me from the King of Spain's vendetta, proud to defend the Duc de Soria, a Henarez—the first to visit them since the Moors ruled.

I had hoped to live off this estate’s income, but Queverdo’s reports were right. He had twenty-two lives at my disposal but not a real one; vast prairies without houses, virgin forests with no furniture. A million piastres and a resident master for fifty years would be needed to make these lands profitable. I must address this.

The conquered have time to reflect. Seeing my noble country reduced to a corpse for monks to exploit moved me to tears; it foreshadows Spain’s grim future.

In Marseilles I learned of Riego's fall. It struck me that my life would be a prolonged, unnoticed martyrdom. Is existence worth living when one can neither die for country nor live for a woman? To love and conquer—this law is engraved on our sabres and echoed in our palaces. Yet my sabre is broken, the palace in ruins, the living spring dried in barren sand.

Here is my last will and testament.

Don Fernand, you now see why I restrained your enthusiasm and urged loyalty to the rey netto. As your brother and friend I implore you to obey; as your master I command. You will approach the King and claim my dignities, property, office, and titles. He may hesitate, but tell him you are loved by Marie Heredia and she can marry none but a Duc de Soria. This will please the King: the Heredia fortune has long been a barrier to my ruin. Your proposal will seem to deprive me of a final resource, and he will gladly hand over my spoils.

You will then marry Marie. Her secret love for you was no secret to me, and I have prepared the old Count for your ascension. Marie and I discharged our family duties; everything favors you. You are handsome and possess Marie's heart. I am an ill-favored grandee for whom she harbors an unconfessed aversion; any reluctance from her due to my misfortunes you will soon overcome.

As Duc de Soria, you will not regret my predecessor nor lose a maravedi. I will keep my mother's diamonds for my independence; the gap they leave can be filled by Marie's jewels. Send them with my nurse, old Urraca—the only servant I shall retain. No one else makes my chocolate as she does.

During our short revolution, my toil was reduced to bare necessities, covered by my salary. You will find the income from the last two years with your steward. This sum is mine, but a Duc de Soria cannot marry without expenses, so we will share it. You will not refuse this wedding gift from your brigand brother; I intend it so.

The barony of Macumer, not Spanish territory, remains mine. Thus I still have a country and a name should I wish to re-enter the world.

Thank Heaven, this concludes our business, and the house of Soria is saved!

Just as I resign myself to being merely Baron de Macumer, the French cannon announce the arrival of the Duc d'Angouleme. You will understand why I must stop here.

October.

When I arrived I had not ten doubloons. A poor leader finds means to live within calamities he cannot avert: for the vanquished Moor, a horse and the desert; for the frustrated Christian, the cloister and a few gold pieces.

My resignation is weariness. I am not ready to abandon all thoughts of life. Ozalga gave letters of introduction, including one to a bookseller who serves our compatriots as Galignani does the English. He found me eight pupils at three francs a lesson. I teach every other day, earning twelve francs—more than I need. When Urraca arrives I will pass on my connection to another Spanish exile.

I lodge in Rue Hillerin-Bertin with a poor widow who takes boarders. My room faces south, overlooking a small garden. It is quiet; I have green trees to gaze on, and I spend one piastre a day. I am amazed at the calm pleasure of this life, reminiscent of Dionysius at Corinth. From sunrise until ten I smoke and enjoy chocolate, contemplating two Spanish plants—a broom rising from a clump of jessamine—gold on white, colors that thrill any scion of the Moors. At ten I give lessons until four, return for dinner, then read and smoke until bed.

I can endure this life of work, meditation, solitude, and society for a long time. Be happy, Fernand; my abdication brings no regrets. Five days have passed since I wrote my will; they feel like five centuries. Honor, titles, wealth—as if they never existed.

Now that the conventional barriers of respect are down, I open my heart to you, dear boy. Though outwardly grave, my heart is full of tenderness and devotion—never granted to any object, not even she who was to be my bride since childhood. This is the source of my political enthusiasm. Spain took the place of a mistress and received my homage. Now Spain is lost. Beggared of all, I gaze upon the ruins of my former self and ponder the mystery of my existence.

Why was life given this carcass, and when will it depart? Why did a race famed for chivalry infuse all its virtues into this last offshoot only for the seed not to sprout—no stem, no flower to scatter its Eastern perfumes? What crime did I commit before birth to inspire no love? Was I destined to be stranded, a useless hulk on a barren shore? In my soul I see deserts where my ancestors roamed under a scorching sun that shrivels life. Proud remnant of a fallen race, vain force, love wasted—an old man in the prime of youth—I shall await death's last grace. Alas! under this murky sky no spark will rekindle these ashes. Thus my last words may echo Christ's: "My God, Thou hast forsaken me!" A cry of agony and terror whose depth few mortals have explored.

You can now understand, Fernand, the joy I find in living through you and Marie. I will watch you with the pride of a creator satisfied with his work. Love each other well and continue to do so; any discord between you would pain me more than it would you.

Our mother had a premonition that events would one day fulfill her wishes. Perhaps a mother's longing is a pact with God. Was she not one of those beings who converse with Heaven and return with visions? How often I read in her brow that she coveted for Fernand the honors and wealth of Felipe! When I mentioned this she would weep, revealing a heart that rightly belonged to both her sons, yet was given to you alone by an irresistible passion.

Her spirit will hover above you as you bow at the altar. Mother, have you no caress for your Felipe now that he has yielded to your favorite even the girl you regretfully thrust into his arms? What I have done pleases our womenfolk, the dead, and the King; it is God's will. Do not resist, Fernand; obey and remain silent.

P.S. Tell Urraca to call me nothing but M. Henarez. Do not mention me to Marie. You must be the sole living soul to know the secrets of the last Christianized Moor, whose veins carry the blood of a great family that began in the desert and is now about to die out in a solitary exile.

Farewell.

VII. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE

What! To be married so soon? This is unheard of. After a month you become engaged to a stranger about whom you know nothing. He may be deaf—there are many kinds of deafness! He could be sickly, tiresome, insufferable!

Don’t you see, Renée, what they want from you? You’re needed to carry on the glorious stock of the l’Estorades, that is all. You will be buried in the provinces. Are these the promises we made each other? Were I you, I’d rather set off to the Hyères islands in a caique, risk capture by an Algerian corsair, and be sold to the Grand Turk. Then I could be a Sultana and make a stir in the harem while I’m young—yes, and afterwards too!

You are leaving one convent for another. I know you; you are a coward, and you will submit to family life with docility. But I will guide you; you must come to Paris. There we shall drive men wild and hold court like queens. Your husband may join the Chamber in three years. I know how those members work; you will work that machine well; you can live in Paris and become, as my mother calls it, a woman of fashion. Oh! do not think I will leave you in your grange!

Monday.

For a fortnight I have been living the life of society: one evening at the Italiens, another at the Grand Opera, and always a ball afterward. Society is bewitching. The Opera’s music enchants me; while my soul is immersed, I am the focus of admiration and opera glasses. But a single glance makes the boldest youth drop his eyes.

I’ve seen charming young men, yet none have stirred me; not one has evoked the emotion I feel when listening to García in his splendid duet with Pellegrini in Otello. How jealous Rossini must have been to express jealousy so well! What a cry in "Il mio cor si divide!" I speak Greek to you, for you’ve not heard García, but you know how jealous I am!

What a wretched dramatist Shakespeare is! Othello wins battles and leaves Desdemona meekly neglected. Such a sheep deserves slaughter. Let the man I love beware of thinking of anything but loving me!

I prefer the long trials of old chivalry. That lout of a young lord who took offense because his sovereign lady sent him among the lions to fetch her glove was impertinent. Doubtless the lady had some exquisite prize for him, which he lost—the puppy!

But I ramble. My father is likely to represent our master the King in Madrid. I say "our" master, for I shall be part of the embassy. My mother wishes to remain; my father will take me to have a woman with him.

This seems simple, but there are complications. In a fortnight I have probed the household’s secrets. My mother would accompany my father to Madrid if he took M. de Canalis as secretary. But the King appoints secretaries; the Duke cannot offend the King, who hates opposition. The cunning diplomat thinks he cut the knot by leaving the Duchess here. M. de Canalis, the great poet of the day, cultivates my mother’s society and studies diplomacy with her from three to five. Diplomacy must be a fine subject, for he keeps at it as regularly as a gambler at the Stock Exchange.

The Duc de Rhetore, our elder brother, solemn and capricious, will remain in Paris. Miss Griffith has discovered Alphonse is in love with a ballet girl at the Opera. How can one fall in love with legs and pirouettes? Our brother only attends when Tullia dances; he applauds and leaves. Two ballet girls in a family are more destructive than the plague. My second brother is with his regiment; I have not yet seen him. Thus I must act as the Antigone of His Majesty's ambassador. Perhaps I may marry in Spain; perhaps my father imagines a marriage without a dowry. He asked if I’d accompany him and offered me the use of his Spanish master.

"Spain, the country for castles in the air!" I exclaimed. "Perhaps you hope it will mean marriages for me!"

He only gave a knowing look. For days he has teased me at lunch; he watches me, and I dissemble, playing cruelly with him as father and ambassador in petto. He asked my opinion of some young men and girls. I answered with inane remarks about hair, faces, and figures. He seemed disappointed by my crassness and inwardly blamed himself for asking.

"Still, father," I added, "don’t suppose I’m saying what I really think: mother made me afraid the other day that I had spoken too frankly about my impressions."

"With your family, you can speak quite freely," said my mother.

"Very well, then," I continued. "The young men I’ve met are too self-centered to excite interest; they’re taken up with themselves. They put on an expression to talk to us, then drop it, apparently satisfied that we don’t use our eyes. The man as he converses is the lover; silent, he is the husband. The girls are so artificial that it’s impossible to know them, except from their dancing; their figures are not a sham. But the alarming thing is society’s brutality. Small incidents when supper is announced give one an idea—comparing small things with great—of what a popular uprising might be. Courtesy is a thin veneer over general selfishness. I imagined society to be very different. Women count for little in it; perhaps this is a survival of Bonapartist ideas."

"Armande is coming on extraordinarily," said my mother.

"Mother, did you think I would never get beyond asking to see Mme. de Staël?"

My father smiled and rose.

Saturday.

I left out one thing. The love we pictured is extremely well hidden; I have seen no trace. True, quick exchanges of glances occur in drawing-rooms, but how colorless it all is! Love as we imagined it—a world of wonders, glorious dreams, charming realities, sorrows that awaken sympathy, smiles bringing sunshine—does not exist. The bewitching words, constant interchange of happiness, misery of absence, joy of the beloved’s presence—where are they? What soil produces these radiant flowers of the soul? Which is wrong? We or the world?

I have seen hundreds of men; not one has moved me. No proof of admiration or devotion on their part, not even a sword drawn in my defense, would have touched me. Love is so rare that one might live a lifetime without meeting the being who can make one happy. The thought chills; if found too late, what then?

For days I have trembled at the thought of women’s destinies and begun to understand why so many wear sad faces beneath social excitement. Marriage is mere chance. Look at yours. A storm of wild thoughts swept me. To be loved every day the same, yet with a difference, to be loved as much after ten years as on the first day—such love demands years. The lover must languish; curiosity must be piqued and satisfied, feelings stirred and reciprocated.

Is there a law for the inner fruits of the heart, as for nature's visible fruits? Can joy be made lasting? In what measure should love mix tears with pleasure? The convent’s monotony seems the only real life; while wealth, splendor, tears, delights, triumph, joy, and the satisfaction of an equal, shared, sanctioned love appear idle visions.

I see no room in this city for the gentle ways of love—precious walks, the full moon on water, the suitor pleading in vain. Rich, young, and beautiful, I have only to love, and love would be my life; yet in three months nothing has appealed to me among the bright, covetous eyes here. No voice has thrilled me, no glance brightened my world.

Music alone has filled my soul; it has even taken the place of our friendship. At night I linger by my window, summoning the future from its mystery. Sometimes, setting out to drive, I dismount and walk the Champs-Élysées imagining the man who will awaken my slumbering soul is near. Instead I meet mountebanks, vendors, jugglers, and secret lovers. I am tempted to stop them, ask, "You who are happy, tell me what is love." But I repress the impulse and return to my carriage, swearing to die an old maid. Love is an incarnation, and how many conditions must be met before it can occur! We are not certain we shall never quarrel with ourselves; how much less certain with another? Only God can solve this.

I begin to think I’ll return to the convent. If I stay in society I may do foolish things, for I cannot reconcile what I see with my sense of delicacy and principle.

Ah! my mother is the happiest, adored by Canalis, her great little man. I sometimes crave to know what goes on between them. Griffith tells me she has passed through such moods; she has longed to lash out at women whose happiness is evident and blackened their characters. According to her, virtue is burying savage instincts in one’s heart. But then what becomes of the heart? It becomes a sink for the worst in us.

It is humiliating that no admirer has yet appeared for me. I am marriageable, but having brothers and a family sensitive about honor may keep men away. If that is why, they are cowards.

The parts of Chimène in the Cid and the Cid himself delight me. What a marvelous play! Farewell.

VIII. THE SAME TO THE SAME January.

Our master is a poor refugee, hiding because of his part in the revolution quelled by the Duc d’Angoulême—a triumph that has led to splendid fêtes. Though a Liberal and likely a man of the people, he has piqued my interest; I suspect he was condemned to death. I make him talk to uncover his secret, but he has a truly Castilian taciturnity, proud as if he were Gonsalvo di Cordova, yet angelic in patience and gentleness. His pride is not irritable like Miss Griffith’s; it belongs to his nature, compelling civility through perfect manners and keeping us at a distance with the respect he shows.

My father says much nobility dwells in Señor Henarez, whom he humorously calls Don Henarez. A few days ago I took the liberty of thus addressing him. He raised his eyes, usually cast down, and flashed a look that abashed me; he certainly has the most beautiful eyes imaginable. I asked if I had offended, and he replied in grand, rolling Spanish:

"I am here only to teach you Spanish."

I blushed and felt snubbed. I nearly retorted sharply, but remembering what our dear mother used to say, I instead replied:

"It would be kind to tell me if you have any complaints."

A tremor passed through him; the blood rose in his olive cheeks. He answered with some emotion:

"Religion must have taught you, better than I can, to respect the unhappy. Had I been a don in Spain and lost everything in Ferdinand VII.'s triumph, your witticism would be unkind; but if I am only a poor language teacher, is it not heartless satire? Neither is worthy of a young lady of rank."

I took his hand, saying:

"In the name of religion, I beg you to pardon me."

He bowed, opened my Don Quixote, and sat.

This little incident disturbed me more than a harvest of compliments and glances. During the lesson I watched him closely, which I could do safely, as he never looks at me.

From my observations, the tutor, whom we assumed to be forty, is under thirty. My governess remarked on the beauty of his black hair and pearly teeth. His eyes are velvet and fire, but he is plain and insignificant. Though Spaniards are often called unclean, this man is meticulously groomed, with hands whiter than his face. He stoops slightly and has an odd, large head. Smallpox scars give his ugliness a piquancy; his prominent forehead and shaggy brows give a harsh, frowning, plaintive look, like a sickly child whose life depends on superhuman care. As my father noted, his features are a shrunken Cardinal Ximenes. The natural dignity of his manners disconcerts the dear Duke, who dislikes him and never feels at ease before superiority.

Once my father knows enough Spanish we will go to Madrid. When Henarez returned two days after his reprimand, I said to show gratitude:

"I have no doubt you left Spain because of political events. If my father is sent there, as seems likely, we might help you obtain a pardon, if you are under sentence."

"It is impossible for anyone to help me," he replied.

"But," I pressed, "is that because you refuse help or because the situation is impossible?"

"Both," he said, bowing, in a tone that closed the matter.

My father's blood boiled. I was offended by this haughty posture and promptly dropped Señor Henarez.

Yet something admirable lay in his refusal of aid. "He would not accept even our friendship," I thought while conjugating a verb. Suddenly I stopped and spoke to him in Spanish. Henarez replied that equality of sentiment is necessary between friends, which did not exist here, making the matter pointless.

"Do you mean equality in feeling, or equality in rank?" I demanded, eager to provoke him.

He raised those awe-inspiring eyes, and mine fell. Dear, this man is a hopeless enigma. He seemed to ask whether my words implied love; the mixture of joy, pride, and agonized doubt in his glance moved me. It was clear that advances, taken lightly in France, might complicate with a Spaniard, and I retreated, feeling foolish.

The lesson ended; he bowed, and his eyes expressed a humble prayer: "Don't trifle with a poor wretch."

This sudden variance from his usual grave dignity made a deep impression. It seems horrible to think and say, but I can’t help believing there are treasures of affection in that man.

All is over, my dear child, and it is Mme. de l'Estorade who writes to you. Between us there is no change; it is only a girl the less.

Don't be troubled; I did not give my consent recklessly. My life is now mapped out, and the certainty of my path suits me. A great moral power has removed chance from my life. We have property to develop and a home to beautify; I have a household to manage and a husband to reconcile to life. I shall likely have children to care for and educate.

Everyday life cannot be cast in a heroic mould. Though there may seem no room for longing after the infinite, I can still explore that boundless sea in our familiar craft. My humble duties do allow for passion; restoring faith in happiness to those who have suffered is a noble task that will relieve the monotony of existence. I see little room for suffering and much good to be done. My love for Louis de l'Estorade does not make my heart race, but there is nothing in him that offends me.

This thought has troubled me, but these faculties remain mine, even if concealed and employed in secret for the welfare of our family. The time for a woman to seek admiration is short; if my life is not grand, it will at least be calm and free from shocks.

Nature has favored our sex with a choice between love and motherhood. I have made mine. My children will be my gods, and this spot of earth my Eldorado.

I count on your kindness to keep me fashionable. My father-in-law is delighted and refuses me nothing; he is turning his house upside down. We are bringing in workmen from Paris to renovate everything.

X. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE January.

Oh, Renée, you have made me miserable for days! Your enchanting beauty, proud features, and graceful manner, combined with your rich soul and exquisite heart, are destined to be wasted in a dull marriage that you will end up despising. I loathe your future children before they are even conceived; they will be monsters!

You seem to accept what lies ahead, without hope or fear. But what if a glorious morning brings the man who could awaken you from this slumber? The thought chills me!

At least you will have a friend. You will become the guardian angel of your valley, learn its beauty until it becomes part of you. As you walk with your husband and children, I can predict your letters: the valley, hills, and meadows will mirror the monotony of your soul. But I will be there, a heart untouched by social pettiness, all your own.

Monday.

My Spaniard is adorably melancholic, with a calm, severe dignity that intrigues me. His solemnity and silence provoke my curiosity. How odd that a language teacher captivates me more than any nobleman or officer I’ve met.

His coldness provokes me. He keeps distance, wrapped in pride and mystery, while I am bold. This amuses me; it’s trivial. What is a man, a Spaniard, a teacher to me? I regard no man as significant, not even a king. I would have made a slave of Napoleon!

Yesterday, I made a remark to M. Henarez that must have struck him; he did not respond, and the lesson ended with a bow implying he would not return. That suits me; I do not want a new Nouvelle Héloïse. I recently read Rousseau and found it distasteful. Passion that argues and moralizes seems detestable.

Clarissa is too pleased with herself and her long letter, but my father praises Richardson as an admirable portrayal of English women. Rousseau’s reads more like a philosophical sermon in letter form.

Love, to me, is a subjective poem. Everything written about it is both false and true. Since you will be an authority on married love, I feel called to remain unmarried and pursue a grand passion, enriching our shared experience.

Tell me every detail of your first days with that strange creature called a husband. I promise the same if I ever find love.

Farewell, poor martyred darling.

XI. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU La Crampade.

Your Spaniard and you make me shudder, my darling. I beg you to dismiss him. Everything you say aligns with the character of dangerous adventurers who risk everything. He cannot be your husband or lover. I will write more about my married life when my heart is free from the anxiety your last letter caused.

XII. MLLE. DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE February.

This morning my father visited, more thoughtful than usual. He gestured for me to sit; I sensed he wanted something.

He began by flattering me, admitting he had misjudged my intelligence. He praised my clear-sightedness, saying I was the only one capable of grasping his political concerns. He explained that France is in a critical position, understood only by the King and a few others. The King is a head without arms, and the nobles lack authority over elected men who refuse to cooperate.

He described the Revolution as still active, embedded in law and thought, and warned that the family has ceased to exist in France. We are left with individuals, and the State treasury has replaced the family spirit. He argued that a country cannot be stable without a foundation of paternal authority.

He urged me to consider the importance of family in establishing a strong state, warning of calamities if our party fails. I interrupted, asking how I could help the State, as I felt no vocation for martyrdom.

He called me a little hussy, and we laughed. He asked for my help in persuading me to sacrifice my inheritance for my younger brother's estate. I questioned whether I could still enjoy life if I gave up my fortune.

He insisted that my future husband would not take me without a dowry; I suggested perhaps an older peer might be an option. He laughed, and we parted on good terms.

I received your letter this morning, which made me reflect on the abyss you warned about. I took precautions regarding Henarez, whose gaze unsettles me. I hesitated to tell my father I wanted no more lessons, but I find myself drawn to him.

His voice is sweetly thrilling, his manners simple and genuine. When he left, he almost reached for my hand to kiss it but stopped, terrified. I smiled, understanding the courage it takes for a plebeian to love a noblewoman.

My smile encouraged him, and I handed him his hat with gravity. His eyes were wet with unshed tears, and in that moment I felt a strange happiness. I cannot explain it, but he has fascinated me.

Friday.

He is truly handsome and speaks admirably, with remarkable intellectual power. He has revealed a new world of thought. He told a story about a valet who died for a queen's glance, and I was captivated.

At a ball, M. de Vandenesse told me Henarez is of noble descent, the Duke de Soria, whose titles were confiscated by King Ferdinand. This left me in suspense until our next lesson.

During our lesson, I confronted him, suspecting he was the Duke de Soria. He denied it, saying he was merely the servant of the King of Spain. His reticence provoked me, and I devised a practical test: I asked him to translate a letter I wrote describing my ideal lover.

He was visibly moved by my words and asked to keep the letter as a memory. He left asking to speak with my father, and from him I learned Henarez is now the Baron de Macumer, with a property in Sardinia.

It has been two weeks since I last saw him, and I wonder what he is doing. I feel he has become necessary to me, and I am torn between love and the reality of my situation.

Sunday.

At the Italian Opera, I felt his gaze upon me; his fiery eyes drew me. He has a genius for love.

XIII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO MLLE. DE CHAULIEU LA CRAMPADE, February.

My dear Louise, I waited to write until I had learned many things for your happiness. The difference between a girl and a married woman is vast; the girl cannot comprehend it, nor can the married woman return to girlhood.

I chose to marry Louis de l'Estorade rather than return to the convent. The seriousness of marriage filled me with terror, but I realized friendship might be the key to a happy marriage. I accepted his love, believing it would not be slavery but a reign.

We agreed to separate marriage from marital rights, to maintain independence. I watched Louis closely, wondering if suffering had softened or hardened him. I concluded his love was a passion, and I could risk much.

I proposed a friendship that would let us bear life's burdens together. I asked him to leave me free, promising to consider his feelings. He was overjoyed, and we married soon after.

I am nearly happy now, but I must conceal my happiness from him, as he is sensitive. For three months we lived as before, and I studied our relationship. I saw how my presence transformed him, and I became accustomed to him.

I realized I could bring new life to my husband before bearing him children. His happiness has brightened our home; even my father has become generous. I manage the household with economy, aiming for pleasure without excess.

I have ambitious plans for Louis, hoping to see him in politics. He has embraced my plans, showing the depth of his affection.

To conclude, here is a letter he wrote, expressing his love and admiration. He sees me as a treasure, and I feel a proud sense of our relationship.

XIV. THE DUC DE SORIA TO THE BARON DE MACUMER MADRID.

My dear brother, I did not make you Duc de Soria to betray the name. I cannot tolerate that you are in exile while we enjoy happiness. Marie and I will not marry until you accept the money Urraca offers.

We have prayed for your happiness; I believe Heaven will send you the love you seek. I have taken Marie, and I will always be grateful to you for that.

You can write to us with confidence through the Nuncio. Farewell, dear brother. Be proud of the happiness you have brought us.

XV. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO MME. DE L'ESTORADE March.

Ah, my love, marriage is making a philosopher of you! Your views on life and duty trouble me. Do you think you can convert me to matrimony with your cautious arithmetic?

We left Blois innocent, armed with the weapons of meditation, and now those weapons have turned against you! If I did not know you were pure, I would think your calculations were vice.

You have made a holocaust of your youth, becoming a miser before your time. If Louis loves you, he will never realize you act from necessity.

Love is the first principle of all virtues; I cannot believe you are justifying your position as a married woman. You are acting a man's part, and Louis will have to play the woman!

Your letter plunged me into thought. I see now the convent cannot replace a mother. Please, do not turn away from my cries!

Monday.

Last night I looked at the stars and saw my Spaniard in a tree, admiring my window. I was overwhelmed with joy and fear.

He blew a letter to me through the window, but I burned it without reading. It felt improper to read it.

At the opera he was present, but I felt no agitation. He looked sad; I learned he had performed a noble action by giving up a rich heiress for another man.

I was wretched for burning his letter. What would he have said? To be loved in secret, to know a man is devoted—how can I resist?

When I returned home, I opened my window, hoping he would be there. He was, and I received another letter, which I read and cherished.

XVI. THE SAME TO THE SAME March.

I am dressed in white, with camellias in my hair. I have a strange longing to delay my decision and make him wait.

What a vision of beauty! I drove my barouche, and when I saw him, I felt a thrill. I complimented him on his horse; he shared it was a gift from his steward.

Henarez is no longer a simple man; he is becoming more distinguished.

Oh, my dear, do my love affairs amuse you? I am a little off my head and must stop.

XVII. MME. DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE CHAULIEU April.

My angel, you have grieved me. You have not realized the force of the word indissoluble in marriage.

Each marriage has its own laws, shaped by place and character. A wife of a leader must resign to his guidance, while a wife of a fool must take control.

You speak of vice, but passion can be admirable when it springs from the heart. Sooner or later, you will understand we are answerable only to God and ourselves for our happiness.

You will find a wife’s calculation necessary to maintain happiness. I have reflected on the duties of a wife and mother, and I believe a sublime hypocrisy is required to attain the ideal of a perfect woman.

I persist in my principles, believing virtue varies with surroundings. If I wished to make Louis unhappy, I would leave the helm in his hands.

I may not have married a man of distinction, but I hope to help him on that path. Five years from now we will see if I was mistaken.

You have taught me what a woman sacrifices in marriage. Regret is not remorse, but I feel the loss of freedom.

You say marriage has made you a philosopher, but I feel far from it. I have read Bonald, who revealed the strength of the family.

Farewell, my dear friend.

Well, my Renee, you are a wonderful woman, and I now agree that we can only be virtuous by cheating. Will that satisfy you? The man who loves us is our property; we can make a fool or a genius of him as we please, though the former is more common. You will make yours a genius and keep the secret—two heroic actions, if you will!

Ah! If there were no afterlife, how nicely you would be sold, for this is the martyrdom you are choosing. You want to make him ambitious and keep him in love! Child, surely the latter alone is sufficient.

Tell me, to what extent is calculation a virtue, or is virtue calculation? You won’t say? Well, we won’t quarrel over that, since we have Bonald to refer to. We are, and intend to remain, virtuous; yet at this moment, I believe that you, with all your little knavery, are a better woman than I.

Yes, I am shockingly deceitful. I love Felipe and conceal it from him with hypocrisy. I long to see him leap from his tree to my balcony—and if he did, how I would wither him with my scorn! I am frank enough with you.

What restrains me? Where is the mysterious power that prevents me from telling Felipe how supremely happy he has made me with his pure, absolute, and overflowing love?

Mme. de Mirbel is painting my portrait, which I intend to give him. What surprises me more every day is the animation love brings to life. Every hour, every action, every trifle is full of interest, creating confusion between the past, present, and future. Is it still so after reaching the heights of happiness? Oh! Tell me, what is happiness? Does it soothe or excite? I am restless; I feel lost, dragged to him despite reason and propriety. At least I can better understand your feelings.

Felipe's happiness lies in feeling himself mine; his aloofness and strict obedience irritate me, just as his profound respect did when he was my Spanish master. I want to cry out to him, "Fool, if you love me as a picture, what will it be when you know the real me?"

Oh! Renee, you burn my letters, don’t you? I will burn yours. If anyone else were to read these thoughts, I would send Felipe to silence them, perhaps even kill the owners for security.

Oh! Renee, how can one fathom the heart of man? My father should introduce me to M. Bonald, since he is so learned; I would ask him. I envy God, who can read the heart's undercurrents.

Does he still worship? That is the question.

If I ever detect the slightest fall in the respect he used to show me as my instructor, I feel I would have the strength to end it. "Why these fine words, these grand resolutions?" you will say. Dear, I will tell you.

My charming father, who treats me with the devotion of an Italian cavaliere servente, had my portrait painted by Mme. de Mirbel. I managed to get a copy made for the Duke and sent the original to Felipe. I included these lines:

"Don Felipe, your single-hearted devotion is met by a blind confidence. Time will show whether this is to treat a man as more than human."

It was a big reward, suggesting both a promise and a challenge, which I intended without fully committing. If his reply includes "Dear Louise!" or even "Louise," he is done for!

Tuesday.

No, he is not done for. The constitutional minister is perfect as a lover. Here is his letter:

"Every moment away from you has been filled with ideal pictures of you, my eyes closed to the outside world, fixed on your image. Henceforth my gaze will rest upon this wondrous ivory—this talisman—since your blue eyes sparkle with life as I look, and paint passes into flesh and blood. If I delayed writing, it was because I could not tear myself away from your presence, which wrung from me all that I was bound to keep secret.

"Yes, closeted with you all last night and today, I have, for the first time, given myself up to complete happiness. Could you see yourself where I have placed you, between the Virgin and God, you might understand the agony of my night. But I would not offend you by speaking of it; for one glance from your eyes, robbed of the tender sweetness which is my life, would be full of torture for me. Queen of my life and soul, oh! that you could grant me but a fraction of the love I bear you!

"This was the burden of my prayer; doubt worked havoc in my soul as I oscillated between belief and despair. A criminal awaiting his verdict is not more racked with suspense than I, as I own my temerity. The smile on your lips, which alone can calm the storm of my dread of displeasing you, haunts me. From my birth, no one, not even my mother, has smiled on me. The beautiful girl designed for me rejected my heart for my brother. In politics, all my efforts have been thwarted. In the eyes of my king, I have read only thirst for vengeance; he has been my enemy since childhood, and the vote of the Cortes that placed me in power was seen as a personal insult.

"Less than this might breed despondency in the stoutest heart. I have no illusions; I know the gracelessness of my person and how difficult it is to do justice to the heart within this rugged shell. To be loved had ceased to be more than a dream when I met you. Thus, when I bound myself to your service, I knew that devotion alone could excuse my passion.

"But as I look upon this portrait and listen to your smile that whispers of rapture, rays of hope pierce the gloom, only to be obscured by rising mists of doubt and fear of your displeasure. It is impossible you should love me yet—I feel it; but in time, as you see the strength, constancy, and depth of my affection, you may yield me some foothold in your heart. If my daring offends you, tell me without anger, and I will return to my former part. But if you consent to try and love me, be merciful and break it gently to one who has placed his happiness in the thought of serving you."

My dear, as I read these last words, he seemed to rise before me, pale as the night when the camellias told their story and he knew his offering was accepted. These words, in their humility, were different from the usual flowery rhetoric of lovers, and a wave of feeling washed over me; it was the breath of happiness.

The weather has been atrocious; impossible to go to the Bois without raising suspicions. Even my mother, who often goes out regardless of rain, remains at home.

Wednesday evening.

I have just seen him at the Opera; he is another man. He came to our box, introduced by the Sardinian ambassador.

Having read in my eyes that this audacity was taken well, he seemed awkwardly conscious of his limbs and addressed the Marquise d'Espard as "mademoiselle." A light brighter than the chandeliers flashed from his eyes. He left with the air of a man unsure of his next move.

"The Baron de Macumer is in love!" exclaimed Mme. de Maufrigneuse.

"Strange, isn’t it, for a fallen minister?" replied my mother.

I had enough presence of mind to regard Mmes. de Maufrigneuse and d'Espard and my mother with curiosity, as if they were speaking a foreign language, but inwardly my soul sank in waves of intoxicating joy. There is only one word to express what I felt: rapture. Such love as Felipe's makes him worthy of mine. I am the very breath of his life; my hands guide his thoughts. I have a mad longing to see him clear every obstacle and stand before me, boldly asking for my hand. Then I would know whether this storm of love would calm at a glance from me.

Ah! my dear, I stopped here, and I am still trembling. As I wrote, I heard a noise outside and rose to see what it was. From my window, I saw him coming along the wall at the risk of his life. I went to the bedroom window and made him a sign; it was enough. He leaped from the wall—ten feet—and then ran along the road, showing me he was not hurt. That he thought of my fear at that moment moved me so much that I am still crying; I don’t know why. Poor ungainly man! What was he coming for? What had he to say to me?

I dare not write my thoughts and will go to bed joyful, thinking of all we would say if we were together. Farewell, fair silent one. I have no time to scold you for not writing, but it has been over a month since I heard from you! Does this mean you are finally happy? Have you lost the "complete independence" you were so proud of, which nearly played me false tonight?

If love is the essence of life, why do philosophers dismiss it in marriage? Society demands that women sacrifice for family, creating discord in marriage. This discord was anticipated, prompting men to develop defenses against women. Without these, we might dismantle their theories through love or aversion.

Marriage, as it stands, embodies two opposing forces the law should reconcile. Your letter challenges the foundations laid by the sage of Aveyron, under which I had once found comfort. Laws crafted by men declare conjugal love devoid of passion acceptable, allowing women to yield without love's sanction, mirroring Nature’s emphasis on procreation.

Once a person, I now feel like property. I have swallowed many tears alone, aching for a comforting smile. Why are our fates so different? You thrive in lawful love, where virtue aligns with pleasure, while I face a future of uncertainty.

You are loved and adored. Embrace this noble romance that has long been our dream. Your beauty, refined and spiritual, was made to enchant. Guard your heart’s secret and test Felipe’s love, but more importantly, test your own. It’s easy to be misled by novelty and the illusion of happiness.

You remain independent, dear friend; I urge you not to rush into marriage without assurance. In intimate conversation, a gesture or word can illuminate hidden depths. You have the courage to explore where others falter.

I watch you with anxiety from afar; my heart is in sync with yours. Write to me about everything; your letters ignite passion in my otherwise mundane life. I oscillate between despair and hope—perhaps expecting too much from life. In youth, we often believe ideals and reality will align.

Seated beneath a rock in my park, I pondered that love in marriage is a fortunate accident, not a universal principle. The philosopher rightly views the family as society’s unit, placing women in subservience, as has been the case historically. The resolution to our dilemma lies in motherhood, which I yearn for to channel my energy and heart.

Louis remains cheerful; his love is active while mine is passive. He enjoys the flowers of my affection without acknowledging the toil behind them. I play the devoted mother, sacrificing for his happiness, which in turn gives me joy. I await the rewards of my sacrifices, hoping motherhood will fulfill my aspirations and compensate for what I surrender.

Dear wedded friend, your letter arrived just as I contemplated a bold step. I feel a strange yearning for the unknown, revealing a conflict between societal and natural laws within me. I want to speak with Felipe alone, under the lime-trees in our garden. This suits the modern girl I’ve become, as my father recognizes.

There is method in this seeming madness: I wish to reward Felipe for his nights beneath my window while testing his reaction to my boldness. If he respects my folly, he may envision me as his wife; if not, farewell, Don Felipe.

Meeting him this way seems less risky than encounters in society where prying eyes abound. I cannot express the agitation this idea brings me, nor how I longed for your company to share the anticipation of our first clandestine meeting.

I sent Felipe a tracing of the garden-key along with a note urging him to stop his reckless behavior, warning that if he fell it would tarnish my reputation. Yesterday I took Griffith to the garden, and after a delicious moment of tension, I saw him approach like a shadow.

When he reached the garden, I introduced him to Griffith, who withdrew a few steps. The night was mild and heavy with scents; I felt intoxicated by the thrill of being almost alone with him. We savored the moment before I urged him to abandon his reckless climbing, fearing scandal.

His pallor revealed fear; I reminded him that if he had been caught, suspicion would fall on my family. He apologized, and I reassured him that my actions were not as mad as they seemed.

After a few moments of silence he expressed joy and reverence for me, acknowledging that my actions were sacred. I told him I was on the path to loving the man who understands me, but that my heart would belong only to someone who truly knows it.

He expressed gratitude for my acceptance; I noted he should prove himself worthy of my affection. We walked and talked; his tenderness reminded me of divine love. His low, melodic voice conveyed his feelings beautifully.

I dismissed him, aware of our meeting’s implications. Griffith remarked on my commitment, but I assured her that in France my intentions were merely exploratory.

Once again I met Felipe; he appeared timid and reverent, which I found appealing. He seemed to sense my power, yet remained unaware of the depth of my feelings. I am still fond of him, but I keep my composure.

Love appears in two forms: one that commands and one that obeys. Can both coexist? Will Felipe ever become my master? These questions unsettle me.

He remains oblivious to my true nature. If I were in his position, I would see Mlle. de Chaulieu as a calculating coquette. I still care for Felipe, but I feel calm and in control now. The thrill of uncertainty has faded, and I fear I may have lost the excitement of our initial encounters.

My dear friend, how can you, who married a broken-down invalid to mother him, understand the shifting drama of love, where death lies in a glance or a light reply?

I reserved one last test for Felipe to see if his love was like a Royalist's for his King, who can do no wrong. He walked with me all night, and not a shadow of suspicion crossed his mind. The next day, he loved me more, but with reverence and humility. He is a true Spaniard, scaling my wall to kiss my hand from my balcony, risking his life.

I spoke to my father, the royal ambassador-to-be, with a smile, saying some friends claim he is marrying Armande to an ambassador's nephew for wealth and title. Your daughter weeps but bows to her father's authority. Some say she hides a worldly soul behind her tears.

"Macumer needs a spur," my father said, smiling at me.

We laughed, but I must explain the joke. A Russian princess had told us of this gentleman, who suffered from sea-sickness and turned back from Italy upon hearing of "crossing" the Alps. "Thank you; I've had quite enough crossings already," he said.

He kissed my hand, moved, and replied, "Wear them for the inscription and the sincere feeling."

I have not written to you, dear, since our marriage nearly eight months ago, and not a line from you! You are inexcusable.

We set off in a post-chaise for the Castle of Chantepleurs, which Macumer has bought in Nivernais, on the banks of the Loire, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants, except my maid, were there before us, and we arrived the next evening after a rapid journey. I slept all the way from Paris to beyond Montargis, resting my head on Felipe's shoulder. His tender affection touched me deeply, and I awoke to find his passionate gaze unchanged. He had kissed me twice on the forehead.

At Briare, we had breakfast in the carriage, followed by a conversation reminiscent of our old talks at Blois, admiring the Loire. By half-past seven, we entered the beautiful avenue leading to Chantepleurs. We dined at eight and retired to our charming Gothic bedroom, comfortable with modern luxuries. Felipe, often thought ugly, appeared beautiful to me in his kindness. Throughout the journey, he felt like an old friend, though he later described the storms of passion he concealed beneath a calm exterior.

"So far, I have found nothing terrible in marriage," I said, gazing out at the moonlit park.

"Why fear it?" he replied. "Have I ever proved false to my promise? Why should I in the future?"

His words and glances were full of mastery, thrilling my heart. I mused on duty and the constraints placed on a woman's delicate feelings. What malevolent force has sought to crush a woman's sensitivity under obligation? Love should guard its own rights, blooming freely under its warmth.

Oh, my noble Renee! I understand you now. The woman who has not used marriage merely to legalize her heart's choice has only motherhood left to turn to. When earthly ties fail, the soul seeks the divine.

A hard truth emerges: only truly great men know how to love. They balance sensual and spiritual forces, while weaker men confuse the two. Great souls express natural instincts with grace, and their passion is tempered by reverence for women. The depth of feeling corresponds to the caliber of one's intellect, which is why only men of genius possess a woman's delicacy. When mind, heart, and senses unite in rapture, we soar to heaven, albeit briefly.

This is the philosophy of my first three months of marriage. Felipe is angelic, another self, and I can think aloud with him. His greatness surpasses my understanding, and our bond deepens with possession. I foresee that years of marriage will only strengthen his affection and enhance our connection. What joy!

Pleasure invigorates me, leaving behind sunshine that becomes part of my being. The intervals between ecstasies are like the brief nights separating long summer days. What happy chance has given me such a destiny? My mother's fears have proven unfounded; your fears and mine have vanished.

We spent seven and a half months at Chantepleurs, like runaway lovers in a blissful solitude. One morning, in a particularly happy moment, I reflected on my life and thought of your prosaic marriage. I grasped the meaning of your life. Oh, my sweet, why do we speak different languages? Your marriage of convenience and my love match are worlds apart. You walk the earth, while I soar in the heavens! Love crowned me queen, while you reign by duty. My heights are so lofty that a fall would shatter me.

But I won't dwell on this. I hesitate to describe the vibrant joy of love's springtime as we know it.

For ten days, we have been in Paris, staying in a lovely house in the Rue du Bac, prepared by the architect who decorated Chantepleurs. I have been listening to Rossini's divine music, which once soothed my restless heart. They say I am more beautiful now, and I take childish pleasure in being called "Madame."

Friday morning.

Renee, my dear, my happiness draws me back to you. I feel I can be more to you than ever before. I have studied your married life alongside my own, and you emerge as great, noble, and splendid in your goodness. I declare myself your humble admirer and friend. My marriage has been a joy; I would have perished had it been otherwise. And you live! What feeds your heart? I will never mock you again. Mockery stems from ignorance; we jest at what we do not understand. "Recruits laugh where veterans are grave," said the Comte de Chaulieu, whose experience has been limited to marches from Paris to Fontainebleau.

I suspect you have not shared everything. You hide wounds, and I believe you suffer. From afar, I have woven romantic theories about you. "She has merely experimented with marriage," I thought, "and what is happiness for me has brought her only pain." Your sacrifice seems devoid of reward, and you would not make it greater than necessary. The axioms of social morality cloak your disappointment. If self-denial is noble, what of my joy, sheltered by the church's canopy and witnessed by the mayor's seal? Is it unnatural?

For the honor of the law and to complete my happiness, I long to see you content. Oh, tell me you see a dawn of love for Louis, who adores you! I return to this midday blaze, fearing it may consume me.

Dear Renee, do you remember how you once said, "I love you so, Louise, that if God appeared to me, I would pray for all your sorrows to be mine"? Now, I take up your prayer, imploring Heaven to grant you a share in my happiness.

I sat alone beneath a barren rock overlooking the Mediterranean, where I’ve planted climbing plants to one day cover it in greenery. I returned to my white grange to share these thoughts with you. For five months the miracle of nature has unfolded within me, yet I remain emotionally detached. My family is excited, but I feel cold and hesitant to share my true feelings. Motherhood, for me, is still an abstract concept.

Louis was equally surprised by my condition, which highlights how little the father’s role matters in this process. My doctor believes that while chance governs childbirth, children born of passionate love tend to be more fortunate. Perhaps motherhood holds joys for you that I may never experience, as your love for Felipe is different from my sense of duty and obligation.

These thoughts weigh on me as I anticipate the joys of family life. Currently I live in a state of expectation, accompanied by physical discomfort. I experience nausea and strange cravings, including an inexplicable desire for rotten fruit. While Louis brings home the finest oranges, I seek out the half-rotten ones, finding them delicious despite their smell. This craving, though odd, has alleviated my sickness and made me feel stronger, suggesting a natural process common among women.

As my condition progresses, I plan to stay within the grounds to avoid being seen. I am curious about when the sense of motherhood truly begins, hoping it won’t coincide with suffering.

Farewell, my fortunate friend! Your love story inspires me, and I long to hear every detail. Please remain faithful to our bond, and I promise to share everything with you.

However, I must confess that after rereading your letter I felt a sense of dread. Your happiness seems to challenge Providence, and I worry that calamity may take offense at your joy. Remember to pray and do good, as religion has become more significant to me since my marriage. Your letter lacks mention of this, and I hope you still invoke God in your happiness.

This concern stems from my deep affection for you. I trust you and Felipe attend church and do good deeds together. My fears may seem primitive, but they arise from a friendship that cares deeply. You deserve happiness, and I cherish the bond we share, even in my uneventful life. God bless you!

XXX. LOUISE DE MACUMER TO RENEE DE L'ESTORADE January 1826.

Macumer just woke me with your husband's letter. Yes, we will be going to Chantepleurs at the end of April. I am delighted to travel, to see you, and to be godmother to your first child. I must have Macumer as godfather; sharing this role would be unbearable. If you could see the look he gave me, you would understand how much he cherishes me!

I am eager for our visit to La Crampade, as I might have a child there too. Can you imagine me torn between you and the infant? If I saw anything taking my place in your heart, I couldn't promise my reaction. Medea may have had a point!

So, my dear, you have the fruit without the flowers; I have the flowers without the fruit. Our lives contrast, but we have enough philosophy to find meaning in it someday. Ten months married is too soon to lose hope.

We are living a joyful life, as happy people do. Society now sees the Baronne de Macumer as prettier than Louise de Chaulieu; love is a wonderful cosmetic. Driving along the Champs-Élysées on a crisp January day, I reflect on how far we've come since last year.

If I lack a mother's joys, you can share them with me; I will learn through sympathy. Yet I cannot imagine anything surpassing the rapture of love. You may laugh, but I often wish to die at thirty, leaving life untainted by disappointment, having lived fully in love. To carry a young heart in an aged body, surrounded by cold faces, would be a torment.

Felipe and I had our first quarrel over this. I insisted he should have the strength to end my life in my sleep at thirty, so I could pass from one dream to another. He refused, turning pale at the thought. This distinguished statesman is surprisingly childlike. Now that I share my thoughts with him, we continually surprise each other.

Felipe and I want to send you a gift, dear mother. We wish to choose something that will bring you joy and remind you of us. Please tell me what you would like. I thought of a luncheon service adorned with baby figures, since lunch is our happiest meal. Let me know quickly, as we must order it soon.

Farewell, dear nursing mother. May you enjoy all the delights of motherhood! I eagerly await your first letter describing your experiences. Some parts of your husband's letter touched me; I will tell my godson how dearly he must love you. Much love, my sweet one.

XXXI. RENEE DE L'ESTORADE TO LOUISE DE MACUMER

It has been nearly five months since the baby was born, and I have found no moment to write. When you become a mother, you will understand. Please write more often! Share your joys; I am happier than you can imagine.

I went to the parish church for the Mass of recovery from childbirth, supported by both grandfathers. I knelt before God, overwhelmed with gratitude. I have so much to tell you, but one memory stands out: my prayer in the church.

Transformed into a joyful mother, I felt the Virgin on the altar bow her head, pointing to the infant Christ, who smiled at me. With a heart full of love, I held little Armand for the priest to bless, anticipating his baptism later.

My child—how sweet that word is! In the last two months I dragged myself through the gardens, scarcely conscious of the precious burden I carried. I was haunted by gloomy forebodings, overshadowing my curiosity about motherhood. My heart did not respond to the thought of the little one, though he announced himself with lively kicks.

When the moment came, I summoned all my strength and braced myself for suffering. I bore the agony marvelously, sinking into a stupor. I felt like two beings—my body in pain and my soul at peace. The pain formed a halo around me, and when the climax came, I screamed, finding strength against the torture. Suddenly, the newborn's cries silenced my pain.

They laid me back in bed, and it felt like paradise despite my exhaustion. Happy faces pointed to the child, and I exclaimed in terror, "It looks like a little monkey! Are you sure it's a child?"

My mother, now the nurse, reassured me, "You've got the finest baby in the world. Don't worry; focus on being a mother." I fell asleep, resolved to let nature take its course.

Waking from the pain of the first days was heavenly. A ray of light pierced the darkness, and my heart and soul emerged from suffering as a flower opens in the sun. The moment the little one latched onto my breast was pure joy—this is the dawn of motherhood!

Here is happiness, though it comes with pangs. Oh, my sweet jealous soul, you will relish this delight that exists only for us, the child, and God. For this tiny creature, all knowledge is in its mother's breast. Its thoughts revolve around this source of life, and its lips bring a sweetness beyond words.

Nursing is a miracle of transformation. The cries go to your heart, and the child's eyes and lips speak volumes. What else matters? The father? You could kill him if he disturbed the baby! The bond between mother and child is profound, and the joy of nurturing is unparalleled.

My little one has become the prettiest darling in five months. He cries little now, content in the love that surrounds him. I strive to envelope him in my affection.

Dear, my feelings for Louis have evolved into a tender fondness, perhaps even surpassing love. This unselfish gratitude reflects the joy of motherhood, lighting the future and recalling fond memories of the past.

The old l'Estorade and his son have redoubled their devotion to me; I feel like a new person. Each time they see me, it brings them joy, which touches me deeply. Louis is ecstatic about having a son and spends hours watching him sleep. He feared he would be childless, but now he works harder, driven by fatherhood.

I grow happier every moment. Each hour strengthens the bond between mother and child. My feelings are natural and enduring, unlike love, which can wane. A mother's love grows with the child's needs, embracing passion, duty, and joy. Here lies a woman's true sphere, where self-sacrifice flourishes without jealousy.

Society aligns with nature in this regard, reinforcing the maternal instinct. A mother cherishes the child who opens up these joys and teaches her the art of motherhood. The right of the eldest child, once part of the natural order, should never have been questioned. A mother learns so much from her child, and her character flourishes in fulfilling life's duties and pleasures. A woman without children is incomplete.

Hasten, my sweetest, to fulfill your mission. Your happiness will multiply with the wealth of my delights.

23rd.

I had to leave you because my godson was crying. I can hear him from the garden. I wanted to say farewell. I reread my letter and am surprised at how ordinary my feelings seem; every mother must have felt this way. But I am as happy now as I once was miserable. This place is my land of promise. The desert is past. A thousand loves, darling. Write to me, for I can now read about your happy love without tears. Farewell.

Do you know, dear, that it has been over three months since I last wrote to you? I am to blame for not answering your last letter, but surely you won’t mind?

Macumer and I took your silence as consent about the baby-wreathed luncheon service, which is now bound for Marseilles. It took six months to complete. When Felipe asked me to see it before packing, I realized we hadn’t exchanged words since your letter that revealed a mother’s heart.

My sweet, it’s this dreadful Paris—my excuse. What is yours? Society is a whirlpool! Didn’t I tell you that in Paris one must conform? It drives out sentiment, monopolizes time, and can consume your heart. Moliere’s Celimene is the masterpiece of the society woman—timeless and apt.

Without my breastplate—the love I have for Felipe—I would be lost. This morning, I told him he is my salvation. My evenings are filled with parties, but at night my heart heals in the embrace of passionate love.

I only dine at home with friends and spend afternoons there one day a week—Wednesday. I’ve entered the social fray, competing with Mmes. d'Espard and de Maufrigneuse, and my house is lively. I allowed myself to become fashionable for Felipe’s pleasure. My mornings are his; from four in the afternoon until two in the morning, I belong to Paris. Macumer is an admirable host—witty, dignified, courteous. No woman could help but love such a husband.

My parents have gone to Madrid. With Louis XVIII gone, the Duchess secured my father’s appointment as attaché. My brother, the Duc de Rhetore, now acknowledges me. My younger brother, the Comte de Chaulieu, owes me gratitude for spending my fortune to secure him an estate and a marriage with Mlle. de Mortsauf, an heiress. The King will confer the de Lenoncourt and de Givry titles on him to preserve their legacy. Mlle. de Mortsauf, sole heiress of the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry, is expected to inherit over one hundred thousand livres a year. My father insisted the de Chaulieu arms appear on the de Lenoncourt escutcheon, making my brother Duc de Lenoncourt. The young de Mortsauf is gravely ill, so the marriage will wait until next winter after mourning.

My father’s plan has justified itself, earning me compliments and smoothing over the explanation of my marriage to society. The Prince de Talleyrand, out of affection for my grandmother, has become warm to Macumer. Society that once criticized me now admires me.

I now reign where I was once insignificant. Macumer is envied as the husband of "the most charming woman in Paris." Many women vie for that title. Men murmur sweet nothings or gaze longingly. This chorus flatters vanity, and I see why women pay dearly for such transient privileges. This triumph is intoxicating; I understand how women become selfish and frivolous under adoration. Society’s demands consume your spirit; you give your all for smiles and jealousy and receive only flattery. You know the cost, yet cannot escape the vortex.

Ah, how I crave a true friend! Felipe’s love and devotion are precious to me, and my heart longs for you! We are joyfully preparing to move to Chantepleurs, where we can escape the farce of Parisian society. After rereading your letter, I realize no fashionable woman in Paris can be a good mother.

Goodbye for now, dear. We will be at Chantepleurs for only a week and will see you around May 10th. We will finally meet again after more than two years! How we have changed! We are both matrons now—one in love, the other in motherhood.

If I haven’t written, it isn’t because I’ve forgotten you. How is my little godson? Is he still lovely? He must be over nine months now. I long to witness his first steps, though Macumer says even precocious infants don’t walk at ten months.

We will have delightful gossip and see if a child truly spoils a pattern.

P.S.—If you reply, address it to Chantepleurs. I’m just leaving.

Master Armand-Louis eats whenever he pleases, and I must constantly change, wash, and dress him. I love watching him sleep, singing to him, and carrying him about, leaving little time for myself. Society has been to you what motherhood is to me!

My sentence is that you return at my first summons. Your letter didn’t mention your next stop, so I’m addressing this to Chantepleurs.

Listen, dear sister of my heart. My focus is your happiness. Your husband commands respect, not only for his gravity but for the power revealed in his plainness and the fire in his eyes. It took me time to converse easily with him. He has been Prime Minister and adores you, which makes him a profound dissembler. Extracting secrets from this diplomatic soul requires skill.

You know my role is reason, while yours is imagination. I am a simple country vicountess, ambitious for my family’s prosperity. Macumer, the Duc de Soria, has a name, and you, a duchess by right, reign in Paris, where it’s hard to maintain power. You have a considerable fortune, which may double if Macumer develops his Sardinian estates. If anyone has the right to be jealous, it’s not you. Thankfully, we both have generous hearts that place our friendship above such pettiness.

I know you must feel ashamed of fleeing. But your flight won’t spare you from my words today. Read carefully, as this concerns you more than Macumer.

Firstly, you do not love him. In two years you will tire of adoration. You will never see Felipe as a husband; he will always be a lover to you. You do not revere him as a woman should her god. I have studied love, and this is not love.

You cannot escape the fate of all queens. One day you will long to be treated as a light-o’-love, to be swept off your feet by a strong man who won’t worship you. Macumer loves you too much to resist or criticize. Sooner or later you will scorn this excessive devotion. He spoils you, as I did at the convent; you are enchanting, and there’s no escaping your charms.

Worse, you are candid, and our happiness often relies on social hypocrisies you won’t embrace. Society won’t tolerate a wife’s power over her husband. The convention is that a man must not show himself as a lover to his wife, nor may a married woman act as a mistress. You both disregard this.

The unpardonable sin in society is to be happy. If happiness exists, it must remain hidden. More importantly, equality between lovers should never appear in marriage, or it undermines society. It’s painful to see a man made a nonentity, and worse to see a capable man reduced to that. You will reduce Macumer to a shadow, and your household will consist of one imperfect person. You will regret it, but by then it will be too late. Women lack the qualities essential for family life. Macumer senses this; his visit to Sardinia suggests he hopes to regain his old self.

You use the power his love gives you. Your position shows in your gestures and tone. You are the wanton your mother called you! Have you ever heard me contradict Louis? I respect his authority in public. If I want to advise him, I wait for private moments, but I never assume the role of mentor. If I didn’t remain a submissive wife publicly, he would lose confidence. The good we do for others is spoiled unless we erase ourselves so they feel no inferiority. There’s sweetness in these hidden sacrifices, and it’s a triumph when you praise Louis.

This is the sum of my observations. Right now, you love Felipe, not as a husband, but as a reflection of yourself. Your father’s words hold truth; beneath your passion lies a selfishness typical of a great lady. How I must love you to speak such bitter truths!

If you promise not to tell the Baron, I’ll share the end of our talk. We praised you, and he soon realized I love you like a sister. I ventured, “Louise has never struggled with life. She has been fortune’s spoiled child, and she might pay for this if you weren’t there to act as father and lover.”

“Ah! but is it possible?” he broke off, on the verge of a precipice. That exclamation sufficed for me. If you had stayed, he would have spoken more freely.

Think of the day when your husband’s strength wanes, when pleasure turns to satiety, and he feels diminished before you. The stings of conscience will awaken remorse, painful for you, as you will feel responsible and end up despising the man you failed to respect. Remember, scorn can lead to hatred. You are too noble to forget Felipe’s sacrifices, but what more can he give when he has already served himself at the banquet? Woe to those who leave no desire unsatisfied! Women are insatiable in love.

Oh, Louise, change while there’s still time. If you treated Macumer as I treat l’Estorade, you could awaken the hero within him. Wouldn’t you feel pride in using your power to elevate a gifted man, as I do with one of lesser stature?

Had you remained, I would have written this anyway, for in conversation you might have interrupted me. But I know you will read this thoughtfully and weigh my warnings. You have everything to be happy; don’t spoil your chances. Return to Paris as soon as Macumer does. The demands of society, which I complain about, are necessary; otherwise you’ll be lost in self-absorption. A married woman shouldn’t be too generous with herself. A mother should reserve sacred hours for herself; even children shouldn’t take her presence for granted.

Farewell, my dear jealous soul! Many women would be flattered by your pangs, but I can only mourn, for what isn’t mother in me is your dear friend. A thousand loves. Make whatever excuse you must for leaving; if you’re unsure of Macumer, I am sure of Louis.

XXXIX. THE BARONNE DE MACUMER TO THE VICOMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE

Your dreadful letter has reached me, forwarded by my steward as I requested. Oh, Renée... I will spare you my turmoil and simply tell you the effect your words had.

We had just returned from a delightful reception given by the ambassador, where I shone and Macumer was utterly captivated. Then I read your harsh reply to my letter, and sobbed. My dear Arab threw himself at my feet, declaring you mad. He led me to our palace balcony; the moonlight made a magnificent scene. We now speak Italian, and his declarations in that language were like poetry. He swore that even if your predictions were true, he would not trade a single moment of our love for a lifetime of convention. He would choose our love and death over a dull existence with children.

While he whispered, a bat's cry startled us, and I fainted. Do not worry—I am better this morning, though that ominous sound haunts me. Upon waking, I knelt before Felipe, looked into his eyes, and confessed: "My love, I may be naive, and Renée might be right. Perhaps I love only your love, but this is the only feeling in my heart. If anything about me disturbs you, tell me. I want to follow your wishes." Macumer was too choked with emotion to answer.

I thank you, Renée; your letter revealed the depths of Macumer's love. Rome is the city of love, where passion should flourish alongside art and faith.

In Venice we shall meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria. If you write, address Paris, as we leave Rome in three days after the ambassador's farewell.

P.S. Dear, naive child, your letter shows you know love only in theory. Love is a force that yields diverse fruits beyond any theory's grasp.

XL. THE COMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE TO THE BARONNE DE MACUMER January 1827.

My father has been elected to the Chamber, my father-in-law has died, and I am near my second confinement; these mark the year’s end. I mention them first to spare you the fright of the black seal.

Your letter from Rome chilled me. You and Felipe are like children. Either he is a deceitful diplomat, or his love for you is like a man’s for a courtesan—lavishing affection while knowing she is unfaithful. Enough. You say I rave, so I will hold my tongue: from our differing fates I draw a harsh lesson—do not love if you wish to be loved.

When Louis joined the provincial Council he received the Legion of Honor cross. Nearly three years on, my father asks the rank of Officer for his son-in-law. I would value your influence with the right person, but do not entangle yourself in my father's affairs. The Comte de Maucombe seeks a marquisate for himself; reserve efforts for me. When Louis becomes a deputy next winter, we will be in Paris to secure him a government post so we may live on his salary. My father sits between center and right; a title will satisfy him. Our family was noble even under King René, and Charles X. will likely grant a Maucombe title. My fear is my father using this to favor my younger brother—if a marquisate is dangled, he will focus on himself.

January 15th.

Ah, Louise, I have been through hell. I tell you this because you are like a sister; still, I doubt I shall ever relive those five ghastly days. The word "convulsions" sickens me. Five days felt like five centuries. A mother who has not suffered this cannot comprehend it. I even envied you for never having had a child!

The night before, the weather was close; I thought my Armand affected by teething. Usually sweet, he was peevish, crying, wanting to play, breaking his toys. I put him to bed beside me, waking often. He had a slight fever but nothing alarming. Toward morning he cried "Mamma!" in a strange voice and twitched. I rose to fetch a drink. As I handed him the cup he was motionless, repeating "Mamma!" in a voice hardly a voice. His hand was stiff. He gulped water convulsively, clung to me, eyes rolling back, limbs rigid. I screamed for Louis.

"A doctor! Quick!... he is dying," I cried.

Louis vanished. Armand gasped "Mamma!" then lost consciousness. Veins on his forehead swelled, and convulsions began. For an hour before doctors arrived I held my lifeless baby—my pride and joy. His eyes! I cannot think of them without horror. My pretty Armand was a mere shell—black, shriveled, misshapen.

Two doctors from Marseilles hovered like birds of ill omen. One blamed brain fever, the other called them ordinary convulsions. Our country doctor offered no opinion. "Teething," said one; "fever," said the other. They agreed on leeches to the neck and ice to the head. To see a child like that—purple, silent—was horrible. At one point I laughed hysterically at the sight of leeches on his neck and the ice cap. They even cut his curls for the ice. The convulsions returned every ten minutes; the child writhed, white then violet, limbs clattering like wood. This unconscious flesh had smiled and said "Mamma!"—the thought pierced my soul. My mother was in Paris; mothers know more about convulsions than doctors.

After four days and nights, the doctors recommended a dreadful ointment to create open sores. Sores on my Armand! Five days earlier he had been playing and laughing. I refused, trusting nature. Louis, who believes doctors, scolded me. But in those moments when the disease mimicked death, I felt that remedy might save him. His skin was dry and rough; the ointment would not work. I wept; the doctors were at dinner!

Alone with him, I stripped off the medical appliances, pressed him to my bosom, and laid my forehead against his, begging God to spare him. For minutes I held him, longing to die with him. Then his limbs relaxed; the writhings ceased; the ghastly colors faded. I screamed; the doctors rushed in.

"He is saved!" cried the oldest doctor.

What music those words were! Two hours later Armand returned to life. I was crushed, saved only by the joy of his recovery. What tortures bind a mother to her child! Must the nails be driven to fasten him to my heart? Was I not mother enough? I who wept at his first words and steps—why these horrors?

Now Armand plays and laughs. What causes this dreadful disease in children? I ponder it, especially as I am again with child. Is it teething? A brain disorder? A nervous susceptibility? Our country doctor blames nervous trouble from teething. I would give every tooth in my head to see Armand's through. One white pearl peeking from swollen gums brings a cold sweat. The heroism with which my angel bore suffering proves he is his mother’s son. A look from him pierces me.

Medical science offers no clear explanation for this tetanus that appears and vanishes swiftly, unpreventable and uncured. One thing is certain: it is hell for a mother to see her child in convulsions. How passionately I hold him now! I could walk forever with him in my arms!

To have endured this six weeks before my confinement made it worse; I feared for the coming child. Farewell, my beloved. Do not wish for a child—this is the essence of my letter!

XLI. THE BARONNE DE MACUMER TO THE VICOMTESSE DE L'ESTORADE Paris.

Poor dear, Macumer and I forgave you upon hearing of your terrible trouble. I felt pain reading of your double agony; there seem compensations in being childless.

I write to tell you Louis has been promoted; he may now wear the ribbon of an Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate, Renée—likely to have a little girl, as you wished!

My brother's marriage to Mlle. de Mortsauf was celebrated on our return. Our gracious King, who is extraordinarily kind, has granted my brother the reversion of the post of first gentleman of the chamber, presently held by his father-in-law, on condition the Mortsauf scutcheon be placed alongside the Lenoncourt’s.

"The office ought to go with the title," he told the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry.

My father is justified a hundredfold. Without my fortune none of this would have happened. My parents came from Madrid for the wedding and will return after the reception I am hosting for the newlyweds.

The carnival will be lively. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria are in Paris, which unnerves me. Marie Heredia is one of Europe’s most beautiful women, and I dislike the way Felipe looks at her. Therefore, I am extra sweet and affectionate. Every look and gesture says what my lips do not: "She could not love like this!" Heaven knows how lovely I am! Yesterday Mme. de Maufrigneuse said, "Dear child, who can compete with you?"

I keep Felipe entertained, making his sister-in-law seem dull by comparison. I am less sorry that a little Abencerrage is not on the way, since the Duchess will likely remain in Paris for her confinement and lose her beauty. If the baby is a boy, he will be named Felipe in honor of the exile. Fate decrees I shall be godmother again.

Goodbye, dear. I plan to go to Chantepleurs early this year: our Italian tour was shockingly expensive. I will leave around the end of March to economize in Nivenais. Besides, I am tired of Paris. Felipe and I long for the tranquility of the park, cool meadows, and the sparkling Loire. Chantepleurs will be delightful after Italy; splendor becomes tiresome, and a lover's gaze is more beautiful than any opera or painting!

We expect you there. Do not fear my jealousy. You are free to test Macumer's heart and expose his doubts. I am utterly indifferent. Since that day in Rome Felipe's love for me has deepened. He just told me (looking over my shoulder) that his sister-in-law, Princess Heredia, his childhood dream, has no brains. Oh, my dear, I am worse than a ballet dancer! If you knew the joy that remark brought me! I pointed out her faulty French—she says "esemple" for "exemple," "sain" for "cinq," "cheu" for "je." She is beautiful but lacks charm and wit. When praised she seems puzzled. Felipe could not have lived two months with Marie after marriage. Don Fernand, the Duc de Soria, suits her perfectly: generous but a spoiled child. I am tempted to be mischievous and make you laugh, but I will refrain. Much love, darling.

For the first time, my dear Renee, I find myself alone and crying. I sat under a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh, a charming view that feels incomplete without children. Nearly three years into my marriage, I have no child! Your quiver full of children drives me to explore my heart.

I would endure any suffering, even what you faced when my godson was born, just to have a cherub of my own, like your Athenais! I can picture her beauty, but you tell me nothing about her—just like you, Renee! I believe you sense my trouble.

Each disappointment plunges me into deep melancholy, leading me to compose sad elegies. When will I embroider little caps and sew lace for a tiny head? Will I never hear "Mamma" from my child or have my dress tugged by a beloved little despot? Will there be no wheelmarks of a carriage on the gravel, no broken toys in the courtyard? I long to witness the unfolding of a precious life—another Felipe, but dearer. I would love a son to understand how a lover can be more to one in his second self.

My park and castle feel cold and desolate. A childless woman is a monstrosity of nature; we exist to be mothers. Oh, my wise friend, how well you understand life! Barrenness is dismal. My life resembles a Gessner or Florian sheepfold, which Rivarol wished to see invaded by a wolf. I have sacrifices to make! If I cannot be a mother, I must indulge in some romantic sorrow.

I shared this with my Moor, bringing tears to his eyes. He cannot bear jokes about his love, so I let him off easily, calling him a paladin of folly.

Sometimes, I feel a desire to go on pilgrimage, to bear my longings to a shrine or a watering place. Next winter, I will seek medical advice. I am too angry with myself to write more. Goodbye.

A whole year has passed without a letter! What does this mean? I am a little hurt. Does your Louis, who visits me almost every other day, make up for your absence? It is not enough to know you are well; I want to know your feelings and thoughts, just as I share everything with you, risking scolding or misunderstanding. Your silence and seclusion in the country, while your husband enjoys Parliamentary honors, worry me. His eloquence has earned him a good position, and he will likely receive a fine post when the session ends. Are you spending your time writing him letters of advice?

Why did you not take the chance to see Paris? I could have enjoyed your company for four months. Louis told me you would fetch him and have your third confinement in Paris—what a terrible mother Gigogne! After bombarding Louis with questions, I learned that his grand-uncle, Athenais' godfather, is very ill. I believe you, as a careful mother, would angle for a legacy from your husband’s last remaining relative. Rest easy, my Renee; we are all working for Louis. Martignac will likely place him in the audit department. But if you won’t tell me why you hide in the country, I shall be cross.

Are you afraid that the political wisdom of the house of l'Estorade should seem to center in you? Or is it the uncle's legacy? Perhaps you fear you would be less to your children in Paris? I would love to know if you are simply too vain to show yourself in Paris for the first time in your current condition! Vain thing! Farewell.

You complain of my silence; have you forgotten my two little brown heads, my subjects and tyrants? I didn’t want to drag a four-year-old and a soon-to-be three-year-old to Paris while expecting again. I didn’t want to trouble you and upset your husband with such a party. I also detest life in hotels.

When I come to Paris for the session, it will be in my own house. Louis' uncle, upon hearing of his grand-nephew's rank, gifted me two hundred thousand francs to buy a house in Paris, and I have tasked Louis with finding one near you. My mother has given me thirty thousand francs for furnishing, and I will do my best not to disgrace you.

I appreciate all you have done for Louis at Court. However, I do not want him in a conspicuous position; it would commit him too much. I prefer the Audit Office for its permanence. Our affairs are in good hands, so you need not worry; once the steward masters the details, I will support Louis.

As for writing long letters, how can I? This one, in which I want to describe my daily routine, will take a week to finish. Who knows if Armand will use it to make caps for his regiments or vessels for his bath? A single day serves as a sample, as they are all alike, characterized by either the children being well or not. In this solitary grange, hours become minutes or minutes hours, depending on their health.

My delightful hours come when they are asleep, and I am no longer needed to rock one or soothe the other. When they sleep beside me, I think, "Nothing can go wrong now." Every mother spends her time imagining dangers for her children. Perhaps Armand will seize razors, or his coat will catch fire, or he might drown in a pond. A mother's life is a series of dramas, both tender and terrible. Each hour has its joys and fears.

At night, I dream of their future, which shines brightly under the watch of their guardian angel. Sometimes, Armand calls me in his sleep; I kiss his forehead and his sister's feet, watching them in their beauty. These are my joys! Just last night, I was roused by our guardian angel to Athenais' cradle; her head was too low, and Armand was uncovered, his feet purple with cold.

"Darling mother!" he cried, flinging his arms around me.

How important it is for a mother to have her children by her side at night! A nurse, however careful, cannot comfort them after a nightmare. They have their dreams, and explaining away those visions is arduous when they are scared and half-awake. I have learned to sleep lightly, seeing them stir through my eyelids. A sigh or rustle wakes me. The demon of convulsions lurks by their beds.

With the first light, my babies stir. Their chatter blends with the morning air, while Nais struggles to reach me, and Armand climbs up with monkey-like agility. They turn my bed into a playground, where I am at their mercy. The baby pulls my hair, and Armand stands guard over my breast, defending his property. Their antics and laughter banish sleep.

Then comes the dressing. I slip on my dressing gown, turn up my sleeves, and don an apron. With Mary’s help, I wash and scrub my little blossoms. I control the bath temperature, as much crying comes from mistakes here. The moment arrives for paper fleets and glass ducks, as amusement is key to getting them thoroughly washed. If you knew the diversions I invent to get them to allow a soft sponge, you would be awed by the ingenuity required in motherhood. Prayers, scoldings, and promises are all in play, and the jugglery must be deft. Providence matches a child's cunning with that of a mother. It takes little to make them laugh; a falling brush or slipping soap brings merry shouts. Our triumphs, though hard-won, are still triumphs, hidden from mortal eyes. Even their father knows nothing of it. Only God, His angels, and perhaps you can fathom the satisfaction Mary and I share when the children are finally clean amid the chaos of a true nursery.

I admit that English women excel in this department. They focus on material well-being, and their arrangements are admirable. My children will always be bare-legged and wear woolen socks. No swaddling or bandages; they will never be left alone. The helplessness of French infants means the nurse has freedom—that explains it all. A true mother is never free.

That is why I do not write. Besides managing the estate, I am raising two children.

Motherhood involves silent self-denial and devotion to minute details. I must watch the soup warming before the fire. Am I the kind of woman to shirk such cares? A child's laugh when they enjoy their food is worth a lifetime of adoration. Armand nods when pleased, and how could I leave the privilege of blowing on the spoonful of soup that is too hot for my little Nais, who still remembers my breast? To mince a chop for Nais, who has just cut her last teeth, requires patience, and only a mother can make an impatient child finish a meal.

No number of servants or English nurses can replace a mother in that daily contest, where gentleness must address the little griefs of childhood. Louise, caring for these innocent darlings engages the whole soul. A crying child condemns mother or nurse, except when the cry stems from natural pain. Now that I have two to care for (and a third on the way), they occupy all my thoughts. Even you, whom I love dearly, have become a memory.

My own dressing is often not complete by two o'clock. I have no faith in mothers whose rooms are immaculate and who look perfect. Yesterday was a lovely April day, and I wanted to take my children for a walk while I still could—time is pressing. Such an expedition is an epic for a mother! I dreamed of it the night before! Armand was to wear a little black velvet jacket, a new collar I made, and a Scotch cap; Nais was to be in white and pink with a delightful baby cap. All was laid out on my bed. The little brown heads had to be brushed, chirping like birds. Armand's hair is in curls, while Nais' is softly brought forward as a border to her cap. When they trotted off to the nursery, my heart raced. To see the children I dressed, their pure skin veined with blue, is a joy unmatched! I long for one more kiss on their white necks, which no woman can rival.

Once outside, triumphant in my efforts, I admired Armand's princely air as he helped Nais along the path. A carriage approached, and I tried to move them out of the way. They tumbled into a puddle, ruining my work! We had to return and change. I took the little one in my arms, forgetting my own ruined dress, while Mary seized Armand, and we re-entered. With a crying baby and a soaked child, what mind has a mother left for herself?

Dinner time arrives, and I have done nothing. Now comes the problem of feeding two children, putting on their bibs, turning up their sleeves, and getting them to eat. Amid these recurring cares, joys, and catastrophes, I neglect myself. If the children are naughty, I often don’t get rid of my curl papers all day. To write you these pages, I have let them cut pictures from my novels, build castles with books, and give Nais my silks to arrange, which has absorbed her completely.

Yet I have nothing to complain of. My children are strong and independent; they amuse themselves more easily than you might think. They find joy in everything; a little freedom is worth many toys. A few pebbles, small shells, and the mysteries of sand bring them pleasure. I watch Armand talking to flowers, flies, and chickens, imitating them. He is friendly with insects and never tires of admiring them. Everything small interests them. Armand is beginning to ask "why" about everything he sees. He even asks what I am saying to his godmother, whom he sees as a fairy. Strange how children hit the mark!

Alas! my sweet, I do not wish to sadden you with my joys. Let me tell you about your godson. The other day, a poor man followed us begging—beggars know a mother with a child cannot resist them. Armand, unaware of hunger and money, held out a trumpet I had just bought him, saying, "Here, take this!"

What joy could compare with that moment?

"May I keep it?" the poor man asked. "I too, madame, have had children," he added, hardly noticing the money I gave him.

I shudder at the thought of Armand going to school, knowing I have only three and a half years left to keep him with me. The flowers of his sunny childhood will fall before the scythe of a public school system; his gracious ways and candor will lose their spontaneity. They will cut the curls I have brushed and kissed so often! What will they do with the thinking being that is Armand?

And what of you? Are you still in love with Felipe? I have no worries about the Saracen. Goodbye; Nais has just tumbled, and if I continue, my letter will become a volume.

My sweet, tender Renee, you will have learned from the papers of the terrible calamity that has overwhelmed me. I could not write even a word. For twenty days, I never left his bedside; I received his last breath and closed his eyes, keeping vigil with the priests and repeating prayers for the dead. The cruel pangs I suffered felt like rightful punishment; yet, when I saw the calm smile on his lips, his last farewell to me, how could I believe I caused his death?

Be it so or not, he is gone, and I am left. To you, who have known us both, what more need I say? These words contain all. I would give my share of Heaven to hear that my prayers could bring him back! To see him again, even for a second, would mean I could breathe again without agony. Will you not come soon and soothe me with such promises? Is your love strong enough to deceive me?

But wait! You told me beforehand that he would suffer through me. Was it so? Yes, I had no right to his love. Like a thief, I took what was not mine, and my frenzied grasp crushed the life out of my bliss. The madness is over, but I feel alone. Merciful God! what torture can exceed the misery in that word?

When they took him away, I lay on the same bed, hoping to die. There was but a door between us, and I thought I could force it! Alas! I was too young for death; after forty days of cruel care and medical science, they slowly nursed me back to life. Now I find myself in the country, seated by my window, surrounded by lovely flowers he made bloom for me, gazing at the splendid view he loved. Ah! dear Renee, no words can express how new surroundings hurt when the heart is dead. I shiver at the sight of the moist earth in my garden, as if I walk on him! When I first went out, I trembled and could not move. It was so sad to see his flowers, and he not there!

My parents are in Spain. You know my brothers, and you are detained in the country. But do not worry about me; two angels of mercy have come to my side. The Duc and Duchesse de Soria rushed to their brother in his illness and have been everything I could wish. The last nights before the end found the three of us gathered in calm, wordless grief around the bed of this great man, rare in any age, head and shoulders above the rest of us. The patient resignation of my Felipe was angelic. His last words to me were simple and frank: "I had almost gone from life without leaving Fernand the Barony of Macumer; I must make a new will. My brother will forgive me; he knows what it is to love!"

I owe my life to my brother-in-law and his wife; they want to take me to Spain!

Ah! Renee, to no one but you can I speak freely of my grief. A sense of my own faults weighs me down, and there is solace in pouring them out to you, poor, unheeded Cassandra. My exactions, jealousy, and nagging unrest wore him to death. My love was dangerous for him because we shared the same sensitive nature; nothing was lost on him, and often my careless words struck straight to his heart. You cannot imagine how submissive he was. I only had to tell him to leave me alone, and he obeyed without a murmur. His last breath was spent blessing me, repeating that a single morning with me was more precious than a lifetime with another woman, even the Marie of his youth. My tears fall as I write.

This is my life now. I rise at midday and go to bed at seven; I linger over meals; I stand motionless for an hour before a single plant; I gaze into the trees; I take a serious interest in mere nothings; I long for shade, silence, and night. I fight through each hour, taking a gloomy pleasure in adding it to the heap of the vanquished. My peaceful park gives me all the company I care for; everything there is full of glorious images of my vanished joy, invisible to others but eloquent to me.

"I cannot away with you Spaniards!" I exclaimed one morning as my sister-in-law embraced me. "You have some nobility that we lack."

Ah! Renee, if I still live, it is because Heaven tempers the sense of affliction to the strength of those who bear it. Only a woman can know what it is to lose a love that was genuine, a passion that satisfied both spirit and flesh. How rare it is to find a man so gifted that to worship him brings no sense of degradation! If such fortune befalls us once, we cannot hope for it again. Men of true greatness, veiled by poetic grace, charm by high spiritual power—beware of love! Love will ruin you and the woman of your heart. This is my cry as I pace my woodland walks.

And he has left me no child! That love, rich in smiles and joy, has left no fruit. I am accursed. Can it be that, like the extremes of polar ice and torrid sand, the purity and vehemence of a single-hearted passion render it barren as hate? Is it only a marriage of reason, like yours, that is blessed with a family? Can Heaven be jealous of our passions? Such wild thoughts.

You are the one person whose company I could endure. Come to me; none but Renee should be with Louise in her sorrow. What a day when I first donned my widow's bonnet! When I saw myself in black, I fell back and wept until night. I weep again recalling that moment of anguish.

Goodbye. Writing tires me; thoughts crowd fast, but I have no heart to express them. Bring your children; you can nurse the baby here without making me jealous; all that is gone, he is not here, and I shall be glad to see my godson. Felipe wished for a child like little Armand. Come, then, come and help me bear my woe.

Yes, Renée, it is true; I have sold my house, Chantepleurs, and the farms in Seine-et-Marne, but I assure you I am neither mad nor ruined.

After settling everything, I have about twelve hundred thousand francs from my late husband’s fortune. In Paris, my house brought ten thousand francs in rent, but delays and legal troubles made life burdensome and frustrating for a twenty-seven-year-old widow.

Now my fortune is secured with the state. Instead of paying taxes, I receive thirty thousand francs every six months from the Treasury, handed to me by a pleasant clerk. If the nation were to fail, I would still be as well off as before, and I have time to save a little capital. Besides, the Comte de l’Estorade is a peer of this semi-republic and a friend at Court, so I have no cause for anxiety.

What drives a woman to such financial calculations? Love, my dear!

Now I must reveal the reasons behind my actions, which may have puzzled you. I am to be married in a village near Paris. I love and am loved. Forgive me for keeping this secret; I wished to spare you and de l’Estorade the inevitable concerns. You know how jealous I can be, and I wanted to avoid the anxiety.

The man I love had thirty thousand francs in debts, which I have paid. You might think him an adventurer, but I investigated him. He has been courting me for nearly two years. I am twenty-seven and he is twenty-three — a significant difference. He is a poet who has lived on very little and spent more time dreaming than working. Practical people often see artists as unreliable, and I understand that skepticism.

Despite his debts and our age gap, I am ready to end a long period of restraint. This is not a naive surrender; my love is profound, and he has waited patiently. He is noble and tender, and his love is genuine. My beloved is Gaston, the illegitimate son of the beautiful Lady Brandon, who died tragically. He was raised by his brother Louis, who has not been heard from in years. Louis, a sailor, wrote encouraging letters, but his absence has left Gaston in despair.

Gaston believed genius would bring fortune, but his life has been hardship. He has struggled to make a name in literature, and his debts have increased. I met him at Mme. d’Espard’s and felt an immediate connection. He has talent and pride, which can intimidate women.

Gaston has no idea of the depth of my love. I am confident in our happiness, and I feel a passion I have never known. I adore him as Felipe once adored me, and I am full of fears of losing him or of becoming less attractive as he remains youthful. Yet I believe I can nurture our love and keep it alive.

If his love should wane, I would blame myself, not him. I know my nature leans toward being a lover rather than a mother. Should disaster come, I beg you, Renée, to care for my children as my legacy. Your qualities would make my death less bitter.

I wish to keep my marriage private, performed in secret, to avoid distressing you. I will retain control of my property, and Gaston will not know its full extent. I plan to assign him a stock yielding twelve thousand francs a year, which he will find on the eve of our wedding. If he refuses it, I would end our engagement.

This long confession has exhausted me. I will continue later; I must spend tomorrow in the country.

October 20th.

I have taken steps to ensure our secrecy. I purchased a small property near Ville-d’Avray: twenty acres, a lakelet, and a charming garden. The area is secluded, surrounded by woods, and beautifully landscaped. My chalet, inspired by Swiss designs, is well-equipped and hidden from view.

The property includes a separate house for staff and a concealed entrance, ensuring privacy. I have carefully selected servants who will be loyal and discreet. All my cherished belongings from Paris have been moved to the chalet, creating a simple but elegant home.

The tranquility of this valley is unparalleled, filled with nature’s sounds. I worry such perfect happiness may attract misfortune, as you have often warned.

When I visited recently, I was moved to tears and paid my architect without hesitation, insisting on confidentiality about my ownership.

Now you understand my mysterious actions and the true purpose behind my investments. Love is serious, and I have removed financial burdens to focus solely on him.

Gaston remains unaware of my plans. We will begin our life at the chalet soon, and while our living will be modest, I intend to invest in my appearance for him. I want to maintain my beauty throughout our marriage so that I am loved as much at the end as at the beginning.

In two days I will be Mme. Gaston. Is it fitting for a Christian to love so deeply?

My man of business remarked that he was losing a client. And you, my dear, may feel you are losing a sister.

Please address future letters to Mme. Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles. We will keep our lives secret from the locals and procure provisions from Paris. Our retreat has been prepared in silence; only the architect knows of our existence.

Farewell. As I write I feel a mix of grief and joy, proving my love for Gaston is as deep as my sorrow in leaving you.

My Dear Daniel,

I need two witnesses for my marriage and ask you to come tomorrow evening with our friend Joseph Bridau. My future wife wishes to live in complete retirement, which explains our rare meetings this past year. After the wedding we will be apart for a long time, but I know you will understand. Friendship can endure absence; while I will miss you, I shall not see you in my own home. She has sacrificed the closeness of a childhood friend for me, and I must do the same.

This proves our love is deep and genuine, founded on mutual understanding. It gives me immense joy, yet I carry a painful secret. You know how desperate my finances have been, and only through your support have I managed. My wife is wealthy; I am not. I once joked about wanting a rich heiress, but now that it is real I feel a bitter humiliation I cannot shake. This shame confirms my love for her, for I do not recoil from it, even if it overwhelms me.

Otherwise, my dreams have come true. She is the most beautiful and noble woman, with a charming personality and a sharp mind. At twenty-seven she is a widow without children, full of life yet occasionally melancholic. Despite her aristocratic background, she loves me despite my humble origins. Our love has grown over time; we share a deep emotional connection and avoid the physical aspect of love out of mutual respect.

One day she confessed that her marriage was her regret, revealing the depth of her feelings. You, Daniel, are a giant among men, and you love a remarkable woman, just as I love my Armande.

---

Louise, can it be that you intend a life of wedded solitude after sacrificing your first husband? You seem to court future misery. I see you are resolved, and the man who won your heart must possess a rare charm. But have you forgotten your earlier criticisms of young men, when you claimed they had tarnished purity?

You are lucky to believe in happiness, and while I cannot blame you, I must warn you. Nature and society conspire against absolute happiness, and I foresee some disaster arising from your boundless joy. I have no objections to your fiancé, but I hope you will describe him to me one day.

I have not told Louis about your marriage; it would only invite his ridicule. I will simply say you wish to keep it secret. You are brave to face winter first, and I will be your discreet friend. Your journey into this emotional wilderness is like an explorer’s perilous expedition, though fortunately only a short distance from Paris. I wish you a safe journey and a swift return.

---

What has happened to you, my dear? After two years of silence, Renee has a right to worry about Louise. Is this what love does, scattering friendships like ours? You must admit that, while I am devoted to my children, a mother’s love is expansive and does not diminish other affections. I miss your letters and long to see your face.

Regarding our situation, I will be brief. Your last letter contained sharp comments about our political position. You mocked us for keeping our posts in the Audit Department, which Louis owes to Charles X’s favor. But how could we provide for Athenais and Rene on an income of forty thousand livres, of which thirty thousand is entailed? We must live on our salary and save the estate’s income for their future. In twenty years we will have saved enough to provide for both children.

Louis has been raised to the Peerage and is now a grand officer of the Legion of Honor. He has made himself useful in the Chamber and was offered an embassy, which I declined for the sake of our children’s education. I will not leave Paris until Rene has finished his schooling.

We could not remain loyal to the elder branch of the dynasty without jeopardizing our children’s prospects. A mother is not called to be a Decius, especially now. In fifteen years, Louis will retire on a good pension, and I hope to secure a post for Armand in the Audit Department.

I am a happy mother; my children give me endless joy. Armand is now a day scholar at Henry IV’s school. I wanted him in public school but could not bear to part with him, so I arranged for him to attend by day and return home each evening. He is the same charming boy you loved, and his tutor praises his progress.

Home education has drawbacks: children raised at home may be exposed too early to the world’s passions and may lack the social skills gained by peers. A mother must keep her children from society’s distractions, as I have done.

Now that I am thirty, I feel a duty fulfilled. My children seem to sense my thoughts and respond. Armand, once dull, has lately excelled, winning prizes for translation and essays. I felt immense pride when his name was called and longed to shout, “I am his mother!”

Rene, inspired by Armand, wants to attend school too. The three children can be noisy, but I cherish every moment. I am the only one allowed to care for them; the joys of motherhood outweigh the challenges.

I must study their characters closely, for a mother alone discerns her children’s natural aptitudes. Home-bred children often develop ease and tact, compensating for lack of innate ability. I believe Armand will become an admirable public servant, while Rene will be a bold sailor.

Nais is so dear to me I can hardly see her as separate from myself. I want her to be happy and will only give her to a man who truly loves her. Yet I worry about her future and the risk of a poor marriage. Your silence has reminded me of the anguish marriage can bring a mother. Farewell for now, dear Louise, and please write back.

So, after two years of silence, you wonder about my lack of letters. My dear Renée, words fail to express my happiness. We bear everything with ease; we are in perfect harmony. There has been no discord in our feelings, and each day brings its own joy. We fear not monotony; rather, we regret that our time together may not suffice for all the poetic expressions of our love. We take more pleasure in each other's company than on the first day, and every hour reveals new reasons to love one another.

Every evening we speak of visiting Paris, of the new boulevards and the finished Madeleine, but come morning we linger over breakfast and take a siesta instead. Gaston gazes at me as if I were a painting, and we both shed tears of joy reflecting on our love. I still play the mistress, pretending to give less than I receive, and I delight in this little play. There is something sweet about passion tempered by tenderness, with Gaston, my master, remaining a timid suitor at my command.

You asked me to describe him, yet it is impossible to portray the man we love without emotion. Society alters men so that the man in public can seem quite different from the man of strong feeling. The charming suitor may lack the deeper qualities a woman values, while an unrefined man may possess profound capacity for love. Our ideal is a man whose outward appearance aligns with his true nature, embodying grace that cannot be acquired. This ideal haunts every woman's imagination, and in Gaston I have found it.

Ah, dear, I never knew love could be so intertwined with youth, talent, and beauty. Gaston is unpretentious, moving with effortless grace. When we walk together, our bodies fit perfectly, seeming a single figure gliding through the woods. Our thoughts, desires, and words harmonize. Sometimes we walk in silence, listening to the rain and admiring the sunset, feeling as if in silent prayer for our happiness. A timid kiss exchanged in nature feels like a divine act of worship.

We return home each time more in love than before. Such passionate love between older married people would be scandalous in Paris; only in the woods can we express it freely.

Gaston is of average height, well-proportioned, and agile. He has a fair complexion, dark hair, and a striking resemblance to Louis XIII. His honorable poverty has spared him the vices that ruin many young men. His teeth are magnificent, and he possesses a robust constitution. His blue eyes, filled with tenderness for me, can flash with intensity at inspiring thoughts.

He has a remarkable temperament, surprising in its evenness. I have heard many tales of women’s troubles with men who are dissatisfied with themselves, but Gaston is free from such burdens. I have learned that marrying an older man can lead to unequal unions, and I urge you not to let Athénaïs marry one.

His smile is a treasure—always fresh and full of unspoken love and gratitude. Our shared experiences have become part of our joy, and even the smallest details of nature remind us of our time together. We share a deep connection, as if we are two editions of the same book. Our ability to view subjects broadly brings constant pleasure, and this unity of thought feels like a pledge of love.

Despite these pleasures, life here is laborious. I do my own room, refusing to let a hired hand intrude on my personal space. My room is meticulously arranged so that Gaston finds it pleasing. I rise early, bathe in cold water to refresh myself before he wakes, emerging like a newly opened flower. He appreciates this freshness without knowing the effort behind it.

My daily grooming is handled by my maid, and I dress several times a day for Gaston’s delight. We take great interest in our flowers and horticulture, surrounding our chalet with beauty. Our garden is a source of pride, and we eagerly tend our fruits and vegetables.

To keep Gaston's mind engaged, I encourage him to finish plays he began during earlier struggles. This literary work requires reflection and inspiration, and I assist him, ensuring our winter evenings are never dull.

Our servants have an easy time; they need no reprimand. They have cleverly presented us as a traveling couple, content with their roles. The gardeners sell surplus produce, and we enjoy an abundance hard to find in Paris.

I strive to fill the role of society for Gaston, ensuring his solitude does not become tedious. I have learned true jealousy—the kind born of love. The thought of him ceasing to love me sends shivers through me, and I tremble before him.

Alas, I am still without a child. I long for the day when our home will be filled with the laughter of children running through our garden. It pains me to see your lovely children, for my life feels narrower without them. Motherhood expands a woman's heart, while love can be selfish. I envy your happiness and the joy you find in your children.

As I approach thirty, I feel the limits of my beauty closing in. Though still attractive, I worry about aging while Gaston remains young. I often ask him to promise he will tell me if his love ever fades, and he swears it will not, finding the idea absurd.

Goodbye, dear friend. Shall we let years pass without writing again? Happiness can be monotonous, and perhaps that is why Dante seems greater in the Paradiso than in the Inferno. I am not Dante, only your friend, and I do not wish to bore you. You can write, as your children provide endless joy, while I... No more of this. A thousand loves.

My dear Louise, I have read your letter repeatedly; it reads more like a child's thoughts than a woman's. You forget that pure love belongs to nature and has been distorted by civilization. Society created the family as the foundation of organized life at the cost of individual desires. A married man dedicates himself to his wife, who benefits from his efforts.

You have taken a misguided path: the dream of being adored suits the young, not a mature woman. Vanity may enjoy inspiring adoration, but true fulfillment is the life of wife and mother. Return to Paris; fear not misfortune but happiness.

Life's simple pleasures—bread, air, silence—sustain us. Passionate love exhausts; motherhood's feelings endure and reward. I chose self-sacrifice and would choose it again. Your life, despite its romantic veneer, is rooted in selfishness; I plead with you as I watch your happiness tested.

Life is balance; excess brings pain or fleeting pleasure. You are unwell because you keep a passion marriage should steady. The glory of home is calmness and shared life, which you overlook. You, more passionate than tender, would be both wife and mistress, undermining marriage's sanctity.

If you could understand a mother's sweet self-discipline. My proud nature softened into gentle melancholy, rewarded by motherhood's joys. I fear your life may darken. I wish you could witness family joys, but my arguments seem futile against your delusions. I had hoped time would restore reason, but your appetite for love seems insatiable. Farewell; I despair my letter, meant to reconcile you with society, only revealed your selfishness.

---

Renee, calamity has struck—no, a thunderbolt for poor Louise. Doubt is my calamity; certainty would be death. The day before yesterday, after finishing my first toilette, I looked for Gaston to walk but found him gone. In the stable his mare Fedelta was in a lather, implying he'd been to Paris without telling me. Suspicion seized me; I fought to compose myself.

When Gaston found me I was pale and shaken. He carried me to the parlor; I would not speak and fled to my room to weep. He waited outside; I said I'd see him when ready. After composing myself he returned, and I asked about mud on Fedelta's legs. He playfully threatened to withhold explanations; yet I saw no disloyalty in his eyes, though I remained unconvinced.

We spent the day together, and my heart bound tighter to him. I hated that mare for my distress. I asked if I bored him; he reassured me. I suggested selling Fedelta; his response crushed me, making me feel inadequate. Alone, I was consumed by despair, surrounded by darkness.

Next morning I told him to take Fedelta to Paris; my tone betrayed turmoil. He urged trust; I tried to suppress suspicion. He left, and I grappled with fear. I felt a strange solemnity in my love and watched him go with a sense of doom.

When he returned late with a whip as a gift I felt it only distracted from something deeper. I later learned he bought it from Verdier, confirming my suspicions. I resolved to keep watch while staying silent.

Days passed; Gaston changed, working harder—seeming to need money. His drawer was empty; he received letters he quickly tore up. Jealousy consumed me; I followed him to the post office and confronted him about a letter from Nathan announcing a play's acceptance.

Despite his success, I couldn't shake that he hid something. I kept suspicions to myself until confirming fears. A growing certainty that he had a rival drove me to discover the truth.

After investigation I learned Gaston visited a woman in Paris; I followed and found she was his brother's widow, legally married. The revelation shattered me: he had been providing for her and her children. I returned tormented by thoughts of betrayal and jealousy.

Despite the pain, my love for Gaston remained. I struggled with his past and wanted to confront him about his feelings for the widow, but feared the truth would destroy me. I even planned my own end, unable to bear the suffering.

---

My dear Louise, I write urgently; your last letter alarmed me. I confided in Louis to save you. Though our methods may seem extreme, the outcome is satisfactory. I even involved the police, kept secret among us.

Gaston is not as you feared. His brother Louis died in Calcutta, leaving a widow and two children. Gaston has secretly supported them, using his earnings for his sister-in-law and her children. The woman you mistook for a rival is not a threat but a mother in need; Gaston acted from kindness and duty.

You should love him more; he has shown sensitivity and care. Welcome the widow and her children into your life—perhaps adopt the boys. This revelation should give you peace, not despair.

Ah, my dear friend, what can I say? It is too late for me. My actions have sealed my fate. Tormented by love for Gaston, I cannot bear losing him. I am filled with despair and long for relief. Come, dear friend, and witness my decline.

My love,—Take the children to Provence without me; I must stay with Louise, who has only a few days left. I cannot leave her or her husband, for whom I worry.

You know the letter that sent me rushing to Ville d'Avray, gathering doctors along the way. Since then I have not left my dear friend, sitting up every night for two weeks.

When I arrived, Louise was with Gaston, looking beautiful and happy, but it was a facade. She whispered, "He must not know; I am dying." A chill ran through me as I felt her burning hand and saw the red spots on her cheeks. I was glad I had left the doctors in the woods until needed.

"Leave us for a little," she told Gaston. "Two women who have not met for five years have secrets to share."

Once alone she collapsed into my arms, unable to hold back her tears. "Tell me about it," I urged. "I have brought the best surgeon and physician from the hospital, along with Bianchon."

"Ah! Have them in at once if they can save me! The passion that drove me to death now cries for life!"

"But what have you done to yourself?"

"I have brought myself to the last stage of consumption in just a few days."

"But how?"

"I got myself into a profuse perspiration at night, then lay by the lake in the dew. Gaston thinks I have a cold, but I am dying!"

"Send him to Paris; I will fetch the doctors myself," I said, rushing out to find them.

Alas! After the consultation, none of the doctors gave me hope; they all believe Louise will die with the fall of the leaves. Her constitution had aided her plan, but a few days of folly made her case desperate.

I cannot express my feelings upon hearing this. I have lived in Louise as much as in my own life. I was crushed and could not escort the doctors out. I was lost in bitter thoughts when I heard Louise's clear voice: "So there is no hope for me!"

She made me get up and took me to her drawing-room. "Stay with me to the end; I won't have sad faces around me. I must keep the truth from him. I know I have the strength to do it. I am full of youth and spirit, and can die standing! I have no regrets. I am dying as I wished, still young and beautiful, in the perfection of my womanhood."

"For him, I can see I would have made his life miserable. My jealousy has already wounded him. When my suspicions met indifference, that would have been my death. I have had my share of life. There are people who have lived sixty years without two years of real life, while my thirty years are doubled by the intensity of my love."

"For him and me, the end is happy. But for you, dear Renée, it is different. You lose a loving sister, and that is a loss that cannot be repaired. You alone have the right to mourn my death."

After a long pause she continued: "The moral of my death is cruel. My dear doctor was right; marriage cannot rest on passion or even love. Your life is noble, giving your husband growing affection, while my passionate eagerness diminished. Twice I have gone astray, and twice Death has reached for my happiness. The first time, he took the noblest of men; now he takes me from my poet husband, with all his beauty and grace."

"I would not complain. I have known two men of nobility—one in mind, the other in form. I die adored—what more could I wish? Perhaps time to draw near to God, whom I may have thought too little of. My spirit will fly towards Him, full of love, praying to be reunited with the two who made my life a heaven. Without them, paradise would be a desert."

"My example would be fatal to others; to meet a Felipe or a Gaston is more than mortals can expect. The doctrine of society regarding marriage aligns with natural law. Women are weak and should sacrifice their will to their husbands, who should lay their selfishness at their feet. The stir women have created by their whining is ridiculous, showing how well we deserve the epithet of children."

She spoke in her gentle voice, expressing grave truths prettily, until Gaston entered with his sister-in-law, the children, and the English nurse he had fetched from Paris.

"Here are the pretty instruments of my torture," she said as her nephews approached. "What a wonderful likeness to their uncle!"

She welcomed Mme. Gaston and insisted she consider the chalet her home, playing the hostess in her best manner.

I wrote to the Duc and Duchesse de Chaulieu, the Duc de Rhetore, and the Duc de Lenoncourt-Givry, as well as to Madeleine. It was time. The next day Louise, exhausted from the exertion, could not go out; she only got up for dinner. That evening Madeleine de Lenoncourt, her brothers, and her mother arrived. The coolness caused by Louise's second marriage faded. Every day since, her father and brothers have visited, and the duchesses spend their evenings at the chalet. Death unites as well as separates; it silences petty feelings.

Louise remains charming, graceful, sensible, witty, and tender. She has retained her perfect tact and lavishes her brilliant mind on us, which made her one of Paris's queens.

"I want to look well even in my coffin," she said with her matchless smile as she lay down on the bed where she would linger for a fortnight.

Her room lacks the sick-chamber atmosphere; medicines and nursing supplies are concealed.

"Is not my deathbed pretty?" she asked the priest who came to confess her.

We watched her like misers. The anxiety and harsh truths have prepared Gaston for the worst. He is brave, but the blow has struck deep. I would not be surprised if he followed her soon. Yesterday, while walking by the lake, he said, "I must be a father to those two children," pointing to his sister-in-law with the boys. "But I want your promise that you will be a second mother to them and persuade your husband to accept the role of guardian."

He spoke simply, like a man who knows his time is short. He wears smiles for Louise, but I see through his facade. He matches her courage.

Louise wishes to see her godson, but I prefer he remains in Provence; she might want to remember him generously, putting me in a difficult position.

Goodbye, my love.

August 25th (her birthday).

Yesterday evening Louise was delirious for a short time, but her babbling was beautiful, showing that even the madness of gifted people is not that of fools. In a faint voice she sang Italian airs from I Puritani, La Sonnambula, Moïse, while we stood around her bed in silence. Not one of us, not even the Duc de Rhetore, had dry eyes, realizing her soul was departing. She could no longer see us, yet her charm lingered in the melody, sweet and otherworldly.

During the night the death agony began. It is now seven in the morning, and I have just raised her from bed. A flicker of strength returned; she wished to sit by the window and asked for Gaston's hand. Then, my love, the sweetest spirit we shall ever see on this earth departed, leaving us with an empty shell.

The last sacrament was administered the evening before, unbeknownst to Gaston, who was sleeping during the ceremony. After she was moved to the window, she asked me to read the De Profundis in French, facing the lovely scene she created. She repeated the words after me and pressed her husband's hands.

August 26th.

My heart is broken. I have just seen her in her winding-sheet; her face is pale with purple shadows. Oh! I want my children! Bring me my children!
