LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES  
By Honoré de Balzac  
Translated by R. S. Scott

DEDICATION  
To George Sand, whose friendship has cheered me through literary trials. May this work reflect the company of great names and noble hearts that accompany my writings.

LETTERS OF TWO BRIDES

FIRST PART

I. LOUISE DE CHAULIEU TO RENEE DE MAUCOMBE. PARIS, September.  
My dearest Renée, I am free; I hope you have also left the convent. My aunt released me, alarmed for my health after your departure left me despondent. In Paris I feel our souls remain connected. Alone, I felt trapped; my aunt cannot grasp my longing for life beyond the convent.

Her parting words mixed affection and warning. Arriving in Paris, no one awaited me; my late grandmother’s empty house overwhelmed me. Philippe, her servant, told me my inheritance is intact, but my father has not restored the family home, and I despair over my prospects.

Settling in, I found comfort in my grandmother’s memories and resolved to make the house mine. I long to hear about your life and hope you are well.

October 19th.  
I met my mother for the first time since my return. She treated me as an equal and wishes to be a friend rather than a strict parent. My father was affectionate too, and I felt a sense of belonging. I am preparing for my debut into society with a generous allowance and a governess, Miss Griffith, to guide me.

II. THE SAME TO THE SAME November 25th.  
I am beginning to feel at home. My father’s allowance lets me buy new clothes for my social debut, and I am eager to enter society. Miss Griffith is a good companion, and my family’s preoccupations leave me free to find my own way.

III. THE SAME TO THE SAME December.  
I am ready to enter society. After much preparation I feel confident in my appearance and plan to present myself as charming and innocent to win favor. Yet I regret my convent past; the transition is overwhelming, and my family remains distant, absorbed in their own affairs. I hope to uncover my family’s mysteries and learn the ways of the society I am about to join.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
On December 15th, I drove through the Champs-Elysees and Bois de Boulogne on a beautiful autumn day. Despite my carefully curated appearance, I received little attention from passersby, except for my approving parents. Griffith scanned the crowd indifferently while I grew frustrated when one man inspected my carriage without noticing me, realizing that beauty in Paris is more common than I expected.

The men I observed were generally unattractive, lacking elegance in their fashion, particularly their hats. I commented to Miss Griffith about their looks, but she defended them as cultivated.

At an evening ball, I stayed close to my mother, who attracted all the attention while I served merely as a pretext for compliments. My dress, which I thought stunning, paled beside the married women’s finery. The men at the ball did not impress me; they seemed worn and lacked the proud bearing of their ancestors. One man stood out for his beauty and intellect but appeared self-absorbed, suggesting intellectuals often prioritize ambition over genuine connections.

Determined not to let society's indifference affect me, I focused on enjoying the dance. Returning home, I shared my experiences with my mother, who advised me to be discreet about my feelings, underscoring the complexities of feminine duplicity.

A fortnight after my arrival at the Castle of Maucombe, my family dined with M. de l'Estorade, a wealthy neighbor whose son had been lost during Bonaparte's reign. After evading conscription, the son joined the Emperor's bodyguard but vanished after the Battle of Leipzig. Eventually he returned from travels through Russia, Poland, and Germany, aided by the French Minister in Berlin. M. de l'Estorade has amassed a fortune of about 250,000 livres.

My parents support a match between me and Louis de l'Estorade, especially since I would be acceptable without a dowry. The dinner marked my first meeting with Louis, who, despite his humble background, is now my intended. The meal was simple, and Louis appeared worn and silent. Despite this, I agreed to marry him, contingent on my ability to improve his estate.

While you prepare for the excitement of Parisian life, I embrace a simpler existence. I am determined to beautify my new home, envisioning a life filled with nature and family, free from past turmoil. My future seems straightforward, with no more Bonaparte to disrupt it.

In letters to Don Fernand Paris, Don Felipe Henarez reassures his brother of his safety amid Spain's political chaos, reflecting on their ancestors' massacre that forced them into survival. Despite Fernand's promises of protection, Felipe remains skeptical, believing true Spaniards do not need reassurances. He recounts his escape from Spain, aided by former foes, and laments the decline of his estates, feeling trapped between life and death.

Felipe urges Fernand to claim his titles and properties from the King, using his affection for Marie Heredia as leverage. He acknowledges their mutual feelings and encourages their union while resigning himself to obscurity. He plans to retain his mother’s diamonds for independence and offers Fernand a wedding gift from his limited savings.

In a subsequent letter, Felipe reflects on his modest life in France, teaching Spanish and finding solace in nature. He expresses no regrets about his abdication, feeling detached from his former wealth. He channels his unexpressed tenderness into his love for Spain and hopes Fernand and Marie will cherish each other.

Louise de Chaulieu writes to Renee de Maucombe, expressing disbelief at her quick engagement and warning against the constraints of marriage. She shares disillusionment with society and men while remaining hopeful for love. In a second letter, she reveals interest in Don Henarez, noting his dignified demeanor amid the superficiality of her social circle. Louise grapples with her emotions and societal expectations, recognizing the rarity of genuine affection.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Mlle. de Chaulieu fears her friend Renée's impending marriage will smother her spirit and urges her to pursue true passion instead of yielding to social expectation. She becomes fascinated by M. Henarez, a melancholic Spanish teacher whose mysterious allure convinces her that genuine passion can exist outside marriage.

Mme. de L'Estorade warns Mlle. de Chaulieu against M. Henarez, calling him a dangerous adventurer and alluding to her own marital troubles. Mlle. de Chaulieu also reflects on her father's political ambitions and France's instability, highlighting the tension between personal desire and family duty.

As her feelings for M. Henarez deepen, she admires his charm and intellect. Mme. de L'Estorade recounts her marriage, contrasting single and married life and stressing the value of friendship and independence. The Duc de Soria urges his brother to accept family support, emphasizing familial bonds despite difficulties.

Mlle. de Chaulieu challenges Mme. de L'Estorade's pragmatic view of marriage, insisting love should guide decisions while she wrestles with her attraction to M. Henarez. After a romantic encounter, she feels exhilarated and uneasy and burns a letter from him, a gesture that symbolizes her emotional conflict.

Preparing for a significant evening, she dresses with white camellias and contemplates love's force and the simplicity of expressing feeling through flowers. The evening deepens her connection with M. Henarez and strengthens her resolve to embrace her emotions. She delights in a new carriage and the empowerment she finds in love, while Mme. de L'Estorade stresses that marriage demands balancing affection with responsibility.

Mlle. de Chaulieu ponders the complexities of her heart and her feelings for Felipe, longing for his reciprocation while chiding her own hypocrisy. She yearns for clarity and hopes for a bold declaration from Felipe, dreaming of the conversations that might bring resolution.
################################################
############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
In her letter, Renée expresses deep dissatisfaction with marriage, feeling it imposes obligations on women and treats them as possessions. She laments emotional sacrifices and yearns for a love that transcends social expectations, hoping motherhood might finally bring fulfillment.

Renée notes her husband Louis is oblivious to her struggles, benefiting from her sacrifices without recognizing their weight. She longs for a child to channel her emotions, believing motherhood could provide joy and purpose.

Louise describes turmoil over a secret meeting with Felipe, a man she desires. Torn between social norms and her instincts, she plans a clandestine encounter in her garden to explore their feelings away from scrutiny. Preparing for the meeting, she reflects on the thrill and anxiety and wishes Renée were there to share the excitement. Their encounter is charged with unspoken emotions: Louise voices fears about the risks while Felipe vows to prove himself worthy.

Their exchange reveals a deepening connection and an awareness of the complexities of love and power in relationships. Both women navigate love, duty, and personal fulfillment, trying to reconcile their aspirations with societal realities.

In a letter to Felipe, Louise expresses disappointment: if he does not find Racine’s Bérénice moving, they lack a true connection. She criticizes his complacency, feeling diminished if he fails to recognize her uniqueness, and warns that any sign of waning passion would plunge her into despair, underscoring her desire for a profound bond.

Felipe responds remorseful for hurting Louise, interpreting her jealousy as love. He longs for closeness and fears she might no longer love him, but her letter restores his hope.

Later, Louise announces her engagement to Felipe, who has accepted her family’s conditions regarding her fortune. She acknowledges her mother’s wisdom about marriage and reflects on love’s complexities, feeling both excitement and anxiety as the wedding approaches. The letters collectively explore themes of love, loyalty, and societal expectation, revealing the emotional depth of relationships in this period.

Dear friend, it has been nearly eight months since our marriage, and your silence is inexcusable.

We traveled to the Castle of Chantepleurs, bought by Macumer in Nivernais, sixty leagues from Paris. Our servants arrived ahead, and we reached the castle the following evening. I slept during the journey, comforted by Felipe's tender presence. His affectionate gestures moved me; I woke to his unchanged, passionate gaze. Though outwardly calm, he later revealed the intense feelings he had hidden.

Settling into our new life, I found marriage less daunting than expected. Felipe reassured me of his fidelity, and I felt a complex blend of joy and deepening love. True happiness in marriage is often unspoken, shadowed by duty. I admire your strength, Renée, and see that those who marry for love, like me, live differently from those who marry for convenience, like you.

We spent seven and a half blissful months at Chantepleurs, like lovers escaping the world. Yet I often thought of you and your pragmatic marriage. Now we are in Paris, enjoying a lovely home in the Rue du Bac; I feel more beautiful and content than ever.

I wish to understand your heart, for I suspect you hide wounds. I long for you to find joy with Louis, who adores you. Remember our youthful vows to share burdens? I now pray for your happiness as fervently as you once prayed for mine.

I have an idea: encourage Louis to pursue a political career. If he becomes a deputy, we could be together in Paris, and perhaps he would gain a title. This might be a new beginning for both of us.

Your letter filled me with joy and a touch of envy. As I sit by the Mediterranean, tending climbing plants, I feel the stirrings of motherhood, which transforms my sense of sacrifice into strength. While you experience love with Felipe, my love is directed toward nurturing future children. I grapple with strange cravings—particularly for rotten fruit—which oddly ease my discomfort. I wonder when the full sense of motherhood will awaken.

As I close, I urge you to remain humble in your happiness. Your love for Felipe seems to defy fate, and I fear such joy may attract misfortune.

We are thrilled to announce that Renée has given birth to a healthy boy, Armand-Louis de l'Estorade, and we hope you will be his godmother. Your help in obtaining a title for my father would greatly benefit our son.

Yours sincerely,  
LOUIS DE L'ESTORADE.

In January 1826, Louise de Macumer writes to her friend Renée de l'Estorade, excited about an April visit to Chantepleurs and Renée's role as godmother to her first child—insisting her husband, Felipe, be godfather. Louise contrasts their lives: Renée as a mother, Louise still childless; both find joy in their roles despite Louise's yearning for motherhood and fear of jealousy.

Louise describes her vibrant life with Felipe and society's view of her beauty since their marriage. She longs for an intense love, even confessing a morbid wish to die young to preserve her happiness—an impulse that led to their first quarrel when she asked Felipe to end her life at thirty, which he refused.

Renée responds nearly five months after giving birth to her son Armand, apologizing for the delay. She recounts the emotional turmoil of childbirth and the overwhelming love she now feels, which surpassed her earlier fears. Nursing forged a deep bond with her child, and her feelings for her husband, Louis, shifted toward profound gratitude. She urges Louise to embrace motherhood, believing it will deepen her happiness. Together, the letters reveal the contrasting yet complementary experiences of two friends navigating love, marriage, and the transformations of parenthood.

Summary of Correspondence Between Mme. de Macumer and Mme. de L'Estorade (March - July 1826)

Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (March 1826)  
I apologize for my late reply; it’s been over three months. Felipe and I assumed your silence meant consent about the baby-wreathed luncheon service, which is now en route to Marseilles. I miss our conversations. Paris is overwhelming, full of social pressures that leave little room for genuine connection; my love for Felipe is my refuge.

I’m busy with gatherings and have acquired a fashionable reputation that pleases Felipe. My parents are in Madrid, and my brother’s marriage to an heiress secures our family’s future. Society’s initial criticism of my marriage has turned to admiration, giving me influence. Yet the constant attention can be intoxicating; I see why some women grow selfish in such an environment.

I long for a true friend and cherish Felipe’s love. We plan to move to Chantepleurs for a respite from Paris, and I look forward to seeing you in May after two years apart.

Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer  
Dear friend, motherhood has occupied me; I am wholly devoted to my son, Armand-Louis. My life is fulfilling, and I eagerly await your visit, hoping my home will be welcoming despite the trials of a teething baby.

Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (April 1826)  
I’m pleased to tell you that your father-in-law is now Comte de l'Estorade, which I arranged before leaving Paris.

Mme. de Macumer to Mme. de L'Estorade (July 1826)  
I must confess I left abruptly because of jealousy. Felipe’s attention to you during our visit distressed me. Your beauty and the joy of your motherhood exposed my insecurities, and I felt envious of your lively home.

Still, I recognize your happiness with L'Estorade, who appears respectable. I’m returning to Chantepleurs hoping to find fulfillment. I know it was foolish, but I needed to escape the discomfort I felt around you.

Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer  
Your sudden departure surprised us, and your letter startled me. You had no reason to be jealous; our conversations were innocent and aimed at your happiness. I urge you to return, for Felipe holds you in deep respect.

I’ve noticed your relationship lacks true partnership. If you continue to dominate, you risk remaining unfulfilled. Genuine love needs balance; embrace a supportive role to preserve Felipe’s dignity.

Reflect on my words. You have a chance at real happiness—don’t squander it. Return to Paris with Felipe; society may help you both grow. Farewell, my dear friend. I hope you find peace and clarity.

Mme. de Macumer (to an unnamed beloved)  
I’m delighted to be in Italy with Macumer, whose Sardinia plans are postponed. This enchanting country could convert any Protestant. Macumer is well received and I’m favored at court. We’ll soon be in Florence, spend a month in Tuscany and Naples, and reach Rome in November. The journey feels like a honeymoon, reviving our passion. Forgive the brevity of my letters; I am fully absorbed in this experience.

Vicomtesse de L'Estorade to Baronne de Macumer (summary)  
A reply awaits at Chantepleurs. She worries over the government’s impending dissolution, which may impede the Crown’s consolidation; her father will stand for election before resigning. She rejoices over her godson’s expected weaning in December and ends with affectionate remarks about the Baronne’s influence in Italy.

I received your distressing letter, forwarded by my steward. Renee, I won't dwell on hurt feelings, but I must tell you how deeply your words affected me.

We had just returned from a charming reception at the ambassador’s, where I felt radiant and Macumer seemed enraptured. Then I read your harsh note and sobbed, barely keeping my composure. Felipe, my dear Arab, insisted you were wrong. He led me to the balcony, professing his love in Italian, declaring that even if your predictions proved true he would not trade our moments for a commonplace life with children.

While he murmured tenderness, a bat’s cry frightened me and I fainted. I am well now. This morning I knelt before Felipe, confessed my fears, and asked him to guide me if I ever strayed from his desire. Your letter frightened me because you are a true friend, and I value your judgment.

Thank you, Renee. Your words revealed the depth of Macumer’s devotion. Rome is the city of love, where passion mingles with art and faith.

We will soon meet the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Venice. If you write, address it to Paris; we leave Rome shortly.

P.S. Your letter shows you know love more in theory than in practice. Love yields many outcomes and resists a single explanation.

My father has been elected to the Chamber, my father-in-law has died, and I am nearing my second confinement. I mention these facts to avoid alarming you with the black seal.

Your letter from Rome unsettled me. You and Felipe behave like children. His devotion may resemble a man’s lavish affection for a courtesan—intense yet aware of infidelities. I draw a bitter lesson from our divergent fates: do not love if you wish only to be loved.

Louis received the cross of the Legion of Honor when he was elected to the provincial Council nearly three years ago. My father has requested the rank of Officer of the Legion for him, and I hope you can help without entangling yourself in my father’s ambitions.

Ah, Louise, I have endured hell. I recount my anguish because you are a sister to me. Five days of torment felt like centuries; a mother who has not suffered cannot understand.

The night before the crisis the weather was sultry, and my son Armand was unusually irritable—attributed at first to teething. In the morning he cried for water with a strange voice and went stiff. I called for Louis, fearing he would die. The doctors arrived but disagreed; they suggested leeches and ice, which to me felt like death.

For an hour I held my motionless child as he convulsed. I felt helpless; my mother was far away. After four days of terror the doctors recommended a painful ointment. I refused, trusting nature instead. In desperation I pressed Armand to me and prayed. Miraculously he began to recover, but I was utterly spent.

Now he plays as I write, yet I remain uneasy about the cause: teething or a nervous disorder? I fear for my unborn child. The agony of watching a child convulse is beyond words.

Dear Renee, Macumer and I forgave your earlier indiscretion when we learned of your distress. Your double anguish touched me, and I find some compensation in our childlessness.

I write to inform you that Louis has been promoted to Officer of the Legion. You are fortunate—perhaps you will have the daughter you wished for.

My brother married Mlle. de Mortsauf upon our return. The King has granted him the reversion of a prestigious post, conditional on a family emblem arrangement.

My parents visited for the wedding and will return to Madrid after my reception tomorrow. The carnival promises to be lively, especially with the Duc and Duchesse de Soria in Paris, which unnerves me. I lavish affection on Felipe to keep his attention from Marie Heredia, who is beautiful but lacks charm.

We plan to retreat to Chantepleurs after March to economize; our Italian tour was costly. Felipe and I long for the countryside’s tranquility after Italy’s extravagance. We look forward to your visit; I will not be jealous. You may explore Macumer’s heart freely. Since Rome, his love for me has deepened.

With love, dear friend.

My daughter Jeanne-Athenais is two months old; my mother and Louis’ grand-uncle are her godparents. I plan to visit Chantepleurs soon, as you worry little about a nursing mother. Your godson can now say your name—Matoumer. He’s growing quickly, but I worry for him while I recover from childbirth; the doctors advise special precautions. My father noticed Felipe looks thin, and I sense something troubles you—your letter lacked warmth. My nurse scolds me for writing, and Mlle. Athenais is hungry. Please write me a long letter.

For the first time, I find myself alone and crying beneath a willow by the Chantepleurs marsh. After nearly three years of marriage without a child, I yearn for motherhood, imagining the joy of a child like your Athenais. Each disappointment deepens my melancholy; I compose elegies and dream of embroidering caps, choosing baby clothes, and hearing "Mamma" from my own child. My home feels desolate and I feel unnatural as a childless woman. I long to express this sorrow—my Moor even wept at my plight. Sometimes I feel driven to go on a pilgrimage for solace. Next winter I intend to consult a doctor.

A year has passed without a letter from you, which pains me. Louis visits often, but I miss your thoughts and feelings. Your seclusion in the country during a time when you could enjoy Paris and your husband's political success worries me; I hope you will come soon so I can know more of your life and family.

You complain of my silence, but my children keep me busy. I did not want to bring them to Paris while expecting another child. I plan to buy a house in Paris with money from Louis' uncle and my mother. My days revolve around caring for my children; I find joy in their sleep and in planning their futures. Motherhood requires self-denial and devotion; I cannot trust anyone else to care for them as I do.

I am overwhelmed with grief after losing Felipe. I stayed by his side until the end, and now I am haunted by memories. I regret the jealousy I imposed and live in a haze of sorrow, wondering if our passion was doomed to be barren. I invite you to visit; your presence would bring comfort.

Dear Renée,

By the time you read this, I will be near you. Louis must remain in Provence for the elections because of Liberal plots against him. I come not to comfort but to share my heart. Weep—tears bring joy when reunited. Each duty done brings you closer. In my arms you will rise, pure and noble, ready to do good in his name. I write amid preparations, often interrupted by the children, especially Armand, who longs to see you.

I have sold my house and properties, but I am neither mad nor ruined. After settling everything, I have about twelve hundred thousand francs left. I invested a million in the Three per Cents at fifty, yielding an income of sixty thousand francs—better than the thirty thousand my properties brought. Managing farms was overwhelming for a widow of twenty-seven; now I receive my income directly from the state, simpler and more secure. You may fear national bankruptcy, but I think it unlikely; even if it occurred, I would remain stable. The Comte de l'Estorade supports my decision.

The real reason for my actions is love. I am to be married soon in a secret ceremony near Paris. I love deeply and am loved in return. My beloved Gaston has courted me for nearly two years. He is twenty-three and had debts of thirty thousand francs, which I have paid. He is a poet struggling for recognition, and I admire his passion.

Despite our age difference and his financial struggles, I am ready to end our long restraint; this is not naive—I am confident in my feelings and his. I have never met a man with such noble feelings, and I am excited about our future.

Gaston is the illegitimate son of Lady Brandon, raised by his brother Louis, who has since disappeared. He has faced many hardships, believing genius would secure success, yet has only accumulated debt. I felt an immediate connection when we met, though he does not yet know of my love.

I am certain of my happiness in marriage, but I fear the potential for loss. I worry about aging and disappointing him; if our love were to fade, I would take the blame. Should that happen, I ask you to care for my children as my legacy.

I plan to keep our marriage private. I have bought a secluded property near Ville-d'Avray, designed for our solitude. The chalet is well-built and hidden from view, ensuring privacy.

In two days I will become Madame Gaston. I am filled with both joy and sorrow—my love for Gaston is immense, and I will miss you dearly. Please address future correspondence to me as Madame Gaston, Poste Restante, Versailles, to keep our lives a secret.

Farewell, my dear friend. The pain of losing you is as profound as my love for Gaston.

Dear Daniel,

I need you and Joseph Bridau to witness my wedding tomorrow. My future wife prefers seclusion, which explains our limited contact. After the wedding we'll be apart for a time, but I trust our friendship can survive. She has given up closeness with a childhood friend for me, and I must accept similar sacrifices.

Our love is deep and sincere, founded on mutual understanding. Yet I feel ashamed: she is wealthy and I am not. Your support has been vital during my struggles, and I feel humiliated by our situation. This shame is part of my love, and I accept it.

Still, my dreams are realized. She is noble, intelligent, and beautiful — the ideal woman. At twenty-seven she is a widow without children, energetic though occasionally melancholy. Our love is pure and has grown beyond mere physical desire; it rests on mutual respect. We have both known disappointments, and she regards her previous marriage as a mistake.

You, my friend, are a giant among men, and I hope you can appreciate the happiness that awaits me.

Mme. de L'Estorade to Mme. de Macumer

Louise, are you truly choosing solitude after your first marriage? It seems you're inviting unhappiness. Perhaps someone has changed your mind about remarrying, but have you forgotten your former criticisms of men?

I cannot fault you for seeking happiness, though I fear for you. Absolute happiness often brings disaster, and your joy may be too burdensome. Tell me more about this man you love.

I have not told Louis of your plans; he would ridicule them. You are brave to face winter alone, and I will support you discreetly. Your venture into this emotional wilderness is perilous, and I hope for your safe return.

Comtesse de L'Estorade to Mme. Marie Gaston (1835)

What has become of you, dear? After two years of silence I worry about Louise. Has love so absorbed you that it eclipses our friendship? I miss your letters and your presence.

As for us, I should answer your remarks about politics. We remain in the Audit Department for our children's sake. Louis has advanced under the new order, becoming a peer and a respected political figure. I declined an embassy to stay in Paris for the children's education.

I am a happy mother and take joy in our children. Armand thrives at school, and I am proud of him. Yet I worry about Nais and the uncertainties of marriage; the thought of her being given to someone unworthy terrifies me. Your silence has revived these fears. Please write soon; I miss you dearly.

After these two years I must share my own happiness, dear Renee. Our love has grown and brings daily joy. We may visit Paris, but for now our leisurely mornings and intimate days content us in seclusion.

Gaston is well-proportioned, naturally graceful, with dark hair and a fair complexion that lends him a tragic beauty. He has no vices, a steady temperament, and his smile delights me.

Our life is rich with simple pleasures. I take pride in our home, rise early to greet Gaston, and help him with his plays, which keeps our evenings lively. The servants live comfortably, and our household is harmonious.

Despite this happiness, I struggle with jealousy and the ache of having no children. As I near thirty I feel the pressure of time while Gaston remains youthful.

Do not let years pass without communication again. My happiness may seem monotonous, but I cherish our friendship and long to hear about your life and children.

With all my love,  
Your friend.

Dear Louise,

Your recent letter reveals a naive view of love that worries me. You ignore that true love often becomes the steady commitment of family life, requiring sacrifices from both partners. Marriage demands devotion and energy and frequently asks something of personal desires.

You chase adoration, a youthful dream; as a mature woman your focus should shift to the duties of wifehood and motherhood. I urge you to return to Paris; it is not misfortune you should fear, but the pursuit of happiness that might lead you astray.

Life’s simple pleasures—bread, air, silence—sustain us. Passionate love can be intoxicating yet ultimately exhausting. I chose a life of self-sacrifice, and despite its burdens I would choose it again. Your romantic notions sound selfish; please reconsider.

Life is about balance; excess brings suffering. You are ill because you cling to a passion marriage should temper. The true glory of home lies in calmness and shared experience, which you seem to undervalue. You risk degrading the sanctity of marriage by pursuing impulses without restraint.

If you could know the joys of family life, you might grasp its lasting happiness. I despair that my attempts to share my contentment with you have only exposed your selfishness. Your love for Gaston appears more about your needs than his true self.

Renee, calamity has struck! My heart is heavy. I discovered Gaston’s mare, Fedelta, caked with Paris mud, and suspected he had been there without telling me. Jealousy consumed me; I could scarcely keep my composure.

When Gaston found me distressed he carried me to the parlor; I shut myself away to weep. Later I confronted him about Paris. Though he professed his love, I remained unconvinced.

Our day was tense, and in a fit of folly I thought of selling Fedelta. When Gaston left for Paris I was tormented by anxiety and imagined every possible betrayal.

He returned late with a whip as a gift; I sensed it concealed something deeper. I later learned he bought it from Verdier, which only stoked my suspicions. I resolved to watch for proof of infidelity.

As days passed Gaston worked diligently on his plays but grew secretive. I followed him to the post office, where he received a letter from Nathan informing him that a play had been accepted. Still, I could not shake the feeling he was hiding something.

I kept silent but vigilant, fearing another woman. My heart was torn between love and suspicion; I felt trapped in a cycle of jealousy.

Gaston’s work prospered, but I noticed an empty drawer and suspected he needed money for a woman. My dread deepened: maybe I had a rival.

I confirmed my worst fear: Gaston was involved with a large Englishwoman who resembled the mother of his children. I watched from afar and my heart broke. He had been concealing this from me; I felt utterly betrayed.

Despite my hurt, my love for Gaston remained; he seemed to love me deeply. Yet I hesitated to confront him about the other woman, unable to voice my pain.

Dear Louise,

I write urgently to say I have shared your distressing letter with Louis. We acted to protect you, even involving the police discreetly.

Gaston is not the man you fear. His brother Louis died in India, leaving a widow and two children. Gaston has been secretly supporting them, which explains his trips to Paris. The woman you suspected is not a rival but his sister-in-law; his visits and gifts were acts of duty and affection for his brother’s family.

This revelation should comfort you. Consider embracing this family—Gaston’s actions spring from kindness and responsibility, not betrayal.

Plan a surprise for him and let this knowledge bring you peace.

Oh, dear friend, it may be too late for me. My actions have led me to despair. I am tormented and cannot bear the thought of losing Gaston. I feel myself fading and long for release from this pain. Come to me; I need your support as I face this darkness alone.

In a letter dated August 7 the Comtesse de L’Estorade explains she chose to stay with her dying friend Louise instead of traveling to Provence with the children. Louise, consumed by advanced consumption, knows her time is short. Though she keeps a cheerful front with her husband Gaston, she confides to the Comtesse about her imminent death.

At Ville d’Avray the Comtesse sees Louise’s health decline. Louise wants to hide her illness from Gaston to preserve dignity. She reflects that her passionate nature contributed to her decline and admits the futility of jealousy in marriage.

Louise shares her thoughts on love and marriage, acknowledging that true happiness cannot rest on passion alone. She feels fortunate to have loved deeply but accepts society’s expectations of women. As family visits, tensions from her second marriage dissolve, letting them unite in her final days.

Despite illness Louise retains charm, even joking about looking good in her coffin. The Comtesse notes how Louise’s spirit captivates those around her; in delirium she sings beautiful melodies that reduce her loved ones to tears.

On August 26 the Comtesse witnesses Louise’s final moments. After a brief rally, Louise dies peacefully, leaving a profound sense of loss. The Comtesse reflects on the void Louise’s death creates and on the deep ties formed through love and sorrow.
