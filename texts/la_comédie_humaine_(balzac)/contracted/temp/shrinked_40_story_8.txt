**Summary of "Paz" (La Fausse Maitresse) by Honoré de Balzac**

**Dedication**
Dedicated to Comtesse Clara Maffei.

**I**
In September 1835, Mademoiselle du Rouvre married Comte Adam Mitgislas Laginski, a Polish exile. The Marquis du Rouvre had squandered much of his fortune, but Clementine inherited wealth from her mother’s family. Although Adam faced prejudice in Paris, he rose in society after purchasing a house on rue de la Pepiniere.

By 1836, Adam was fashionable, and Clementine became a prominent figure in Parisian society, living in a luxurious home with modern features. After two years of marriage, Clementine grew curious about Captain Paz, who managed their affairs. Adam explained that Paz was a nobleman from a poor branch of his family and a close friend who fought for Poland.

Paz managed Adam's finances, ensuring their lavish lifestyle despite limited income. He was devoted to Adam, often sacrificing his own needs, and their friendship was built on mutual respect. Clementine expressed a desire to know Paz better, intrigued by his character. Adam recounted their bond formed through shared hardships during the Polish revolution, emphasizing that their connection transcended social hierarchies.

The narrative explores themes of friendship, loyalty, and social complexity in 19th-century Paris, revealing the intricacies of love amid class distinctions. Paz lived a secluded life, largely unnoticed by fashionable circles. Curiosity arose when Comtesse Laginska rode in the Bois de Boulogne with her husband and a stranger. Clementine insisted that Thaddeus Paz join them for dinner and later the opera, compelling him to dress for the occasion. At dinner, Thaddeus acted like a subordinate, frustrating Adam, who tried to encourage him to be more lively.

After dinner, Thaddeus agreed to go to the opera but remained detached, disappointing Clementine. Adam defended Thaddeus, suggesting he was merely trying to appear disagreeable. Later, Clementine noticed lights in Thaddeus's apartment, indicating he had not gone to bed.

Thaddeus was tormented by unrequited love for Clementine, which intensified upon her return from a trip. He felt joy and suffering, knowing he was essential to the household's stability, especially given Adam's financial irresponsibility. His love for Clementine was profound yet silent, as he struggled to maintain his friendship with Adam while harboring feelings for his wife.

Clementine, busy with social obligations, seemed to forget Thaddeus, who returned to his routine. One day, she spotted him in the Champs-Elysées and invited him to dinner. Thaddeus, anxious, accepted and attempted to engage more, sharing stories that captivated Clementine. The evening ended with a moment of intimacy, but he hesitated to confess his feelings.

As time passed, Clementine's social life consumed her, yet she occasionally thought of Thaddeus, who remained a steadfast presence. Their interactions sparked a deeper connection, leading her to invite him to dinner again. During this dinner, Thaddeus became more animated, sharing tales of his experiences, and Clementine began to see him in a new light.

Meanwhile, Thaddeus became infatuated with a circus performer named Malaga, fabricating a story about his love for her to distract from his feelings for Clementine. He supported Malaga financially but remained emotionally distant. When rumors circulated about their relationship, Malaga confronted him, leading to a fallout when she rejected his support.

Caught between his unexpressed love for Clementine and the consequences of his actions with Malaga, Thaddeus faced emotional turmoil. This culminated in a letter from Malaga, pleading for his return and expressing despair over their separation. Reflecting on the situation, Thaddeus found humor in the absurdity of his circumstances, realizing the depth of his feelings for both women and the challenges of his unfulfilled desires.

################################################
Clementine returned home, and Paz saw her looking more beautiful than ever. After dinner, a discussion arose between the count and his wife about Thaddeus, who had left a letter from Malaga with Adam. Adam lamented Thaddeus's downfall due to his infatuation with a circus performer, while Clementine expressed disdain for such women. Adam defended Thaddeus, suggesting he would sacrifice Malaga if necessary.

Paz, secretly supporting Malaga, was seen by Adam as a loyal friend. Clementine's contempt for Thaddeus grew as she noticed changes in him. Thaddeus, feeling her disdain, fell into despair but found fleeting joy in his admiration for her, leading to his decline.

While nursing Adam through a serious illness, Thaddeus suppressed his feelings for Clementine. Unaware of his sacrifices, she began to appreciate his management of her household but still believed he should abandon Malaga.

During carnival season, Thaddeus proposed a night out with Malaga, knowing Clementine would be there. Her disappointment deepened upon seeing them together, and she confronted him, demanding he end his relationship with Malaga if he wished to remain in her good graces. Thaddeus struggled between his love for Clementine and loyalty to Malaga.

As Adam recovered, Thaddeus received a letter appointing him captain of an expedition to Khiva and decided to leave Paris. Clementine realized her feelings for him too late, and his departure left her with longing and regret. Thaddeus wrote a heartfelt letter expressing his unrequited love, and three years later, Clementine still hoped to see him again, highlighting the complexities of love and societal expectations.
