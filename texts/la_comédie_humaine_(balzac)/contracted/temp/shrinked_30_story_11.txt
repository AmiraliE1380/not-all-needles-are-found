“Ah! madame,” said the doctor, “I have some appalling stories, but each has its proper hour in conversation—you know Chamfort’s joke to the Duc de Fronsac: ‘Between your sally and the present moment lie ten bottles of champagne.’”

“But it is two in the morning, and the story of Rosina has prepared us,” the hostess replied.

“Tell us, Monsieur Bianchon!” they all cried.

The doctor bowed and began.

About a hundred paces from Vendôme, on the bank of the Loir, stands an old brown house with very high roofs, solitary and utterly neglected—no tannery or tavern near it. A garden slopes to the river where once-clipped box shrubs now run wild; willows rooted in the stream form a half-hidden fence. Weeds have taken the bank; fruit trees, neglected for ten years, no longer bear, their suckers forming a thicket; espaliers look like a little copse. The once-graveled paths are overgrown—indeed there is no trace of a path. From the hilltop where the ruins of the Dukes of Vendôme cling, one can see that this spot must once have been the pride of a country gentleman devoted to horticulture. An arbor’s wreck and a partially standing table testify to that bygone care. The garden’s aspect suggests provincial peace—the epitaph of a worthy tradesman. To complete the mournful air, one wall bears a sundial with the motto “Ultimam cogita” (think on your last).

The house itself is dreadfully dilapidated: the roof leaking and warped, shutters always closed, swallows nesting on balconies, doors shut. Grasses green the flagstones of the steps; ironwork is rusty; moon, sun, winter, and snow have eaten into the wood, peeling paint and warping boards. The silence is broken only by birds, cats, polecats, rats, and mice; to the observer an invisible hand has inscribed one single word over it all: Mystery.

From the street you see a large gate with a round-arched top, full of children's holes; the gate, as the narrator later learned, had been blocked for ten years. Through breaches you view the courtyard: the same ruinous state as the garden side—paving-stones choked with weeds, cracked walls, blackened coping laced with pellitory, disjointed stone steps, a rotten bell-cord, broken gutter-spouts. What fire from heaven, what decree, has struck this dwelling? Has God been mocked, or was France betrayed? Reptiles crawl over it but give no answer. The empty, deserted house is a vast enigma.

It was formerly a small fiefed estate called La Grande Bretèche. During the doctor’s stay in Vendôme—where he had been left to attend a rich patient—this odd dwelling became one of his keenest pleasures. Better than a ruin, it carried a secret, an unrevealed thought: the slow destruction of a standing house seemed like an avenging hand at work. Many evenings he climbed the wild hedge surrounding the enclosure, braved scratches to enter the ownerless garden, and lingered for hours in its disorder. He would weave romances and indulge in melancholia there, relishing the unwritten poetry of ignorance; had he known the reason for the neglect, the charm would have been gone. To him the place represented various phases of human misfortune: sometimes a graveyard without the dead, sometimes a lepers’ home, sometimes the house of the Atridae—but above all provincial life, contemplative and like an hour-glass. He often wept there; he never laughed.

Occasionally he felt involuntary terrors at the dull hum of a passing wood-pigeon overhead. The damp earth required watching for lizards, vipers, and frogs; a cold would seem to settle on your shoulders like the Commendatore’s hand on Don Giovanni’s neck. One evening a weathercock turned in the wind, its creaking like a cry from the house just as the doctor was finishing a gloomy drama to account for this enormous air of woe. He returned to his inn in a pensive mood. After supper, the hostess came into his room with a mysterious air to announce, “Monsieur, here is Monsieur Regnault.”

“Who is Monsieur Regnault?” the doctor asked.

“What, sir, do you not know Monsieur Regnault?—Well, that’s odd,” she said and left.

A tall, slim man in black appeared: hat in hand, receding forehead, small pointed head, colorless face like dirty water—a sort of usher. His coat was old, worn at the seams, but he wore a diamond in his shirt frill and gold rings in his ears.

“Monsieur,” the doctor asked, “whom have I the honor of addressing?”

The man sat by the fire, rubbed his hands and answered, “Dear me, it is very cold. I am Monsieur Regnault.”

“My curiosity is piqued,” the doctor said.

“I am notary at Vendôme,” Regnault replied. He quickly informed the doctor that the latter’s habit of walking in La Grande Bretèche’s garden constituted a misdemeanor since the estate was enclosed property, and as executor under the will of the late Comtesse de Merret he begged the doctor to desist. He insisted he meant no offense and would be glad to allow free access if he were not bound by the testatrix’s instructions: he had never set foot in the house since the will was read. They had done nothing there but verify doors and windows to assess taxes; administering Madame de Merret’s estate had been the most important event of his life. His account was long and voluble, but the doctor pressed him for the reason that La Grande Bretèche was left to ruin.

Regnault delighted in relating his own history and the tale of Madame de Merret. He had been head-clerk in a Paris office that went bankrupt; unable to buy a practice in Paris in 1816, he came to Vendôme, bought his predecessor’s business, and married into local relations. Three months after being licensed, just before his marriage, he was sent for to the Chateau of Merret by Madame la Comtesse. The Count had gone to Paris and died two months previously after falling into dissipation. On the day he left, Madame la Comtesse had quitted La Grande Bretèche, having dismantled it; some said she had burned the furniture and hangings, perhaps in the Merret meadow. Regnault had been summoned: the Countess was very ill and seemed unlikely to survive the night, having received the Sacrament. The maid who met him at the door was now a servant in the inn.

When Regnault reached the chateau about eleven, he climbed the great staircase, crossed large, lofty, dark rooms—cold and damp—and reached the state bedroom where the Countess lay. Rumors had painted her as a coquette; reality confounded that image. The enormous, old-fashioned room, papered with brown and filled with dust, had only an old Argand lamp for light. The bed was antique, the tester hung with flowered chintz; on a small table lay an Imitation of Christ and the lamp; a deep armchair for the maid and two small chairs formed the whole furnishing; there was no fire. If you had seen that vast room you would have felt transported into a romance; it was icy, funereal.

Madame de Merret lay propped up, waxen and narrow-faced. Her hair, abundant but white as linen, was shown by a lace cap; her large black eyes, dimmed by fever, hardly moved beneath bony brows. Her forehead was clammy; her fleshless hands looked like bones covered with soft skin; veins and muscles showed—she must have once been striking, but disease had consumed her to a phantom. Her lips, pale-violet, scarcely moved when she spoke. Though Regnault’s work had exposed him to many deaths and last wishes, this solitary figure in her vast chateau was the most affecting: no sound, almost no breath, yet an immense presence. When her eyes moved, she tried to raise a hand, then, with difficulty, drew from under her pillow a sealed paper. “I place my will in your hands—Oh! God! Oh!” she uttered, then clutched a crucifix, kissed it, and died. Regnault remembered a joy stamped on her last glance, an expression that made him shudder, and he took away the will: she had appointed him her executor.

The will proved extraordinary. She left most of her property to the Vendôme hospital, with a few legacies. As for La Grande Bretèche, she ordered that, for fifty years from her death, it should remain precisely in the state it was at her death: nobody, whoever he might be, should enter the apartments, no repairs were to be made, and watchmen were even to be paid if necessary to secure this injunction. At the expiration of the term, if the will had been dutifully executed, the house would become the executor’s heirs’ property; otherwise it would revert to the heirs-at-law under terms set in a codicil not to be opened for fifty years. The will had not been contested. Regnault’s account thrilled him and he enjoyed the triumph of such a legal contrivance.

The doctor, vivid imaginings spinning into a gothic romance, pressed Regnault for the specifics of the Countess’s eccentric injunctions, but the notary affected comical reticence. Eventually he regaled the doctor further with town gossip—diffuse, contradictory opinions that nearly put the doctor to sleep. Regnault’s monotone and self-satisfaction were too much for curiosity, and the notary took his leave, teasingly suggesting that many would wish to live fifty years longer, and hinting cryptically about age.

After Regnault left, the doctor, still stirred, sat in his armchair when his landlady, Madame Lepas, entered. A buxom, florid Flemish woman with the keen scrutiny of an innkeeper, she had overheard the notary’s history. The doctor repeated the story of Madame de Merret, and Madame Lepas leaned close, an instinctive mix of constable, spy, and dealer in her look.

When asked about Monsieur de Merret, she described him as extremely tall, a good gentleman from Picardy, taciturn and hot-tempered but generous in paying his debts, and well-liked by the common folk despite his haughty manner. He and Madame de Merret had once been a handsome couple; she was pretty, sweet, and wealthy—about twenty thousand francs a year. They were a splendid match and their wedding had filled the town. Were they happy? “So-so,” Madame Lepas said—the common folk were not intimate with their life. Madame de Merret was kind and pleasant but must sometimes have endured her husband’s temper; being of noble birth, his ways passed as his place required.

“But there must have been some catastrophe for Monsieur and Madame de Merret to part so violently?” the doctor pressed.

“I did not say there was any catastrophe,” she replied. Yet she then confided what she knew. During the Emperor’s time, when Spaniards and prisoners of war were sent here, she was required to lodge a young Spaniard sent to Vendôme on parole. Despite the parole, he had to present himself daily to the sub-prefect. He was a Spanish grandee with a fanciful name—Bagos de Férédia or something alike—which she had entered in her books. For a Spaniard he was notably handsome though short—no more than five feet two or three—but well made, with small, fastidious hands; he kept his hands, she said, as carefully as a woman keeps brushes. He had thick black hair, a flame in his eye, a somewhat coppery complexion the hostess admired, and the finest linen she had ever seen—finer than anything even princesses had worn at her inn, despite having housed personages like General Bertrand, the Duc and Duchesse d’Abrantes, Monsieur Descazes, and the King of Spain. The Spaniard ate little but had such polite, amiable ways that he was impossible to dislike; he spoke scarcely at all, answering rarely if spoken to—a manner the hostess guessed might be a national affectation.

He was devout: he read his breviary like a priest and went to mass regularly. Most notably, he always took his station within two yards of Madame de Merret’s chapel. At his first attendance there no one suspected intent; he never raised his eyes above his book, poor young man. In the evenings he walked the hill among the ruins of the old castle—a pastime that must have reminded him of Spain, which, they said, is all hills.

Thus the scene closes on the picture of La Grande Bretèche, the Countess’s strange last will that sealed the house for half a century, and the shadow of a Spanish exile whose daily position near the Countess’s chapel and his solitary walks among the ruins suggest a link between foreign passion and domestic catastrophe—an implication the doctor, Regnault, and Madame Lepas allow the listener to supply, while the dilapidated house stands mute and enigmatic by the Loir.

One evening soon after his arrival he stayed out late. I grew uneasy when he did not return until midnight, but we were used to his whims; he had the key, and we did not wait. He lived in a house we owned on Rue des Casernes. One of our stable-boys thought he had seen the Spanish Grandee swimming in the river. When he did come back I warned him about the weeds; he seemed annoyed at being seen.

Eventually one morning we found he had not come back at all. Searching his things I discovered a note in his drawer, fifty doubloons—about five thousand francs—and a sealed box of diamonds worth ten thousand francs. The note said that if he did not return he left the money and diamonds to fund masses in gratitude for his escape.

My husband went searching and returned with the Spaniard’s clothes, found under a stone on a breakwater near la Grande Bretèche. He had come early, unseen. After reading the letter my husband burned the clothes and, in keeping with Count Férédia’s wishes, we announced that he had escaped. The sub-prefect sent the constabulary after him, but he was never caught. Lepas thought the Spaniard had drowned. I suspected otherwise, believing him implicated in Madame de Merret’s affair; Rosalie had told me the crucifix her mistress cherished and was buried with was ebony and silver, and early in his stay Monsieur Férédia had owned a similar crucifix that I never saw again. “Should I feel remorse about the Spaniard’s fifteen thousand francs?” my hostess asked. “Aren’t they truly mine?”

“Certainly. But have you ever questioned Rosalie?” I asked.

“Oh, I have, sir. But what can be done? That girl is like a wall. She knows something, but it’s impossible to make her talk.”

After a brief chat, my hostess left me with vague sinister thoughts, romantic curiosity, and a religious dread—like entering a dark church at night with a faint light beneath a high vault. La Grande Bretèche, with its overgrown grass, shuttered windows, rusty ironwork, and deserted rooms, suddenly loomed in my imagination. I felt compelled to explore the house and uncover the heart of this solemn story that had claimed three lives.

Rosalie became the most intriguing person in Vendôme. Observing her, I sensed some inner thought beneath her blooming health—a sorrow or hope; she seemed to keep a secret, like devout souls who pray excessively or a woman haunted by a past crime. Yet she was simple and clumsy, her vacant smile devoid of malice; one would deem her innocent seeing the large checked kerchief tucked into her lilac-and-white-striped gown. “No,” I resolved, “I will not leave Vendôme without knowing la Grande Bretèche’s full story. To achieve this, I will make advances toward Rosalie if necessary.”

“Rosalie!” I called one evening.

“Your servant, sir?”

“You’re not married?” She flinched.

“Oh! There’s no shortage of men if I ever fancy being miserable!” she laughed, then regained composure.

“Yes; you’re attractive enough to never lack suitors! But tell me, Rosalie, why did you become an inn-servant after leaving Madame de Merret? Didn’t she leave you a small annuity?”

“Oh yes, sir. But my position here is the best in all Vendôme.”

Her response seemed evasive. Rosalie appeared central to the affair; she seemed bound to its core. It was not a matter for ordinary courtship; this girl contained the last chapter of a romance. From that moment my attention fixed on her. Observing her further, I noticed admirable qualities; she was clean, neat, and undeniably attractive, soon embodying every charm. A fortnight after the notary’s visit, one early morning I said to Rosalie:

“Come, tell me all you know about Madame de Merret.”

“Oh!” she replied, “I will tell you, but keep it a secret.”

“Of course, my child; I will guard your secrets with a thief’s honor, the most loyal known.”

“If it’s all the same to you,” she said, “I’d prefer it be with your own.”

She adjusted her head-kerchief and settled in to tell the tale; a certain posture of confidence is necessary for storytelling. If I were to reproduce Rosalie’s diffuse eloquence a whole volume would scarcely suffice; as her account lies between the notary’s gossip and Madame Lepas’s version, I will summarize it concisely.

Madame de Merret’s bedroom at la Grande Bretèche was on the ground floor; a small cupboard in the wall, about four feet deep, served as her wardrobe. Three months before the evening in question Madame de Merret had been seriously ill; her husband had taken to sleeping on the first floor. One night he returned later than usual from the club, delayed by animated discussions and losses at billiards. He had previously relied on Rosalie to tell him if his wife was in bed; that evening he decided to check on her, perhaps to share his misfortunes or seek comfort. He entered thinking he heard a closet door shut; Madame de Merret stood alone by the fireplace, looking anxious.

“You’re very late,” she said huskily.

Monsieur de Merret remained silent as Rosalie entered, which startled him. He began pacing, arms crossed.

“Have you received bad news, or are you unwell?” his wife asked timidly while Rosalie helped her undress. He did not answer.

“You can go, Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said; “I can manage my curl-papers myself.” She wanted to be alone with her husband. Once Rosalie left—or lingered in the passage—Monsieur de Merret confronted his wife, saying coldly, “Madame, there’s someone in your cupboard!” She calmly replied, “No, monsieur.”

That “No” pierced him; he did not believe it, yet his wife appeared more saintly than ever. He moved to open the closet door, but Madame de Merret took his hand and said with strange emotion, “Remember, if you find no one there, everything must end between us.”

Her extraordinary dignity filled him with deep respect and inspired a resolve like a legend: “No, Josephine,” he said, “I won’t open it. Regardless of the outcome, we would be forever parted. I know your soul’s purity; you wouldn’t commit a deadly sin to save your life.” She looked haggard.

“Here is your crucifix,” he continued. “Swear to me before God that there’s no one in that closet; I will believe you—I will never open that door.”

Madame de Merret took the crucifix and swore, “I swear it.”

“Louder,” he insisted; “repeat: ‘I swear before God that there is nobody in that closet.’” She repeated the words without flinching.

“That will do,” said Monsieur de Merret coldly. After a moment, he remarked, observing the ebony-and-silver crucifix, “You have a fine piece of work there, which I’ve never seen before.”

“I found it at Duvivier’s; last year, when that troop of Spanish prisoners passed through Vendôme, he bought it from a Spanish monk.”

“Indeed,” he said, hanging the crucifix back on its nail, and he rang for Rosalie.

He waited for her to arrive. “Go to bed, all of you,” he instructed, beckoning Jean, his coachman and confidential servant, to come close. He whispered, “When they’re all asleep—mind, asleep—you understand?—come down and tell me.”

Monsieur de Merret kept his gaze on his wife while giving orders, returning to her at the fireside to discuss billiards and the club. When Rosalie returned she found them conversing amiably.

Not long before, Monsieur de Merret had had new ceilings installed in all the ground-floor reception rooms. Plaster was scarce in Vendôme; he had ordered a large quantity, knowing he could sell any excess. This circumstance inspired his plan.

“Gorenflot is here, sir,” Rosalie whispered.

“Tell him to come in,” he replied aloud.

Madame de Merret paled at the sight of the mason. “Gorenflot,” said her husband, “go fetch some bricks from the coach-house; bring enough to wall up the cupboard door; you can use the leftover plaster as cement.” Then, pulling Rosalie and the mason close, he whispered: “Listen, Gorenflot, you’ll sleep here tonight; tomorrow morning I’ll provide you a passport to take you abroad to a place I’ll specify. I’ll give you six thousand francs for your journey. You must live in that town for ten years; if you don’t like it, you may settle elsewhere, but it must be in the same country. Go through Paris and wait there until I join you. I’ll give you an agreement for six thousand francs more, to be paid upon your return, provided you fulfill the conditions of the bargain. In return, you must keep silent about what you do tonight. Rosalie, I’ll secure ten thousand francs for you, to be paid on your wedding day—contingent on your marrying Gorenflot; but to marry, you must remain silent. If not, no wedding gift!”

“Rosalie,” Madame de Merret said, “come and brush my hair.”

Her husband quietly paced the room, watching the door, the mason, and his wife, without showing suspicion. Gorenflot made some noise; Madame de Merret seized a moment when he was unloading bricks to say to Rosalie: “My dear child, I’ll give you a thousand francs a year if you tell Gorenflot to leave a crack at the bottom.” Then she added casually, “You’d better help him.”

Both husband and wife remained silent while Gorenflot walled up the door; the silence on Monsieur’s part was deliberate—he did not want to let his wife speak ambiguously. When the wall was half built, the cunning mason broke one of the two panes in the top of the door with his pick. Madame de Merret understood that Rosalie had spoken to him. They all then saw the face of a dark, gloomy-looking man with black hair and fiery eyes.

Before her husband turned around the poor woman nodded to the stranger the signal of “Hope.”

At four in the morning in September the work was completed. The mason was placed in charge of Jean, and Monsieur de Merret slept in his wife’s room.

The next morning he casually mentioned, “Oh, by the way, I must go to the Maire for the passport.” He donned his hat, took a few steps toward the door, paused, and grabbed the crucifix. His wife trembled with joy—she thought he would go to Duvivier’s.

As soon as he left Madame de Merret rang for Rosalie and cried in a terrible voice, “The pick! Bring the pick! We must work. I saw how Gorenflot did it yesterday; we have time to make a gap and build it up again.”

In an instant Rosalie brought a cleaver and began demolishing the wall with an intensity beyond words. She had removed a few bricks when, turning to deliver a stronger blow, she saw Monsieur de Merret behind her. She fainted.

“Lay madame on her bed,” he said coldly.

Foreseeing what would happen in his absence, he had set this trap for his wife; he had merely written to the Maire and summoned Duvivier. The jeweler arrived just as the disorder in the room was being repaired.

“Duvivier,” Monsieur de Merret asked, “didn’t you buy any crucifixes from the Spaniards who passed through town?”

“No, monsieur.”

“Very well; thank you,” he said, casting a fierce glare at his wife. “Jean,” he added to his valet, “you can serve my meals here in Madame de Merret’s room. She is ill, and I won’t leave her until she recovers.”

The cruel man remained in his wife’s room for twenty days. During the early days, when there was some noise in the closet and Josephine wanted to intercede for the dying man, he would cut her off, saying without allowing her to speak, “You swore on the Cross that there was no one there.”

After Bianchon finished this story, all the ladies rose from the table, shaken by the last words.
