The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hôtel de Ville ending at the Rue Martroi, was marked by a turnstile that remained until 1823. Less than six feet wide, it filled with gutter water and was perpetually muddy; summer sunlight touched it only briefly and a dampness lingered in the air.

Residents lit lamps at five and kept them burning through winter. A passerby approaching the Marais felt he moved through cellars, as on many Paris streets. The corner house at Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie showed its age; iron rings still hinted at chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat dampness, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door had a closed arch crowned by a keystone carved with a female head. Three high windows, protected by iron bars, admitted light to a small dwelling within. Curious passersby could glimpse two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier, preparing a stew of porters’ wives. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished yet kept clean and thrifty.

The old woman, pale and wrinkled, seemed as fixed as the walls. She sat by the hearth with a look of mischief and resignation as her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. The mother, with spectacles and deliberate fingers, occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with a downcast gaze.

Their modest home reflected the lives of working-class women in Paris. The girl, despite the surroundings, retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes suggesting a spirit not yet dimmed by hardship. The mother speculated about passersby, dreaming of a better future for her daughter, while the girl remained absorbed in her embroidery.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn demeanor intrigued her. She guessed he was a clerk from the Prefect’s office, and her excitement was evident. The girl, Caroline, stirred from indifference at her mother’s remarks, but the stranger had already gone.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, certain he would pass again. When he did, Caroline caught sight of him and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noticing suffering marked on his face. This was the first time a passerby had pierced her indifference; normally she was absorbed by work and the daily grind.

The man’s presence lingered in Caroline’s mind, standing out among the many faces she barely registered. For the first time she felt compassion for someone beyond her own struggles. As she resumed her embroidery, she found herself looking forward to seeing him again, intrigued by the possibility that this stranger might become a connection capable of altering the course of her life.

For over two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, not always passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same hour. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted air—lost in thought and scarcely noticing her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning Caroline’s lively presence caught him; he paused to watch her at the window. That pause began a series of furtive glances that suggested a growing understanding.

In the weeks that followed he began to pass twice daily, and their brief encounters became a routine both anticipated. Their quiet exchanges conveyed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he knew when she was anxious about work. Yet both remained cautious, fearing a closer bond might bring complications or heartbreak.

Madame Crochard, frustrated by her daughter’s indecision, grew displeased with the Gentleman, who had once drawn her benign attention. As winter approached, life’s hardships wore on Caroline, and the Gentleman observed how they affected her spirit. One night he overheard a conversation between mother and daughter that revealed severe financial strain. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May Caroline suggested a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more sombre than usual—unexpectedly accompanied them. On the carriage ride Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman space for a light-hearted conversation that revealed their growing fondness. The countryside’s beauty and the warmth of their exchange cheered him, and he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they traded personal stories: Caroline spoke of family sacrifices and her mother’s efforts; the Gentleman, later called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. The day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter and joy, a sharp contrast to earlier somber interactions.

As evening approached Caroline voiced the happiness she felt, prompting Roger to consider the possibility of joy beyond that day. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands—a discreet, significant gesture. Madame Crochard, though pretending not to notice, was aware. The moment marked an important step in their relationship, full of unspoken promises and hope for a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where new apartments suit honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived for over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, carefully furnished with fresh paint and elegant, English-influenced decor.

Her bedroom has gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable seating, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy atmosphere. As she readies for Roger's visit, her hairdresser flatters her about his admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline dresses in a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla and steps onto the balcony, heart racing as she watches for Roger. After fifteen minutes her impatience grows, but she brightens when she finally sees him approaching.

They embrace passionately, reveling in their reunion after three days apart. Roger then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline; she protests the idea of enjoying the theatre without him and voices deep affection. Despite this, she quickly focuses on the joy of being together. She expresses her wish to take the name Caroline de Bellefeuille, signaling commitment, and as they reminisce she sits on his lap and says she'll use her earnings to help the poor, emphasizing gratitude for their life together.

Her excitement grows as she plays a sonata, but Roger, mindful of obligations, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell; she watches him drive away, savoring the parting and longing for his return.

Five years after settling on Rue Taitbout, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille sits serenely with her four-year-old son Charles playing with a rocking horse. Now twenty-four, she radiates a beauty shaped by happiness and fulfillment. She has embraced motherhood, dedicating herself to nurturing her children and cultivating their appreciation for goodness and beauty. Despite simple pleasures, she remains uninterested in societal expectations, never craving luxury or status.

As she rocks her infant daughter Eugenie, Caroline reflects on the past, especially joyful visits to Bellefeuille with Roger. Their love has flourished in the simplicity of life together, free from social complications. Caroline wonders about their relationship, curious why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day for them, Caroline prepares dinner, eager for Roger's arrival. When he comes, he presents a document detailing Eugenie’s marriage portion, prompting a discussion about unequal financial provisions for their children. Roger explains he believes Charles, as a boy, should learn self-sufficiency, while Eugenie, as a girl, deserves a fortune.

Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a lantern show for Charles. As night deepens, Roger must leave; Caroline feels the familiar pang of separation. He reassures her he has three days off, but duty soon calls.

Shortly after this anniversary, Caroline hurries to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The widow’s acquaintances, not realizing Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest for last rites. Caroline arrives to a scene of confusion and deceit: the old women hope to benefit from Madame Crochard’s wealth. Despite their motives, Caroline’s love is evident as she tries to care for her mother.

Madame Crochard, in her final moments, expresses regret over their estrangement and reveals that the priest made her confess about Caroline’s benefactor. This disclosure hints at a complex web of relationships and secrets to unfold.

To clarify deeper connections, we turn back to events before Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805, a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, finds himself in a precarious position after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s residence. He is unexpectedly offered a chance to advance his career, thanks to his uncle’s influence.

Granville receives a letter from his father urging him to return home quickly; a potential marriage to Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems could secure his future. The letter outlines the financial benefits of the union, despite social stigma surrounding the Bontems family. Granville, torn between ambition and feeling, recalls his childhood affection for Angelique, whom he has not seen in years.

The next day he travels to Bayeux, where his father greets him and takes him to the Bontems’ home. The atmosphere is stifling, dominated by the widow’s strict religious practices. Granville senses dread as he contemplates the marriage, but his father’s insistence weighs heavily.

During a church service, Granville glimpses Angelique, whose beauty captivates him anew. Their brief interaction rekindles his feelings, though he is acutely aware of family constraints. As the service ends, Granville’s impatience leads him to approach Angelique, setting the stage for a reunion that could alter their lives.

The intertwining stories of Caroline and Granville reveal complexities of love, duty, and societal expectation, as both navigate desire against familial obligation and personal ambition.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her dreary drawing-room regularly. He watched Angelique prepare her trousseau; she never introduced religious topics, and when Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked practices she quietly returned it to its bag with a serene smile. She firmly believed in the Church, insisting a mother must have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, captivated by her conviction, was sometimes tempted to adopt her beliefs, though he mistook his desire for her as love. Angelique, balancing feeling and duty, left him uncertain about his own sentiments. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling religion in Angelique, thinking it would help her face life’s challenges.

At their marriage-contract signing Madame Bontems required Granville to promise respect for Angelique’s religious practices and freedom of conscience. Angelique’s innocent gaze convinced him; their spiritual guide, the Abbe Fontanon, approved. Soon after they married and moved to Paris, Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to take a large ground-floor apartment in the Marais, near a church and chapel, arguing it would benefit future children. Granville, who preferred a more fashionable district, acquiesced. He concentrated on his study and left decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their home. But Granville was dismayed by the apartment’s cold, solemn atmosphere, reflecting Angelique’s austere upbringing.

Despite efforts to understand her choices, he grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm at home, attributing the sober decor to her Bayeux upbringing. He tried to comfort Angelique, believing happiness would come from her love and gentleness rather than material comforts.

From April through winter their life seemed harmonious: Granville kept busy with work, and he remained unaware of strict religious observances Angelique enforced—Lenten meals and other austerities she passed off as carelessness. He accompanied her to church on Sundays but did not grasp the depth of her fervor until a disruptive event.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had earlier guided Angelique, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he saw as her moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax life. Angelique adopted stricter practices, which led to misunderstandings about social obligations. Granville grew frustrated by her frequent excuses to avoid social events, especially balls.

After a particularly tense evening he confronted Angelique about her demeanor and attire, urging her to embrace her social role. Arguments escalated; Angelique cited prohibitions from her director against attending balls. Granville insisted her priest overstepped his bounds, and their dispute turned theological, culminating in Angelique writing to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply sided with Granville, asserting a wife should accompany her husband even if it risked compromising her virtue. Rather than resolve matters, this deepened their divide; Angelique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Granville, frustrated by her strict observances, began asserting his own preferences—ordering meat daily despite her objections.

As the household atmosphere grew oppressive, Granville withdrew from social life, feeling weighed down by Angelique’s bigotry. The home became stifling and gloomy, affecting everyone, including the servants. He recognized their house had turned into a place devoid of joy, marked by rigid piety.

His discontent deepened as Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity. He felt trapped in a marriage where duty replaced affection; her devotion left little room for love. The children, raised under Angelique’s strict rules, suffered from the oppressive climate, prompting Granville to send his sons to school early and resolve to marry off his daughters as soon as possible.

Granville’s isolation grew as he struggled with his marriage. He sought solace in work, attempting to write a book on law, while Angelique tried to convert him back to her views, adding to his frustration. Their relationship deteriorated, leaving him increasingly alienated from his wife and their home, which had become a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

The history of the de Granville household between 1806 and 1825 was marked by an absence of notable events. Madame de Granville remained unchanged after losing her husband's affection, continuing to pay for Masses to discern her faults and restore their relationship. Despite her fervent prayers, Granville, now a prominent judge, took to living in the entresol of their home to avoid her. Each morning a housekeeper relayed messages between them; Granville consistently declined breakfast, citing important court business.

Madame de Granville, thirty-five and looking older, awaited her husband's return, eager to engage him with her narrow-minded remarks. When Granville was offered the presidency of a provincial court, he declined, raising suspicions among the Countess's friends and her confessor. They speculated his refusal stemmed from an illicit affair, which deeply troubled Madame de Granville, who believed her husband incapable of such sins.

During Lent in 1822 her health deteriorated from stress and low fever, worsened by Granville's indifference. Though she tried to be more affectionate, her bigotry often resurfaced and undermined her efforts. By late May, as she improved, she sought guidance from the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: Granville had been living in sin with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering their fortune.

In disbelief, Madame de Granville struggled with the revelation, torn between faith and betrayal. The Abbe urged her to forgive and wait for Granville's enlightenment, but she was consumed by a whirlwind of emotions. After a tumultuous day she left home determined to confront her husband.

Meanwhile, Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense moment: she voiced hatred for his wife, whom she blamed for their unhappiness. Roger reassured her, hoping for a future together, but their conversation was interrupted by Madame de Granville's sudden fainting. Upon regaining consciousness she confronted Granville, questioning his love and fidelity. Granville, however, accused her of sacrificing his happiness to piety, claiming she had failed to fulfil her role as a loving wife.

Their exchange exposed deep-seated issues. Granville argued that true love requires sacrifice and understanding; Madame de Granville clung to moral righteousness. His coldness and her desperate pleas highlighted the chasm between them. He explained that, while he had sought happiness elsewhere, she had neglected their relationship in favor of religious devotion.

As the conversation escalated, Madame de Granville's jealousy and anger flared, bringing a painful realization of irreconcilable differences. Granville's indifference and admission of loving another devastated her. Despite tears and pleas for forgiveness, he remained resolute, saying he could not rekindle a love that had faded after years of neglect.

The dialogue culminated in a stark acknowledgment of their failed marriage: Granville insisted love cannot be commanded or revived at will. Madame de Granville was left to grapple with the consequences of their choices and the uncertain future of their relationship.

Early in December 1833, a white-haired, worn man, aged by grief rather than years, walked the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused before a modest two-story house; a dim light flickered through attic windows, some patched with paper. As he watched, a young man emerged. Recognizing him, the young man approached hesitantly. “What, is it you, Monsieur le Président? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up, we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

The Comte de Granville replied, “But, my dear sir, I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me. A physician of your celebrity must know that at that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” said Horace Bianchon. “You are not in the habit of going about Paris on foot. When a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville answered.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” the doctor exclaimed. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” Granville said with melancholy indifference.

“Then do not stand about,” Bianchon urged, leading the Count toward the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe you are bent on robbing me of your last illness, dying by some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time past I have observed at a window on the third floor of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

Granville paused, as if in pain. “I take as great an interest in that garret as a citizen of Paris must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon said eagerly, “I can tell you—”

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant of that attic is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, I stopped to look solely for the pleasure of indulging in conjectures as numerous and as idiotic as those of idlers who see a building left half-finished. For nine years I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but to unreasoning animals, plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “Yes—my eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The other will, through her sister’s connections, make a good match. My sons have succeeded; the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orléans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares, their own anxieties. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, if one had tried to fill the void I have here,” he said, striking his breast, “that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” he added with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, and humane! If I were not myself a living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg of you to feel the same with regard to me. The events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and living as yours to such a pitch of indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering with aversion.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe to be frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me. Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty, and her love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed with every conceivable vice. This fellow is a gambler, and it is hard to say which he is more addicted to—wine or women; he has committed acts deserving punishment by law. To him, this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; and many times she has seen the wretch she adores rob her even of the money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold piece quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? But work is wearing her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children have not strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still. Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” Granville said.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a form of misery of which the joys to me would seem cheaply purchased with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon had assumed he was feeling for. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV. have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? And is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the name Granville shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm tightly.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, his voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon replied, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

They reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussée-d’Antin. A night scavenger, with a shriveled face, stood by the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger answered.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You’re the man for me.” Granville handed him a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencil of Charlet and Callot, the brush of Teniers and Rembrandt to capture the night scene accurately.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” Granville said, pointing out the scavenger’s grotesque face to Bianchon, who stood stupefied. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

Granville left Bianchon standing like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, where a carriage waited at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered the room. “What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” Granville said. “Sit down,” he gestured to a chair. “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand imperiously.

He paced in silence; his son remained quiet.

“My son,” he began in a mild, fatherly voice, causing the young lawyer to start, “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugène.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum; use it as you see fit in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, now or later. Eugène, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall tomorrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not a part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he continued, involuntarily shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important of all which society requires of us. Remember to study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law. But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”
