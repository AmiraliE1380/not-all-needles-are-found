The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, once a dark, winding lane near the Hotel de Ville, ended at the Rue Martroi and was marked by a turnstile that stood until 1823. Less than six feet wide at its broadest, it often pooled gutter water and was perpetually muddy; in summer sunlight briefly reached it, but dampness lingered.

Residents lit lamps at five in June and kept them burning through winter. Approaching the Marais felt like walking through cellars, as many old Paris streets were similarly gloomy. The corner house at the junction of Rue du Tourniquet and Rue de la Tixeranderie showed its age: iron rings remained from chains once used for public safety.

Built to combat humidity, the house had cellars raised above ground and an entrance reached by three steps. The door bore a closed arch with a keystone carved as a female head. Three high windows, protected by iron bars, lit a small dwelling within. Observers could see two beds draped in green serge and an old woman tending a brazier, preparing a stew typical of porters’ wives. The kitchen-dining room was sparsely furnished yet kept with evident cleanliness and thrift.

The old woman’s pale, wrinkled face seemed as much part of the house as its walls. She sat by the fireplace with an expression of mischief mixed with resignation, while her daughter worked at an embroidery frame. With slow fingers and antiquated spectacles, the mother occasionally glanced out the window; the daughter bent over her work with focused modesty.

Their modest home reflected the lives of working-class Parisian women. Despite surroundings of poverty, the girl retained youthful charm—rosy cheeks and bright eyes suggesting a spirit not wholly dimmed by hardship. The old woman speculated about passersby and hoped for a better future for her daughter; the girl, absorbed in embroidery, usually kept her gaze down.

One August day in 1815 the mother noticed a new face among the regular passersby: a tall, thin man in black whose solemn bearing intrigued her. She fancied he was a clerk from the Prefet’s office, and her excitement was apparent. Caroline, the girl, roused from indifference by her mother’s remarks, glimpsed the stranger, but he had already passed.

The old woman eagerly awaited his return, convinced he would come by again. When he did, Caroline caught another glimpse and felt an unfamiliar sympathy, noting the traces of suffering on his face. This was the first time a passerby had stirred such feeling in her; she usually regarded passersby with indifference, focused only on her work and daily routine.

The man’s presence left a lasting impression on Caroline, differing sharply from other sight-seers. For the first time she felt compassion for someone beyond her own troubles. As she resumed her embroidery, she found herself looking forward to another sight of him, intrigued by the possibility that this new acquaintance might alter the course of her life.

For over two months the “Black Gentleman” behaved erratically, not always passing the Rue du Tourniquet at the same time. Madame Crochard noticed his distracted manner—often lost in thought and seldom acknowledging her or her daughter, Caroline. One mid-September morning, Caroline’s lively presence caught his eye; he watched the animated scene at the window, and a silent connection began, made of fleeting glances that suggested a deeper understanding.

Weeks passed and the Gentleman began to pass twice daily; their brief encounters became a routine they both anticipated. Their silent exchanges conveyed mutual concern: Caroline sensed when he was troubled, and he could tell when she was anxious about her work. Yet both remained cautious, fearing that greater intimacy might bring complications or heartache.

Madame Crochard, irritated by her daughter’s indecision, turned her displeasure toward the Gentleman, who had once been the object of her mild approval. As winter neared, the hardships of life weighed on Caroline, and the Gentleman noticed how it wore on her spirit. One night he overheard a mother-and-daughter conversation that made their financial struggles plain. Touched, he secretly left money for them; Caroline found it the next day, and a moment of silent gratitude passed between them.

In early May, Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—more somber than usual—unexpectedly joined them. During the journey Madame Crochard pretended to sleep, giving Caroline and the Gentleman a chance for light-hearted talk that revealed their growing affection. The countryside’s beauty and the warmth of their exchange lifted his spirits; he began to see Caroline differently.

Their bond deepened as they shared personal stories. Caroline spoke of her family’s hardships and her mother’s sacrifices. The Gentleman, now called Roger, was drawn to her honesty and resilience. Their day in Montmorency brimmed with laughter and joy, a sharp contrast to their previously somber meetings.

As evening approached, Caroline voiced her newfound happiness, and Roger reflected on the possibility of joy beyond that day. Their shared understanding hinted at a deeper bond. Preparing to leave, they playfully held hands, solidifying the connection. Madame Crochard pretended not to notice, but the gesture marked a meaningful step in their relationship—an unspoken promise and the hope of a brighter future together.

The adventures of Roger and Caroline continue in modern Paris, where newly built apartments seem ideal for honeymooners. In a pristine building on Rue Taitbout, Caroline has lived over a month in a charming second-floor apartment, furnished with care and style. The rooms, freshly painted and elegantly appointed, reflect current trends influenced by English design.

Caroline's bedroom has gray hangings trimmed with green silk, comfortable chairs, and a writing table for love letters. A bronze clock showing Love crowning Psyche and a Gothic-patterned carpet complete the cozy setting. As she prepares for Roger's visit, her hairdresser flatters her, reminding her of Roger's admiration.

On a chilly September day in 1816, Caroline wears a green grenadine gown trimmed with chinchilla. Eagerly, she steps onto the balcony to watch for Roger; her heart races as she anticipates his arrival. After a quarter-hour of waiting, impatience replaces excitement, but she brightens when she finally sees him approaching.

Roger arrives, and they embrace passionately, rejoicing in their reunion after three long days apart. He then tells her he must attend a meeting with the Attorney-General, which disappoints Caroline. She playfully objects to the idea of enjoying the theatre without him, declaring her deep affection.

Despite the setback, Caroline quickly turns back to the joy of being with Roger. They share tender moments, and she expresses her wish to take his name—Caroline de Bellefeuille—as a sign of commitment. As they reminisce, Caroline perches on Roger's lap and talks about using her earnings to help the poor, underscoring her gratitude for their life together.

Her excitement grows while she plays a sonata on the piano, but Roger, mindful of his obligations, prepares to leave. They exchange a playful farewell, and Caroline watches him drive away, savoring the moment and longing for his return.

Five years after Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille settled in her home on Rue Taitbout, she sits serenely with her four-year-old son, Charles, who energetically plays with a rocking horse. Caroline, now twenty-four, radiates beauty shaped by happiness and fulfillment. She has embraced motherhood, focusing on nurturing her children and cultivating their appreciation for goodness and beauty. Despite these humble pleasures, she remains blissfully indifferent to societal expectations, having never desired luxury or status.

As she rocks her infant daughter, Eugenie, Caroline reflects on the joyful times with Roger during visits to Bellefeuille. Their love has flourished in the simplicity of their shared life, free from the complications of social convention. Caroline wonders quietly why Roger seeks happiness in secrecy, curious about the nature of their relationship.

On May 6, 1822, the anniversary of a pivotal day in their lives, Caroline prepares for dinner, eagerly anticipating Roger's arrival. He brings a document outlining Eugenie’s marriage portion, prompting a discussion about different financial provisions for their children. Roger explains he believes Charles, as a boy, should learn self-sufficiency, while Eugenie, as a girl, deserves a fortune.

Their evening is warm and lively, ending with a magical lantern show for Charles. But as night deepens, Roger must leave, and Caroline feels the bittersweet pang of separation. He reassures her he has three days off, yet the weight of duty looms.

Shortly after this anniversary, Caroline hurries to her mother, Madame Crochard, who is gravely ill. The old widow’s acquaintances, unaware Caroline is her daughter, have summoned a priest to offer last rites. Caroline arrives to a scene of confusion and deceit: the old women hoping to benefit from Madame Crochard’s wealth. Despite their intentions, Caroline’s love for her mother shows as she tries to care for her.

Madame Crochard, in her final moments, expresses regret over estrangement from Caroline and reveals that the priest has made her confess about Caroline’s benefactor. This revelation hints at a complex web of relationships and secrets to follow.

To understand these connections, we look back to events leading up to Madame Crochard’s death. In November 1805, a young barrister, Monsieur de Granville, finds himself in a precarious position after leaving a gathering at the High Chancellor’s residence and is offered a chance to advance his career through his uncle’s influence.

Granville receives a letter from his father urging a swift return home: a possible marriage to Mademoiselle Angelique Bontems could secure his future. The letter details the financial benefits of the union, despite the social stigma attached to the Bontems family. Torn between ambition and feeling, Granville recalls childhood affection for Angelique, whom he has not seen in years.

The next day he travels to Bayeux, greeted by his father and taken to the Bontems’ home, where the atmosphere is stifling under the widow’s strict religious observances. Granville dreads the match, but his father’s insistence weighs heavily.

During a church service, Granville glimpses Angelique, whose beauty captivates him anew. Their brief interaction rekindles his feelings, though he is acutely aware of family constraints. As the service ends, his impatience leads him to approach Angelique, setting the stage for a reunion that could alter their lives.

The intertwining stories of Caroline and Granville reveal the complexities of love, duty, and social expectation, as both characters navigate personal desires against familial obligations and ambitions.

For about two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville’s engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he visited her regularly in her dreary drawing-room. During these visits he observed Angelique marking her trousseau; she never initiated discussions about religion. When Granville playfully handled her rosary or mocked religious practices, she would quietly return it to its bag with a serene smile. She firmly believed in the Church’s teachings, insisting a mother should have a religion and questioning how one could judge between disbelievers and God.

Granville, captivated by her conviction, sometimes felt tempted to adopt her beliefs, mistakenly equating his desire for her with love. Angelique was content to reconcile feeling and duty, leaving Granville unsure about his own emotions. He was grateful to Madame Bontems for instilling religious principles in Angelique, which he thought would help her face life’s challenges.

On the day of their marriage contract, Madame Bontems required Granville to promise to respect Angelique’s religious practices and allow her freedom of conscience. Angelique’s innocent gaze convinced him to agree; the Abbe Fontanon, who guided their consciences, smiled approvingly. Shortly after their wedding the couple moved to Paris, where Granville became public prosecutor.

Angelique persuaded him to choose a large ground-floor apartment in the Marais, close to a church and chapel, arguing it would benefit their future children. Granville, who had preferred a more fashionable area, yielded. He set up his study and left the home decoration to Angelique, who delighted in furnishing their new home. But Granville was dismayed by the cold, solemn atmosphere she created, which reflected her austere upbringing.

Despite efforts to understand her choices, Granville grew increasingly frustrated by the lack of warmth and charm. He attributed the decor to Angelique’s Bayeux background, where taste seemed less refined, and tried to comfort her by insisting happiness came from her love and gentleness rather than material things.

From April through winter the couple appeared harmonious, Granville kept busy with work, and he remained largely unaware of Angelique’s strict religious observances—Lenten meals she disguised as carelessness. He accompanied her to church on Sundays but did not grasp her fervor until a significant event disrupted their lives.

In November 1808 the Canon of Bayeux, who had guided Angelique, arrived in Paris alarmed by what he saw as her moral decline and sought to reclaim her from a lax lifestyle. Angelique adopted stricter practices, which caused misunderstandings, particularly over social obligations. Granville grew irritated by her frequent excuses to avoid events, especially balls.

After a tense evening he confronted her about her demeanor and attire, urging her to embrace her social role. Arguments escalated: Angelique cited her director’s prohibitions against attending balls; Granville insisted her priest was overstepping. The dispute became theological and culminated in Angelique writing to the Pope for guidance.

The Pope’s reply affirmed Granville’s position, saying a wife should accompany her husband even if it meant compromising her virtue. Rather than resolve matters, this deepened the divide; Angelique and her director dismissed the Pope’s advice. Frustrated by her strict observances, Granville began to assert his preferences more forcefully, ordering meat daily despite her objections.

As the home atmosphere grew oppressive, Granville withdrew from social life, weighed down by Angelique’s bigotry. The household became stifling and gloom-filled, affecting everyone including the servants. He recognized that their home had been transformed into a place devoid of joy, marked by rigid piety.

Granville’s discontent deepened as Angelique’s beauty was overshadowed by austerity. He felt trapped in a marriage where duty had replaced love, and her devotion left little room for affection. Their children, raised under her strict rules, suffered from the oppressive climate; Granville sent his sons to school early and resolved to marry off his daughters as soon as they came of age.

His isolation increased as he struggled with his marriage’s reality. He sought solace in work, attempting to write a book on law, but Angelique’s attempts to reconvert him only added to his frustration. The relationship deteriorated: Granville felt increasingly alienated from his wife and their home, which had become a place of coldness and rigidity rather than warmth and companionship.

The history of the de Granville household between 1806 and 1825 was marked by few events. Madame de Granville remained unchanged after losing her husband's affection, continuing to pay for Masses hoping to understand her faults and restore their relationship. Despite her fervent prayers, Granville, now a prominent judge, had taken to living in the entresol of their home to avoid her. Each morning a housekeeper relayed messages between them, with Granville consistently declining breakfast invitations, citing important court business.

Madame de Granville, now thirty-five and looking older, awaited her husband's return, eager to engage him with her narrow-minded remarks. When Granville was offered the presidency of a provincial court, he declined, prompting the Countess's friends and confessor to suspect an illicit affair. They speculated his refusal stemmed from a liaison, which deeply troubled Madame de Granville, who believed her husband incapable of such sins.

During Lent in 1822 her health deteriorated from stress and low fever, exacerbated by Granville's indifference. Despite attempts to be more affectionate, her bigotry often resurfaced, undoing her efforts. By late May, as she recovered, she reflected on her past and sought guidance from the Abbe Fontanon, who delivered devastating news: Granville had been living in sin with a concubine for seven years, fathering two children and squandering their fortune.

In disbelief, Madame de Granville struggled with the revelation, torn between faith and betrayal. The Abbe urged her to forgive and wait for Granville's enlightenment, but she was consumed by a whirlwind of emotions. After a tumultuous day, she left home, determined to confront her husband.

Meanwhile, Roger and Mademoiselle de Bellefeuille were in a tense moment: she expressed hatred for his wife, whom she blamed for their unhappiness. Roger reassured her, hoping for a future together, but their conversation was interrupted by Madame de Granville's sudden fainting. Upon regaining consciousness she confronted Granville, questioning his love and fidelity. Granville accused her of sacrificing his happiness to her piety, claiming she had failed to fulfill her role as a loving wife.

Their exchange revealed deep issues in their marriage. Granville insisted true love requires sacrifice and understanding, while Madame de Granville clung to moral righteousness. His coldness and her desperate pleas for love highlighted the chasm between them. He explained that while he had sought happiness elsewhere, she had neglected their relationship for religious devotion.

As the conversation escalated, Madame de Granville's jealousy and anger flared, producing a painful recognition of their irreconcilable differences. Granville's indifference to her suffering and his acknowledgment of love for another woman left her devastated. Despite tears and pleas for forgiveness, Granville remained resolute, saying he could not rekindle a love that had faded through years of neglect.

The dialogue culminated in a stark acknowledgment of their failed marriage: love could not be commanded or revived at will. Madame de Granville's despair deepened as she faced the reality of her husband's emotional detachment, left to grapple with the consequences of their choices and the future of their relationship.

Early in December 1833, a man with white hair and worn features, aged by grief rather than years, walked along the Rue Gaillon at midnight. He paused to look up at a modest two-story house, where a dim light flickered through the attic windows, some patched with paper. As he watched, a young man emerged from the house. Recognizing him, the young man approached hesitantly. “What, is it you, Monsieur le President? Alone at this hour, so far from the Rue Saint-Lazare. Allow me to give you my arm. The pavement is so greasy this morning that if we do not hold each other up, we shall find it hard to escape a tumble.”

The Comte de Granville replied, “But, my dear sir, I am no more than fifty-five, unfortunately for me. A physician of your celebrity must know that at that age a man is still hale and strong.”

“Then you are in waiting on a lady, I suppose,” said Horace Bianchon. “You are not in the habit of going about Paris on foot. When a man keeps such fine horses—”

“Still, when I am not visiting in the evening, I commonly return from the Courts or the club on foot,” Granville responded.

“And with large sums of money about you, perhaps!” exclaimed the doctor. “It is a positive invitation to the assassin’s knife.”

“I am not afraid of that,” Granville answered with melancholy indifference.

“Then do not stand about,” Bianchon urged, leading the Count towards the boulevard. “A little more and I shall believe that you are bent on robbing me of your last illness, dying by some other hand than mine.”

“You caught me playing the spy,” Granville admitted. “For some time past, I have observed at a window on the third floor of your house the shadow of a person who seems to work with heroic constancy.”

Granville paused, as if in pain. “I take as great an interest in that garret as a citizen of Paris must feel in the finishing of the Palais Royal.”

“Well,” Bianchon said eagerly, “I can tell you—”

“Tell me nothing,” Granville interrupted. “I would not give a centime to know whether the shadow that moves across that shabby blind is that of a man or a woman, nor whether the inhabitant of that attic is happy or miserable. Though I was surprised to see no one at work there this evening, I stopped to look solely for the pleasure of indulging in conjectures as numerous and as idiotic as those of idlers who see a building left half-finished. For nine years, I have ceased to wonder that old men amuse themselves with growing flowers and planting trees; the events of life have taught them disbelief in all human affection. I grew old within a few days. I will no longer attach myself to any creature but to unreasoning animals, or plants, or superficial things. I think more of Taglioni’s grace than of all human feeling. I abhor life and the world in which I live alone. Nothing, nothing can move or interest me.”

“But you have children?” Bianchon asked.

“My children!” Granville repeated bitterly. “Yes—my eldest daughter is the Comtesse de Vandenesse. The other will, through her sister’s connections, make a good match. My sons have succeeded; the Viscount was public prosecutor at Limoges and is now President of the Court at Orleans; the younger is public prosecutor in Paris. My children have their own cares, their own anxieties. If one of those hearts had been devoted to me, if one had tried to fill the void I have here,” he said, striking his breast, “that one would have failed in life, sacrificing it to me. And why should he? To bring sunshine into my few remaining years—and would he have succeeded? Might I not have accepted such generosity as a debt? But, doctor,” he added with deep irony, “it is not for nothing that we teach them arithmetic and how to count. At this moment perhaps they are waiting for my money.”

“O Monsieur le Comte, how could such an idea enter your head—you who are kind, friendly, and humane! If I were not myself a living proof of the benevolence you exercise so liberally—”

“To please myself,” Granville replied. “I pay for a sensation, as I would pay a pile of gold to recover the most childish illusion that would make my heart glow. I help my fellow-creatures for my own sake, just as I gamble; I look for gratitude from none. I should see you die without blinking; and I beg of you to feel the same with regard to me. The events of life have swept over my heart like the lavas of Vesuvius over Herculaneum. The town is there—dead.”

“Those who have brought a soul as warm and as living as yours to such a pitch of indifference are indeed guilty!” Bianchon exclaimed.

“Say no more,” Granville said, shuddering with aversion.

“You have a malady which you ought to allow me to treat,” Bianchon insisted.

“What, do you know of a cure for death?” Granville cried irritably.

“I undertake, Monsieur le Comte, to revive the heart you believe to be frozen.”

“Are you a match for Talma, then?” Granville asked satirically.

“No, Monsieur le Comte. But Nature is as far above Talma as Talma is superior to me. Listen: the garret you are interested in is inhabited by a woman of about thirty, and her love is carried to fanaticism. The object of her adoration is a young man of pleasing appearance but endowed with every conceivable vice. This fellow is a gambler, and it is hard to say which he is more addicted to—wine or women; he has committed acts deserving punishment by law. To him, this unhappy woman sacrificed a life of ease, a man who worshiped her, and the father of her children.”

“What is wrong, Monsieur le Comte?” Granville asked.

“Nothing. Go on,” he replied.

“She has allowed him to squander a perfect fortune; she would give him the world if she had it; she works night and day; and many times she has seen the wretch she adores rob her even of the money saved for her children’s clothes and food. Only three days ago she sold her hair, the finest I ever saw; he came in, and she could not hide the gold piece quickly enough. For a smile, for a kiss, she gave up the price of a fortnight’s life and peace. Is it not dreadful, and yet sublime? But work is wearing her cheeks hollow. Her children’s crying has broken her heart; she is ill, and at this moment on her wretched bed. This evening they had nothing to eat; the children have not strength to cry; they were silent when I went up.”

Bianchon stood still. Granville, despite himself, put his hand into his waistcoat pocket.

“I can guess, my young friend, how it is that she is yet alive if you attend her,” Granville said.

“O poor soul!” Bianchon cried. “Who could refuse to help her? I only wish I were richer, for I hope to cure her of her passion.”

“But how can you expect me to pity a form of misery of which the joys to me would seem cheaply purchased with my whole fortune!” Granville exclaimed, withdrawing his hand empty of the notes Bianchon had assumed he was feeling for. “That woman feels; she is alive! Would not Louis XV. have given his kingdom to rise from the grave and have three days of youth and life? And is not that the history of thousands of dead men, thousands of sick men, thousands of old men?”

“Poor Caroline!” Bianchon lamented.

At the mention of the name, Granville shuddered and grasped the doctor’s arm tightly.

“Her name is Caroline Crochard?” he asked, his voice breaking.

“Then you know her?” Bianchon replied, astonished.

“And the wretch’s name is Solvet. Ay, you have kept your word!” Granville exclaimed. “You have roused my heart to the most terrible pain it can suffer until it is dust. That emotion, too, is a gift from hell, and I always know how to pay those debts.”

By this time, they had reached the corner of the Rue de la Chaussee d’Antin. A night scavenger, with a shriveled face, stood by the curb.

“Do you ever pick up a thousand-franc note?” Granville asked.

“Now and then, master,” the scavenger replied.

“And you restore them?”

“It depends on the reward offered.”

“You're the man for me,” Granville said, handing him a thousand-franc note. “Take this, but remember, I give it to you on condition of your spending it at the wineshop, getting drunk, fighting, beating your wife, blacking your friends’ eyes. That will give work to the watch, the surgeon, the druggist—perhaps to the police, the public prosecutor, the judge, and the prison warders. Do not try to do anything else, or the devil will be revenged on you sooner or later.”

A draughtsman would need the pencil of Charlet and Callot, the brush of Teniers and Rembrandt to capture the night scene accurately.

“Now I have squared accounts with hell and had some pleasure for my money,” Granville said, pointing out the scavenger’s grotesque face to Bianchon, who stood stupefied. “As for Caroline Crochard—she may die of hunger and thirst, hearing the heartrending cries of her starving children, convinced of the baseness of the man she loves. I will not give a sou to rescue her; and because you have helped her, I will see you no more—”

Granville left Bianchon standing like a statue and walked briskly to the Rue Saint-Lazare, where he found a carriage waiting at his door.

“Monsieur, your son, the attorney-general, came about an hour ago,” the servant said. “He is waiting for you in your bedroom.”

Granville dismissed the servant and entered the room. “What motive can be strong enough to require you to infringe the order I have given my children never to come to me unless I send for them?” he asked his son.

“Father,” the younger man replied tremulously, “I hope you will forgive me when you have heard me.”

“Your reply is proper,” Granville said. “Sit down,” he gestured to a chair. “But whether I walk up and down or take a seat, speak without heeding me.”

“Father,” the son continued, “this afternoon, at four o’clock, a very young man who was arrested in the house of a friend of mine, whom he had robbed, appealed to you. He says he is your son.”

“His name?” Granville asked hoarsely.

“Charles Crochard.”

“That will do,” Granville said, waving his hand imperiously.

He paced the room in silence, and his son remained quiet.

“My son,” he began, his voice mild and fatherly, causing the young lawyer to start. “Charles Crochard spoke the truth. I am glad you came to me tonight, my good Eugene.” He handed him a bundle of banknotes. “Here is a considerable sum of money; you can make any use of it in this matter. I trust you implicitly and approve whatever arrangements you may make, either now or in the future. Eugene, my dear son, kiss me. We part perhaps for the last time. I shall tomorrow crave my dismissal from the King, and I am going to Italy.

“Though a father owes no account of his life to his children, he is bound to bequeath to them the experience Fate sells him dearly—is it not a part of their inheritance? When you marry,” he continued, involuntarily shuddering, “do not undertake it lightly; that act is the most important of all which society requires of us. Remember to study the character of the woman who is to be your partner; consult me too, I will judge of her myself. A lack of union between husband and wife, from whatever cause, leads to terrible misfortune; sooner or later we are always punished for contravening the social law. But I will write to you on this subject from Florence. A father who presides over a supreme court of justice must not have to blush in the presence of his son. Good-bye.”
