The Rue du Tourniquet-Saint-Jean, a narrow, often flooded street near the Hôtel de Ville that once ended at the Rue Martroi with a turnstile until 1823, resembled many old Paris lanes where historical oddities lingered. A corner house bore iron rings—remnants of chains used for public safety—and had elevated cellars with high barred windows.

Inside, an old woman kept a fire to cook stew in a clean, sparsely furnished room. Her pale face betrayed a life of poverty, while her daughter Caroline worked diligently at an embroidery frame. They lived modestly by lamplight, with a few frail window plants. Passersby wondered how Caroline kept her color in such dim surroundings. The mother, hopeful for her daughter’s future, watched for suitors. One day she noticed a regularly passing man in a brown coat. Encouraged, Caroline became curious about him, and a new dynamic began.

For more than two months the "Black Gentleman" avoided the Rue du Tourniquet, puzzling Madame Crochard. One September day, Caroline’s vivacity caught his eye; a meaningful exchange of glances established a silent connection. He began to pass by regularly, and mutual interest grew through small acts of attention.

As winter went on, their daily sightings became a comfort. Caroline sensed when the stranger was troubled; he noticed her struggles. They remained cautious and spoke little. Madame Crochard, frustrated with her daughter's hesitance and bitter as winter strained their finances, fumed quietly. One night the Gentleman overheard their dire situation and secretly left money for Caroline. She showed gratitude with a gesture he pretended not to notice.

In May, Caroline proposed a trip to Montmorency, and the Gentleman—now identified as Roger—unexpectedly joined them. He was captivated by her beauty and innocence; their conversation flowed easily amid the picturesque landscape. By the time they reached the village of Saint-Leu, Caroline affectionately called him "Monsieur Roger."

Madame Crochard, first skeptical, joined their enjoyment though somewhat oblivious to the romance. After a pleasant meal they went to a village ball where Caroline and Roger danced with hands intertwined. As the day ended, Roger showed a hint of melancholy; Caroline asked why happiness should be confined to the outing. In a tender moment he kissed her hand, cementing their bond.

The tale follows Caroline and the Black Gentleman as their silent bond grows into a deeper connection, offering hope despite uncertain prospects.

The narrative shifts to more modern Paris, where Caroline and Roger live in a newly furnished apartment on Rue Taitbout. As September 1816 begins, Caroline prepares for Roger’s visit in a green dress. They embrace passionately on his arrival, but Roger must attend a meeting, disappointing her. He suggests she and her mother go to the theater, promising to join if possible.

Five years later, Mademoiselle Caroline de Bellefeuille enjoys a quiet life with her four-year-old son, Charles. On May 6, 1822, she prepares for a special dinner, recalling happier times. A few days later she rushes to visit her ailing mother, Madame Crochard, who is surrounded by friends and a priest. Unbeknownst to Caroline, these friends plot to profit from any inheritance.

The story also introduces Monsieur de Granville, a young barrister balancing career and personal aims. He receives a letter from his father urging him to marry Mademoiselle Angélique Bontems, heiress to a fortune. Granville reflects on his childhood affection for Angélique but feels conflicted, believing he might find greater happiness elsewhere. Captivated anew by Angélique’s beauty during a church service, he yearns for a deeper connection. Thus the lives of Caroline, Roger, and Granville begin to intersect amid social pressures and expectations.

For two weeks after the Vicomte de Granville's engagement to Mademoiselle Bontems, he observed her as she prepared her trousseau. Although Angélique rarely discussed religion, she emphasized its importance in raising children, and Granville agreed to respect her practices on their wedding day. After their marriage they moved to Paris, where Angélique chose a large ground-floor apartment near a church, reflecting her strict upbringing.

Granville, absorbed in his role as public prosecutor, left home decoration to Angélique, who filled their space with austere furnishings. He found the decor cold and uninviting, at odds with the vibrant Parisian life he expected. Their early married life appeared harmonious: Granville immersed in work while Angélique adhered closely to her religious practices.

But Granville soon felt constrained by Angélique's beliefs, especially when she declined social invitations. Their first major conflict erupted when Granville insisted she attend a ball; she refused, prompting a bitter argument about her duties as his wife. Her refusal to conform to societal expectations created a rift, exacerbated by a priest who encouraged her rigid interpretation of faith.

Granville's attempts to assert authority met resistance and ultimately with Angélique seeking guidance from the Pope, deepening their divide. He grew frustrated as the household tightened under her bigotry; despite having children, he felt isolated, unable to share warmth with his family because her unwavering commitment to faith left life devoid of affection.

Between 1806 and 1825, the Comte and Comtesse de Granville's household was marked by emotional strife. Madame de Granville, sensing her husband's affection waning, continued unchanged in prayer, hoping for his love while he withdrew. She awaited his return each day with a mix of hope and resentment. Her attempts to engage him often increased tension, as she asserted moral superiority while he became more detached. When Granville declined a prestigious judicial position, rumors among her friends led to damaging insinuations of infidelity.

Unaware of his true life, Madame de Granville clung to the belief that he was incapable of wrongdoing. Her health deteriorated under the pressure of suspicion and religious fervor. In 1822, during Lent, she fell ill; efforts to be more affectionate only revealed the depth of their estrangement.

In late May, after a brief recovery, she learned from the Abbé Fontanon that Granville had been living with a mistress for seven years and had fathered two children. The revelation shattered her illusions and she confronted him. Granville coldly asserted that her devotion to religious duties had neglected his emotional needs. He argued that true love requires sacrifice and understanding, qualities he felt absent in their marriage, and criticized her prioritizing spiritual duties over their relationship. Despite her pleas, he remained resolute that his love had faded.

In December 1833, a weary Comte de Granville walked along Rue Gaillon and noticed a dim light in an attic window. Young Horace Bianchon expressed concern for his safety. Granville claimed indifference to human emotions, preferring the company of plants and animals. Bianchon revealed that the attic housed Caroline Crochard, devoted to a gambler named Solvet. Granville was initially indifferent but was moved upon hearing her name; nonetheless he refused to help her.

Returning home, Granville found his son Eugène waiting. Eugène told him that a young man, Charles Crochard, recently arrested, claimed to be Granville's son. Granville confirmed this and gave Eugène money to assist, hoping to impart some hard-earned wisdom about marriage and the need to understand one’s partner.

The narrative closes with Granville preparing to leave for Italy, reflecting on familial complexities and the burdens of his past—an arc that underscores indifference, sacrifice, and the tension between emotional detachment and the desire for connection.
