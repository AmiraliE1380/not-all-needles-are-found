Railroads threaten to make certain transportation services around Paris obsolete. The Touchards, a father-son team, previously dominated travel within a 45-mile radius but faced competition from local services, particularly the coucous on shorter routes. In 1822, they contended with three rivals on the lucrative Paris to Beaumont-sur-Oise route, despite extending their service to Chambly.

The Isle-Adam route had less competition, with only two coaches operating smoothly. Pierrotin, a popular coachman, coordinated schedules with his rival to benefit customers from the “Lion d’Argent” inn, where the innkeeper’s wife managed bookings.

On a Saturday morning in 1822, Pierrotin awaited passengers outside the inn, having ordered a new coach to expand his business but facing financial strain. He needed to raise a thousand francs for a luxurious vehicle, as his inherited coucou was cramped.

A footman approached, seeking a ride for his master, the Comte de Serizy, who wished to travel incognito. Eager for a generous tip, Pierrotin agreed, reflecting on the count’s steward, Monsieur Moreau, a respected figure who often employed him. 

Monsieur Huguet de Serizy, a State councillor, faced personal struggles despite public success, including unfulfilled love for his wife. Meanwhile, the count sought to purchase a farm encroaching on his estate, but Moreau conspired to inflate its price, raising suspicions about his integrity. As Pierrotin left the café, he encountered Madame Clapart and her son Oscar, who grappled with feelings of inadequacy as they prepared for their journey.

Tensions existed between the Moreaus and the Reyberts due to reminders of Madame Moreau's past. Ten days later, Oscar met Maitre Desroches, who took an interest in him. For two years, Oscar lived a monk-like existence, preparing for law school and facing rigorous examinations. Despite his progress, Oscar felt trapped, envying well-dressed young men. In November 1825, after passing his exams, Oscar received funds from Moreau to upgrade his wardrobe and began working as a second clerk. The next day, Georges Marest, now head-clerk of notary Hannequin, invited Oscar and his colleagues to a lavish gathering at the Rocher de Cancale. Oscar's superior, Desroches, tasked him with retrieving a crucial legal document and warned him about Derville, who might hinder their efforts. Eager to prove himself, Oscar accepted the challenge.

At the event, the clerks were excited to mingle with high society, unaware that the Marquise was actually Mademoiselle Agathe-Florentine Cabirolle, a successful danseuse. At the Rocher de Cancale, Oscar met various guests and was charmed by Georges. The night led to intoxication among the clerks, and Oscar lost significant money in a game of vingt-et-un, including five hundred francs from Desroches. Overwhelmed, he accepted a thousand francs from Florentine but lost even more.

By morning, Oscar was scolded by his uncle Cardot for his late-night revelry. Tensions escalated when Florentine revealed his gambling losses, prompting Cardot to chastise him. Distraught, Oscar begged for help, and Cardot reluctantly gave him five hundred francs, warning it would be the last time. Desroches, furious, decided to dismiss Oscar.

Feeling the weight of his mistakes, Oscar resolved to change and enlisted in the cavalry. He excelled, earning honors for bravery. Years later, he encountered Georges, whose life had declined, reflecting on their contrasting circumstances. Oscar matured into a respectable man, marrying Georgette Pierrotin and becoming a collector, embodying the lessons learned from his past.
