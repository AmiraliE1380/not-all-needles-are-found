November 1809, Napoleon at his height. After Wagram, peace talks with Austria dragged on as courts orbited him and he staged entertainments for his planned marriage to an Austrian archduchess. Diamonds and gilt masked austerity: leaders indulged in wealth paid by soldiers.

Louis XV–style hedonism foreshadowed doom. The craze for soldiers suited Napoleon: repeated campaigns kept peace fragile and mistresses risked widowhood. Soldiers promised glory or romanticized death; the culture celebrated bravery and jeweled officers like Murat.

Comte de Gondreville (Citizen Malin) delayed entertainment to outshine rivals and flatter Napoleon. His salons hosted diplomats; a quarrel with Josephine, hinting at divorce, kept him away. Parisian beauties and financiers traded wealth and feigned friendship.

These remarks explain the little imbroglio and reflect the tone of Paris drawing-rooms.

“Look toward the pedestal supporting that candelabrum—do you see the young lady with her hair a la Chinoise? She’s in the corner to the left, with bluebells in her chestnut curls. She’s so pale you might think she’s ill, delicate-looking, and very small. Now she’s turning her head; her almond-shaped blue eyes seem made for tears. She’s leaning forward to see Madame de Vaudremont below the crowd.”

“I see her now. You only had to mention her lovely complexion; I would have recognized her. She has the whitest skin here, but she’s either a prude or a coquette; her bodice barely hints at her beauty. What shoulders! What lily-whiteness!”

“Who is she?” asked the first speaker.

“I don’t know.”

“An aristocrat! Do you want to keep them all to yourself, Montcornet?”

“You of all people to tease me!” Montcornet smiled. “Do you think you can insult a poor general like me just because you’re a rival of Soulanges? Or is it because I’ve only just arrived in this Promised Land? Come, Monsieur le Maître des Requêtes, let us glean in the field you can only hold precariously. We have a right to live! If you knew the German women, you might help me with the Parisian you love best.”

“Well, General, since you’ve noticed that lady, have you seen her dance?”

“Where have you been? There’s a triple rank of the most undaunted coquettes of Paris between her and the swarm of dancing men. It’s a miracle anyone could spot her in that corner, buried in gloom despite the candles. Between her and us is a sparkle of diamonds and glances, so many floating plumes and fluttering lace that it’s impossible for any dancer to detect her among those stars. How could you think she’s the wife of some sous-préfet from Lippe or Dyle?”

“Oh, he will be!” exclaimed the Master of Appeals.

“I doubt it,” laughed the Colonel of Cuirassiers. “She seems as raw in intrigue as you are in diplomacy. I bet you don’t know how she got there.”

The lawyer looked with a mix of contempt and curiosity.

“Well,” Montcornet continued, “she probably arrived punctually at nine, the first of the company, and embarrassed the Comtesse de Gondreville, who can’t think straight. Repulsed by the mistress of the house and driven into the corner by jealous ladies, she became a victim of their coalition against her. Each of those sweet faces would have conspired to keep the men from taking her out. That’s how those tender-looking women would have formed a coalition against the stranger without saying a word beyond, ‘Do you know that little woman in blue?’ If you care to run the gauntlet of flattering glances and inviting questions, try to get through the rampart defending that Queen of Dyle, Lippe, or Charente. You’ll see how even the dullest woman will invent a wile to keep the plaintive stranger in the shadows. Doesn’t she look like an elegy?”

“Do you think so, Montcornet? Then she must be married?”

“Why not a widow?”

“She would be less passive,” said the lawyer, laughing.

“She might be the widow of a gambler,” replied the handsome Colonel.

“Of course; since the peace, there are many widows of that sort!” said Martial. “But my dear Montcornet, we are simpletons. That face is too innocent; there’s too much youth and freshness for her to be married. What splendid flesh-tints! Nothing has sunk in the modeling of her nose. Her lips and chin are as fresh as a white rosebud, though her expression is veiled by clouds of sadness. Who could make that young creature weep?”

“Women cry for little,” said the Colonel.

“I don’t know,” replied Martial; “but she’s not crying because she’s left without a partner; her grief is not new. It’s clear she beautified herself for this evening with intention. I would wager she’s in love already.”

“Bah! She’s perhaps the daughter of some German princeling; no one talks to her,” said Montcornet.

“Dear! How unhappy a poor child may be! Can there be anything more graceful and refined than our little stranger? Yet not one of those furies around her, who think they can feel, will say a word to her. If she would only speak, we’d see if she has fine teeth.”

“Bless me, you boil over like milk at the least increase of temperature!” cried the Colonel, nettled at finding a rival in his friend.

“What!” exclaimed the lawyer, ignoring the Colonel’s question. “Can nobody here tell us the name of this exotic flower?”

“Some lady companion!” said Montcornet.

“A companion! Wearing sapphires fit for a queen and a dress of Malines lace? Tell that to the marines, General. You wouldn’t shine in diplomacy if you jump from a German princess to a lady companion.”

Montcornet stopped a little fat man with iron-gray hair and clever eyes—Comte de Gondreville—mingling among the groups.

“Gondreville, my friend,” said Montcornet, “who is that charming little woman sitting under that huge candelabrum?”

“The candelabrum? Ravrio’s work; Isabey made the design.”

“Oh, I recognized your lavishness and taste; but the lady?”

“Ah! I don’t know. Some friend of my wife’s, no doubt.”

“Or your mistress, you old rascal.”

“No, on my honor. The Comtesse de Gondreville is the only one capable of inviting people no one knows.”

Despite the jest, Gondreville smiled. Montcornet returned to his companions, who resumed their fruitless inquiries about the unknown. Montcornet leaned toward Martial and whispered:

“Mind what you’re about. Madame de Vaudremont has been watching you for some time with ominous attentiveness; she can guess by the movement of your lips what you say to me. Our eyes have already told her too much; she’s thinking even more than we are about the little blue lady.”

“That’s too old a trick in warfare, my dear Montcornet! However, what do I care? Like the Emperor, when I make a conquest, I keep it.”

“Martial, your fatuity cries out for a lesson. You, a civilian, and so lucky as to be the husband-designate of Madame de Vaudremont, a widow of two-and-twenty with four thousand napoleons a year—who slips such a diamond on your finger,”—he took Martial’s left hand to show the ring—“and you affect the Lovelace, as if you were a colonel obliged to maintain the military reputation! Fie! Just think of all you may lose.”

“At any rate, I shall not lose my liberty,” replied Martial with a forced laugh.

He cast a passionate glance at Madame de Vaudremont, who responded only with a smile of unease, having seen the Colonel examining the lawyer’s ring.

“Listen to me, Martial. If you flutter around my young stranger, I shall set to work to win Madame de Vaudremont.”

“You have my full permission, my dear Cuirassier, but you will not gain this much,” and the Maître des Requêtes clicked his thumb-nail mockingly under a tooth.

“Remember that I am unmarried,” said the Colonel; “my sword is my whole fortune; and such a challenge is setting Tantalus down to a banquet he cannot devour.”

“Prrr.”

This defiant roll of consonants was Martial’s only reply. The fashion of the time displayed men in white breeches and silk stockings; Montcornet in his Cuirassiers uniform showed a stalwart, commanding figure. At thirty-five, with a black moustache, broad forehead, and aquiline nose, he carried an air of superiority from command—pleasing to a woman not seeking to make a slave of her husband. He smiled as he looked at his friend, the lawyer Martial de la Roche-Hugon—small, Provençal, a favorite of Napoleon, the sort of man whose pliancy, intrigue, and knowledge of manners promised advancement. Martial had learned to conceal ambition under the guise of a lady-killer; his face had the diplomat’s tinned-iron brightness, concealing emotion even while he plotted for the future.

The two friends parted with a cordial handshake. The introductory tune called the ladies to form for a new quadrille, clearing the men from the space they’d occupied. Their whispered conversation had occurred during the interval between dances. The chandeliers and flambeaux illuminated faces and failed to conceal the faint expressions from the keen-sighted countess or the artless stranger. This espionage of thoughts is perhaps one of the pleasures idle people find in society.

To understand the interest of the conversation, one must relate an incident that would serve as an invisible bond, drawing together the actors scattered through the rooms.

At about eleven o’clock, as dancers returned to their seats, the company observed the entrance of the handsomest woman in Paris: Madame de Vaudremont, queen of fashion, who made it a rule never to appear until a party had reached a pitch of excitement where faces and dresses still shone. She never lingered until flowers drooped, hair fell from curls, or frills tumbled—she left before fatigue spoiled her image. She paused on the threshold, shooting swift glances to assure herself her dress eclipsed others.

Madame de Vaudremont entered with the Comte de Soulanges, one of the bravest colonels of the Guards’ Artillery and the Emperor’s favorite—an association that had a transient, curious air. Women rose and men hurried to the doorway to see them. Some joked that women are as curious about a man faithful to his passion as men are about a woman hard to enthrall.

The Comte de Soulanges, about thirty-two, had a nervous temperament suggesting fine qualities; his slender, pale appearance and black, lively eyes belied his later oratorical renown under the Restoration. The Comtesse de Vaudremont, tall, somewhat plump, dazzlingly white-skinned, with a small head carried well, inspired love by manner and appearance. They became the center of attention for a few minutes, but saw their awkwardness and let the curiosity die down.

Martial, watching with jealous anxiety from a group by the fireplace, felt his success might be precarious. The Countess thanked Soulanges coldly, made a slight bow, and sat beside Madame de Gondreville, which eased Martial’s tension. Seeing Soulanges still standing near Vaudremont, apparently unaware of the glance exchanging their roles, the volcanic Provençal knit his brows and smoothed his curls to maintain composure. He took Montcornet’s hand, but was elsewhere in thought.

Soulanges sat calmly admiring the scene of women and jewels. His rival’s indifference unsettled Martial. Unable to control his impatience, Martial approached Madame de Vaudremont with a bow. Soulanges cast Martial a covert glance and turned away. A hush fell; everyone expected some outbreak. Suddenly, Soulanges’s pale face flushed scarlet and he fixed his gaze on the floor, passing in front of Martial toward a cardroom. Martial and the company took it as public surrender—Soulanges had yielded his place in the Countess’s favor.

Martial raised his head proudly and looked at the strange lady under the candelabrum. He sat near Madame de Vaudremont and, distracted, missed her words behind her fan: “Martial, please don’t wear that ring you took from me. I have my reasons and will explain them when we leave. You must give me your arm to go to the Princess de Wagram’s.”

“Why did you come in with the Colonel?” asked Martial.

“I met him in the hall,” she replied. “But leave me now; everybody is looking at us.”

Martial returned to the Colonel. It was then the little blue lady became the object of curiosity affecting Montcornet, Soulanges, Martial, and Madame de Vaudremont.

After their challenge, the Baron flew to Madame de Vaudremont and led her into the most brilliant quadrille. The intoxication and turmoil of the dance tempted Martial to yield to the charm that had drawn his gaze to the fair stranger. Though he tried to hide glances from Vaudremont’s watchful eyes, he was soon detected; and when she pressed and teased him, his absent-mindedness left him unable to answer the intimate question, “Do you like me very much this evening?”

While Martial danced, Montcornet moved from group to group seeking information about the unknown. Finding Gondreville apparently free, he seized a space next to the pedestal of the candelabrum and, through compliments to neighboring dowagers, worked into the empty seat beside the stranger, risking wax and shadow.

Too tactful to address her directly, Montcornet began with the plain woman on his left: banal compliments intended to draw out his right-hand neighbor. The stranger paid no heed; the officer prepared to ask, “And you, madame?” hoping to learn much. Instead he saw tears in her eyes; she stared at Madame de Vaudremont as if absorbed.

“You are married, no doubt, madame?” he asked.

“Yes, monsieur.”

“And your husband is here, of course?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

“And why remain in this spot? Is it to attract attention?”

She smiled sadly.

“Allow me the honor, madame, of being your partner in the next quadrille, and I will ensure not to bring you back here. When so many are ready to ascend the throne, and Royalty is the mania of the day, I cannot imagine you will refuse the title of Queen of the Ball that your beauty may claim.”

“I do not intend to dance, monsieur.”

Her curt refusal forced Montcornet to withdraw. Martial, who had guessed the request and refusal, smiled, stroking his chin and flashing the diamond on his finger.

“What are you laughing at?” asked the Comtesse de Vaudremont.

“At the poor Colonel’s failure—”

“I begged you to take your ring off,” said the Countess, interrupting.

“I did not hear you.”

“If you hear nothing this evening, at least you see everything, Monsieur le Baron,” she said, vexed.

“That young man is displaying a very fine diamond,” the stranger remarked to Montcornet.

“Splendid,” he replied. “The man is Baron Martial de la Roche-Hugon, one of my best friends.”

“I thank you for telling me his name,” she said; “he seems an agreeable man.”

“Yes, but he is rather fickle.”

“He seems to be on the best terms with the Comtesse de Vaudremont?” she asked, watching the Countess.

“On the very best.”

The unknown girl turned pale.

“Hallo!” thought the soldier, “she is in love with that lucky devil Martial.”

“I fancied Madame de Vaudremont had long been devoted to M. de Soulanges,” the girl added, recovering from her clouded beauty.

“For a week past, the Countess has been faithless,” said Montcornet. “But you must have seen poor Soulanges when he came in; he’s still trying to disbelieve in his disaster.”

“Yes, I saw him,” she said. Then: “Thank you very much, monsieur,”—a tone of dismissal.

At that moment the quadrille ended. Montcornet withdrew, telling himself, “She is married.”

“Well, valiant Cuirassier,” cried Martial, drawing the Colonel aside into a window-bay for fresh air, “how are you getting on?”

“She is a married woman, my dear fellow.”

“What does that matter?”

“Oh, deuce take it! I’m a decent sort of man,” said Montcornet. “I have no intention of courting a woman I cannot marry. Besides, she told me she would not dance.”

“Colonel, I will bet a hundred napoleons to your gray horse that she will dance with me this evening.”

“Done!” Montcornet put his hand to the wager. “Meanwhile, I’m going to look for Soulanges; he might know the lady, as she seems interested in him.”

“You’ve lost, my good fellow,” cried Martial, laughing. “My eyes have met hers, and I know what they mean. My dear friend, you owe me no grudge for dancing with her after she refused you?”

“No, no. Those who laugh last, laugh longest. But I’m an honest gambler and a generous enemy, Martial, and I warn you, she is fond of diamonds.”

With that they parted. Montcornet found Soulanges in the cardroom at a bouillotte table. Though their soldierly comradeship was slight, Montcornet was alarmed to see the prudent artillery colonel playing with a frenzy that could lead to ruin. He watched as heaps of gold and notes were piled on the fateful cards; silent men formed a ring about the players. Short words—pass, play, I stop, a thousand Louis, taken—were spoken; otherwise the five men at the table conversed only with their eyes.

As Montcornet approached, Soulanges, pale and agitated, was winning. Names of men who rose from the table—Field-Marshal the Duc d’Isemberg, Keller, a famous banker—marked the scale of losses; they had been cleaned out. Soulanges swept up gold and notes without counting them; his lips curled in bitter scorn, defiant of fortune rather than grateful.

“Courage,” the Colonel cried, seizing the Comte de Soulanges from the gaming table. “Come with me. I have good news, but on one condition.”

“That you will answer a question,” Soulanges asked, gathering his winnings. The players fell silent as he rose, some relieved. A diplomat sneered at soldiers’ camaraderie; another replied, “When we say military men, we do not mean civil, Monsieur le Ministre.”

Montcornet drew Soulanges aside. “The Emperor praised you this morning, and your promotion to field-marshal is certain.”

Soulanges, puzzled, muttered that “The Master does not love the Artillery.” Montcornet persisted: the Emperor favored the nobility and would not disgrace those who married in Paris during the campaign. Then, lowering his voice, he asked if Soulanges knew the pale woman sitting beneath the great candelabrum.

At the mention, the Count’s face brightened and then collapsed. “If anyone else had asked me that I would have cracked his skull with this gold,” he said, seizing the Colonel’s hand. His voice changed: he begged to be left alone and confessed a despair that made him feel like “blowing out my brains tonight.” The gaiety of the ball, the music and laughter, “were killing him.” Montcornet, seeing him inconsolable, withdrew.

Montcornet then found Madame de Vaudremont agitated. She wanted to attend the Grand Duchess of Berg’s ball but planned first to stop at the Princesse de Wagram’s. Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon amused himself by flirting with dowagers. Montcornet teased that he bet the Countess would stay at their ball, then hinted the real cause: she feared seeing Martial at the feet of the woman under the candelabrum.

The Countess pretended surprise, then admitted the truth: Madame de Lansac, “as malicious as an old monkey,” had told her that Monsieur de la Roche-Hugon flirted with a stranger who “sat like a skeleton at a feast.” The Countess loathed the pale, cruelly beautiful face—“my evil genius”—and blamed Madame de Lansac, who came to balls to watch everything while pretending to sleep. She implored Montcornet to urge Martial, his friend, not to make her unhappy.

Montcornet responded that he had just left a man who threatened to thrash Martial flat if he spoke to that lady and was ready to “blow his brains out.” Still, Montcornet knew Martial well; such threats might provoke him. He planned to discover who the blue-dressed stranger was during the next dance.

At such entertainments, old women like Madame de Lansac gathered to observe the affairs of youth. The Duchess of Lansac was a shrewd, mischievous dowager who read hearts as if reading a book: a faint frown, a quiver, the slightest curl of the lips betrayed feelings. From her armchair she watched the young coquette disguise annoyance as cheerfulness; the Countess felt as much sorrow as she feigned. The Countess had believed Martial a man of talent who could bring power into her life; now she realized her mistake. Her sudden passion for Martial, though recent, caused acute pain, as if a healthy limb had been amputated.

Sensing an opportunity, the Duchess dismissed her Ambassador and concentrated on the couple. Her sardonic glance frightened the Countess, who suspected the dowager had contrived the stranger’s presence. The Duchess’s triumphant look across the room gave the pale woman under the chandelier a flush of hope; the Countess saw the alliance and resolved to learn the stranger’s identity.

The Baron de la Roche-Hugon, unable to learn the blue lady’s name, turned to the Comtesse de Gondreville and then to the Duchess. Catching the Duchess’s look and reviving Martial’s hopes, he approached. The Duchess, with sardonic irony, admitted she knew the stranger’s family. The Baron pressed: “Why does she not dance? She is such a pretty creature.” He offered a crafty bargain: if the Duchess revealed the woman’s identity, he would press for the restitution of the woods of Navarreins from the Commissioners of Crown Lands on the Emperor’s behalf—a favour of political and financial weight.

This revealed the Baron's ambition. The Duchess, who had once helped her husband obtain a title and whose arms had a connection to the younger branch of Navarreins, played the proposal like a card. She answered, deceptively grave, that the Baron should bring the Comtesse de Vaudremont to her and she would reveal the mystery. She observed that all eyes were on the corner where her “protégée” sat modestly, receiving homage intended for the Countess. Fixing her eyes on Madame de Vaudremont, she added a look that plainly said she would rather the Countess learn the stranger’s name from her than from any other.

The Duchess’s attitude provoked the Countess, who rose, approached, and took the chair Martial had placed for her. Without acknowledging Martial, she said to the Duchess that she admitted her ignorance and did not know whether the knowledge would serve her good or ill. The Duchess pressed the young woman’s hand, replying in a low, almost compassionate tone, “Poor child!”

They understood that Martial was an obstacle; the Countess imperiously dismissed him, and the Baron glared—one of those decisive looks men use that can sway blinded hearts but seems ridiculous to women beginning to judge their suitors.

Martial, a worldly man with wit, did not want a breach with a woman favored at Court whom the Emperor wished to see married. He thought jealousy the surest way to learn why the Countess kept him at arm’s length and withdrew willingly as a new quadrille began.

The Baron leaned against a marble console, watching the two women talk. Comparing the Countess with the mysterious newcomer, he vacillated between grasping an available fortune and indulging a caprice. The light outlined his anxious face against the curtains, giving him the look of an evil genius. Observers could have said, with derision, “There is another poor wretch who seems to be enjoying himself!”

Meanwhile Montcornet, leaning in the doorway between ballroom and cardroom, laughed quietly. He enjoyed watching the emotional turmoil—the Countess’s pain, Martial’s agitation—and wondered about the link between Soulanges’ gloomy looks and the fair unknown’s face, which shifted between hope and dread. As Colonel of the Imperial Guard, Montcornet enjoyed admiring smiles; he was a significant match in military society.

It was nearly midnight. Conversation, gambling, dancing, flirtations, rivalries, and scheming reached fever pitch. Madame de Lansac seized the moment to speak plainly to the Countess: she offered the sort of worldly counsel a woman of experience gives a younger one at a ball.

“You are now at an age when I made many mistakes,” she said. “Seeing you endure a thousand deaths, I thought I might give you some charitable advice. To go wrong at two-and-twenty is to spoil your future; it is like tearing the gown you must wear. My dear, you must learn to choose between pleasure and happiness. You want to marry Martial, who is not wise enough to be a good husband, nor passionate enough to be a lasting lover. He is in debt; he will squander your fortune. He looks aged and ill, and will be a wreck in three years. He is a man of intrigue, perhaps clever, but too presumptuous to have real merit. He is calculating you as if you were an investment.”

She counseled the Countess to marry for rank and security: find an older man with an assured position, “a widow’s marriage should not be a trivial love affair,” she warned. A new alliance should be a sound speculation; in marrying again the Countess should at least hope to be addressed as “Madame la Maréchale.”

The Duchess’s advice mixed pity with hard practicality: prudence, rank, and financial security took precedence over a romantic match with Martial, whom she portrayed as opportunistic. The Countess listened, torn between feeling and counsel. Around them the salon’s intrigues continued—wagers, furtive glances, jealous men and calculating women—while the mystery of the pale stranger and the designs of the Duchess, the Colonel, the Baron, and Martial spun like the dance itself. Each player sought advantage amid love, ambition, and social maneuvering, and the pale woman under the candelabrum remained the pivot of jealousies and schemes, the cause of both Soulanges’ despair and the Countess’s troubled heart.

As she spoke, both women turned to Colonel Montcornet’s handsome face.

The Duchess bluntly reproached the Countess: if she preferred flirting to marriage she should know better than anyone how to stir and then calm trouble, but must not ruin families or wives’ happiness. From her own experience she warned that a moment of vanity can destroy a virtuous woman and leave one mortally hated. “One salmon is worth a thousand frogs,” she quoted: genuine affection brings more happiness than fleeting passions. She had come to preach; the Countess was the reason for her visit to this lower-class house. They had once received actors in the boudoir, never in the drawing room. If the Countess must play with men, she should toy only with those whose lives were unsettled and without duties—otherwise they would not forgive the errors that made them happy. The Duchess pointed to Soulanges as an example: the Countess had turned him into a lovesick fool for fifteen months. He had been married two years to a charming wife he loved but neglected; the wife lived in tears. The Countess’ flirtation had affected his whole life—his remorse had been far worse than any pleasure she’d given him—and then the Countess had abandoned him.

Madame de Lansac took Madame de Vaudremont’s hand and rose. “There,” she said, pointing to a pale, trembling figure by candlelight, “that is my grandniece, the Comtesse de Soulanges. Today she finally yielded to my persuasion and left the sorrowful room where her child gives her little consolation. You think her charming? Imagine how she must have looked when happiness and love lit that now-blighted face.”

The Countess looked away, lost in thought. The Duchess led her toward the card-room door and nodded toward Soulanges.

He confirmed the Duchess’s words: pale, rigid in an easy chair, his indifference and the tension in his brow revealing deep suffering. Players passed him as if he were dead. The image of the tearful wife and morose husband, separated amid festivity, struck the Countess with foreboding about her own future. Not wholly hardened, she pressed the Duchess’s hand in a childlike thanks.

“My dear child,” the old lady whispered, “remember, we can repel a man’s attentions as easily as we attract them.”

“She is yours if you’re not a fool,” Madame de Lansac murmured to Montcornet, while the Countess remained absorbed in compassion for Soulanges, still loving him enough to wish to restore his happiness.

“Oh! I will talk to him!” the Countess declared.

“Do nothing of the sort!” the Duchess returned, sitting again. “Choose a good husband and shut your door to my nephew. A wife cannot accept her husband’s heart as a gift from another woman; she is far happier believing she has won it back. By bringing my niece here I believe I’ve given her a chance to regain her husband’s affection. All I need from you is to play the Colonel.” She pointed to Montcornet, and the Countess smiled.

When they were alone, Martial asked if she knew the name of the unknown woman. “Yes,” she replied, playful. Martial, still believing she loved him, adopted a charming air. “Will you be annoyed if I seem to care about that name?”

“Will you be annoyed if a remnant of affection prevents me from telling you? And if I forbid you to approach that young lady? It could be dangerous.”

“To lose your favor would be worse than losing my life.”

“Martial,” she said sternly, “she is Madame de Soulanges. Her husband would kill you if he knew.”

“Ha! So the Colonel can leave the man in peace who has taken his love, yet would fight for his wife! What a reversal of principles! I beg you to let me dance with her. You’ll see how little love that icy heart could feel for you; if the Colonel allows me to dance with his wife after letting me—”

“But she loves her husband.”

“An even greater obstacle I shall enjoy overcoming.”

“But she is married.”

“A whimsical objection!”

“Ah!” the Countess said with a bitter smile, “you punish us for both our faults and our repentance!”

“Do not be angry!” Martial begged. “Forgive me. I will think no more of Madame de Soulanges.”

“You deserve that I should send you to her.”

“I’m off then,” he laughed, “and I’ll return more devoted to you than ever. You’ll see that the prettiest woman cannot capture the heart that is yours.”

“Meaning you want to win Colonel Montcornet’s horse?”

“Ah! Traitor!” he cried, playfully threatening his friend. The Colonel smiled and joined them; the Baron slid into a seat near the Countess with sardonic flair: “Here, madame, is a man who boasted he could win your favor in one evening.”

He left, sure he had piqued the Countess’ pride and done Montcornet a service, unaware of Madame de Vaudremont’s irony: both men were unconsciously drawn toward one another.

At that moment the lawyer approached Madame de Soulanges, who sat pale and timid, alive only in her eyes. Her husband entered the ballroom with flashing anger. The Duchess, ever watchful, rushed to her nephew, asking for his arm to find her carriage, feigning boredom to prevent a scene. Before leaving she shot a knowing glance at her niece, signaling her to take revenge.

Madame de Vaudremont noticed the exchange and felt a sudden realization: the duplicitous Duchess, who preached morality, might be plotting. Pride stirred more than curiosity to unravel the intrigue, and in her distraction she lost control. The Colonel, misreading her embarrassment, grew more ardent. Old diplomats, entertained by the unfolding drama, perhaps had never seen so many simultaneous intrigues; the passions of the two couples were plain in the crowded rooms, reflected on surrounding faces and amplifying their own impotence.

Finally, the Baron found a seat beside Madame de Soulanges. Up close he admired her beauty—the fresh neck, the graceful figure emphasized by the fashionable sash tied under the bosom—and was captivated. “You haven’t danced once this evening, madame,” he said softly. “Not for lack of a partner, I presume?”

“I never go to parties; I’m quite unknown,” she replied coldly, unaware of her aunt’s signal to attract the Baron.

Martial, adjusting the diamond on his hand, caught the Countess’s eye and felt a spark of hope. She blushed and looked at him with an indefinable expression. “Do you enjoy dancing?” he asked.

“Yes, very much, monsieur.”

Their eyes met; the earnestness in her voice touched him. “Then, am I too bold in offering to be your partner for the next quadrille?”

Artless confusion colored her cheeks. “But, monsieur, I have already refused one partner—a military man—”

“Was it that tall cavalry colonel over there?”

“Precisely.”

“Oh! He’s a friend of mine; don’t worry. Will you grant me the favor I hope for?”

“Yes, monsieur.”

Her tone revealed a new, deep emotion that stirred Martial’s world-weary heart. Overcome with shyness, he lost some confidence; then his southern charm ignited. The quadrille formed, and standing beside her eased his nerves. For men like Martial, dancing was a way to express themselves and attempt to win a woman’s heart by physical grace; his movements suggested he intended to show every charm he possessed.

He led his conquest into the quadrille, where the most brilliant women prized dancing above all. As the orchestra played, the Baron surveyed the ladies forming the square with pride. Madame de Soulanges’ dress rivaled Madame de Vaudremont’s; a flattering murmur betrayed the men’s admiration. Her blush increased her charm. When she raised her eyes she looked at her partner as if to transfer that glory to him; she seemed to prize his opinion, mixing innocence and vanity with the first stirrings of affection. Modest and inexperienced in the ballroom, she nevertheless knew when to glance at him.

When a new figure by the dancer Trenis brought Martial face to face with the Colonel, the latter joked, “I have won your horse.” Montcornet retorted, “Yes, but you have lost eighty thousand francs a year!” Martial dismissed it lightly: “What do I care? Madame de Soulanges is worth millions!”

After the quadrille whispers circulated. The less attractive women made moral comments about Martial and the Comtesse; the prettier women marveled at her apparent ease in surrendering to him. The men, puzzled by the Baron’s luck, did not think him particularly attractive. A few sympathetic women advised caution, warning that if a mere glance or graceful dance could compromise a young wife, many would be ruined.

Only Martial grasped his happiness. During the last figure, as the ladies formed the moulinet, he clasped the Countess’s hand and felt her response through the thin glove. As the quadrille ended he urged her not to return to her hiding corner: Was admiration the only fruit she derived from her jewels? Come and enjoy the scene and yourself.

Yielding to his charm, Madame de Soulanges walked through the crowded rooms with him, hesitating at each doorway until he reassured her, “He is not here.” They reached a grand picture gallery set for a lavish collation. As supper approached, Martial led her to an oval boudoir overlooking the garden, draped with flowers and blue hangings. The noise faded. Startled, the Countess resisted, then sat on an ottoman after a quick glance in a mirror, as if reassured they could be seen.

“This room is charming,” she remarked, admiring the decor. “All here is love and delight!” Martial, deeply moved, watched her face in the dim light: unease, modesty, eagerness—an expression that captivated him. She smiled, calming her inner struggle. Flirtatiously she took his left hand and slipped a ring from it, exclaiming at the diamond.

Martial, intoxicated by her touch, urged her to wear it, “in memory of this hour, and for the love of—” He broke off, seized by her rapture. When she asked if he gave it to her, he answered he wished he had the whole world to offer. “Will you accept only my diamond?” she pressed. “You will never take it back?” “Never,” he assured her. She placed the ring on her finger. As Martial moved to embrace her, she suddenly rose and said clearly, “I accept the diamond, monsieur, with less scruple because it belongs to me.”

The Baron was taken aback. “Monsieur de Soulanges took it from my dressing-table and told me he had lost it,” she said. Martial protested irritably that it had been given to him by Madame de Vaudremont. “Precisely,” she replied with a smile. “My husband borrowed it, gave it to her, and she gifted it to you. This ring has made a little journey, that is all. It may reveal secrets to me.” She touched a spring in the ring; a lock of M. de Soulanges’ hair appeared. Stunned, she fled into the crowded rooms, leaving Martial confounded.

He noticed the Colonel and Madame de Vaudremont laughing nearby; the Colonel teased, “Will you have my horse to chase after your prize?” Martial took their banter in stride. Grateful they kept silence about the evening’s events, he felt he had exchanged his horse for a young wife.

As the Comtesse de Soulanges drove home across Paris to the Faubourg Saint‑Germain, anxiety consumed her. Before leaving the Hotel Gondreville she had tried to find her aunt and husband, who had departed without her. Disturbing suspicions plagued her: she had silently witnessed her husband’s torment since Madame de Vaudremont had ensnared him and had hoped for his repentance. Now she feared she had erred by consenting to her aunt’s scheme.

The evening darkened her innocent spirit. Alarmed first by her husband’s suffering, she grew more distressed at her rival’s beauty and the weight of societal corruption. Crossing the Pont Royal, she discarded the hair from the diamond—the only token of pure affection—and wept, recalling a long-standing grief and shuddering at the thought that a woman seeking peace must bury her pain without complaint.

“Alas!” she pondered. “What can women do when they do not love? What sustains their devotion? I cannot believe, as my aunt claims, that reason alone is enough.”

Still sighing, she arrived home and rushed upstairs, startled to find her husband by the fire. “How long has it been since you attended balls without telling me?” he demanded, his voice strained. “You know a woman is out of place without her husband. You compromised yourself by hiding in the dark corner.”

“Oh, my dear Léon,” she coaxed, “I couldn’t resist the joy of seeing you without your knowing. My aunt took me to the ball, and I was very happy there!”

Her words softened the Count’s sternness; he had dreaded her return, fearing she would learn of his infidelity. Delighted to see him smile and grateful for his presence in a room he had frequented less often, the Countess looked at him tenderly, blushing. Her kindness further enchanted Soulanges, especially after the misery he had endured at the ball. He took her hand and kissed it gratefully; gratitude often accompanies love.

General Montcornet did not marry Madame de Vaudremont despite their brief understanding; she later perished in the tragic fire that marred the ball hosted by the Austrian ambassador for Napoleon’s marriage to the daughter of Emperor Joseph II.
