If the French have a strong aversion to traveling, the English have a propensity for it; both nations have reasons. Other countries may offer pleasant scenery and comfort, but the unique charms of France—its wit, conversation, and unspoken understanding—are hard to replicate. Many Frenchmen therefore take pleasure in encountering familiar ways abroad, comforts reminiscent of Paris.

In 1836, during the Sardinian Court’s stay in Genoa, two notable Parisians found themselves in a palazzo, a remnant of Genoese nobility. Genoa’s evenings, especially after rain, create a serene atmosphere suited to friends and Italian cuisine. At a dinner hosted by the French Consul-General were the celebrated Mademoiselle des Touches, known as Camille Maupin, and her companions: the painter Léon de Lora and the critic Claude Vignon.

Camille had gone to Florence on business and brought Léon to see Italy. Their stop in Genoa was a courtesy to the Consul-General, who wished to honor her presence. Despite her fame, Camille avoided formal engagement with the ambassador and preferred to enjoy the evening without pressure. The dinner was lively, conversation flowing, though literature was seldom mentioned until the Consul-General shared a personal story to change the mood.

The Consul-General, thirty-four, bore a striking resemblance to Lord Byron, with a dreamy expression that charmed many. He had married Onorina Pedrotti, the only child of a wealthy banker, after a period of reluctance; their union had been influenced by the ambassador’s intervention and by the Consul’s own hidden struggles. Onorina, a beautiful Genoese woman, embodied her heritage’s elegance. Their family life seemed harmonious, though Camille perceived an underlying melancholy in the Consul.

The evening’s debate turned to women’s morality and accountability in cases of infidelity. The three women present judged the woman harshly, while the men argued for a more nuanced view. To shift the mood, the Consul-General recounted a pivotal moment from his youth.

At twenty-two, his uncle, the Abbé Loraux, arranged for him to become secretary to Comte Octave, a prominent statesman. The uncle’s influence secured a position promising a bright future, despite the young man’s initial innocence and naivety, sheltered from society’s complexities.

As he settled into his role, he observed the Count’s character and burdens. Outwardly composed and pious, the Count harbored a profound sadness the Consul gradually understood. Their relationship deepened: the Count treated him with kindness and respect, often blurring lines between master and mentor. The Consul was struck by the Count’s intellect, devotion to duty, and the contrast between public success and private sorrow.

The Count’s meticulous nature and love for flowers revealed a tenderness that mirrored his own faded beauty. His life was marked by relentless duty and a search for a happiness he could not attain; outward devotion to work and religion masked an ongoing yearning for inner peace. This tension between public expectation and private despair shaped their relationship and the Consul’s understanding of human complexity.

The Consul-General’s story, rich in feeling, captivated his audience, leaving the guests to ponder the depths of the Count’s character and the struggles beneath seemingly perfect lives.

“On a certain Monday,” he resumed, “M. le Président de Grandville and M. de Serizy (then Vice‑President of the Council of State) met at Comte Octave’s house. They formed a committee of three with me as secretary. The Count had secured my appointment as Auditor to the Council of State. All the necessary documents for their inquiry lay in the library; MM. de Grandville and de Serizy relied on the Count for the preliminary examination. To avoid carrying all the papers to M. de Serizy, they agreed to meet first in the Rue Payenne. The Tuileries valued this work, and it led to my appointment as Master of Appeals that year.

“Though the Comtes de Grandville and de Serizy, who rarely dined out, were still discussing late into the evening, a servant interrupted me: ‘MM. the Cures of Saint‑Paul and of the White Friars have been waiting in the drawing‑room for two hours.’

“It was nine o’clock.

“‘Well, gentlemen, you find yourselves compelled to dine with priests,’ said Comte Octave. ‘I do not know whether Grandville can overcome his horror of a priest’s gown—’

“‘It depends on the priest.’

“‘One of them is my uncle, and the other is the Abbé Gaudron,’ I said. ‘Do not be alarmed; the Abbé Fontanon is no longer second priest at Saint‑Paul.’

“‘Well, let us dine,’ replied the Président de Grandville. ‘A bigot frightens me, but there is no one so cheerful as a truly pious man.’

“We entered the drawing‑room. The dinner was delightful. Politicians with real knowledge make excellent storytellers; my uncle’s wit was refined and elegant, characteristic of those who hide thought under a black robe. There was nothing vulgar in this light talk; I compared it to Rossini’s music.

“The Abbé Gaudron, as M. de Grandville observed, resembled more a Saint Peter than a Saint Paul: a peasant full of faith, whose ignorance of worldly matters enlivened the talk with guileless amazement. They began discussing a social issue—adultery. My uncle pointed out the contradiction between civil and religious law left after the Revolution.

“‘In the eyes of the Church,’ he said, ‘adultery is a crime; in your tribunals, it is a misdemeanor. Adultery drives to the police court in a carriage instead of standing at the bar. Napoleon’s Council of State, touched with tenderness towards erring women, was quite inefficient. Should they not have harmonized civil and religious law and sent the guilty wife to a convent, as of old?’

“‘To a convent!’ said M. de Serizy. ‘They must first have created convents; monasteries were being turned into barracks. Besides, think of what you say, M. l’Abbé—give to God what society would have none of?’

“‘Oh!’ said Comte de Grandville, ‘you do not know France. They had to leave the husband free to take proceedings; well, there are not ten cases of adultery brought up in a year.’

“‘M. l’Abbé preaches for his own saint,’ said Comte Octave; ‘in the East, woman was merely a luxury, regarded as a chattel; no virtues were demanded of her but obedience and beauty. By exalting the soul above the body, modern Europe—daughter of Christ—invented indissoluble marriage and made it a sacrament.’

“‘Ah! the Church saw the difficulties,’ exclaimed M. de Grandville.

“‘This institution has given rise to a new world,’ the Count continued with a smile. ‘But its practices will never match a climate where women marry at seven and are old at twenty‑five. The Catholic Church overlooked half the globe. Let us discuss Europe only.

“‘Is woman our superior or inferior? That is the real question. If woman is inferior, placing her on such a high level as the Church does required severe punishments for adultery. Formerly, cloister or death summed up legislation. But practice has since modified the law. The throne served as a hotbed for adultery, and the growth of this inviting crime marks the decline of Catholic dogmas. Nowadays, where the Church demands only sincere repentance from the erring wife, society is satisfied with a brand instead of execution. The law still condemns the guilty, but it no longer terrifies. In short, there are two moral standards: that of the world and that of the Code. Where the Code is weak, the world is audacious and satirical. Few judges would not gladly have committed the faults they condemn. The world punishes a blunder after encouraging hypocrisy. The entire marriage law seems to require reconstruction. The French law would be perfect if it excluded daughters from inheriting.’

“‘We three know the question thoroughly,’ said Comte de Grandville with a laugh. ‘I have a wife I cannot live with. Serizy has a wife who will not live with him. As for you, Octave, yours ran away from you. So we represent every case of the conjugal conscience, and if divorce is ever brought in again, we shall form the committee.’

“Octave’s fork dropped, breaking his glass and plate. He turned pale and shot a thunderous glare at M. de Grandville, hinting at my presence.

“‘Forgive me, my dear fellow. I did not see Maurice,’ the President continued. ‘Serizy and I, after witnessing your marriage, became your accomplices; I did not think I was committing an indiscretion in front of these venerable priests.’

“M. de Serizy changed the subject, recounting his efforts to please his wife without success. The old man concluded that human sympathies and antipathies cannot be regulated; social law is most perfect when closest to natural law. Nature does not consider the affinities of souls; her aim is the propagation of the species. Thus, the Code, in its current form, wisely leaves latitude to chance. The incapacity of daughters to inherit while male heirs exist is an excellent provision, whether to prevent degeneration or to make households happier by abolishing scandalous unions.

“‘But then,’ he exclaimed, lifting his hand in disgust, ‘how are we to perfect legislation in a country that insists on bringing together seven or eight hundred legislators! After all, if I am sacrificed,’ he added, ‘I have a child to succeed me.’

“‘Setting aside the religious question,’ my uncle remarked, ‘Nature only owes us life; society owes us happiness. Are you a father?’ he asked.

“‘And I—do I have any children?’ said Comte Octave in a hollow voice, making such an impression that the topic of wives and marriage was dropped.

“When coffee was served, the two Counts and the two priests quietly left, noticing that poor Octave had fallen into a melancholy fit, oblivious to their departure. My patron sat in an armchair by the fire, crushed.

“‘You now know the secret of my life,’ he said to me when we were alone. ‘After three years of marriage, I came home one evening to find a letter from the Countess announcing her flight. The letter was dignified; women often retain some virtues even when committing such a sin. The story now is that my wife went abroad on a ship that was wrecked; she is presumed dead. I have lived alone for seven years! Enough for tonight, Maurice. We will discuss my situation when I am ready to speak of it. When suffering from a chronic disease, it takes time to adjust to the idea of improvement. That improvement often seems merely another aspect of the complaint.’

“I went to bed greatly agitated; the mystery seemed more obscure than ever. I anticipated a strange drama, realizing that the events leading the Countess to leave such a noble and loving man must have been extraordinary. M. de Grandville’s remark had illuminated the caverns I had been navigating, and though the light was dim, I could perceive their vastness. I could imagine the Count’s suffering without grasping its depths. His sallow face, parched temples, overwhelming studies, and moments of absentmindedness stood out vividly during the twilight of mental questioning before sleep.

“Oh! how I loved my poor master! He seemed sublime. I read a poem of melancholy, seeing perpetual activity in the heart I had accused of being torpid. Must not supreme grief eventually lead to stagnation? Had this judge, with so much power, ever sought revenge? Was he feeding on her long agony? Is it not remarkable to harbor anger for ten years in Paris? What had Octave done since this great misfortune—when the separation of husband and wife has become a social question?

“We allowed a few days to pass, for great sorrows have their own diffidence; but eventually, one evening, the Count said gravely:

“‘Stay.’

“This, as nearly as may be, is his story.

“My father had a ward, rich and lovely, who was sixteen when I returned from college to live in this old house. Honorine, raised by my mother, was just awakening to life. Full of grace and childish ways, she dreamed of happiness as one dreams of jewels; even religion was poetry to her innocent heart. She viewed the future as a perpetual fête, awaiting marriage without desiring it, her imagination untainted by corruption. We lived together like siblings.

“After a year, I asked her one day in the garden, by the basin where we fed the fish, if she would like to marry me. She told my mother, who insisted we wait eighteen months to see if our feelings remained. When I was twenty-six and Honorine nineteen, we married. Out of respect for my parents we did not modernize the house; I introduced my wife to society as a duty to guide her.

“I later realized marriages like ours often harbor hidden dangers. The husband becomes a teacher, and love can wither under such authority. I trusted her innocence too much. Who can say if empires and happiness are destroyed by too much confidence or too much severity? I recall the self-reproaches I made, like an anatomist seeking a disease’s cause. His merciful indulgence reminded me of Christ’s compassion for the woman taken in adultery.

“It was eighteen months after my father’s death, shortly after my mother passed, that I received Honorine’s farewell letter. What delusion had led her to leave? I was stunned for a month, then chose ignorance, learning too much about such matters through her misfortunes. So far, Maurice, the story is commonplace, but one word changes everything: I love Honorine, and I have never ceased to worship her. Since her departure, I have lived on memories, recalling pleasures she may not have appreciated.

“Do not make a hero of me; I sought no diversion. I was either too young or too in love; I have not met another woman like her. After struggling to forget, I stood on the brink of infidelity, but Honorine’s memory stopped me. I fled temptation, haunted by thoughts of her. My life is a constant paroxysm of fears, joy, and despair.

“I felt no anger towards her, like a father seeing a beloved child in danger. I understood I had idealized my wife, believing she shared my intoxication. An uncritical passion in a husband can lead to a wife’s infidelity. I may have exhausted her with my love before she was ready. Too young to understand that a wife’s constancy is the seed of maternal devotion, she mistook our early marriage for life itself. In her cruel position, she was defenseless against any man who stirred her deeply. I, who should have been wise, failed to grasp the unwritten laws of a woman’s heart until it was too late. I absolved her and condemned myself. Yet love held me like a desperate passion.

“Society laughs at such a predicament. Where it pities a lover, it mocks a husband who cannot keep his wife. I had to remain silent. Serizy is happy; he can see and protect his wife, enjoying the joys of a benefactor. But I have nothing; I live solely on a love that starves. I loathe prostitution and remain faithful under a spell. Without religious faith, I might have killed myself. I threw myself into work, fevered and sleepless.

“You can imagine my feelings when he concluded with this revelation: the interior drama interests nobody; one can only know another’s sorrow vaguely. I calm despair by contemplating a miniature of her, kissing her brow, breathing her fragrance. I have fits of collapse, wild hilarity, and terrors like a murderer meeting a sergeant of police.

“The real drama is this: you think I am occupied with the Council of State, the Chamber, the Courts, Politics. Seven hours at night suffice for that. Honorine is my true concern. To recover my wife is my only study; to guard her without her suspecting my presence, to satisfy the little pleasures she allows herself, to be always near her like a sylph without her knowing it—that is my life. For seven years, I have never gone to bed without checking on her night-lamp or her shadow on the curtains.

“She left my house, taking only the dress she wore that day. Eighteen months later she was deserted by her lover, who was appalled by poverty. Honorine has sixty thousand francs a year of her own. In November 1820, I persuaded the best accoucheur to act as a humble suburban apothecary and induced the parish priest to assist her as an act of charity. Then, to hide my wife, secure her against discovery, and find a devoted housekeeper who would be my confidante—it was a task worthy of Figaro! Finding where my wife had taken refuge was not difficult for me.

“After three months of desperation, I devoted myself to Honorine, with God as my only witness. Love must have its daily food. Should I not protect this child, whose guilt sprang from my imprudence, against further disaster? At seven months, her infant died, happily for her and for me. For nine months she lay between life and death, deserted when she needed a man’s support. But this arm was extended over her head. Honorine was cared for as she would have been in her home. When she recovered and asked who had assisted her, she was told it was the Sisters of Charity, the Maternity Society, and the parish priest.

“This woman, whose pride is almost a vice, has shown resistance in misfortune that I sometimes call obstinacy. Honorine was determined to earn her living. For five years, I have lodged her in the Rue Saint-Maur, in a charming little house where she makes artificial flowers. She believes she sells her elegant creations to a shop, earning twenty francs a day, living as if she had fifteen thousand. She is devoted to flowers, paying a gardener a hundred crowns, which costs me twelve hundred in wages. I promised the gardener a market-garden with a house close to the porter’s lodge in the Rue Saint-Maur, held in the name of a clerk of the law courts. The smallest indiscretion would ruin the gardener’s prospects. Honorine has her little house, a garden, and a splendid hothouse for a rent of five hundred francs a year. She lives under the name of her housekeeper, Madame Gobain, an old woman of impeccable discretion whose affection Honorine has won. For three years, Honorine has been happy, believing she owes her comfort to her own toil.

“Oh! I know what you are about to say,” cried the Count, seeing a question in my eyes. “Yes, I have made the attempt. My wife was living in the Faubourg Saint-Antoine. One day, believing in a chance for reconciliation, I wrote her a letter, which I rewrote twenty times. I will not describe my agonies. I went from the Rue Payenne to the Rue de Reuilly like a condemned wretch going to execution. It was dark and foggy; I went to meet Madame Gobain. Honorine, recognizing my handwriting, threw the letter into the fire without reading it, exclaiming, ‘I leave this tomorrow.’

“What a dagger-stroke to a man who takes pleasure in trickery! I had just succeeded in deceiving my wife—sending her an Indian shawl, presented as the property of an actress, which I had wrapped myself in for a night! My life may be summed up in two words: I love, and I wait! I have in Madame Gobain a faithful spy on the heart I worship. I visit the old woman every evening to hear about Honorine’s day; for a single exclamation might reveal the secrets of that soul which is willfully deaf and dumb. Honorine is pious; she attends church and prays, but she has never confessed or taken Communion, foreseeing what a priest would say. She will not heed advice to return to me. This horror of me overwhelms me, for I have never harmed her. I have always been kind. Even if I was hasty in teaching her, is that a reason for her determination, which only the most implacable hatred could inspire? Honorine has never told Madame Gobain who she is; she keeps absolute silence about her marriage, so the housekeeper cannot speak in my favor. The others know nothing; they live in awe of the Prefect of Police and respect the power of a Minister. Hence, it is impossible for me to penetrate her heart; the citadel is mine, but I cannot enter it. I have no means of action. An act of violence would ruin me forever.

“How can I argue against reasons of which I know nothing? Should I write a letter, have it copied by a public writer, and laid before Honorine? But that risks a third removal, which cost me fifty thousand francs. The last purchase was made in the name of the secretary who succeeded you. The unfortunate man, unaware of my vigilance, was caught opening a box containing a private agreement. I coughed, and he panicked; the next day, I forced him to sell the house to the man in whose name it now stands, and I turned him out.

“If I did not feel my noblest faculties satisfied, happy, and expansive; if I were not playing the role of divine fatherhood; if I did not drink in delight by every pore, I might believe I was a monomaniac. Sometimes at night, I hear the jingling bells of madness. I dread the violent transitions from feeble hope to complete despair. Recently, I considered the tragic end of Lovelace and Clarissa Harlowe, thinking that if Honorine were the mother of my child, she must return under her husband’s roof.

“I have such complete faith in a happy future that ten months ago, I bought one of the finest houses in the Faubourg Saint-Honoré. If I win back Honorine, I will not allow her to see this house again, nor the room from which she fled. I intend to place my idol in a new temple, where she may feel that life is altogether new. That house is being transformed into a marvel of elegance and taste. I was inspired by a poet who, mad with love for an actress, bought the finest bed in Paris without knowing how she would reward his passion. Well, one of the coldest lawyers, thought to be the gravest adviser of the Crown, was stirred to the depths of his heart by that anecdote. The orator of the Legislative Chamber can understand the poet who fed his ideal on material possibilities. Three days before Maria Louisa’s arrival, Napoleon threw himself on his wedding bed at Compiègne. All great passions share the same impulses. I love as a poet—like an emperor!”

As I heard the last words, I believed Count Octave’s fears were realized; he rose, walking and gesticulating, but stopped, shocked by the vehemence of his own words.

“I am very ridiculous,” he added after a long pause, looking at me, as if craving pity.

“No, monsieur, you are very unhappy.”

“Ah yes!” he said, resuming his confidences. “From the violence of my speech, you must believe in the intensity of a physical passion that has absorbed me for nine years; but that is nothing compared to the worship I feel for her soul, mind, and heart—all the divinities of Love. By retrospection, I now see the graces of Honorine’s mind and heart I overlooked in happiness—like all who are happy. Day by day, I appreciate my loss, discovering the exquisite gifts of that capricious and proud young creature who has grown strong under poverty and the cowardly shock of desertion. And that heavenly blossom is fading in solitude and hiding! The law we discussed is a squad of gendarmes—my wife seized and dragged away by force! Would that not be to triumph over a corpse? Religion has no hold on her; she craves its poetry, prays, but does not heed the Church’s commandments. I have exhausted all mercy, kindness, and love; I am at my wits’ end. Only one chance of victory remains: the cunning and patience of bird-catchers who finally trap the wariest birds. Hence, Maurice, when M. de Grandville’s indiscretion revealed my life’s secret to you, I regarded it as fate’s decree, one of the utterances gamblers listen for in their most impassioned play. Have you enough affection for me to show me romantic devotion?”

“I see what you are coming to, Monsieur le Comte,” I interrupted. “Your first secretary tried to open your deed box. I know your second’s heart—he might fall in love with your wife. Can you send him into the fire? Can anyone put their hand into a brazier without burning it?”

“You are a foolish boy,” replied the Count. “I will send you well-gloved. It is not my secretary who will be lodged in the Rue Saint-Maur in the little garden-house I have at his disposal. It is my distant cousin, Baron de l’Hostal, a lawyer high in office…”

After a moment of silent surprise, the gate bell rang and a carriage entered. The footman announced Madame de Courteville and her daughter. The Count had family connections on his mother’s side. Madame de Courteville, a widow, had a daughter and no fortune. What could a woman of nine-and-twenty be compared to a lovely young girl of twenty, ideal for a mistress?

“Baron, and Master of Appeals, until you get something better, and this old house settled on her—would you not have enough reasons not to fall in love with the Countess?” he whispered as he introduced us.

I was dazzled, not so much by advantages I had never dreamed of, but by Amelie de Courteville, whose beauty was adorned by a mother’s artful clothing.

“But I will not talk of myself,” said the Consul after a pause.

Three weeks later, I moved into the gardener’s cottage, cleaned, repaired, and furnished with the celerity explained by three words: Paris; French workmen; money! I was as much in love as the Count could desire as security. Would the prudence of a young man of five-and-twenty be equal to the part I was undertaking, involving a friend’s happiness? I confess I counted on my uncle’s advice; for I had been authorized by the Count to confide in him if necessary. I engaged a garden, devoted myself to horticulture, and worked frantically, turning up the soil and appropriating it to flower cultivation. Like the maniacs of England or Holland, I claimed devotion to one kind of flower, especially dahlias. My conduct, even in small details, was dictated by the Count, whose intellect focused on the tragi-comedy enacted in the Rue Saint-Maur. As soon as the Countess went to bed, around eleven, Octave, Madame Gobain, and I held council. I heard the old woman’s report on Honorine’s daily activities, meals, frame of mind, and plans. Octave lived only for that hour.

For two months, while I worked in the garden, I never saw the little house where my fair neighbor lived. I had not even inquired whether I had a neighbor, though the Countess’ garden was divided from mine by a paling, along which she had planted cypress trees already four feet high. One morning, Madame Gobain announced that an eccentric neighbor intended to build a wall between the two gardens at the year’s end. I will say nothing of the curiosity that consumed me to see the Countess! My wish almost extinguished my budding love for Amelie de Courteville. My scheme for building a wall was indeed a dangerous threat: no fresh air for Honorine, whose garden would then be a narrow alley. This dwelling, once a summer villa, was like a house of cards; the garden front imitated a trellis with flowers up to the second floor, a charming Pompadour style. My wall would cut away three-quarters of the plot.

“The Countess was in despair.

“‘My good Gobain,’ she said, ‘what sort of man is this florist?’

“‘He seems to dislike women,’ said the housekeeper. ‘He is the nephew of a Paris cure. It is quite possible that this priest encourages his nephew in his love of flowers, to prevent anything worse…’

“‘What do you mean?’

“‘Well,’ said Gobain, ‘he is a little cracked!’

“Now a harmless lunatic is the only man no woman ever distrusts in matters of sentiment. You will see how wise the Count was in choosing this disguise for me.

“‘What ails him then?’ asked the Countess.

“‘He has studied too hard,’ replied Gobain; ‘he is misanthropic and has his reasons for disliking women…’

“‘Madmen frighten me less than sane folks; I will speak to him myself! Tell him that I beg him to come here. If I do not succeed, I will send for the cure.’

“The day after this conversation, as I walked along my graveled path, I caught sight of the half-opened curtains and a woman’s face peering out. Madame Gobain called me. I hastily glanced at the Countess’ house, shrugging rudely to express indifference.

“‘Madame,’ said Gobain, ‘the madman bid me leave him in peace, saying that even a charcoal seller is master in his own premises, especially when he has no wife.’

“‘He is perfectly right,’ said the Countess.

“‘Yes, but he ended by saying, “I will go,” when I told him that he would greatly distress a lady living in retirement, who found her greatest solace in growing flowers.’

“Next day, a signal from Gobain informed me that I was expected. After the Countess’ breakfast, when she was walking in front of her house, I broke some palings and approached her. I had dressed like a countryman, in old gray flannel trousers, heavy wooden shoes, a shabby shooting coat, a peaked cap, a ragged bandana, and hands soiled with mold.

“‘Madame,’ said the housekeeper, ‘this good man is your neighbor.’

“The Countess was not alarmed. At last, I saw the woman whose conduct and my husband’s confidences had made me so curious to meet. It was early May. The air was pure, the weather serene; the fragrance of spring formed a setting for this creature of sorrow. As I saw Honorine, I understood Octave’s passion: ‘A heavenly flower!’

“Her pallor struck me with its unique tone of white—there are as many tones of white as of red or blue. At the slightest emotion, blood mounted under the surface in rosy flushes. The sunshine through the acacia cast a pale gold glow around Honorine, like the ambient glory Raphael and Titian gave the Virgin. Her brown eyes expressed tenderness and vivacity, their brightness reflected through long downcast lashes. Merely lifting her eyelids, Honorine could cast a spell; she could freeze or give life with a look. Her light-brown hair framed a high, dreamy brow. The mouth was voluptuous. Her head bore a stamp of nobility that would defy the outrages of time.

“Though slight, Honorine was not thin; her figure could revive love when it believed itself exhausted. She embodied the idea of mignonne, one of those pliant women who allow themselves to be petted. Her small feet made a light sound on the gravel, harmonizing with the rustle of her dress, producing a feminine music distinct from others. Her gait bore the pride of her race; even a working man would make way. Gay and tender, haughty and imposing, she combined incompatible qualities yet remained a child. But this child could be as strong as an angel; once hurt, she would be implacable.

“Coldness on that face must be death to those on whom her eyes had smiled, for whom her lips had parted, whose souls had drunk the melody of her voice. Inhaling the perfume of violets that accompanied her, I understood how the memory of this wife had arrested the Count on the threshold of debauchery. Honorine inspired devotion, chivalrous devotion, regardless of reward. A man seeing her must think of offering his life to procure one day’s happiness for her.

“Many women study their expressions; in her it was the outcome of a delightful nature that went straight to the heart. If I tell you all this, it is because her soul, thoughts, and exquisite heart are what we are concerned with, and you would have blamed me had I not sketched them for you.

“I was near forgetting my part as a half-crazy lout, clumsy and by no means chivalrous.

“‘I am told, madame, that you are fond of flowers?’

“‘I am an artificial flower-maker,’ she said. ‘After growing flowers, I imitate them, like a mother painting her children… That shows I am poor and unable to pay for the concession I long to obtain from you.’

“‘But how,’ said I, grave as a judge, ‘can a lady of such rank ply so humble a calling? Have you, like me, good reasons for employing your fingers to keep your brains from working?’

“‘Let us stick to the question of the wall,’ she said with a smile.

“‘Why, we have begun at the foundations,’ said I. ‘Must I not know which of us ought to yield to the other in behalf of our suffering, or, if you choose, our mania?—Oh! what a charming clump of narcissus!’

“She had created a perfect museum of flowers and shrubs, arranged as only the sun might see, prompted by artistic genius; even the most heartless landlord must have treated it with respect. The garden breathed comforting scents, suggesting only sweet thoughts. I looked alternately at the mass of narcissus and at the Countess, pretending to be far more in love with the flowers than with her, to maintain my part.

“‘So you are very fond of flowers?’ she said.

“‘They are,’ I replied, ‘the only beings that never disappoint our care and affection.’ I spoke at length comparing botany and the world, and we ended miles away from the dividing wall, which the Countess naturally brought us back to; for women, when not in love, have all the cold blood of an experienced attorney.

“‘If you insist on my leaving the paling,’ said I, ‘you will learn all the secrets of gardening I want to hide; I am seeking a blue dahlia, a blue rose; I am crazy for blue flowers. Is not blue the favorite color of superior souls? We are neither of us really at home; we might as well make a little door of open railings to unite our gardens… You, too, are fond of flowers; you will see mine, I shall see yours. If you receive no visitors at all, I have none but my uncle, the Cure of the White Friars.’

“‘No,’ said she, ‘I will give you the right to come into my garden at any hour. Come and welcome; you will be admitted as a neighbor with whom I hope to keep good terms. But I like my solitude too well to burden it with any loss of independence.’

“‘As you please,’ said I, and with one leap I was over the paling.

“For a fortnight I seemed to take no heed of my neighbor. Towards the end of May, one lovely evening, we were out on opposite sides of the paling, both walking slowly. Having reached the end, we exchanged a few civil words; she found me in deep dejection and spoke of hopefulness in brief sentences like lullabies. I then leaped the fence and found myself at her side again. The Countess led me into the house, wishing to soothe my sadness. At last, I had penetrated the sanctuary where everything harmonized with the woman I have tried to describe.

“Exquisite simplicity reigned there. The little house was a dainty box of eighteenth-century art. The dining-room was painted in fresco with flower garlands; the staircase was charmingly decorated in monochrome. The little drawing-room was faded but hung with panels of tapestry; upstairs was one bedroom, a dressing-room, and a library used as her workroom. The kitchen was in the basement, raised by several steps outside. Only the lead cornices were visible. In this retreat, one was a hundred leagues from Paris.

“But for the bitter smile that occasionally played on her lips, one might have believed the violet buried in her thicket of flowers was happy. In a few days we reached a degree of intimacy born of proximity and the Countess’ conviction that I was indifferent to women. A look would have spoiled everything, and I never allowed a thought of her to be seen in my eyes. Honorine regarded me as an old friend. Her manner stemmed from a kind of pity. Her looks, voice, and words showed she was a hundred miles away from coquetry. She soon granted me the right to enter her pretty workshop, full of books and curiosities, as smart as a boudoir where elegance emphasized the vulgarity of her tools. The Countess had poetized a thing far from poetry—a manufacture.

“Perhaps of all the work a woman can do, making artificial flowers allows her the most grace. For coloring prints one must bend over a table; embroidery entails consumption; engraving requires minute care. But making flowers and light articles permits a pretty woman to remain herself—chatting, laughing, singing, thinking.

“There was certainly an artistic feeling in how the Countess arranged petals on a long table. The saucers of color were white china, arranged so the eye could see each shade. A little cabinet with a hundred drawers held the steel molds; a Japanese bowl held the paste. Under her eyes, in a Venetian glass shaped like a flower-cup, was the living model she strove to imitate. She had a passion for achievement; she attempted the most difficult things—tiny corollas, wildflowers—in movements like an accomplished pianist. Her fingers seemed fairies, infinite in the actions of twisting, fitting, and pressure.

“I could not tire of admiring her shape a flower. She displayed the genius of a painter in bold attempts; she copied faded flowers and yellowing leaves. ‘This art,’ she said, ‘is in its infancy. If the women of Paris had a little of the genius of the harem, they would lend a complete language of flowers to the wreaths they wear. To please my taste as an artist, I have made drooping flowers with leaves the hue of Florentine bronze, such as are found before or after winter. Would not such a crown on the head of a young woman whose life is a failure have a certain poetical fitness? How many things a woman might express by her head-dress! Are there not flowers for drunken Bacchantes, flowers for gloomy and stern bigots, pensive flowers for bored women? Botany can express every sensation and thought of the soul.’

“She employed me to stamp out leaves, cut material, and prepare wires. My affected desire for occupation made me skilled. We talked as we worked. When idle I read new books to her, to keep up my part as a man weary of life, gloomy and soured. My person led to adorable banter about my resemblance—except for his club foot—to Lord Byron. It was tacitly acknowledged that her troubles far outweighed mine, though the causes I assigned for my misanthropy might have satisfied Young or Job.

“I will say nothing of the shame that tormented me as I inflicted false wounds on my heart to excite this enchanting woman’s compassion. Her renunciation of love, her dread of what is called happiness, she proclaimed with vehemence and candor. These happy days proved to me that a woman’s friendship is far superior to her love.

“I suffered the revelations of my sorrows to be dragged from me with as many grimaces as a young lady allows before the piano, so conscious of the annoyance to follow. The Countess found in me such an exact counterpart of her antipathy to love that I fancied she was content with the chance that had brought a sort of Man Friday to her desert island. Solitude weighed on her. At the same time there was nothing of the coquette in her; nothing remained of a woman; she did not feel she had a heart, except in the ideal world where she found refuge. I compared the two lives—his, all activity and emotion; hers, all inaction and quiescence. My misanthropy allowed cynical sallies against both sexes, hoping to bring Honorine to confidence; but she was not to be caught, and I began to understand that mulish obstinacy is common among women.

“‘The Orientals are right,’ I said one evening, ‘when they shut you up and regard you merely as playthings. Europe has been punished for admitting you to society on an equal footing. Woman is the most dishonorable and cowardly being to be found. Nay, and that is where her charm lies. Once a woman has inspired a man’s passion, she is sacred to him forever; but to you women, the man you loved is as nothing; more, you feel the popular calumny that, when you have had your fill, nothing should survive but the remembrance of pleasure.’

“‘God has, no doubt, reserved such perfect bliss for Paradise,’ she said. ‘But if your argument seems witty to you, it has the disadvantage of being false. What can those women be who give themselves up to a succession of loves?’ she asked, looking at me as the Virgin in Ingres’ picture looks at Louis XIII offering his kingdom.

“‘You are an actress in good faith,’ said I. ‘Still, lovely as you are, you have loved; ergo, you forget.’

“‘I!’ she exclaimed, evading my question, ‘I am not a woman. I am a nun, and seventy-two years old!’

“‘Then, how can you so positively assert that you feel more keenly than I? Sorrow has but one form for women. The only misfortunes they regard are disappointments of the heart.’

“She looked at me sweetly and persisted in her willfulness.

“‘I am a nun,’ she said, ‘and you talk to me of the world where I shall never again set foot.’

“‘Not even in thought?’ I said.

“‘Is the world so much to be desired?’ she replied. ‘Oh! when my mind wanders, it goes higher. The angel of perfection, the beautiful angel Gabriel, often sings in my heart. If I were rich, I should work all the same, to keep me from soaring on the many-tinted wings of the angel and wandering in fancy. There are meditations that ruin us women! I owe much peace to my flowers, though sometimes they fail to occupy me. I feel some great event is impending; my life is about to change; I listen vaguely; I have no liking for my work, and after fatigues I find everyday life once more. Is this a warning from heaven? I ask myself…’

“After three months of this struggle between two diplomats—costumed melancholy and a woman whose disgust for life made her invulnerable—I told the Count it was impossible to draw this tortoise from her shell; it must be broken. The evening before, Honorine had exclaimed: ‘Lucretia’s dagger wrote in letters of blood the watchword of woman’s charter: Liberty!’ From that moment, the Count left me free to act.

“‘I have been paid a hundred francs for the flowers and caps I made this week!’ Honorine exclaimed gleefully one Saturday evening when I visited her in the ground-floor sitting-room, which the unavowed proprietor had had regilt.

“It was ten o’clock. The twilight of July and a glorious moon lent us misty light. Gusts of mingled perfumes soothed the soul; the Countess jingled the five gold pieces given to her by a supposititious dealer in fashionable frippery, another of Octave’s accomplices.

“‘I earn my living by amusing myself,’ she said; ‘I am free, when men, armed with their laws, have tried to make us slaves. I have transports of pride every Saturday! I like M. Gaudissart’s gold pieces as much as your double liked Mr. Murray’s.’

“‘This is not becoming in a woman,’ said I.

“‘Pooh! Am I a woman? I am a boy with a soft soul, that is all; a boy whom no woman can torture…’

“‘Your life is the negation of your whole being,’ I replied. ‘What? You, on whom God lavished His choicest treasures of love and beauty, do you never wish—’

“‘For what?’ said she, disturbed by a speech that contradicted my assumed role.

“‘For a pretty little child, with curling hair, running among the flowers, calling you mother!’

“A prolonged silence followed. Leaning on her sofa, the Countess did not faint, but froze under a nervous attack, feeling like the influence of an insidious poison. I called Madame Gobain, who led her mistress away, laid her on her bed, unlaced and undressed her, restoring not life, but consciousness of dreadful suffering. I walked the path behind the house, weeping and doubting my success. I wished to abandon the part of the bird-catcher I had assumed. Madame Gobain found me with my face wet with tears and hastily went up to say to the Countess: ‘What has happened, madame? Monsieur Maurice is crying like a child.’

“Roused to action by the evil interpretation that might be put on our behavior, she summoned strength to put on a wrapper and come down to me.

“‘You are not the cause of this attack,’ she said. ‘I am subject to these spasms, a sort of cramp of the heart…’

“‘And will you not tell me of your troubles?’ I asked, wiping my tears. ‘Have you not just now told me that you have been a mother and so unhappy as to lose your child?’

“‘Marie!’ she called as she rang the bell. Gobain came in.

“‘Bring lights and some tea,’ said she, with calm decision.

“When the housekeeper had lit the tapers and closed the shutters, the Countess showed a mute countenance; her indomitable pride and gravity had reasserted mastery. She said:

“‘Do you know why I like Lord Byron so much? It is because he suffered as animals do. Of what use are complaints when they are not an elegy like Manfred’s, nor bitter mockery like Don Juan’s, nor a reverie like Childe Harold’s? Nothing shall be known of me. My heart is a poem that I lay before God.’

“‘If I chose—’ said I.

“‘If?’ she repeated.

“‘I have no interest in anything,’ I replied, ‘so I cannot be inquisitive; but if I chose, I could know all your secrets by tomorrow.’

“‘I defy you!’ she exclaimed, uneasy.

“‘Seriously?’

“‘Certainly,’ she said, tossing her head. ‘If such a crime is possible, I ought to know it.’

“‘In the first place, madame,’ I went on, pointing to her hands, ‘were those pretty fingers made for toil? Then you call yourself Madame Gobain, you who, on receiving a letter, said to Marie: “Here, this is for you?” Marie is the real Madame Gobain; so you conceal your name behind that of your housekeeper. Fear nothing, madame, from me. You have in me the most devoted friend you will ever have: friend, do you understand me? I give this word its sacred and pathetic meaning, so profaned in France. Your friend, who will defend you against everything, only wishes your happiness. Who can tell whether the pain I have involuntarily caused you was not a voluntary act?’

“‘Yes,’ she replied with threatening audacity, ‘I insist on it. Be curious, and tell me all that you can find out about me; but,’ and she held up her finger, ‘you must also tell me by what means you obtain your information. The preservation of the small happiness I enjoy here depends on the steps you take.’

“‘That means you will fly—’

“‘On wings!’ she cried, ‘to the New World—’

“‘Where you will be at the mercy of the brutal passions you inspire,’ I interrupted. ‘Is it not the essence of genius and beauty to shine and attract men’s gaze? Paris is a desert with Bedouins; it is the only place where those who must work can hide. What have you to complain of? Who am I? An additional servant—M. Gobain, that is all. If you have to fight a duel, you may need a second.’

“‘Never mind; find out who I am. I have already said that I insist. Now, I beg that you will,’ she said with the grace you ladies command.

“‘Well, then, tomorrow, at the same hour, I will tell you what I may have discovered,’ I replied. ‘But do not therefore hate me! Will you behave like other women?’

“‘What do other women do?’

“‘They lay upon us immense sacrifices, and when we have made them, they reproach us for it later as if it were an injury.’

“‘They are right if the thing required appears to be a sacrifice!’ she replied pointedly.

“‘Instead of sacrifices, say efforts and—’

“‘It would be an impertinence,’ said she.

“‘Forgive me,’ said I. ‘I forget that woman and the Pope are infallible.’

“‘Good heavens!’ she said after a pause, ‘only two words would be enough to destroy the peace so dearly bought, and which I enjoy like a fraud—’

“She rose and paid no further heed to me.

“‘Where can I go?’ she said. ‘What is to become of me? Must I leave this quiet retreat, which I arranged with such care to end my days in?’

“‘To end your days!’ I exclaimed with alarm. ‘Has it never struck you that a time would come when you could no longer work, when competition will lower the price of flowers and articles of fashion—?’

“‘I have already saved a thousand crowns,’ she said.

“‘Heavens! what privations such a sum must represent!’ I exclaimed.

“‘Leave me till tomorrow. This evening I am not myself; I must be alone. Must I not save my strength in case of disaster? If you should learn anything, others besides you would be informed, and then—Good-night,’ she added shortly, dismissing me with an imperious gesture.

“The battle is to-morrow, then,” I said with a smile, trying to seem indifferent. As I walked down the avenue the phrase kept ringing in my ears.

Octave shared Honorine’s agitation. The Count and I paced together until two in the morning, near the Bastille’s trenches like generals awaiting a decisive hour. We lay awake in opposite moods—one hopeful, the other dreading reunion—aware that real drama lay less in events than in feeling, in that spiritual world where Octave and Honorine existed as lofty beings.

The next evening I arrived at ten and entered Honorine’s pale blue-and-white bedroom—her sanctuary. She seemed about to speak but fell silent at my respectful manner.

“Madame la Comtesse,” I said gravely.

She sank back, wrapped in grief with a pose that might have pleased a painter.

“You are the wife of a noble, respected man,” I went on. “You and he are lofty natures. Do you know where you live?”

“In my own house,” she answered, astonished.

“In Count Octave’s,” I corrected. “You have been deceived. M. Lenormand is only a front. The seclusion, the income, the protections around you—all come from him. He has explained your disappearance plausibly; he even hopes you escaped the Cécile’s wreck. He hides you; his devotion has lasted seven years.”

She trembled, defiant; her dry eyes eventually yielded helpless tears. She had believed herself free—marriage weighed on her like a prison.

“I will go!” she cried. “He forces me to it; I will go where no one will come after me.”

“Kill yourself?” I asked. “You must have powerful reasons not to return.”

“Certainly I have!” she said.

“Then tell me, tell my uncle. We will find a solution. Your soul is pure; if you have sinned it is expiated. If you wish to evade the Count’s tyranny I will help; he shall never find you.”

“Oh! there is always a convent!” she said.

“Yes, but the Count, as Minister, could bar you from many. Yet I will save you—if you prove you cannot and ought not to return. Do not fear fall from his power into mine. You shall have peace and solitude.”

“And how? By what means?” she asked.

“That is my secret. Prove this life is impossible for you, and I will decide in your favor.”

She insisted that no man could understand the scruples of her conscience; only God. Another man had once had all her love—there could never be two loves for her. “I esteem, respect, venerate him,” she said of Octave, “but I can never love him. I had but one heart. My husband had the girl; a worthless lover had the woman—there is nothing left! I will not be a prostitute.”

“You love him,” I observed.

“I esteem him. I respect him. I will not return but to a convent. I could not bear to live with the perpetual memory of my sin. Marriage would make me guilty before him; his generosity would make my shame evident. I would kill him by coldness and comparisons; I might die the victim of his impatience. No position in that life but hides a snare. For five years I have wandered in a sandy desert of the future; my soul is possessed by repentance. Religion tells me to accept suffering as punishment; I lack the energy to be comforted by that. If I were a girl now I should choose him; that is why I refuse—because I cannot be better treated because of my sin. One must be either loved cleanly or not at all.”

I tried to show reason: at thirty, attractive, with hidden powers, she might live elsewhere. Her devotion of nine years to the Count served as a guarantee for her tranquillity. I urged her to confide with my uncle, a priest of weight equal to a minister, gentle as Fenelon, who would understand.

She was suffocated by the choice. I left at midnight and found the Count nearby; he had been drawn to the spot by irresistible attraction.

“What a night my poor child will go through!” he exclaimed when I told him. “Suppose I went to her—what if she saw me suddenly?”

“At this moment she might throw herself from the window,” I answered. “She is one of those Lucretias.”

“You are young,” he said; “you don’t know that a soul tossed by such choices has a will like a storm-lashed lake; the wind may drive it to either shore.”

“And you would accept the equal chances?” I asked.

“Well, come,” he said; “I have an opium draught Desplein prepared to send me to sleep without risk.”

Next day at noon Gobain brought a letter: Honorine had gone to bed at six, exhausted, and after taking a chemist’s draught had fallen asleep. He gave me a copy of the letter she had written in anguish. Its main points were these: she knew conscience and civil and religious law told her to reconcile with Octave; society and marriage would ratify forgiveness, and she ought to sacrifice her egoism for family. But she said she could not live with the memory of her sin. She would rather die than be the wife who cannot love. A widow might remarry and forget, but she could not love Octave; every meeting of eyes would revive her guilt. Marriage would force a hypocritical life of feigned joy; she could not accept the degradation. The child she had borne and nursed for seven months—the memory—would haunt any future children. Her suffering was her expiation; she saw only two hells—the solitude she chose, and the hell under Count Octave’s roof. God might give strength to others; she had not that energy. She asked me to tell where to find solitude, peace, and silence.

After delivering the letter I went to Rue Payenne. Anxiety had conquered the opium. Octave paced his garden like a madman. I gave him the letter.

“She is mine!” he cried, joy lighting his face. He waved me away; I understood how extreme happiness and extreme pain obey the same law. Later I saw Madame de Courteville and Amelie dining with the Count. Seeing Mademoiselle de Courteville beside him made me compare kinds of love: Honorine’s tragic, demanding love seemed more sublime than Amelie’s peaceful constancy; the former called for special generosity, the latter settled into comfortable motherhood. At twenty-five I did not trust myself; I resolved to do my part.

I returned to the Count to announce his relatives’ arrival and saw him grow young at hope. He asked me why I looked changed.

“Monsieur le Comte—” I began.

“No longer Octave?” he said; “you, to whom I shall owe my life, my happiness—”

“My dear Octave, if you bring the Countess back I have studied her—she must never see me again; she must never know that Maurice was your secretary. Never mention my name to her, or all will be undone... You got me an appointment as Maître des Requêtes—get me instead a diplomatic post abroad, and do not think of my marrying Amelie—Oh! do not be uneasy,” I added quickly. “I will play my part to the end.”

“Poor boy!” he said, holding my hand.

We agreed on the evening’s role, and I returned to Honorine’s. It was August; a hot storm had left the air heavy. She sat beneath an arbor in a loose white muslin dress. She did not rise but motioned me to sit.

“Now, is not life deadlocked for me?” she asked.

“Life as you have made it,” I answered. “But not the life I propose; if you choose you may be very happy...”

“How?” she asked.

“Your letter is in the Count’s hands.”

She started, then came inside. “You—a friend? Say rather a traitor! A spy sent by my husband.”

Women’s instinct is as sharp as men’s perspicacity. I told her there was but one man who could answer such a letter; she must read his reply. I had forwarded her letter through my uncle and asked him what he would reply if his wife wrote thus. In presence of that priest and me she must read the Count’s answer out of respect for dignity, law, and God.

My uncle’s entrance into that little Pompadour drawing-room at ten was formidable. His silver hair and calm face soothed Honorine. He handed her the Count’s letter. It said, essentially: if she had trusted him and read a letter he’d written five years before, she could have avoided long privations; he blamed himself for failing to sense danger and begged mercy. He proposed a new life: not a forced conjugal passion but a relation of brotherly tenderness, a home in Faubourg Saint-Honoré where she would be unassailed, occupied as she pleased, with legitimate protection and fortune for good works. He did not want to force her by Church or law; he wanted her consent. He asked only to watch over her and to be permitted the tender care of a father or brother, making life equable and genial. If that compact did not suit her, he would accept her refusal and free her completely.

Honorine folded the letter to her bosom and thanked my uncle, saying she would take the Count’s permission to remain where she was. My involuntary cry of “Ah!” betrayed me; she had been testing whether I was actor or spy. She read my emotion as love and answered with a mischievous glance: “Ah, Maurice, you know how to love.”

My uncle signaled me to leave. On the threshold Honorine took my hand, pressed it, said “We shall meet again...”

“No,” I replied, wringing her hand so she cried out. “You love your husband. I leave tomorrow.”

I rushed away. My uncle explained my agitation as a touch of madness; I did not contest the explanation.

Six days later I set out as vice-consul to a Spanish commercial town. After I had established myself Octave sent a letter:

If he were happy he would not write, but he was in a new life of suffering. When I left, he had not yet been received at the pavilion, though a letter promised. After waiting a month, he called; Honorine had had to dress. At last, overcome with trembling, he told her he loved her; she replied that there was no crime in loving one’s wife. He urged her to give up toil; she had sixty thousand francs a year of her own and should not squander it. He offered certificates worth twelve thousand francs each—symbolic gifts of freedom. She accepted, and with a look he understood he had offered not money but emancipation. “I am conquered,” she said, holding out her hand, which he kissed.

For two months a springtime of love gave him bliss; she was no longer afraid; she studied him. When he proposed going to England to resume rank and life in the new house she was alarmed. “Why not live always as we are?” she asked. He feared he might force a consent that was not full. She finally consented in a manner he could accept: she would return as his wife when he wished, but she warned that resignation was not the love he sought. “I do love you,” she told him, “but not as you wish. I love your soul... I love you enough to die in your service without regret. It will be my expiation.” She knelt and added, “And perhaps I shall not die!” For two months he struggled within.

I did not reply to that letter. Two months later the papers announced the Comtesse Octave’s return on an English vessel, restored after contrived adventures. When I moved to Genoa I received formal notice of the birth of a son to the Count and Countess. I sat two hours holding that news on a bench. Two months later, urged by Octave, by M. de Grandville, by M. de Serizy, and broken by my uncle’s death, I agreed to marry.

Six months after the Revolution of July a final letter concluded the story. Honorine wrote that she was dying—though she was a mother—and declared she had played her part as wife, deceiving her husband, and had had a happiness as genuine as an actress’ tears. She confessed to the “malady of memory”: she could never twice feel that identity of love which unites a woman with a man. To the last she cried in the confessional and to her husband, “Have mercy!” but there was none. She nursed the saintly uncle in his last illness; he showed her the way to heaven and bade her persevere in duty. Yet memory consumed her. Although the doctors ascribed death to a plausible complaint, she knew it was the strain of acting a life without love that was killing her. She asked me to be her little Count’s guardian and left a codicil with her wish. “Do not mourn for me,” she said; “I have been dead this many a day if Saint Bernard was right that where there is no love there is no life.”

“And the Countess died,” said the Consul, closing the pocket-book.

“Is the Count still living?” asked the Ambassador; since July he had vanished from politics.

“Do you remember seeing me go to the steamboat with—” the Consul-General asked.

“A white-haired man? an old man?” the painter interrupted.

“An old man of forty-five, going to Southern Italy for health and amusement. That man was my poor friend and patron, passing through Genoa to take leave of me and to place his will in my hands. He appointed me his son’s guardian. I had no need to tell him of Honorine’s wishes.”

“Does he suspect himself of murder?” Mademoiselle des Touches asked the Baron de l’Hostal.

“He suspects the truth,” replied the Consul, “and that suspicion is killing him. I stayed on board the steam packet until it departed; a small boat brought me back. We sat a long time taking leave—for ever, I fear. God only knows how much we love those in whose hands we place the confidant of our love when she who inspired it is no more.

“The Count went to the prow and looked down at the Mediterranean. ‘Ought we not, in the interests of human nature, to inquire what irresistible power leads us to sacrifice an exquisite creature to the most fugitive of pleasures, and in spite of reason? In my conscience I heard cries. Honorine was not alone in her anguish. And yet I would have it!... I am consumed by remorse. In the Rue Payenne I was dying of joys not had; now I shall die in Italy of joys I have had... Wherein lay the discord between two equally noble natures, I dare assert?’”

A profound silence fell on the terrace. The Consul turned to the two women and asked, “Was she virtuous?”

Mademoiselle des Touches rose, took his arm, and said, “Are not men wrong too when they marry a young girl while cherishing an angelic image. They compare us to unknown rivals and find us wanting?”

“Mademoiselle, you would be right if marriage were based on passion; and that was the mistake of those two, who will soon be no more. Marriage with mutual, heart-deep love would be Paradise.”

Mademoiselle des Touches turned and Claude Vignon whispered: “A bit of a coxcomb is M. de l’Hostal.”

“No,” she replied; “he has not yet guessed that Honorine would have loved him.—Oh!” she cried, seeing the Consul’s wife approaching, “his wife was listening! Unhappy man!”

At eleven the guests walked home along the seashore. Mademoiselle des Touches reflected that Honorine was rare, perhaps the most extraordinary exception in intellect—a pearl—and that for such souls solitude and prayer might suffice. Leon de Lora admired the idea of a woman who could live by a studio and withdraw from the world. Claude Vignon scoffed; the Ambassador recalled other similar tragedies. Camille Maupin, leaning on the quay’s balustrade, stood silent, pensively admiring a woman who had lived and died sustained by a memory no law could touch.
