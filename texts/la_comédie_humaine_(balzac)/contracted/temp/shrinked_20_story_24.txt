                             DEDICATION

  To Mademoiselle Anna Hanska:

  Dear Child,--You, the joy of the household, you, whose pink or
  white pelerine flutters in summer among the groves of
  Wierzschovnia like a will-o’-the-wisp, followed by the tender eyes
  of your father and your mother,--how can I dedicate to _you_ a
  story full of melancholy? And yet, ought not sorrows to be spoken
  of to a young girl idolized as you are, since the day may come
  when your sweet hands will be called to minister to them? It is
  difficult, Anna, to find in the history of our manners and morals
  a subject that is worthy of your eyes, that no choice has been
  left me; but perhaps you will be made to feel how fortunate your
  fate is when you read the story sent to you by
                                                    Your old friend,
                                                         De Balzac.

PIERRETTE

I. THE LORRAINS

At dawn one October day in 1827 a sixteen-year-old youth, plainly dressed, stood in a small square of Lower Provins. The mills along the river already whirred; their wheels, echoed from the Upper Town, deepened the morning silence so that even a distant diligence seemed near. The two long sides of the oblong square, separated by a linden avenue, bore the simple fronts of bourgeois life—few porte-cochères, little commerce; vines and roses climbed to second-story windows, scenting the air. One end opened on the main street, gardens touching the river; the other opened onto a street parallel to it.

At that quieter end the lad recognized the house he sought: a restored front of white grooved stone, closed gray blinds, slender iron balconies with yellow rosettes. Three dormer windows pierced the slate roof, the central one capped with a weather-vane of a hunter shooting a hare. Three stone steps led to the door; a leaden pipe poured sink-water into a gutter on one side, showing the kitchen; on the other, two closely shuttered windows with heart-shaped openings admitted light to the dining room. Vent-holes to the cellar had painted iron shutters cut in open-work. Everything looked new—too neat—suggesting the paltry taste and self-satisfaction of a retired petty shopkeeper.

The youth took pleasure tinged with sadness in noting these details; his eyes roved from the kitchen to the roof. A calico curtain at one garret window caught the rosy glow of the rising sun; the other attics lacked that sign of comfort, and his face brightened. He stepped back, leaned against a linden, and began to sing in the drawling tone of western France a Breton ditty often sung to newly married couples:

  “We’ve come to wish you happiness in marriage,
    To m’sieur your husband
    As well as to you:

  “You have just been bound, madam’ la mariee,
    With bonds of gold
    That only death unbinds:

  “You will go no more to balls or gay assemblies;
    You must stay at home
    While we shall go.

  “Have you thought well how you are pledged to be
    True to your spouse,
    And love him like yourself?

  “Receive these flowers our hands do now present you;
    Alas! your fleeting honors
    Will fade as they.”

This native air, tender and melancholy, would awaken a Breton maiden’s memories of her noble old land. As he finished the first couplet, the singer, never taking his eyes from the attic curtain, saw no sign of life. While he sang the second, the curtain stirred. At “Receive these flowers,” a face appeared; a white hand cautiously opened the casement, and a girl inclined her head as he closed with the melancholy thought, “Alas! your fleeting honors will fade as they.”

To her the young workman produced a yellow flower—furze, common in Brittany and sometimes found in La Brie.

“Is it really you, Brigaut?” she asked in a low voice.

“Yes, Pierrette, yes. I am in Paris. I have started to make my way; but I’m ready to settle here, near you.”

A first-floor window beneath Pierrette’s garret creaked. The girl showed terror and whispered: “Run away!”

The lad sprang like a frightened frog to a bend in the street near a projecting mill; but his hob-nailed shoes clattered on the stones, distinct from the mill’s music, and no doubt heard by the person who opened the window.

That person was an old maid. No man would have torn himself from a morning nap to hear a minstrel in a jacket; only a maid wakes to songs of love. She opened her blinds with the furtive motion of a bat, peered about, saw nothing, and only caught the sound of retreating footsteps. Few sights are more dreadful than the matutinal apparition of an ugly old maid at a window. Deprived of her public embellishments, her night-gown and black silk cap revealed a harsh, almost witch-like countenance—wrinkled temples and nape, a breast left exposed by a gaping garment, a long angular face whose lack of harmony suggested an unfeeling spirit. Usually softened by a commercial smirk in public, here she showed a keen, watchful severity. She owned the house with her brother, who slept so soundly that nothing could rouse him.

The old maid stretched her neck, raised pale blue eyes with short lashes toward the attic, tried to glimpse Pierrette, and, finding it useless, drew in like a tortoise and closed the blinds. Street silence resumed, broken only by peasants entering town or the rare early riser.

When there is an old maid in a house, watch-dogs are needless; not the slightest event escapes her. This trifling incident would thus awaken grave suppositions and set in motion one of those obscure family dramas—none the less terrible because secret.

Pierrette did not go back to bed. Brigaut’s arrival was an immense event to her. Night had been her only refuge from daily vexations and fault-findings; sleep seemed her paradise, waking her torment. Brigaut’s song had first stirred childhood memories, then, as she sprang from bed and reached the casement, recognition dispelled doubt. She saw the square Breton jacket with bobtails, printed cotton waistcoat, linen shirt fastened by a gold heart, the large collar, earrings, stout shoes, uneven blue-gray trousers—all the humble durable garments of Breton peasantry. The white-horn buttons made her heart beat. The broom filled her eyes with tears; then terror: perhaps her cousin sleeping below had heard her. The fear was justified; the old maid had appeared, and Pierrette made Brigaut a terrified sign which he obeyed without understanding—an ingenuous, absolute devotion that reveals rare childhood affection. Whoever saw him run would have loved the chivalry of his feeling.

Jacques Brigaut was worthy of Pierrette Lorrain, who was just fifteen. Two children, Pierrette could not help crying as she watched him flee. Then she sat in a shabby armchair before a small table topped by a mirror. Resting her elbows, head in hands, she thought for an hour, recalling the Marais, the village of Pen-Hoel, perilous pond voyages in a boat untied from an old willow by little Jacques; the faces of her grandparents and mother’s sufferings; Major Brigaut’s handsome face—her carefree childhood seemed a luminous joy against the gloomy present.

Her chestnut hair escaped in disorder from a cambric cap she had made; little ringlets loosened from gray curl-papers at each temple, a heavy braid hung half unplaited. Her whiteness betrayed the malady of girlhood—chlorosis—draining color, destroying appetite, disorganizing the body. Waxy tones marked her flesh; neck and shoulders showed wasted arms, flung forward and crossed upon the table. Bare feet and a night-gown barely reaching her knees revealed enervated legs, blue veins, and flaccid muscles. Cold, which she heeded not, turned her lips violet; a sad smile drew up the corners of a sensitive mouth with small ivory teeth. Despite the pallor, she was lovely: roundness of face, vivacious brown eyes with black flecks and golden reflections, brilliant pupils full of an eager, melancholy intelligence. Pierrette was made for gaiety but was sad. Her long eyelashes lay upon cheek-bones sharpened by suffering; paleness made her features finely distinct. The ear alone was a little masterpiece of modeling.

Her history explains how she came to this. Pierrette’s mother was a Demoiselle Auffray of Provins, half-sister by her father to Madame Rogron, mother of the present owners of the square house. Monsieur Auffray married young and again near sixty-nine. By his first marriage he had a plain daughter married at sixteen to innkeeper Rogron; by his second he had a charming daughter born when Madame Rogron was already fifty with grown children.

The younger daughter married at eighteen a Breton officer, Lorrain, captain in the Imperial Guard. Seeking advancement to colonelcy, he exchanged into a line regiment. While he and his wife enjoyed Paris on the Auffray allowance or toured Germany, old Auffray, once a grocer, died at eighty-eight without a will. His estate was administered by Madame Rogron and her husband, who so managed it for their benefit that little remained for the widow beyond the house she lived in on the little square and a few acres. This widow, the mother of Madame Lorrain, was thirty-eight at her husband’s death; unwisely she remarried, selling the house and land to her step-daughter Madame Rogron, then married a young physician, Neraud, who wasted the fortune. She died two years later in grief and poverty.

Thus the share of the father’s estate that should have gone to Madame Lorrain nearly vanished, reduced to a small sum of eight thousand francs. Major Lorrain was killed at Montereau, leaving his widow, then twenty-one, with a daughter of fourteen months and only a pension and an eventual inheritance from his parents, Monsieur and Madame Lorrain, retail shopkeepers at Pen-Hoel in the Marais. These Lorrains sold wood, slates, tiles, pipes; from incapacity or ill-luck their business fared poorly. The failure of Collinet at Nantes amid the 1814 upheavals and a drop in colonial products deprived them of twenty-four thousand francs they had deposited with that house.

The arrival of their daughter-in-law, with her pension of eight hundred francs, was welcome; the eight thousand francs sent by her half-brother and sister Rogron were placed in the Lorrains’ business. They gave her a mortgage on a little house they owned in Nantes, let for three hundred francs and worth barely ten thousand.

Madame Lorrain the younger, Pierrette’s mother, died in 1819. The child was small, delicate, and the damp Marais did not suit her. The Lorrains persuaded her to stay with them for her health; she was petted, and her death brought honor to the old couple.

Some said Brigaut, an old Vendeen who had served under Charette and other leaders in the wars against the Republic, had influence over the young wife’s willingness to remain in the Marais. In Pen-Hoel he was called Major Brigaut and was constantly seen in the Lorrains’ parlor beside the window. Toward the last, the curate urged old Madame Lorrain to have her daughter-in-law marry Brigaut, promising to secure him the post of justice of the peace through the Vicomte de Kergarouet’s influence. The young woman’s death ended the plan.

Pierrette was left with her grandparents, who owed her four hundred francs a year—interest on the small property placed in their hands. This sum supported her. Growing less able, they soon met an active competitor who ruined them: with connivance, the Lorrains borrowed on notes they could not meet and were driven into bankruptcy. Pierrette’s claim on the Nantes house was subordinated to her grandmother’s legal rights, enforced to obtain daily bread. The house sold for nine thousand five hundred francs, of which one thousand five hundred went for costs; the remaining eight thousand came to Madame Lorrain, who lived on its income in a humble quarter at Nantes called Saint-Jacques, where the two old people had bed and board for a small payment.

As it was impossible to keep Pierrette with them, the old Lorrains wrote to her uncle and aunt Rogron in Provins. These Rogrons were dead, and the letter might have been lost, but the post persistently traced it to Monsieur Rogron, son of the deceased, a mercer in the rue Saint-Denis in Paris; curiosity about a possible inheritance ensured its transmission, and the Treasury its fee.

These Rogrons, toward whom the old Lorrains, though dreading to part with their dear granddaughter, stretched their supplicating hands, became unexpectedly the masters of Pierrette’s destiny. It is therefore indispensable to explain both their antecedents and their character.

Pere Rogron, the innkeeper of Provins who had married old Auffray’s daughter by his first wife, presented the coarse, jovial visage of a man given to good cheer: an inflamed face, veiny nose, Bacchic redness, short, fat, pot-bellied, with stout legs and thick hands. He had the shrewdness of a Swiss innkeeper rather than any beauty; his wife resembled him perfectly. Rogron loved good living and being waited on by pretty girls. He belonged to the frank, brutal egoists who gave themselves up openly to their vices: grasping, selfish, indecent, always gratifying whims, spending until his teeth failed. In old age he sold his inn, collected as much as possible of his late father-in-law’s property, and moved into the decrepit Auffray house in the Provins square, bought cheaply from Pierrette’s grandmother.

The Rogrons had income from twenty-seven local lots totaling about two thousand francs a year, and twenty thousand francs from selling their inn. Though Auffray’s house was dilapidated, it sheltered the Rogrons—old rats in wrack and ruin. Rogron took to horticulture, expanding the garden to the river and building a stone embankment where aquatic plants grew.

Early in their marriage the couple had two children, a son and a daughter, both plainly unattractive, the result, the narrative implies, of being put out to cheap nursing and neglect. These children were raised coarsely—left to cry while wet-nurses worked in the fields, shut in damp, dark rooms—so their features coarsened and their voices grew harsh. Their mother’s vanity was mortified; she tried stern correction, but the father’s severity made her measures seem kind. Generally left to run in stables, courtyards, or streets, sometimes whipped, sometimes sent to their grandfather who disliked them, the children suffered. The Rogrons justified seizing most of “the old scoundrel’s” property by accusing him of injustice to the children. Still, Rogron sent his son to school and kept him from conscription by buying a cartman as his man. When his daughter Sylvie was thirteen he apprenticed her in Paris with six hundred francs for board; two years later his son Jérôme-Denis followed. Asked what he would do with them, Rogron was blunt: when they were old enough he would give them a kick and tell them to make their own way, adding that his father had given him three kicks and one louis, whereas he would give them ten louis and only one kick. “After I’m gone, what’s left will be theirs,” he said. “Mine owe me their life. I’ve fed them; I don’t ask anything from them—I call that quits.”

Sylvie, apprenticed in the rue Saint-Denis to shopkeepers from Provins, became “at par” in two years, supporting herself without costing her parents anything. By nineteen she earned four hundred francs; by twenty she was second demoiselle in the Maison Julliard, wholesale silk dealers at the “Chinese Worm.” Jérôme entered the Maison Guepin, a large wholesale mercer at the “Three Distaffs.” By twenty-one Sylvie was forewoman at a thousand francs a year; Jérôme at eighteen was head-clerk with a salary of twelve hundred francs.

The siblings met on Sundays for small amusements; they dined out and visited the suburban pleasure grounds. Around the end of 1815 they pooled about twenty thousand francs and bought, from Madame Guenée, the stock and good-will of the “Family Sister,” a large retail establishment in the quarter. Sylvie kept the books and did the writing; Jérôme was master and head-clerk. By 1821, after five years, competition was fierce and maintaining the business and reputation cost all they had.

Though scarcely forty, Sylvie’s natural ugliness, hard work, and crabbed look made her seem older. Jérôme presented a silly face to customers: a retreating forehead with folds, close-cut grizzled hair, and a dull, lifeless expression; his bluish eyes showed no fire or thought. Short and fat like his father, he suggested feeble bluster rather than brute obesity, the livid flabbiness peculiar to those confined to back-shops and counting-rooms, forever tying bundles and snarling at clerks.

The small measure of intelligence the siblings possessed had been entirely absorbed by their business: acquiring and keeping money, learning market laws and usages. Thread, needles, ribbons, pins, buttons, and tailors’ furnishings had taken all their capacity. Outside business they knew little; Paris meant the Rue Saint-Denis and nothing beyond. Their narrow natures saw no field except the shop. They excelled in nagging clerks and young women and proving them to blame. Their happiness lay in busy counters; on “fine days” when Parisians strolled without buying, the Rogrons felt their day overcast. “Bad weather for sales,” they would say.

Rogron’s facility at tying parcels made him admired by apprentices: he could fold and tie while observing the street and shop, handing a parcel back with “Here it is, madame; nothing else today?” Yet he would have been ruined without his sister. Sylvie had common sense and a genius for trade; she advised purchases and sent him to remote parts of France to save trifles. Her shrewdness, unused to matters of the heart, drifted into speculation. The need to pay for a business kept the machine going and gave it infernal activity.

Rogron was effectively only head-clerk; he understood nothing of the business as a whole. Self-interest—the great motor of the mind—failed to instruct him. He was often aghast when his sister ordered some article sold below cost to clear obsolete stock, yet he later admired her. Incapable of reasoning, he subordinated himself to his sister because “she is my elder.” A life long solitary, reduced to mere needs and deprived of youthful pleasures, explains the clownish face, feebleness of mind, and vacant silliness. Sylvie had steadily prevented him from marrying—afraid to lose power and seeing only expense and injury in a younger, probably prettier woman.

Silliness may be loud or silent; Rogron’s loquacious silliness was intolerable. He chattered to clerks, explaining minutiae seasoned with flat jokes—the “chaff” of shopkeeping—and had acquired a stock phraseology. The petty retailer learns to utter words and phrases with no meaning but marked success; he explains manufacture details customers know nothing of, which gives a passing superiority; remove him from his thousand explanations and he is, relative to thought, like a fish out of water.

The Rogrons lacked the feelings that vivify the heart. Their natures were shriveled and hard, hardened by toil, privation, and the memory of suffering during long apprenticeship to life. Neither complained. They were not implacable so much as impracticable toward others in misfortune. To them, virtue, honor, and loyalty meant simply paying bills. Irritable and irritating, sordid in economy, they bore an ill reputation among the rue Saint-Denis merchants. Had it not been for their connection with Provins, where they went several times a year and could close the shop for a day or two, they would have had no clerks or young women. Old Rogron sent them all the unfortunate youngsters of his neighborhood—parents hoping to set them in Paris—by boasting of his son’s success. Parents, attracted by the prospect of strict training and possible succession, sent whichever child was in the way. But when clerks or young women found a way to escape, they fled with rejoicing, which further damaged the Rogrons’ name. New victims arrived yearly by the indefatigable old father’s efforts.

From fifteen Sylvie had two faces—the amiable seller and the sour spinster. Her acquired countenance was masterful mimicry: smiles and a soft wheedling voice gave commercial charm. Her true face, half-seen behind blinds, could frighten the resolute. When the Lorrains’ letter reached them they were in mourning for their father, from whom they inherited the Auffray house—taken, the narrative suggests, as good as stolen from Pierrette’s grandmother—and lands bought by usurious loans and mortgages on peasant property. Annual stock-taking was over; the “Family Sister” price had, at last, been paid in full. The Rogrons owned roughly sixty thousand francs worth of merchandise, forty thousand in bank or cash, and the value of their business. Sitting on a bench covered in striped-green Utrecht velvet behind their private counter, the siblings discussed what to do.

All retail shopkeepers dream of becoming bourgeois. By selling the good-will of their business they would have over one hundred and fifty thousand francs, not counting their father’s inheritance. By investing their available property in public funds they could each secure about four thousand francs a year; the proceeds from selling the business would let them repair and improve the inherited house, making it a good investment. They could then live in Provins. Their forewoman was the daughter of a rich farmer at Donnemarie, burdened with many children; in five years the farmer had lost seven children, which made the forewoman an object of interest. Rogron had tried and failed to make her his wife; she resisted. Sylvie opposed the match and aimed instead to make the shrewd young woman their successor.

To understand such shopkeepers one must dig beneath the thin subsoil of their lives. The Parisian shopkeeper nourishes his soul on hope: dreams of a country-house ten miles from Paris with a “park,” plaster statues, fountains squirtting threads of water—on which he will spend a mint—or distinction in the National Guard. Provins filled the Rogrons with fanatical longings. Provins, blessed in their eyes, rivaled any romantic region: roses of renown, medicinal springs, ruins, ramparts, ravines, gardens and rivers—an entire ideal of return and rest. Provins seemed the promised land rising from the muck of Saint-Denis.

Crossing the gray plains between La Ferté-Gaucher and Provins, a hill reveals a town watered by two rivers and a verdant valley of enchanting lines. From Paris you pass through Provins along a road cluttered with beggars and the blind; from Troyes you approach through the valley. The château and ramparts terrace the hillside; the new town lies below. Upper and Lower Provins differ: the upper is airy, steep, solemn, with imposing ruins; the lower is a town of mills, inns, shops, retired merchants, diligences, carriages, and wagons. Its historical memories, melancholy ruins, ravines, and rivers banked with gardens excite the love of all its children, who, like provincial migrants, leave to make fortunes and always return. “Die in one’s form,” the proverb for hares and faithful souls, might be Provins’ motto.

Thus Jérôme and Sylvie constantly thought of Provins. While Jérôme sold thread he pictured the upper town; as he piled button cards he contemplated the valley; when he rolled ribbons he followed the rivers. He dreamed of improving the little square house: a new front, bedrooms, a salon, billiard-room, dining-room, and an English pleasure-ground where the kitchen garden lay, with lawns, grottos, fountains, and statuary. Though the budget could not bear it, he wanted everything he saw—and then something else. He admired upholsterers’ windows while walking and told his sister he had found pieces “that will just do.” Sylvie, pen in hand, envisioned receiving the bourgeoisie of Provins, looking at herself in salon mirrors and planning her cap. The rue Saint-Denis atmosphere grew intolerable; its market mud made them long for the fragrance of Provins roses. They were genuine nostalgics and monomaniacs, delayed only by the need to sell tapes and bobbins before leaving Paris.

The Lorrains’ letter arrived amid these meditations. The Rogrons knew little of their cousin Pierrette Lorrain. Their father had taken possession of the Auffray property after they left home and had said little of his affairs; they barely remembered Aunt Lorrain. It took an hour’s genealogical discussion to make her out as the younger sister of their own mother by Auffray’s second marriage. It struck them that that second marriage had harmed their interests by dividing the Auffray property between two daughters; their father had once sneered about it.

They considered the Lorrains’ request through these recollections, which biased them against Pierrette. Taking charge of an orphan cousin who might become legal heir if neither married was troublesome. They had never seen her; what burden would a young girl be? Would she commit them to obligations? Could they send her away if they disliked her? Wouldn’t they have to marry her? If Jérôme later found a suitable Provins heiress, they ought to preserve property for his children. A yoke-mate, Sylvie declared, should be a stupid, rich, ugly girl who would let herself be governed. They decided to refuse the Lorrain request. Sylvie agreed to write the answer. Business urgency delayed her, and when the forewoman offered to buy the stock and good-will of the “Family Sister,” which the Rogrons accepted, the matter dropped from the old maid’s mind.

Sylvie Rogron and her brother departed for Provins four years before Brigaut’s arrival altered Pierrette’s life. Their doings after reaching Provins are as necessary to relate as their Paris life; Provins was destined to be no less fateful to Pierrette than the commercial antecedents of her cousins.

When a petty shopkeeper returns from Paris to the provinces a trace of Parisian airs clings for a time, but soon local habits reassert themselves, leaving only superficial marks. For those who have risen from retail to substantial bourgeois life, the change is like an illness: the perpetual chatter of the shop does not fit provincial silence, Parisian bustle collides with small-town stillness. Those who have saved property indulge long-nursed desires—travel, municipal posts, hunting, speculation. The Rogrons, brother and sister, seized by the urge to remodel their old house, resolved to make it charming.

Their fixed idea produced, under Brigaut the builder’s anxious supervision, a handsome front, interior arrangements, and elaborate furnishings on the square of Lower Provins. The contractor fussed over every nail, demanded signatures on plans, and explained the manufacture and price of each item. For choicer pieces he assured the Rogrons they had once belonged to local notables. The siblings wanted to match Provins’ rich bourgeoisie.

“Oh, if Monsieur Garceland has it, put it in,” said Mademoiselle Rogron. “His taste is good.”

“Sylvie, see, he wants ovolos in the cornice.”

“What an odd name!”

“It means eggs—from the Latin ovum.”

“What queer fellows you architects are!” mocked Rogron.

When the Rogrons entered provincial high life they found, among those who had returned from Paris, the Julliards, the Guepin clan, Madame Guenee, and others who had spread through town and married into respectability. The mayor, Garceland, was a Guepin son-in-law; the curate, Abbé Peroux, was brother to Madame Julliard; the judge, Monsieur Tiphaine junior, was related to Madame Guenee. The queen of the town was the beautiful Madame Tiphaine junior—Mélanie—a clever, delicate, ambitious young woman taken from a convent to marry in the provinces to please her mother. Handsomely dowered and desirous of advancement, she aimed to have Monsieur Tiphaine elected deputy and later promoted to a Paris judgeship. To that end she flattered every vanity and pleased all parties; twice a week she received Provins’ bourgeoisie in her Upper-town salon and became its pride. Everyone awaited Monsieur Tiphaine’s nomination; as future deputy he would reward the town.

Madame Guenee had married her daughters into leading families and married the collector Garceland herself; these women formed a court around Madame Tiphaine. Monsieur Julliard, eldest son of the old merchant, had fallen under Madame Tiphaine’s influence, acting on her suggestion to start a journal. For two years he published the “Bee-hive,” filled with literary, archaeological, and medical articles by the family; advertisements and subscriptions kept it going. Occasional incomprehensible verses addressed “TO HER!!!” bound the clans together. Madame Tiphaine’s salon naturally became the first in town; a few aristocrats had a separate salon at the old Comtesse de Breautey’s.

During their first six months the Rogrons were received, first by Madame Julliard the elder and Madame Garceland, then, only after difficulty, by Madame Tiphaine. Society aims to amalgamate people of equal wealth, education, manners, accomplishments, and character. The Guepins, Guenees, and Julliards were better positioned than the Rogrons, whose father had been held in contempt for conduct connected with the Auffray property—facts known to notary Auffray.

In Madame Tiphaine’s salon everything was harmonious; people understood one another. The Rogrons fancied acceptance would come after a visit to Mayor Garceland. Sylvie practiced the boston; Rogron, unable to play, twirled his thumbs and could talk of nothing but his new house. Words choked him; he would rise to speak, become frightened, and sit down with a comic distortion of the lips. Sylvie betrayed herself at cards—sharp, irritable when she lost, insolent when she won; nagging and quarrelsome, she became a scourge. Yet they possessed silly, open ambition and tried to take a place in a town already dominated by a close corporation of twelve allied families. Having spent thirty thousand francs restoring their house, and possessing at least ten thousand francs a year between them, they believed themselves wealthy and sought to impress a society that quickly measured their vulgarity, ignorance, and envy.

At their presentation to Madame Tiphaine she had already observed them and remarked to Julliard junior, who lingered, that she meant her salon “not to be an inn.” She wanted it select and certainly not to admit the Rogrons. Monsieur Tiphaine added that people who could not throw off the shop and polish manners ought to stay at home, and Julliard agreed the Rogrons lacked heart, mind, and manners. Madame Tiphaine scoffed at Mademoiselle Sylvie’s voice and hands. After Julliard left, Mélanie observed she had “aborigines enough” to receive and would deprive herself of the Rogrons’ company. Monsieur Tiphaine warned this would make enemies and noted the Rogrons might join the opposition, which would serve his cause by giving him triumph.

Word spread next day that the Rogrons had failed to gain admittance to Madame Tiphaine’s salon; her “inn” remark was much admired. A month passed before she returned Sylvie’s visit; in the provinces such insolence is remembered. During Sylvie’s uncomfortable evening at Madame Tiphaine’s a scene occurred over cards: Sylvie accused old Madame Julliard of cheating. Subsequently the hostess arranged the card-tables so Sylvie could only wander between them, glared at scornfully; at Madame Julliard senior’s they played whist, a game Sylvie did not know. Invitations stopped; the Rogrons continued to pay evening calls, where satirical people teased them about eggs in their cornice and their wine cellar.

At last the house was finished. The Rogrons proposed sumptuous dinners to repay civility and exhibit luxury; curiosity made people accept. The first dinner invited town leaders: the Tiphaines, the Julliards, the Lesourds, the curé, and the Garcards. It was one of those long provincial dinners from five to nine. Madame Tiphaine had introduced the Parisian custom of leaving once coffee was served, and that evening, anxious to go, she slipped away; the Rogrons accompanied her to the door and returned disconcerted at not keeping her. The rest, with cruel provincial promptness, prepared to imitate her fashion.

At Madame Tiphaine’s the company pressed her to describe the Rogron house. She sketched the gilded front door, a long corridor separating the house unevenly, a painted portico and lawn with a sun dial and a painted plaster Spartacus, a staircase painted to imitate black marble with a walnut balustrade, cellar stairs beneath it, a dining-room opening by folding doors to a salon overlooking the garden, and an ante-chamber corridor. The Rogrons boasted of using only French woods—chestnut floors, sideboards, chairs—with white calico curtains edged in red, brass lion-paw rods, a gilded dial, restaurant-like lamps, an ornate barometer Rogron adored, a porcelain stove, crimson and gold paper like that in restaurants. Furnishings were clean, new, and harsh.

The dining-room, she conceded, had character but was marred by five engravings—Poniatowski, Napoleon, the defence at Clichy, two Mazepas—framed in vile gilt. She preferred Madame Julliard’s Louis XV pastels and old provincial silver and china: “The provinces are provinces; they are only ridiculous when they mimic Paris.” The salon was all red—curtains, furniture, mantelpiece—heavy common candelabra and a garish clock, Roman standards with Greek foliage, an inevitable ornamental lion holding a ball, applied mouldings over the chimney, a chandelier wrapped in green muslin, walls papered in red flock to imitate velvet with chromo-lithographs in stucco frames. Furniture in cashmere and elm sat in rigid uniformity—two sofas, two easy-chairs, two armchairs, six chairs—an alabaster vase a la Medicis under glass, card-tables before the windows, an Aubusson carpet in a vulgar pattern. The room looked as if no one lived there: no books, no engravings, no knick-knacks, no flowers—cold and barren like Mademoiselle Sylvie. “Buffon says the style is the man; salons have styles,” Mélanie observed.

From this sketch one sees the Rogrons’ house and the absurdities Brigaut had foisted—new inventions, fantastic ornaments, devices against smoke and damp, painted marquetry panels, colored glass, superfine locks—vulgarities that make a house costly and gratify bourgeois taste.

No one chose to visit the Rogrons; their social plans failed and invitations were refused—“already engaged” to Madame Garceland and others. The Rogrons had supposed a few dinners would secure position; only young men who would mock them and habitual diners accepted. Sylvie, frightened at the forty thousand francs swallowed by her “dear house,” resorted to economy. She gave no more dinners, dismissed her cook, took a country girl, and "did the cooking for pleasure."

Fourteen months after returning, they had fallen into solitude and idleness. Banished from society, Sylvie developed a strong hatred for the Tiphaines, Julliards, and the rest of the “clique”; she wanted a rival circle, but the lesser bourgeoisie comprised only small shopkeepers with limited time and stained reputations like the lawyer Vinet and the Bonapartist Baron Gouraud, whom Rogron began to befriend despite warnings.

The siblings sat by the stove recalling former business and customers they wanted to forget. Ennui weighed on them by the second winter. They did not know how to spend days: they lingered in bed, dressed slowly, and Rogron fussed—shaving, examining his face, asking Sylvie about imagined changes, quarreling over hot water, inspecting shrubs, joinery, settling walls, fretting about a sick hen or damp spots. The barometer became his confidant; breakfast took ages; their digestion remained perfect. They read the “Bee-hive” and the “Constitutionnel,” the latter shared by Vinet and Baron Gouraud. Rogron delivered the paper to Gouraud, enjoyed long yarns, and worried over warnings he’d received—yet foolishly repeated “the clique’s” supposed malfeasances to the colonel, who in turn talked about Madame Tiphaine and the ministerialists.

Around two o’clock Rogron walked, happy if a shopkeeper stopped him to ask news. He gathered gossip, wandered to the Upper town or ravines, met old men and learned scholars. He was bewildered hearing the deputy-judge Desfondrilles, an archaeologist, speak to the learned Martener about Provins’ springs and catacombs. Desfondrilles thought Provins might rival Spa; Martener explained towns rarely recover from ruin. Rogron learned Provins had been a capital in the twelfth century and that the Upper town stood on extensive catacombs like cathedral naves—an archaeological hobby that gave him topics for evenings.

Rogron’s walks yielded bits of news he narrated to Sylvie. He repeatedly asked acquaintances, “Well, what’s the news?” then flung himself on the sofa as if exhausted. Dinner came after multiple trips to the kitchen and clock comparisons. When they stayed home, evenings were barren. The two, having nothing to grind between their rusty wheels, creaked at each other. Rogron spoke of marrying in despair; Sylvie, realizing the need for a third person, remembered their cousin Pierrette Lorrain whom no one had inquired about—perhaps presumed dead.

Sylvie, too careful to lose anything, pretended to have found the Lorrains’ letter to justify Pierrette’s coming. She answered Madame Lorrain’s letter half affectionately, half commercially, blaming delay on a change of abode and hinting Pierrette might inherit twelve thousand francs a year if Jérôme did not marry.

Like caged beasts, the siblings impatiently awaited the cousin. Three days after writing they wondered when she would arrive. Sylvie perceived in this spurious benevolence a way to recover social standing. She went to call on Madame Tiphaine to mention Pierrette’s coming, deploring the girl’s misfortune and posing as eager to succor her.

“You have been rather long in discovering her,” said Madame Tiphaine with sarcasm.

A few words by Madame Garceland during cards recalled the shameful conduct of old Rogron over the Auffray property; the notary explained the iniquity. Monsieur Tiphaine asked, “Where is the little girl now?”

“In Brittany,” said Rogron.

“Brittany is a large place,” remarked Monsieur Lesourd.

“Her grandfather and grandmother Lorrain wrote to us—when was that?” Rogron asked his sister.

Sylvie, distractedly asking Madame Garceland where she had bought her gown’s material, answered hastily, “Before we sold the business.”

“And have you only just answered the letter, mademoiselle?” the notary asked.

Sylvie turned red. “We wrote to the Institution of Saint-Jacques,” Rogron said.

“That is an almshouse for old people,” said Desfondrilles, who knew Nantes. “They receive no one under sixty.”

“She is there, with her grandmother,” Rogron insisted.

“The mother had a little fortune, the eight thousand francs your grandfather left to her,” the notary said, intentionally blundering.

“Ah!” said Rogron, missing the sarcasm.

“Then you know nothing about your cousin’s position or means?” asked Monsieur Tiphaine.

“If Monsieur Rogron had known it,” the deputy-judge said, “he would never have left her so long in such an establishment. I remember a Nantes house belonging to Monsieur and Madame Lorrain was sold under court order, and Mademoiselle Lorrain’s claim was swallowed up. I was commissioner at the time.”

The notary spoke of Colonel Lorrain, who, had he lived, would have been amazed that his daughter was in such an institution. The Rogrons retreated, blaming a malicious world. Sylvie saw her professed benevolence had failed; she had lost ground. Thereafter the Rogrons no longer concealed their hatred of that class. The brother told Sylvie of scandals Colonel Gouraud and lawyer Vinet had hinted—accusations that the Tiphaines’ fortunes were stolen—“and such people set up to be proud!” he fumed.

Once they began to denounce “the clique,” they unwittingly set out to have a society of their own: their house was to become a rendezvous for the floating elements of the liberal party. Colonel Gouraud and the lawyer Vinet, feeling ostracized and sharing opinions, had watched the Rogrons with curiosity. Both professed patriotism partly to become consequential. The Liberals in Provins were few—an old soldier who kept a café, an innkeeper, Monsieur Cournant the notary, Doctor Neraud, and a few farmers. The colonel and the lawyer, delighted to find a fool whose money might aid their schemes and whose house could host meetings, exploited the Rogrons’ ill will toward the upper class. They already subscribed to the “Constitutionnel”; it would not be hard for the colonel to convert the ex-mercer into a Liberal, though Rogron knew so little of politics he might take Sergeant Mercier’s exploits for those of a brother shopkeeper.

Pierrette’s expected arrival hastened their plans. Gouraud, a veteran accustomed to horrors, felt little repelled by the thought of a poor cousin and began eyeing Sylvie’s fortune. The colonel, short and fat with bushy tufts of facial hair, a faded red face, a sabre-cut on his nose, thick nasal speech, and a cavalier build, had a Legion of Honor cross and about three thousand francs a year in retirement pay. He was jolly yet lawless, contemptuous of convention, and saw in the Rogrons an opportunity.

The lawyer Vinet, tall and thin, had liberal opinions but little talent and earned meagerly. In Provins, lawyers often plead for themselves; the court was unfavorable to Vinet because of his opinions. He had earlier seduced a rich girl near Coulommiers, forcing her parents to marry her to him; the Chargeboeuf family’s influence had failed to secure him advancement. Repulsed, ashamed, and driven by poverty, he embraced Liberalism hoping opposition victory would lift him. He lived miserably in the Upper town; his wife seldom left. Vinet’s mind grew elastic, his gall increased; he had a viperish look—slit mouth, gleaming eyes behind glasses, a rasping voice, greenish jaundiced skin that betrayed balked ambition. He could talk and argue shrewdly, and his strength lay in a readiness to do anything legal for advantage. This future parliamentary athlete would have a terrible influence on Pierrette’s fate. At present he aimed to found a newspaper at Provins. After studying the Rogrons from a distance (with the colonel’s aid), he concluded the brother might be made useful.

“One woman or another—handsome or ugly—you don’t care; marry Mademoiselle Rogron and we can organize something at once,” Vinet suggested, nudging the colonel.

“I have thought of it,” replied Gouraud, “but they’ve sent for Colonel Lorrain’s daughter; she’s their next of kin.”

“You can get them to make a will in your favor. Ha! you’d get a very comfortable house.”

“As for the little girl—well, let’s see her,” said the colonel, leering, showing how little respect he felt for any girl.

################################################
After her grandparents were placed in the hospital where they expected to end their days, Pierrette, young and proud, could not bear living on charity; she was relieved when she learned she had wealthy relations. Brigaut, the son of her mother’s friend—the major’s son and Pierrette’s childhood companion—had apprenticed as a cabinet-maker in Nantes. Hearing she was leaving, he offered his savings, sixty francs earned as an apprentice, to pay her way to Paris; she accepted with the simple indifference of true affection.

Brigaut had been a weekly companion and protector, delighting in sheltering Pierrette. Often they had sat together planning the future: he would use his plane to win their fortune while she waited. In October 1824, just after her eleventh birthday, the old Lorrains and Brigaut, tearful, entrusted her to the conductor of the diligence from Nantes to Paris, begging him to place her safely on the Provins coach. Poor Brigaut ran beside the coach until exhausted; both he and Pierrette cried as she disappeared.

The Lorrains and Brigaut knew so little of the world that Pierrette arrived in Paris penniless. The conductor—aware of her “rich” relations—covered her hotel bills and persuaded his colleague on the Provins route to accept him as guarantor, treating the child like a parcel. Four days after leaving Nantes, about nine on a Monday night, an older messenger-conductor brought Pierrette, whose sole luggage was a small bundle with two dresses, two chemises, and two pairs of stockings, to the house of Mademoiselle Rogron in the Grand’Rue. He announced the child and demanded forty-seven francs, twelve sous, for expenses, taking payment from Sylvie and Rogron while chiding them for their stinginess. The couple, flattered and astonished, paid.

Thus Pierrette Lorrain was thrust into her cousins’ life: tossed from the Saint-Jacques asylum to their dining-room with no intermediate tenderness. Shy and speechless, she would have charmed most people in her coarse blue flannel petticoat, cambric apron, thick shoes, blue stockings, white kerchief, and red mittens bought by the conductor. Her washed Breton cap framed a fresh, rosy face, and she seemed innocently radiant in the warm room.

Sylvie, the old maid, and her brother Rogron inspected her with blunt curiosity. The maid Adele fussed with her bedding and noted that the child’s belongings were worthless. They carried Pierrette up to a modest bedroom: a little bed with a calico curtain, a walnut bureau, a small table, and a mirror—neat but shabby. Pierrette was amazed by the fine cotton sheets and the general comfort. The relatives immediately began to speak about her clothing: they would buy her new clothes at once.

The Rogrons were stingy, proud of their household and furniture, and accustomed to economizing. For them, Pierrette’s arrival was both a responsibility and an opportunity to display their thrift and taste. They calculated the cost of outfitting the child and, to their dismay, estimated roughly three hundred francs for clothes and making—an expense Sylvie resented but accepted. Pierrette, accustomed to the free affection of Brittany, felt a new aversion to these relatives whose welcome lacked the warmth she had known at Pen-Hoel. There, she had been loved and free: playing on the pond, roaming woods and fields with Brigaut, and sharing simple family affection. Now, among the Rogrons, she missed the considerate looks and spontaneous kindness even conductors had shown her.

From the start Pierrette was hurt by the constant remarks about cleanliness and noise: she was scolded for running early and tracking the floor, told to be quiet and tidy, and told she must not disturb her “cousin.” Sylvie and Rogron loved their house and its furniture obsessively; Sylvie devoted herself to dusting and preserving the household’s appearance with a zeal akin to a cabinet-maker’s precision. This devotion to things more than people shaped the way they treated Pierrette: as another object to keep neat and economical.

During her first week in Provins Pierrette’s time was consumed by fittings and sewing. She knew nothing of needlework; her simple answers—she had played in Brittany and been loved—only increased their impatience. The seamstresses liked her better than the Rogrons did, but Sylvie worried about the cost and fretted that the child would “ruin” them. Determined that Pierrette should appear as well dressed as the other respectable children, Sylvie bought fashionable bronzed-kid boots, fine cotton stockings, a corset, a blue reps dress, a silk-lined cape, and matching underclothes—down to embroidered drawers and a velvet bonnet like the sub-prefect’s daughter’s. These purchases flattered Sylvie’s vanity and her standing among the town’s ladies.

Dressed to please the Provins society, Pierrette became beloved by the town’s mothers and children. The ladies kissed and praised her; invitations multiplied. Yet these triumphs bred Sylvie’s jealousy: she had taken Pierrette in to reflect well on herself, not to see the child admired elsewhere. When Pierrette thrived in the welcoming homes of other women—whose kind manners contrasted with her cousins’ harshness—Sylvie resented that those mothers invited Pierrette but rarely visited the Rogrons. At this point the child’s joy only magnified the cousins’ selfish motives: they had taken her for their own self-regard, not from maternal affection.

The fine clothes and social success marked a turning point. Pierrette, used to rough play and unselfconscious living, was careless with her new garments—natural behavior that enraged her cousins who valued money above the child’s ease. Their reprimands were not maternal but mercantile; they feared expense and loss of face. These criticisms reawakened their old shopkeeper instincts: accustomed to domineering and reproving clerks, they now exerted petty tyranny over Pierrette, practicing despotism to fill their days. Where larger hearts expand through kindness, these narrow ones did so by persecuting the weaker.

Pierrette, sensitive and trusting, began to shrink under their constant fault-finding. Her instinctive tenderness and vivacity—gifts of love—recoiled from their hardness. She tried to curb her gaiety to avoid scoldings; after a month she became so subdued Rogron wondered if she were ill. When Pierrette tore her best reps dress at Madame Tiphaine’s—one of the friends whose house had been closed to her by Sylvie—she feared the savage reprimands awaiting her at home. Madame Tiphaine secretly replaced the material, but when Rogron discovered the deception he forbade further visits to those women. Thus ended the first phase of Pierrette’s life in Provins—a three-month period that had been bearable because it contained some joy mixed with cold kindness and sharp reproaches.

Meanwhile, the colonel and Vinet, local men with social ambitions and partisan feelings, watched these events with sly calculation. They visited Rogron and Sylvie with restrained flattery and suggestions, exploiting their resentment at slurs from the town’s “ministry supporters.” They proposed that Sylvie form an opposition salon and cultivate their own circle, offering Vinet’s agreeable wife and the colonel’s company as assets—an enticing plan that worked on Sylvie’s vanity. Their insinuations poisoned the couple’s view of Provins society and provided a social rationale for excluding the Tiphaine circle that had welcomed Pierrette.

Vinet next introduced his wife to Sylvie. Madame Vinet, gentle, modest, and resigned to a modest position, accepted Sylvie’s airs with deference; her meekness pleased the self-important aunt. The colonel and Vinet’s influence grew, subtly shaping the household atmosphere and further isolating Pierrette. Under their counsel, Sylvie became more protective of her house and more controlling over the child’s company—another restraint on Pierrette’s liberty.

Confined largely to the Rogrons’ rooms and garden, not allowed out except under supervision, Pierrette was constantly corrected: “Don’t touch that,” “Let that alone,” must be heard wherever she moved. She was drilled in carriage and behavior; Sylvie, rigid and officious, insisted on erect posture and forbade natural movements, sometimes resorting to slaps on the back to make the child stand straight. Each correction taught Pierrette to repress her natural vivacity and to adopt a frozen, servile comportment.

So the Breton child who had lived like Paul and Virginia—free, caressed, running through fields, fishing, making snow-men, and confiding in Brigaut—was slowly transformed. The warmth and spontaneity that had characterized her life at Pen-Hoel were clipped by the Rogrons’ narrow thrift, vanity, and tyranny, and by the external encouragement of men like Vinet and the colonel. Pierrette learned to stifle her laughter, hide her delight, and appear as an obedient little automaton—the more painfully aware of her constraint because she could still glimpse, in other houses and in memories, the life she had lost.

One evening early in Pierrette’s second stay at her cousins’ house, the child, unseen all evening by the three guests, came in to kiss them good-night. Sylvie turned her cheek coldly to avoid kissing her; the motion was so cruel that Pierrette burst into tears.

“Did you prick yourself, little girl?” said the atrocious Vinet.

“What is the matter?” asked Sylvie, severely.

“Nothing,” said the child, going up to Rogron.

“Nothing? That’s nonsense; nobody cries for nothing,” Sylvie snapped.

“My rich cousin isn’t as kind to me as my poor grandmother was,” sobbed Pierrette.

“Your grandmother took your money,” Sylvie retorted, “and your cousin will leave you hers.”

The colonel and the lawyer exchanged glances.

“I would rather be robbed and loved,” said Pierrette.

“Then you shall be sent back whence you came.”

“What has the dear little thing done?” Madame Vinet asked.

Vinet gave his wife the stern look with which men enforce dominion. The hapless child, repeatedly punished for lacking that which was wanted of her—a fortune—took up her cards. Sylvie, throwing up her head so violently that the yellow wall-flowers in her cap nodded, declared, “She is always looking about to annoy us. She opened my watch to see the inside, meddled with the wheel and broke the mainspring. Mademoiselle pays no heed to what is said to her. I am all day long telling her to take care of things, and I might just as well talk to that lamp.”

Ashamed before strangers, Pierrette crept from the room.

“I am thinking all the time how to subdue that child,” Rogron said.

“Isn’t she old enough to go to school?” Madame Vinet suggested, but was silenced by a look from her husband, who had concealed his own and the colonel’s schemes.

“This is what comes of taking charge of other people’s children!” cried the colonel. “You may yet have some of your own. Why don’t you both marry?”

Sylvie smiled at the colonel; for the first time in her life she met a man for whom the idea she might marry did not seem absurd.

“Madame Vinet is right,” Rogron said; “perhaps teaching would keep Pierrette quiet. A master wouldn’t cost much.”

“If you will be security for that opposition journal I mentioned,” Vinet said, “you will find an excellent master in the managing editor; we intend to engage that poor schoolmaster deprived by the clergy’s encroachments. My wife is right; Pierrette is a rough diamond needing polishing.”

While Vinet explained how the paper would benefit them all, Pierrette dissolved in tears. She blamed her cousin and believed charity should be whole. She hated her handsome frocks and their cost, and resolved to behave so no fault could be found. She recalled Brigaut’s gift of his savings, thought her troubles could not worsen, unaware further misfortunes were being planned in the salon.

Soon Pierrette had a writing-master to teach reading, writing, and ciphering. This was said to bring enormous injury to the Rogrons’ house: ink-spots on tables, furniture, and her clothes; copy-books and pens abandoned; sand scattered; torn books. She was told to earn her living and not be a burden; such remarks choked her. She restrained tears or was scolded for weeping as an insult to her cousins’ kindness. Rogron found his mode—he scolded her as he scolded clerks, summoned her from play to study, made her repeat lessons, becoming almost savage. Sylvie taught her the little she knew of women’s work. Neither had softness. Their narrow minds, finding pleasure in worrying the child, passed from outward kindness into severity, justified by what they called Pierrette’s self-will. They could not adapt instruction to the pupil’s intelligence—a difference between public and private education. Pierrette took an age to learn the rudiments and was called stupid, clumsy, awkward for trifles. Abused incessantly in words and by harsh looks, she acquired the submissive habits of a sheep, daring nothing of her own impulse, awaiting her cousins’ orders and sheltering her mind in passive obedience. Her brilliant colors faded. At times she complained of feeling ill; when asked “Where?” she answered, “Everywhere.”

“Nonsense! who ever heard of suffering everywhere?” Sylvie scoffed. Rogron lectured that “everywhere” meant nowhere—“you’re complaining for complaining’s sake.”

Pierrette fell silent, for her girlish remarks were met with ignorant commonplaces she knew were ridiculous.

“You complain,” Rogron said, “but you’ve got the appetite of a monk.”

Only Adele, the fat servant, did not bruise the little flower. She warmed Pierrette’s bed on the sly after Sylvie scolded her. Sylvie’s belief was firm: children should be hardened—they had been. “You’ll make Pierrette a peakling,” she said, using their word for a puny, suffering being.

The child’s natural affection was treated as dissimulation. Her fresh devotion was pitilessly crushed. If she tried to soften their ferocity, they accused her of ulterior motives. “Tell me at once what you want?” Rogron would say, “you are not coaxing me for nothing.” Neither brother nor sister believed in affection; Pierrette’s whole being was affection. Colonel Gouraud, anxious to please Sylvie, approved all she did. Vinet encouraged them, attributing her so-called misdeeds to Breton obstinacy as incurable. Flattered, Rogron agreed to go security for the “Courrier de Provins,” and Sylvie invested five thousand francs.

The colonel and lawyer then took the field. They got a hundred shares of five hundred francs among farmers and the “independents” and buyers of national-domain lands—whose fears they exploited. Each shareholder subscribed to the paper; the judicial advertisements were split between the “Bee-hive” and the “Courrier.” The paper’s first issue contained a pompous eulogy of Rogron, presenting him as Provins’ Laffitte. The public, nudged thus, expected contested elections. Madame Tiphaine, dreaming of taking her husband to Paris as deputy, despaired when an article in the new paper attacked her and Julliard junior: “There is always a scoundrel close to a dupe,” she said.

As the “Courrier” spread, Vinet bought new clothes and adopted the gray slouch hat of liberals; his wife took a servant and appeared as the wife of a prominent man. Vinet proved grateful out of policy. He and his ally Cournant, a liberal notary opposed to the ministerial Auffray, became close advisers to the Rogrons, performing services: leases made by old Rogron in 1815 were expiring, and horticulture and gardening had developed near Provins. The lawyer and notary worked to increase the Rogrons’ rentals. Vinet won two suits involving planting trees and five hundred poplars; the proceeds, with the Rogrons’ savings—who for three years had set aside six thousand francs a year at high interest—were invested in improved lands. Vinet also carried out the ejectment of peasants who had defaulted on debts secured by the elder Rogron. The cost of the fine house was thus partly recouped. Their lands, chosen by their father’s innkeeper’s eye, were divided into small holdings, the largest not exceeding five acres, rented to safe tenants whose other holdings secured leases. By 1826 these investments brought in five thousand francs a year; taxes charged to tenants, no buildings needed insurance or repairs.

By the end of Pierrette’s second period in Provins, life had become so harsh—cold indifference, silly fault-finding, total absence of affection—that she felt a chill like a grave creeping round her. She thought of escaping on foot to Brittany to her grandparents. Two events prevented it: old Lorrain died, and Rogron was appointed her guardian. Had the grandmother died first, Rogron, advised by Vinet, would have claimed Pierrette’s eight thousand francs and reduced the old man to penury.

“You may, perhaps, inherit from Pierrette,” Vinet said with a horrid smile. “Who knows who may live and who may die?”

Illuminated by that remark, Rogron pressured Madame Lorrain until she secured for Pierrette the reversion of the eight thousand francs at her death.

Deeply shocked and on the point of making her first communion—a reason to abandon escape—Pierrette’s ceremony led to changes at the Rogrons’. Sylvie learned that Monsieur le curé Peroux taught the Julliards, Lesourds, Garcelands; so she insisted Pierrette be instructed by the vicar, Monsieur Habert, a priest thought to belong to the Congregation—zealous, feared, austere, ambitious. His sister, an unmarried woman about thirty, kept a school for young ladies; both siblings were thin, yellow, black-haired.

Like a true Breton girl, rooted in Catholic practice and poetry, Pierrette opened to the priest’s words. Suffering predisposes to devotion; many young girls are inclined to mysticism, and the priest found good soil. He changed the current of her thoughts: Pierrette loved Jesus as taught at first communion—as a celestial bridegroom—giving meaning to her sufferings; she saw God’s finger in events. Her soul, hurt though she could not accuse her cousins of outright wrong, took refuge in Faith, Hope, Charity. She abandoned thoughts of escape. Surprised by the priest’s effect on Pierrette, Sylvie too became pious under his influence. Jerome Rogron, however, who could not be gripped by this so-called Jesuit (then the late Constitutionnel was a stronger influence), remained faithful to Colonel Gouraud, Vinet, and Liberalism.

Mademoiselle Rogron befriended Mademoiselle Habert; the two spinsters loved each other as sisters. Mademoiselle Habert offered to take Pierrette to her school to spare Sylvie the annoyance of education, but the Rogrons declared the house would be too lonely without Pierrette; their attachment seemed excessive.

When Gouraud and Vinet learned of Mademoiselle Habert’s appearance, they feared the priest had matrimonial designs for his sister like the colonel’s plans for Sylvie. “Your sister wants to get you married,” Vinet told Rogron. “With whom?” Rogron asked. “With that old sorceress of a schoolmistress,” the colonel sneered. Rogron naively replied that she hadn’t spoken of it. Sylvie was certain an old maid like Mademoiselle Habert would make good progress toward salvation, increasing the priest’s influence, potentially converting Rogron and sending Pierrette to a convent. The two liberals, alarmed, saw in the priest and his sister adversaries as strong as themselves. Monsieur and Mademoiselle Habert visited the Rogrons nightly and could play whist and boston; their assiduity matched that of the colonel and Vinet. Each side suspected the other, and the salon turned into a battlefield, each wanting the whole prey.

The liberals made the first move. Vinet, pursuing self-interest, contacted Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and her mother, living in poverty in Troyes on two thousand francs a year. Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, who believed in love-based marriage until twenty-five, was persuaded by Vinet’s promise that, joining means with his, she could live in Provins and marry the fool Rogron, taking her place in the best society.

Their arrival was a great reinforcement for the liberal party and a shock to Provins’ aristocrats and the Tiphaine clique. Madame de Breautey, horrified that two women of rank should be misled, begged them to come to her and railed against the selfishness degrading France—the consequence, she said, of materialism and the laws’ emphasis on money: nobility and beauty no longer mattered. Such creatures as the Rogrons, Vinets, could now contend with the King of France.

Bathilde de Chargeboeuf had not only beauty but dress; she was dazzlingly fair. At twenty-five her figure was developed; her throat, hair, smile, carriage, features, eyes, and motions were noble and high-bred. Her hands and feet were beautiful; health gave her perhaps too much the look of a handsome barmaid—“but that can’t be a defect in the eyes of a Rogron,” Madame Tiphaine sighed. Bathilde’s dress on first appearing at the Rogrons was simple: a brown merino gown edged with green embroidery, low-necked, with a tulle fichu carefully drawn down though opening a little in front, caught with a sevigne. She removed her velvet bonnet and shawl to reveal gold ear-drops and wore a black velvet ribbon with a heart—tricks of a girl seeking marriage. She entreated Rogron to fasten a cuff-button to show him her wrist; the dazzled fool refused, masking his emotions with indifference. Sylvie and Celeste Habert were deceived by his manner; Vinet was not.

The colonel, courting Sylvie as Bathilde courted Rogron, put on a clean shirt each evening, velvet stocks, a new blue surtout, a red rosette, even refrained from smoking. He adopted the air of a party leader preparing to drive out the Bourbons.

Vinet and the colonel played a crueler trick: they insinuated the priest sympathized with their ideas; soon Provins called him a liberal ecclesiastic. When the bishop heard, Monsieur Habert was summoned and admonished to stop visiting the Rogrons; his sister persisted. Thus the Rogron salon became established and powerful.

Before the year ended political intrigues matched the matrimonial plots. The Villele ministry fell in 1826; Vinet, the Liberal candidate who had borrowed money to buy a domain to qualify, nearly defeated Monsieur Tiphaine, who saved the election by two votes. The Rogrons’ salon was Liberal headquarters; habitués included notary Cournant and Doctor Neraud, whose stormy youth gave way to study and, Liberals claimed, more capability than the aristocratic Martener. The Rogrons themselves hardly understood their present triumph.

Bathilde, to whom Vinet had portrayed Pierrette as an enemy, was disdainful of the girl. It seemed everyone’s schemes demanded humiliation of that poor victim. Madame Vinet could do little for Pierrette, crushed as she was by her husband’s will. Pierrette often pressed against Madame Vinet as if divining her thoughts, asking to be shown a stitch or taught embroidery. Treated gently, she could learn and succeed marvellously. But after the arrival of Madame and Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, Madame Vinet ceased to visit the Rogrons.

Sylvie, now thinking of marrying, considered Pierrette an obstacle. Nearly fourteen, the pallid whiteness of her skin—symptomatic of illness overlooked by the ignorant old maid—made her exquisitely lovely. Sylvie thought to balance Pierrette’s cost by making her a servant. The habitués advised sending away Adele: why not have Pierrette keep the house and cook? If needed, Sylvie could employ the colonel’s woman-of-all-work. The lawyer counseled every girl should learn to keep house and know prices. Pierrette, generous and self-devoted, offered willingly, pleased to earn the bitter bread she ate. Adele was sent away, and Pierrette lost the only protector she had.

Crushed physically and mentally, she was treated with less consideration than a servant—she belonged to them. She was scolded for the smallest dust on a glass globe or mantelpiece. The ornaments she once admired became odious. No matter how she strove to do right, her cousins always found cause to reproach her. In two years she received no praise or kindly word; happiness was simply not being scolded. She bore with angelic patience the morose ill-humor of two celibates to whom tenderness was unknown and who daily made her feel her dependence.

Such life, pressed between two vise-like jaws, worsened her health. She began to feel violent internal distresses, secret pangs undermining her strength and arresting development. Slowly, through dreadful hidden sufferings, the poor child came to the state in which the companion of her childhood had found her when he sang the Breton ditty at dawn on that October day.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
Before relating the domestic drama Jacques Brigaut’s coming provoked in the Rogron family, it is best to explain how the lad came to be in Provins; he is a somewhat mute personage on the scene.

When he ran from the house Brigaut was not only frightened by Pierrette’s gesture, he was horrified by the change he saw in his little friend. He could scarcely recognize the voice, the eyes, the gestures that had once been lively, gay, and tender. When he had gained some distance his legs trembled; hot flushes ran down his back. He had seen Pierrette’s shadow, but not herself. Climbing to the Upper town, he found a spot from which he could see the square and the house where Pierrette lived. He gazed at it mournfully, lost in thoughts that rent his heart, revealing to his own soul the extent of his love for his little adopted sister.

Passion between two children of opposite sexes is rare. The charming story of Paul and Virginia, like that of Pierrette and Brigaut, does not answer that strange moral fact. Modern history shows only a few such examples. Such was the sudden perfect affection in the Breton workman. Pierrette and he had often protected each other; he had blissfully given her money for her journey, had almost killed himself running after the diligence when she left. Pierrette had known nothing of this; for him, the recollection warmed his hard life. For Pierrette’s sake he had struggled to improve himself; he had learned his trade for her; he had come to Paris to make his fortune for her. After a fortnight in the city he could not resist seeing her, and he had walked from Saturday night to Monday morning. He intended to return to Paris, but the sight of his little friend nailed him to Provins. A wonderful, unconscious magnetism acted upon him. Tears rolled from his eyes when they rose in hers. If to her he was Brittany and childhood, to him she was life itself.

At sixteen Brigaut had not yet learned to draw or model a cornice; ignorant of much, he had nevertheless earned, by piece-work during his apprenticeship, some four or five francs a day. On this he could live in Provins and be near Pierrette; he chose the best cabinet-maker in town and meant to learn the rest by working for him, thus keeping watch over his darling.

Brigaut’s mind was made up. He went back to Paris, fetched his certificate, tools, and baggage, and three days later was a journeyman in the establishment of Monsieur Frappier, the best cabinet-maker in Provins. Active, steady workmen, not given to junketing, are rare, and masters hold to young men like Brigaut. By the end of the month he was made foreman and was fed and lodged by Frappier, who taught him arithmetic and line drawing. The house and shop were in the Grand’Rue, not a hundred feet from the little square where Pierrette lived.

Brigaut buried his love and committed no imprudence. He made Madame Frappier tell him all she knew about the Rogrons, including how their father had laid hands on the property of old Auffray, Pierrette’s grandfather. He obtained other information about the brother and sister. He met Pierrette sometimes in the market with her cousin and shuddered to see the heavy basket she carried. On Sundays he went to church to look for her, dressed in his best clothes. There, for the first time, he became aware that Pierrette was Mademoiselle Lorrain. Pierrette saw him and made a hasty sign to keep out of sight. To him there was a world in that little gesture, as there had been earlier in the sign from her window telling him to run away. Ah! what a fortune he must make in the coming ten years to marry his little friend, to whom, he was told, the Rogrons were to leave their house, a hundred acres of land, and twelve thousand francs a year, not counting their savings!

The persevering Breton set about thoroughly educating himself in his trade; for principles of the craft he could learn in Provins as well as Paris and thus remain near Pierrette, to whom he became anxious to explain his projects and the protection he could offer. He was determined to know the reason for her pallor and the debility beginning to show in the eyes; he would also discover the cause of the sufferings that made her look as if death might take her at any moment. The two signs—the gestures not denying friendship but imploring caution—alarmed him. Evidently Pierrette wished him to wait; otherwise there was danger. As she left the church she gave him one look, and he saw her eyes full of tears. But he could as soon have squared the circle as have guessed what had happened in the Rogrons’ house during the fortnight since his arrival.

It was not without keen apprehension that Pierrette came downstairs the morning after Brigaut had invaded her dreams. She thought Sylvie must have heard the song, or she would not have risen and opened her window; but Pierrette was ignorant of the powerful reasons that made the old maid so alert. For the last eight days strange events and bitter feelings had agitated the minds of the Rogron salon’s chief personages. These hidden matters, carefully concealed by all concerned, were destined to fall like an avalanche on Pierrette. Such secret schemes and calculations lie at the base of great revolutions, whether political, social, or domestic; their subtle significance cannot be rendered by a simple narrative. To set down all circumlocutions, oratorical precautions, protracted conversations, and honeyed words that gloss venom would make as long a book as Clarissa Harlowe.

Mademoiselle Habert and Mademoiselle Sylvie were equally desirous of marrying, but Celeste Habert was ten years older and could hope her children would inherit all the Rogron property. Sylvie was forty-two, an age at which marriage is perilous. In confiding to each other their ideas, Celeste, instigated by her vindictive brother the priest, enlightened Sylvie as to the risks. Sylvie trembled; she was terribly afraid of death, an idea that shakes all celibates. But at that time the Martignac ministry came to power—a Liberal victory overturning Villele. The Vinet party carried their heads high in Provins. Vinet himself became a personage, the Liberals prophesying his advancement to deputy and attorney-general. The colonel would be made mayor. Ah, to reign as Madame Garceland reigned! Sylvie could not resist that hope; she determined to consult a doctor, though it would cover her with ridicule. Consulting Monsieur Neraud, the Liberal physician and rival of Martener, would be a blunder. Celeste offered to hide Sylvie in her dressing-room while she herself consulted Monsieur Martener on this delicate matter. Whether Martener was Celeste’s accomplice need not be discovered; at any rate he told Celeste that even at thirty the danger, though slight, did exist. “But,” he added, “with your constitution, you need fear nothing.”

“But how about a woman over forty?” asked Celeste.

“A married woman who has had children has nothing to fear.”

“But I mean an unmarried woman, like Mademoiselle Rogron, for instance?”

“Oh, that’s another thing,” said Martener. “Successful childbirth is then one of those miracles God sometimes allows, but rarely.”

“Why?” asked Celeste.

The doctor gave a terrifying pathological account: the elasticity of youthful muscles and bones does not exist later, especially in sedentary women. “So you think an unmarried woman ought not to marry after forty?”

“Not unless she waits some years,” replied the doctor. “But then, of course, it is not marriage; it is only an association of interests.”

The interview’s clinical result was clear: an unmarried woman would make a great mistake marrying after forty. When the doctor departed Celeste found Sylvie in a frightful state, green and yellow, pupils dilated.

“Then you really love the colonel?” asked Celeste.

“I still hoped,” replied Sylvie.

“Well, then, wait!” cried Mademoiselle Habert, Jesuitically, aware that time would rid her of the colonel.

Sylvie’s new devotion to the church warned her that the morality of such a marriage might be doubtful. She sounded her conscience in the confessional. The stern priest explained Church opinions, which see marriage primarily as propagation and rebuke second marriages and all passion without social purpose. Sylvie’s perplexities were great. These internal struggles gave extraordinary force to her passion, investing it with that attraction which, from Eve’s day, the forbidden has for women. Mademoiselle Rogron’s perturbation did not escape the lynx-eyed lawyer.

One evening, after the game had ended, Vinet approached Sylvie, took her hand, and led her to a sofa.

“Something troubles you,” he said.

She nodded sadly. He let the others depart; Rogron walked home with the Chargeboeufs, and when Vinet was alone with the old maid he wormed the truth from her.

“Cleverly played, abbé!” he thought. “But you’ve played into my hands.”

The foxy lawyer, more decided even than the doctor, advised marriage in ten years. Inwardly he vowed the whole Rogron fortune should go to Bathilde. He rubbed his hands and hurried home. The influence exercised by Monsieur Habert, physician of the soul, and by Vinet, doctor of the purse, balanced each other. Rogron had no piety; the black-robed pair were fairly matched.

On discovering Celeste’s victory in her anxiety to marry Rogron over Sylvie, the lawyer saw his chance to drive the colonel from the field. He knew Rogron well enough to be certain he could marry him to Bathilde; Jerome had already inwardly succumbed to her charms, and Vinet knew the first time the pair were alone the marriage would be settled. Rogron had reached the point of keeping his eyes fixed on Celeste to avoid looking at Bathilde. Vinet had now learned Sylvie’s secrets and saw the force of her love for the colonel. He fully understood the struggle of such passion in an old maid gripped by religion and saw a way to rid himself of Pierrette and the colonel alike by making each the cause of the other’s overthrow.

The next day, after court, Vinet met the colonel and Rogron walking their daily walk. Whenever the three men were seen in company the town talked. This triumvirate, held in horror by the sub-prefect, the magistracy, and the Tiphaine clique, was a pride to the Liberals. Vinet, sole editor of the “Courrier,” was head of the party; the colonel, its arm; Rogron, by his purse, its nerves. The Tiphaines declared the three men always plotted evil to the government; the Liberals admired them as defenders of the people. When Rogron turned to go home, recalled by dinner-hour, Vinet stopped the colonel by taking Gouraud’s arm.

“Well, colonel,” he said, “I am going to take a fearful load off your shoulders; you can do better than marry Sylvie; if you play your cards properly you can marry that little Pierrette in two years’ time.”

He then related the Jesuit’s manoeuvre and its effect on Sylvie.

“What a skulking trick!” cried the colonel; “and spreading over years, too!”

“Colonel,” said Vinet gravely, “Pierrette is a charming creature; with her you can be happy for the rest of your life; your health is so sound that the difference in ages won't seem disproportionate. But you mustn’t think it easy to change a dreadful fate to a pleasant one. To turn a woman who loves you into a friend and confidant is as perilous as crossing a river under fire. You must study the position and manoeuvre your forces with the same wisdom you have displayed and which has won us our present position. If I get to be attorney-general you shall command the department. Oh! if you had been an elector we should be further advanced; I would have bought votes by threatening men with the loss of their places, and we should have had a majority.”

The colonel had long thought about Pierrette but concealed his feelings with utmost dissimulation. His roughness to the child was only a mask. She could not understand why the man who claimed to be her father’s old comrade usually treated her so ill when sometimes, if alone with her, he would chuck her under the chin and give a friendly kiss. After Vinet’s conversation about Sylvie’s fears, Gouraud began to seek opportunities to find Pierrette alone; the rough colonel made himself as soft as a cat. He told her how brave her father was and what a misfortune it had been that she lost him.

A few days before Brigaut’s arrival Sylvie had suddenly come upon Gouraud and Pierrette talking together. Jealousy rushed into her heart with monastic violence. Jealousy, credulous and suspicious, gives fancy freedom but not intelligence; it filled her with fantastic ideas. When she heard Brigaut’s ditty a few mornings later she jumped to the conclusion that the man who had used the words “Madam’ le mariee,” addressing them to Pierrette, must be the colonel. She was certain, for she had noticed a change in his manners for a week. He was the only man who, in her solitary life, had ever paid her attention. Consequently she watched him with all her eyes and mind; by hope and despair she brought the matter to enormous proportions and saw all things in a mental mirage. By dint of staring she saw nothing. Alternately she repelled, admitted, and conquered the idea of rivalry. She compared herself with Pierrette: forty-two with gray hair, versus Pierrette delicately fair with soft eyes. She had heard men of fifty apt to love young girls of that kind. Before the colonel came to the house Sylvie had heard in the Tiphaines’ salon strange stories of his life and morals. Old maids preserve exaggerated Platonic sentiments; they hold to fixed doctrines like those with little experience of life. The mere thought of being jilted by the colonel was torture to Sylvie. She lay in bed going over her desires, Pierrette’s conduct, and the song that had awakened her with the word “marriage.” Foolishly, instead of looking through the blinds to see the lover, she opened her window without reflecting that Pierrette would hear her. If she had had the instinct of a spy she would have seen Brigaut, and the fatal drama begun would never have taken place.

It was Pierrette’s duty, weak as she was, to unbar and open the wooden shutters of the kitchen, then the glass door from corridor to garden. She fetched various brooms and utensils with a care and particularity no servant, not even a Dutchwoman, gives her work. She hated reproof. Happiness for her was seeing her cousin’s cold blue eyes—not satisfied but calm—after glancing about with the look of an owner. Pierrette’s skin was moist with labor when she returned to put the kitchen in order and light the stove so she might carry up hot water to her two cousins (a luxury she never had), and to prepare the dining-room fire. For these she fetched wood from the cellar, leaving warm rooms for damp air; such sudden transitions, made quickly to escape harsh words, aggravated her condition. She did not know she was ill, yet she suffered. She began to have strange cravings; she liked raw vegetables and salads and ate them secretly. The innocent child was unaware her condition was serious and required utmost care. If Neraud, the Rogrons’ doctor, had told this to Pierrette before Brigaut’s coming she would have only smiled; life was so bitter she could smile at death. But now her feelings changed; the child, whose physical suffering was increased by Breton homesickness, was suddenly content to be in Provins. The sight of that yellow flower, the song, the presence of her friend revived her like rain on a parched plant. Unconsciously she wanted to live and even thought she did not suffer.

Pierrette slipped timidly into her cousin’s bedroom, made the fire, left the hot water, said a few words, and went to wake Rogron and do the same for him. Then she went down to take in the milk, bread, and other provisions. She stood some time on the door-sill hoping Brigaut would come; by then he was already on his way to Paris.

She had finished arranging the dining-room and was busy in the kitchen when she heard Sylvie coming down. Mademoiselle Rogron appeared in a brown silk dressing-gown with a cap, false front awry, night-gown showing above the silk, slippers down at heel. She cast an eye about and came straight to Pierrette, who awaited orders.

“Ha! here you are, lovesick young lady!” said Sylvie mockingly.

“What is it, cousin?”

“You came into my room like a sly cat and crept out the same way, though you knew I had something to say to you.”

“To me?”

“You had a serenade this morning, as if you were a princess.”

“A serenade!” exclaimed Pierrette.

“A serenade!” mimicked Sylvie. “And you’ve a lover, too.”

“What is a lover, cousin?”

Sylvie avoided answering. “Do you dare tell me a man did not come under your window and talk to you of marriage?”

Persecution had taught Pierrette the wariness of slaves; she answered bravely: “I don’t know what you mean—”

“Who means?—your dog?” said Sylvie sharply.

“I should have said ‘cousin,’” replied the girl humbly.

“And didn’t you get up and go in your bare feet to the window?—which will give you an illness; and perhaps you didn’t talk to your lover, either?”

“No, cousin.”

“You have many faults, but I did not think you told lies. You will have to explain this affair to your cousin and to me, or I shall take severe measures.”

The old maid, exasperated with jealousy and curiosity, meant to frighten the girl. Pierrette, who endured more than she had strength for, kept silence. Silence is the weapon by which such victims sometimes conquer; it baffles envy’s charges and suspicion’s skirmishings and is absolute. Sylvie watched Pierrette narrowly. The girl colored, but the color came out in patches on her cheekbones—burning and significant spots. A mother would have changed tone at once, taken the child on her lap, and questioned her; she would have seen the signs of weakness and known that digestive disturbance and other infirmities threatened the lungs. But an old maid who never awakened family instincts or knew the precautions required for adolescence had neither indulgence nor the compassionate intelligence of a mother; Pierrette’s sufferings only hardened her.

“She blushes, she is guilty!” thought Sylvie.

Pierrette’s silence thus was turned to her injury.

“Pierrette,” Sylvie continued, “before your cousin comes down we must have some talk together. Come,” she said softer, “shut the street door; if any one comes they will ring and we shall hear them.”

In spite of the damp mist rising from the river, Sylvie took Pierrette along the winding gravel path across the lawn to the rock terrace—an ornamental quay covered with iris and aquatic plants. She changed tactics, thinking firmness might catch Pierrette tripping; the hyena became a cat.

“Pierrette,” she said, “you are no longer a child; you are nearly fifteen; it is not surprising you should have a lover.”

“But, cousin,” said Pierrette, raising her eyes with angelic sweetness to Sylvie’s cold face, “what is a lover?”

It would have been impossible for Sylvie to define a lover truthfully and decently. Instead of seeing adorable innocence, she considered the question insincere.

“A lover, Pierrette, is a man who loves us and wishes to marry us.”

“Ah,” said Pierrette, “when that happens in Brittany we call the young man a suitor.”

“Well, remember that in owning your feelings for a man you do no wrong, my dear. The wrong is in hiding them. Have you pleased some of the men who visit here?”

“No.”

“Do you love any of them?”

“No.”

“Certain?”

“Quite certain.”

“Look at me, Pierrette.”

Pierrette looked.

“A man called to you this morning in the square.”

Pierrette lowered her eyes.

“You went to your window, you opened it, and you spoke to him.”

“No cousin, I went to look out and I saw a peasant.”

“Pierrette, you have much improved since your first communion; you have become pious and obedient, you love God and your relations; I am satisfied with you. I don’t say this to puff you up with pride.”

The horrible creature had mistaken submission and the silence of wretchedness for virtues.

Pierrette raised grateful eyes to her cousin, feeling she could almost forgive the suffering she had caused.

“But if it is all hypocrisy, if I find you a serpent that I have warmed in my bosom, you will be a wicked girl, an infamous creature!”

“I think I have nothing to reproach myself with,” said Pierrette, painfully shocked by the sudden change from praise to hyena tones.

“You know that to lie is a mortal sin?”

“Yes, cousin.”

“Well, you are now under the eye of God,” said the old maid, with a solemn gesture to the sky; “swear to me that you did not know that peasant.”

“I will not swear,” said Pierrette.

“Ha! he was no peasant, you little viper.”

Pierrette rushed away like a frightened fawn. Sylvie called her in a dreadful voice.

“The bell is ringing,” she answered.

“Artful wretch!” thought Sylvie. “She is depraved in mind; now I am certain the little adder has wound herself round the colonel. She has heard us say he was a baron. To be a baroness! little fool! Ah! I’ll get rid of her; I’ll apprentice her out, and soon too!”

Sylvie was so lost in thought she did not notice her brother coming down the path lamenting the frost’s injury to his dahlias.

“Sylvie! what are you thinking about? I thought you were looking at the fish; sometimes they jump out of the water.”

“No,” said Sylvie.

How did you sleep?” he began to tell his dreams. “Don’t you think my skin is getting tabid?”—a Rogron word.

Ever since Rogron had desired to marry Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf he had been uneasy about himself and his health. At this moment Pierrette came down and called that breakfast was ready. At sight of her, Sylvie’s skin turned green and yellow; her bile was in commotion. She looked at the corridor floor and declared Pierrette ought to rub it.

“I will rub it now if you wish,” said the little angel, unaware of the injury such work may do a girl.

The dining-room was irreproachably in order. Sylvie pretended through breakfast to want this and that—which she would never have thought to ask in a quieter mood—only to make Pierrette rise again and again just as she began to eat. But mere teasing was not enough; she wanted fault and was angry with herself for not finding one. She scarcely answered her brother’s remarks yet kept her eyes on him and avoided Pierrette. Pierrette, deeply conscious, brought milk mixed with cream in a large silver goblet after heating it in a bain-marie. The cousins poured coffee. When Sylvie carefully prepared hers she saw an atom of coffee-grounds floating on the surface. On this the storm broke.

“What is the matter?” asked Rogron.

“The matter is that mademoiselle has put dust in my milk. Do you suppose I am going to drink coffee with ashes in it? Well, I am not surprised; no one can do two things at once. She wasn’t thinking of the milk! a blackbird might have flown through the kitchen today and she wouldn’t have seen it! and then it was my coffee, ha! that didn’t signify!”

As she spoke she laid aside the coffee-grounds that had run through the filter.

“But, cousin, that is coffee,” said Pierrette.

“Oh! then it is I who tell lies, is it?” cried Sylvie, blasting her with a fearful flash from her eyes.

Organizations not exhausted by emotion often have much vital fluid at their service. This extreme clearness of eye in anger was marked in Mademoiselle Rogron because she often exercised it in her shop to inspire dependents with fear.

“You had better dare to give me the lie!” continued Sylvie; “you deserve to be sent from the table to eat by yourself in the kitchen.”

“What’s the matter with you two?” cried Rogron, “you are as cross as bears this morning.”

“Mademoiselle knows what I have against her,” said Sylvie. “I leave her to make up her mind before speaking to you; for I mean to show her more kindness than she deserves.”

Pierrette looked out the window to avoid her cousin’s eyes, which frightened her. “Look at her! she pays no attention to what I am saying than if I were that sugar-basin! And yet mademoiselle has a sharp ear; she can hear and answer from the top of the house when some one talks to her from below. She is perversity itself,—perversity, I say; and you needn’t expect any good of her; do you hear me, Jerome?”

“What has she done wrong?” asked Rogron.

“At her age, too! to begin so young!” screamed Sylvie.

Pierrette rose to clear the table to give herself something to do; she could hardly bear the scene. Though such language was not new, she had never grown used to it. Her cousin’s rage seemed to accuse her of a crime. She imagined what fury would be if they knew about Brigaut; perhaps they would send him away and she would lose him. All the rapid thoughts of a slave came to her, and she resolved to keep absolute silence about a circumstance her conscience told her was innocent. But the cruel words and wounding suspicion so shocked her that as she reached the kitchen she was taken with a convulsion of the stomach and turned deadly sick. She dared not complain; she was not sure anyone would help her. When she returned to the dining-room she was white as a sheet and, saying she was not well, went to bed, dragging herself up step by step by the baluster and thinking she was going to die. “Poor Brigaut!” she thought.

“The girl is ill,” said Rogron.

“She ill! That’s only shamming,” replied Sylvie loudly that Pierrette might hear. “She was well enough this morning, I can tell you.”

This last blow struck Pierrette to the earth; she went to bed weeping and praying to God to take her out of this world.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
For a month Rogron had stopped bringing the Constitutionnel to Gouraud; the colonel came instead to fetch his paper, gossip, and, when the weather allowed, take him for a walk. Certain of encountering the colonel, Sylvie dressed as coquettishly as she could — a green gown, yellow shawl edged with red, and a white bonnet with gray feathers — hoping to impress him. About the hour when the colonel usually came she stationed herself in the salon with her brother, keeping him at home in a dressing-gown and slippers.

“It is a fine day, colonel,” Rogron said as Gouraud entered heavily. “But I’m not dressed; my sister wanted to go out, and I was going to keep the house. Wait for me; I’ll be ready soon.” He left Sylvie alone with the colonel.

“Where were you going? You’re dressed divinely,” said Gouraud, noticing a certain solemnity on the pock-marked face of the old maid.

“I wanted very much to go out, but my little cousin is ill, and I cannot leave her.”

“What is the matter with her?”

“I don’t know; she had to go to bed.”

Gouraud’s distrust had been increased by his alliance with Vinet. Vinet controlled the paper, reigned as master, took the revenues; the colonel, the responsible editor, earned little. Vinet and Cournant had done the Rogrons great services; Gouraud, a half-pay colonel, could do nothing. Who was to be deputy, chief authority, or consulted by the liberals? Vinet. Moreover, Gouraud knew as well as Vinet the depth of Rogron’s passion for the beautiful Bathilde de Chargeboeuf — a passion he kept hidden, trembling at her voice, daring not to hope. When the colonel hinted he might ask for her hand, Rogron turned pale and since then had shown coldness and even hatred to Gouraud.

So Vinet reigned supreme in the Rogron household while the colonel had no hold there except by a mendacious affection for Sylvie, whose feelings were unclear. When the lawyer advised him, purportedly, to break with Sylvie and marry Pierrette, Gouraud suspected that Vinet aimed to separate him from Sylvie and, by exploiting her fears, move the Rogron property into Bathilde’s hands.

Left alone with Sylvie, the colonel’s suspicion was immediately aroused; he saw she was on guard and had planned to see him alone. He attributed the conference to Vinet’s trick and went on the defensive—alert, listening, ready. The colonel never believed a woman’s words; when Sylvie said Pierrette had gone to bed before midday, he concluded Sylvie had locked her up in jealousy.

“She is getting to be quite pretty, that little thing,” he said lightly.

“She will be pretty,” Mademoiselle Rogron replied.

“You ought to send her to Paris and put her in a shop,” Gouraud continued. “She would make her fortune. The milliners all want pretty girls.”

“Is that your advice?” Sylvie asked, troubled.

“Good!” thought the colonel. “Vinet advised me to marry Pierrette just to spoil my chance with the old harridan.” Out loud he resumed: “What else can you do with her? There’s that Bathilde de Chargeboeuf, noble and well-connected, reduced to single-blessedness—nobody will have her. Pierrette has nothing, and she’ll never marry. As for beauty, what is it? Youth and beauty are common and silly. At forty-eight,” he added to match Sylvie’s age, “after surviving the retreat from Moscow, a man is broken down; I’m nothing but an old fellow now. A woman like you would pet me and care for me, and her money, joined to my poor pension, would give me ease. I would prefer such a woman to a little minx who would worry me when I’m old. I’m too much a man to stand a popinjay like Julliard addressing verses to my wife in the newspapers. No, I will never make an age-disproportionate marriage. Between ourselves, I would not wish to have children.”

Sylvie’s face revealed nothing to the colonel, and her next question confirmed Vinet’s perfidy.

“Then you don’t love Pierrette?” she asked.

“Heavens! are you out of your mind, my dear Sylvie?” he cried. “Can those who have no teeth crack nuts? Thank God I’ve got some common-sense and know what I’m about.”

Sylvie, reassured, resolved not to show her own hand and thought herself very shrewd for putting her ideas into her brother’s mouth.

“Jerome,” she said, “thought of the match.”

“How could your brother take up such an incongruous idea? Why, only a few days ago, to find out his secrets, I told him I loved Bathilde. He turned as white as your collar.”

“My brother! does he love Bathilde?” asked Sylvie.

“Madly,—and yet Bathilde is only after his money,” thought the colonel approvingly. “I can’t understand why he told you that about Pierrette. No, Sylvie,” he said, taking her hand and drawing nearer, “since you have opened this matter, let me tell you that I desire no wife but you. Though such a marriage may look like one of convenience, I feel sincere affection for you.”

“But if I wish you to marry Pierrette? if I leave her my fortune—eh, colonel?”

“But I don’t want to be miserable in my home, and in less than ten years see verses addressed to my wife in the papers. No, I will never make a disproportionate marriage.”

“Well, colonel, we will talk seriously of this another time,” said Sylvie, casting him a glance she thought full of love, though in truth it resembled the leer of an ogress. Her thin, bluish lips drew back from yellow teeth; she thought she smiled.

“I’m ready,” said Rogron, appearing and taking off the colonel, who bowed in a lover-like way to the old maid.

Gouraud determined to press the marriage with Sylvie and make himself master of the house; he resolved to rid himself, through his influence during the honeymoon, of Bathilde and Mademoiselle Habert. During their walk he told Rogron he had been joking before; he had no real intention of aspiring to Bathilde and was not rich enough to marry a woman without fortune. He then confessed his true wish: he had long chosen Sylvie for her good qualities and aspired to be Rogron’s brother-in-law.

“Ah, colonel, my dear baron! if nothing is wanting but my consent you have it with no further delay than the law requires,” cried Rogron, delighted to be rid of his formidable rival.

Sylvie spent the morning planning the new household: to build a second storey for her brother and furnish the rest for herself and her husband; but she was still suspicious and resolved to test the colonel’s heart and ensure Pierrette had no private intercourse with him. She was determined to keep Pierrette under observation.

Pierrette came down before dinner to lay the table. Sylvie, forced to cook, had sworn at that “cursed Pierrette” for a spot on her gown—wasn’t it plain that if Pierrette had done her work properly Sylvie wouldn’t have got a grease-spot on her silk dress? Sylvie’s reasoning became a hammer: “You lied about this morning, so you lie about everything.” She battered the poor girl continually.

To Pierrette’s astonishment, Sylvie sent her to dress in her best clothes after dinner. Suspicion makes an old maid inventive; Sylvie intended to consult Vinet after searching for evidence and would keep Pierrette near to judge by her face whether the colonel had told the truth.

That evening the Chargeboeuf ladies were first to arrive. At Vinet’s advice Bathilde had dressed elaborately—blue velveteen, transparent fichu, garnet pendants, ringlets, a coquettish ribbon at her throat, black satin slippers and gray stockings; she moved with queenly manners and girlish coquetry aimed at Rogron. Her mother was calm and dignified, with an aristocratic insolence. Bathilde was intelligent; Vinet had, during their two-month stay at his house, discovered and strengthened her ambitions. Wounded by the fruitlessness of her beauty and the contempt of an age that worshipped money, she sought to marry for freedom and influence rather than for love or motherhood. Rogron was a means to the position she coveted; she expected to make a deputy of him and avenge herself on her family who had ignored her when she had no money. Vinet urged her toward this strategy.

“If I could only have married you, Bathilde, I should to-day be Keeper of the Seals,” he had said. He taught her how a wife could secure advancement for a man of modest ability, citing examples and promising to obtain a deputy seat from Provins and a place as receiver-general for Rogron. He argued that if Rogron died she could later marry a titled man; in short, place herself well and the Chargeboeufs would serve them. Vinet’s tone turned Bathilde into a small edition of Catherine de’ Medici.

Vinet came nightly to the Rogrons, presenting himself in the trappings of success—gold spectacles, silk waistcoat, white cravat, black coat, thin boots, and a gold watch. The once pale, snarling Vinet had become a man of apparent importance, with the cool assurance of a lawyer who knows the hidden places of the law. His bitterness had softened into perfidy masked by courtesy.

“Good-evening, my dear; how are you?” Madame de Chargeboeuf greeted Sylvie.

Bathilde went straight to the fireplace, took off her bonnet, looked at herself in the glass, and placed her pretty foot on the fender for Rogron to admire.

“What is the matter with you?” she said to him. “You have not bowed to me. Pray why should we put on our best velvet gowns to please you?”

She pushed past Pierrette to lay down her hat, which the girl took and held like a servant. There is no gentler cruelty than the disdain of a young woman who thinks herself superior in birth or fortune. Bathilde’s “Thank you, mademoiselle,” was a poem in scorn. She was Bathilde, the other Pierrette; the Chargeboeuf meant wealth and poise, the Lorrain meant dependence. Pierrette, small and weak, lived on charity; Bathilde, tall and lively, wore velvet and expected to be adored. The proud little Breton girl understood the tragic contrast.

“Good-evening, little girl,” Madame de Chargeboeuf said with condescending grandeur.

Vinet completed the insult by looking fixedly at Pierrette and exclaiming, “Oh! oh! oh! how fine we are to-night, Pierrette!”

“Fine!” the poor child said; “you should say that to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, not to me.”

“Oh! she is always beautifully dressed,” replied the lawyer. “Isn’t she, Rogron?” he added, turning to the master of the house and grasping his hand.

“Yes,” said Rogron.

“Why do you force him to say what he does not think?” Bathilde asked. “Nothing about me pleases him. Isn’t that true?” She went up to Rogron and stood before him. “Look at me, and say if it isn’t true.”

Rogron looked at her from head to foot and gently closed his eyes like a cat enjoying a scratch.

“You are too beautiful,” he said; “too dangerous.”

“Why?” she asked.

Rogron looked at the fire and was silent. Just then Mademoiselle Habert entered, followed by the colonel.

Celeste Habert, the common enemy to Sylvie, could only reckon Sylvie on her side; nevertheless everyone showed her civility and attention, each undermining her in his or her own way. Her brother, though no longer on the scene, was aware of what was going on and, perceiving his sister’s hopes were killed, became an implacable antagonist to the Rogrons.

Mademoiselle Habert could be pictured by anyone familiar with schoolmistresses: she had a cap worn with a lawyerly seriousness of purpose, a stern eye, a grim mouth, limp strings on her cap, and habitually took snuff rather ungracefully. Her manner was that of one who had kept an institution for young ladies.

The company took to boston. Mademoiselle Habert sat opposite Sylvie, with the colonel at her side opposite Madame de Chargeboeuf. Bathilde sat near her mother and Rogron. Sylvie placed Pierrette between herself and the colonel; Rogron set a second card-table in case more guests arrived. Two lamps on the chimney-piece and candles at forty sous a pound lit the tables.

“Come, Pierrette, take your work, my dear,” said Sylvie with treacherous softness, seeing the girl watching the colonel’s game.

Sylvie affected kindness to Pierrette before company, a deception that irritated the honest Breton and increased her contempt. Pierrette took up her embroidery but still watched Gouraud’s play. Gouraud behaved as if unaware of her presence, which Sylvie noticed and deemed suspicious. Presently she undertook a grande misère in hearts, the pool full of counters.

More guests arrived, among them the deputy-judge Desfondrilles, who had lately associated with the Vinets. He stood before the chimney-piece, back to the fire, tails of his coat over his arms, watching the salon where Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf seemed made to shine.

Silence reigned. Pierrette watched the game; Sylvie’s attention was distracted by the misère.

“Play that,” Pierrette said, pointing to a heart in the colonel’s hand.

The colonel began a sequence in hearts; the hearts lay between himself and Sylvie; he won her ace, though protected by five small hearts.

“That’s not fair!” she cried. “Pierrette saw my hand, and the colonel took her advice.”

“But, mademoiselle,” said Celeste, “it was the colonel’s game to play hearts after you began them.”

Desfondrilles smiled at the scene: he enjoyed watching Provins’s self-interests play out.

“Yes, it was certainly the colonel’s game,” said Cournant the notary, not fully aware of the quarrel’s origin.

Sylvie cast a feline, cruel look at Mademoiselle Habert.

“Pierrette, you did see my hand,” Sylvie fixed on the girl.

“No, cousin.”

“I was looking at you all,” Desfondrilles said, “and I can swear Pierrette saw no one’s hand but the colonel’s.”

“Pooh!” Gouraud said, alarmed. “Little girls know how to slip their eyes into everything.”

“Ah!” cried Sylvie.

“Yes,” continued Gouraud. “I dare say she looked into your hand to play you a trick. Didn’t you, little one?”

“No,” the truthful Breton said. “I wouldn’t do such a thing; if I had, it would have been in my cousin’s interests.”

“You know you are a story-teller and a little fool,” cried Sylvie. “After what happened this morning, do you suppose I can believe a word you say? You are a—”

Pierrette did not wait for the sentence; foreseeing a torrent of insults, she rushed away without a light and ran to her room. Sylvie turned white with anger and muttered, “She shall pay for this!”

“Shall you pay for the misère?” Madame de Chargeboeuf asked; at that moment Pierrette struck her head against the passage door left open.

“Good! I’m glad of it,” cried Sylvie as they heard the blow.

“She must be hurt,” said Desfondrilles.

“She deserves it,” replied Sylvie.

“It was a bad blow,” said Mademoiselle Habert.

Sylvie thought she might avoid paying her misère if she went to see Pierrette, but Madame de Chargeboeuf stopped her.

“Pay us first,” she said, laughing; “you will forget it when you come back.”

The remark, based on the old maid’s trickery and her bad faith in paying card debts, was approved by the others. Sylvie sat down and thought no more of Pierrette — an indifference that surprised no one. When the game was over, about half past nine, she flung herself into an easy chair at the corner of the fireplace and did not rise as the guests departed. The colonel was torturing her; she did not know what to think of him.

“Men are so false!” she cried as she went to bed.

Pierrette had given herself a frightful blow on the head, just above the ear where girls part their hair; the next day a large swelling appeared.

“God has punished you,” Sylvie said at breakfast. “You disobeyed me; you treated me with disrespect in leaving the room before I had finished my sentence; you got what you deserved.”

“Nevertheless,” said Rogron, “she ought to put on a compress of salt and water.”

“Oh, it is nothing at all, cousin,” Pierrette said.

The poor child had reached a point where even such a remark seemed to her a proof of kindness.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
The week ended as it had begun: unrelenting torment. Sylvie grew more inventive in her tyranny, refining cruelties. Pierrette bore her vague pains and the real agony in her head in silence, with a stubborn Breton resolution not to complain. Her thoughts turned constantly to Brigaut—fearful he might be endangered if discovered, but comforted that he was in Provins. Seeing him had been like an exile’s look at his country or a martyr’s glance to heaven.

Brigaut had read that look. While planing planks or taking measures, he sought a way to communicate. He chose the simplest: at a given hour Pierrette was to lower a letter on a string from her window. Sustained by the hope of contact, Pierrette clung to this arrangement; each throb of pain she silenced by saying to herself, “Brigaut is here!”

One morning Brigaut, lying in wait at the market, slipped a note to Pierrette while coolly buying fruit, joking with the seller. Though his blood was stirred, he acted with outward composure. Pierrette, inwardly agitated, pocketed the note, her cheeks burning. Sylvie, who did not notice or recognize Brigaut’s accent, paid him no attention.

That evening Pierrette read Brigaut’s letter, which told her plainly that he watched her at night, that they must exchange letters by a string, that he had earned wages, could support them back in Brittany, and that he longed to free her from the “monsters” who had reduced the colonel’s daughter to servitude. He wrote of praying for her, offering his savings and his willingness to serve her, and ended, “Not good-by, my dear, Pierrette, but to-night.” The letter sustained her so intensely that she risked fetching pen, paper, and ink from the dining-room and, before midnight, wrote him back.

Pierrette’s letter was simple and devout: she declared that only Brigaut and her grandmother loved her; she described the harsh treatment—being spoken to like a dog, forced to scrub and carry heavy baskets until her arms were cut—and spoke of feverish pains in head, bones, and back, and of hiding her tears. She pleaded to return to Brittany and to her grandmother, asked whether her grandmother had taken a sum rumored to be hers, and begged Brigaut to love her well. She fastened her letter to a crust of bread to weigh the string and lowered it at midnight. Brigaut pulled the cord, broke it free, and left. They stood a long hour exchanging looks and signs before parting; Pierrette fell asleep with his letter under her pillow.

Next morning Sylvie noticed Pierrette’s brighter face and, instead of scolding, began to spy with new, jealous watchfulness. At dinner she accused Pierrette of pretending illness to annoy them and resolved to observe her closely that evening. Pierrette, encouraged by Brigaut’s promise that he would soon bring good news and perhaps help her return to Brittany, endured the surveillance. She stayed up until midnight to lower another string—a cord she’d tied from bits of twine. Brigaut’s reply, left that night, instructed that he would cry like an owl when he came and asked patience, promising rescue or an appeal to the law if his private attempts failed. He wrote of watching her and of hoping soon to free her.

Brigaut also wrote an urgent, different letter to Madame Lorrain at Nantes, pleading that the grandmother come immediately lest Pierrette die from ill-treatment; he enclosed Pierrette’s letter and urged her to justify herself against rumors that she had taken Pierrette’s money. Brigaut feared the grandmother might be dead.

Pierrette read the second Jem of hope and clung to it like a thirsty traveler seeing palms. Though she realized Brigaut did not know the full depth of her fall, his words were salvation. Sylvie, however, had heard the owl-cry and Pierrette moving, and she rose to peer through the blinds. One night she surprised a light in Pierrette’s attic; searching, she found a cord Pierrette had forgotten and seized it. Pierrette sewed her letters into her corset and covered them with calico.

Sylvie devoted the next nights to spying. She even sent Pierrette to confession to gain an opportunity for a room search, which revealed nothing. Her fury grew into a kind of occupation; jealousy energized her. Pierrette’s fever increased; her head pains worsened. At last she fainted during a social evening when the colonel held her in his arms. Sylvie pretended Pierrette’s faint was a sham, but in unfastening her gown she felt papers under the corset. She touched them, then let the girl go, declaring the episode proof of deceit.

That night, alone and bitter, Sylvie confided in Vinet, the lawyer. He urged political pragmatism and revenge: the colonel was a gambler and in trouble; Sylvie should not marry him, but could make a party of advantage—arrange Rogron’s marriage to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and leave property away from Pierrette and Gouraud, striking both. Vinet promised to secure offices and alliances. The suggestion delighted Sylvie, who already disliked the colonel and Gouraud; she resolved to leave Pierrette with nothing.

Later, at about one in the morning, three owl-like cries—one of Brigaut’s signals—awoke Pierrette. She opened the window, threw down a ball of silk, and freed a letter, but Sylvie heard the rustle and rushed up. Seeing Brigaut running, she burst in upon Pierrette as she unwound the silk. A terrible struggle followed. Pierrette, in a desperate and noble resistance, kept the letter clenched in her hand; Sylvie, furious and merciless, seized her wrist, tried to force open her fingers, even sunk her nails in and then, in a frenzy, bit the wrist. She struck the closed fist on the window-sill and mantel, and beat it until it bled. Pierrette cried for help.

At that moment loud knocks sounded at the front door. Rogron, alarmed, opened and nearly stumbled over Brigaut, who had forced his way in with a sort of phantom beside him. Sylvie’s eyes fell on the corset and the hidden letters; she sprang to claim the prize. But a tall, gray woman—ragged, aged, noble in presence—had just appeared. The grandmother (Madame Lorrain), having arrived from Nantes, took Pierrette in her arms and carried her out with Brigaut and Frappier, leaving Sylvie aghast. The old woman cast one accusing look at Sylvie before leaving. Sylvie, trembling and perplexed, returned to bed and, later, inspected the letters she had ripped from the corset and read them with shocked curiosity but without guessing the full consequences.

The grandmother’s arrival was the culmination of a backstory: she had only recently come into forty-two thousand francs. Years earlier, after the Lorrains’ ruin, Monsieur Collinet had gone to America, rebuilt his fortune, and returned to make reparation. He restored both principal and interest to the widow, and pledged to help pay the husband’s debts—enabling Madame Lorrain to dream of living with her granddaughter again and making a match for her with Brigaut. So when Brigaut’s letter and Pierrette’s were delivered, the old woman, who had borne loneliness with resignation, hurried by the fastest coach to Provins.

At Frappier’s the sight of Pierrette—wounded, exhausted, her hand still clenched and bleeding—stole the breath from those present. Brigaut, falling on his knees, picked up the letter that had been forced from Pierrette’s fingers. The grandmother, a figure of concentrated anguish and wrath, knelt and vowed on the floor; then, seeing how ill Pierrette was, insisted they fetch the best physician in Paris. She took from a paper a bundle of bank-bills and bade Brigaut go at once. Frappier suggested consulting the local doctor, Martener, who at once came. Martener noted the bruised and bloody hand and, feeling Pierrette’s head, suspected an abscess above the left ear. He insisted she must tell all and proposed to summon a Paris surgeon for consultation. He prepared a quieting draught to give Pierrette sleep.

Under the grandmother’s patient questioning, Pierrette recounted her martyrdom: the continual insults, excessive household labors, the wounds from Sylvie’s attack and the head pain. Martener judged she had been neglected and required immediate, careful treatment. He sent Brigaut for Horace Bianchon, the celebrated physician in Paris, and the boy left by diligence. Meanwhile the grandmother—in a voice of authority earned by suffering—promised Pierrette a home and that Brigaut would live with them.

Pierrette, soothed by the presence and reassurance of her grandmother and by the sleeping draught, found some rest. The old woman watched over her, kissing brow and hands as if tending a saintly sufferer; the arrival of rescue had quieted the distraught child’s spirit.

Thus, the two letters that Brigaut had risked to deliver, the silent endurance of Pierrette, Sylvie’s malignant surveillance and brutal assault, Vinet’s political scheming, and the grandmother’s sudden return with means combined to end the immediate danger: Pierrette was removed from her persecutors into the care of a woman determined to seek the best medical help and to restore her grandchild’s comfort.

############# MIDDLE OF THE STORY. #############
################################################
At nine that morning Monsieur Martener told Monsieur Tiphaine about the scene between Pierrette and Sylvie, the moral and physical tortures the Rogrons had inflicted, and the illnesses their cruelty had produced. Tiphaine summoned Auffray the notary, one of Pierrette’s maternal relatives.

The feud between the Vinet and Tiphaine factions was bitter. Rogron supporters had spread scandalous stories about Madame Tiphaine’s mother’s liaison with the banker du Tillet and her father’s bankruptcy; private wounds in Provins quickly assumed public, partisan dimensions.

Tiphaine seized Pierrette’s case as an opportunity to humiliate the Rogrons, hosts of a salon where anti-monarchist plans and an opposition journal had emerged. The public prosecutor was notified; a secret consultation with Auffray and Martener decided to have Auffray appointed guardian so he could convene a Family Council and, backed by three doctors’ testimony, demand Pierrette’s release. The procureur, Lesourd, would push the matter toward a criminal inquiry.

By midday the town buzzed. Pierrette’s faint cries the night before became a public horror. A crowd gathered at Frappier’s shop as he told how Pierrette had been brought to him with broken fingers and a bloody hand. Doctor Bianchon’s post-chaise arrived about one; Martener and the hospital surgeon were summoned. The Rogrons were accused of deliberate ill-treatment and nearly killing their cousin.

Vinet, at the law courts, rushed to the Rogrons. Sylvie, evasive about the night, admitted nothing until pressed. Vinet warned that criminal charges might follow depending on Pierrette’s injuries. Under scrutiny Sylvie confessed jealousy and, painfully, that her suspicions were baseless. Vinet exploited their terror, insisting on a strong motive to represent them publicly; they contrived Rogron’s immediate marriage to Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf, to be announced that day with banns posted Sunday. Sylvie agreed to appear in the marriage contract as assigning her capital to her brother while retaining the income. Vinet urged antedating the contract to bind the Chargeboeufs publicly and preserve their visits. He promised to defend them if they signed.

By noon the lawyer had full authority to draw the contract and defend the Rogrons. At twelve a petition was made to Monsieur Tiphaine, sitting in chambers, against Brigaut and the widow Lorrain for abducting the minor Pierrette from her guardian’s house—turning the defenders into complainants. The judge postponed the hearing till four; Tiphaine wanted Auffray at the hearing with the doctors’ report.

The marriage announcement and Sylvie’s concessions alienated two supporters, Mademoiselle Habert and Colonel Gouraud, who ostentatiously remained with the Rogrons but meant to hurt them. When Martener mentioned Pierrette’s head injury, Céleste and the colonel told of a blow Pierrette had given herself the evening she was forced to leave the salon and recited Mademoiselle Rogron’s heartless remarks. Vinet had foreseen such opposition, but he had secured the Rogrons’ fortune for Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf and planned to use that alliance to dominate Provins socially and politically.

From midday to four the Tiphaine circle checked on Pierrette. She, suffering, was unaware of the tumult; reunited with her grandmother and Brigaut, she found comfort. Bianchon protested at such barbarity and insisted all town physicians examine the case; Dr. Neraud, friend of the Rogrons, was present. The unanimous report explained Pierrette’s malady, a sadly common but horrifying condition.

At four, President Tiphaine took his seat. Madame Lorrain, Auffray, Brigaut and others entered the court; Vinet alone faced them. He claimed strangers had forced entry into the Rogron home and abducted the minor. Auffray, as surrogate guardian, presented the physicians’ report—signed by a Paris authority and all Provins doctors—asserting that Pierrette’s near-fatal condition resulted from ill-treatment by Rogron and his sister. Auffray asked that Pierrette not be returned to the guardian’s household but remain with a family member designated by the judge and proposed convening a Family Council to consider deposing the guardian.

Vinet objected that he had not seen the report to rebut it. The judge replied that the report might have been submitted to the procureur du roi; the case was heard. Tiphaine’s order forbade returning Pierrette to her guardian until the Family Council met, commissioned the attending physician and the hospital surgeon to inspect her, and stipulated that if her injuries became alarming the criminal courts would take up the matter, without prejudice to Auffray’s civil suit.

Vinet scoffed, calling it fuss over a girl supposedly in an intrigue with an apprentice. He left to rally his party, arguing the Tiphaines made an unjust spectacle of Rogron. To his supporters, Sylvie’s intervention to seize letters and her supposed severity were natural; Rogron bore limited responsibility. Vinet added partisan color to the dispute.

Frappier’s house was later judged unsuitable for Pierrette; she was moved to Auffray’s with care and ostentation. Laid on a stretcher, accompanied by a Gray Sister and her grandmother, she passed under many windows, her pallor advantaging the Auffrays’ case. Installed on the second floor, with Martener and the hospital surgeon attending, Pierrette was tenderly cared for.

That evening exaggerations multiplied on both sides. The Rogron salon was crowded; Vinet had rallied the Liberals, the Chargeboeuf ladies dined with the Rogrons to sign the contract. He downplayed the Pierrette episode, confident the Royal courts would vindicate them; to some the Chargeboeuf alliance wiped the Rogrons’ slate clean and painted Pierrette as depraved. In Madame Tiphaine’s salon, vengeance for past slanders was taken out on the Rogrons, now monsters to be tried. Journalists offered opposing accounts: one article suggested no case existed; the Rogron paper hesitated lest it slander, advising patience for the law.

The Family Council, selected by the juge de paix, included Rogron and the two Auffrays as nearest relatives, Monsieur Ciprey (maternal nephew), Mademoiselle Habert, Pierrette’s confessor, and Colonel Gouraud, a friend of her father. The judge’s choice seemed impartial; Habert and Gouraud were considered Rogron friends. Rogron requested Vinet for the council, thereby postponing it until late December when Tiphaine would be in Paris—a delaying tactic.

Vinet spoke three hours before the council, attempting to prove an alleged intrigue between Pierrette and Brigaut to justify Sylvie’s severity, and portraying the guardian’s delegation of duties as natural. Despite his efforts the Council unanimously removed Rogron's guardianship; Auffray became guardian and Ciprey surrogate. Testimony included Adele the servant against her former masters, Mademoiselle Habert recounting Mademoiselle Rogron’s cruel remarks, and Brigaut’s letter from Pierrette describing her ill-treatment. The council concluded Pierrette’s illness resulted from neglect by the guardian, and the case would proceed publicly. Rogron, advised by Vinet, opposed the council’s report being accepted by the court. Authorities intervened as Pierrette’s condition worsened; the trial, placed on the docket, was postponed until March 1828.

Meanwhile Rogron married Mademoiselle de Chargeboeuf; Sylvie moved to the second floor to share with her. Madame Rogron supplanted Madame Tiphaine socially. Tiphaine, backed by Nucingen and du Tillet, advanced to civil judge and moved to Paris; his son inherited and sold the Provins house—evidence, in Vinet’s narrative, of Tiphaine’s contempt for the town. The promotion had little benefit to the Vinet faction, but Vinet exploited the events politically. Thus the brutal martyrdom Pierrette endured—leading to the trepanning performed by Martener under Bianchon’s advice—was mired in legal delay while she languished in agonizing pain.

Martener and the Auffrays grew fond of Pierrette and her grandmother, impressed by the grandmother’s Roman steadiness. Martener, who had begun his career in Paris but returned to provincial life, devoted himself to curing her, treating her with reverence. His dedication influenced the Auffrays’ household to remain reverent and quiet; even the children kept silence. The Auffray house, on a terrace beneath the chateau ruins overlooking the valley, became her refuge. Pierrette’s room above Auffray’s study allowed her to see the valley, a landscape she had rarely known.

Brigaut, unable to work, visited thrice daily, sorrowful and faithful. The grandmother, concealing her despair to comfort Pierrette, made her a Breton cap that recalled earlier happier times. Pierrette’s delicate features and serious expression rendered her an embodiment of melancholy; all served her with devotion. Madame Martener sent a piano to amuse her; music transfixed her. Her religious comforters admired her saintly resignation.

Martener corresponded with Bianchon and sought to drain the abscess; periodic improvements—a better appetite, some color—cheered them, but the skull disease remained grave. Bianchon came from Paris with the celebrated Desplein, who warned Martener that only a miracle could save the child: caries of the bone had begun and at her age the bone was fragile. The operation was performed in early March 1828 by the great surgeon, but Desplein cautioned the odds. Martener had even proposed a daring procedure akin to lithotrity—passing a hollow instrument into the head—that Desplein refused. Returning from Paris, Martener announced to the Auffrays, Madame Lorrain, the priests and Brigaut that science could do no more; recovery was in God’s hands. They responded with masses and vows.

While Pierrette lay dying, Vinet calumniated her in court. The judge accepted the Family Council’s report; Vinet appealed. The procureur du roi required further evidence, including testimony from Pierrette. When Desfondrilles came to the Auffrays to receive it, Pierrette was dying, with her confessor near for extreme unction. She begged forgiveness for her cousins, saying judgment belonged to God; she told her grandmother to leave all to Brigaut and to give Adele a thousand francs, insisting if Adele had stayed with the Rogrons she would not be dying.

At three o’clock on the Tuesday of Easter week, on a bright day, Pierrette died. Her grandmother kept vigil through the night and sewed her shroud with trembling fingers. Brigaut went to Frappier’s to make the coffin. Frappier watched as Brigaut planed, his plane cutting ribbons of wood wet with tears. He fashioned the coffin alone and lined it with lead; by chance the lead cost exactly the sum Pierrette had given Brigaut for her journey—the coincidence overwhelmed him. He burned his tools, settled his accounts, and bid farewell.

At the Auffray home, the room where Pierrette lay was crowded. Rogron’s emissaries stood near the body, and a surgeon in a dissecting apron prepared instruments. Madame Lorrain, on her knees, cried out; she believed they intended to open the body and probe her head as they had while she lived. Brigaut, who had just brought the coffin, seized a chisel, threatened the men, and swore he would kill any who touched her. Auffray and the curate tried to restrain him; the lawyers and doctors left. The plumber soldered the lead to make the coffin less likely to be opened. With Pierrette dead, the guardianship suit collapsed; Rogron did not dare attend her funeral.

Brigaut left Provins and walked to Paris, petitioned the Dauphiness to join the Royal guard and was admitted. When the Algerian expedition opened he sought service and rose: he became sub-lieutenant, then advanced; he distinguished himself and rose to major, later receiving a commission as an officer—always taciturn, solitary, trusted, and never wounded. He inherited forty-six thousand francs from old Madame Lorrain, who died in Paris in 1829.

In public life, Vinet rose: elected deputy in 1830, later procureur-general; secure in influence, he remained powerful. Rogron became receiver-general in the same town where Vinet held legal functions; Tiphaine, after aligning with the July dynasty, became president of the Royal court there. The ex-beautiful Madame Tiphaine and Madame Rogron lived amicably; Vinet was close with Tiphaine. Rogron, declining in health and witless, made absurd remarks; Sylvie managed his property, lent money at high interest, and lived frugally.

General Baron Gouraud married Mademoiselle Matifat, daughter of a druggist, as Vinet had arranged; he became a departmental commander and later a peer of France for his conduct during the riots under Casimir Périer. None of those involved in Pierrette’s death showed remorse. Desfondrilles, to further his prospects, was installed as president of the Provins court through Vinet’s efforts. Sylvie maintained a small circle.

Locals would later downplay the scandal. Travelers returning from Paris who had visited Mademoiselle Rogron heard softened versions blaming poor constitution or an imprudent liaison. Frappier was mocked as given to his wine; Habert’s motives were dismissed as personal.

Two men remembered Pierrette daily: Doctor Martener and Major Brigaut; they alone knew the hideous truth. To give full scale to that truth one thinks of Beatrice Cenci—another young woman condemned by intrigues like those that destroyed Pierrette. As in those cases, legality serves scoundrelism unless one believes in a higher justice.
