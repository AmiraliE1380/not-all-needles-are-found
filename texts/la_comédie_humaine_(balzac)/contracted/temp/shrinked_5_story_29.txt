EUGENIE GRANDET

I

In some provincial towns certain houses give off a melancholy like that of cloisters, moorlands, or ruins. Within them silence reigns, life seems frozen, and the routine is punctuated only by the cold, solitary figure at a window.

Such a somber air hung over a house in Saumur, at the end of a steep street leading to the château. The street, now seldom used, has a clean, dry pebble pavement and the stillness of ancient houses three centuries old. Built of wood, these buildings have unique features prized by artists and antiquarians.

Passing them, one notices great oaken beams, elaborately carved; some façades are covered with slate, others have worn window-sills barely holding flower-pots. Doors studded with large nails bear traces of history—Protestant marks or the insignia of forgotten bourgeois families. In these structures the history of France seems recorded.

Beside a dilapidated house stands a country gentleman's mansion, its stone arch still faintly showing armorial bearings—worn by revolutions since 1789. The merchants’ ground floors are not modern shops but simple dark rooms without glass fronts, where goods are exposed plainly. A young girl knitting calls to her parents to attend customers, served with varying courtesy.

In this region the weather shapes commerce. Merchants, wine-growers, and coopers watch the sky anxiously, fearing frost or rain that could ruin them. On Saturdays vineyards call their owners away and the streets fall silent, where life unfolds plainly and neighbors see one another’s doings.

The old town of Saumur, scene of this tale, contains a particularly melancholy mansion owned by Monsieur Grandet. To grasp the house’s meaning one must know the man.

Monsieur Grandet began as a master cooper in 1789 and married the daughter of a rich wood merchant. Using his savings and his wife’s dowry he bought prime vineyard land during the sale of church property. He rose in reputation as protector of local nobility and supplier to Republican armies, accumulating wealth and influence.

By 1806 Grandet was mayor and continued to swell his fortune through shrewd investments in vineyards and estates. That year he inherited three fortunes, cementing his standing in Saumur. His estate included vast vineyards, farms, and an abbey, making him among the region’s wealthiest men.

The townspeople took pride and curiosity in his wealth, speculating about its extent. He had a knack for timing sales to secure maximum profit, and his financial shrewdness made him both admired and feared.

By 1816 estimates put his fortune at nearly four million francs, and many thought he kept an equal sum in cash. His reputation overshadowed earlier mockery; his acts came to be regarded as community law. Frugality governed his life; he rarely spent beyond necessities.

Grandet’s manner was plain and unpretentious. He spoke little and stuttered if required to converse at length—a seeming lack of eloquence that concealed calculation in business. He would never commit to anything without consulting his wife, whom he kept in a subservient state.

Physically he was stout, with a round face and a crafty look. His dress was simple and practical, signaling his thrift. Only a few had access to his house, among them a young magistrate, C. de Bonfons, who hoped to marry Grandet’s daughter, Eugénie.

Social life in Saumur centred on competition for Eugénie’s hand, chiefly between the Cruchots and the des Grassins. The Cruchots, a powerful family, wanted Eugénie for their nephew; the des Grassins sought a match for their son Adolphe. This rivalry buzzed through the town with much speculation about Grandet’s intentions for his daughter.

In early 1811 the Cruchots gained advantage when the estate of Froidfond came up for sale. They negotiated its purchase for Grandet, who paid in gold—an act that further displayed his wealth and influence. This acquisition echoed through the region and confirmed Grandet as a formidable figure in Saumur.

It is now clear what is meant by “the house of Monsieur Grandet,” a cold, silent dwelling above the town, sheltered by the ruins of the ramparts. The entrance has two pillars and an arch of tufa, a soft white stone that endures only about two centuries. Numerous irregular holes, weathered by time, give the arch and walls a jail-like look. Above the arch is a crumbling bas-relief of the four seasons, topped by a plinth where plants, including a small cherry tree, have taken root.

The door is solid oak, brown and split, secured by iron bolts. A small, rusted grating in the middle panel lets inquisitive visitors glimpse a dark, slimy vault leading to a garden enclosed by thick, damp walls, remnants of the ramparts that border neighboring gardens.

The principal ground-floor room is a large hall serving as antechamber, salon, office, boudoir, and dining room. It is where the barber cuts Monsieur Grandet’s hair and where local farmers and officials conduct business. The hall, with two street-facing windows, is entirely wooden, with gray panels and a ceiling of exposed beams. An old brass clock and a greenish mirror adorn the mantel above a white stone chimney. Antique chairs in faded tapestry furnish the room, and in each corner are closets or buffets with dirty shelves.

An old card table stands between the windows, topped with a barometer and two pastel portraits of Madame Grandet’s ancestors. Red gros de Tours curtains, a rare luxury, contrast with Monsieur Grandet’s frugality. A straw chair by the window lets Madame Grandet watch passersby; a work table and Eugenie’s armchair occupy the rest. For fifteen years, Madame and Mademoiselle Grandet’s lives have revolved around constant work; fires are lit only on specific dates, and they rely on a foot-warmer for warmth in chilly months.

La Grande Nanon, the family servant, has been with Monsieur Grandet thirty-five years, earning only sixty francs a year but saving enough to invest in an annuity. Despite an unattractive appearance, she was welcomed into the household and worked tirelessly—cooking, washing, and managing domestic affairs with unwavering loyalty. Grandet’s rare acts of kindness, such as giving her an old watch, were regarded as monumental gestures.

In 1811, during a difficult grape harvest, Grandet’s treatment of Nanon combined affection and control: he depended on her labor while she remained devoted. Nanon’s gratitude for her employment and for the meager kindnesses she received formed a bond both simple and complex, reflecting the dynamics of power and dependence in their relationship.

The household displayed a stark contrast between Nanon’s careworn but efficient domain and the expectations of other servants in Saumur. Nanon’s kitchen was a model of cleanliness and order; she often worked late into the night and slept in a small closet-like space. The upper floors, in contrast to the hall’s modest comforts, were bare and unadorned.

On a November evening in 1819, Nanon lit the fire for the first time that season, coinciding with Eugenie’s birthday. The family prepared for a gathering, with local notables arriving to pay their respects. Grandet presented Eugenie with a gold coin, a tradition he had maintained for years, while Madame Grandet gave her a new dress. The family’s finances and habits were steeped in tradition, with Grandet urging Eugenie to save for her future marriage.

As the evening progressed, the arrival of guests, including the des Grassins family, introduced a competitive tone. Madame des Grassins, a lively woman, and her son Adolphe presented gifts to Eugenie, sparking jealousy among the other guests. Interactions were laced with underlying tensions: compliments and attentions masked rivalry and calculation. Grandet observed the dynamics with a mix of pride and possessiveness over his daughter.

The gathering, full of laughter and light games, concealed the deeper motivations of the attendees, who were subtly vying for a share of Grandet’s wealth. The scene blended familial warmth with cold calculations of social status, Grandet at the center, manipulating relationships for his benefit. The simplicity of Eugenie and her mother’s lives contrasted sharply with the materialism of their visitors, revealing the complexities of human relations and the hidden motives behind polite society.

As the evening unfolded, a loud knock at the door signaled an unexpected interruption that would change the course of the night.

Nanon took a candle and opened the door, followed by her master, Monsieur Grandet. His wife, alarmed, called to him, and the players exchanged worried glances. Monsieur des Grassins suggested they all go, sensing something ominous. When a young man appeared with a porter carrying trunks, Grandet brusquely told his wife to return to the game and left to speak with the newcomer.

The players sat but did not resume. Madame des Grassins asked if the traveler was from Saumur; her husband said he was from Paris. The notary noted the time, confirming the diligence was on schedule. Abbe Cruchot asked if the gentleman was young; des Grassins confirmed he was, with heavy luggage.

Eugenie remarked on Nanon's absence, and the president suggested the newcomer might be a relative. Madame Grandet gently urged them to continue, sensing her husband’s annoyance. Adolphe, trying to be discreet, hinted the young man might be a cousin, but his mother silenced him.

Grandet returned alone; the young traveler entered and received polite bows from the men and ceremonious curtsies from the women. Madame Grandet offered him warmth, but Grandet, absorbed in a letter, dismissed her concern. The stranger, Charles Grandet, thanked Eugenie for her hospitality but insisted he was not tired.

Madame des Grassins asked if he had come from the capital. Charles, adjusting his eyeglass, surveyed the room and its occupants. He noted the loto game that had been underway while Eugenie observed him with admiration and curiosity. At twenty-two, handsome and confident, he aimed to impress his provincial relatives with Parisian sophistication.

Sent by his father to stay with his uncle in Saumur, Charles intended to display his fashionable attire and lifestyle. He brought luxurious clothing and accessories—a hunting costume, various waistcoats, a gold toilet set—and, despite his father's instructions to travel modestly, had taken a private coupe to flaunt his means.

His elegance contrasted sharply with the provincial guests. The Cruchots, shabby and unkempt, were taken aback; locals reacted with both admiration and disdain at his nonchalance and fashion. Eugenie, captivated by her cousin's charm, longed for the world he represented; enchanted by his appearance and the delicate items he carried, she felt a deep desire to connect with him.

The loto ended when Nanon returned to ask for sheets for Charles’s bed. Madame Grandet followed her; the guests, sensing the evening's end, began to leave. Eugenie, seized by a new impulse, excused herself to help her mother and Nanon prepare Charles’s room, eager to make it comfortable and elegant. She suggested improvements—a warming pan, a wax candle instead of tallow—using her birthday as an excuse to persuade Nanon.

While Eugenie fussed over the room, Madame des Grassins engaged Charles, subtly vying for his attention and hinting at social opportunities in Saumur. He responded graciously, while the others speculated about his intentions and possible interest in Eugenie. Abbe Cruchot made sly comments that revealed the underlying tensions and ambitions among the guests.

Grandet read a letter from his brother that disclosed a dire financial situation and pleaded for his son to be cared for. The letter described the brother's impending bankruptcy and his hope that Grandet would shield Charles from the fallout. Grandet masked his emotions as he processed the implications; his demeanor shifted, revealing a complex interplay of familial duty and personal greed.

When the guests departed, Grandet turned his attention to his nephew and offered him a modest room, downplaying his wealth. The evening closed with speculation: Charles's presence could unsettle relationships in Saumur. The Cruchots and des Grassins recognized the need to unite against this common rival, determined to prevent Eugenie from falling for her cousin and to manipulate the situation to their advantage.

################################################
When the four relatives were left alone, Monsieur Grandet told his nephew Charles it was too late to discuss the matters that had brought him and laid out the household schedule: breakfast at eight, a light lunch at noon, dinner at five. He claimed people might think him wealthy but insisted he was actually poor and worked hard for his money. He told Nanon to bring candles for Charles; Charles assured his aunt he needed nothing and bade them goodnight.

Charles took a candle from Nanon, so worn it resembled tallow. Grandet led him through the house, avoiding the cold stairwell. As they ascended, Charles’s expectations dwindled; the staircase was shabby, and he wondered why his father had sent him to such a place. On the landing he noticed the doors, especially one that had been walled up, which led to Grandet’s office — the secretive room where Grandet conducted his business and was rumored to hoard his wealth.

Across from the office, Eugenie, Grandet’s daughter, sat in her room. After a brief goodnight she and her mother retired. Grandet showed Charles to his attic room, warning him to call Nanon if he needed anything, because the dog could be dangerous. Nanon appeared with a warming pan, which Grandet dismissed with a grumble as he left.

Charles surveyed the shabby decor of his room and questioned his father’s intentions in sending him there; he felt out of place and uncertain of his purpose. Meanwhile Eugenie rose early and began her morning routine with a new sense of beauty and desire. Dressing, she admired her reflection but still felt inadequate beside her cousin Charles.

As she dressed, she became aware of the garden and courtyard, which now seemed full of beauty where they had once seemed dull. Her heart swelled with unfamiliar emotions that mirrored her budding feelings for Charles; she longed to impress him and felt urgency to help and soften his situation.

Eugenie hurried to Nanon, asking her to prepare some cream for Charles’s breakfast. Nanon reminded her they needed Grandet’s permission for provisions. When Grandet entered, he and Nanon engaged in a rare quarrel over supplies, revealing his miserly nature. Eventually, begrudgingly, he agreed to give some flour and butter for a galette.

After breakfast Grandet took Eugenie for a walk to the fields, where they met Cruchot, the notary. Grandet talked about his land and the recent sale of poplar trees, showing his calculating side. Cruchot mentioned gossip that Charles might be a suitor for Eugenie. Grandet reacted vehemently, saying he would rather throw his daughter into the Loire than marry her to her cousin.

The declaration shocked Eugenie, dashing her hopes. She returned home troubled by her father’s harshness and what it meant for her feelings toward Charles. Back at the house, Grandet received news of his brother’s suicide — a development that would have dire consequences for Charles. As the family sat down for breakfast, the atmosphere was heavy with unspoken grief and tension.

Eugenie, feeling deep empathy for Charles, began to cry, grasping the weight of the situation for him. Grandet remained indifferent, absorbed in his own concerns. Eugenie and her mother shared a moment of mutual understanding; Eugenie expressed her desire to do something kind for Charles and, despite the risk of Grandet’s wrath, she and Nanon conspired to prepare a better breakfast for him.

As they worked, Eugenie’s affection for Charles deepened; she felt an urgent need to show him kindness and support amid their family’s troubles.

After two hours of preparation, Eugenie managed to create a simple breakfast that departed from the family's usual customs. The midday meal was typically taken standing and consisted of bread, fruit, and wine. As she set the table with an armchair for her cousin Charles, she felt anxious about her father's reaction. Madame Grandet reassured her she would take responsibility if her husband returned.

Charles, dressed with Parisian flair, joined them, cheerfully asking after their well-being. He mentioned he rarely ate before midday but was glad to have something after his difficult journey. He jokingly asked for a partridge, which Eugenie wished she could provide. As they sat, Charles’s charming demeanor captivated Eugenie; she admired his elegance and felt a flutter of affection.

Their conversation revealed the stark contrast between their lives: Charles was used to luxury, Eugenie to modesty. When Nanon brought eggs, Charles expressed delight, forgetting his earlier request. As they enjoyed breakfast, he complimented Eugenie, making her blush and dismissing the idea of ridicule—he said he disliked making fun of others.

Eugenie admired Charles’s ring; he showed it to her and their fingers briefly touched, sparking a connection. The atmosphere shifted when Nanon brought coffee, which Charles criticized as outdated. The women exchanged worried glances, anticipating Monsieur Grandet’s return.

When Grandet entered, he quickly assessed the scene with suspicion, commenting on the breakfast and noticing the sugar. He questioned its source, creating tension. Charles, unaware of the household’s dynamics, asked for sugar for his coffee, but Grandet dismissed him, suggesting he add more milk instead. Eugenie bravely set the sugar back on the table, defying her father’s oppressive control.

Grandet’s demeanor changed as he addressed Charles, hinting at bad news. The young man, still oblivious, inquired about his father's health. Grandet then revealed the devastating truth: Charles’s father had died by suicide, leaving him without means. The news struck Charles; he broke down in tears, overcome by grief.

Eugenie and her mother, witnessing his sorrow, felt deep compassion. Grandet remained detached, immediately focusing on the financial implications. He explained bankruptcy to Eugenie, emphasizing the dishonor it brought; she struggled to grasp the harsh realities he described and felt sympathy for her cousin’s plight.

Grandet said Charles would be sent to the West Indies to seek fortune. Eugenie expressed a desire to help; Grandet reluctantly agreed to fund the journey, and for a moment there was warmth between him and Eugenie. Still, the house remained heavy with grief as Charles’s cries echoed.

Eugenie and her mother went to comfort him. They found Charles in his room mourning his father. Eugenie, moved by his pain, urged him to preserve his honor, though he was lost in sorrow and could think of little else.

As they left him to grieve, Eugenie felt a stirring in her heart, recognizing the depth of her feelings for her cousin. The day transformed her perspective; she resolved to share in his mourning, a significant shift in her life. The quiet of their home was now laden with loss, and Eugenie found herself drawn into the entwined complexities of love and sorrow.

About four o’clock, a sharp knock at the door startled Madame Grandet.

“What can have happened to your father?” she asked her daughter.

Grandet entered joyfully, rubbing his hands. “Wife,” he said, “I’ve trapped them all! Our wine is sold! The Dutch and the Belgians have gone. I pretended to do nothing in the market-place, and the Belgian came to me in despair. The owners of the good vineyards are holding back their vintages, but I didn’t stop them. I made a deal for my vintage at two hundred francs a puncheon, half down. He paid me in gold; here are six louis for you. In three months, wines will have fallen.”

His quiet words were laced with sarcasm that would shock the townspeople of Saumur, already panicking over the news of his sale.

“Did you have a thousand puncheons this year, father?” Eugenie asked.

“Yes, little one.”

“Then that makes two hundred thousand pieces of twenty sous each?”

“Yes, Mademoiselle Grandet.”

“Then, father, you can easily help Charles.”

Grandet’s cold rage surfaced; he had forgotten his nephew until now. “What’s this? Ever since that dandy came into my house, everything goes wrong! You act as if you have the right to buy sugar-plums and throw feasts. I won’t have it! I know my duty! I won’t take lessons from you or anyone else. I’ll do what’s proper for my nephew, and you needn’t meddle. Don’t speak of this again, or I’ll send you to the Abbaye des Noyers with Nanon!”

“Where is that fellow? Has he come down yet?” he asked.

“No, my friend,” Madame Grandet replied.

“What is he doing then?”

“He is weeping for his father,” Eugenie said.

Momentarily at a loss, Grandet paced then retreated to his secret room to consider investing in public funds. His thinning forest land had yielded him six hundred thousand francs, and with the recent sale he had brought his total to nine hundred thousand. The prospect of earning twenty percent from the funds tempted him.

Nanon knocked to summon him to dinner. As he descended, he thought, “I’ll do it; I shall get eight percent interest. In two years, I’ll have fifteen hundred thousand francs, which I will draw out in gold.”

At dinner he learned his nephew refused to eat.

“So much saved,” he remarked.

“That’s so,” Nanon agreed.

“Bah! He won’t cry long. Hunger drives wolves out of the woods.”

Madame Grandet suggested they put on mourning.

“What will you invent next to spend money on? Mourning is in the heart, not in clothes,” he retorted.

“But mourning for a brother is necessary; the Church commands it,” she insisted.

“Buy your mourning out of your six louis. A hat-band is enough for me.”

Eugenie sewed in silence while Grandet calculated his profits. The town buzzed with news of his sale and his brother’s failure.

Nanon’s spinning was the only sound in the house.

“Nothing should be wasted,” Grandet said, lost in thoughts of future wealth.

“I will bid my nephew good-night and see if he will eat anything,” he declared.

Madame Grandet and Eugenie listened from the landing as Grandet entered Charles’s room.

“Well, nephew, you are in trouble. Yes, weep, that’s natural. A father is a father; but we must bear our troubles patiently. I am a good uncle to you. Come, take courage! Will you have a little glass of wine?” he offered, noticing the lack of light in the room. “What’s this? A wax candle! How did they filch a wax candle?”

Hearing this, Madame Grandet and Eugenie retreated to their rooms, frightened.

“Madame Grandet, have you found a mine?” Grandet asked his wife.

“My friend, wait; I am saying my prayers,” she replied, trembling.

“The devil take your good God!” he growled.

Grandet’s miserly focus on the present, disregarding any belief in a future life, reflects a broader trend where money governs laws, politics, and morals.

“Madame Grandet, have you done?” he asked again.

“I am praying for you,” she said.

“Very good! Good-night; tomorrow morning we will talk.”

She went to bed anxious about the conversation to come. Eugenie, worried for her cousin, kissed her mother’s brow. “Oh! my good mother,” she said, “tomorrow I will tell him it was I.”

“No; he would send you to Noyers. Leave me to manage it; he cannot eat me,” her mother replied.

“Do you hear, mamma?” Eugenie pressed.

“What?”

“He is weeping still.”

“Go to bed, my daughter; you will take cold.”

Thus passed a day that would weigh heavily on Eugenie. Her dormant emotions surged. Restless, she woke at intervals, imagining his suffering. In early morning light she hurried to his room and found him asleep in an armchair, exhausted from grief. She admired his handsome face, now marked by sorrow.

“Pardon me, my cousin,” he said, waking.

“There are hearts who hear you, cousin, and we thought you might need something. You should go to bed; you tire yourself by sitting thus,” she urged.

“That is true,” he replied.

“Well, then, adieu!” she said, retreating, feeling ashamed and happy.

Eugenie’s innocent boldness marked a turning point. She worried what Charles would think of her visit, hoping he would believe she loved him.

The next morning, she and her mother waited anxiously for Grandet. When he arrived, he seemed absent-minded, kissing Eugenie and sitting to breakfast without recalling his threats.

“What has become of my nephew? The lad gives no trouble,” he remarked.

“Monsieur, he is asleep,” Nanon answered.

“So much the better; he won’t want a wax candle,” Grandet said, mocking.

This unexpected kindness surprised Madame Grandet.

A poor sleeper, Grandet passed nights calculating plans, driven to win the commercial game of life. He sought to impose upon others, viewing it as a sign of power.

That day he devised a scheme to help Charles while preserving his own finances: save his brother’s honor without spending a penny, manipulating the Parisians for sport.

In Grandet’s absence, Eugenie found joy in caring for her cousin, preparing his breakfast with love. She listened for him, eager to know how he felt.

“Will you breakfast downstairs, or in your room?” she asked through the closed door.

“Where you like,” he replied.

“How do you feel?” she inquired.

“Dear cousin, I am ashamed of being hungry,” he admitted.

“Well, then, we will bring your breakfast to your room, so as not to annoy my father,” she decided, rushing to the kitchen.

Eugenie’s heart soared as she prepared for Charles’s arrival. After setting the table, she and her mother joined him, offering comfort and care.

As they shared breakfast, Charles felt the warmth of their kindness, which contrasted sharply with the indifference he had known in Paris.

Eugenie’s innocence and genuine affection drew Charles closer, and they shared a silent understanding of mutual feeling.

When Grandet returned, he found the women at their work, unaware of the emotional connection between his nephew and daughter.

After breakfast, Grandet invited the Cruchots for dinner, surprising everyone.

As the day unfolded, the atmosphere shifted, hinting at the complexities of love, family, and the burdens of wealth that would shape their lives.

At this moment, the town of Saumur was more excited about the dinner Grandet hosted for the Cruchots than it had been the previous night during the sale of his vintage, which had been seen as a betrayal of the wine-growing community. Grandet, who considered himself superior to the community he often deceived, paid little heed to their opinions.

The des Grassins soon learned of Guillaume Grandet's failure and death and decided to visit that evening to offer sympathy and to understand why Grandet had invited the Cruchots. At five o'clock, Monsieur C. de Bonfons and his uncle, the notary, arrived in their Sunday best. The dinner was somber: Grandet grave, Charles silent, Eugenie mute, and Madame Grandet speaking little, making it a proper meal of condolence. After dinner Charles asked to retire to write a letter.

Once the women left, Grandet deployed the shrewdness that had earned him the nickname "the old dog." Had he aimed higher than the mayoralty of Saumur, he might have been a national asset; beyond Saumur he would have struggled.

“M-m-mon-sieur le p-p-president, you said t-t-that b-b-bankruptcy--” Grandet began, using his stutter, a practiced tactic learned long ago to manipulate conversations. The Cruchots, impatient, began to mimic him. The stutter, taught by a past encounter with a cunning Jew, helped him frustrate opponents; now he needed to avoid responsibility while steering the discussion.

“M-m-monsieur de B-B-Bonfons,” he stammered again, “you-ou said th-th-that b-b-bankruptcy c-c-could, in some c-c-cases, b-b-be p-p-prevented b-b-by--”

“By the courts of commerce themselves. It is done constantly,” replied Monsieur C. de Bonfons, trying to help.

Grandet feigned humility and listened. The president explained how a respected man facing insolvency could have a receiver appointed by the court, distinguishing liquidation from failure. Grandet, eager to understand, asked if liquidation could be managed without the courts.

“Yes, if the merchant does not file his schedule and no creditor appears, the family or heirs can liquidate the business,” the president said.

“Ah!” the notary interjected. “That would be the right thing to do. You will save your name.”

Grandet acknowledged his brother bore the Grandet name and weighed the advantages of liquidation for his nephew, while disclaiming knowledge of Parisian tricks and stressing his focus on vineyards and business in Saumur.

“B-b-because, don’t you see, Monsieur de B-Bonfons, a man must l-l-look b-b-before he l-leaps,” he said, wanting to be sure he grasped the implications.

The president assured him debts could be bought at a discount, and handled properly, Grandet could emerge unscathed. Grandet asked how negotiating debts worked.

“Legally, if you acquire title to all the debts, your brother or his heirs will owe nothing,” the president explained.

“Very g-good,” Grandet repeated, still processing.

The president offered to go to Paris to negotiate with creditors, suggesting Grandet could provide some assets to facilitate the process. Grandet hesitated to commit, the notary urged clarity, and then the des Grassins arrived, interrupting.

They expressed condolences for the family's misfortunes, and the notary, eager to present Grandet well, declared he intended to liquidate his brother's debts honorably. This surprised the des Grassins, who had expected selfishness.

“Ah! I was sure of it,” exclaimed the banker, praising Grandet's honor.

Grandet, cautious, said he would not bind himself to anything beyond his means. Madame des Grassins proposed she could go to Paris, attempting to outmaneuver the president. Grandet, favoring the banker, pulled him aside to discuss a separate investment in government bonds.

“Say nothing to the Cruchots; that’ll annoy them,” Grandet whispered, preferring discretion about his plans.

Des Grassins agreed to leave the next day and to meet again for final instructions. After they departed, Grandet said he needed time to deliberate.

As news of Grandet's supposed generosity spread, the town began to admire him, forgiving his earlier action against the wine community. The enthusiasm was fleeting, reflecting the townspeople's tendency to quickly forget past grievances.

After closing the door, Grandet summoned Nanon and instructed her to keep quiet about his plans. He prepared for a secret journey to sell gold in Angers while the market was favorable. He took precautions to avoid attracting attention, ensuring no one would suspect his departure or the true purpose of his trip.

In the early hours, as he and Nanon prepared to leave, Eugenie, worried about Charles, thought she heard a cry and considered checking on him, but was distracted by seeing her father and Nanon carrying a heavy keg. Grandet, focused on his plans, told Nanon to tell his wife he had gone to the country and hurried off in the carriage, determined to reach Angers before morning.

“My father has gone,” thought Eugenie, listening from the top of the stairs. Silence enveloped the house, and the distant rumble of the carriage faded. Suddenly she heard a cry from her cousin’s room, a sound that pierced the walls. A thin line of light shone through the door, illuminating the old staircase.

“He suffers!” she exclaimed, rushing up. A second moan brought her to the landing. The door was ajar; she pushed it open. Charles was asleep, his head drooping over the armchair, pen fallen from his hand. His labored breathing alarmed Eugenie, and she entered the room.

“He must be very tired,” she thought, noticing sealed letters on the table addressed to various tradesmen. “He’s settling his affairs to leave France at once.” Her gaze fell on two open letters, and the words “My dear Annette” struck her like a blow. Her heart raced, and she felt rooted to the spot.

“His dear Annette! He loves! He is loved! No hope! What does he say to her?” Thoughts swirled. “Should I read it? No, I mustn’t.” Curiosity overwhelmed her. She looked at Charles, gently adjusted his head, and kissed his hair. “Dear Annette!” a voice screamed in her mind.

“I must read it,” she decided, turning away in shame. For the first time she felt the struggle between good and evil within her. Passion and curiosity won out. As she read, her heart swelled; the intensity of her feelings made the joys of first love even more precious.

My dear Annette,--Nothing could have separated us but the great misfortune that has now overwhelmed me. My father has killed himself; his fortune and mine are lost. I am orphaned at an age when I am still a child, yet I must rise as a man from this abyss. I have spent half the night facing my position. If I wish to leave France an honest man, I have not a hundred francs to try my fate in the Indies or America. I cannot remain in Paris, where the disdain shown to a ruined man would kill me. Your love cannot draw me back. Alas! I have no money to go to you for a last kiss to give me strength for my forlorn enterprise.

“Poor Charles! I did well to read the letter. I have gold; I will give it to him,” thought Eugenie, wiping her eyes and continuing.

I have never thought of the miseries of poverty. I have not even a sou for an outfit. If I have nothing, I shall go to Nantes and ship as a common sailor, starting anew like others who have begun without a sou and returned wealthy. I have faced my future coolly. It seems more horrible for me than for others because I have been so petted by my parents and blessed by your love. Such happiness could not last. Nevertheless, I feel more courage than a careless young man is supposed to feel. Dear Anna, if to keep me in Paris you were to sacrifice your luxury, we would still not have enough for my extravagant living. I would never accept such sacrifices. We must part now and forever—

“He gives her up! Blessed Virgin! What happiness!” Eugenie quivered with joy. Charles stirred, and a chill of terror ran through her, but he did not wake, and she resumed reading.

When shall I return? I do not know. The climate of the West Indies ages a European, especially one who works hard. In ten years, your daughter will be eighteen; she will be your companion, your spy. Society will be cruel, and your daughter perhaps more so. Keep in your soul the memory of our happiness, and be faithful, if you can, to the memory of your poor friend. I cannot exact such faithfulness, for I must conform to my new life; I must think of marriage, which becomes a necessity. I have found a cousin here in Saumur whose face, manners, and heart would please you—

Eugenie noted the letter’s abrupt end. She defended him, thinking how an innocent girl might not perceive the coldness in his words. To young girls raised in purity, all is love when they enter the realm of passion. They see their lover through the lens of their own emotions. Eugenie’s heart echoed with the words, “My dear Annette, my loved one,” as if they were the sweetest language of love.

Charles, though mourning his father, was more affected by the loss of his fortune than by genuine feelings. His parents had indulged him, preventing him from making the harsh calculations many sons face. The liberality of his father had instilled in him a real love, devoid of self-interest.

Yet Charles was a product of Paris, taught by society to calculate everything. He had been molded by a world in which more crimes are committed in thought than punished. He had learned to admire nothing and to see self-interest as the driving force behind actions. Annette had compelled him to think seriously about his future, teaching him lessons of worldly prudence.

“You are very foolish, Charles,” she would say. “I shall have a great deal of trouble teaching you to understand the world. You behaved extremely ill to Monsieur des Lupeaulx. I know he is not honorable, but wait until he is out of power, then you may despise him.”

Charles had not yet applied the maxims of Parisian morality; he still possessed the beauty of inexperience. However, he had been unknowingly inoculated with selfishness. The seeds of Parisian political economy lay dormant in his heart, waiting to emerge when he faced real life.

Most young girls succumb to the tender promises such appearances offer. Eugenie, despite her innocence, was enchanted by Charles’s demeanor, unaware of the coldness in his heart. A chance encounter with his last heartfelt words stirred her emotions. She laid down the letter, filled with love, and watched her sleeping cousin, vowing to love him always. She then glanced at the other letter, seeking further proof of his noble qualities.

My dear Alphonse,--When you receive this letter, I shall be without friends. While I doubt the world’s friendship, I have never doubted yours. Please settle my affairs and get as much as you can from my possessions. I intend to go to the Indies. I have written to those I owe money, and you will find a list of their names. I do not wish to keep anything except perhaps a few baubles for my journey. I will send you a power of attorney for the sales. Keep Briton for yourself; I would rather give him to you than see him sold. Farry, Breilmann, & Co. built me a comfortable carriage, which they have not yet delivered; persuade them to keep it without payment. If they refuse, do what you can to avoid dishonor in my present circumstances. I owe the British Islander six louis, which I lost at cards; don’t fail to pay him—

“Dear cousin!” whispered Eugenie, running back to her room with a candle. A thrill of pleasure coursed through her as she opened an old oak cabinet and found a red velvet purse with gold tassels, a relic from her grandmother. She weighed it in her hand and began counting her hidden treasures: twenty portugaises, five genovines, three gold quadruples, a hundred Dutch ducats, and a few rare rupees. She realized she possessed about five thousand eight hundred francs, which could fetch nearly six thousand.

Filled with joy, she thought only of her cousin and ran upstairs without hesitation. The secret misery of Charles made her forget propriety. Standing at the threshold, she held the candle and purse; when Charles awoke, he was speechless with surprise. She placed the candle on the table and said, “My cousin, I must beg pardon for a wrong I have done you; but God will pardon me if you will help me to wipe it out.”

“What is it?” he asked, rubbing his eyes.

“I have read those letters.”

Charles blushed. “How did it happen?” he asked.

“I do not know. I am tempted not to regret reading them; they have shown me your heart, your soul, and your plans.”

“My dear cousin—”

“Hush! Not so loud; we must not wake others. Here,” she said, opening her purse, “are the savings of a poor girl who wants nothing. Charles, accept them! This morning I was ignorant of the value of money; you have taught me. It is just a means. A cousin is almost a brother; you can surely borrow from your sister.”

Eugenie, confident, was taken aback by his silence. “Oh! you will not refuse?” she implored, kneeling. “I will never rise until you have taken that gold! My cousin, I implore you, answer me! Let me know if you respect me, if you are generous—”

As he heard her noble distress, tears fell from Charles onto her hands. Eugenie sprang to the purse and poured its contents onto the table. “Ah! yes, yes, you consent?” she cried, weeping with joy. “Fear nothing, my cousin; you will be rich. This gold will bring you happiness; someday you shall return it to me—are we not partners?”

Charles finally expressed his feelings. “Yes, Eugenie; my soul would be small indeed if I did not accept. And yet—gift for gift, confidence for confidence.”

“What do you mean?” she asked, frightened.

“Listen, dear cousin; I have here—” He pointed to a square leather-covered box. “This is something as precious to me as life itself. My mother would sell the gold she lavished on this dressing-case, but I cannot. Eugenie, you shall be its guardian. Never did friend commit anything more sacred to another. Let me show it to you.”

He opened the box, revealing a beautifully crafted dressing-case. “What you admire is nothing,” he said, revealing a hidden drawer. “Here is something worth the whole world.” He drew out two portraits, masterpieces of Madame Mirbel, set with pearls.

“Oh, how beautiful! Is it the lady to whom you wrote that—”

“No,” he replied, smiling. “This is my mother, and here is my father, your aunt and uncle. Eugenie, I beg you, keep my treasure safely. If I die and your fortune is lost, this gold and these pearls will repay you. You alone are worthy to keep them. But destroy them at last, so they may pass into no other hands.” Eugenie was silent. “Ah, yes, say yes! You consent?” he urged.

Hearing the same words she had used to him, she looked at him with love, her first look of womanhood. He took her hand and kissed it. “Angel of purity! Between us, money is nothing. Feeling, sentiment, must be all henceforth.”

“You are like your mother—was her voice as soft as yours?”

“Oh! much softer—”

“Yes, for you,” she said, lowering her eyelids. “Come, Charles, go to bed; I wish it; you must be tired. Good-night.” She gently disengaged her hand, and he followed her to her room, lighting the way. At the threshold, he said, “Ah! why am I ruined?”

“What matter? My father is rich; I think so,” she replied.

“Poor child!” said Charles, leaning against the wall. “If that were true, he would not have let my father die; he would not let you live this way.”

“But he owns Froidfond.”

“What is Froidfond worth?”

“I don’t know; but he has Noyers.”

“Nothing but a poor farm!”

“He has vineyards and fields.”

“Mere nothing,” Charles said disdainfully. “If your father had only twenty-four thousand francs a year, do you think you would live in this cold, barren room?” He glanced at the old cabinet. “Ah! there you will keep my treasures,” he said.

“Go and sleep,” she urged, preventing him from entering her disordered room.

They exchanged good-nights with smiles and fell asleep in the same dream; from that moment Charles began to wear roses with his mourning.

The next day, before breakfast, Madame Grandet found Eugenie in the garden with Charles. He was still sad, burdened by his misfortune. “My father will not be home until dinner,” Eugenie said, noticing her mother’s anxious look.

The connection between Eugenie and Charles was evident in their shared thoughts and Eugenie’s sweet voice. Charles spent the morning in the hall, his sadness respected by the three women, each occupied with tasks. Grandet had neglected his affairs, and tradesmen came to discuss business. Madame Grandet and Eugenie listened to the endless chatter of workmen and farmers, while Nanon prepared the produce they brought.

Around five, Grandet returned from Angers, having made fourteen thousand francs from the exchange on his gold. He had left Cornoiller at Angers to look after the horses, which were nearly foundered.

“I am hungry,” he said upon entering.

Nanon called from the kitchen, “Haven’t you eaten since yesterday?”

“Nothing,” he replied.

Nanon brought in soup just as Des Grassins arrived to take Grandet’s orders. Grandet had not even noticed his nephew. “Go on eating, Grandet,” said the banker. “Do you know what gold is worth in Angers? They have come from Nantes after it? I shall send some of ours.”

“Don’t send any,” Grandet replied. “They have enough. We are such old friends, I ought to save you from a loss of time.”

“But gold is worth thirteen francs fifty centimes.”

“Say was worth—”

“Where have they got any?”

“I went to Angers last night,” Grandet said quietly.

The banker was astonished, and a whispered conversation began between them, often glancing at Charles. Des Grassins looked surprised, likely learning of Grandet’s plans to invest a sum that would yield him a hundred thousand francs a year.

“Monsieur Grandet,” Des Grassins said to Charles, “I am going to Paris; if you have any commissions—”

“None, monsieur, I thank you,” Charles replied.

“Thank him better than that, nephew. Monsieur is going to settle the affairs of the house of Guillaume Grandet.”

“Is there any hope?” Charles asked eagerly.

“What!” exclaimed his uncle with feigned pride. “Are you not my nephew? Your honor is ours. Is not your name Grandet?”

Charles rose, kissed his uncle, turned pale, and left the room. Eugenie looked at her father with admiration.

“Well, good-bye, Des Grassins; it is all in your hands. Decoy those people as best you can; lead them by the nose.”

The two men shook hands, and Grandet returned to his armchair, asking Nanon for some black-currant ratafia. Too excited to sit still, he got up, looked at a portrait, and began to sing, doing what Nanon called his dancing steps.

“Dans les gardes francaises
J’avais un bon papa.”

Nanon, Madame Grandet, and Eugenie exchanged silent glances. Grandet’s joviality frightened them when it reached its peak. The evening passed quickly, and Grandet chose to go to bed early, expecting everyone else to follow suit. As for Madame Grandet, she lived according to her husband’s will. During the two hours of digestion, Grandet, more jovial than ever, shared his peculiar sayings, one of which captured his mindset: “You have no sooner put your lips to a glass than it is empty! Such is life. You can’t have and hold. Gold won’t circulate and stay in your purse. If it were not for that, life would be too fine.” He was cheerful and generous, offering Nanon some ratafia, which she gladly accepted.

The old man, preoccupied with avarice and the prospect of sending Charles away without further expense, remained indifferent to the children’s interactions. He busied himself with agricultural tasks, leaving the two young people free to express their feelings under Madame Grandet’s watchful eye.

For Eugenie, love blossomed in the wake of their shared secret. Their relationship deepened, marked by tender glances and gentle words suggestive of childhood innocence. Eugenie took joy in comforting Charles, seeking to alleviate his pain with the sweetness of new love. Their connection, forged in mourning, felt pure and sacred. Charles, having known only the tumult of worldly passion, embraced this genuine affection and appreciated the simplicity of their lives together.

After breakfast, while Grandet attended his fields, Charles enjoyed the pleasure of being with Eugenie and her mother, finding beauty in their modest existence. He had once thought such morals existed only in Germany, but now he saw them as real and enchanting. Eugenie became his ideal, and he cherished her innocence; she surrendered to the current of their love, unaware of the separation that loomed.

Three days after des Grassins’ departure, Grandet took Charles to the Civil courts so he could renounce his rights to his father’s estate. The act felt like a painful betrayal. Charles also arranged powers of attorney and obtained a passport, selling his belongings to prepare for the journey. Grandet admired Charles’s new appearance in mourning clothes, seeing him as a man ready to seek his fortune.

Charles gathered his valuables intending to sell them, but Grandet offered to buy them instead, valuing them at nearly a thousand francs. Grateful, Charles accepted. He gifted his mother’s thimble to Madame Grandet, who was deeply touched. Grandet then insisted on paying for Charles’s passage to the Indies, further cementing their bond.

As Charles prepared to leave, Eugenie expressed her sadness; he acknowledged the weight of his obligations. He had changed from boy to man, and Eugenie admired his newfound strength. The family donned mourning attire for a Requiem Mass for Guillaume Grandet, and during breakfast Charles received letters from Paris about his affairs, which he shared with Eugenie.

In the garden, Charles revealed his affairs were well managed and that he had arranged for his belongings to be sent to Nantes. He spoke plainly about their impending separation and urged Eugenie not to tie her future to his uncertain fate. Despite this, their love remained strong, and they shared a passionate kiss, affirming their bond.

When Charles announced his departure, Grandet showed unexpected interest, personally packing Charles’s things and ensuring they were sent to Nantes. The day of departure was emotional as the family escorted him to the diligence. Grandet offered measured encouragement, and Charles expressed gratitude for his uncle’s support.

After Charles left, the house felt empty to Eugenie, who wished to keep his room as it was. She and Nanon conspired to preserve the status quo, hoping for his return. Eugenie’s beauty changed as she embraced her love, becoming more radiant and dignified. She followed Charles’s voyage on a map, longing for his well-being and reflecting on their moments together.

Eugenie’s love became a constant presence, filling her thoughts and shaping her days. While she maintained a cheerful facade before her father, she confided in Nanon about her feelings for Charles. Nanon, despite her own unfulfilled desires, empathized with Eugenie, recognizing the depth of her mistress’s love and the pain of their separation.

Charles lived in their thoughts; Eugenie often studied her aunt’s portrait searching for his features. One Sunday, Madame Grandet discovered the truth about the gold Eugenie had given Charles, and panic followed.

“What will you say to your father on New Year’s Day when he asks to see your gold?” Madame Grandet cried, terrified. The two women spent a morning in dread, missed high Mass, and feared the drama ahead.

Madame Grandet fretted, “What will become of us?” Her anxiety left her unprepared for winter and chilled by her husband’s impending wrath. She suggested consulting Monsieur des Grassins in Paris, but Eugenie insisted it was too late.

On January 1, 1820, a cold morning, Madame Grandet asked for a fire in her room, hoping to avoid a confrontation. Grandet, in a surprisingly good mood, agreed and revealed he had received a large sum from his investments. As he prepared breakfast he boasted about his financial success, oblivious to the tension. The two women wished him a happy New Year, but his talk of money only deepened their unease.

After breakfast Grandet demanded to see Eugenie’s gold and insisted she return it. Cornered, Eugenie claimed she no longer had it. Grandet’s anger grew; he accused her of deceit and demanded to know what she had done with the gold. Eugenie defended her actions, asserting her independence. Grandet, furious, threatened punishment: confinement to her room on bread and water until she obeyed. Heartbroken, Eugenie fled to her mother, who was also strained by the situation.

Grandet, still seething, accused Eugenie of giving the gold to Charles, whom he deemed unworthy. Madame Grandet pleaded for their daughter’s forgiveness, but Grandet remained obstinate, insisting on his authority. Eugenie, feeling the weight of his anger, resolved to stand firm in her love for Charles. Nanon, the servant, feared for Eugenie and vowed to support her.

That evening Grandet dined alone for the first time in years. Regular guests arrived, surprised by the absence of Madame Grandet and Eugenie; Grandet dismissed their concern, yet the household’s tension was palpable.

Later, Nanon secretly brought Eugenie a hare pâté, determined she would not live on bread alone. Their bond deepened as they navigated Grandet’s harshness and the looming threat of his wrath, united in secrecy and quiet resistance.

For several months the old wine-grower visited his wife’s room at all hours, never mentioning their daughter Eugenie, who remained confined. Madame Grandet's health deteriorated, and Grandet grew harsher and unyielding, even making mistakes in business. The townspeople speculated about the family, and it became known that Eugenie was kept in her room on bread and water by her father's orders, with only Nanon providing secret meals. The community condemned Grandet, and when Eugenie attended Mass her gentle demeanor drew curiosity and sympathy.

Eugenie’s chief sorrow was her mother’s failing health. She often blamed herself for Madame Grandet’s suffering, and though her mother tried to comfort her, she longed for Eugenie’s happiness. Madame Grandet urged reconciliation between father and daughter, asking Grandet to bring Eugenie back into the family, but he remained indifferent as his wife's condition worsened.

As spring approached, Madame Grandet confided her worries to the Cruchots, who were appalled by Eugenie’s treatment and discussed the legal implications of her confinement. Eugenie, overhearing them, insisted on obedience to her father and begged them to remain silent, believing criticism would tarnish the family's honor.

One day Grandet, walking in the garden, hesitated between his stubbornness and a desire to embrace his daughter. At that moment the notary Cruchot visited, warning him about his wife’s health and the potential consequences of her death for Eugenie’s inheritance. Realizing the legal ramifications—how her inheritance could force him to sell property—Grandet grew agitated at the thought of losing control over his wealth.

Cruchot advised that Grandet treat Eugenie kindly to avoid legal and financial trouble. Feeling cornered, Grandet began to reconsider his harshness. He returned home determined to reconcile, but found Eugenie and her mother admiring a dressing-case of gold given to Eugenie by Charles. Seeing the gold, Grandet became possessive and aggressive, prompting a confrontation in which Eugenie pleaded for the dressing-case to be returned to its rightful owner.

In desperation Eugenie threatened to harm herself if her father did not relent. Shocked, Grandet ultimately backed down and allowed her to keep the dressing-case. He then tried to show affection to his wife and daughter, scattering coins on the bed and promising to be kinder.

Despite this temporary tenderness, Madame Grandet’s health continued to decline and in October 1822 she died peacefully. Her last words to Eugenie emphasized seeking happiness in heaven and expressed deep concern for her daughter's future in a selfish world.

On the day after her father's death, Eugenie felt a renewed attachment to her childhood home, where she had endured much suffering, including her mother's loss. Familiar objects moved her to tears, and she was surprised by her father's sudden tenderness. He took her to breakfast and watched her with an almost kind gaze, prompting the household to think his mind was failing. But during the family’s mourning dinner he revealed his intent.

“Dear child,” he said after the meal, “you are now your mother’s heiress, and we have some matters to settle.” Despite Eugenie's reluctance, he pressed to settle inheritance issues that evening. The notary, Maitre Cruchot, explained Grandet did not want to divide property or pay taxes on possible funds; to avoid this, he needed Eugenie to renounce her rights to her mother’s estate so he could retain control.

Confused, Eugenie asked for the deed to sign. Grandet, visibly moved, proposed instead that she trust him and accept a monthly allowance of a hundred francs. She agreed; he clasped her hand and declared they were quits. The next morning she signed away her inheritance. A year later he had not paid the promised money. When she reminded him, he awkwardly offered jewels.

Over the next two years Grandet trained Eugenie in running the household and finances, instilling his frugality. By the third year she became mistress of the house, and their monotonous life went on for five years. Eugenie's sadness was well known; she never spoke of it. Her only company was the Cruchots and their friends, who played whist with her father.

In 1827 Grandet, now eighty-two, suffered a stroke. As he declined Eugenie devoted herself to his care, feeling increasingly isolated. His death was uneventful; he remained fixated on hidden wealth until the end. When the priest came for last rites, Grandet's avarice surfaced in his final words, urging Eugenie to take care of his riches.

After his death Eugenie was left alone with only Nanon, her loyal servant and sole confidante. Maitre Cruchot informed her she had inherited seventeen million francs. Despite this fortune, her thoughts centered on finding her cousin Charles. In the empty house she and Nanon lamented their isolation while news of her wealth spread through the province.

Eugenie’s first act was to secure an annuity for Nanon, who soon married Antoine Cornoiller and became Eugenie’s housekeeper. Cornoiller managed the estate; life for the farmers continued unchanged. At thirty Eugenie had never known joy; her childhood had been shadowed by her mother’s suffering and an unfulfilled love for Charles, who had left her with only memories.

Her mourning delayed any thoughts of marriage, and the Cruchots surrounded her with attention, hoping to win her favor. They praised her beauty; flattery became daily. Monsieur de Bonfons, a prominent acquaintance, sought to ingratiate himself with her while the Cruchots discussed their own prospects, Eugenie remaining the center of attention.

As spring approached Madame des Grassins tried to promote a match between Eugenie and the Marquis de Froidfond, stressing his noble lineage. Eugenie’s indifference was misread as interest, fueling speculation about her future. Despite the attention she was haunted by Charles’s absence; he had not contacted her in seven years.

While events unfolded in Saumur, Charles prospered in the Indies. His commercial ventures initially yielded six thousand dollars, and he quickly learned that trading in human beings was the most profitable enterprise. He went to Africa, bought enslaved people, and combined that trade with other lucrative goods. Ambition drove him: he wanted to return to Paris with a fortune and regain a higher social position.

Experience changed Charles. He grew skeptical, lost a moral compass, and came to see that what one society condemned could be applauded in another. His heart hardened; he became ruthless, engaging in exploitative trades, customs fraud, and buying pirate-captured goods cheaply to resell at profit. Although he once carried memories of Eugenie, his various escapades distanced him from his past; he began to view his family with contempt and regarded Eugenie as no more than a creditor.

This explains Charles Grandet’s silence. In the Indies and elsewhere he used the alias "Shepherd" to protect his identity while pursuing wealth by unscrupulous means. His efforts succeeded: by 1827 he returned to Bordeaux aboard the Marie Caroline with gold-dust worth nearly two million francs, planning further profit at the Paris mint.

On the ship he met Monsieur d’Aubrion, a gentleman tied to the royal court, returning with his family. The d’Aubrions, once prominent, were now financially strained. Madame d’Aubrion, eager to secure her family’s future, sought a wealthy husband for her plain daughter. She viewed Charles as a suitable son-in-law and promised to help him obtain a noble title and a comfortable income through marriage. The match would raise both families’ status and allow them to live comfortably in Paris.

Intoxicated by ambition, Charles entertained the marriage to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion as a path to social prominence. He consulted Annette, who encouraged him, recognizing the advantage of securing an influential, affluent husband for her friend. Now more confident and assertive after his Indies career, Charles felt ready to embrace this new life.

Des Grassins, aware of Charles’s return and impending marriage, approached him about settling his father’s debts. Charles, newly wealthy, dismissed Des Grassins’s concerns, asserting that his father’s financial failures were no longer his problem. He was preoccupied with his new identity as the Comte d’Aubrion and believed wealth would insulate him from his family’s past.

In August, Eugenie sat on the bench where she and Charles had once pledged their love, reflecting on their past. She received a letter from him that she opened with trembling expectation and dread. The letter detailed his success and changed life but delivered a painful blow: he was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion in a marriage of convenience intended to secure his social ambitions. He declared that love in marriage was a delusion and that their youthful dreams could not be pursued given the divergence of their lives.

Eugenie was devastated, feeling abandoned and heartbroken. Her enduring love for Charles sharply contrasted with his pragmatic approach to marriage. As she absorbed the news, she remembered her mother’s warning about suffering and endurance. In her sorrow she resolved to live a life of prayer and acceptance, acknowledging that her dreams had been shattered.

Eugenie returned from the garden with care, avoiding the corridor haunted by memories of her cousin. The day was pivotal: the parish priest, related to the Cruchots and allied with Monsieur de Bonfons, had been urged to talk to her about marriage. Eugenie, consumed by unhappiness, expressed a wish to withdraw from the world and live only for God. Madame des Grassins arrived, driven by a blend of vengeance and despair, to reveal that Charles Grandet had returned from the Indies and was engaged to Mademoiselle d’Aubrion. Taken aback, Eugenie kept her composure. Madame des Grassins produced a letter from her husband outlining Charles’s circumstances, including his father’s bankruptcy and the marriage’s implications. Contempt and sorrow filled Eugenie; she returned the letter unread.

Eugenie asked the priest whether remaining a virgin after marriage would be a sin, betraying her inner conflict. When he left, she sequestered herself in her father’s secret room, skipped dinner, and avoided guests. Later, when she joined the company, curiosity about her situation charged the evening. At a surprising moment she asked Monsieur de Bonfons to stay—an act taken as a promise of marriage.

Privately, Eugenie told the president she could offer only friendship and requested that he settle her uncle’s debts in exchange for her hand. The eager president accepted. After he departed, Eugenie wept, confronting the weight of her choice.

The president hurried to Paris, met creditors, and arranged payment of all debts, interest included. He then carried a letter from Eugenie to Charles explaining her decision and the conditions of their relationship. Charles, initially excited, was shocked upon learning of Eugenie’s fortune and the consequences of her actions.

Madame d’Aubrion, unaware of the president’s interventions, remained confident of the marriage. The president returned to Saumur and announced his marriage to Eugenie. Despite her wealth, she remained devoted to charitable works and retained a simple lifestyle. Her husband embarked on a political career but died shortly after being elected deputy, truncating his ambitions.

Widowed, Eugenie lived a life of piety and charity, dedicating her fortune to the community while eschewing worldly desires. Rumors of a new marriage, notably involving the Marquis de Froidfond, circulated, but Eugenie remained resolute in her independence, sustained by loyal servants Nanon and Cornoiller.

Eugenie’s story highlights the tension between wealth and personal fulfillment, showing her struggle to meet societal expectations while staying true to her values. She became revered in her community, embodying both the burdens and blessings of inheritance.
